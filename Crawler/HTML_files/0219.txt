	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Necronym</h1>

<p>A <b>necronym</b> (from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> words νεκρός, <i>nekros</i>, "dead" and ὄνομα <i>ónoma</i>, "name") is a reference to, or <a href="/wiki/Name" title="Name">name</a> of, a person who has <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">died</a>.  Many <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">cultures</a> have <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">taboos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tradition" title="Tradition">traditions</a> associated with referring to such a person.  These vary from the extreme of never again speaking the person's real name, often using some <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/circumlocution" class="extiw" title="wikt:circumlocution">circumlocution</a> instead, to the opposite extreme of commemorating it incessantly by naming other things or people after the deceased.
</p><p>For instance, in some cultures it is common for a newborn child to receive the name (a necronym) of a <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">relative</a> who has recently died, while in others to reuse such a name would be considered extremely inappropriate or even forbidden. While this varies from culture to culture, the use of necronyms is quite common.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Necronym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Composer <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_van_Beethoven" title="Ludwig van Beethoven">Ludwig van Beethoven</a>, born in 1770, had a brother named Ludwig Maria who was born in 1769 and lived for only six days. <a href="/wiki/Vincent_van_Gogh" title="Vincent van Gogh">Vincent van Gogh</a> had a brother of the same name who was born, and died, on March 30, 1852, exactly one year before the painter's birth. The artist <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador Dalí">Salvador Dalí</a> was born nine months and ten days after his brother, also named Salvador, died from gastroenteritis at the age of one year and nine months.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi_Jew" class="mw-redirect" title="Ashkenazi Jew">Ashkenazi Jewish</a> culture, it is a custom to name a child after a beloved relative who died as a way of honoring the deceased. Often the child will share the same <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_name" title="Hebrew name">Hebrew name</a> as the namesake but not the given name in the vernacular language (e.g. English).
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Japan" title="Culture of Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buddhist" class="mw-redirect" title="Buddhist">Buddhist</a> families usually obtain a necronym, called a <i><a href="/wiki/Dharma_name" title="Dharma name">kaimyō</a></i>, for a deceased relative from a Buddhist priest in exchange for a donation to the temple.  Traditionally, the deceased were thereafter referred to by the necronym, as a sign of pious respect.  This name was often the only one inscribed on <a href="/wiki/Gravestone" class="mw-redirect" title="Gravestone">gravestones</a> in the past, though now it is more common to have the necronym in addition to the given name.
</p><p>Necronyms were also commonly used during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> as a means of protecting an <a href="/wiki/Intelligence_officer" title="Intelligence officer">intelligence officer</a>'s true identity. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet</a> <a href="/wiki/KGB" title="KGB">KGB</a> agent <a href="/wiki/Konon_Molody" title="Konon Molody">Konon Molody</a> was only known as Gordon Lonsdale (the true Lonsdale was a <a href="/wiki/Canadians" title="Canadians">Canadian</a> born two years after Molody and died in 1943 when he was 19) in the United States. Molody adopted the name when he was 32, 11 years after the real Lonsdale's death.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historiography">Historiography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Necronym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historiography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The practice of bestowing necronyms has sometimes caused confusion for <a href="/wiki/Historian" title="Historian">historians</a>. This is primarily because of the two birth certificates or records that could be present at a given time. This confusion often stems from the inability to differentiate the records of each child. One such example is the case of <a href="/wiki/Shigechiyo_Izumi" title="Shigechiyo Izumi">Shigechiyo Izumi</a> (1865?–1986), accepted in 1986 as the world's oldest man by <i><a href="/wiki/Guinness_World_Records" title="Guinness World Records">The Guinness Book of World Records</a></i>, but who was in fact possibly born in 1880, after one previous brother whose name he assumed upon his death.
</p>
