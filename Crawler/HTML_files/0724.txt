	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Denim</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Denim</b> is a sturdy <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a> warp-faced<sup id="cite_ref-warp-faced_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-warp-faced-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">textile</a> in which the <a href="/wiki/Weft" class="mw-redirect" title="Weft">weft</a> passes under two or more <a href="/wiki/Warp_(weaving)" class="mw-redirect" title="Warp (weaving)">warp</a> threads. This <a href="/wiki/Twill" title="Twill">twill</a> weaving produces a diagonal ribbing that distinguishes it from <a href="/wiki/Cotton_duck" title="Cotton duck">cotton duck</a>. While a denim predecessor known as <a href="/wiki/Dungaree_(fabric)" title="Dungaree (fabric)">dungaree</a> has been produced in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> for hundreds of years, denim itself was first produced in the French city of <a href="/wiki/N%C3%AEmes" title="Nîmes">Nîmes</a> under the name “serge de Nîmes”.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The most common denim is <a href="/wiki/Indigo_dye" title="Indigo dye">indigo denim</a>, in which the <a href="/wiki/Warp_(weaving)" class="mw-redirect" title="Warp (weaving)">warp</a> thread is dyed, while the <a href="/wiki/Weft" class="mw-redirect" title="Weft">weft</a> thread is left white. As a result of the warp-faced twill weaving, one side of the textile is dominated by the blue warp threads and the other side is dominated by the white weft threads. This causes <a href="/wiki/Jeans" title="Jeans">blue jeans</a> to be white on the inside. The indigo dyeing process, in which the core of the warp threads remains white, creates denim's signature fading characteristics.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_origin">Etymology and origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name "denim" derives from&#32;<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>&#32;<i> serge de Nîmes</i>, meaning '<a href="/wiki/Serge_(fabric)" title="Serge (fabric)">serge</a> from <a href="/wiki/N%C3%AEmes" title="Nîmes">Nîmes</a>'.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Denim was traditionally colored blue with <a href="/wiki/Indigo_dye" title="Indigo dye">indigo dye</a> to make <a href="/wiki/Jeans" title="Jeans">blue jeans</a>, although "jean" formerly denoted a different lighter, cotton fabric. The contemporary use of the word "jeans" comes from the French word for <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>: Gênes.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Denim has been used in the United States since the mid-19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Denim initially gained popularity in 1873 when <a href="/wiki/Jacob_W._Davis" title="Jacob W. Davis">Jacob W. Davis</a>, a tailor from Nevada, manufactured the first pair of rivet-reinforced denim pants. At this time, clothes for Western labourers, such as teamsters, surveyors, and miners, were not very durable. His concept for making reinforced jeans was inspired when a customer requested a pair of durable and strong pants for her husband to chop wood. When Davis was about to finish making the denim jeans, he saw some copper rivets lying on a table and used the rivets to fasten the pockets. Soon, the popularity of denim jeans began to spread rapidly, and Davis was overwhelmed with requests. He soon sold 200 pairs to workers in need of heavy work clothing. Nevertheless, because of the production capacity in his small shop, Davis was struggling to keep up with the demand. He then wrote a proposal to dry goods wholesaler <a href="/wiki/Levi_Strauss_%26_Co." title="Levi Strauss &amp; Co.">Levi Strauss &amp; Co.</a> that had been supplying Davis with bolts of denim fabric. Davis's proposal was to patent the design of the rivet-reinforced denim pant, with Davis listed as inventor, in exchange for certain rights of manufacture. Levi Strauss &amp; Co. was so impressed by the possibilities for profit in the manufacture of the garment that they then hired Davis to be in charge of the mass production in San Francisco.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Throughout the 20th century denim was used for cheap durable uniforms like those issued to staff of the French national railways.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In the postwar years, Royal Air Force overalls for dirty work were named "denims." These were a one-piece garment, with long legs and sleeves, buttoned from throat to crotch, in an olive drab denim fabric.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Creating_denim">Creating denim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Creating denim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All denim goes through generally the same process to creation.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Cotton is harvested by hand or machine.</li>
<li>A cotton gin separates the cotton fiber from the seeds.</li>
<li>The fiber is put into bales. A bale weighs around 550 pounds and can make around 400 pairs of jeans.</li>
<li>The cotton fiber is then spun into yarn.</li>
<li>The yarn is dyed giving it color such as the classic denim blue.</li>
<li>The yarn is then woven in a shuttle loom or projectile loom into denim.</li>
<li>The denim is then sent to manufacturer for use.</li></ol>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Stone_washing" title="Stone washing">Stone washing</a></div>
<p><b>Dry</b> or <b>raw denim</b> (contrasted with "washed denim") is denim that is not washed after having been dyed during production.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Denim_Fibers.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Denim_Fibers.jpg/220px-Denim_Fibers.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Denim_Fibers.jpg/330px-Denim_Fibers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Denim_Fibers.jpg/440px-Denim_Fibers.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Denim_Fibers.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Denim fibers from an old pair of jeans through a microscope</div></div></div>
<p>Over time dry denim will fade, considered fashionable in some circumstances. During the process of wear, fading will usually occur on those parts of the article that receive the most stress. On a pair of jeans, this includes the upper thighs, the ankles, and the areas behind the knees.
</p><p>After being made into an article of clothing, most denim articles are washed to make them softer and to reduce or eliminate <a href="/wiki/Shrinkage_(fabric)" title="Shrinkage (fabric)">shrinkage</a> (which could cause the article to not fit properly after its owner washes it). This process is known as <a href="/wiki/Sanforization" title="Sanforization">sanforization</a>. In addition to being sanforized, "washed denim" is sometimes artificially distressed to produce a "worn" look. Much of the appeal of artificially distressed denim is that it resembles dry denim which has faded. In jeans made from dry denim, such fading is affected by the body of the person who wears them and by the activities of their daily life. This process creates what many enthusiasts feel to be a look more "natural" than artificially distressed denim.<sup id="cite_ref-100th_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100th-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>To facilitate the natural distressing process, some wearers of dry denim will abstain from washing their jeans for more than six months.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
Most dry denim is made with 100% cotton and comes from several different countries. 
</p><p>Dry denim also varies in weight, typically measured by the weight of a yard of denim in ounces. 12 oz. or less is considered light denim, 12 oz. to 16 oz. is considered mid-weight, and over 16 oz. is considered heavyweight. Heavier denim is much more rigid and resistant to wear, but can also take a larger number of wears to break in and feel comfortable.<sup id="cite_ref-100th_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100th-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Full_Count_Denim_Fade.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Full_Count_Denim_Fade.jpg/220px-Full_Count_Denim_Fade.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Full_Count_Denim_Fade.jpg/330px-Full_Count_Denim_Fade.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Full_Count_Denim_Fade.jpg/440px-Full_Count_Denim_Fade.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4622" data-file-height="3456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Full_Count_Denim_Fade.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Natural fading on a worn pair of selvedge jeans. Sometimes referred to as 'whiskers' or 'honeycombs'</div></div></div>
<p>Patterns of fading in jeans caused by prolonged periods of wearing them without washing are a way of "personalizing" the garment.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>These patterns have specific names:
</p>
<ul><li>combs or honeycombs – meshes of faded line-segments that form behind the knees<sup id="cite_ref-fade_types_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fade_types-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>whiskers – faded streaks that form radially from the crotch area<sup id="cite_ref-fade_types_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fade_types-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>stacks – irregular bands of fading above the ankle caused by according of the fabric due to contact with the foot or shoe<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Shapira2016_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shapira2016-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>train tracks – fading along the out-seams due to abrasion<sup id="cite_ref-Shapira2016_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shapira2016-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Selvedge">Selvedge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Selvedge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Full_Count_Selvedge_ID.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Full_Count_Selvedge_ID.jpg/220px-Full_Count_Selvedge_ID.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="294" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Full_Count_Selvedge_ID.jpg/330px-Full_Count_Selvedge_ID.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Full_Count_Selvedge_ID.jpg/440px-Full_Count_Selvedge_ID.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3456" data-file-height="4614" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Full_Count_Selvedge_ID.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Selvedge ID visible at the interior of a pair of jeans</div></div></div>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Selvedge" class="mw-redirect" title="Selvedge">Selvedge</a></i> (or <i>selvage</i>) is the edge of a fabric as it comes from the <a href="/wiki/Loom" title="Loom">loom</a>. Selvedges are woven or knit so that they will not fray, ravel, or curl.
</p><p><i>Selvedge denim</i> refers to a unique type of selvedge that is made by passing one continuous cross-yarn (the <a href="/wiki/Weft" class="mw-redirect" title="Weft">weft</a>) back and forth through the vertical <a href="/wiki/Warp_(weaving)" class="mw-redirect" title="Warp (weaving)">warp</a> beams. This is traditionally finished at both edges with a contrasting warp (most commonly red); that is why this type of denim is sometimes referred to as "red selvedge." This method of weaving the selvage is possible only when using a <a href="/wiki/Shuttle_loom" class="mw-redirect" title="Shuttle loom">shuttle loom</a>.
</p><p>Shuttle looms weave a narrower 30-inch fabric, which is on average half the width of modern shuttleless <a href="/wiki/Sulzer_(manufacturer)" title="Sulzer (manufacturer)">Sulzer looms</a>. Consequently, a longer piece of fabric is required to make a pair of jeans from selvedge denim (approximately three yards).
</p><p>To maximize yield, most jeans are made from wide denim and have a straight outseam that utilizes the full width of the fabric, including the edges. Selvedge denim has come to be associated with premium quality jeans, which show the finished edges from the loom rather than the overlocked edges that are shown on other jeans.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dyeing">Dyeing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dyeing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Denim was originally dyed with <a href="/wiki/Indigo_dye" title="Indigo dye">indigo dye</a> extracted from plants, often from the <i><a href="/wiki/Indigofera" title="Indigofera">Indigofera</a></i> family. In South Asia, indigo dye was extracted from the dried and fermented leaves of <i><a href="/wiki/Indigofera_tinctoria" title="Indigofera tinctoria">Indigofera tinctoria</a></i>; this is the plant that is now known as "true indigo" or "natural indigo". In Europe, use of <i><a href="/wiki/Isatis_tinctoria" title="Isatis tinctoria">Isatis tinctoria</a></i>, or woad, can be traced back to the 8th century BC, although it was eventually replaced by <i>Indigofera tinctoria</i> as the superior dye product. However, most denim today is dyed with synthetic <a href="/wiki/Indigo_dye" title="Indigo dye">indigo dye</a>. In all cases, the yarn undergoes a repeated sequence of dipping and oxidation — the more dips, the stronger the color of the indigo.<sup id="cite_ref-Bojer_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bojer-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Prior to 1915, cotton yarns were dyed using a skein dyeing process, in which individual <a href="/wiki/Hank_(textile)" title="Hank (textile)">skeins</a> of yarn were dipped into dye baths. Rope dyeing machines were developed in 1915, and slasher or sheet dyeing machines were developed in the 1970s; both of these methods involve a series of rollers that feed continuous yarns in and out of dye vats. In rope dyeing, continuous yarns are gathered together into long ropes or groups of yarns – after these bundles are dyed, they must be re-beamed for weaving. In sheet dyeing, parallel yarns are laid out as a sheet, in the same order in which they will be woven; because of this, uneven circulation of dye in the dye bath can lead to side-to-side color variations in the woven cloth. Rope dyeing eliminates this possibility, because color variations can be evenly distributed across the warp during beaming.<sup id="cite_ref-Bojer_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bojer-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Colored_denim">Colored denim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Colored denim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Denim fabric dyeing is divided into two categories: <a href="/wiki/Indigo_dye" title="Indigo dye">indigo dyeing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dye" title="Sulfur dye">sulfur dyeing</a>. Indigo dyeing produces the traditional blue color or shades similar to it. Sulfur dyeing produces speciality black colors and other colors, such as red, pink, purple, grey, rust, mustard, and green.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stretch_denim">Stretch denim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Stretch denim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Stretch denim incorporates an <a href="/wiki/Elastomer" title="Elastomer">elastic component</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Spandex" title="Spandex">spandex</a>. This creates a certain amount of "give" in garments made from stretch denim.
</p><p>Only a small percentage (about 3%) of spandex is required within the fabric to create a significant stretching capacity of about 15%. However, this feature will shorten the wearing life of the garment.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vehicles">Vehicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Vehicles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1974_AMC_Gremlin_with_Levi%E2%80%99s_option_at_2015_AMO_show_1of5.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/1974_AMC_Gremlin_with_Levi%E2%80%99s_option_at_2015_AMO_show_1of5.jpg/220px-1974_AMC_Gremlin_with_Levi%E2%80%99s_option_at_2015_AMO_show_1of5.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/1974_AMC_Gremlin_with_Levi%E2%80%99s_option_at_2015_AMO_show_1of5.jpg/330px-1974_AMC_Gremlin_with_Levi%E2%80%99s_option_at_2015_AMO_show_1of5.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/1974_AMC_Gremlin_with_Levi%E2%80%99s_option_at_2015_AMO_show_1of5.jpg/440px-1974_AMC_Gremlin_with_Levi%E2%80%99s_option_at_2015_AMO_show_1of5.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1974_AMC_Gremlin_with_Levi%E2%80%99s_option_at_2015_AMO_show_1of5.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/AMC_Gremlin" title="AMC Gremlin">AMC Gremlin</a> with Levi's trim and upholstery</div></div></div>
<p>Starting with the 1973 model year, <a href="/wiki/American_Motors" class="mw-redirect" title="American Motors">American Motors Corporation</a> (AMC) offered a regular production option consisting of a <a href="/wiki/Levi%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="Levi&#39;s">Levi's</a> interior trim package.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Over the years it was available on the <a href="/wiki/AMC_Gremlin" title="AMC Gremlin">Gremlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/AMC_Hornet" title="AMC Hornet">Hornet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/AMC_Pacer" title="AMC Pacer">Pacer</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Jeep" title="Jeep">Jeep</a> models.
</p><p>Although the car's jean material looks just like the real thing, AMC used spun nylon that was made to imitate denim. This was because real denim fabric is not tough enough for automobile use and cannot pass fire resistance safety standards. The copper rivets were the actual versions and the seat design included traditional contrasting stitching with the Levi's tab on both the front seat backs. The option also included unique door panels with Levis trim and removable map pockets, as well as "Levi's" decal identification on the front fenders. The Levi's interior was available through the 1978 <a href="/wiki/AMC_Concord" title="AMC Concord">AMC Concord</a>.
</p><p>A Levi's trim package was also made available by AMC on most Jeeps, including the CJ series, Cherokee, Wagoneer, and J series pickup trucks in 1975. This consisted of denim-like vinyl upholstery and a matching canvas top.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> This option was available on all CJ models in blue or tan, and was the standard trim on the top-level Renegade versions.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Between 1973 and 1975 Volkswagen produced the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Beetle" title="Volkswagen Beetle">Jeans Beetle</a>, which had all-denim trim.  They also repeated this concept in some later models.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art">Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Denim&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Denimu_Art_Cheyenne_Diner.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Denimu_Art_Cheyenne_Diner.jpg/220px-Denimu_Art_Cheyenne_Diner.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="111" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Denimu_Art_Cheyenne_Diner.jpg/330px-Denimu_Art_Cheyenne_Diner.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Denimu_Art_Cheyenne_Diner.jpg/440px-Denimu_Art_Cheyenne_Diner.jpg 2x" data-file-width="822" data-file-height="413" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Denimu_Art_Cheyenne_Diner.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Cheyenne has Gone made of only denim by Ian Berry</div></div></div>
<p>British artist <a href="/wiki/Denimu" title="Denimu">Ian Berry</a> has been making art with only denim for well over a decade<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and is famed around the world for his <a href="/wiki/Photorealism" title="Photorealism">photorealistic</a> pieces all hand cut out of only denim of portraits and scenes.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>He has made pieces of <a href="/wiki/Ayrton_Senna" title="Ayrton Senna">Ayrton Senna</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Armani" title="Giorgio Armani">Giorgio Armani</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lapo_Elkann" title="Lapo Elkann">Lapo Elkann</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Debbie_Harry" title="Debbie Harry">Debbie Harry</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Saunders" title="Jennifer Saunders">Jenifer Saunders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eunice_Olumide" title="Eunice Olumide">Eunice Olumbide</a> OBE<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> among others.
</p><p>In 2013 he was named as one of the top 30 Artists under 30 in the World by Art Business News<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
