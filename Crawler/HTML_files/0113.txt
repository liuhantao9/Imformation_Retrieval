<p><b>Álava</b> (<small>IPA:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">[ˈalaβa]</a></span> in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>) or <b>Araba</b> (<small>IPA:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Basque" title="Help:IPA/Basque">[aˈɾaba]</a></span> in <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">Basque</a>, dialectal: <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Basque" title="Help:IPA/Basque">[aˈɾaβa]</a></span>), officially <b>Araba/Álava</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Spain" title="Provinces of Spain">province of Spain</a> and a historical territory of the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">Basque Country</a>, heir of the ancient <a href="/wiki/Basque_se%C3%B1or%C3%ADos#Lords_of_Álava" title="Basque señoríos">Lordship of Álava</a>, former medieval Catholic bishopric and now Latin <a href="/wiki/Titular_see" title="Titular see">titular see</a>.
</p><p>Its capital city, <a href="/wiki/Vitoria-Gasteiz" title="Vitoria-Gasteiz">Vitoria-Gasteiz</a>, is also the seat of the political main institutions of the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">autonomous community</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It borders the Basque provinces of <a href="/wiki/Biscay" title="Biscay">Biscay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gipuzkoa" title="Gipuzkoa">Gipuzkoa</a> to the north, the community of <a href="/wiki/La_Rioja_(Spain)" title="La Rioja (Spain)">La Rioja</a> to the south, the <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Burgos" title="Province of Burgos">province of Burgos</a> (in the community of <a href="/wiki/Castile_and_Le%C3%B3n" title="Castile and León">Castile and León</a>) to the west and the community of <a href="/wiki/Navarre" title="Navarre">Navarre</a> to the east. The <a href="/wiki/Trevi%C3%B1o_enclave" title="Treviño enclave">Enclave of Treviño</a>, surrounded by Alavese territory, is however part of the province of Burgos, thus belonging to the autonomous community of Castile and León, not Álava.
</p><p>It is the largest of the three provinces in the Basque Autonomous Community in geographical terms, with 2,963 <a href="/wiki/Square_kilometre" title="Square kilometre">km<sup>2</sup></a>, but also the least populated with 328,868 inhabitants (2018).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%81lava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Built around the Roman mansion Alba located on the road <a href="/wiki/Ab_Asturica_Burdigalam" title="Ab Asturica Burdigalam">ab Asturica Burdigalam</a> (possibly the current village of <a href="/wiki/Alb%C3%A9niz,_%C3%81lava" title="Albéniz, Álava">Albéniz</a> near <a href="/wiki/Agurain/Salvatierra" title="Agurain/Salvatierra">Agurain</a>), it has sometimes been argued the name may stem from that landmark. However, according to the <a href="/wiki/Euskaltzaindia" title="Euskaltzaindia">Royal Academy of the Basque Language</a>, the origin may be another: The name is first found on Muslim chronicles of the 8th century referring to the Alavese Plains (Spanish <i>Llanada Alavesa</i>, Basque <i>Arabako Lautada</i>), <i>laua</i> in old Basque (currently <i>lautada</i>) with the Arab article added (<a href="/wiki/Al-" class="mw-redirect" title="Al-">al</a> + laua), developing into Spanish <b>Álava</b> and Basque <b>Araba</b> (a typical development of l to r between vowels).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demography_and_rural_landscape">Demography and rural landscape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%81lava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Demography and rural landscape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The province numbers 51 <a href="/wiki/Municipality" title="Municipality">municipalities</a>, a population of 315,525 inhabitants in an area of 3,037&#160;km<sup>2</sup> (1,173&#160;sq&#160;mi), with an average of 104.50 inhabitants/km<sup>2</sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-alava_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alava-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The vast majority of the population clusters in the capital city of Álava, <a href="/wiki/Vitoria-Gasteiz" title="Vitoria-Gasteiz">Vitoria-Gasteiz</a>, which also serves as the capital of the Autonomous Community, but the remainder of the territory is sparsely inhabited with population nuclei distributed into seven counties (<i>kuadrillak</i> or <i>cuadrillas</i>): <a href="/wiki/Cuadrilla_de_A%C3%B1ana" title="Cuadrilla de Añana">Añana</a>; <a href="/wiki/Ayala/Aiara" title="Ayala/Aiara">Ayala</a>; <a href="/wiki/Campezo" class="mw-redirect" title="Campezo">Campezo</a>; <a href="/wiki/Laguardia,_%C3%81lava" title="Laguardia, Álava">Laguardia</a>; <a href="/wiki/Salvatierra,_%C3%81lava" class="mw-redirect" title="Salvatierra, Álava">Salvatierra</a>; <a href="/wiki/Vitoria-Gasteiz" title="Vitoria-Gasteiz">Vitoria-Gasteiz</a>; <a href="/wiki/Zuya" class="mw-redirect" title="Zuya">Zuya</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_and_human_geography">Physical and human geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%81lava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical and human geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Álava is an inland territory and features a largely transitional climate between the humid, Atlantic neighbouring northern provinces and the dry and warmer lands south of the <a href="/wiki/Ebro" title="Ebro">Ebro</a> River. According to the relief and landscape characteristics, the territory is divided into five main zones: 
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Gorbea" title="Gorbea">Gorbea</a> Foothills: Green hilly landscape.</li>
<li>The Valleys: Low valleys, drier, sparsely populated.</li>
<li>The Plains: Heartland of Álava comprising Vitoria and Salvatierra-Agurain, with a central urban area and crop landscape prevailing around and bounded south and north by the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Mountains" title="Basque Mountains">Basque Mountains</a>.</li>
<li>The Alavese Mountains: Higher forest lands.</li>
<li>The Alavese Rioja: Oriented to the south on the left bank of the <a href="/wiki/Ebro" title="Ebro">Ebro</a> River, perfect for vineyards.</li>
<li>Ayala: The area clustering around the <a href="/wiki/Nervi%C3%B3n" title="Nervión">Nervión</a> River, with <a href="/wiki/Amurrio" title="Amurrio">Amurrio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laudio/Llodio" title="Laudio/Llodio">Laudio</a> as its major towns. The region shows close bonds with Bilbao and Biscaye and an industrial landscape.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Legunbe_Arabako_lautada_Aratz_Aizkorri.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Legunbe_Arabako_lautada_Aratz_Aizkorri.JPG/220px-Legunbe_Arabako_lautada_Aratz_Aizkorri.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Legunbe_Arabako_lautada_Aratz_Aizkorri.JPG/330px-Legunbe_Arabako_lautada_Aratz_Aizkorri.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Legunbe_Arabako_lautada_Aratz_Aizkorri.JPG/440px-Legunbe_Arabako_lautada_Aratz_Aizkorri.JPG 2x" data-file-width="962" data-file-height="610" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Legunbe_Arabako_lautada_Aratz_Aizkorri.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tip of the Burunda corridor in Navarre, opening on the Alavese Plains, with the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Mountains" title="Basque Mountains">Basque Mountains</a> <a href="/wiki/Aratz" title="Aratz">Aratz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aizkorri" title="Aizkorri">Aizkorri</a> on the right</div></div></div>
<p>Unlike Biscay and Gipuzkoa, but for Ayala and <a href="/wiki/Aramaio" title="Aramaio">Aramaio</a>, the waters of Álava pour into the <a href="/wiki/Ebro" title="Ebro">Ebro</a> and hence to the Mediterranean by means of two main waterways, i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Zadorra" title="Zadorra">Zadorra</a> (main axis of Álava) and <a href="/wiki/Bayas_(river)" title="Bayas (river)">Bayas</a> Rivers. In addition, the Zadorra Reservoir System harvests a big quantity of waters that supply not only the capital city but other major Basque towns and cities too, like <a href="/wiki/Bilbao" title="Bilbao">Bilbao</a>.
</p><p>While in 1950 agriculture and farming shaped the landscape of the territory (42.4% of the working force vs 30.5% in industry and construction), the trend shifted gradually during the 60s and 70s on the grounds of a growing industrial activity in the Alavese Plains (<i>Llanada Alavesa</i>), with the main focus lying on the industrial estates of Vitoria-Gasteiz (Gamarra, Betoño and Ali Gobeo) and, to a lesser extent, <a href="/wiki/Agurain/Salvatierra" title="Agurain/Salvatierra">Salvatierra-Agurain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asparrena" title="Asparrena">Araia</a>. At the turn of the century, only 2% of the working Alavese people was in agriculture, while 60% was in the tertiary sector and 32% in manufacturing.<sup id="cite_ref-alava_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-alava-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Industry associated with iron and metal developed earlier in the Atlantic area much in tune with Bilbao's economic dynamics, with droves of people flocking to and clustering in <a href="/wiki/Amurrio" title="Amurrio">Amurrio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laudio" class="mw-redirect" title="Laudio">Laudio</a>, which have since become the third and second main towns of Álava.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecclesiastical_history">Ecclesiastical history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%81lava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ecclesiastical history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bishopric">Bishopric</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%81lava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Bishopric">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Arab invasion of the <a href="/wiki/Ebro" title="Ebro">Ebro</a> valley in the 8th century, many Christians of the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Calahorra" class="mw-redirect" title="Diocese of Calahorra">Diocese of Calahorra</a> sought refuge in areas further north free of Arab rule.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The diocese called Álava or <b>Armentaria</b> was established in 870 on terrirory split off from the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Calahorra" class="mw-redirect" title="Diocese of Calahorra">Diocese of Calahorra</a>. From then until the 11th century the names of several bishops of this see are recorded, the best known being the last, Fortún, who in 1072 went to Rome to argue before <a href="/wiki/Pope_Alexander_II" title="Pope Alexander II">Pope Alexander II</a> in defence of the <a href="/wiki/Mozarabic_Rite" title="Mozarabic Rite">Mozarabic Rite</a>, which King <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_VI_of_Le%C3%B3n_and_Castile" title="Alfonso VI of León and Castile">Alfonso VI of León and Castile</a> had decreed should be replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Rite" title="Roman Rite">Roman Rite</a>.
</p><p>The see was suppressed in 1088, when it was merged into the <a href="/wiki/Diocese_of_Naj%C3%A9ra" class="mw-redirect" title="Diocese of Najéra">Diocese of Najéra</a>, another <a href="/wiki/Suffragan" class="mw-redirect" title="Suffragan">suffragan</a> of the Metropolitan <a href="/wiki/Archdiocese_of_Tarragona" class="mw-redirect" title="Archdiocese of Tarragona">Archdiocese of Tarragona</a>. 
The territory of the diocese of Álava, which corresponded more or less to that of the present diocese of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Vitoria" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Vitoria">Vitoria</a>, was (re)absorbed into that of Calahorra when Najéra was suppressed in 1170, when King <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_VIII_of_Castile" title="Alfonso VIII of Castile">Alfonso VIII of Castile</a> conquered <a href="/wiki/La_Rioja_(Spain)" title="La Rioja (Spain)">La Rioja</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ES_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ES-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gcatholic.org_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gcatholic.org-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Titular_see">Titular see</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=%C3%81lava&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Titular see">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>No longer a residential bishopric, Álava is today listed by the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Titular_see" title="Titular see">titular see</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> since the diocese was nominally restored in 1969 as <a href="/wiki/Titular_bishopric" class="mw-redirect" title="Titular bishopric">Titular bishopric</a> of Álava (español) / Alava (Curiate Italian and Latin), Latin adjective Alaven(sis).
</p><p>It has had the following incumbents, so far of the fitting episcopal (lowest) rank:<sup id="cite_ref-gcatholic.org_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gcatholic.org-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Stanisław Smolenski (1970.01.14 – death 2006.08.08) as <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_Bishop" class="mw-redirect" title="Auxiliary Bishop">Auxiliary Bishop</a> of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Krakow" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Krakow">Kraków</a> (<a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>) (1970.01.14 – 1992.02.01) and on emeritate</li>
<li>Mario Iceta Gavicagogeascoa (2008.02.05 – 2010.08.24) as Auxiliary Bishop of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Bilbao" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Bilbao">Bilbao</a> (Basque <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>) (2008.02.05 – 2010.08.24); later succeeded Bishop of Bilbao (2010.08.24 – ...)</li>
<li>Nelson Francelino Ferreira (2010.11.24 – 2014.02.12) as Auxiliary Bishop of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_S%C3%A3o_Sebasti%C3%A3o_do_Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of São Sebastião do Rio de Janeiro">São Sebastião do Rio de Janeiro</a> (<a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>) (2010.11.24 – 2014.02.12); later Bishop of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Valen%C3%A7a" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Valença">Valença</a> (Brazil) (2014.02.12 – ...)</li>
<li>Carlos Lema Garcia (2014.04.30 – ...), as Auxiliary Bishop of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of São Paulo">São Paulo</a> (Brazil)</li></ul>
