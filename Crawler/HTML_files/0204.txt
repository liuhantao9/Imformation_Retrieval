	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Diminutive</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table role="presentation" class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000">

<td class="mbox-text plainlist">Look up <i><b><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/diminutive" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:diminutive">diminutive</a></b></i> in Wiktionary, the free dictionary.</td></tr>

<p>A <b>diminutive</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Word" title="Word">word</a> that has been modified to convey a slighter degree of its root meaning, to convey the smallness of the object or quality named, or to convey a sense of intimacy or endearment.<sup id="cite_ref-UKgov_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UKgov-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> A <span class="nowrap"><b>diminutive form</b></span> (<a href="/wiki/List_of_glossing_abbreviations" title="List of glossing abbreviations">abbreviated</a> <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;"><b>DIM</b></span></span>) is a word-formation device used to express such meanings; in many languages, such forms can be translated as "little" and diminutives can also be formed as multi-word constructions such as "<a href="/wiki/Tiny_Tim_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Tiny Tim (disambiguation)">Tiny Tim</a>". Diminutives are often employed as <a href="/wiki/Nicknames" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicknames">nicknames</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pet_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Pet name">pet names</a>, when speaking to small children, and when expressing extreme tenderness and intimacy to an adult. The opposite of the diminutive form is the <a href="/wiki/Augmentative" title="Augmentative">augmentative</a>. Beyond the <i>diminutive form</i> of a single word, a <i>diminutive</i> can be a multi-word name, such as "Tiny Tim" or "Little Dorrit".
In many languages, formation of diminutives by adding <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffixes</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Productivity_(linguistics)" title="Productivity (linguistics)">productive</a> part of the language.<sup id="cite_ref-UKgov_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UKgov-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> For example, in Spanish <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">gordo</i> can be a nickname for someone who is overweight, and by adding an <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">ito</i> suffix, it becomes <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">gordito</i> which is more affectionate.  A <span class="nowrap"><b>double diminutive</b></span> (example in Polish: <i lang="pl" title="Polish language text">dzwon</i> → <i lang="pl" title="Polish language text">dzwonek</i> → <i lang="pl" title="Polish language text">dzwoneczek</i>; example in Italian: <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">casa</i> → <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">casetta</i> → <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">casettina</i>) is a diminutive form with two diminutive suffixes rather than one. While many languages apply a grammatical diminutive to <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">nouns</a>, a few – including <a href="/wiki/List_of_diminutives_by_language#Dutch" title="List of diminutives by language">Dutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_diminutives_by_language#Latin" title="List of diminutives by language">Latin</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_diminutives_by_language#Polish" title="List of diminutives by language">Polish</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_diminutives_by_language#Macedonian" title="List of diminutives by language">Macedonian</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_diminutives_by_language#Czech" title="List of diminutives by language">Czech</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_diminutives_by_language#Russian" title="List of diminutives by language">Russian</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_diminutives_by_language#Estonian" title="List of diminutives by language">Estonian</a> – also use it for <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjectives</a> (in Polish: <i lang="pl" title="Polish language text">słodki</i> → <i lang="pl" title="Polish language text">słodziutki</i> → <i lang="pl" title="Polish language text">słodziuteńki</i>) and even other <a href="/wiki/Lexical_category" class="mw-redirect" title="Lexical category">parts of speech</a>. In English the alteration of meaning is often conveyed through <a href="/wiki/Clipping_(morphology)" title="Clipping (morphology)">clipping</a>, making the words shorter and more <a href="/wiki/Colloquial" class="mw-redirect" title="Colloquial">colloquial</a>. Diminutives formed by adding affixes in other languages are often longer and (as colloquial) not necessarily understood.
</p><p>Diminutives in <a href="/wiki/Isolating_language" title="Isolating language">isolating languages</a> may <a href="/wiki/Grammaticalization" title="Grammaticalization">gramaticalize</a> strategies other than suffixes or prefixes. In <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin Chinese</a>, for example, other than the nominal prefix 小 <i>xiǎo</i> and nominal suffixes 儿/兒 <i>-r</i> and 子 <i>-zi</i>, <a href="/wiki/Reduplication" title="Reduplication">reduplication</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Productive_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Productive (linguistics)">productive</a> strategy, e.g., <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">舅</span> → <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">舅舅</span> and <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">看</span> → <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">看看</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In formal <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin</a> usage, the use of diminutives is relatively infrequent, as they tend to be considered to be rather colloquial than formal. Some <a href="/wiki/Wu_Chinese" title="Wu Chinese">Wu Chinese</a> dialects use a tonal affix for nominal diminutives; that is, diminutives are formed by changing the tone of the word.
</p><p>In some contexts, diminutives are also employed in a <a href="/wiki/Pejorative" title="Pejorative">pejorative</a> sense, to denote that someone or something is weak or childish. For example, one of the last of the Western Roman emperors was named <a href="/wiki/Romulus_Augustus" class="mw-redirect" title="Romulus Augustus">Romulus Augustus</a>, but this was diminuted to "Romulus Augustulus" to express his powerlessness.
</p>
