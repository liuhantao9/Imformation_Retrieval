<p>The <b>Army Reserve</b> is the active-duty <a href="/wiki/Volunteer_Reserves_(United_Kingdom)" title="Volunteer Reserves (United Kingdom)">volunteer reserve</a> force and integrated element of the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a>. It should not be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Regular_Reserve_(United_Kingdom)" title="Regular Reserve (United Kingdom)">Regular Reserve</a> whose members have formerly served full-time. The Army Reserve was previously known as the <b><a href="/wiki/Territorial_Force" title="Territorial Force">Territorial Force</a></b> from 1908 to 1921, the <b>Territorial Army</b> (TA) from 1921 to 1967, the <b>Territorial and Army Volunteer Reserve</b> (TAVR) from 1967 to 1979, and again the <b>Territorial Army</b> (TA) from 1979 to 2014.
</p><p>The Army Reserve was created as the Territorial Force in 1908 by the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_War" title="Secretary of State for War">Secretary of State for War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Haldane,_1st_Viscount_Haldane" title="Richard Haldane, 1st Viscount Haldane">Richard Haldane</a>, when the <a href="/wiki/Territorial_and_Reserve_Forces_Act_1907" title="Territorial and Reserve Forces Act 1907">Territorial and Reserve Forces Act 1907</a> combined the previously civilian-administered <a href="/wiki/Volunteer_Force_(Great_Britain)" class="mw-redirect" title="Volunteer Force (Great Britain)">Volunteer Force</a>, with the mounted <a href="/wiki/Yeomanry" title="Yeomanry">Yeomanry</a> (at the same time the <a href="/wiki/Militia#United_Kingdom" title="Militia">Militia</a> was renamed the <a href="/wiki/Special_Reserve" title="Special Reserve">Special Reserve</a>). Most Volunteer infantry units had unique identities, but lost these in the reorganisation, becoming Territorial battalions of Regular Army infantry regiments. Only one infantry unit, the <a href="/wiki/London_Regiment_(1993)" title="London Regiment (1993)">London Regiment</a>, has maintained a separate identity.
</p><p>Its original purpose was home defence, although the establishment of the Territorial and Army Volunteer Reserve in 1967 involved a restructuring and revised doctrine leading to the provision of routine support for the regular army overseas. Reservists in the past also served as <a href="/wiki/Constable" title="Constable">constables</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bailiff" title="Bailiff">bailiffs</a>, even holding positions of civic duty as overseer of their parish. The more modern Yeomen of the 18th century were cavalry-based units, which were often used to suppress riots (see the <a href="/wiki/Peterloo_Massacre" title="Peterloo Massacre">Peterloo Massacre</a>). Several units that are now part of the Army Reserve bear the title "militia",<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> reflecting their origins as part of <a href="/wiki/Militia_(United_Kingdom)" title="Militia (United Kingdom)">that organisation</a> prior to the formation of the Special Reserve in 1907.
</p><p>During periods of <a href="/wiki/Total_war" title="Total war">total war</a>, the Army Reserve is incorporated by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Prerogative" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Prerogative">Royal Prerogative</a> into Regular Service under one code of Military Law for the duration of hostilities or until de-activation is decided upon. After the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, for example, the Army Reserve – or Territorial Army as it was known then – was not demobilised until 1947. Army Reservists normally have a full-time civilian job or career, which in some cases provides skills and expertise that are directly transferable to a specialist military role, such as <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">NHS</a> employees serving in Reservist <a href="/wiki/Army_Medical_Services" title="Army Medical Services">Army Medical Services</a> units. All Army Reserve personnel have their civilian jobs protected to a limited extent by law should they be compulsorily mobilised. There is, however, no legal protection against discrimination in employment for membership of the Army Reserve in the normal course of events (i.e. when not mobilised).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_Reserve_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_to_First_World_War">Formation to First World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_Reserve_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation to First World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Territorial_Force" title="Territorial Force">Territorial Force</a></div>
<p>The Territorial Force was originally formed by the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_War" title="Secretary of State for War">Secretary of State for War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Burdon_Haldane" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard Burdon Haldane">Richard Burdon Haldane</a>, following the enactment of the <a href="/wiki/Territorial_and_Reserve_Forces_Act_1907" title="Territorial and Reserve Forces Act 1907">Territorial and Reserve Forces Act 1907</a> which combined and re-organised the old <a href="/wiki/Volunteer_Force_(Great_Britain)" class="mw-redirect" title="Volunteer Force (Great Britain)">Volunteer Force</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Yeomanry" title="Yeomanry">Yeomanry</a>. As part of the same process, remaining units of <a href="/wiki/Militia_(United_Kingdom)" title="Militia (United Kingdom)">militia</a> were converted to the <a href="/wiki/Special_Reserve_(militia)" class="mw-redirect" title="Special Reserve (militia)">Special Reserve</a>. The TF was formed on 1 April 1908 and contained fourteen <a href="/wiki/Infantry" title="Infantry">infantry</a> <a href="/wiki/Division_(military)" title="Division (military)">divisions</a>, and fourteen mounted yeomanry <a href="/wiki/Brigade" title="Brigade">brigades</a>. It had an overall strength of approximately 269,000.
</p><p>The individual units that made up each division or brigade were administered by County Associations, with the county's <a href="/wiki/Lord_lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord lieutenant">lord lieutenant</a> as president. The other members of the association consisted of <i>military members</i> (chosen from the commanding officers of the units), <i>representative members</i> (nominated by the <a href="/wiki/County_council" title="County council">county councils</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county boroughs</a> in the lieutenancy county) and <i>co-opted members</i> (often retired military officers). Associations took over any property vested in the volunteers or yeomanry under their administration. Each regiment or battalion had a <a href="/wiki/Standing_army" title="Standing army">Regular Army</a> <a href="/wiki/Officer_(armed_forces)" title="Officer (armed forces)">officer</a> attached as full-time adjutant.
</p><p>The use of the word <i>territorial</i> signified that the volunteers who served with the force were under no obligation to serve overseas—in 1910, when asked to nominate for Imperial Service overseas in the event of mobilisation, less than 10% of the Force chose to do so. In August 1914, after the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>, territorial units were given the option of serving in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and, by 25 August, in excess of seventy <a href="/wiki/Battalion" title="Battalion">battalions</a> had volunteered. This question over the availability of territorial divisions for overseas service was one of <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Kitchener,_1st_Earl_Kitchener" title="Herbert Kitchener, 1st Earl Kitchener">Lord Kitchener</a>'s motivations for raising the <a href="/wiki/Kitchener%27s_Army" title="Kitchener&#39;s Army">New Army</a> separately.
</p><p>Territorial formations initially saw service in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British India</a> and other <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">Empire</a> garrisons, such as <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>, thereby releasing regular units for service in France and enabling the formation of an additional five regular army divisions (for a total of eleven) by early 1915. Several reserve units were also deployed with regular formations and the first territorial unit to see action on the Western Front was the <a href="/wiki/32_(Scottish)_Signal_Regiment" class="mw-redirect" title="32 (Scottish) Signal Regiment">Glasgow Territorial Signallers Group, Royal Engineers</a> at the <a href="/wiki/First_Battle_of_Ypres" title="First Battle of Ypres">First Battle of Ypres</a> on 11 October 1914. The first fully Territorial division to join the fighting on the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">Western Front</a> was the <a href="/wiki/46th_(North_Midland)_Division" title="46th (North Midland) Division">46th (North Midland) Division</a> in March 1915, with divisions later serving in <a href="/wiki/Gallipoli" title="Gallipoli">Gallipoli</a> and elsewhere. As the war progressed, and casualties mounted, the distinctive character of territorial units was diluted by the inclusion of conscript and New Army drafts. Following <a href="/wiki/Armistice_of_11_November_1918" title="Armistice of 11 November 1918">the Armistice</a> all units of the Territorial Force were gradually disbanded.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Interwar_re-establishment_and_the_Second_World_War">Interwar re-establishment and the Second World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_Reserve_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Interwar re-establishment and the Second World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Territorial Force (TF) was officially reconstituted in 1921 by the <a href="/wiki/Territorial_Army_and_Militia_Act_1921" title="Territorial Army and Militia Act 1921">Territorial Army and Militia Act 1921</a> and renamed in October as the <a href="/wiki/Territorial_Army_(United_Kingdom)" class="mw-redirect" title="Territorial Army (United Kingdom)">Territorial Army</a> (TA).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The First-Line divisions (that were created in 1907 or 1908) were reconstituted in that year. The TA's intended role was to be the sole method of expanding the size of the <a href="/wiki/British_Armed_Forces" title="British Armed Forces">British Armed Forces</a>, when compared to the varied methods used during the First World War including the creation of Kitchener's Army. All TA recruits were required to take the general service obligation: if the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Government of United Kingdom">British Government</a> decided, territorial soldiers could be deployed overseas for combat that avoided the complications of the TF, whose members were not required to leave Britain unless they volunteered for overseas service.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllport2015323_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllport2015323-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFrench200153_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrench200153-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPerry198841–42_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPerry198841–42-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESimkins200743–46_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimkins200743–46-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The composition of the divisions was altered, with a reduction in the number of infantry battalions required. There was also a reduced need for cavalry, and of the 55 yeomanry regiments, only the 14 most senior retained their horses. The remaining yeomanry were converted to artillery or armoured car units or disbanded.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The amalgamation of 40 pairs of infantry battalions was announced in October 1921.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> As part of the post-war "<a href="/wiki/Geddes_Axe" title="Geddes Axe">Geddes Axe</a>" financial cuts, the TA was further reduced in size in 1922: artillery batteries lost two of their six guns, the established size of infantry battalions was cut and ancillary medical, veterinary, signals and <a href="/wiki/Royal_Army_Service_Corps" title="Royal Army Service Corps">Royal Army Service Corps</a> units were either reduced in size or abolished.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The bounty was also reduced to £3 for trained men and £2.10s 0d for recruits, which resulted in finding £1,175,000 of the total savings required from the army as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> An innovation in 1922 was the creation of two Air Defence Brigades to provide anti-aircraft defence for London.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the 1930s, <a href="/wiki/Causes_of_World_War_II" title="Causes of World War II">tensions increased</a> between <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Germany</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">allies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBell19973–4_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBell19973–4-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In late 1937 and throughout 1938, German demands for the annexation of  <a href="/wiki/Sudetenland" title="Sudetenland">Sudetenland</a> in Czechoslovakia led to an <a href="/wiki/International_crisis" title="International crisis">international crisis</a>. To avoid war, the British <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Neville_Chamberlain" title="Neville Chamberlain">Neville Chamberlain</a> met with German <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany" title="Chancellor of Germany">Chancellor</a> <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> in September and brokered the <a href="/wiki/Munich_Agreement" title="Munich Agreement">Munich Agreement</a>. The agreement averted a war and allowed Germany to <a href="/wiki/Annexation" title="Annexation">annex</a> the Sudetenland.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBell1997258–275_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBell1997258–275-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Although Chamberlain had intended the agreement to lead to further peaceful resolution of issues, relations between both countries soon deteriorated.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBell1997277–278_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBell1997277–278-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> On 15 March 1939, Germany breached the terms of the agreement by invading and occupying <a href="/wiki/Second_Czechoslovak_Republic" title="Second Czechoslovak Republic">the remnants of the Czech state</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBell1997281_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBell1997281-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 29 March, Secretary of State for War <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Hore-Belisha" title="Leslie Hore-Belisha">Leslie Hore-Belisha</a> announced plans to increase the TA from 130,000 to 340,000 men and double the number of TA divisions.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGibbs1976518_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGibbs1976518-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The plan was for existing TA units to recruit over their establishments (aided by an increase in pay for Territorials, the removal of restrictions on promotion which had hindered recruiting, construction of better-quality barracks and an increase in supper rations) and then form second-line divisions from cadres that could be increased.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGibbs1976518_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGibbs1976518-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMessenger199447_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMessenger199447-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In April, limited <a href="/wiki/Conscription" title="Conscription">conscription</a> was introduced. This resulted in 34,500 twenty-year-old militiamen being conscripted into the regular army, initially to be trained for six months before deployment to the forming second-line units.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMessenger199449_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMessenger199449-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFrench200164_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrench200164-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> It was envisioned that the duplicating process and recruiting the required numbers of men would take no more than six months. Some TA divisions had made little progress by the time the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">Second World War</a> began; others were able to complete this work within a matter of weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPerry198848_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPerry198848-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELevy200666_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELevy200666-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The total strength of the TA was to be 440,000: the field force of the Territorial Army was to rise from 130,000 to 340,000, organised in 26 divisions, while an additional 100,000 all ranks would form the anti-aircraft section.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The forming Second Line formations were given liberty to be numbered and named as they saw fit, with some using related names and numbers from the First World War. The TA's war deployment plan envisioned the divisions being deployed, as equipment became available, in waves to reinforce the <a href="/wiki/British_Expeditionary_Force_(World_War_II)" title="British Expeditionary Force (World War II)">British Expeditionary Force</a> (BEF) that had already been dispatched to Europe. The TA would join regular army divisions completed their training, with the final divisions of the entire force deployed one year after the war began.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGibbs1976455,_507,_514–515_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGibbs1976455,_507,_514–515-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> A mixture of First and Second Line formations were deployed for combat overseas. After <a href="/wiki/Victory_over_Japan_Day" title="Victory over Japan Day">VJ Day</a> in August 1945, the Territorial Army was significantly reduced, with all Second Line and several First-Line Divisions disbanded.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postwar_reforms_and_Cold_War">Postwar reforms and Cold War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_Reserve_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Postwar reforms and Cold War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1947, the TA was restructured and expanded through the reactivation of some of the 1st Line divisions that were initially disbanded after the war, keeping its former role of supplying complete divisions to the regular Army until 1967. For the first time, TA units were formed in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. The manoeuvre divisions established or re-established in 1947 were:<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/42nd_(East_Lancashire)_Infantry_Division" title="42nd (East Lancashire) Infantry Division">42nd (Lancashire) Infantry Division</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/43rd_(Wessex)_Infantry_Division" title="43rd (Wessex) Infantry Division">43rd (Wessex) Infantry Division</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/44th_(Home_Counties)_Division#Post_Second_World_War" title="44th (Home Counties) Division">44th (Home Counties) Infantry Division</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/49th_(West_Riding)_Infantry_Division" title="49th (West Riding) Infantry Division">49th (West Riding &amp; North Midland) Armoured Division</a> (49th (West Riding and North Midland) Division/District in 1961)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/50th_(Northumbrian)_Infantry_Division" title="50th (Northumbrian) Infantry Division">50th (Northumbrian) Infantry Division</a> (50th Northumbrian District by 1966)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/51st_(Highland)_Division" title="51st (Highland) Division">51st</a>/<a href="/wiki/52nd_(Lowland)_Infantry_Division" title="52nd (Lowland) Infantry Division">52nd (Lowland) Infantry Division</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/53rd_(Welsh)_Infantry_Division" title="53rd (Welsh) Infantry Division">53rd (Welsh) Infantry Division</a> (until 1968, when superseded by <a href="/wiki/Headquarters_Wales" title="Headquarters Wales">Headquarters Wales</a> District)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/56th_(London)_Infantry_Division" title="56th (London) Infantry Division">56th (London) Armoured Division</a></li></ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/16th_Airborne_Division" title="16th Airborne Division">16th Airborne Division</a>, a totally TA formation, was also raised at this time, under the command of <a href="/wiki/Major-general_(United_Kingdom)" title="Major-general (United Kingdom)">Major-General</a> <a href="/wiki/Roy_Urquhart" title="Roy Urquhart">Robert E. "Roy" Urquhart</a>. 52nd (Lowland) Division was re-established as a tenth, 'mixed' division in March 1950.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The territorials also provided much of the <a href="/wiki/Anti-aircraft_warfare" title="Anti-aircraft warfare">anti-aircraft</a> cover for the United Kingdom until 1956. In that year, <a href="/wiki/Anti-Aircraft_Command" title="Anti-Aircraft Command">Anti-Aircraft Command</a> and 15 anti-aircraft regiments of the Royal Artillery were disbanded, with nine others passing into "suspended animation" as new <a href="/wiki/English_Electric_Thunderbird" class="mw-redirect" title="English Electric Thunderbird">English Electric Thunderbird</a> <a href="/wiki/Surface_to_Air_Missile" class="mw-redirect" title="Surface to Air Missile">Surface to Air Missile</a> units replaced them.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> On 20 December 1955, the Secretary of State for War informed the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a> that the armoured divisions and the 'mixed' division were to be converted to infantry, and the 16th Airborne Division reduced to a parachute brigade group.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Only two divisions (43rd and 53rd), two armoured brigades, and a parachute brigade were to remain allocated for NATO and the defence of Western Europe; the other eight divisions were placed on a lower establishment for home defence only.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The territorial units of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Armoured_Corps" title="Royal Armoured Corps">Royal Armoured Corps</a> were also reduced in number to nine armoured regiments and eleven reconnaissance regiments. This was effected by the amalgamation of pairs of regiments, and the conversion of four RAC units to an infantry role. The new parachute brigade group become the <a href="/wiki/44th_Parachute_Brigade_(V)" title="44th Parachute Brigade (V)">44th Independent Parachute Brigade Group</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>British forces contracted dramatically as the end of <a href="/wiki/National_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="National Service">conscription</a> in 1960 came in sight as announced in the <a href="/wiki/1957_Defence_White_Paper" title="1957 Defence White Paper">1957 Defence White Paper</a>. On 20 July 1960, a reorganisation of the TA was announced in the House of Commons. The territorials were to be reduced from 266 fighting units to 195. There was to be a reduction of 46 regiments of the Royal Artillery, 18 battalions of infantry, 12 regiments of the Royal Engineers and two regiments of the Royal Corps of Signals.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The reductions were carried out in 1961, mainly by amalgamating units. Thus, on 1 May 1961, the TA divisional headquarters were merged with regular army districts, which were matched with Civil Defence Regions to aid mobilisation for war.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The number of infantry brigades was reduced from 31 to 23, and the two armoured brigades were disbanded.
</p><p><span id="TAVR"></span>This was followed by a complete reorganisation announced in the <a href="/wiki/1966_Defence_White_Paper" title="1966 Defence White Paper">1966 Defence White Paper</a> from 1 April 1967, when the title <b>Territorial and Army Volunteer Reserve</b> (TAVR) was adopted, that abolished the former regimental and divisional structure of the TA.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Units in the new TAVR were divided into four categories:<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>TAVR I: Units available for all purposes, the "Ever Readies", often drawn from the former <a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_Emergency_Reserve&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Army Emergency Reserve (page does not exist)">Army Emergency Reserve</a></li>
<li>TAVR II: Units with a <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> role, specifically support for the <a href="/wiki/British_Army_of_the_Rhine" title="British Army of the Rhine">British Army of the Rhine</a></li>
<li>TAVR III: Home Defence units</li>
<li>TAVR IV: Consisting of bands and the University-based Officer Training Corps</li></ul>
<p>TAVR I and II units were known as "Volunteers", and those in TAVR III as "Territorials". These terms were often incorporated into the unit titles.
</p><p>The TAVR III was disbanded in 1969, with the units being reduced to eight-man "cadres". The cadres became part of a "sponsoring" TAVR II unit, although continuing to wear the badges and perpetuating the traditions of their forebears. An increase in the size of the TAVR in 1971 led to the formation of a number of battalions based on these cadres.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1979, the Territorial Army title was restored and, in the following years, its size was somewhat increased, with the regimental system being progressively reinstated. Some brigades were re-formed which consisted mostly of TA units.
</p><p>The TA was thus re-roled into its modern form. Instead of supplying complete combat divisions, its function was to round out regular formations by supplying units of up to battalion size (including infantry, light artillery and <a href="/wiki/Formation_reconnaissance_regiment" title="Formation reconnaissance regiment">formation reconnaissance</a>), and to supply extra support functions, such as engineers, medical units and military police.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1998_onwards">1998 onwards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_Reserve_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1998 onwards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Defence_Review" title="Strategic Defence Review">Strategic Defence Review</a> of 1998, the TA's size of around 56,200 was further reduced. The Infantry suffered most, with 87 companies in 33 battalions reducing to 67 companies in 15 battalions. As of 2006, the Territorial Army had an authorised strength of 42,000 though recruiting difficulties put the actual strength of the TA significantly below that figure (manning is currently at approx 82% across the board, which equates to 34,000, while the actual strength of certain units is almost a skeleton crew, making them unsustainable without direct intervention by full-time personnel).
</p><p>Army Reservists have seen service in a number of conflicts that the UK has been involved with since 1945. However, they served in particularly large numbers in two conflicts: the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Suez_Crisis" title="Suez Crisis">Suez Crisis</a>; both occurred during the 1950s and, on each occasion, the entire TA was called up. Throughout the Cold War, however, the Territorial Army was never regarded as a particularly usable force overseas, either by the Government of the day or by the Regular Army.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This was due to the fact that the entire Territorial Army had to be mobilised by <a href="/wiki/Royal_Prerogative" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Prerogative">Royal Prerogative</a> in a wartime scenario, as occurred in the World Wars, with no flexibility to use smaller formations or specialists if required and, as a result, relied purely on territorials willing to volunteer their services. Therefore, its role was, at least unofficially, seen as home defence and, as a result, the TA was not used in conflicts such as the 1982 <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a> and 1991 <a href="/wiki/Gulf_War" title="Gulf War">Gulf War</a><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> (205 Scottish General Hospital was mobilised as a unit based in <a href="/wiki/Riyadh" title="Riyadh">Riyadh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a>, during the 1991 Gulf War and a number of TA staff officers and others volunteered and served during the conflict, either in supporting roles in Germany or within 1 (UK) Armoured Division in the Middle East.) Some 550 TA personnel volunteered for and deployed on Op RESOLUTE in December 1995; the UK's contribution to the NATO mission to enforce peace in the Former Yugoslavia. The Government subsequently passed the <a href="/wiki/Reserve_Forces_Act_1996" title="Reserve Forces Act 1996">Reserve Forces Act 1996</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> which enables individual Reservists to be compulsorily called up for deployment, with certain <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/caveat" class="extiw" title="wikt:caveat">caveats</a> exempting those in full-time education and other compassionate reasons, as well as providing protection by employment law for members' civilian jobs should they be mobilised, which has led to the Army Reserve increasingly providing routine support for the Regular Army overseas including delivery of composite units to release regular units from standing liabilities; including Bosnia, Kosovo, Cyprus and the Falkland Islands.
</p><p>In 2003, 9,500 reservists, the vast majority of them from the Reserve, were mobilised to take part in <a href="/wiki/Operation_TELIC" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation TELIC">Operation TELIC</a>, the invasion of <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a>; in contrast, only some 420 Regular Reservists were called-up. Approximately 1,200 members of the Army Reserve deployed annually on <a href="/wiki/Tour_of_duty" title="Tour of duty">tours of duty</a> in Iraq, <a href="/wiki/Operation_HERRICK" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation HERRICK">Operation HERRICK</a> in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> and elsewhere, normally on six month-long <a href="/wiki/Roulement" title="Roulement">roulements</a>. They cannot be used in operations for more than twelve months in any three-year period – making most of those who have already served ineligible for call up for two years afterwards, although reservists may choose to volunteer for additional deployments.
</p><p>Army Reservists have a minimum commitment to serve 27 training days per annum, or 19 days for some national units. This period normally includes a two-week period of continuous training either as an Army Reserve unit, on courses or attached to a Regular unit. Army Reserve soldiers are paid at a similar rate as their regular equivalents while engaged on military activities. Soldiers of the Army Reserve often serve alongside their regular counterparts, including operations in Afghanistan where 1,000 out of the total 10,100 deployed have been Reservists<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, around 10% of the total. The annual budget of the Army Reserve is approximately £350 million – around 1.3% of the total <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_and_federations_by_military_expenditures" class="mw-redirect" title="List of countries and federations by military expenditures">defence budget</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2011_onwards_and_renaming">2011 onwards and renaming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_Reserve_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2011 onwards and renaming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Army_Reservists_Applying_Camouflage_MOD_45156159.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Army_Reservists_Applying_Camouflage_MOD_45156159.jpg/220px-Army_Reservists_Applying_Camouflage_MOD_45156159.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Army_Reservists_Applying_Camouflage_MOD_45156159.jpg/330px-Army_Reservists_Applying_Camouflage_MOD_45156159.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Army_Reservists_Applying_Camouflage_MOD_45156159.jpg/440px-Army_Reservists_Applying_Camouflage_MOD_45156159.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4288" data-file-height="2848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Army_Reservists_Applying_Camouflage_MOD_45156159.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Army Reservists applying camouflage during a training exercise</div></div></div>
<p>Under the "Future Reserves 2020" (FR20) plan outlined by then Secretary of State for Defence <a href="/wiki/Liam_Fox" title="Liam Fox">Liam Fox</a> on 18 July 2011, the Ministry of Defence promised to provide more money to train more Army Reservists with the objective of more frequently deploying entire Army Reserve units<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> (much like <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Reserve" title="United States Army Reserve">United States Army Reserve</a> and <a href="/wiki/Army_National_Guard" title="Army National Guard">Army National Guard</a> units). Under the reform plan, the total force will be restructured so that, by 2020, the British Army will have 120,000 soldiers, of which 84,000 will be Regulars and 35,000 Reservists (a ratio of 70/30). The Territorial Army was renamed under that plan, becoming the Army Reserve.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Basic_training">Basic training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_Reserve_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Basic training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Selection_and_Training_in_the_British_Army" title="Selection and Training in the British Army">Selection and Training in the British Army</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soldiers">Soldiers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_Reserve_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Soldiers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For Army Reserve soldiers, recruit training is structured into two phases: Phase 1, also known as the Common Military Syllabus (Recruit) (CMS(R)), and Phase 2, specialist training.<sup id="cite_ref-Ministry_of_Defence_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ministry_of_Defence-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Phase 1</b>
</p><p>In Phase 1, recruits cover the Common Military Syllabus (Reserve)14 (CMS(R)14). Phase 1 A is a series of 4 training weekends at regional Army Training Units (ATUs), or the recruit could attend a consolidated Phase 1 A week long course. Phase 1 training concludes with a two-week Phase 1 B training course normally held at the <a href="/wiki/Army_Training_Centre,_Pirbright" title="Army Training Centre, Pirbright">Army Training Centre, Pirbright</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Army_Training_Regiment" class="mw-redirect" title="Army Training Regiment">Army Training Regiment</a>, Winchester, or Grantham. Recruits to the <a href="/wiki/4th_Battalion,_The_Parachute_Regiment" class="mw-redirect" title="4th Battalion, The Parachute Regiment">4th Battalion, The Parachute Regiment</a><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Honourable_Artillery_Company" title="Honourable Artillery Company">Honourable Artillery Company</a><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> complete their equivalent of CMS(R) within their own units.
</p><p><b>Phase 2</b>
</p><p>Phase 1 is followed by Phase 2, a further period of specialist training specific to the type of unit the recruit is joining. This is normally conducted by the Arm or Service that the recruit is joining, for example for infantry units, Phase 2 consists of the two-week Combat Infantryman's Course (TA) (CIC (TA)) held at the <a href="/wiki/Infantry_Training_Centre_(British_Army)" title="Infantry Training Centre (British Army)">Infantry Training Centre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Catterick_Garrison" title="Catterick Garrison">Catterick</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ministry_of_Defence_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ministry_of_Defence-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Officers">Officers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Army_Reserve_(United_Kingdom)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Officers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To gain a commission, Potential Officers have to pass through four modules of training, which together form the Army Reserve Commissioning Course.
</p><p><b>Module A</b> consists of basic field training and elementary military skills. This can be completed at either a UOTC over a number of weekends, or over 2 weeks at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Military_Academy_Sandhurst" title="Royal Military Academy Sandhurst">Royal Military Academy Sandhurst</a> (RMAS)
</p><p><b>Module B</b> covers training in Tactics, Leadership, Doctrine and Navigation, both in theory and in practice, with a focus on the section battle drills and the platoon combat estimate. This training can either be spread over 10 weekends at a UOTC, or 2 weeks at the RMAS.
</p><p><b>Module C</b> builds on the Tactics, Leadership, Doctrine and Navigation taught in Module B, with a greater focus on the theory behind these constructs. CBRN training is also added at this point, and Officer Cadets undergo a number of field exercises to test their military and leadership skills. Module C can only be undertaken at the RMAS.
</p><p><b>Module D</b> Once the Officer Cadet has completed their ArmyOfficer Selection Board, they can complete this final module, after which they will become commissioned officers in the British Army. Based at the RMAS, this module consists primarily of a prolonged field exercise, followed by drill training in preparation for the passing out parade.
</p><p>On successful completion of Module D, the Officer Cadets receive their <a href="/wiki/Commission_(document)" title="Commission (document)">Commission</a> and become <a href="/wiki/Second_Lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Second Lieutenant">Second Lieutenants</a>. Further training that is required prior to them being considered for operational deployment and promotion to <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant" title="Lieutenant">Lieutenant</a> includes:
</p><p><b>Post Commissioning Training</b> (formerly known as Module 5), again run at an OTC, over 3 weekends.
</p><p><b>Special To Arm training</b> is specific to the type of unit the <a href="/wiki/Subaltern" title="Subaltern">Subaltern</a> is joining, and covers a 2-week period. This is increasingly integrated with the tactics phase of a Regular training course. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Platoon_leader" title="Platoon leader">Platoon Commander's</a> Battle Course held at the <a href="/wiki/Infantry_Battle_School" title="Infantry Battle School">Infantry Battle School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brecon" title="Brecon">Brecon</a> which is integrated within a Regular training course, or the <a href="/wiki/Light_Cavalry" class="mw-redirect" title="Light Cavalry">Light Cavalry</a> Commander's Course held at the Reconnaissance &amp; Armoured Tactics Division in <a href="/wiki/Warminster" title="Warminster">Warminster</a> which is run separately to Regular training courses.
</p>
