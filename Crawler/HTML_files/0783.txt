<p><b>Infantry</b> is a military specialization that engages in <a href="/wiki/Military_combat" class="mw-redirect" title="Military combat">military combat</a> on foot, distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">cavalry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Artillery" title="Artillery">artillery</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tank" title="Tank">tank</a> forces. Also known as <b>foot soldiers</b> or <b>infanteers</b>, infantry traditionally relies on moving by foot between combats as well, but may also use <a href="/wiki/Riding_animal" class="mw-redirect" title="Riding animal">mounts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Military_vehicle" title="Military vehicle">military vehicles</a>, or other transport. Infantry make up a large portion of all <a href="/wiki/Armed_forces" class="mw-redirect" title="Armed forces">armed forces</a> in most nations, and typically bear the largest brunt in <a href="/wiki/Warfare" class="mw-redirect" title="Warfare">warfare</a>, as measured by <a href="/wiki/Casualty_(person)#Military_usage" title="Casualty (person)">casualties</a>, deprivation, or <a href="/wiki/Stress_(biology)" title="Stress (biology)">physical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psychological_stress" title="Psychological stress">psychological stress</a>.
</p><p>The first military forces in history were infantry. In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_history" title="Ancient history">antiquity</a>, infantry were armed with an early <a href="/wiki/Melee_weapon" title="Melee weapon">melee weapon</a> such as a <a href="/wiki/Spear" title="Spear">spear</a>, <a href="/wiki/Battle_axe" title="Battle axe">axe</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sword" title="Sword">sword</a>, or an early <a href="/wiki/Ranged_weapon" title="Ranged weapon">ranged weapon</a> like a <a href="/wiki/Javelin" title="Javelin">javelin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sling_(weapon)" title="Sling (weapon)">sling</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Bow_and_arrow" title="Bow and arrow">bow</a>, with a few infantrymen having both a melee and a ranged weapon. With the <a href="/wiki/History_of_gunpowder" title="History of gunpowder">development of gunpowder</a>, infantry began converting to primarily <a href="/wiki/Firearm" title="Firearm">firearms</a>. By the time of <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_warfare" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleonic warfare">Napoleonic warfare</a>, infantry, cavalry, and artillery formed a basic triad of ground forces, though infantry usually remained the most numerous. With <a href="/wiki/Armoured_warfare" title="Armoured warfare">armoured warfare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armoured_fighting_vehicle" title="Armoured fighting vehicle">armoured fighting vehicles</a> have replaced the horses of cavalry, and <a href="/wiki/Airpower" title="Airpower">airpower</a> has added a new dimension to ground combat, but infantry remains pivotal to all modern <a href="/wiki/Combined_arms" title="Combined arms">combined arms</a> operations.
</p><p>Infantry have much greater local <a href="/wiki/Situational_awareness" class="mw-redirect" title="Situational awareness">situational awareness</a> than other military forces, due to their inherent intimate contact with the battlefield ("boots on the ground"); this is vital for engaging and <a href="/wiki/Infiltration_tactics" title="Infiltration tactics">infiltrating</a> enemy positions, holding and defending ground (any <a href="/wiki/Tactical_objective" title="Tactical objective">military objectives</a>), securing battlefield victories, maintaining military area control and security both at and behind the <a href="/wiki/Front_line" title="Front line">front lines</a>, for capturing ordnance or materiel, taking prisoners, and <a href="/wiki/Military_occupation" title="Military occupation">military occupation</a>. Infantry can more easily recognise, adapt and respond to local conditions, weather, and changing enemy weapons or tactics. They can operate in a wide range of terrain inaccessible to <a href="/wiki/Military_vehicle" title="Military vehicle">military vehicles</a>, and can operate with a lower <a href="/wiki/Military_logistics" title="Military logistics">logistical</a> burden. Infantry are the most easily delivered forces to ground combat areas, by simple and reliable <a href="/wiki/Marching" title="Marching">marching</a>, or by trucks, sea or air transport; they can also be inserted directly into combat by <a href="/wiki/Amphibious_landing" class="mw-redirect" title="Amphibious landing">amphibious landing</a>, or for <a href="/wiki/Air_assault" title="Air assault">air assault</a> by <a href="/wiki/Parachute" title="Parachute">parachute</a> (<a href="/wiki/Airborne_forces" title="Airborne forces">airborne</a> infantry) or <a href="/wiki/Helicopter" title="Helicopter">helicopter</a> (<a href="/wiki/Airmobile" class="mw-redirect" title="Airmobile">airmobile</a> infantry). They can be augmented with a variety of <a href="/wiki/Crew-served_weapon" title="Crew-served weapon">crew-served weapons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armoured_personnel_carrier" title="Armoured personnel carrier">armoured personnel carriers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Infantry_fighting_vehicle" title="Infantry fighting vehicle">infantry fighting vehicles</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_and_terminology">Etymology and terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infantry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology and terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tafel_Io.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Tafel_Io.jpg/240px-Tafel_Io.jpg" decoding="async" width="240" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Tafel_Io.jpg/360px-Tafel_Io.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Tafel_Io.jpg/480px-Tafel_Io.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2803" data-file-height="1858" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tafel_Io.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Various infantry of the 17th through 18th century (<a href="/wiki/Halberdier" class="mw-redirect" title="Halberdier">halberdier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arquebus" title="Arquebus">arquebusier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pikeman" class="mw-redirect" title="Pikeman">pikeman</a>, and mix of <a href="/wiki/Musketeer" title="Musketeer">musketeers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grenadier" title="Grenadier">grenadiers</a>) of <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Duchy of Württemberg">Duchy of Württemberg</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:M2_loading.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/M2_loading.jpg/150px-M2_loading.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="226" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/M2_loading.jpg/225px-M2_loading.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/M2_loading.jpg/300px-M2_loading.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1660" data-file-height="2500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:M2_loading.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Infantry of the US <a href="/wiki/3rd_Armored_Cavalry_Regiment" class="mw-redirect" title="3rd Armored Cavalry Regiment">3rd Armored Cavalry Regiment</a> enter their <a href="/wiki/M2_Bradley" title="M2 Bradley">M2 Bradley</a> <a href="/wiki/IFV" class="mw-redirect" title="IFV">IFV</a> during a combat patrol, <a href="/wiki/Tall_Afar" class="mw-redirect" title="Tall Afar">Tall Afar</a>, Iraq, 2006</div></div></div>
<p>In English, use of the term <i>infantry</i> began about the 1570s, describing <a href="/wiki/Soldier" title="Soldier">soldiers</a> who march and fight on foot. The word derives from <a href="/wiki/Middle_French" title="Middle French">Middle French</a> <i>infanterie</i>, from older <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> (also Spanish) <i>infanteria</i> (foot soldiers too inexperienced for cavalry), from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/infans" class="extiw" title="wikt:infans">īnfāns</a></i> (without speech, newborn, foolish), from which English also gets <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/infant" class="extiw" title="wikt:infant">infant</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The individual-soldier term <b>infantryman</b> was not coined until 1837.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In modern usage, foot soldiers of any era are now considered infantry and infantrymen.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From the mid-18th century until 1881 the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> named its infantry as numbered regiments "of Foot" to distinguish them from <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">cavalry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dragoon" title="Dragoon">dragoon</a> regiments (see <a href="/wiki/List_of_Regiments_of_Foot" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Regiments of Foot">List of Regiments of Foot</a>).
</p><p>Infantry equipped with special weapons were often named after that weapon, such as <a href="/wiki/Grenadier" title="Grenadier">grenadiers</a> for their <a href="/wiki/Grenade" title="Grenade">grenades</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Fusilier" title="Fusilier">fusiliers</a> for their <i>fusils</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup> These names can persist long after the weapon speciality; examples of infantry units that retained such names are the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Irish_Fusiliers" title="Royal Irish Fusiliers">Royal Irish Fusiliers</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Grenadier_Guards" title="Grenadier Guards">Grenadier Guards</a>.
</p><p>More commonly in modern times, infantry with <a href="/wiki/Special_forces" title="Special forces">special tactics</a> are named for their roles, such as <a href="/wiki/Commando" title="Commando">commandos</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Rangers" title="United States Army Rangers">rangers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sniper" title="Sniper">snipers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marines" title="Marines">marines</a>, (who all have additional training) and <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a> (who have limited training); they are still infantry due to their expectation to fight as infantry when they enter combat.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dragoon" title="Dragoon">Dragoons</a> were created as <a href="/wiki/Mounted_infantry" title="Mounted infantry">mounted infantry</a>, with horses for travel between battles; they were still considered infantry since they dismounted before combat. However, if <a href="/wiki/Light_cavalry" title="Light cavalry">light cavalry</a> was lacking in an army, any available dragoons might be assigned their duties; this practise increased over time, and dragoons eventually received all the weapons and training as both infantry and cavalry, and could be classified as both. Conversely, starting about the mid-19th century, regular cavalry have been forced to spend more of their time dismounted in combat due to the ever-increasing effectiveness of enemy infantry firearms. Thus most cavalry transitioned to mounted infantry. As with grenadiers, the <i>dragoon</i> and <i>cavalry</i> designations can be retained long after their horses, such as in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dragoon_Guards" title="Royal Dragoon Guards">Royal Dragoon Guards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Lancers" title="Royal Lancers">Royal Lancers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Royal_Hussars" title="King&#39;s Royal Hussars">King's Royal Hussars</a>.
</p><p>Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Motorised_infantry" class="mw-redirect" title="Motorised infantry">motorised infantry</a> have trucks and other unarmed vehicles for non-combat movement, but are still infantry since they leave their vehicles for any combat. Most modern infantry have vehicle transport, to the point where infantry being motorised is generally assumed, and the few exceptions might be identified as modern <i>light infantry</i>, or "leg infantry" colloquially. <a href="/wiki/Mechanised_infantry" class="mw-redirect" title="Mechanised infantry">Mechanised infantry</a> go beyond motorised, having transport vehicles with combat abilities, <a href="/wiki/Armoured_personnel_carrier" title="Armoured personnel carrier">armoured personnel carriers</a> (APCs), providing at least some options for combat without leaving their vehicles. In modern infantry, some APCs have evolved to be <a href="/wiki/Infantry_fighting_vehicle" title="Infantry fighting vehicle">infantry fighting vehicles</a> (IFVs), which are transport vehicles with more substantial combat abilities, approaching those of <a href="/wiki/Light_tank" title="Light tank">light tanks</a>. Some well-equipped mechanised infantry can be designated as <i>armoured infantry</i>.  Given that infantry forces typically also have some tanks, and given that most armoured forces have more mechanised infantry units than tank units in their organisation, the distinction between mechanised infantry and armour forces has blurred.
</p><p>The terms "infantry", "armour", and "cavalry" used in the official names for <a href="/wiki/Military_unit" class="mw-redirect" title="Military unit">military units</a> like <a href="/wiki/Division_(military)" title="Division (military)">divisions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brigade" title="Brigade">brigades</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Regiment" title="Regiment">regiments</a> might be better understood as a description of their expected balance of defensive, offensive, and mobility roles, rather than just use of vehicles. Some modern mechanised infantry units are termed <i>cavalry</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Armoured_cavalry" title="Armoured cavalry">armoured cavalry</a></i>, even though they never had horses, to emphasise their combat mobility.
</p><p>In the modern <a href="/wiki/US_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="US Army">US Army</a>, about 15% of soldiers are officially <i>Infantry</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Basic_Training" title="United States Army Basic Training">basic training</a> for all new US Army soldiers includes use of infantry weapons and tactics, even for tank crews, artillery crews, and base and logistical personnel.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infantry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_infantry" title="History of infantry">History of infantry</a></div>
<p>The first warriors, adopting <a href="/wiki/Hunting_weapon#History" title="Hunting weapon">hunting weapons</a> or improvised <a href="/wiki/Melee_weapon" title="Melee weapon">melee weapons</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Kelly_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> before the existence of any organised military, likely started essentially as loose groups without any organisation or formation. But this changed sometime before <a href="/wiki/Recorded_history" title="Recorded history">recorded history</a>; the first <a href="/wiki/List_of_empires" title="List of empires">ancient empires</a> (2500–1500 BC) are shown to have some soldiers with standardised military equipment, and the training and discipline required for battlefield formations and manoeuvres: <a href="/wiki/Regular_army" title="Regular army">regular infantry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Though the main force of the army, these forces were usually kept small due to their cost of training and upkeep, and might be supplemented by local short-term mass-conscript forces using the older <a href="/wiki/Irregular_infantry" class="mw-redirect" title="Irregular infantry">irregular infantry</a> weapons and tactics; this remained a common practice almost up to modern times.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Before the adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Chariot" title="Chariot">chariot</a> to create the first mobile fighting forces <abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;2000 BC</span>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> all armies were pure infantry. Even after, with a few exceptions like the <a href="/wiki/Mongol_Empire" title="Mongol Empire">Mongol Empire</a>, infantry has been the largest component of most armies in history.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Classical_Antiquity" class="mw-redirect" title="Classical Antiquity">Classical Antiquity</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 8th century BC to 15th century AD), infantry are categorised as either <a href="/wiki/Heavy_infantry" title="Heavy infantry">heavy infantry</a> or <a href="/wiki/Light_infantry" title="Light infantry">light infantry</a>. Heavy infantry, such as Greek <a href="/wiki/Hoplite" title="Hoplite">hoplites</a>, Macedonian <a href="/wiki/Phalangites" class="mw-redirect" title="Phalangites">phalangites</a>, and Roman <a href="/wiki/Legionaries" class="mw-redirect" title="Legionaries">legionaries</a>, specialised in <a href="/wiki/Close_order_formation" title="Close order formation">dense, solid formations</a> driving into the main enemy lines, using weight of numbers to achieve a <a href="/wiki/Decisive_victory" title="Decisive victory">decisive victory</a>, and were usually equipped with heavier <a href="/wiki/Weapon" title="Weapon">weapons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Body_armor" title="Body armor">armour</a> to fit their role. Light infantry, such as Greek <a href="/wiki/Peltasts" class="mw-redirect" title="Peltasts">peltasts</a>, Balearic <a href="/wiki/Sling_(weapon)" title="Sling (weapon)">slingers</a>, and Roman <a href="/wiki/Velites" title="Velites">velites</a>, using open formations and greater manoeuvrability, took on most other combat roles: <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance" title="Reconnaissance">scouting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Screening_(tactical)" title="Screening (tactical)">screening</a> the army on the march, <a href="/wiki/Skirmishing" class="mw-redirect" title="Skirmishing">skirmishing</a> to delay, disrupt, or weaken the enemy to prepare for the main forces' battlefield attack, protecting them from <a href="/wiki/Flanking_maneuver" title="Flanking maneuver">flanking manoeuvers</a>, and then afterwards either pursuing the fleeing enemy or covering their army's retreat.
</p><p>After the fall of Rome, the quality of heavy infantry declined, and warfare was dominated by <a href="/wiki/Heavy_cavalry" title="Heavy cavalry">heavy cavalry</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> such as <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knights</a>, forming small elite units for decisive <a href="/wiki/Shock_combat" class="mw-redirect" title="Shock combat">shock combat</a>, supported by peasant infantry <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militias</a> and assorted light infantry from the lower classes. Towards the end of Middle Ages, this began to change, where more professional and better trained light infantry could be effective against knights, such as the <a href="/wiki/English_longbow" title="English longbow">English longbowmen</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years&#39; War">Hundred Years' War</a>. By the start of the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, the infantry began to <a href="/wiki/Infantry_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Infantry revolution">return to dominance</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Swiss_pikemen" class="mw-redirect" title="Swiss pikemen">Swiss pikemen</a> and German <a href="/wiki/Landsknechts" class="mw-redirect" title="Landsknechts">Landsknechts</a> filling the role of heavy infantry again, using <a href="/wiki/Pike_square" title="Pike square">dense formations of pikes</a> to drive off any cavalry.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Dense formations are vulnerable to ranged weapons. Technological developments allowed the raising of large numbers of light infantry units armed with ranged weapons, without the years of training expected for traditional high-skilled archers and slingers. This started slowly, first with <a href="/wiki/Crossbow" title="Crossbow">crossbowmen</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Hand_cannon" title="Hand cannon">hand cannoneers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arquebus" title="Arquebus">arquebusiers</a>, each with increasing effectiveness, marking the beginning of <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_warfare" title="Early modern warfare">early modern warfare</a>, when <a href="/wiki/Firearm" title="Firearm">firearms</a> rendered the use of heavy infantry obsolete. The introduction of <a href="/wiki/Musketeer" title="Musketeer">musketeers</a> using <a href="/wiki/Bayonet" title="Bayonet">bayonets</a> in the mid 17th century began replacement of the pike with the <a href="/wiki/Infantry_square" title="Infantry square">infantry square</a> replacing the pike square.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>To maximise their firepower, musketeer infantry were trained to fight in wide lines facing the enemy, creating <a href="/wiki/Line_infantry" title="Line infantry">line infantry</a>. These fulfilled the central battlefield role of earlier heavy infantry, using ranged weapons instead of melee weapons. To support these lines, smaller infantry formations using dispersed <a href="/wiki/Skirmish_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Skirmish line">skirmish lines</a> were created, called light infantry, fulfilling the same multiple roles as earlier light infantry. Their arms were no lighter than line infantry; they were distinguished by their skirmish formation and flexible tactics.
</p><p>The modern <a href="/wiki/Rifleman" title="Rifleman">rifleman</a> infantry became the primary force for taking and holding ground on battlefields worldwide, a vital element of <a href="/wiki/Combined_arms" title="Combined arms">combined arms</a> combat. As firepower continued to increase, use of infantry lines diminished, until all infantry became light infantry in practice.
</p><p>Modern classifications of infantry have expanded to reflect modern equipment and tactics, such as <a href="/wiki/Motorised_infantry" class="mw-redirect" title="Motorised infantry">motorised infantry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mechanised_infantry" class="mw-redirect" title="Mechanised infantry">mechanised or armoured infantry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mountain_infantry" class="mw-redirect" title="Mountain infantry">mountain infantry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marine_infantry" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine infantry">marine infantry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Airborne_infantry" class="mw-redirect" title="Airborne infantry">airborne infantry</a>.
</p>

<p>An infantryman's equipment is of vital concern both for the man and the military. The needs of the infantryman to maintain fitness and effectiveness must be constantly balanced against being overburdened. While soldiers in other military branches can use their mount or vehicle for carrying equipment, and tend to operate together as crews serving their vehicle or ordnance, infantrymen must operate more independently; each infantryman usually having much more personal equipment to use and carry. This encourages searching for ingenious combinations of effective, rugged, serviceable and adaptable, yet light, compact, and handy infantry equipment.
</p><p>Beyond their main arms and armour, each infantryman's "military kit" includes <a href="/wiki/Combat_boot" title="Combat boot">combat boots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Battledress" class="mw-redirect" title="Battledress">battledress</a> or combat <a href="/wiki/Military_uniform" title="Military uniform">uniform</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camping_gear" class="mw-redirect" title="Camping gear">camping gear</a>, heavy weather gear, <a href="/wiki/Survival_kit#Military_kits" title="Survival kit">survival gear</a>, secondary weapons and <a href="/wiki/Ammunition" title="Ammunition">ammunition</a>, weapon service and repair kits, health and hygiene items, <a href="/wiki/Mess_kit#Military_mess_kit" title="Mess kit">mess kit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Field_ration" title="Field ration">rations</a>, filled <a href="/wiki/Canteen_(bottle)" title="Canteen (bottle)">water canteen</a>, and all other consumables each infantryman needs for the expected duration of time operating away from their unit's base, plus any special mission-specific equipment. One of the most valuable pieces of gear is the <a href="/wiki/Entrenching_tool" title="Entrenching tool">entrenching tool</a>—basically a folding <a href="/wiki/Spade" title="Spade">spade</a>—which can be employed not only to dig important defences, but also in a variety of other daily tasks, and even sometimes as a weapon.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Infantry typically have shared equipment on top of this, like tents or heavy weapons, where the carrying burden is spread across several infantrymen. In all, this can reach 25–45&#160;kg (60–100&#160;lb) for each soldier on the march.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Such heavy infantry burdens have changed little over centuries of warfare; in the late Roman Republic, legionaries were nicknamed <i><a href="/wiki/Marian_reforms" title="Marian reforms">Marius' mules</a></i> as their main activity seemed to be carrying the weight of their legion around on their backs.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;note 2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When combat is expected, infantry typically switch to "packing light", meaning reducing their equipment to weapons, ammo, and bare essentials, and leaving the rest with their transport, at camp or rally point, in temporary hidden caches, or even (in emergencies) discarding whatever may slow them down.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Additional specialised equipment may be required, depending on the mission or to the particular terrain or environment, including <a href="/wiki/Satchel_charge" title="Satchel charge">satchel charges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demolition" title="Demolition">demolition</a> tools, <a href="/wiki/Land_mine" title="Land mine">mines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Barbed_wire" title="Barbed wire">barbed wire</a>, carried by the infantry or attached specialists.
</p><p>Historically, infantry have suffered high casualty rates from <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">disease</a>, exposure, exhaustion and privation — often in excess of the casualties suffered from enemy attacks.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Better infantry equipment to support their health, energy, and protect from environmental factors greatly reduces these rates of loss, and increase their level of effective action. Health, energy, and morale are greatly influenced by how the soldier is fed, so militaries often standardised field rations, starting from <a href="/wiki/Hardtack" title="Hardtack">hardtack</a>, to US <a href="/wiki/K-ration" title="K-ration">K-rations</a>, to modern <a href="/wiki/MRE" class="mw-redirect" title="MRE">MREs</a>.
</p><p>Communications gear has become a necessity, as it allows effective command of infantry units over greater distances, and communication with artillery and other support units. Modern infantry can have <a href="/wiki/GPS" class="mw-redirect" title="GPS">GPS</a>, encrypted individual communications equipment, surveillance and night vision equipment, advanced intelligence and other high-tech mission-unique aids.
</p><p>Armies have sought to improve and standardise infantry gear to reduce fatigue for extended carrying, increase freedom of movement, accessibility, and compatibility with other carried gear, such as the US <a href="/wiki/All-purpose_Lightweight_Individual_Carrying_Equipment" title="All-purpose Lightweight Individual Carrying Equipment">All-purpose Lightweight Individual Carrying Equipment</a> (ALICE).
</p>

<p>Infantrymen are defined by their primary arms – the <a href="/wiki/Personal_weapons" class="mw-redirect" title="Personal weapons">personal weapons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Body_armour" class="mw-redirect" title="Body armour">body armour</a> for their own individual use. The available technology, resources, history, and society can produce quite different weapons for each military and era, but common infantry weapons can be distinguished in a few basic categories.<sup id="cite_ref-Germany_at_War_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Germany_at_War-19">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-List_of_weapons_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-List_of_weapons-20">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><u>Ranged combat weapons</u>: javelins, slings, <a href="/wiki/Blowgun" title="Blowgun">blowguns</a>, bows, crossbows, hand cannons, arquebuses, muskets, grenades, <a href="/wiki/Flamethrower" title="Flamethrower">flamethrowers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-List_of_weapons_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-List_of_weapons-20">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><u><a href="/wiki/Close_combat" title="Close combat">Close combat</a> weapons</u>: bludgeoning weapons like <a href="/wiki/Club_(weapon)" title="Club (weapon)">clubs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flail_(weapon)" title="Flail (weapon)">flails</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mace_(bludgeon)" title="Mace (bludgeon)">maces</a>; <a href="/wiki/Bladed_weapons" class="mw-redirect" title="Bladed weapons">bladed weapons</a> like swords, <a href="/wiki/Dagger" title="Dagger">daggers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Axe" title="Axe">axes</a>; <a href="/wiki/Pole_weapons" class="mw-redirect" title="Pole weapons">pole weapons</a> like spears, halberds, <a href="/wiki/Naginata" title="Naginata">naginata</a>, and pikes.<sup id="cite_ref-List_of_weapons_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-List_of_weapons-20">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><u>Both ranged and close weapons</u>: the bayonet fixed to a firearm allows infantrymen to use the same weapon for both ranged combat and close combat. This started with muskets and continued with <a href="/wiki/Rifle" title="Rifle">rifles</a> to <a href="/wiki/Automatic_firearm" title="Automatic firearm">automatic firearms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-List_of_weapons_20-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-List_of_weapons-20">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Use of the bayonet has declined with modern automatic firearms, but still generally kept as a weapon of last resort.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Infantrymen often carry secondary or back-up weapons, sometimes called a <a href="/wiki/Side_arm" title="Side arm">sidearm</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ancillary_weapon" title="Ancillary weapon">ancillary weapons</a> in modern terminology, either issued officially as an addition to the soldier's standard arms, or acquired unofficially by any other means as an individual preference. Such weapons are used when the primary weapon is no longer effective, such it becoming damaged, running out of ammunition, malfunction, or in a change of tactical situation where another weapon is preferred, such as going from ranged to close combat. Infantry with ranged or pole weapons often carried a sword or dagger for possible hand-to-hand combat.<sup id="cite_ref-Germany_at_War_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Germany_at_War-19">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Pilum" title="Pilum">pilum</a></i> was a javelin of the Roman legionaries threw just before drawing their primary weapon, the <i><a href="/wiki/Gladius" title="Gladius">gladius</a></i> (short sword), and closing with the enemy line.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Modern infantrymen now treat the bayonet as a backup weapon, but may also have <a href="/wiki/Handgun" title="Handgun">handguns</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pistol" title="Pistol">pistols</a>. They may also deploy anti-personnel mines, booby traps, incendiary or explosive devices defensively before combat.
</p><p>Some non-weapon equipment are designed for close combat shock effects, to get and psychological edge before melee, such as <a href="/wiki/War_flag" title="War flag">battle flags</a>, <a href="/wiki/War_drum" class="mw-redirect" title="War drum">war drums</a>, brilliant <a href="/wiki/Military_uniform" title="Military uniform">uniforms</a>, fierce <a href="/wiki/Body_paint" class="mw-redirect" title="Body paint">body paint</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tattoo" title="Tattoo">tattoos</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Battle_cry" title="Battle cry">battle cries</a>. These have become mostly only ceremonial since the decline of close combat military tactics.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Protection">Protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infantry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Infantry have employed many different methods of protection from enemy attacks, including various kinds of armour and other gear, and tactical procedures.
</p><p>The most basic is <a href="/wiki/Personal_armour" class="mw-redirect" title="Personal armour">personal armour</a>. This includes <a href="/wiki/Shield" title="Shield">shields</a>, <a href="/wiki/Military_helmet" class="mw-redirect" title="Military helmet">helmets</a> and many types of armour – <a href="/wiki/Gambeson" title="Gambeson">padded linen</a>, leather, <a href="/wiki/Lamellar_armour" title="Lamellar armour">lamellar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mail_(armor)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mail (armor)">mail</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plate_armor" class="mw-redirect" title="Plate armor">plate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">kevlar</a>. Initially, armour was used to defend both from ranged and close combat; even a fairly light shield could help defend against most slings and javelins, though high-strength bows and crossbows could penetrate most armour at very close range. Infantry armour had to compromises between protection and coverage, as a full suit of attack-proof armour would be too heavy to wear in combat.
</p><p>As firearms improved, armour for ranged defence had to be thicker and stronger. With the introduction of the heavy arquebus to piece standard steel armour, it was proved easier to make heavier firearms than heavier armour; armour transitioned to be only for close combat purposes. Pikemen armour tended to be just steel helmets and breastplates, and gunners little or no armour. By the time of the musket, the dominance of firepower shifted militaries away from any close combat, and use of armour decreased, until infantry typically went without any armour.
</p><p>Helmets were added back during World War I as artillery began to dominate the battlefield, to protect against their <a href="/wiki/Fragmentation_(weaponry)" title="Fragmentation (weaponry)">fragmentation</a> and other blast effects beyond a direct hit. Modern developments in bullet-proof composite materials like kevlar have started a return to body armour for infantry, though the extra weight is a notable burden.
</p><p>In modern times, infantrymen must also often carry protective measures against <a href="/wiki/Chemical_warfare" title="Chemical warfare">chemical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biological_warfare" title="Biological warfare">biological</a> attack, including <a href="/wiki/Gas_mask" title="Gas mask">gas masks</a>, counter-agents, and protective suits. All of these protective measures add to the weight an infantryman must carry, and may decrease combat efficiency. Modern militaries are struggling to balance the value of personal body protection versus the weight burden and ability to move under such weight.
</p>

<p>Early crew-served weapons were <a href="/wiki/Siege_weapon" class="mw-redirect" title="Siege weapon">siege weapons</a>, like the <a href="/wiki/Ballista" title="Ballista">ballista</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trebuchet" title="Trebuchet">trebuchet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Battering_ram" title="Battering ram">battering ram</a>. Modern versions include <a href="/wiki/Machine_guns" class="mw-redirect" title="Machine guns">machine guns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anti-tank_missiles" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-tank missiles">anti-tank missiles</a>, and infantry <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(weapon)" title="Mortar (weapon)">mortars</a>.
</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tactical_formation" title="Tactical formation">Tactical formation</a></div>
<p>Beginning with the development the first regular military forces, close-combat regular infantry fought less as unorganised groups of individuals and more in coordinated units, maintaining a defined <a href="/wiki/Tactical_formation" title="Tactical formation">tactical formation</a> during combat, for increased battlefield effectiveness; such infantry formations and the arms they used developed together, starting with the spear and the shield.
</p><p>A spear has decent attack abilities with the additional advantage keeping opponents at distance; this advantage can be increased by using longer spears, but this could allow the opponent to side-step the point of the spear and close for <a href="/wiki/Hand-to-hand_combat" title="Hand-to-hand combat">hand-to-hand combat</a> where the longer spear is near useless. This can be avoided when each spearman stays side-by-side with the others in close formation, each covering the ones next to him, presenting a solid wall of spears to the enemy that they cannot get around.
</p><p>Similarly, a shield has decent defence abilities, but is literally hit-or-miss; an attack from an unexpected angle can bypass it completely. Larger shields can cover more, but are also heavier and less manoeuvrable, making unexpected attacks even more of a problem. This can be avoided by having shield-armed soldiers stand close together, side-by-side, each protecting both themselves and their immediate comrades, presenting a solid <a href="/wiki/Shield_wall" title="Shield wall">shield wall</a> to the enemy.
</p><p>The opponents for these first formations, the close-combat infantry of more <a href="/wiki/Tribal_society" class="mw-redirect" title="Tribal society">tribal societies</a>, or any military without regular infantry (so called "<a href="/wiki/Barbarian" title="Barbarian">barbarians</a>") used arms that focused on the individual – weapons using personal strength and force, such as larger swinging swords, axes, and clubs. These take more room and individual freedom to swing and wield, necessitating a more loose organisation. While this may allow for a fierce running attack (an initial shock advantage) the tighter formation of the heavy spear and shield infantry gave them a local manpower advantage where several might be able to fight each opponent.
</p><p>Thus tight formations heightened advantages of heavy arms, and gave greater local numbers in melee. To also increase their staying power, multiple rows of heavy infantrymen were added. This also increased their shock combat effect; individual opponents saw themselves literally lined-up against several heavy infantryman each, with seemingly no chance of defeating all of them. <i>Heavy infantry</i> developed into huge solid block formations, up to a hundred meters wide and a dozen rows deep.
</p><p>Maintaining the advantages of heavy infantry meant maintaining formation; this became even more important when two forces with heavy infantry met in battle; the solidity of the formation became the deciding factor. Intense discipline and training became paramount. Empires formed around their military.
</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Military_organization" title="Military organization">Military organization</a></div>
<p>The organization of military forces into regular military units is first noted in Egyptian records of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kadesh" title="Battle of Kadesh">Battle of Kadesh</a> (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;1274 BC</span>). Soldiers were grouped into units of 50, which were in turn grouped into larger units of 250, then 1,000, and finally into units of up to 5,000 – the largest independent command. Several of these Egyptian "divisions" made up an army, but operated independently, both on the march and tactically, demonstrating sufficient military <a href="/wiki/Command_and_control" title="Command and control">command and control</a> organisation for basic battlefield manoeuvres. Similar hierarchical organizations have been noted in other ancient armies, typically with approximately 10 to 100 to 1,000 ratios (even where <a href="/wiki/Base_10" class="mw-redirect" title="Base 10">base 10</a> was not common), similar to modern <a href="/wiki/Section_(military_unit)" title="Section (military unit)">sections (squads)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Company_(military_unit)" title="Company (military unit)">companies</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Regiment" title="Regiment">regiments</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The training of the infantry has differed drastically over time and from place to place. The cost of maintaining an army in fighting order and the seasonal nature of warfare precluded large permanent armies.
</p><p>The antiquity saw everything from the well-trained and motivated citizen armies of Greek and Rome, the tribal host assembled from farmers and hunters with only passing acquaintance with warfare and masses of lightly armed and ill-trained militia put up as a last ditch effort.
</p><p>In medieval times the foot soldiers varied from peasant levies to semi-permanent companies of mercenaries, foremost among them the Swiss, English, <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">Aragonese</a> and German, to men-at-arms who went into battle as well-armoured as knights, the latter of which at times also fought on foot.
</p><p>The creation of <a href="/wiki/Standing_army" title="Standing army">standing armies</a>—permanently assembled for war or defence—saw increase in training and experience. The increased use of firearms and the need for drill to handle them efficiently.
</p><p>The introduction of national and mass armies saw an establishment of minimum requirements and the introduction of special troops (first of them the engineers going back to medieval times, but also different kinds of infantry adopted to specific terrain, bicycle, motorcycle, motorised and mechanised troops) culminating with the introduction of highly trained special forces during the first and second World War.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Combat_role">Combat role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infantry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Combat role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a branch of the <a href="/wiki/Armed_forces" class="mw-redirect" title="Armed forces">armed forces</a>, the role of the infantry in warfare is to engage, fight, and kill the enemy at close range—using either a firearm (<a href="/wiki/Rifle" title="Rifle">rifle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pistol" title="Pistol">pistol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Machine_gun" title="Machine gun">machine gun</a>), an edged-weapon (<a href="/wiki/Fairbairn%E2%80%93Sykes_fighting_knife" title="Fairbairn–Sykes fighting knife">knife</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bayonet" title="Bayonet">bayonet</a>), or bare hands (<a href="/wiki/Hand-to-hand_combat" title="Hand-to-hand combat">close quarters combat</a>)—as required by the mission to hand; thus
</p>

<ul><li>in the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Army" title="Australian Army">Australian Army</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Army" title="New Zealand Army">New Zealand Army</a> the role of the infantry is "to seek out and close with the enemy, to kill or capture him, to seize and hold ground, to repel attack, by day or night, regardless of season, weather or terrain".<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Army" title="Canadian Army">Canadian Army</a>, the role of the infantry is "to close with, and destroy the enemy".<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">U.S. Army</a>, the "infantry closes with the enemy, by means of fire and maneuver, in order to destroy or capture him, or to repel his assault by fire, close combat, and counterattack".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>in the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Marine_Corps" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Marine Corps">U.S. Marine Corps</a>, the role of the infantry is to "locate, close with, and destroy the enemy with fire and maneuver, and to repel the enemy assault by fire and close combat".<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Beginning with the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a> of the early 19th century, artillery has become an increasingly dominant force on the battlefield. Since <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, combat aircraft and armoured vehicles have also become dominant. However, the most effective method for locating all enemy forces on a battlefield is still the infantry patrol, and it is the presence or absence of infantry that ultimately determines whether a particular piece of ground has been captured or held. In 20th and 21st century warfare, infantry functions most effectively as part of a <a href="/wiki/Combined_arms" title="Combined arms">combined arms</a> team including artillery, armour, and combat aircraft. Studies have shown that of all casualties, 50% or more were caused by artillery; about 10% were caused by machine guns; 2–5% by rifle fire; and 1% or less by hand grenades, bayonets, knives, and unarmed combat combined.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Several infantry divisions both Allied and Axis in the European theatre of WWII suffered higher than 100% combat and non combat casualties and some above 200%, meaning that the number of service personnel that became casualties was greater than the sum of the divisions' available service positions at full strength.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. </p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Attack_operations">Attack operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infantry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Attack operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Attack operations are the most basic role of the infantry, and along with defence, form the main stances of the infantry on the battlefield. Traditionally, in an open battle, or <a href="/wiki/Meeting_engagement" title="Meeting engagement">meeting engagement</a>, two armies would manoeuvre to contact, at which point they would form up their infantry and other units opposite each other. Then one or both would advance and attempt to defeat the enemy force. The goal of an attack remains the same: to advance into an enemy-held <i>objective,</i> most frequently a hill, river crossing, city or other dominant terrain feature, and dislodge the enemy, thereby establishing control of the objective.
</p><p>Attacks are often feared by the infantry conducting them because of the high number of casualties suffered while advancing to close with and destroy the enemy while under enemy fire. In <a href="/wiki/Mechanised_infantry" class="mw-redirect" title="Mechanised infantry">mechanised infantry</a> the armoured personnel carrier (APC) is considered the assaulting position. These APCs can deliver infantrymen through the front lines to the battle and—in the case of <a href="/wiki/Infantry_fighting_vehicle" title="Infantry fighting vehicle">infantry fighting vehicles</a>—contribute supporting firepower to engage the enemy. Successful attacks rely on sufficient force, preparative <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance" title="Reconnaissance">reconnaissance</a> and battlefield preparation with bomb assets. Retention of discipline and cohesion throughout the attack is paramount to success. A subcategory of attacks is the <a href="/wiki/Ambush" title="Ambush">ambush</a>, where infantrymen lie in wait for enemy forces before attacking at a vulnerable moment. This gives the ambushing infantrymen the combat advantage of surprise, concealment and superior firing positions, and causes confusion. The ambushed unit does not know what it is up against, or where they are attacking from.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Patrol_operations">Patrol operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infantry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Patrol operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:YON_642.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/YON_642.jpg/220px-YON_642.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/YON_642.jpg/330px-YON_642.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/YON_642.jpg/440px-YON_642.jpg 2x" data-file-width="768" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:YON_642.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Indonesian_Army" title="Indonesian Army">Indonesian Army</a> <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_Army_Infantry_Battalions" class="mw-redirect" title="Indonesian Army Infantry Battalions">Infantry</a> soldiers from the 642nd Infantry Battalion line up before deployment to the international Border of Indonesia and <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">Papua New Guinea</a> in 2013 for border patrol operations in dense forests and mountainous terrain</div></div></div>
<p>Patrolling is the most common infantry mission. Full-scale attacks and defensive efforts are occasional, but patrols are constant. Patrols consist of small groups of infantry moving about in areas of possible enemy activity to locate the enemy and destroy them when found. Patrols are used not only on the front-lines, but in rear areas where enemy infiltration or insurgencies are possible.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pursuit_operations">Pursuit operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infantry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Pursuit operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Pursuit is a role that the infantry often assumes. The objective of pursuit operations is the destruction of withdrawing enemy forces which are not capable of effectively engaging friendly units, before they can build their strength to the point where they are effective. Infantry traditionally have been the main force to overrun these units in the past, and in modern combat are used to pursue enemy forces in constricted terrain (urban areas in particular), where faster forces, such as armoured vehicles are incapable of going or would be exposed to ambush.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defence_operations">Defence operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infantry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Defence operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Defence operations are the natural counter to attacks, in which the mission is to hold an objective and defeat enemy forces attempting to dislodge the defender. Defensive posture offers many advantages to the infantry, including the ability to use terrain and constructed fortifications to advantage; these reduce exposure to enemy fire compared with advancing forces. Effective defence relies on minimising losses to enemy fire, breaking the enemy's cohesion before their advance is completed, and preventing enemy penetration of defensive positions.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Escort_operations">Escort operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infantry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Escort operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Escorting consists of protecting support units from ambush, particularly from hostile infantry forces. Combat support units (a majority of the military) are not as well armed or trained as infantry units and have a different mission. Therefore, they need the protection of the infantry, particularly when on the move. This is one of the most important roles for the modern infantry, particularly when operating alongside armoured vehicles. In this capacity, infantry essentially conducts patrol on the move, scouring terrain which may hide enemy infantry waiting to ambush friendly vehicles, and identifying enemy strong points for attack by the heavier units.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:VanDoos_Urban_Warfare_training.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/VanDoos_Urban_Warfare_training.JPG/220px-VanDoos_Urban_Warfare_training.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/VanDoos_Urban_Warfare_training.JPG/330px-VanDoos_Urban_Warfare_training.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/VanDoos_Urban_Warfare_training.JPG/440px-VanDoos_Urban_Warfare_training.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:VanDoos_Urban_Warfare_training.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Canadian soldiers of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_22e_R%C3%A9giment" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal 22e Régiment">Royal 22<sup>e</sup> Régiment</a></div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Base_defence">Base defence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infantry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Base defence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Infantry units are tasked to protect certain areas like command posts or airbases. Units assigned to this job usually have a large number of military police attached to them for control of checkpoints and prisons.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Manoeuvring_operations">Manoeuvring operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infantry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Manoeuvring operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Maneouvering consumes much of an infantry unit's time. Infantry, like all combat arms units, are often manoeuvred to meet battlefield needs, and often must do so under enemy attack. The infantry must maintain their cohesion and readiness during the move to ensure their usefulness when they reach their objective. Traditionally, infantry have relied on their own legs for mobility, but mechanised or armoured infantry often uses trucks and armoured vehicles for transport. These units can quickly disembark and transition to light infantry, without vehicles, to access terrain which armoured vehicles can't effectively access.
</p>
<h3><span id="Reconnaissance.2Fintelligence_gathering"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconnaissance/intelligence_gathering">Reconnaissance/intelligence gathering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infantry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Reconnaissance/intelligence gathering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Surveillance operations are often carried out with the employment of small recon units or sniper teams which gather information about the enemy, reporting on characteristics such as size, activity, location, unit and equipment. These infantry units typically are known for their stealth and ability to operate for periods of time within close proximity of the enemy without being detected. They may engage high-profile targets, or be employed to hunt down terrorist cells and insurgents within a given area. These units may also entice the enemy to engage a located recon unit, thus disclosing their location to be destroyed by more powerful friendly forces.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_reserve_force">Military reserve force</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infantry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Military reserve force">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some assignments for infantry units involve deployment behind the front, although patrol and security operations are usually maintained in case of enemy infiltration. This is usually the best time for infantry units to integrate replacements into units and to maintain equipment. Additionally, soldiers can be rested and general readiness should improve. However, the unit must be ready for deployment at any point.
</p>
<h3><span id="Construction.2Fengineering"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction/engineering">Construction/engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infantry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Construction/engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_(military)" title="Pioneer (military)">Pioneer (military)</a></div>
<p>This can be undertaken either in reserve or on the front, but consists of using infantry troops as labor for construction of field positions, roads, bridges, airfields, and all other manner of structures. The infantry is often given this assignment because of the physical quantity of strong men within the unit, although it can lessen a unit's morale and limit the unit's ability to maintain readiness and perform other missions. More often, such jobs are given to specialist engineering corps.
</p>
<h3><span id="Raids.2Fhostage_rescue"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Raids/hostage_rescue">Raids/hostage rescue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infantry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Raids/hostage rescue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Infantry units are trained to quickly mobilise, infiltrate, enter and neutralise threat forces when appropriate combat intelligence indicates to secure a location, rescue or capture high-profile targets.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Urban_combat">Urban combat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infantry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Urban combat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Urban combat poses unique challenges to the combat forces. It is one of the most complicated type of operations an infantry unit will undertake. With many places for the enemy to hide and ambush from, infantry units must be trained in how to enter a city, and systematically clear the buildings, which most likely will be booby trapped, in order to kill or capture enemy personnel within the city. Care must be taken to differentiate innocent civilians who often hide and support the enemy from the non-uniformed armed enemy forces. Civilian and military casualties both are usually very high.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Day_to_day_service">Day to day service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infantry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Day to day service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alarmposten1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Alarmposten1.jpg/220px-Alarmposten1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Alarmposten1.jpg/330px-Alarmposten1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Alarmposten1.jpg/440px-Alarmposten1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alarmposten1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/German_Army" title="German Army">German Army</a> mechanised infantry (<a href="/wiki/Panzergrenadier" title="Panzergrenadier">Panzergrenadiers</a>) on an alert post during an exercise in 2006</div></div></div>
<p>Because of an infantryman's duties with firearms, explosives, physical and <a href="/wiki/Combat_stress_reaction" title="Combat stress reaction">emotional stress</a>, and physical violence, casualties and deaths are not uncommon in both war and in peacetime training or operations. It is a highly dangerous and demanding combat service; in World War II, military doctors concluded that even physically unwounded soldiers were psychologically worn out after about 200 days of combat.
</p><p>The physical, mental, and environmental operating demands of the infantryman are high. All of the combat necessities such as ammunition, weapon systems, food, water, clothing, and shelter are carried on the backs of the infantrymen, at least in light role as opposed to mounted/mechanised. Combat loads of over 36&#160;kg (80&#160;lbs) are standard, and greater loads in excess of 45&#160;kg (100&#160;lbs) are very common.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-medical_dept_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-medical_dept-31">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> These heavy loads, combined with long foot patrols of over 40&#160;km (25&#160;mi) a day, in any climate from 43 to −29&#160;°C (109 to −20&#160;°F) in temperature, require the infantryman to be in good physical and mental condition. Infantrymen live, fight and die outdoors in all types of brutal climates, often with no physical shelter. Poor climate conditions adds misery to this already demanding existence. Disease epidemics, frostbite, heat stroke, trench foot, insect and wild animal bites are common along with stress disorders and these have sometimes caused more casualties than enemy action.<sup id="cite_ref-medical_dept_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-medical_dept-31">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Infantrymen are expected to continue with their combat missions despite the death and injury of friends, fear, despair, fatigue, and bodily injury.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rangervietnam1969.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Rangervietnam1969.jpg/220px-Rangervietnam1969.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="172" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Rangervietnam1969.jpg/330px-Rangervietnam1969.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Rangervietnam1969.jpg/440px-Rangervietnam1969.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="1000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rangervietnam1969.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>U.S. Army Rangers, Vietnam, 1969</div></div></div>
<p>Some infantry units are considered <a href="/wiki/Special_Forces" class="mw-redirect" title="Special Forces">Special Forces</a>. The earliest Special Forces commando units were more highly trained infantrymen, with special weapons, equipment, and missions. Special Forces units recruit heavily from regular infantry units to fill their ranks.
</p><p>Foreign and domestic militaries typically have a slang term for their infantrymen. In the U.S. military, the slang term among both Marine and Army infantrymen for themselves is "grunt." In the British Army, they are the "squaddies." The infantry is a small close-knit community, and the slang names are terms of endearment that convey mutual respect and shared experiences.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Air_force_and_naval_infantry">Air force and naval infantry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Infantry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Air force and naval infantry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Naval infantry, commonly known as <a href="/wiki/Marines" title="Marines">marines</a>, are primarily a category of infantry that form part of the naval forces of states and perform roles on land and at sea, including <a href="/wiki/Amphibious_warfare" title="Amphibious warfare">amphibious operations</a>, as well as other, naval roles. They also perform other tasks, including land warfare, separate from naval operations.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Air_force_infantry" class="mw-redirect" title="Air force infantry">Air force infantry</a> and base defence forces, such as the <a href="/wiki/RAF_Regiment" title="RAF Regiment">Royal Air Force Regiment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Airfield_Defence_Guards" title="Airfield Defence Guards">Royal Australian Air Force Airfield Defence Guards</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paskhas" title="Paskhas">Indonesian Air Force Paskhas Corps</a>, are used primarily for ground-based defence of air bases and other air force facilities. They also have a number of other, specialist roles. These include, among others, <a href="/wiki/CBRN_defense" title="CBRN defense">Chemical, Biological, Radiological and Nuclear (CBRN) defence</a> and training other airmen in basic ground defence tactics.
</p>
