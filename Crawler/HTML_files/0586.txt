<p><b>St Peter's Church</b>, also known as <b>Huddersfield Parish Church</b>, is a <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish church</a> in <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>, West Yorkshire. There has been a church on the site since the 11th century, but the current building dates from 1836. It is situated on the Kirkgate near <a href="/wiki/A62_road" title="A62 road">Southgate</a> in the centre of the town. It is a Grade II* <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-list_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-list-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-exagi_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-exagi-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Peter%27s_Church,_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first church on the site was built in the eleventh century by Walter de Lacy, the second son of <a href="/wiki/De_Lacy" title="De Lacy">Ilbert de Lacy</a>. Reputedly, Walter decided to build the church after he survived being thrown from his horse and landing in a swamp. The church was rebuilt in the sixteenth century. From 1759 to 1771, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Venn_(Clapham_Sect)" title="Henry Venn (Clapham Sect)">Henry Venn</a> was vicar at the church.<sup id="cite_ref-exagi_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-exagi-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the early 19th century the town of Huddersfield was growing rapidly as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> and the parish church was too small to cope. In order to address this, <a href="/wiki/Holy_Trinity_Church,_Huddersfield" title="Holy Trinity Church, Huddersfield">Holy Trinity Church</a> was opened in 1819 as a <a href="/wiki/Chapel_of_ease" title="Chapel of ease">chapel of ease</a> within the parish of St Peter's, before becoming a parish church in its own right in 1857.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the 1830s the 16th-century parish church needed urgent repairs, and it was therefore decided to rebuild it again, so the <a href="/wiki/Local_church" class="mw-redirect" title="Local church">congregation</a> constructed the current church. The architect for the church was <a href="/wiki/James_Pigott_Pritchett" title="James Pigott Pritchett">James Pigott Pritchett</a> and construction was carried out from 1834 to 1836. To keep costs down during construction the bricks used were from the previous church on the site, this meant that repairs were needed overcome the deficient materials.<sup id="cite_ref-list_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-list-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hudd_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hudd-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1851, two <a href="/wiki/Vestry" title="Vestry">vestries</a> were added at the eastern and northern ends of the church. In 1852, windows designed by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Willement" title="Thomas Willement">Thomas Willement</a> were installed in the church. In 1866, it was realised that the stonework of the church needed repairing. In 1873, new pews and a pulpit were installed. In 1879, a choir vestry was added.<sup id="cite_ref-list_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-list-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In 1908 an organ was installed by local builder <a href="/wiki/Conacher_and_Co" title="Conacher and Co">Conacher and Co</a>. It was restored in 1984 by Philip Wood of Huddersfield.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1921 to 1923, the <a href="/wiki/Chancel" title="Chancel">sanctuary</a> was reordered. A <a href="/wiki/Baldachin" title="Baldachin">baldachin</a> and the east window, designed by <a href="/wiki/Ninian_Comper" title="Ninian Comper">Ninian Comper</a>, as part of a war memorial was also installed in the church. In the 1940s, the south <a href="/wiki/Transept" title="Transept">transept</a> was redesigned with a screen by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Thompson_(designer)" title="Robert Thompson (designer)">Robert Thompson</a> being added. In the 1980s, a new <a href="/wiki/Altar" title="Altar">altar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dais" title="Dais">dais</a> were also installed.<sup id="cite_ref-list_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-list-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Around 2012, the roof, ceiling and tower were repaired. Other essential repairs were also made, helped by a grant from <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 17 February 2017, the church was the location of the funeral of <a href="/wiki/Gorden_Kaye" title="Gorden Kaye">Gorden Kaye</a>, the Huddersfield-born television actor and star of <i><a href="/wiki/%27Allo_%27Allo!" title="&#39;Allo &#39;Allo!">'Allo 'Allo!</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parish">Parish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_Peter%27s_Church,_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Parish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On weekdays, the church is open from 9:00am until 4:00pm and on Saturdays from 10:00am to 2:00pm. On Sundays, there is a <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a> communion service at 8:00am, a parish communion service at 10:00am and <a href="/wiki/Evensong" title="Evensong">evensong</a> at 3:00pm.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
