<p><b>Hanson Limited</b>, formerly <b>Hanson Trust plc</b>, is a British based international building materials company, headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Maidenhead" title="Maidenhead">Maidenhead</a>. The company has been a subsidiary of the German company <a href="/wiki/HeidelbergCement" title="HeidelbergCement">HeidelbergCement</a> since August 2007, and was formerly listed on the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a> and a constituent of the <a href="/wiki/FTSE_100_Index" title="FTSE 100 Index">FTSE 100 Index</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hanson_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hanson was built up by James Hanson, later <a href="/wiki/James_Hanson,_Baron_Hanson" title="James Hanson, Baron Hanson">Lord Hanson</a>, and Gordon White, later <a href="/wiki/Gordon_White,_Baron_White_of_Hull" title="Gordon White, Baron White of Hull">Baron White of Hull</a>, who set up Hanson Trust in 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Their policy was to purchase under-performing assets and make them a source of profit. 
</p><p>Hanson and White were willing to take a wide range of measures to do so, including mass redundancies, and therefore attracted opposition and accusations that they were "<a href="/wiki/Asset_stripping" title="Asset stripping">asset strippers</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-obituary_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obituary-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> but from 1979 the company was successful from the shareholders' point of view and respected during the early 1980s, with Hanson (who gave millions of pounds to the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_party_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Conservative party (UK)">Conservatives</a>) admired by <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the most notable takeovers, at least to the general public, was the acquisition in 1983 of the <a href="/wiki/United_Drapery_Stores" title="United Drapery Stores">United Drapery Stores</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-obituary_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obituary-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or UDS Group, which owned many of Britain's most well known high street clothes shops and department stores, including <a href="/wiki/John_Collier_(retailer)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Collier (retailer)">John Collier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Shops" title="Richard Shops">Richard Shops</a> and the chain of <a href="/wiki/Allders" title="Allders">Allders</a> department stores. To fund this purchase, Hanson broke up UDS and sold John Collier to a management buy out team, and Richard Shops to <a href="/wiki/Habitat_(retailer)" title="Habitat (retailer)">Habitat</a>, keeping only the core department store business.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hanson_Ketton_Cement_Plant.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Hanson_Ketton_Cement_Plant.jpg/220px-Hanson_Ketton_Cement_Plant.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Hanson_Ketton_Cement_Plant.jpg/330px-Hanson_Ketton_Cement_Plant.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Hanson_Ketton_Cement_Plant.jpg/440px-Hanson_Ketton_Cement_Plant.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="667" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hanson_Ketton_Cement_Plant.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Hanson cement works in Ketton</div></div></div>
<p>In January 1986, Hanson bought <a href="/wiki/Smith_Corona" title="Smith Corona">SCM</a>, an American chemicals to <a href="/wiki/Typewriters" class="mw-redirect" title="Typewriters">typewriters</a> business. This included the paper division that was formerly the <a href="/wiki/Allied_Paper_Corporation" title="Allied Paper Corporation">Allied Paper Corporation</a>. Hanson sold most of the SCM business units and the headquarters building in New York City for a significant profit.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Its most significant single purchase, however, was probably its takeover of <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Tobacco" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Tobacco">Imperial Tobacco</a> Group in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-obituary_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obituary-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Hanson paid £2.5&#160;billion for the group then undertook a major reorganisation; divestitures netted £2.3&#160;billion, leaving Hanson with the hugely profitable tobacco business for "next to nothing."<sup id="cite_ref-obituary_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obituary-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Hanson sold off the food brand, <a href="/wiki/Golden_Wonder" title="Golden Wonder">Golden Wonder</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Dalgety_plc" title="Dalgety plc">Dalgety plc</a> in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 1988, Hanson went on to buy <a href="/wiki/Consolidated_Gold_Fields" title="Consolidated Gold Fields">Consolidated Gold Fields</a> for £3.5bn.<sup id="cite_ref-obituary_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obituary-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <i>Gold Survey</i> was taken on by a new company, now known as <a href="/wiki/GFMS" title="GFMS">GFMS</a>. An attempt in September 1991 to purchase <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Chemical_Industries" title="Imperial Chemical Industries">Imperial Chemical Industries</a>, once seen by many in Britain as the nation's leading company but then in decline, was highly controversial, and ended in failure.<sup id="cite_ref-obituary_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obituary-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Hanson did secure the takeover of <a href="/wiki/Beazer" title="Beazer">Beazer</a>, a major housebuilder, that year.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the mid 1990s, <a href="/wiki/Conglomerate_(company)" title="Conglomerate (company)">conglomerates</a> were no longer popular with the investment community. Some of the manufacturing businesses were spun off as US Industries in February 1995.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In January 1996, Hanson ended its time as a diversified conglomerate by breaking itself up into four separate listed companies: Hanson plc, <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Tobacco" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Tobacco">Imperial Tobacco</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Energy_Group" title="The Energy Group">The Energy Group</a> and <a href="/wiki/Millennium_Chemicals" title="Millennium Chemicals">Millennium Chemicals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> This deal cost Lord Hanson £95&#160;million by August 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lord Hanson stepped down as chairman in December 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-obituary_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obituary-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Led by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Dougal" title="Andrew Dougal">Andrew Dougal</a>, chief executive from 1997 until 2002,<sup id="cite_ref-Carillion_profile_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carillion_profile-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Hanson focused on building materials, becoming the world's biggest aggregates supplier and the second largest supplier of ready-mixed concrete.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian99_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian99-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In November 1999, Hanson acquired Australian building materials business <a href="/wiki/Hanson_Australia" title="Hanson Australia">Pioneer International</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Dougal quit the group in early 2002 to "rebalance" his life, leaving with a controversially large pay-off (variously reported at between £400,000 and £660,000, plus a pension top-up of £636,700).<sup id="cite_ref-Osborne-20Apr2002_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Osborne-20Apr2002-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cope-12Mar2003_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cope-12Mar2003-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Thisismoney_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thisismoney-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2007, <a href="/wiki/HeidelbergCement" title="HeidelbergCement">HeidelbergCement</a> announced its intent to purchase Hanson PLC for £11 per share, a deal worth approximately £8&#160;billion. 
</p><p>This deal made the combined company the second largest cement and building materials company in the world. The transaction was completed through Heidelberg subsidiary Lehigh UK on 22 August 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In December 2014, Heidelberg Cement agreed to sell its Hanson Building Products division to the private equity firm <a href="/wiki/Lone_Star_Funds" title="Lone Star Funds">Lone Star</a> for £900&#160;million.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hanson_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hanson's two largest markets are the United Kingdom and the United States, and it is also active in many other countries. It is the world's leading producer of aggregates (particles of rock, gravel and sand), and a major producer of bricks and concrete pipes.
</p>
