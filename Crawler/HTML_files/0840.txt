<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:YorkshireCCCFirstClassKit.svg" class="image"><img alt="YorkshireCCCFirstClassKit.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/93/YorkshireCCCFirstClassKit.svg/260px-YorkshireCCCFirstClassKit.svg.png" decoding="async" width="260" height="260" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/93/YorkshireCCCFirstClassKit.svg/390px-YorkshireCCCFirstClassKit.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/93/YorkshireCCCFirstClassKit.svg/520px-YorkshireCCCFirstClassKit.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="296" data-file-height="296" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:YorkshireCCCFirstClassKit.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>
<p><b>Yorkshire County Cricket Club</b> is one of eighteen <a href="/wiki/First-class_cricket" title="First-class cricket">first-class</a> <a href="/wiki/County_cricket" title="County cricket">county clubs</a> within the domestic <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a> structure of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>. It represents the <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">historic county</a> of <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>. The club's limited overs team is called the <b>Yorkshire Vikings</b>. Yorkshire teams formed by earlier organisations, essentially the old <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_Cricket_Club" title="Sheffield Cricket Club">Sheffield Cricket Club</a>, played top-class cricket from the 18th century and the county club has always held first-class status. Yorkshire have competed in the County Championship since the official start of the competition in 1890 and have played in every top-level domestic cricket competition in England.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Yorkshire are the most successful team in English cricketing history with 33 <a href="/wiki/County_Championship" title="County Championship">County Championship</a> titles, including one shared. The team's most recent Championship title was in 2015, following on from that achieved in 2014. The club's limited-overs kit colours are Cambridge blue, Oxford blue, and yellow with <a href="/wiki/Mazars" title="Mazars">Mazars</a> as the main sponsor. Yorkshire play most of their home games at the <a href="/wiki/Headingley_Cricket_Ground" title="Headingley Cricket Ground">Headingley Cricket Ground</a> in <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>. Another significant venue is at <a href="/wiki/North_Marine_Road_Ground,_Scarborough" title="North Marine Road Ground, Scarborough">North Marine Road Ground, Scarborough</a>, which houses the annual <a href="/wiki/Scarborough_Festival" title="Scarborough Festival">Scarborough Festival</a>. Yorkshire has historically used other locations including <a href="/wiki/Bramall_Lane" title="Bramall Lane">Bramall Lane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>, which was the club's original home; <a href="/wiki/Park_Avenue_(stadium)" title="Park Avenue (stadium)">Horton Park Avenue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a>; <a href="/wiki/St_George%27s_Road_Cricket_Ground,_Harrogate" title="St George&#39;s Road Cricket Ground, Harrogate">St George's Road Cricket Ground, Harrogate</a>; <a href="/wiki/The_Circle,_Kingston_upon_Hull" title="The Circle, Kingston upon Hull">The Circle, Kingston upon Hull</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Acklam_Park" title="Acklam Park">Acklam Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middlesbrough" title="Middlesbrough">Middlesbrough</a>. The team drew an average attendance of 8,417 to seven home games in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_County_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earliest_cricket_in_Yorkshire">Earliest cricket in Yorkshire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_County_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Earliest cricket in Yorkshire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The earliest certain reference to cricket in Yorkshire dates from 1751 when local matches were held in <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a> and a game took place on or soon after Monday, 5 August at Stanwick, near <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_North_Yorkshire" title="Richmond, North Yorkshire">Richmond</a>, between the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Cleveland" title="Duke of Cleveland">Duke of Cleveland</a>'s XI and <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Northumberland" title="Earl of Northumberland">Earl of Northumberland</a>'s XI; the same teams having earlier played a game at <a href="/wiki/Durham,_England" title="Durham, England">Durham</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-GB18_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GB18-6">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sheffield_Cricket_Club" title="Sheffield Cricket Club">Sheffield Cricket Club</a> was probably formed about this time and there are references to Sheffield matches in <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a> in 1757 and at <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> in 1761.<sup id="cite_ref-GB18_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GB18-6">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> A club was formed in <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> in 1784.<sup id="cite_ref-H8_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H8-7">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bedale" title="Bedale">Bedale</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_Yorkshire" title="North Yorkshire">North Yorkshire</a> was a noted centre in the early 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-K9_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K9-8">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> But cricket in most rural areas was slow to develop.<sup id="cite_ref-W7_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W7-9">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Yorkshire cricket became centred around <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>, where it was more organised than in the rest of the county.<sup id="cite_ref-W7_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W7-9">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1771, Sheffield played semi-regular matches against <a href="/wiki/Nottingham_Cricket_Club" title="Nottingham Cricket Club">Nottingham Cricket Club</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Nottingham was generally the better side and Sheffield sometimes played with more players to give them a greater chance of victory.<sup id="cite_ref-H10_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H10-11">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, the Sheffield player <a href="/wiki/Tom_Marsden" title="Tom Marsden">Tom Marsden</a> was regarded as one of the leading players in the country in the 1820s.<sup id="cite_ref-H10_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H10-11">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cricket increased in popularity after one of the 1827 <a href="/wiki/Roundarm_trial_matches" title="Roundarm trial matches">roundarm trial matches</a> was played at the purpose-built <a href="/wiki/Darnall_New_Ground" title="Darnall New Ground">Darnall New Ground</a> in Sheffield to evaluate the new style of <a href="/wiki/Roundarm_bowling" title="Roundarm bowling">roundarm bowling</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> After this match, many new cricket clubs were formed in the county.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1833, "Yorkshire" was first used as a team name, although it contained 11 Sheffield players, for a game against <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_county_cricket_teams" title="Norfolk county cricket teams">Norfolk</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park_(cricket_ground)" title="Hyde Park (cricket ground)">Hyde Park Ground</a> in Sheffield.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The name may have arisen from a need to match the status of Norfolk as a county rather than a city. There were some differences in the organisation of the Yorkshire team vis-à-vis those called Sheffield as it included three <a href="/wiki/Amateur_status_in_first-class_cricket" title="Amateur status in first-class cricket">amateurs</a> while Sheffield teams were entirely professional.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Yorkshire, as such, played intermittently over the next thirty years but was not organised in any formal way.<sup id="cite_ref-W7_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W7-9">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Some of their opponents were <a href="/wiki/Sussex_county_cricket_teams" title="Sussex county cricket teams">Sussex</a> in 1835; <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Cricket_Club" title="Manchester Cricket Club">Manchester</a> in 1844 and 1845; and <a href="/wiki/Kent_County_Cricket_Club" title="Kent County Cricket Club">Kent</a> in 1849. Also in 1849, Yorkshire played against a "Lancashire" team for the first time, though it was really a Sheffield v Manchester match.<sup id="cite_ref-H12_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H12-16">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> By 1855, Sheffield and Yorkshire were playing at <a href="/wiki/Bramall_Lane" title="Bramall Lane">Bramall Lane</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-H12_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H12-16">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_club">Origin of club</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_County_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Origin of club">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:RogerIddison.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A man in a striped shirt and a bowler hat standing with his arms folded looking off camera." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cf/RogerIddison.jpg/100px-RogerIddison.jpg" decoding="async" width="100" height="302" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cf/RogerIddison.jpg/150px-RogerIddison.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/cf/RogerIddison.jpg/200px-RogerIddison.jpg 2x" data-file-width="486" data-file-height="1467" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:RogerIddison.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Roger_Iddison" title="Roger Iddison">Roger Iddison</a>, the first captain of Yorkshire: he led the team until 1872.</div></div></div>
<p>On 7 March 1861, during a meeting at the <a href="/wiki/Adelphi_Hotel_(Sheffield)" title="Adelphi Hotel (Sheffield)">Adelphi Hotel</a> in Sheffield, a Match Fund Committee was established to run Yorkshire county matches. The committee was made up from the management committee of the Bramall Lane ground and representatives from clubs willing to pay <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">£</a>1 to the fund.<sup id="cite_ref-H14_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H14-17">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> But the committee was unable to persuade other clubs that it was not seeking to promote Sheffield cricket and a lack of funds prevented some matches being played in 1862.<sup id="cite_ref-H14_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H14-17">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By this time, there were several cricketers with good reputations and the county team was one of the strongest in England.<sup id="cite_ref-H14_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H14-17">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Consequently, on 8 January 1863, Yorkshire County Cricket Club was formed. Membership was unlimited and cost a minimum of 10<a href="/wiki/Shilling_(British_coin)" title="Shilling (British coin)">s</a> and 6<a href="/wiki/Penny_(British_pre-decimal_coin)" title="Penny (British pre-decimal coin)">d</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-H14_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H14-17">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Like most first-class cricket clubs of the time, Yorkshire relied on private patronage with administrators "paying to serve" and "moneyed enthusiasts" acting as ready match sponsors.<sup id="cite_ref-K10_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K10-18">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The majority of players were freelance professionals who were paid a usual match fee of £5, from which all travel and accommodation had to be paid.<sup id="cite_ref-K10_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K10-18">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Travel could be arduous, living away from home could be "rough" and sometimes the match fee was not enough to cover expenses, especially if, as was often a problem with early Yorkshire cricketers, "the ale-house was a temptation".<sup id="cite_ref-K14_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K14-19">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first club President was former player <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Barker_(cricketer,_born_1812)" title="Thomas Barker (cricketer, born 1812)">Thomas Barker</a>, who had become <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_Sheffield" class="mw-redirect" title="Mayor of Sheffield">Mayor of Sheffield</a>, although he probably never attended any meetings. <a href="/wiki/Michael_Ellison" title="Michael Ellison">Michael Ellison</a> was the first club Treasurer and at some point early in Yorkshire's history, he assumed the <a href="/wiki/President_(corporate_title)" title="President (corporate title)">Presidency</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-H14_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H14-17">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Most official accounts record Ellison as Yorkshire's first President.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Wostinholm" title="Joseph Wostinholm">Joseph ("J. B.") Wostinholm</a> became the first of four long-serving club Secretaries in 1864.<sup id="cite_ref-H14_17-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H14-17">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The first team <a href="/wiki/Captain_(cricket)" title="Captain (cricket)">captain</a> was <a href="/wiki/Roger_Iddison" title="Roger Iddison">Roger Iddison</a>, a professional cricketer.<sup id="cite_ref-K13_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K13-21">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The objective of the club was to play matches "either in Sheffield or in any other towns of the county according as arrangements may be made."<sup id="cite_ref-H14_17-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H14-17">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Other locations in <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> were unable or unwilling to host fixtures in the first years of the club, and Bradford and York continued to attempt to organise games in competition with Yorkshire, sometimes causing confusion among other counties.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Attempts to form an alternative Yorkshire team continued intermittently until 1884<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> although, by 1873, most clubs had accepted the authority of the Sheffield-based county club.<sup id="cite_ref-Hodgson,_p.21_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hodgson,_p.21-24">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1863.E2.80.931882"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1863–1882">1863–1882</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_County_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1863–1882">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GeorgeFreeman.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A cricketer holding a ball posing before a painted backdrop of a field" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/GeorgeFreeman.jpg/150px-GeorgeFreeman.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="261" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/GeorgeFreeman.jpg/225px-GeorgeFreeman.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/GeorgeFreeman.jpg/300px-GeorgeFreeman.jpg 2x" data-file-width="906" data-file-height="1577" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GeorgeFreeman.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/George_Freeman_(cricketer)" title="George Freeman (cricketer)">George Freeman</a>, a key factor in Yorkshire's success in the late 1860s, played for the team mainly between 1865 and 1870, taking 209 wickets in 32 matches.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Yorkshire played its initial <a href="/wiki/First-class_cricket" title="First-class cricket">first-class</a> match against <a href="/wiki/Surrey_County_Cricket_Club" title="Surrey County Cricket Club">Surrey</a> at <a href="/wiki/The_Oval" title="The Oval">The Oval</a> on 4, 5 and 6 June 1863. Surrey scored 315 runs, to which Yorkshire replied with 257. Surrey were bowled out for 60 before the match was drawn.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The team recorded their first win in the following match against the strong <a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire_County_Cricket_Club" title="Nottinghamshire County Cricket Club">Nottinghamshire</a> team and although they lost the return match, won two and lost only one of the four matches played in their first season.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In 1864, the team won two and lost four of seven matches.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1865, the club and players became involved in a dispute. Five players – Roger Iddison, <a href="/wiki/George_Anderson_(cricketer)" title="George Anderson (cricketer)">George Anderson</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Atkinson_(cricketer)" title="George Atkinson (cricketer)">George Atkinson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Rowbotham" title="Joseph Rowbotham">Joseph Rowbotham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ned_Stephenson" title="Ned Stephenson">Ned Stephenson</a> – refused to play against Surrey.<sup id="cite_ref-K10_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K10-18">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This concerned a controversy that arose in 1862 when Iddison and Anderson played for an All-England team against Surrey at <a href="/wiki/The_Oval" title="The Oval">The Oval</a> and the Kent bowler <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Willsher" title="Edgar Willsher">Edgar Willsher</a> was repeatedly no-balled for using an overarm action by an <a href="/wiki/Umpire" class="mw-redirect" title="Umpire">umpire</a> whom, they claimed, had been appointed by Surrey specifically for that purpose.<sup id="cite_ref-K10_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K10-18">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Anderson stated in 1865 that he "would not play against those who have combined to sweep us from the cricket field altogether if they could".<sup id="cite_ref-K10_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K10-18">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The dispute was about a point of principle and centred on the right of bowlers to use an overarm action, which had been legalised ahead of the 1864 season.<sup id="cite_ref-K12_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K12-30">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Consequently, with several important players missing, Yorkshire did not win a game in 1865 and were forced to cancel some matches for the 1866 season. The Yorkshire Committee and the players came to an understanding in early 1867 after the players apologised, but Anderson never represented the county again.<sup id="cite_ref-H18_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H18-31">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With the player dispute resolved, Yorkshire won all seven of their matches in 1867, defeating Surrey, <a href="/wiki/Lancashire_County_Cricket_Club" title="Lancashire County Cricket Club">Lancashire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire_County_Cricket_Club" title="Cambridgeshire County Cricket Club">Cambridgeshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> As a result, the sporting press proclaimed Yorkshire to be the <a href="/wiki/County_Championship#.22Champion_County.22" title="County Championship">"Champion County"</a> for the first time.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The following year, <a href="/wiki/John_Thewlis_senior" title="John Thewlis senior">John Thewlis</a> scored the first <a href="/wiki/Century_(cricket)" title="Century (cricket)">century</a> for Yorkshire in <a href="/wiki/First-class_cricket" title="First-class cricket">first-class cricket</a>, against <a href="/wiki/Surrey_County_Cricket_Club" title="Surrey County Cricket Club">Surrey</a> at <a href="/wiki/The_Oval" title="The Oval">The Oval</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-Hodgson,_p.21_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hodgson,_p.21-24">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> in 1869, Joseph Rowbotham became the first man to score two centuries in a season for Yorkshire.<sup id="cite_ref-H22_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H22-34">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Yorkshire won four games in both 1868 and 1869; one sports publication regarded Yorkshire as equal champions in the latter year.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The team won six out of seven in 1870 to be acclaimed as Champion County again.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Much of Yorkshire's success in these years came from the bowling combination of <a href="/wiki/George_Freeman_(cricketer)" title="George Freeman (cricketer)">George Freeman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tom_Emmett" title="Tom Emmett">Tom Emmett</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following Freeman's retirement from regular <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a> after 1870, Yorkshire declined, winning fewer games in 1871 and 1872 as <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire_County_Cricket_Club" title="Gloucestershire County Cricket Club">Gloucestershire</a> rose to a position of dominance in County Cricket, driven by the success of <a href="/wiki/W._G._Grace" title="W. G. Grace">W. G. Grace</a> and his brothers. Conscious of the need to strengthen the club, Yorkshire instituted a Colts team of young players,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> but replaced Iddison as captain at the end of the 1872 season. Rowbotham, another professional, assumed the position.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> For the 1873 season, county cricket moved a step closer towards an organised competition when the counties agreed qualification rules for players to be eligible for a team; for many years, this was considered to be start of the <a href="/wiki/County_Championship" title="County Championship">County Championship</a>. However, the counties did not organise a formal competition and the "Champion County" was still decided by the press; some publications disagreed.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Yorkshire_CCC_1875.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Yorkshire_CCC_1875.jpg/300px-Yorkshire_CCC_1875.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="217" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Yorkshire_CCC_1875.jpg/450px-Yorkshire_CCC_1875.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Yorkshire_CCC_1875.jpg/600px-Yorkshire_CCC_1875.jpg 2x" data-file-width="864" data-file-height="625" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Yorkshire_CCC_1875.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Yorkshire team in 1875 was captained by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Rowbotham" title="Joseph Rowbotham">Joseph Rowbotham</a>. <i>Back row</i>: G. Martin (umpire), <a href="/wiki/John_Thewlis_senior" title="John Thewlis senior">John Thewlis</a>. <i>Middle row</i>: <a href="/wiki/George_Pinder_(cricketer)" title="George Pinder (cricketer)">George Pinder</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Ulyett" title="George Ulyett">George Ulyett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Armitage" title="Tom Armitage">Tom Armitage</a>, Joseph Rowbotham, <a href="/wiki/Allen_Hill" title="Allen Hill">Allen Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Greenwood" title="Andrew Greenwood">Andrew Greenwood</a>. <i>Front row</i>: <a href="/wiki/Tom_Emmett" title="Tom Emmett">Tom Emmett</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Hicks_(cricketer)" title="John Hicks (cricketer)">John Hicks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ephraim_Lockwood" title="Ephraim Lockwood">Ephraim Lockwood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Ullathorne" title="Charles Ullathorne">Charlie Ullathorne</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Despite containing a side with effective players, Yorkshire did not perform as well as expected over the next seasons. The bowling attack was strong, the team contained some experienced players and critics believed <a href="/wiki/Ephraim_Lockwood" title="Ephraim Lockwood">Ephraim Lockwood</a> to be the best professional batsman in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> In 1873, Yorkshire won seven games and lost five and for the following season, <a href="/wiki/Luke_Greenwood" title="Luke Greenwood">Luke Greenwood</a> replaced Rowbotham as captain.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The team had an improved record, but although second only to Gloucestershire, Yorkshire lost heavily in both encounters between the teams.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;note 4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-W40_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W40-48">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Greenwood retired, Rowbotham resumed the captaincy for 1875,<sup id="cite_ref-W40_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-W40-48">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> and Lockwood took over in 1876.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> The team did not perform particularly well in either season,<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> but their worst performance came in 1877, winning two games – but none of the last ten – and losing seven.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> This placed them eighth out of nine counties.<sup id="cite_ref-H28_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H28-52">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tom_Emmett" title="Tom Emmett">Tom Emmett</a> then took over as Captain, but the following two seasons brought mixed results as the team displayed inconsistency, often losing to teams they should comfortably have beaten.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Derek Hodgson, in his official county history, suggests a lack of discipline in the team throughout these years was to blame; contemporary reports suggested the team drank too much alcohol to be effective.<sup id="cite_ref-H28_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H28-52">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The players also suffered from public attention, receiving generous hospitality at times which impacted on their performances.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> In these seasons, the team often began well only to lose form later in the season and the Yorkshire committee was reluctant to replace the experienced cricketers with younger players. However, off the field, Yorkshire became increasingly successful, and the profits made by the club paid for improvements to be made to the Bramall Lane Ground and increased player wages through the introduction of travelling expenses and talent money where good performances were financially rewarded.<sup id="cite_ref-H35_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H35-46">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> An influx of what proved to be effective new players saw Yorkshire finish second to Lancashire in the unofficial Championship for 1881, but more significant was the debut for the county of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Hawke,_7th_Baron_Hawke" title="Martin Hawke, 7th Baron Hawke">Lord Hawke</a>, then aged 21.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The team faded again in 1882 and Hawke, who had refused the captaincy earlier, was appointed team captain at the end of the season, the first amateur to hold this position.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Previous captains had all been professionals: Roger Iddison (1863–72), Joseph Rowbotham (1873 and 1875), Luke Greenwood (1874), Ephraim Lockwood (1876–77) and Tom Emmett (1878–82). Hawke remained in charge for 28 seasons until 1910, during which time the team won eight County Championships.<sup id="cite_ref-cahawke_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cahawke-57">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1883.E2.80.931918"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1883–1918">1883–1918</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_County_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 1883–1918">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Yorkshire_County_Cricket_Club_(1883%E2%80%931918)" title="History of Yorkshire County Cricket Club (1883–1918)">History of Yorkshire County Cricket Club (1883–1918)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lord_Hawke_-_Vanity_Fair-alt-edit-2-cropped.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A painting of a cricketer sitting in a chair wearing a striped blazer." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Lord_Hawke_-_Vanity_Fair-alt-edit-2-cropped.jpg/150px-Lord_Hawke_-_Vanity_Fair-alt-edit-2-cropped.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="258" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Lord_Hawke_-_Vanity_Fair-alt-edit-2-cropped.jpg/225px-Lord_Hawke_-_Vanity_Fair-alt-edit-2-cropped.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Lord_Hawke_-_Vanity_Fair-alt-edit-2-cropped.jpg/300px-Lord_Hawke_-_Vanity_Fair-alt-edit-2-cropped.jpg 2x" data-file-width="538" data-file-height="924" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lord_Hawke_-_Vanity_Fair-alt-edit-2-cropped.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Caricature of <a href="/wiki/Lord_Hawke" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Hawke">Lord Hawke</a> by <i><a href="/wiki/Leslie_Ward" title="Leslie Ward">Spy</a></i>, first published in <i><a href="/wiki/Vanity_Fair_(British_magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Vanity Fair (British magazine)">Vanity Fair</a></i> on 24 September 1892 with the caption "Yorkshire Cricket".</div></div></div>
<p>In an obituary tribute, the editor of <i><a href="/wiki/Wisden_Cricketers%27_Almanack" title="Wisden Cricketers&#39; Almanack">Wisden Cricketers' Almanack</a></i> said that Lord Hawke's "strength of character was tested" when, as a young man on leaving <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Cambridge University">Cambridge University</a>, he undertook the responsibility of captaining the Yorkshire side, composed at that time of "elements that were not entirely harmonious". Owing to Hawke's "tact, judgment and integrity", he moulded the Eleven into "the best and probably the most united county cricket team in England".<sup id="cite_ref-wistrib_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wistrib-58">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Yorkshire to 1883 was widely seen as an idiosyncratic team and, though Hawke's primary task as captain was to lead the team to fulfilment of its potential, his biggest challenge was to unite the club's geographical and social factions.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> At the end of the 1882 season, in addition to appointing Hawke as captain, the committee agreed to reorganise itself for the first time since the club's foundation and began a process which eventually made the club representative of the whole county and not just Sheffield.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hawke succeeded Emmett in 1883 and remained as official captain for 27 years, but at first he was careful to take his time and did not make too many changes. Yorkshire improved in 1883 and finished second behind Nottinghamshire in the unofficial County Championship.<sup id="cite_ref-H42_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H42-61">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> However, the remainder of the 1880s was disappointing for the team and its supporters. Kilburn said that Yorkshire "continued to be an unreliable side, mingling brilliant achievement with miserable performance".<sup id="cite_ref-K19_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K19-63">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> The basic problem was that the older players were past their peak while younger replacements did not make the progress expected.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> A poor season on the field in 1889 was reflected by reduced income and changes were made with several good new players being introduced.<sup id="cite_ref-H53_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H53-66">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> These included <a href="/wiki/Jack_Brown_(cricketer)" title="Jack Brown (cricketer)">Jack Brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Hunter_(English_cricketer)" title="David Hunter (English cricketer)">David Hunter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Jackson" title="Stanley Jackson">Stanley Jackson</a>, while <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Peel" title="Bobby Peel">Bobby Peel</a> was becoming increasingly effective as a bowling all-rounder.<sup id="cite_ref-H53_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H53-66">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-H51_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H51-67">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> They were followed by <a href="/wiki/John_Tunnicliffe" title="John Tunnicliffe">John Tunnicliffe</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Denton_(cricketer)" title="David Denton (cricketer)">David Denton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ted_Wainwright" title="Ted Wainwright">Ted Wainwright</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Hirst" title="George Hirst">George Hirst</a>. Hawke worked on fielding practice and the players became specialised and efficient as fielders.<sup id="cite_ref-H66_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H66-68">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1893, the club's initial reorganisation was completed and was finalised after Ellison died in 1898 and Hawke assumed the club presidency as well as captaincy.<sup id="cite_ref-S468_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S468-69">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> When Wostinholm died in 1902 after being club secretary for 38 years, the county offices were moved from Sheffield to the more central location of Leeds.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was in 1893 that the team finally came good and Yorkshire won their first official County Championship. Hodgson wrote that it was "perhaps ... the first confirmation of Hawke's striving for teamwork and discipline".<sup id="cite_ref-H59_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H59-71">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> Yorkshire achieved second and third places in 1894 and 1895.<sup id="cite_ref-ccposn_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ccposn-72">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> The team continued to develop as Brown and Tunnicliffe established an effective opening partnership backed up by Denton and Jackson while Peel, Wainwright and Hirst carried the bowling attack.<sup id="cite_ref-H66_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H66-68">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Yorkshire historian R.S. Holmes described Yorkshire as "prodigious" in 1896, when they won their second title with some outstanding batting performances including a championship record total of 887 against <a href="/wiki/Warwickshire_County_Cricket_Club" title="Warwickshire County Cricket Club">Warwickshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-H66_68-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H66-68">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> Hawke began the practice of paying the professionals over the winter, initially £2 per week; the scheme was later modified to include bonuses.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> Peel was sacked in 1897 after appearing drunk on the field and was replaced in 1898 by <a href="/wiki/Wilfred_Rhodes" title="Wilfred Rhodes">Wilfred Rhodes</a>, who took 141 wickets in his debut season.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> In the same season, Brown and Tunnicliffe established a record partnership for the first wicket when they scored 554 against <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire_County_Cricket_Club" title="Derbyshire County Cricket Club">Derbyshire</a> at Chesterfield. Yorkshire won their third Championship in 1898 and narrowly failed in 1899 when only a defeat late in the season by <a href="/wiki/Kent_County_Cricket_Club" title="Kent County Cricket Club">Kent</a> prevented the retention of the title.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lot0622.jpg" class="image"><img alt="A cricketer about to bowl" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/54/Lot0622.jpg/150px-Lot0622.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="206" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/54/Lot0622.jpg/225px-Lot0622.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/54/Lot0622.jpg/300px-Lot0622.jpg 2x" data-file-width="582" data-file-height="798" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lot0622.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Wilfred_Rhodes" title="Wilfred Rhodes">Wilfred Rhodes</a> made his debut in 1898 and went on to take a record 3,598 wickets for Yorkshire.</div></div></div>
<p>Between 1900 and 1902, Yorkshire lost only twice in the County Championship, both times to Somerset, and won the County Championship in all three seasons largely thanks to their two outstanding all-rounders Hirst and Rhodes.<sup id="cite_ref-H255_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H255-76">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> When Joseph Wolstinholm retired as club secretary after the 1902 season, he was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Toone" title="Frederick Toone">Frederick Toone</a> who held the post until his death in June 1930 and formed a successful liaison with Hawke. Toone and Hawke worked together to improve the terms and conditions of professional players' contracts. To 1914, they were paid £5 for a home match and £6 for an away match with a £1 win bonus. Players who had received their county cap were obliged to join the Cricketers' Friendly Society and were paid a winter wage of £2 a week.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Yorkshire remained a strong championship contender through the 1903 to 1914 seasons and won a further three titles in this period, also finishing as runners-up three times.<sup id="cite_ref-S468_69-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S468-69">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> They won their seventh title in 1905 after being third and second in 1903 and 1904 respectively. In 1906, George Hirst achieved a unique "double-double" by scoring 2,385 runs and taking 208 wickets.<sup id="cite_ref-S468_69-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S468-69">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> The 1906 championship was decided on the last day of the season. Yorkshire lost to Gloucestershire by a single run and were overtaken by Kent, who won their last match against Hampshire by an innings. Having finished third in 1907, Yorkshire went through the 1908 season unbeaten and bowled Northamptonshire out for 27 and 15, the aggregate score of 42 being the lowest in English first-class cricket.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> Yorkshire finished third in 1909 but then dropped to eighth and seventh in the next two seasons before a recovery in 1912 brought their last title before the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>. While Hirst, Rhodes and Denton continued to excel, Yorkshire gained much in the last four years before the war from two new all-rounders, <a href="/wiki/Major_Booth" title="Major Booth">Major Booth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alonzo_Drake" title="Alonzo Drake">Alonzo Drake</a>, both of whom were an outstanding success.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> Another newcomer was <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Dolphin" title="Arthur Dolphin">Arthur Dolphin</a>, who replaced the retired <a href="/wiki/David_Hunter_(English_cricketer)" title="David Hunter (English cricketer)">David Hunter</a> as first-choice wicketkeeper.<sup id="cite_ref-K56_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K56-80">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> In 1913 and the unfinished 1914 season, Yorkshire finished second and fourth. Lord Hawke played only a few matches in 1909 and formally resigned as captain in 1910.<sup id="cite_ref-K56_80-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-K56-80">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> He was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Everard_Radcliffe" title="Everard Radcliffe">Everard Radcliffe</a>, who held the post until the end of the 1911 season; and then by Sir <a href="/wiki/Archibald_White_(cricketer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Archibald White (cricketer)">Archibald White</a>, who led the team until the outbreak of the First World War in August 1914.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1919.E2.80.931945"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1919–1945">1919–1945</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_County_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 1919–1945">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Yorkshire won the first post-war Championship in 1919, a year which saw the debuts of <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Sutcliffe" title="Herbert Sutcliffe">Herbert Sutcliffe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emmott_Robinson" title="Emmott Robinson">Emmott Robinson</a>, and remained a dominant force in the County Championship until the Second World War. They won every year from 1922 to 1925 and seven more times in the 1930s. The team won 25 games in 1923, for instance, with Rhodes and <a href="/wiki/Roy_Kilner" title="Roy Kilner">Roy Kilner</a> doing the double in these matches alone, while four other batsmen scored 1,000 runs and three other bowlers, including <a href="/wiki/George_Macaulay" title="George Macaulay">George Macaulay</a>, took 100 wickets.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Yorkshire's quintessential rivalry is with <a href="/wiki/Lancashire_County_Cricket_Club" title="Lancashire County Cricket Club">Lancashire</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Roses_Match" title="Roses Match">Roses Match</a>. The importance of this match reached a peak during the inter-war period when, for many years, Yorkshire and Lancashire were the dominant teams in English cricket. The writings of <a href="/wiki/Neville_Cardus" title="Neville Cardus">Neville Cardus</a> at this time were instrumental in emphasising the sense of rivalry between the two teams.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Wilfred Rhodes retired in 1930, taking 73 wickets and scoring 478 runs in his final season at the age of 53. <a href="/wiki/J._M._Kilburn" class="mw-redirect" title="J. M. Kilburn">J. M. Kilburn</a> wrote in the <i><a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="Yorkshire Post">Yorkshire Post</a></i>: "He had bowled at <a href="/wiki/W.G._Grace" class="mw-redirect" title="W.G. Grace">W.G. Grace</a>, and he bowled at <a href="/wiki/Don_Bradman" title="Don Bradman">Don Bradman</a>. At 20, at 30, at 40 and at 50 he had shown himself master of his world, and his kingdom was never usurped." Rhodes was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Hedley_Verity" title="Hedley Verity">Hedley Verity</a>, another skilful slow left armer. In 1932 Verity performed the greatest bowling feat in first-class cricket - ten wickets for ten runs against Nottinghamshire at Headingley. <a href="/wiki/Percy_Holmes" title="Percy Holmes">Percy Holmes</a> and Sutcliffe had a record opening stand of 555 against Essex at Leyton in 1932. <a href="/wiki/Len_Hutton" title="Len Hutton">Len Hutton</a> began his career in the 1930s.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1946.E2.80.931970"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1946–1970">1946–1970</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_County_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 1946–1970">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Large crowds flocked to the cricket after the Second World War with 47,000 people attending the 3 days of the Roses Match at Bramall Lane in 1946. Yorkshire won the first post-war Championship. New players after the war included spinner <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Wardle" title="Johnny Wardle">Johnny Wardle</a>, all-rounder <a href="/wiki/Brian_Close" title="Brian Close">Brian Close</a> and fast bowler <a href="/wiki/Fred_Trueman" title="Fred Trueman">Fred Trueman</a>. Another newcomer <a href="/wiki/Bob_Appleyard" title="Bob Appleyard">Bob Appleyard</a> became the first bowler to take 200 wickets in his first full season in 1951. The 1950s were dominated by Surrey, who won seven successive championships. Yorkshire had internal problems which were resolved before the 1959 season in which Yorkshire recovered the title under <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Burnet" title="Ronnie Burnet">Ronnie Burnet</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The 1960s saw a new Yorkshire team emerge that dominated English cricket. Brian Close was made captain in 1963 and won the Championship in his first season. The team included Fred Trueman, all-rounder <a href="/wiki/Ray_Illingworth" title="Ray Illingworth">Ray Illingworth</a>, wicket-keeper <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Binks" title="Jimmy Binks">Jimmy Binks</a> and Test batsmen <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Boycott" title="Geoffrey Boycott">Geoffrey Boycott</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doug_Padgett" title="Doug Padgett">Doug Padgett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phil_Sharpe_(cricketer)" title="Phil Sharpe (cricketer)">Phil Sharpe</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Hampshire" title="John Hampshire">John Hampshire</a>. The team began to break up after winning a third successive title in 1968 and Close was controversially sacked in 1970.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1971.E2.80.932000"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1971–2000">1971–2000</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_County_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 1971–2000">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There followed a long-running current of unrest in the club. There was sadness too in 1973 when Bramall Lane, the club's first home, was closed to cricket after over 400 first-class matches and was converted into a specialist football stadium.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Geoff Boycott captained Yorkshire for most of the 1970s, but success eluded the team even when Boycott left Test cricket for three years to concentrate on the county game. He was sacked as captain amid much furore after the 1978 season. Ray Illingworth returned from Leicestershire as team manager and, in 1982 at the age of 50, took over the captaincy. Yorkshire finished bottom of the 17-strong County Championship for the first time in 1983 but won the John Player (later National) League for the first time. There was further controversy when Boycott was not offered a new contract. The outcome of this was that the general committee resigned and Boycott, having already been elected to the new committee, was reinstalled as a player. Meanwhile, Brian Close became chairman of the cricket committee. Success continued to elude Yorkshire although <a href="/wiki/Phil_Carrick" title="Phil Carrick">Phil Carrick</a> led the team to a Benson and Hedges Cup triumph in 1987.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Yorkshire put themselves at a disadvantage from 1968 until 1992 by insisting that all its players must have been born within the <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">historic county boundaries</a> of <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> while all the other county teams strengthened themselves by signing overseas Test players. In 1992, the birth qualification rule was first modified to include those educated within the county, a dispensation that allowed <a href="/wiki/Michael_Vaughan" title="Michael Vaughan">Michael Vaughan</a> to play; and was then abandoned altogether. Yorkshire's first overseas player that season was 19-year-old <a href="/wiki/Sachin_Tendulkar" title="Sachin Tendulkar">Sachin Tendulkar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2001_to_present">2001 to present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_County_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 2001 to present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Yorkshire finally won the County Championship again in 2001 when the captain was <a href="/wiki/David_Byas" title="David Byas">David Byas</a>. Yorkshire have had mixed success in the first decade of the 21st century but finished a close third in the 2010 championship under <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Gale" title="Andrew Gale">Andrew Gale</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>There had for many years been a controversial issue about the apparent inability of players from Yorkshire's large ethnic minority population to make their way at the club. Tendulkar was the first Asian player to represent Yorkshire, but he was an overseas player. It was not until 2003 that Dewsbury's <a href="/wiki/Ismail_Dawood" title="Ismail Dawood">Ismail Dawood</a> became the first British-born Asian to play for Yorkshire. He was followed by <a href="/wiki/Ajmal_Shahzad" title="Ajmal Shahzad">Ajmal Shahzad</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adil_Rashid" title="Adil Rashid">Adil Rashid</a> who have both represented England. In 2007, <a href="/wiki/Azeem_Rafiq" title="Azeem Rafiq">Azeem Rafiq</a> as a member of Yorkshire's academy team became the first player of an Asian background to captain England at any level when he was appointed U15s captain.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> In the summer of 2012, Rafiq stepped in for the injured Andrew Gale to captain the team in 6 T20 matches, becoming the first player of Asia origin to lead the county, as well as the youngest captain in the club's history.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The club were relegated at the end of a disappointing 2011 season, leading to major restructuring in the coaching staff, with former Yorkshire and Australia player <a href="/wiki/Jason_Gillespie" title="Jason Gillespie">Jason Gillespie</a> brought in as coach.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After regaining promotion swiftly, Gillespie set about revamping the side, enticing Ryan Sidebottom to return to his home county, as well as signing Jack Brooks and Liam Plunkett, allowing Yorkshire to build a fearsome pace attack. With the excellent batting of Gary Ballance, Alex Lees and Adam Lyth, coupled with the signing of New Zealand's <a href="/wiki/Kane_Williamson" title="Kane Williamson">Kane Williamson</a> as overseas player, Yorkshire took Durham to the last game of the season in chasing the title in 2013, eventually finishing runners up.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014, in a bid to regain a domestic trophy, and as a statement of intent, Yorkshire announced the signing of Australian opening batsman <a href="/wiki/Aaron_Finch" title="Aaron Finch">Aaron Finch</a> to join the side for the domestic <a href="/wiki/NatWest_t20_Blast" class="mw-redirect" title="NatWest t20 Blast">NatWest t20 Blast</a> competition, and fill in for Kane Williamson during the county matches where he was away with New Zealand.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> Finch, who hit a world record 156 off 63 balls in an international T20 for Australia, is widely regarded as one of the best and most explosive T20 batsmen around. Although Yorkshire were unable to progress past the Group stages of the T20 Blast, they sealed their 32nd County Championship title with a game to spare with victory over Nottinghamshire at Trent Bridge on 12 September 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> Yorkshire followed up a successful 2014 with an even better 2015 winning their 33rd County Championship title with record wins and points total.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In July 2018, they were one of the six teams invited to play in the first edition of the <a href="/wiki/Abu_Dhabi_T20_Trophy" title="Abu Dhabi T20 Trophy">Abu Dhabi T20 Trophy</a>, which Yorkshire finished second in their group.<sup id="cite_ref-ADT20_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADT20-90">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Badge_and_colours">Badge and colours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_County_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Badge and colours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lord Hawke, in the early days of his captaincy, designed the white rose badge. Copying the idea from Lancashire, who already had adopted the red rose as a symbol, Hawke designed a rose which, unlike Lancashire's, was not a real flower. He created a rose with eleven petals, to represent the eleven players of the team, based on the hedge rose. Only players who had received their <a href="/wiki/County_cap" class="mw-redirect" title="County cap">county cap</a> were allowed to wear the badge. It was not until the 1980s it was allowed by the committee to be placed on merchandise as a marketing device.<sup id="cite_ref-H77_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H77-91">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> Yorkshire's club colours are dark blue, light blue and gold; these are knitted in bands forming the v-neck of each player's sweater.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ground_history">Ground history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_County_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Ground history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HeadingleyEast.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/HeadingleyEast.JPG/250px-HeadingleyEast.JPG" decoding="async" width="250" height="93" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/HeadingleyEast.JPG/375px-HeadingleyEast.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/HeadingleyEast.JPG/500px-HeadingleyEast.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2199" data-file-height="820" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HeadingleyEast.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Headingley_Carnegie_Cricket_Ground" class="mw-redirect" title="Headingley Carnegie Cricket Ground">Headingley</a> – East Stand</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Yorkshire_County_Cricket_Club_grounds" title="List of Yorkshire County Cricket Club grounds">List of Yorkshire County Cricket Club grounds</a></div>
<p>As with all county cricket clubs, Yorkshire represents the historic county and not any modern or current administrative unit. In Yorkshire's case, this means the three <a href="/wiki/Riding_(division)" class="mw-redirect" title="Riding (division)">ridings</a> and the City of <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>, although the club played some home matches outside the historic borders at Sheffield's <a href="/wiki/Abbeydale_Park" title="Abbeydale Park">Abbeydale Park</a>, which was <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">historically</a> part of <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a>, from 1974 to 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The club was founded on 8 January 1863 in the Adelphi Hotel, Sheffield and was initially based at Bramall Lane. Yorkshire first played at North Marine Road, Scarborough in <a href="/wiki/1878_English_cricket_season" title="1878 English cricket season">the 1878 season</a>. This remains the venue for the annual <a href="/wiki/Scarborough_Festival" title="Scarborough Festival">Scarborough Festival</a> matches. Headingley was first established in 1888 and Test cricket was first played there in 1899, eight years later than it hosted its inaugural <a href="/wiki/First-class_cricket" title="First-class cricket">first-class</a> match when Yorkshire played <a href="/wiki/Kent_County_Cricket_Club" title="Kent County Cricket Club">Kent</a> in 1891, the year in which the club's headquarters moved there. The "out grounds" in Hull, Sheffield, Bradford, Middlesbrough and Harrogate were used with great success until the 1970s.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HeadingleyWest.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/HeadingleyWest.JPG/250px-HeadingleyWest.JPG" decoding="async" width="250" height="66" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/HeadingleyWest.JPG/375px-HeadingleyWest.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/HeadingleyWest.JPG/500px-HeadingleyWest.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2272" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HeadingleyWest.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Headingley – West Stand</div></div></div>
<p>On 31 December 2005, Yorkshire purchased the Headingley cricket ground for £12&#160;million from the <i>Leeds Cricket, Football and Athletic Company</i>, parent company of the <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Rhinos" title="Leeds Rhinos">Leeds rugby league club</a>, with the help of a £9&#160;million loan from <a href="/wiki/Leeds_City_Council" title="Leeds City Council">Leeds City Council</a>. This purchase ensures that Test cricket continues at the venue with a 15-year staging agreement. On 11 January 2006, the stadium was officially renamed the Headingley Carnegie Stadium as a result of sponsorship from <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Metropolitan_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Leeds Metropolitan University">Leeds Metropolitan University</a> and the club announced plans on 11 January 2006 to rebuild the stand next to the rugby ground with 3,000 extra seats, taking capacity to 20,000.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> The club also announced plans to redevelop the Winter shed (North) stand on 25 August 2006 providing a £12.5&#160;million pavilion complex.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The following table gives the number of <a href="/wiki/First-class_cricket" title="First-class cricket">first-class (FC)</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_A_cricket" title="List A cricket">List A (LA)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Twenty20" title="Twenty20">Twenty20 (T20)</a> matches played by Yorkshire at each of their home venues:<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Club_captains">Club captains</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_County_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Club captains">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl><dd><i>For the full list of club captains, see: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Yorkshire_County_Cricket_Club_captains" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Yorkshire County Cricket Club captains">List of Yorkshire County Cricket Club captains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Category:Yorkshire_cricket_captains" title="Category:Yorkshire cricket captains">Category:Yorkshire cricket captains</a></i></dd></dl>
<p>Four Yorkshire players – Stanley Jackson, Len Hutton, Ray Illingworth and Michael Vaughan – have captained <a href="/wiki/English_cricket_team" class="mw-redirect" title="English cricket team">England</a> to success in <a href="/wiki/The_Ashes" title="The Ashes">The Ashes</a> yet none of them was club captain at the time (Ray Illingworth had just left Yorkshire for <a href="/wiki/Leicestershire_County_Cricket_Club" title="Leicestershire County Cricket Club">Leicestershire</a> when he became captain of England; he later returned to Yorkshire).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>From 1883 to 1959 inclusive, Yorkshire always had an amateur club captain. The extent of leadership given by these gentlemen has long been a subject of discussion. Hawke and Sellers are generally held to have been autocratic and decisive, but in fact both relied heavily on sound professional advice. At the other extreme, Wilfred Rhodes is supposed to have been the <i>de facto</i> captain from 1920 to 1930, but it was Major <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Lupton" title="Arthur Lupton">Arthur Lupton</a> who restored discipline to the side when a row erupted between Yorkshire and Middlesex in 1924.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lord Hawke famously said at the Yorkshire Annual General Meeting in 1925: "Pray God, no professional shall ever captain England. I love and admire them all, but we have always had an amateur skipper and when the day comes when we shall have no more amateurs captaining England it will be a thousand pities."<sup id="cite_ref-Gibson_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibson-98">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> In view of this, it is perhaps surprising that, when Arthur Lupton retired at the end of the 1927 season, Hawke was one of the sponsors of the suggestion that <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_captaincy_affair_of_1927" title="Yorkshire captaincy affair of 1927">Herbert Sutcliffe should become Yorkshire captain</a>. In the event, there was sufficient opposition to the idea amongst the Yorkshire committee and players (some of the latter felt that Wilfred Rhodes, as senior professional, had a prior claim), that the proposal was dropped.<sup id="cite_ref-Gibson_98-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gibson-98">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1960, <a href="/wiki/Vic_Wilson_(cricketer)" title="Vic Wilson (cricketer)">Vic Wilson</a> became Yorkshire's first professional captain since Tom Emmett when he succeeded Ronnie Burnet. Brian Close, who took over in 1963, has been Yorkshire's most successful professional captain with four County Championships.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Brand.2C_ownership.2C_finances_and_sponsorship"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Brand,_ownership,_finances_and_sponsorship">Brand, ownership, finances and sponsorship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_County_Cricket_Club&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Brand, ownership, finances and sponsorship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The club was founded in 1863 to be owned by its members who have elected various officials including the club's general committee, which existed until 2002 when it was replaced by a board of management headed by a chief executive. The office of club President still exists (see list below) and (in 2017) there are seven board members including the chief executive and the director of cricket. In addition to the board members, there are a director of finance and a human resources manager who is also personal assistant (PA) to both the board and chief executive. Among other roles are coaching, groundstaff, physiotherapy, operations, marketing, retail and community development. The once-influential post of club Secretary effectively ceased in 2002 although the club did have a company secretary for three years. That function has now disappeared under the broader structure and the present secretary as such is the finance director.<sup id="cite_ref-staff_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-staff-100">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup> Like all county clubs, Yorkshire relies heavily on sponsorship and numerous companies have formed deals with the club over many years. Current partners and sponsors can be found on the club website.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
