Basque: <i lang="eu">Nafarroako Foru Komunitatea</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Basque" title="Help:IPA/Basque">[nafaro.ako foɾu komunitate.a]</a></span>), is an <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_communities_of_Spain" title="Autonomous communities of Spain">autonomous community</a> and <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Spain" title="Provinces of Spain">province</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, bordering the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">Basque Autonomous Community</a>, <a href="/wiki/La_Rioja_(Spain)" title="La Rioja (Spain)">La Rioja</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">Aragon</a> in Spain and <a href="/wiki/Nouvelle-Aquitaine" title="Nouvelle-Aquitaine">Nouvelle-Aquitaine</a> in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. The capital city is <a href="/wiki/Pamplona" title="Pamplona">Pamplona</a> (or <i>Iruñea</i> in <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">Basque</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first documented use of a name resembling <i>Navarra</i>, <i>Nafarroa</i>, or <i>Naparroa</i> is a reference to <i>navarros</i>, in <a href="/wiki/Eginhard" class="mw-redirect" title="Eginhard">Eginhard</a>'s early-9th-century chronicle of the feats of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Auñamendi_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Auñamendi-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Other <a href="/wiki/Royal_Frankish_Annals" title="Royal Frankish Annals">Royal Frankish Annals</a> feature <i>nabarros</i>. There are two proposed etymologies for the name.<sup id="cite_ref-Auñamendi_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Auñamendi-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Basque <i>nabar</i> (declined <a href="/wiki/Absolutive_case" title="Absolutive case">absolute</a> <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">singular</a> <i>nabarra</i>): "brownish", "multicolour" (i. e. in contrast to the green mountainous lands north of the original County of Navarre).</li>
<li>Basque <i>naba</i> (or Spanish <i>nava</i>): "valley", "plain" + Basque <i>herri</i> ("people", "land").</li></ul>
<p>The linguist <a href="/wiki/Joan_Coromines" title="Joan Coromines">Joan Coromines</a> considers <i>naba</i> to be linguistically part of a wider <a href="/wiki/Vasconic" class="mw-redirect" title="Vasconic">Vasconic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Aquitanian_language" title="Aquitanian language">Aquitanian language</a> substrate, rather than Basque <i>per se</i>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiquity">Antiquity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Antiquity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Vascones" title="Vascones">Vascones</a>, a pre-Roman tribe, populated the southern slopes of the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a>, including the area which would ultimately become Navarre. In the mountainous north, the Vascones escaped large-scale Roman settlement, except for some coastal areas—for example <a href="/wiki/Oiasso" title="Oiasso">Oiasso</a> (in what is now <a href="/wiki/Gipuzkoa" title="Gipuzkoa">Gipuzkoa</a>)—and the flatter areas to the south, <a href="/wiki/Calahorra" title="Calahorra">Calagurris</a> (in what is now La Rioja), which were amenable to <a href="/wiki/Latifundium" title="Latifundium">large-scale Roman farming</a>—vineyards, olives, and wheat crops. There is no evidence of battles fought or general hostility between Romans and Basques, as they had the same enemies.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kingdom_of_Navarre">Kingdom of Navarre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Kingdom of Navarre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Navarre" title="Kingdom of Navarre">Kingdom of Navarre</a></div>
<p>Neither the <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">Visigoths</a> nor the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a> ever completely subjugated the area. The Vascones (to become the Basques) assimilated neighbouring tribes as of the 7th century AD. In the year&#160;778, the Basques defeated a Frankish army at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Roncevaux_Pass" title="Battle of Roncevaux Pass">Battle of Roncevaux Pass</a>.
</p><p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Roncevaux_Pass_(824)" title="Battle of Roncevaux Pass (824)">Battle of Roncevaux Pass (824)</a>, the Basque chieftain <a href="/wiki/%C3%8D%C3%B1igo_Arista_of_Pamplona" title="Íñigo Arista of Pamplona">Iñigo Arista</a> was elected <a href="/wiki/King_of_Pamplona" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Pamplona">King of Pamplona</a> supported by the <a href="/wiki/Muwallad" class="mw-redirect" title="Muwallad">muwallad</a> <a href="/wiki/Banu_Qasi" title="Banu Qasi">Banu Qasi</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tudela,_Navarre" title="Tudela, Navarre">Tudela</a>, establishing a Basque kingdom that was later called Navarre.<sup id="cite_ref-Collins1990_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Collins1990-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> That kingdom reached its zenith during the reign of <a href="/wiki/Sancho_III_of_Navarre" class="mw-redirect" title="Sancho III of Navarre">Sancho III</a>, comprising most of the Christian realms to the south of the Pyrenees, and even a short overlordship of <a href="/wiki/Gascony" title="Gascony">Gascony</a> (in the early 11th century).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollins1990181_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollins1990181-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When Sancho&#160;III died in 1035, the kingdom was divided between his sons.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It never fully recovered its political power, while its commercial importance increased as traders and pilgrims (<a href="/wiki/Occitan_language#Occitan_in_Spain" title="Occitan language">the <i>Francs</i></a>) poured into the kingdom via the <a href="/wiki/Way_of_Saint_James" class="mw-redirect" title="Way of Saint James">Way of Saint James</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollins1990214–215_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollins1990214–215-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In 1200, Navarre lost the key <a href="/wiki/Basque_Autonomous_Community" class="mw-redirect" title="Basque Autonomous Community">western Basque districts</a> to <a href="/wiki/Alphonse_VIII_of_Castile" class="mw-redirect" title="Alphonse VIII of Castile">Alphonse VIII of Castile</a>, leaving the kingdom landlocked.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollins1990185_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollins1990185-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Navarre then contributed with a small but symbolic force of 200 knights to the decisive <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Las_Navas_de_Tolosa" title="Battle of Las Navas de Tolosa">Battle of Las Navas de Tolosa</a> in 1212 against the <a href="/wiki/Almohad_Caliphate" title="Almohad Caliphate">Almohads</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Jim%C3%A9nez_dynasty" title="Jiménez dynasty">native line of kings</a> came to an end in 1234; their heirs intermarried with French dynasties.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollins1990232_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollins1990232-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> However, the Navarrese kept most of their strong <a href="/wiki/Fueros_of_Navarre" title="Fueros of Navarre">laws and institutions</a>. The death of <a href="/wiki/Blanche_I_of_Navarre" title="Blanche I of Navarre">Queen Blanche I</a> (1441) inaugurated a civil war period between the Beaumont and Agramont confederacies with the intervention of the Castilian-Aragonese <a href="/wiki/House_of_Trast%C3%A1mara" title="House of Trastámara">House of Trastámara</a> in Navarre's internal affairs.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMonreal/Jimeno201210–15_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMonreal/Jimeno201210–15-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Spanish_conquest_of_Iberian_Navarre" title="Spanish conquest of Iberian Navarre">In 1512, Navarre was invaded</a> by <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_the_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferdinand the Catholic">Ferdinand the Catholic</a>'s troops,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Catherine_of_Navarre" title="Catherine of Navarre">Queen Catherine</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_III_of_Navarre" title="John III of Navarre">King John III</a> withdrawing <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Navarre#Independent_Navarre_north_of_the_Pyrenees" title="Kingdom of Navarre">to the north of the Pyrenees</a>, and establishing a Kingdom of Navarre-<a href="/wiki/Viscounty_of_B%C3%A9arn" title="Viscounty of Béarn">Béarn</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Jeanne_d%27Albret" title="Jeanne d&#39;Albret">Queen Joan III</a> as of 1555.
</p><p>To the south of the Pyrenees, Navarre was annexed to the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Crown of Castile</a> (1515), but kept a separate ambiguous status, and a shaky balance up to 1610—<a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_France" title="Henry IV of France">King Henry III</a> ready to march over Spanish Navarre. A Chartered Government was established (the <i>Diputación</i>), and the kingdom managed to keep home rule. Tensions with the Spanish government came to a head as of 1794, when Spanish premier <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Godoy" title="Manuel Godoy">Manuel Godoy</a> attempted to suppress Navarrese and Basque self-government altogether, with the end of the <a href="/wiki/First_Carlist_War" title="First Carlist War">First Carlist War</a> (1839–1841) definitely bringing the kingdom and its home rule (<i>fueros</i>) to an end.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollins1990275_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollins1990275-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Loss_of_home_rule">Loss of home rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Loss of home rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>After the 1839 <a href="/wiki/Convention_of_Vergara" title="Convention of Vergara">Convention of Bergara</a>, <a class="external text" href="https://es.wikisource.org/wiki/Ley_de_Confirmaci%C3%B3n_de_Fueros">a reduced version of home rule (<i>fueros</i>)</a> was passed in 1839. However, the 1841 <a class="external text" href="https://es.wikisource.org/wiki/Ley_de_Modificaci%C3%B3n_de_fueros_de_Navarra_de_16_de_agosto_de_1841">Act for the Modification of Fueros</a> (later called the "Compromise Act", <i>Ley Paccionada</i>) definitely made the kingdom into a province after a compromise was reached by the Spanish government with officials of the Provincial Council of Navarre. The relocation of customs from the Ebro river to the Pyrenees in 1841 prompted the collapse of Navarre’s customary cross-Pyrenean trade and the rise of smuggling.
</p><p>Amid instability in Spain, Carlists took over in <a href="/wiki/Southern_Basque_Country" title="Southern Basque Country">Navarre and the rest of the Basque provinces</a>. An actual Basque state was established during the <a href="/wiki/Third_Carlist_War" title="Third Carlist War">Third Carlist War</a> with <a href="/wiki/Estella-Lizarra" title="Estella-Lizarra">Estella</a> as its capital (1872–1876), but King <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_XII_of_Spain" title="Alfonso XII of Spain">Alfonso XII</a>'s restoration in the throne of Spain and a counter-attack prompted the Carlist defeat. The end of the <a href="/wiki/Third_Carlist_War" title="Third Carlist War">Third Carlist War</a> saw <a href="/wiki/End_of_Basque_home_rule_in_Spain" title="End of Basque home rule in Spain">a renewed wave of Spanish centralisation</a> directly affecting Navarre.
</p><p>In 1893–1894 the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gamazada&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gamazada (page does not exist)">Gamazada</a></i> popular uprising took place centred in Pamplona against Madrid's governmental decisions breaching the 1841 chartered provisions. Except for a small faction (the so-called <i>Alfonsinos</i>), all parties in Navarre agreed on the need for a new political framework based on home rule within the <i><a href="/wiki/Laurak_Bat" class="mw-redirect" title="Laurak Bat">Laurak Bat</a></i>, the Basque districts in Spain. Among these, the <a href="/wiki/Navarrese_electoral_Carlism_(Restoration)" class="mw-redirect" title="Navarrese electoral Carlism (Restoration)">Carlists stood out</a>, who politically dominated the province, and resented an increased string of rulings and laws passed by Madrid, as well as left leaning influences. Unlike Biscay or Gipuzkoa, Navarre did not develop manufacturing during this period, remaining a basically rural economy.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Republic_and_military_uprising">Republic and military uprising</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Republic and military uprising">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1932, a <a href="/wiki/Southern_Basque_Country" title="Southern Basque Country">Basque Country</a>'s <a class="external text" href="https://es.wikisource.org/wiki/Estatuto_General_del_Estado_Vasco_aprobado_en_la_Magna_Asamblea_de_Municipios_Vascos_celebrada_en_Estella_(Lizarra)_el_d%C3%ADa_14_de_Junio_de_1931">separate statute</a> failed to take off over disagreements on the centrality of Catholicism, a scene of political radicalisation ensued dividing the leftist and rightist forces during the <a href="/wiki/2nd_Spanish_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="2nd Spanish Republic">2nd Spanish Republic</a> (1931–1939). Thousands of landless labourers occupied properties of wealthy landowners in October 1933, leaving the latter eager for revenge.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The most reactionary and clerical Carlists came to prominence, ideologues such as <a href="/wiki/V%C3%ADctor_Pradera_Larumbe" title="Víctor Pradera Larumbe">Víctor Pradera</a>, and an understanding with <a href="/wiki/General_Mola" class="mw-redirect" title="General Mola">General Mola</a> paved the way to the Spanish Nationalist uprising in Pamplona (18 July 1936).
</p><p>The triumphant military revolt was followed by a terror campaign in the rearguard against blacklisted individuals considered to be progressive ("reds"), mildly republicans, or just inconvenient.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The purge especially affected southern Navarre along the Ebro banks, and counted on the active complicity of the clergy, who adopted the fascist salute and even involved in murderous tasks.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Victims_of_Civil_War_in_Navarra" class="mw-redirect" title="Victims of Civil War in Navarra">The killing</a> took a death toll of at least 2,857, plus a further 305 dying in prisons (ill-treatment, malnutrition).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The dead were buried in mass graves or discarded into chasms abounding on the central hilly areas (Urbasa, etc.). Basque nationalists were also chased to a lesser extent, e.g. Fortunato Aguirre, a <a href="/wiki/Basque_Nationalist_Party" title="Basque Nationalist Party">Basque nationalist</a> and mayor of Estella (and co-founder of <a href="/wiki/Osasuna" class="mw-redirect" title="Osasuna">Osasuna</a> Football Club), was executed in September 1936. Humiliation and silence ensued for the survivors. Pamplona became the rebel launching point against the Republic during the <a href="/wiki/War_in_the_North" title="War in the North">War in the North</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_scene">Post-war scene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Post-war scene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As a reward for its support in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a> (Navarre sided for the most part with the military uprising), Franco allowed Navarre, as it happened with <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lava" title="Álava">Álava</a>, to maintain during his dictatorship a number of prerogatives reminiscent of <a href="/wiki/Fueros_of_Navarre" title="Fueros of Navarre">the ancient Navarrese liberties</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The bleak post-war years were shaken by shortage, famine, and smuggling, with the economy relying on agriculture (wheat, vineyards, olive, barley), and a negative migration balance.
</p><p>The victors came to cluster around two main factions, <a href="/wiki/Carlism#Spanish_Civil_War_and_Franco_regime_(1936–1975)" title="Carlism">Carlists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Falange" class="mw-redirect" title="Falange">Falangists</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> while the totalitarian ultra-Catholic environment provided fertile grounds for another religious group, the <a href="/wiki/Opus_Dei" title="Opus Dei">Opus Dei</a>, to found their <a href="/wiki/University_of_Navarre" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Navarre">University of Navarre</a> (1952), ever more influential in Pamplona.
</p><p>The coming of the society of consumption and incipient economic liberalisation saw also the establishment of factories and workshops during the early 1960s (automobile manufacturing and accessories, etc.), especially around the overgrown capital. It was followed by labour and political unrest. In the run-up to Spanish democracy (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_constitutional_referendum,_1978" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish constitutional referendum, 1978">Constitution ratified in 1978</a>), Navarre plunged into a climate of violence practised by <a href="/wiki/ETA_(separatist_group)" title="ETA (separatist group)">ETA</a>, police forces, and state-sponsored paramilitary groups, extending through the 1980s and beyond.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tension_during_the_Spanish_transition">Tension during the Spanish transition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tension during the Spanish transition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Officials and figures with good connections to the Navarrese regional government went on to join <a href="/wiki/Adolfo_Suarez" class="mw-redirect" title="Adolfo Suarez">Adolfo Suárez</a>’s <a href="/wiki/Union_of_the_Democratic_Centre_(Spain)" title="Union of the Democratic Centre (Spain)">UCD</a>, later splitting into the party <a href="/wiki/Uni%C3%B3n_del_Pueblo_Navarro" class="mw-redirect" title="Unión del Pueblo Navarro">UPN</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Jes%C3%BAs_Aizp%C3%BAn_Tuero" title="Jesús Aizpún Tuero">Jesús Aizpún Tuero</a> (1979), refusing to join a democratic constitutional process on the grounds that Navarre’s charters (or <i>fueros</i>) remained in place. They also refused to join the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Autonomy_of_the_Basque_Country" title="Statute of Autonomy of the Basque Country">Basque process</a> to become an autonomous community, where recently legalised Basque nationalist and leftist parties held a majority.
</p><p>A continuation of the institutional framework inherited from the dictatorship and its accommodation into the Spanish democracy was guaranteed by the Betterment (“Amejoramiento”), a Navarre-only solution considered ‘an upgrade’ of its former status issued from the (remains of the) charters. In a 3-year span, the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_of_Navarre" title="Socialist Party of Navarre">Spanish Socialists in Navarre</a> veered in their position, quit the Basque process, and joined the arrangement adopted for Navarre (Chartered Community of Navarre, 1982). The reform was not ratified by referendum, as demanded by Basque nationalist and minority progressive forces.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Institutions_and_status">Institutions and status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Institutions and status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the end of <a href="/wiki/Francoist_Spain" title="Francoist Spain">Franco's dictatorship</a>, Navarre became one of the 17 <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_communities_of_Spain" title="Autonomous communities of Spain">Autonomous Communities</a> in Spain. The community ceremonies, education, and social services, together with housing, urban development, and environment protection policies are under the responsibility of Navarre's political institutions. As in the rest of communities, Navarre has a <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Navarre" title="Parliament of Navarre">Parliament</a> elected every four years, and the majority in this Parliament determines the president of the Community, who is in charge of Navarre's government.
</p><p>Unlike most other autonomous communities of Spain (but like the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">Basque Autonomous Community</a>), Navarre has almost full responsibility for collecting and administering taxes which must follow the overall guidelines established by the Spanish government but may have some minor differences.
</p><p>The first 3 presidents of the community belonged to the extinct <a href="/wiki/Union_of_the_Democratic_Centre_(Spain)" title="Union of the Democratic Centre (Spain)">Union of the Democratic Centre</a> (UCD) party. After 1984 the government was ruled by either the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_of_Navarre" title="Socialist Party of Navarre">Socialist Party of Navarre</a> (PSN–PSOE, one of the federative components of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Socialist_Workers%27_Party" title="Spanish Socialist Workers&#39; Party">Spanish Socialist Workers' Party</a>, main centre-left wing party in Spain) or the <a href="/wiki/Navarrese_People%27s_Union" title="Navarrese People&#39;s Union">Navarrese People's Union</a> (UPN) (a Navarrese party that had a long alliance with the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Party_(Spain)" title="People&#39;s Party (Spain)">People's Party</a> (PP), main right-wing party in Spain). However, in 2015 Uxue Barkos (<a href="/wiki/Geroa_Bai" title="Geroa Bai">Geroa Bai</a>) became president with the support of <a href="/wiki/EH_Bildu" title="EH Bildu">EH Bildu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Podemos_(Spanish_political_party)" title="Podemos (Spanish political party)">Podemos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Izquierda-Ezkerra" title="Izquierda-Ezkerra">Izquierda-Ezkerra</a>. She is the first <a href="/wiki/Basque_nationalism" title="Basque nationalism">Basque nationalist</a> president in Navarre.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Basque_nationalism" title="Basque nationalism">Basque nationalist</a> parties also represent a sizeable part of the vote (around 31% in the <a href="/wiki/Navarrese_regional_election,_2015" class="mw-redirect" title="Navarrese regional election, 2015">2015 elections</a>), and a majority in most of the northern areas. Basque nationalist parties have as a key point in their agendas to <a href="/wiki/Southern_Basque_Country" title="Southern Basque Country">merge Navarre into the Basque Autonomous Community</a> by referendum (as predicted in the Spanish constitution). All Spain-based parties, as well as UPN and PSN, oppose this move.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Present-day_political_dynamics">Present-day political dynamics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Present-day political dynamics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Politics in Navarre has been marked by fierce rivalry between two blocks representing different national identities that are part of Navarre society: Basque nationalist EH Bildu and pro-Basque Geroa Bai parties, on the one side, and the institutional pro-Spanish parties, UPN, PP and PSN on the other. Parties on the pro-Basque spectrum demand further sovereignty in internal affairs of Navarre and closer relationship with the districts of the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Autonomous_Community" class="mw-redirect" title="Basque Autonomous Community">Basque Autonomous Community</a>. In the past, pro-Basque parties were excluded from key political posts and institutions. Another 2013-2014 controversy refers to the alleged ideological profiling of public school Basque language teachers, billed as "<a href="/wiki/ETA_(separatist_group)" title="ETA (separatist group)">ETA</a> supporting teachers".
</p><p>Since the establishment of Navarre's present status (the <i>Amejoramiento</i>, the 'Betterment') in 1982, the successive regional governments ruled by UPN and PSN have been shaken by frequent political instability and <a href="/wiki/Corruption_in_Navarre" title="Corruption in Navarre">corruption scandals</a>, with UPN's Miguel Sanz's term being the most stable and longest, extending from 2001 to 2011. Between 2012 and 2014, a series of corruption scandals broke out involving regional president <a href="/wiki/Yolanda_Barcina" title="Yolanda Barcina">Yolanda Barcina</a> and other regional government officials that included influence peddling, embezzlement, misappropriation of funds and mismanagement leading to the <a href="/wiki/Caja_Navarra_scandal" title="Caja Navarra scandal">bankruptcy of Caja Navarra</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> By November 2012, the PSN—UPN's standing ally in Navarre up to that point—backed down on its support of UPN, but refused to impeach Yolanda Barcina or search new political alliances, leaving a deadlocked government. The regional president, widely questioned in Navarre as of 2012 and relying only on the PP central government's backup, went on to urge the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Court_of_Spain" title="Constitutional Court of Spain">Constitutional Court</a> to challenge several decisions made by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Navarre" title="Parliament of Navarre">Parliament of Navarre</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the latest scandal and corruption allegations affecting a secretary of her cabinet (Lourdes Goicoechea, regional public finance secretary) in February 2014,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> the Spanish home office secretary <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Fern%C3%A1ndez_D%C3%ADaz" title="Jorge Fernández Díaz">Jorge Fernández Díaz</a> stepped in warning leading members of PSN that "Navarre is strategic for Spain", and asserting that any other political alliance means "supporting ETA". The Justice secretary in Madrid <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Ruiz_Gallard%C3%B3n" class="mw-redirect" title="Alberto Ruiz Gallardón">Alberto Ruiz Gallardón</a> in turn stated that "the worst political error is not corruption" but getting along with <a href="/wiki/Bildu" class="mw-redirect" title="Bildu">Bildu</a> (a Basque pro-independence coalition).<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In May 2015, the elections for Navarre Parliament left a better result for pro-Basque parties, which managed to establish an alliance, <a href="/wiki/Uxue_Barkos" title="Uxue Barkos">Uxue Barkos</a> from Geroa Bai being elected president of Navarre for the period 2015-2019. June 2019 elections, however, turned the tide, when rightist forces reunited in the platform <a href="/wiki/Navarra_Suma" title="Navarra Suma">Navarra Suma</a>, made up of UPN, PP and <a href="/wiki/Citizens_(Spanish_political_party)" title="Citizens (Spanish political party)">Ciudadanos</a>, and garnered 20 MPs, 40% of the seats in the Parliament of Navarre.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2017, the Navarrese parliament passed a law splitting teachers aspiring to work in the state-run education network into two different professional categories, one for those qualified in <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">Basque</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, and another for Spanish monolinguals, so thwarting with the vote of <a href="/wiki/Izquierda-Ezkerra" title="Izquierda-Ezkerra">Izquierda-Ezkerra</a> (integrated in the regional government) the new progressive government's plan to have just one; the latter echoes a long-running demand of education unions.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In July 2018, the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Court_of_Spain" title="Constitutional Court of Spain">Constitutional Court of Spain</a> suspended the Far Right's and Civil Servants' Victims Act passed by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Navarre" title="Parliament of Navarre">Parliament of Navarre</a> in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Three months later, the Chief Executive Officer of the <a href="/wiki/National_Police_Corps" title="National Police Corps">National Police</a> in Navarre stepped down for the disclosure of a fake <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> account he owned that praised <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Tejero" title="Antonio Tejero">Antonio Tejero</a>, as well as Vox leader <a href="/wiki/Santiago_Abascal" title="Santiago Abascal">Santiago Abascal</a> as a new <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Antonio_Primo_de_Rivera" title="José Antonio Primo de Rivera">Jose Antonio</a>, also insulting a number of Catalan and Basque nationalist and leftist figures.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_climate">Geography and climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Geography and climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bardenas_Reales_4.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Bardenas_Reales_4.jpg/210px-Bardenas_Reales_4.jpg" decoding="async" width="210" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Bardenas_Reales_4.jpg/315px-Bardenas_Reales_4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Bardenas_Reales_4.jpg/420px-Bardenas_Reales_4.jpg 2x" data-file-width="797" data-file-height="530" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bardenas_Reales_4.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bardenas_Reales" title="Bardenas Reales">Bardenas Reales</a></div></div></div> 
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hayedo_de_Irati_1._Oto%C3%B1o_de_2011.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Hayedo_de_Irati_1._Oto%C3%B1o_de_2011.JPG/180px-Hayedo_de_Irati_1._Oto%C3%B1o_de_2011.JPG" decoding="async" width="180" height="270" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Hayedo_de_Irati_1._Oto%C3%B1o_de_2011.JPG/270px-Hayedo_de_Irati_1._Oto%C3%B1o_de_2011.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Hayedo_de_Irati_1._Oto%C3%B1o_de_2011.JPG/360px-Hayedo_de_Irati_1._Oto%C3%B1o_de_2011.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2442" data-file-height="3664" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hayedo_de_Irati_1._Oto%C3%B1o_de_2011.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/European_beech" class="mw-redirect" title="European beech">European beech</a> in <a href="/wiki/Irati_Forest" title="Irati Forest">Irati Forest</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Navarre consists of 272 <a href="/wiki/List_of_municipalities_in_Navarre" title="List of municipalities in Navarre">municipalities</a> and has a total population of 601,874 (2006), of whom approximately one-third live in the capital, <a href="/wiki/Pamplona" title="Pamplona">Pamplona</a> (195,769 pop.), and one-half in the capital's metropolitan area (315,988 pop.). There are no other large municipalities in the region. The next largest are <a href="/wiki/Tudela,_Navarre" title="Tudela, Navarre">Tudela</a> (32,802), <a href="/wiki/Bara%C3%B1%C3%A1in" title="Barañáin">Barañáin</a> (22,401), <a href="/wiki/Burlada" title="Burlada">Burlada</a> (18,388), <a href="/wiki/Estella_-_Lizarra" class="mw-redirect" title="Estella - Lizarra">Estella - Lizarra</a> (13,892), <a href="/wiki/Zizur_Mayor" class="mw-redirect" title="Zizur Mayor">Zizur Mayor</a> (13,197), <a href="/wiki/Tafalla" title="Tafalla">Tafalla</a> (11,040), <a href="/wiki/Villava/Atarrabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Villava/Atarrabia">Villava/Atarrabia</a> (10,295), and <a href="/wiki/Anso%C3%A1in" title="Ansoáin">Ansoáin</a> (9,952).
</p><p>Despite its relatively small size, Navarre features stark contrasts in geography, from the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a> mountain range that dominates the territory to the plains of the Ebro river valley in the south. The highest point in Navarre is <a href="/wiki/Mesa_de_los_Tres_Reyes" title="Mesa de los Tres Reyes">Mesa de los Tres Reyes</a>, with an elevation of 2,428&#160;metres (7,965&#160;feet).
</p><p>Other important mountains are <a href="/wiki/Txamantxoia" title="Txamantxoia">Txamantxoia</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kartxela&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kartxela (page does not exist)">Kartxela</a>, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Larra-Belagua_Massif&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Larra-Belagua Massif (page does not exist)">Larra-Belagua Massif</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_de_Alaiz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sierra de Alaiz (page does not exist)">Sierra de Alaiz</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Untzueko_Harria&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Untzueko Harria (page does not exist)">Untzueko Harria</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_de_Leyre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sierra de Leyre (page does not exist)">Sierra de Leyre</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sierra_del_Perd%C3%B3n&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sierra del Perdón (page does not exist)">Sierra del Perdón</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montejurra" title="Montejurra">Montejurra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fort_San_Crist%C3%B3bal_(Spain)" title="Fort San Cristóbal (Spain)">Ezkaba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monte_Ori" class="mw-redirect" title="Monte Ori">Monte Ori</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sierra_de_Cod%C3%A9s" title="Sierra de Codés">Sierra de Codés</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urbasa" title="Urbasa">Urbasa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andia" title="Andia">Andia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Aralar_Range" title="Aralar Range">Aralar Range</a>.
</p><p>In the north, climate is affected by the Atlantic Ocean leading an Oceanic west coast climate (Köppen: Cfb) At central Navarre the summer precipitations start to lower, leading to a Mediterranean climate (Köppen: Csa and Csb) At the southernmost part of Navarre the climate is cool semi-arid (Köppen: Bsk)
</p><p>The sole official weather station of Navarre is located in Pamplona in its north-western corner and has summer highs of 28&#160;°C (82&#160;°F) and lows of 14&#160;°C (57&#160;°F), while winter highs are 9&#160;°C (48&#160;°F) and lows 1&#160;°C (34&#160;°F) with moderate precipitation year-round.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_heritage">Cultural heritage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cultural heritage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Navarre is a mixture of its <a href="/wiki/Basque_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Basque people">Vasconic</a> tradition, the Trans-Pyrenean influx of people and ideas and <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediterranean">Mediterranean</a> influences coming from the <a href="/wiki/Ebro" title="Ebro">Ebro</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ebro" title="Ebro">Ebro</a> valley is amenable to <a href="/wiki/Wheat" title="Wheat">wheat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetables</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Olive_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Olive tree">olive trees</a> as in <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">Aragon</a> and <a href="/wiki/La_Rioja_(Spain)" title="La Rioja (Spain)">La Rioja</a>. It was a part of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>, inhabited by the <a href="/wiki/Vascones" title="Vascones">Vascones</a>, later controlled on its southern fringes by the Muslims <a href="/wiki/Banu_Qasi" title="Banu Qasi">Banu Qasi</a>, whose authority was taken over by the <a href="/wiki/Taifa" title="Taifa">taifa</a> kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Tudela,_Navarre" title="Tudela, Navarre">Tudela</a> in the 11th&#160;century.
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">Reconquista</a>, Navarre gained little ground at the expense of the Muslims, since its southern boundary had already been established by the time of the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Las_Navas_de_Tolosa" title="Battle of Las Navas de Tolosa">Battle of Las Navas de Tolosa</a> in 1212. Starting in the 11th&#160;century, the <a href="/wiki/Way_of_Saint_James" class="mw-redirect" title="Way of Saint James">Way of Saint James</a> grew in importance. It brought pilgrims, traders and Christian soldiers from the North. <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language#Occitan_in_Spain" title="Occitan language">Gascons and Occitanians</a> from beyond the <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">Pyrenees</a> (called <i>Franks</i>) received self-government and other privileges to foster settlement in Navarrese towns, and they brought their crafts, culture and <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance languages</a>.
</p><p>Jews and Muslims were persecuted both north and south of Navarre, expelled for the most part during the late 15th&#160;century to the early 16th&#160;century. The kingdom struggled to maintain its separate identity in 14th and 15th centuries, and after <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">King Ferdinand V</a> forcibly conquered Navarre after the death of his wife Queen Isabella, he extended the Castilian expulsion and forcible integration orders applicable to conversos and mudejars of 1492 to the former kingdom. Therefore, <a href="/wiki/Tudela,_Navarre" title="Tudela, Navarre">Tudela</a> in particular could no longer serve as a refuge after the Inquisitors were allowed.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Energy_policy">Energy policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Energy policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Update plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Update" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Ambox current red.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/42px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png" decoding="async" width="42" height="34" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/63px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Ambox_current_red.svg/84px-Ambox_current_red.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="360" data-file-height="290" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section needs to be <b>updated</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please update this article to reflect recent events or newly available information.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">June 2012</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_Spain" title="Wind power in Spain">Wind power in Spain</a></div>
<p>Navarre leads Europe in its use of <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> technology and was planning to reach 100% renewable electricity generation by 2010. By 2004, 61% of the region's electricity was generated by renewable sources consisting of 43.6% from 28 <a href="/wiki/Wind_farm" title="Wind farm">wind farms</a>, 12% from over 100 small-scale <a href="/wiki/Water_turbine" title="Water turbine">water turbines</a>, and 5.3% from 2 <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> and 2 <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a> plants. In addition, the region had what was then Spain's largest <a href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" class="mw-redirect" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a> power plant at Montes de Cierzo de <a href="/wiki/Tudela,_Navarre" title="Tudela, Navarre">Tudela</a> (1.2 MWp capacity) plus several hundred smaller photovoltaic installations.
</p><p>Developments since 2004 have included further photovoltaic plants at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Larri%C3%B3n&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Larrión (page does not exist)">Larrión</a> (0.25 MWp)<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>  and another at <a href="/wiki/Castej%C3%B3n,_Navarre" title="Castejón, Navarre">Castejón</a> (2.44 MWp), also once the largest in Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Languages">Languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_Iberian_Peninsula_1030-es.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Map_Iberian_Peninsula_1030-es.svg/220px-Map_Iberian_Peninsula_1030-es.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Map_Iberian_Peninsula_1030-es.svg/330px-Map_Iberian_Peninsula_1030-es.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Map_Iberian_Peninsula_1030-es.svg/440px-Map_Iberian_Peninsula_1030-es.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="827" data-file-height="574" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_Iberian_Peninsula_1030-es.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The Iberian Peninsula in 1030</i>. The first evidence of written <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance</a> in central Spain and of written <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">Basque</a> is in the <a href="/wiki/Glosas_Emilianenses" title="Glosas Emilianenses">Glosas Emilianenses</a>, from <a href="/wiki/La_Rioja_(Spain)" title="La Rioja (Spain)">La Rioja</a>, a territory that was part of Navarre for some time. The map shows <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Navarre" title="Kingdom of Navarre">the Kingdom of Pamplona</a> through the years <a href="/wiki/1029" title="1029">1029</a> y <a href="/wiki/1035" title="1035">1035</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Presently, Spanish is predominantly spoken in most of the autonomous community, except for north-eastern areas, where Basque continues to be the prevailing language. According to official statistics, Spanish is the mother tongue of 81.9% of the population, Basque is 5.7% of the population's mother tongue, and 3.8% of the population has both languages as their mother tongue while 6.1% of the population have another language as their <a href="/wiki/Mother-tongue" class="mw-redirect" title="Mother-tongue">mother tongue</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_shift">Language shift</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Language shift">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>
The number of people that can speak Basque has increased in Navarre lately,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> after a steady historic retreat. In 2011, 13.6% of the population in Navarre considered themselves to be speakers of Basque and another 14.5% considered themselves semi-speakers of Basque.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Historically, Basque is the <i>lingua navarrorum</i>, as it appears in documents of the Middle Ages, such as a document by the king <a href="/wiki/Sancho_the_Wise" class="mw-redirect" title="Sancho the Wise">Sancho the Wise</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The kingdom cemented its roots in the predominantly Basque-speaking domain of Pamplona and surrounding areas.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> In the midst of contemporary scholarly debates on the existence of Navarre and its laws prior to the king's authority, the Navarrese author Garcia de Gongora states as follows in 1626:<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style></p><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Two languages are spoken across the kingdom, Basque and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Romance</a>, but most properly the Cantabric [language] Basque, the original and most ancient, brought along by <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_the_Basques#Tubalism" title="Origin of the Basques">its creator, the patriarch Tubal</a>, devoid of mingling with others; it has always been preserved there, except in the Ribera and the bordering areas of Castile and Aragon, where Romance is spoken.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite>García de Gongora (pseudonym of Juan Sada Amezqueta)<sup id="cite_ref-Monreal_55-61_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monreal_55-61-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jos%C3%A9_Moret&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="José Moret (page does not exist)">José Moret</a>, chronicler of the kingdom, called Navarre and its bordering provinces "the lands of Basque", claiming also that Tubal founded the Kingdom of Navarre.<sup id="cite_ref-Monreal_55-61_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Monreal_55-61-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> However, Basque underwent a gradual erosion, accelerated following the conquest of the kingdom in the early 16th century due to the homogenizing push of the new Castilian authorities and the neglect of its own elites, among other reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> By 1778, 121,000 inhabitants out of 227.000 were Basque speakers, 53% of its population, still the largest amount of Basque speakers across <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(greater_region)" title="Basque Country (greater region)">all Basque territories</a>. However, the number of speakers dropped sharply in the 19th century. In 1936, Basque speakers accounted for a 17% of the total Navarrese population.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other languages have been spoken, but have disappeared, such as <a href="/wiki/Navarro-Aragonese" title="Navarro-Aragonese">Navarro-Aragonese</a>, a Romance language that was spoken in the Middle Ages around the central part of the <a href="/wiki/Ebro" title="Ebro">Ebro</a> basin. Starting in the late 11th century, the influx of pilgrims and colonizers from Toulouse and surrounding areas (<i>Francs</i>) who settled in separate boroughs along the Way of Saint James rendered <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">Occitan</a> the status language of the kingdom up to early-14th century. <a href="/wiki/Navarro-Aragonese" title="Navarro-Aragonese">Navarro-Aragonese</a> became the written language in court and royal administration by 1329, when it reached official status.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> However, from the 15th century onwards the language grew closer to Castilian (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>) and eventually merged with it.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Other languages which at some point held a status or were spoken in certain communities and periods are <a href="/wiki/Erromintxela_language" title="Erromintxela language">Erromintxela</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_division_of_the_territory_and_legal_consequences">Linguistic division of the territory and legal consequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Linguistic division of the territory and legal consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Navarra_-_Zonificacion_linguistica.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Navarra_-_Zonificacion_linguistica.png/220px-Navarra_-_Zonificacion_linguistica.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="238" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Navarra_-_Zonificacion_linguistica.png/330px-Navarra_-_Zonificacion_linguistica.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Navarra_-_Zonificacion_linguistica.png/440px-Navarra_-_Zonificacion_linguistica.png 2x" data-file-width="731" data-file-height="792" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Navarra_-_Zonificacion_linguistica.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Distribution by municipality of the Basque-speaking zone, mixed-speaking Zone and the non-Basque speaking zone through the modification of 2017.</div></div></div>
<p>According to the 1978 Spanish constitution and the <a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amejoramiento_de_Navarra" class="extiw" title="es:Amejoramiento de Navarra">Amejoramiento del Fuero</a>, a Navarrese law establishing the basic institutional make-up of the chartered community of Navarre, Spanish is the official language of Navarre while the <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">Basque</a> language is also the official language in Basque-Speaking areas. <a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ley_Foral_del_Euskera" class="extiw" title="es:Ley Foral del Euskera">The Statutory Law of Basque of 1986</a> regrouped these areas, creating the <a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ley_Foral_del_Euskera#Zona_vascófona" class="extiw" title="es:Ley Foral del Euskera"><i>Basque-Speaking Zone</i></a>, an area north-east of Navarre in which Basque is the co-official language with Spanish. This law recognizes Spanish and Basque as Navarre's <i>lenguas propias (</i>a Spanish legal term meaning that a language in an autonomous community in Spain has co-official status along with Spanish)<i>,<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> according to the </i>Foral Law 18/1986<i> of Basque.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup></i>
</p><p>This law divides Navarre into three linguistically distinct areas, a <i><a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ley_Foral_del_Euskera#Zona_vascófona" class="extiw" title="es:Ley Foral del Euskera">Basque-Speaking Zone</a></i>, where Basque is the dominant language, a <i><a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ley_Foral_del_Euskera#Zona_vascófona" class="extiw" title="es:Ley Foral del Euskera">Mixed-Speaking Zone</a></i>, where Basque and Spanish are both dominant, and a <i><a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ley_Foral_del_Euskera#Zona_vascófona" class="extiw" title="es:Ley Foral del Euskera">Non-Basque Speaking Zone</a></i>, where Spanish is the dominant language. In the latter, the public entities of Navarre are required to use only Spanish, but in the mixed area the use of Basque is also confined to certain position. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Denomination_of_local_entities">Denomination of local entities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Denomination of local entities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The official denomination of Navarran municipalities and villages are regulated according to the <i>Foral Basque Law.</i><sup id="cite_ref-:0_46-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> It distinguishes three different types of formulas: 
</p>
<ul><li><b>Unique denominations:</b> the use of Basque in legal documents is the same when compared with Spanish independently. Examples: Lantz or Beintza-Labaien.</li>
<li><b>Compounded denominations:</b> They have a unique denomination formula formed from the Spanish and Basque toponyms in Spanish or Basque and are united by the symbol "/". Its use (the compounded denomination) is the same in Spanish as in Basque. Examples: Auritz/Burguete, Roncesvalles/Orreaga, Luzaide/Valcarlos or Doneztebe/Santesteban.</li>
<li><b>Double denominations:</b> The toponym, in Basque or Spanish is dependent on the language and how it is used in the text. Examples: Pamplona&lt;&gt;Iruña, Villava&lt;&gt;Atarrabia, Aibar&lt;&gt;Oibar.</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Basque_dialects_in_Navarre">Basque dialects in Navarre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Basque dialects in Navarre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Basque in Navarre has various dialects (there are nine according to the classification of the General Basque Dictionary or the <a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_Academia_de_la_Lengua_Vasca" class="extiw" title="es:Real Academia de la Lengua Vasca">Royal Academy of the Basque Language</a>). According to the most recent classification of <a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koldo_Zuazo" class="extiw" title="es:Koldo Zuazo">Koldo Zuazo</a>, the most widespread dialect is <a href="/wiki/Upper_Navarrese_dialect" title="Upper Navarrese dialect">Upper Navarrese</a>, spoken in the northern part of Navarre. In localities such as Basaburua Mayor, Imoz and other border localities with Gipuzkoa, the dialect of Central Basque is spoken and in the central part of the Pyrenees in Navarre a variety of <a href="/wiki/Navarro-Lapurdian_dialect" title="Navarro-Lapurdian dialect">Navarro-Lapurdian</a> is spoken.
</p><p>On the east of the Pyrenees in Navarre, Basque used to be spoken in the valleys of <a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roncal" class="extiw" title="es:Roncal">Roncal</a> y <a href="/wiki/Salazar_Valley" title="Salazar Valley">Salazar</a>, but it disappeared near the end of the twentieth century; the last person who spoke it in Roncal died in 1991 and in Salazar the language also disappeared because the last person who spoke it fluently died during the first years of the twenty-first century. Apart from dialects, sub-dialects from Basque also exist and there are also differences in vocabulary in local linguistic communities.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_traits_of_the_Spanish_spoken_in_Navarre">Linguistic traits of the Spanish spoken in Navarre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Linguistic traits of the Spanish spoken in Navarre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are a number of features of Spanish as spoken in Navarre that are either exclusive to the area or shared only with neighbouring areas (mainly <a href="/wiki/Arag%C3%B3n" class="mw-redirect" title="Aragón">Aragón</a> and <a href="/wiki/La_Rioja_(Spain)" title="La Rioja (Spain)">La Rioja</a>), such as the predominance of the <a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">diminutive</a> with <i>-ico</i> or the use of the conditional verb tense in place of the preterite of the subjunctive (for example, using <i>podría</i> instead of <i>pudiera</i>). There are also differences in the vocabulary of Spanish speakers from Navarre,<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> including the presence of words of Basque origin, which is in some cases due to a <a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sustrato_vasco_en_lenguas_romances" class="extiw" title="es:Sustrato vasco en lenguas romances">Basque substrate</a>, or long-standing contact and commercial exchanges with areas of Navarre in which Basque is spoken.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
