<p>An <b>identifier</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Name" title="Name">name</a> that identifies (that is, labels the <a href="/wiki/Identity_(philosophy)" title="Identity (philosophy)">identity</a> of) either a unique object or a unique <i>class</i> of objects, where the "object" or class may be an idea, physical [countable] object (or class thereof), or physical [noncountable] substance (or class thereof). The abbreviation <b>ID</b> often refers to identity, identification (the process of identifying), or an identifier (that is, an <a href="/wiki/Instantiation_principle" title="Instantiation principle">instance</a> of identification). An identifier may be a word, number, letter, symbol, or any combination of those.
</p><p>The words, numbers, letters, or symbols may follow an <a href="/wiki/Code" title="Code">encoding system</a> (wherein letters, digits, words, or symbols <i>stand for</i> (represent) ideas or longer names) or they may simply be arbitrary. When an identifier follows an encoding system, it is often referred to as a <b>code</b> or <b>ID&#160;code</b>.  For instance the <a href="/wiki/ISO/IEC_11179" title="ISO/IEC 11179">ISO/IEC 11179</a> metadata registry standard defines a code as <i>system of valid symbols that substitute for longer values</i> in contrast to identifiers without symbolic meaning. Identifiers that do not follow any encoding scheme are often said to be <b>arbitrary&#160;IDs</b>; they are arbitrarily assigned and have no greater meaning. (Sometimes identifiers are called "codes" even when they are actually arbitrary, whether because the speaker believes that they have deeper meaning or simply because they are speaking casually and imprecisely.)
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Unique_identifier" title="Unique identifier">unique identifier</a> (<b>UID</b>) is an identifier that refers to <i>only one instance</i>—only one particular object in the universe. A <a href="/wiki/Part_number" title="Part number">part number</a> is an identifier, but it is not a <i>unique</i> identifier—for that, a <a href="/wiki/Serial_number" title="Serial number">serial number</a> is needed, to identify <i>each instance</i> of the part design. Thus the <i>identifier</i> "Model&#160;T" identifies the <i>class</i> <i>(model)</i> of automobiles that Ford's <a href="/wiki/Ford_Model_T" title="Ford Model T">Model&#160;T</a> comprises; whereas the <i>unique identifier</i> "Model&#160;T&#160;Serial Number&#160;159,862" identifies one specific member of that class—that is, one particular Model&#160;T car, owned by one specific person.
</p><p>The concepts of <i>name</i> and <i>identifier</i> are <a href="/wiki/Denotation" title="Denotation">denotatively</a> equal, and the terms are thus denotatively <a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">synonymous</a>; but they are not always <a href="/wiki/Connotation" title="Connotation">connotatively</a> synonymous, because <b><a href="/wiki/Code_name" title="Code name">code names</a></b> and <b>ID numbers</b> are often connotatively distinguished from names in the sense of traditional <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">natural language</a> naming. For example, both "<a href="/wiki/Jamie_Zawinski" title="Jamie Zawinski">Jamie Zawinski</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Netscape" title="Netscape">Netscape</a> employee number 20" are identifiers for the same specific human being; but normal English-language connotation may consider "Jamie Zawinski" a "name" and not an "identifier", whereas it considers "Netscape&#160;employee&#160;number&#160;20" an "identifier" but not a "name". This is an <a href="/wiki/Emic" class="mw-redirect" title="Emic">emic</a> indistinction rather than an <a href="/wiki/Etic" class="mw-redirect" title="Etic">etic</a> one.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metadata">Metadata</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Identifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Metadata">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a>, an identifier is a language-independent label, sign or token that uniquely identifies an object within an <a href="/wiki/Identification_scheme" title="Identification scheme">identification scheme</a>. The suffix "identifier" is also used as a <a href="/wiki/Representation_term" title="Representation term">representation term</a> when naming a <a href="/wiki/Data_element" title="Data element">data element</a>.
</p><p>ID codes may inherently carry <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a> along with them. For example, when you know that the food package in front of you has the identifier "2011-09-25T15:42Z-MFR5-P02-243-45", you not only have that data, you also have the metadata that tells you that it was packaged on September 25, 2011, at 3:42pm UTC, manufactured by Licensed Vendor Number 5, at the Peoria, IL, USA plant, in Building 2, and was the 243rd package off the line in that shift, and was inspected by Inspector Number 45.
</p><p>Arbitrary identifiers might lack metadata. For example, if a food package just says 100054678214, its ID may not tell anything except identity—no date, manufacturer name, production sequence rank, or inspector number. In some cases, arbitrary identifiers such as sequential serial numbers leak information (i.e. the <a href="/wiki/German_tank_problem" title="German tank problem">German tank problem</a>). Opaque identifiers—identifiers designed to avoid leaking even that small amount of information—include "really <a href="/wiki/Opaque_pointer" title="Opaque pointer">opaque pointers</a>" and <a href="/wiki/Universally_unique_identifier#Version_4_(random)" title="Universally unique identifier">Version 4 UUIDs</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_computer_science">In computer science</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Identifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In computer science">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a>, identifiers (IDs) are <a href="/wiki/Lexical_(semiotics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lexical (semiotics)">lexical</a> <a href="/wiki/Token_(parser)" class="mw-redirect" title="Token (parser)">tokens</a> that name <a href="/wiki/Entity_class" class="mw-redirect" title="Entity class">entities</a>. Identifiers are used extensively in virtually all <a href="/wiki/Information_processing_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Information processing system">information processing systems</a>. Identifying entities makes it possible to refer to them, which is essential for any kind of symbolic processing.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_computer_languages">In computer languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Identifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In computer languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Identifier_(computer_languages)" title="Identifier (computer languages)">Identifier (computer languages)</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Computer_language" title="Computer language">computer languages</a>, identifiers are <a href="/wiki/Token_(parser)" class="mw-redirect" title="Token (parser)">tokens</a> (also called <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbols</a>) which name language entities. Some of the kinds of entities an identifier might denote include <a href="/wiki/Variable_(programming)" class="mw-redirect" title="Variable (programming)">variables</a>, <a href="/wiki/Data_type" title="Data type">types</a>, <a href="/wiki/Label_(programming_language)" class="mw-redirect" title="Label (programming language)">labels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subroutine" title="Subroutine">subroutines</a>,  and <a href="/wiki/Modular_programming" title="Modular programming">packages</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ambiguity">Ambiguity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Identifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ambiguity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Identifiers_.28IDs.29_versus_Unique_identifiers_.28UIDs.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Identifiers_(IDs)_versus_Unique_identifiers_(UIDs)">Identifiers (IDs) versus Unique identifiers (UIDs)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Identifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Identifiers (IDs) versus Unique identifiers (UIDs)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Unique_identifier" title="Unique identifier">Unique identifier</a></div>
<p>Many resources may carry multiple identifiers. Typical examples are:
</p>
<ul><li>One person with multiple names, nicknames, and forms of address (titles, salutations)
<ul><li><i>For example:</i> One specific person may be identified by all of the following identifiers: Jane&#160;Smith; Jane&#160;Elizabeth&#160;Meredith&#160;Smith; Jane&#160;E.&#160;M.&#160;Smith; Jane&#160;E.&#160;Smith; Janie&#160;Smith; Janie; Little&#160;Janie (as opposed to her mother or sister or cousin, Big&#160;Janie); Aunt&#160;Jane; Auntie&#160;Janie; Mom; Grandmom; Nana; Kelly's&#160;mother; Billy's&#160;grandmother; Ms.&#160;Smith; Dr.&#160;Smith; Jane&#160;E.&#160;Smith,&#160;PhD; and Fuzzy (her jocular nickname at work).</li></ul></li>
<li>One document with multiple versions<sup id="cite_ref-paid_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-paid-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>One substance with multiple names (for example, <a href="/wiki/Chemical_Abstracts_Service" title="Chemical Abstracts Service">CAS</a> index names versus <a href="/wiki/IUPAC" class="mw-redirect" title="IUPAC">IUPAC</a> names;<sup id="cite_ref-icn_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-icn-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/International_Nonproprietary_Name" class="mw-redirect" title="International Nonproprietary Name">INN</a> generic drug names versus <a href="/wiki/United_States_Adopted_Name" title="United States Adopted Name">USAN</a> generic drug names versus brand names)</li></ul>
<p>The inverse is also possible, where multiple resources are represented with the same identifier (discussed below).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Implicit_context_and_namespace_conflicts">Implicit context and namespace conflicts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Identifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Implicit context and namespace conflicts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Naming_collision" title="Naming collision">Naming collision</a></div>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Code" title="Code">codes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nomenclature" title="Nomenclature">nomenclatural</a> systems originate within a small <a href="/wiki/Namespace" title="Namespace">namespace</a>. Over the years, some of them bleed into larger namespaces (as people interact in ways they formerly hadn't, e.g., cross-border trade, scientific collaboration, military alliance, and general cultural interconnection or assimilation). When such dissemination happens, the limitations of the original naming convention, which had formerly been latent and moot, become painfully apparent, often necessitating <a href="/wiki/Retronym" title="Retronym">retronymy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">synonymity</a>, translation/<a href="/wiki/Transcoding" title="Transcoding">transcoding</a>, and so on. Such limitations generally accompany the shift away from the original context to the broader one. Typically the system shows implicit context (context was formerly assumed, and narrow), lack of capacity (e.g., low number  of possible IDs, reflecting the outmoded narrow context), lack of <a href="/wiki/Extensibility" title="Extensibility">extensibility</a> (no features defined and reserved against future needs), and lack of specificity and disambiguating capability (related to the context shift, where longstanding uniqueness encounters novel nonuniqueness). Within computer science, this problem is called <a href="/wiki/Naming_collision" title="Naming collision">naming collision</a>. The story of the origination and expansion of the <a href="/wiki/CODEN" title="CODEN">CODEN</a> system provides a good case example in a recent-decades, technical-nomenclature context. The capitalization variations seen with <a href="/wiki/Proper_noun#Specific_designators" title="Proper noun">specific designators</a> reveals an instance of this problem occurring in <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">natural languages</a>, where the proper&#160;noun/common&#160;noun distinction (and its complications) must be dealt with. A universe in which every object had a UID would not need any namespaces, which is to say that it would constitute one gigantic namespace; but human minds could never keep track of, or semantically interrelate, so many UIDs.
</p>
