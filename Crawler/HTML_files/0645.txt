	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Windlass</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>The <b>windlass</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;w&#39; in &#39;wind&#39;">w</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span></span>/</a></span></span> is an apparatus for moving heavy weights. Typically, a windlass consists of a horizontal cylinder (barrel), which is rotated by the turn of a crank or belt. A <a href="/wiki/Winch" title="Winch">winch</a> is affixed to one or both ends, and a cable or rope is wound around the winch, pulling a weight attached to the opposite end. The oldest depiction of a windlass for raising water can be found in the Book of Agriculture published in 1313 by the Chinese official <a href="/wiki/Wang_Zhen_(inventor)" title="Wang Zhen (inventor)">Wang Zhen</a> of the Yuan Dynasty (<abbr title="floruit (&quot;flourished&quot; –&#160;known to have been active at a particular time or during a particular period)">fl.</abbr> 1290–1333).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
The Greek scientist <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">Archimedes</a> was the inventor of the windlass.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Windlass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Vitruvius" title="Vitruvius">Vitruvius</a>, a military engineer writing about 28&#160;BC, defined a machine as "a combination of timber fastened together, chiefly efficacious in moving great weights." About a century later, <a href="/wiki/Hero_of_Alexandria" title="Hero of Alexandria">Hero of Alexandria</a> summarized the practice of his day by naming the "five <a href="/wiki/Simple_machine" title="Simple machine">simple machines</a>" for "moving a given weight by a given force" as the lever, windlass, screw for power, wedge, and tackle block (pulley). Until nearly the end of the nineteenth century it was held that these "five mechanical powers" were the building blocks from which all more complex assemblages were constructed.' <sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>During the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> the windlass was used to raise materials for the construction of buildings such as in <a href="/wiki/Chesterfield#Religious_sites" title="Chesterfield">Chesterfield's crooked spire church</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>By the <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">Late Middle Ages</a> most European <a href="/wiki/Crossbow" title="Crossbow">crossbows</a> employed a windlass as a cocking mechanism, which helped to pull heavier crossbows, but were used in England as early as 1215.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Windlasses are sometimes used on boats to raise the <a href="/wiki/Anchor" title="Anchor">anchor</a> as an alternative to a vertical <a href="/wiki/Capstan_(nautical)" title="Capstan (nautical)">capstan</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Anchor_windlass" title="Anchor windlass">anchor windlass</a>).</li>
<li>The handle used to open <a href="/wiki/Lock_(water_transport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lock (water transport)">locks</a> on the UK's <a href="/wiki/Canals_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Canals of the United Kingdom">inland waterways</a> is called a <a href="/wiki/Lock_(water_transport)#Windlass_(&quot;lock_key&quot;)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lock (water transport)">windlass</a>.</li>
<li>Windlass can be used to raise water from a <a href="/wiki/Water_well" class="mw-redirect" title="Water well">well</a>. The oldest description of a <a href="/wiki/Water_well" class="mw-redirect" title="Water well">well</a> windlass, a rotating wooden rod installed across the mouth of a well, is found in <a href="/wiki/Isidore_of_Seville" title="Isidore of Seville">Isidore of Seville</a>'s (<abbr title="circa">c.</abbr> 560–636) <i>Origenes</i> (XX, 15, 1–3).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Windlass have also been used in <a href="/wiki/Gold_mining" title="Gold mining">gold mining</a>. A windlass would be constructed above a shaft which allowed heavy buckets to be hauled up to the surface.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This process would be used until the shaft got below 40 metres deep when the windlass would be replaced by a 'whip' or a <a href="/wiki/Whim_(mining)" title="Whim (mining)">'whim'</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Differential_windlass">Differential windlass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Windlass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Differential windlass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Differential_pulley" title="Differential pulley">Differential pulley</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Comparison_differential_pulley_windlass.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Comparison_differential_pulley_windlass.svg/220px-Comparison_differential_pulley_windlass.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Comparison_differential_pulley_windlass.svg/330px-Comparison_differential_pulley_windlass.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Comparison_differential_pulley_windlass.svg/440px-Comparison_differential_pulley_windlass.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="384" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Comparison_differential_pulley_windlass.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Comparison of a differential pulley or chain hoist (left) and a differential windlass or Chinese windlass (right). The rope of the windlass is depicted as spirals for clarity, but is more likely helices with axes perpendicular to the image.</div></div></div>
<p>In a <b>differential windlass</b>, also called a <b>Chinese windlass</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-Chinese_windlass_OED_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chinese_windlass_OED-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Morris_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Morris-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Knight_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Knight-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> there are two coaxial drums of different radii <i>r</i> and <i>r′</i>. The rope is wound onto one drum while it unwinds from the other, with a <a href="/wiki/Movable_pulley" class="mw-redirect" title="Movable pulley">movable pulley</a> hanging in the <a href="/wiki/Bight_(knot)" title="Bight (knot)">bight</a> between the drums. Since each turn of the crank raises the pulley and attached weight by only <span class="nowrap"><span class="texhtml">π</span>(<i>r</i> − <i>r′</i>)</span>, very large <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_advantage" title="Mechanical advantage">mechanical advantages</a> can be obtained.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_windlass">Spanish windlass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Windlass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spanish windlass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A Spanish windlass is a device for tightening a rope or cable by twisting it using a stick as a lever. The rope or cable is looped around two points so that it is fixed at either end. The stick is inserted into the loop and twisted, tightening the rope and pulling the two points toward each other. It is commonly used to move a heavy object such as a pipe or a post a short distance. It can be an effective device for pulling cars or cattle out of mud.<sup id="cite_ref-torture_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-torture-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> A Spanish windlass is sometimes used to tighten a <a href="/wiki/Tourniquet" title="Tourniquet">tourniquet</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Straitjacket" title="Straitjacket">straitjacket</a>. A Spanish windlass trap can be used to kill small game. An 1898 report to the US Senate Committee on Foreign Relations about an American vessel captured by a Spanish gunboat described the Spanish windlass as a torture device.<sup id="cite_ref-senate_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-senate-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> One of the captives' wrists were tied together. The captor then twisted a stick in the rope until it tightened and caused the man's wrists to swell.
</p>
