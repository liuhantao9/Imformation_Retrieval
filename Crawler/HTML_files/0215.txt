	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Patronymic surname</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Patrilineality" title="Patrilineality">Patrilineality</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>patronymic surname</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> originated from the <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> of the father or a <a href="/wiki/Patrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrilineal">patrilineal</a> <a href="/wiki/Ancestor" title="Ancestor">ancestor</a>. Different cultures have different ways of producing <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> surnames.
</p><p>For example, early patronymic <a href="/wiki/Welsh_surnames" title="Welsh surnames">Welsh surnames</a> were the result of the <a href="/wiki/Anglicisation" title="Anglicisation">Anglicizing</a> of the historical Welsh naming system, which sometimes had included references to several generations: e.g., Llywelyn ap Gruffydd ap Morgan (Llywelyn son of Gruffydd son of Morgan), and which gave rise to the quip, "as long as a Welshman's pedigree."
</p><p>As an example of Anglicization, the name Llywelyn ap Gruffydd was turned into Llywelyn Gruffydds; i.e., the "ap" meaning "son of" was replaced by the genitive suffix "-s." Some Welsh surnames, such as <a href="/wiki/John_(surname)" title="John (surname)">John</a> or <a href="/wiki/Howell_(surname)" class="mw-redirect" title="Howell (surname)">Howell</a>, did not acquire the suffix "-s." In some other cases the suffix was affixed to the surname much later: 18th or 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Likewise, in some cases the "ap" coalesced into the name in some form, as in Broderick (ap Rhydderch), Price (ap Rhys) and Upjohn (ap John).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
