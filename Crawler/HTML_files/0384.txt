	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Monogram</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Monogram_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Monogram (disambiguation)">Monogram (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Monograph" title="Monograph">Monograph</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nomogram" title="Nomogram">Nomogram</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:VOC_stone.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/VOC_stone.jpg/220px-VOC_stone.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/VOC_stone.jpg/330px-VOC_stone.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/VOC_stone.jpg/440px-VOC_stone.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2816" data-file-height="2112" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:VOC_stone.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Monogram logo of the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company" title="Dutch East India Company">Dutch East India Company</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Vereenigde_Oost-Indische_Compagnie" class="mw-redirect" title="Vereenigde Oost-Indische Compagnie">Vereenigde Oost-Indische Compagnie</a>", or "VOC" in Dutch), formerly above the entrance to the <a href="/wiki/Castle_of_Good_Hope" title="Castle of Good Hope">Castle of Good Hope</a> in South Africa</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>monogram</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Motif_(art)" class="mw-redirect" title="Motif (art)">motif</a> made by overlapping or combining two or more letters or other <a href="/wiki/Grapheme" title="Grapheme">graphemes</a> to form one <a href="/wiki/Symbol" title="Symbol">symbol</a>. Monograms are often made by combining the initials of an individual or a company, used as recognizable symbols or <a href="/wiki/Logo" title="Logo">logos</a>. A series of uncombined initials is properly referred to as a cypher (e.g. a <a href="/wiki/Royal_cypher" title="Royal cypher">royal cypher</a>) and is not a monogram.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monogram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer_Monogramm.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer_Monogramm.svg/100px-Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer_Monogramm.svg.png" decoding="async" width="100" height="81" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer_Monogramm.svg/150px-Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer_Monogramm.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer_Monogramm.svg/200px-Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer_Monogramm.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="416" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer_Monogramm.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The "AD" monogram that <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a> used as a signature</div></div></div>
<p>Monograms first appeared on coins, as early as 350BC. The earliest known examples are of the names of Greek cities which issued the coins, often the first two letters of the city's name. For example, the monogram of <a href="/wiki/Achaea_(ancient_region)" title="Achaea (ancient region)">Achaea</a> consisted of the letters <a href="/wiki/Alpha" title="Alpha">alpha</a> (Α) and <a href="/wiki/Chi_(letter)" title="Chi (letter)">chi</a> (Χ) joined together.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Monograms have been used as signatures by artists and <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">craftsmen</a> on paintings, sculptures and pieces of furniture, especially when <a href="/wiki/Guilds" class="mw-redirect" title="Guilds">guilds</a> enforced measures against unauthorized participation in the trade. A famous example of a monogram serving as an artist's signature is the "AD" used by <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Christograms">Christograms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monogram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Christograms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Christogram" title="Christogram">Christogram</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sarcophage_de_Drausin_03_chrisme.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Sarcophage_de_Drausin_03_chrisme.jpg/100px-Sarcophage_de_Drausin_03_chrisme.jpg" decoding="async" width="100" height="97" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Sarcophage_de_Drausin_03_chrisme.jpg/150px-Sarcophage_de_Drausin_03_chrisme.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Sarcophage_de_Drausin_03_chrisme.jpg/200px-Sarcophage_de_Drausin_03_chrisme.jpg 2x" data-file-width="715" data-file-height="690" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sarcophage_de_Drausin_03_chrisme.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Chi-Rho" class="mw-redirect" title="Chi-Rho">Chi-Rho</a>, a monogram of the first two letters of the Greek word for <a href="/wiki/Christ_(title)" title="Christ (title)">Christ</a></div></div></div>
<p>Over the centuries, monograms of the name of Jesus Christ have been used as <a href="/wiki/Christian_symbolism" title="Christian symbolism">Christian symbols</a>. The <a href="/wiki/IX_monogram" title="IX monogram">IX monogram</a> consists of the initial Greek letters of the name "Jesus Christ," "I" for Ιησούς, (<a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> in Greek) and "X" for Χριστος (<a href="/wiki/Christ_(title)" title="Christ (title)">Christ</a> in Greek). The "IHS" Christogram, denoting the first three letters of the Greek name of Jesus, is usually written as a cypher, but sometimes as a monogram.
</p><p>Perhaps the most significant <a href="/wiki/Christogram" title="Christogram">Christogram</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Chi_Rho" title="Chi Rho">Chi Rho</a>, formed from the first two letters of Χριστος. The symbol was used by the Roman emperor <a href="/wiki/Constantine_I_(emperor)" class="mw-redirect" title="Constantine I (emperor)">Constantine I</a> (r. 306–337) as part of a <a href="/wiki/Military_standard" class="mw-redirect" title="Military standard">military standard</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_monograms">Royal monograms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monogram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Royal monograms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Signum_manus_of_Charlemagne.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Signum_manus_of_Charlemagne.svg/170px-Signum_manus_of_Charlemagne.svg.png" decoding="async" width="170" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Signum_manus_of_Charlemagne.svg/255px-Signum_manus_of_Charlemagne.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Signum_manus_of_Charlemagne.svg/340px-Signum_manus_of_Charlemagne.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="938" data-file-height="875" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Signum_manus_of_Charlemagne.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cross-signature of <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a></div></div></div>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Signum_manus" title="Signum manus">Signum manus</a></i> (sometimes also known as <i>Chrismon</i>) refers to the medieval practice, current from the <a href="/wiki/Merovingian_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Merovingian period">Merovingian period</a> until the 14th century in the <a href="/wiki/Frankish_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankish Empire">Frankish Empire</a> and its successors, of signing a document or charter with a special type of monogram or royal cypher.
</p><p>Monograms of the names of <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarchs</a> are used as part of the insignia of public organizations in kingdoms, such as on police badges. This indicates a connection to the ruler. However, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_cypher" title="Royal cypher">royal cypher</a>, so familiar on <a href="/wiki/Pillar_box" title="Pillar box">pillar boxes</a>, is not technically a monogram, since the letters are not combined.
</p><p>Royal monograms often appear on coins, frequently surmounted by a crown. Countries that have employed this device in the past include <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and many <a href="/wiki/List_of_historic_states_of_Germany" title="List of historic states of Germany">German states</a>. Today, several <a href="/wiki/Danish_krone" title="Danish krone">Danish coins</a> carry the monogram of <a href="/wiki/Margrethe_II_of_Denmark" title="Margrethe II of Denmark">Margrethe II</a>, while the current Norwegian 1 <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_krone" title="Norwegian krone">Krone coin</a> has the "H5" monogram of <a href="/wiki/Harald_V_of_Norway" title="Harald V of Norway">Harald V</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Obverse_and_reverse" title="Obverse and reverse">obverse</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The only countries using the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">Euro</a> to have a royal monogram as their <a href="/wiki/Identifying_marks_on_euro_coins" title="Identifying marks on euro coins">national identifying mark</a> are <a href="/wiki/Belgian_euro_coins" title="Belgian euro coins">Belgium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monegasque_euro_coins" class="mw-redirect" title="Monegasque euro coins">Monaco</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In Thailand, royal monograms appear on the individual flag for each major royal family member. 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Individual_monograms">Individual monograms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monogram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Individual monograms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dress_shirt_monogram_locations.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Dress_shirt_monogram_locations.svg/220px-Dress_shirt_monogram_locations.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="311" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Dress_shirt_monogram_locations.svg/330px-Dress_shirt_monogram_locations.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Dress_shirt_monogram_locations.svg/440px-Dress_shirt_monogram_locations.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="744" data-file-height="1052" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dress_shirt_monogram_locations.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dress shirt monogram locations</div></div></div>
<p>An individual's monogram is often a very fancy piece of art used for stationery, for adorning luggage, for embroidery on clothing, and so forth. These monograms may have two or three letters.
</p><p>A basic 3-letter monogram has the initial of the individual's last name (surname) set larger, or with some special treatment in the center, while the first name initial appears to the left of it and the middle name initial appears to the right of it. There is a difference in how this is written for men and women. For example, if the individual's name is Mary Ann Jones, and Jones is the surname, then the arrangement of letters would be thus: <b>M<big>J</big>A</b>, with the surname initial set larger in the center, the <b>M</b> for Mary to the left and the <b>A</b> for Ann to the right.<sup id="cite_ref-3LM_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3LM-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Traditionally, individual monograms for men<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> are based on the order of the name. The name Kyle George Martin would be written (<b>KGM</b>).
</p><p>Married or engaged couples may use two-letter monograms of their entwined initials, for example on <a href="/wiki/Wedding_invitations" class="mw-redirect" title="Wedding invitations">wedding invitations</a>. Married couples may also create three-letter monograms incorporating the initial of their shared surname. For example, the monogram <b>M<big>J</big>A</b> might be used for Michael and Alice Jones.<sup id="cite_ref-3LM_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3LM-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> However, monogramming etiquette for the married couple varies according to the item being monogrammed. Linens, for example, typically list the woman's given initial first, followed by the couple's shared surname initial and then the man's given initial (<b>A<big>J</big>M</b>).
Monograms can often be found on custom dress shirts where they can be located in a number of different positions.
</p><p>Some personal monograms have become famous symbols in their own right and instantly recognizable to many, such as <a href="/wiki/J.R.R._Tolkien" class="mw-redirect" title="J.R.R. Tolkien">J.R.R. Tolkien</a>'s monogram.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_monograms">Other monograms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monogram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other monograms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:112px;"><a href="/wiki/File:UT%26T_text_logo.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/UT%26T_text_logo.svg/110px-UT%26T_text_logo.svg.png" decoding="async" width="110" height="134" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/UT%26T_text_logo.svg/165px-UT%26T_text_logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/UT%26T_text_logo.svg/220px-UT%26T_text_logo.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="90" data-file-height="110" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:UT%26T_text_logo.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="University of Texas at Austin">UT</a> monogram</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Logo" title="Logo">Logo</a></div>
<p>Some companies and organizations adopt a monogram for a logo, usually with the letters of their <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronym</a>. For example, as well as having an official <a href="/wiki/Seal_(emblem)" title="Seal (emblem)">seal</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Texas_Longhorns" title="Texas Longhorns">Texas Longhorns</a> logo, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="University of Texas at Austin">University of Texas at Austin</a> uses a "UT" monogram (in the same color as the Longhorns logo, <a href="/wiki/Burnt_orange" class="mw-redirect" title="Burnt orange">burnt orange</a>). The <a href="/wiki/New_York_Yankees" title="New York Yankees">New York Yankees</a> baseball team also uses a monogram on their <a href="/wiki/Ball_cap" class="mw-redirect" title="Ball cap">ball cap</a> insignia.  The <a href="/wiki/Consolidated_Edison" title="Consolidated Edison">Consolidated Edison</a> logo, with a rounded "E" nested inside a "C," has been described as a "classic emblem."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The "VOC" monogram logo of the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company" title="Dutch East India Company">Dutch East India Company</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Vereenigde_Oost-Indische_Compagnie" class="mw-redirect" title="Vereenigde Oost-Indische Compagnie">Vereenigde Oost-Indische Compagnie</a>" in Dutch) was possibly the first globally recognized <a href="/wiki/Corporate_logo" class="mw-redirect" title="Corporate logo">corporate logo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>]]
</p><p>Many fashion companies have a monogram for a logo, including <a href="/wiki/Louis_Vuitton" title="Louis Vuitton">Louis Vuitton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fendi" title="Fendi">Fendi</a>. The connected "CC" company logo, created by <a href="/wiki/Coco_Chanel" title="Coco Chanel">Coco Chanel</a>, is one of the most recognizable monograms internationally.
</p><p>Athletes have also been known to brand merchandise with their monogram logo; notably <a href="/wiki/Tiger_Woods" title="Tiger Woods">Tiger Woods</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roger_Federer" title="Roger Federer">Roger Federer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resistance_symbols_in_wartime">Resistance symbols in wartime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monogram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Resistance symbols in wartime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:62px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kotwica_symbol.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Kotwica_symbol.svg/60px-Kotwica_symbol.svg.png" decoding="async" width="60" height="79" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Kotwica_symbol.svg/90px-Kotwica_symbol.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Kotwica_symbol.svg/120px-Kotwica_symbol.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="425" data-file-height="560" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kotwica_symbol.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i lang="pl" title="Polish language text">Polska Walcząca</i> - "Fighting Poland"</div></div></div>
<p>A notable example of a royal monogram is the <a href="/wiki/H7_(monogram)" title="H7 (monogram)">H7</a> monogram of King <a href="/wiki/Haakon_VII_of_Norway" title="Haakon VII of Norway">Haakon VII of Norway</a>. While in exile during World War II, Haakon VII spearheaded the Norwegian resistance to the <a href="/wiki/Occupation_of_Norway_by_Nazi_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Occupation of Norway by Nazi Germany">German occupation</a>, and H7 became a symbol used by the Norwegian populace to mark solidarity and loyalty to the King, and adherence to the <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_resistance_movement" title="Norwegian resistance movement">Norwegian resistance movement</a>. The act of drawing or creating a H7 symbol in German-occupied Norway was punishable by imprisonment.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Similarly, in <a href="/wiki/Poland_in_World_War_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Poland in World War II">Poland</a> during the war, the "PW" monogram was used as a resistance symbol, known as 'The Anchor' (Polish: <i lang="pl"><a href="/wiki/Kotwica" title="Kotwica">Kotwica</a></i>), due to its characteristic shape. Its meaning varied, as the initials were useful for many different slogans, such as 'Poland Fights', 'Warsaw Uprising', 'Polish Army', and others. Like the Norwegian example above, its use was punished by the Nazi occupation authorities.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese">Japanese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Monogram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Japanese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Japanese_rebus_monogram" title="Japanese rebus monogram">Japanese rebus monogram</a></div>
<p>A Japanese rebus monogram is a monogram in a particular style, which spells a name via a <a href="/wiki/Rebus" title="Rebus">rebus</a>, as a form of <a href="/wiki/Japanese_wordplay" title="Japanese wordplay">Japanese wordplay</a> or <a href="/wiki/Visual_pun" title="Visual pun">visual pun</a>. Today they are most often seen in corporate logos or product logos.
</p>
