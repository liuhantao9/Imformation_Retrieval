<p>The <b>Federal Constitutional Court</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Bundesverfassungsgericht</i>; abbreviated: <i><i lang="de" title="German language text">BVerfG</i></i>) is the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_court" title="Supreme court">supreme</a> <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_court" title="Constitutional court">constitutional court</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Federal Republic of Germany</a>, established by the constitution or <a href="/wiki/Basic_Law_for_the_Federal_Republic_of_Germany" title="Basic Law for the Federal Republic of Germany">Basic Law</a> (<i><i lang="de" title="German language text">Grundgesetz</i></i>) of Germany. Since its inception with the beginning of the post-<a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> republic, the court has been located in the city of <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a>, which is also the seat of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Court_of_Justice" title="Federal Court of Justice">Federal Court of Justice</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The main task of the Federal Constitutional Court is <a href="/wiki/Judicial_review" title="Judicial review">judicial review</a>, and it may declare legislation <a href="/wiki/Constitutionality" title="Constitutionality">unconstitutional</a>, thus rendering them ineffective. In this respect, it is similar to other supreme courts with judicial review powers, yet the court possesses a number of additional powers, and is regarded as among the most interventionist and powerful national courts in the world. Unlike other <a href="/wiki/Supreme_court" title="Supreme court">supreme courts</a>, the constitutional court is not an integral stage of the judicial or appeals process (aside from cases concerning constitutional or public international law), and does not serve as a regular <a href="/wiki/Appellate_court" title="Appellate court">appellate court</a> from lower courts or the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_court#Germany" title="Supreme court">Federal Supreme Courts</a> on any violation of federal laws.
</p><p>The court's jurisdiction is focused on constitutional issues and the compliance of all governmental institutions with the constitution. Constitutional amendments or changes passed by the Parliament are subject to its judicial review, since they have to be compatible with the most basic principles of the <i><i lang="de" title="German language text">Grundgesetz</i></i> defined by the <a href="/wiki/Eternity_clause#Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Eternity clause">eternity clause</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scope">Scope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Constitutional_Court&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Scope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Basic Law of the Federal Republic of Germany stipulates that all three branches of the state (the legislature, executive, and judiciary) are bound directly by the constitution in Article 20, Section 3 of the document. As a result, the court can rule acts of any branches unconstitutional, whether as formal violations (<a href="/wiki/Ultra_vires" title="Ultra vires">exceeding powers</a> or violating procedures) or as material conflicts (when the civil rights prescribed in the <i>Grundgesetz</i> are not respected).
</p><p>The powers of the Federal Constitutional Court are defined in article 93 of the <i>Grundgesetz</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
This constitutional norm is set out in a federal law, the Federal Constitutional Court Act (BVerfGG), which also defines how decisions of the court on material conflicts are put into force. The Constitutional Court has therefore several strictly defined procedures in which cases may be brought before it:
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Verfassungsbeschwerde" title="Verfassungsbeschwerde">Constitutional complaint</a></b>: By means of the <i>Verfassungsbeschwerde</i> (constitutional complaint) any person may allege that his or her constitutional rights have been violated. Although only a small fraction of these are actually successful (ranging around 2.5% since 1951), several have resulted in major legislation being invalidated, especially in the field of taxation. The large majority of the court's procedures fall into this category; 135,968 such complaints were filed from 1957 to 2002.</li>
<li><b>Abstract regulation control</b>: Several political institutions, including the governments of the <i><a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">Bundesländer</a></i> (states), may bring a federal law before the court if they consider it unconstitutional. A well-known example of this procedure was the <a href="/wiki/German_Constitutional_Court_abortion_decision,_1975" title="German Constitutional Court abortion decision, 1975">1975 abortion decision</a>, which invalidated legislation intended to decriminalise <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a>.</li>
<li><b>Specific regulation control</b>: Any regular court which is convinced, that a law in question for a certain case is not in conformance with the constitution must suspend that case and bring this law before the Federal Constitutional Court.</li>
<li><b>Federal dispute</b>: Federal institutions, including members of the <a href="/wiki/Bundestag" title="Bundestag">Bundestag</a>, may bring internal disputes over competences and procedures before the court.</li>
<li><b>State–federal dispute</b>: The <i><a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">Länder</a></i> may bring disputes over competences and procedures between the states and federal institutions before the court.</li>
<li><b>Investigation committee control</b></li>
<li><b>Federal election scrutiny</b>: Violations of election laws may be brought before the court by political institution or any involved voter.</li>
<li><b>Impeachment procedure</b>: Impeachment proceedings may be brought against the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Germany" title="President of Germany">Federal President</a>, a judge, or a member of one of the Federal Supreme Courts, by the Bundestag, the <a href="/wiki/Bundesrat_of_Germany" title="Bundesrat of Germany">Bundesrat</a> or the federal government, based on violation of constitutional or federal law.</li>
<li><b>Prohibition of a political party</b>: Only the Constitutional Court has the power to ban a <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">political party</a> in Germany. This has happened just twice, both times in the 1950s: the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Reich_Party" title="Socialist Reich Party">Socialist Reich Party</a> (SRP), a <a href="/wiki/Neo-Nazism" title="Neo-Nazism">neo-Nazi</a> group, was banned in 1952, and the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Germany" title="Communist Party of Germany">Communist Party of Germany</a> (KPD) was banned in 1956. In the case of a third political party, the <a href="/wiki/National_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="National Democratic Party of Germany">National Democratic Party of Germany</a> (NPD) has been brought by many other political parties before the court on more than one occasion. Due to logistical reasons, however, all of these attempts have failed. The court opted to rule that NPD was constitutional in 2003 after it was learned that the German federal government had injected many of its officials as spies into the party for surveillance and security. Three judges objected to continuing which was sufficient as banning a party requires a two-thirds majority. The court itself did not choose to ban the party. In another instance, back in 2016, it was decided by the second senate of the Federal Constitutional Court that the appeal to ban NPD should be rejected because the party itself had such a small proportion of support and influence that it could be sufficiently argued that it was virtually nonexistent at any level of government in the country. Banning NPD was subsequently seen as pointless since it was believed that what few supporters that it had would either (a) proceed to form a new political party with a different title or (b) move to support the <a href="/wiki/Alternative_for_Germany" title="Alternative for Germany">Alternative for Germany Party</a> (AfD) - another far-right political party in Germany which is more popular than NPD and is quite arguably seen by many people as constitutionally antithetical. Despite this particular attempt at trying to ban the party, however, the Federal Constitutional Court did openly assert that NPD is unconstitutional due to the contents of its ideology and manifesto - even going as far as to say that the political party should not receive any state funding grants to garner support and influence for its causes. No further attempt has since been made to ban the National Democratic Party of Germany.</li></ul>
<p>Up to 2009, the Constitutional Court had struck down more than 600 laws as unconstitutional.<sup id="cite_ref-davids_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-davids-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Constitutional_Court&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The court consists of two senates, each of which has eight members, headed by a senate's chairman. The members of each senate are allocated to three chambers for hearings in constitutional complaint and single regulation control cases. Each chamber consists of three judges, so each senate chairman is at the same time a member of two chambers.
</p><p>Decisions by a senate require a majority. In some cases a two-thirds vote is required (§ 15 IV 1 BVerfGG). Decisions by a chamber need to be unanimous. A chamber is not authorized to overrule a standing precedent of the senate to which it belongs; such issues need to be submitted to the senate as a whole. Similarly, a senate may not overrule a standing precedent of the other senate, and such issues will be submitted to a plenary meeting of all 16 judges (the Plenum).
</p><p>Unlike all other German courts, the court often publishes the vote count on its decisions (though only the final tally, not every judge's personal vote) and even allows its members to issue a <a href="/wiki/Dissenting_opinion" title="Dissenting opinion">dissenting opinion</a>. This possibility, introduced only in 1971, is a remarkable deviation from German judicial tradition.
</p><p>One of the two senate chairmen is also the President of the court, the other one being the Vice President. The presidency alternates between the two senates, i.e. the successor of a President is always chosen from the other senate. The 9th and current president of the court is <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Vo%C3%9Fkuhle" title="Andreas Voßkuhle">Andreas Voßkuhle</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Democratic_function">Democratic function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Constitutional_Court&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Democratic function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="rquote pullquote floatright" role="presentation" style="border-collapse:collapse; border-style:none; float:right; margin:0.5em 0.75em; width:33%;">

<tbody><tr style="text-align:left; vertical-align:top;">
<td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:&#39;Times New Roman&#39;,serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">“
</td>
<td style="padding:0 10px;">[the] court is an important institution for the protection of <a href="/wiki/Minority_rights" title="Minority rights">minority interests</a><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</td>
<td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:&#39;Times New Roman&#39;,serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; text-align:right; vertical-align:bottom; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">”
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td class="rquotecite" colspan="3" style="padding-top:10px; font-size:smaller; line-height:normal; text-align:right;">
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The Constitutional Court is able to actively administer the law and ensure that political and bureaucratic decisions comply with the rights of the individual enshrined in the Basic Law. Specifically, it can vet the democratic and constitutional legitimacy of bills proposed by federal or state government, scrutinise decisions (such as those relating to taxation) by the administration, arbitrate disputes over the implementation of law between states and the federal government, and (most controversially) ban non-democratic political parties.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The Constitutional Court enjoys more public trust than the federal or state parliaments, which some say derives from the German enthusiasm for the rule of law.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appointment_of_judges">Appointment of judges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Constitutional_Court&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Appointment of judges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The court's judges are elected by the <a href="/wiki/Bundestag" title="Bundestag">Bundestag</a> (the German parliament) and the <a href="/wiki/Bundesrat_(Germany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bundesrat (Germany)">Bundesrat</a> (a legislative body that represents the sixteen state governments on the federal level). According to the <a href="/wiki/Basic_Law_for_the_Federal_Republic_of_Germany" title="Basic Law for the Federal Republic of Germany">Basic Law</a>, each of these bodies selects four members of each senate. The election of a judge requires a two-thirds vote. The selection of the chairman of each senate alternates between Bundestag and Bundesrat and also requires a two-thirds vote.
</p><p>Until 2015 the Bundestag has delegated this task to a special committee (<i>Richterwahlausschuss</i>, judges election committee), consisting of a small number of Bundestag members. This procedure has caused some constitutional concern and was considered to be unconstitutional by many scholars. In 2015 the <i>Bundesverfassungsgerichtsgesetz</i> (law code of the Federal Constitutional Court) was changed in this aspect, ruling that the Bundestag elects judges to the court by secret ballot in the plenum, requiring a candidate to get a two-thirds majority, that has to equal at least an absolute majority of members of the Bundestag. The <i>Richterwahlausschuss</i> now only has to nominate a candidate.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This new procedure was applied for the first time in September 2017, when <a href="/w/index.php?title=Josef_Christ&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Josef Christ (page does not exist)">Josef Christ</a> was elected to the first senate as the successor of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wilhelm_Schluckebier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wilhelm Schluckebier (page does not exist)">Wilhelm Schluckebier</a>.
In the Bundesrat, a chamber in which the governments of the sixteen German states are represented (each state has 3 to 6 votes depending on its population, which it has to cast <i>en bloc</i>), a candidate currently needs at least 46 of 69 possible votes.
</p><p>The judges are elected for a 12-year term, but they <a href="/wiki/Mandatory_retirement" title="Mandatory retirement">must retire</a> upon reaching the age of 68. A re-election is not possible. A judge must be at least 40 years old and must be a well-trained jurist. Three out of eight members of each senate have served as a judge on one of the federal courts. Of the other five members of each senate, most judges previously served as an academic jurist at a university, as a public servant or as a lawyer. After ending their term, most judges withdraw themselves from public life. However, there are some prominent exceptions, most notably <a href="/wiki/Roman_Herzog" title="Roman Herzog">Roman Herzog</a>, who was elected <a href="/wiki/President_of_Germany" title="President of Germany">President of Germany</a> in 1994, shortly before the end of his term as president of the court.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidents_of_the_court">Presidents of the court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Constitutional_Court&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Presidents of the court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The court's head is the President of the Federal Constitutional Court, who chairs one of the two senates and joint sessions of the court, while the other senate is chaired by the Vice President of Federal Constitutional Court. The right to elect the President and the Vice President alternates between the <a href="/wiki/Bundestag" title="Bundestag">Bundestag</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bundesrat_of_Germany" title="Bundesrat of Germany">Bundesrat</a>. If the President of the Federal Constitutional Court leaves office, i.e. when his or her term as judge at the court ends, the legislative body, whose turn it is to choose the President, has to elect one of the judges of the senate, of which the former President wasn't a member, with a two-thirds-majority. The same applies, if the office of Vice President falls vacant. The given legislative body is free to elect the judge it prefers, but since 1983, the sitting Vice President has always been elected as President.
</p><p>As he or she is the highest ranking representative of the judicial branch of government, the President of the Federal Constitutional Court ranks 5th in the <a href="/wiki/German_order_of_precedence" title="German order of precedence">German order of precedence</a>.

<th colspan="6"><b><a href="/wiki/Dignity" title="Dignity">Human dignity</a></b>
</th></tr>
<tr>
<td>1993
</td>
<td>2 BvF 2/90<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</td>
<td><i> (None) </i>
</td>
<td>The <a href="/wiki/Bundestag" title="Bundestag">federal lawmakers</a> permitted <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a> within twelve weeks after <a href="/wiki/Implantation_(human_embryo)" title="Implantation (human embryo)">implantation</a>. To be legal the expectant mother had to go to a pregnancy consultation minimum three days in advance and the abortion has to be their own decision.
</td>
<td>
<ul><li>If a pregnancy is not the result of a <a href="/wiki/Rape" title="Rape">criminal interaction</a> or a threat to the mother's life an abortion violates the <a href="/wiki/Right_to_life" title="Right to life">right to life</a> of an <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryo</a>. As a result, in the last case, abortion has to be prohibited.</li></ul>
</td>
<td>Following the decision the lawmakers changed the criminal law. They prohibited abortion within twelve weeks but after using a pregnancy consultation all participants go unpunished.
</td></tr>

</td>
<td>1 BvR 426/02<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>

<td>Benetton II
</td>
<td>The <a href="/wiki/Federal_Court_of_Justice" title="Federal Court of Justice">Federal Court of Justice</a> prohibited the <a href="/wiki/Stern_(magazine)" title="Stern (magazine)">magazine Stern</a> to publish a shocking advertisement of the <a href="/wiki/Benetton_Group" title="Benetton Group">Benetton Group</a>. The advertisement showed a bare gluteal with a stamp:”<a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV-positive</a>”
</td>
<td>
<ul><li>Human dignity is absolute. All <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_rights" title="Fundamental rights">fundamental rights</a> are substantiations of human dignity therefore there is no trade-off of human dignity and any fundamental right possible.</li></ul>
</td>
<td>The case was remanded to the Federal Court of Justice for a second time. After Benetton II the plaintiff abandoned the lawsuit. A final decision was unnecessary.
</td></tr>

<td>1 BvR 357/05<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>

<td>civil aviation security act decision

</th></tr>
<tr>
<td>1957
</td>
<td>1 BvR 253/56<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</td>
<td>Elfes-Decision (Elfes-Urteil)
</td>
<td>The left-wing party member of the centre-right <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_of_Germany" title="Christian Democratic Union of Germany">CDU</a> Wilhelm Elfes was accused of working against the constitution but therefor was never convicted. Based on this indictment he was denied a passport multiple times.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Elfes litigated against the decision.

<ul><li>The right to <a href="/wiki/Civil_liberties" title="Civil liberties">personal liberty</a> is to be construed in a broad way.</li>
<li>Invention of “Heck’s Formula” (named after the rapporteur of the case: Justice Heck). The court can only overview cases if one condition apply:</li></ul>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><ul><li>The impact of a constitutional norm was misjudged</li></ul></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><ul><li>Application of the law was discretionary</li></ul></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><ul><li>Violation of <a href="/wiki/Judicial_restraint" title="Judicial restraint">judicial restraint</a></li></ul></dd></dl></dd></dl>
</td>
<td>Elfes lost his specific case but the court cemented personal liberty in general. Justice Heck defined the limits of the court relative to the specialised court system.
</td></tr>

<td>1958
</td>
<td>1 BvR 400/51<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</td>
<td><a href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%BCth_Decision&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lüth Decision (page does not exist)">Lüth-Decision</a> (Lüth-Urteil)
</td>
<td>The court of Hamburg prohibited <a href="/wiki/Erich_L%C3%BCth" title="Erich Lüth">Erich Lüth</a> to call for boycott of the movie <a href="/wiki/Immortal_Beloved_(1951_film)" title="Immortal Beloved (1951 film)">Immortal Beloved</a>. Lüth justified his action because director <a href="/wiki/Veit_Harlan" title="Veit Harlan">Veit Harlan</a> also was responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Antisemitism" title="Antisemitism">antisemetic</a> movie <a href="/wiki/Jud_S%C3%BC%C3%9F_(1940_film)" title="Jud Süß (1940 film)">Jud Süß</a> in 1940.
</td>
<td>
<ul><li>German <a href="/wiki/Basic_Law_for_the_Federal_Republic_of_Germany" title="Basic Law for the Federal Republic of Germany">Basic Law</a> binds <a href="/wiki/Private_law" title="Private law">private law</a> indirectly</li>
<li>The Federal Constitutional Court is not a regular appellate court on violation of federal law. The court only overviews violation of the Basic Law</li></ul>
</td>
<td>With the Lüth-Decision the court defined and restricted its own power. But on the other hand it expanded the effective range of the Basic Law beyond the tension of government and people to the private law. The Basic Law does not bind citizens but it binds the lawmakers in creating private law and the <a href="/wiki/Judiciary" title="Judiciary">judiciary</a> in interpreting it.
</td></tr>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_European_constitutional_questions">Impact on European constitutional questions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Federal_Constitutional_Court&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Impact on European constitutional questions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 12 September 2012 the Court stated that the question of whether the <a href="/wiki/European_Central_Bank" title="European Central Bank">ECB</a>'s decision to finance European constituent nations through the purchase of bonds on the secondary markets was <a href="/wiki/Ultra_vires" title="Ultra vires">ultra vires</a> because it exceeded the limits established by the German act approving the <a href="/wiki/European_Stability_Mechanism" title="European Stability Mechanism">ESM</a> was to be examined.<sup id="cite_ref-eprs_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eprs-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  This demonstrates how a citizen's group has the ability to affect the conduct of European institutions. On 7 February 2014, the Court made a preliminary announcement on the case, which was to be published in full on 18 March. In its ruling, the Court decided to leave judgment to the Court of Justice of the EU (<a href="/wiki/Court_of_Justice_of_the_European_Union" title="Court of Justice of the European Union">CJEU</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-eprs_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eprs-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
