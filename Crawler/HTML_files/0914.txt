It is 17 miles (27&#160;km) north east of <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> and in 2011 had a population of 15,481.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Todmorden is at the confluence of three steep-sided <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennine</a> valleys and is surrounded by moorlands with outcrops of <a href="/wiki/Aeolian_processes" title="Aeolian processes">sandblasted</a> <a href="/wiki/Gritstone" title="Gritstone">gritstone</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">historic boundary</a> between <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a> is the <a href="/wiki/River_Calder,_West_Yorkshire" title="River Calder, West Yorkshire">River Calder</a> and its tributary, the Walsden Water, which run through the town. The administrative border was altered by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1888" title="Local Government Act 1888">Local Government Act 1888</a> placing the whole of the town within the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding</a>.
</p><p>The town is served by <a href="/wiki/Todmorden_railway_station" title="Todmorden railway station">Todmorden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walsden_railway_station" title="Walsden railway station">Walsden</a> railway stations.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Todmorden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponymy">Toponymy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Todmorden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Toponymy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The name Todmorden first appears in 1641. The town had earlier been called Tottemerden, Totmardene, Totmereden or Totmerden. The generally accepted meaning of the name is Totta's boundary-valley, probably a reference to the valley running north-west from the town.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Alternative suggestions have been proposed, such as the speculation "maybe fancifully" that the name derives from two words for death: <i>tod</i> and <i>mor</i> (as in <i>mort</i>), meaning "death-death-wood",<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> or that the name meant "marshy home of the fox", from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old English language">Old English</a>.
</p><p>'Tod' is an informal, shorthand name for Todmorden, often used in everyday conversation.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pagan_prehistory">Pagan prehistory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Todmorden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pagan prehistory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1898 Blackheath Barrow—a <a href="/wiki/Ring_cairn" title="Ring cairn">ring cairn</a> monument situated above Cross Stone in Todmorden<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>—was excavated and proved to be a site of "surpassing archaeological interest", according to J. Lawton Russell, one of the men who carried out the excavation.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  Various <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> items were discovered, including <a href="/wiki/Sepulchre" class="mw-redirect" title="Sepulchre">sepulchral</a> urns, a human skull, teeth and hands.
</p><p>Russell contended that Blackheath Barrow was primarily a religious site, specifically intended for the "performance of funeral rites", as there was no evidence that it had been settled for domestic use. Of particular interest were the four <a href="/wiki/Cairn" title="Cairn">cairns</a>, positioned at the <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_direction" title="Cardinal direction">cardinal</a> points of the compass, and it has been suggested that this indicates "a ritual evocation of the airts, or spirits of the four directions, with obvious correlates in relation to spirits in the land of the dead".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The various finds from the 1898 dig are now housed in the Todmorden Library, on permanent display.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Todmorden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tod_1800s.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Tod_1800s.jpg/200px-Tod_1800s.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="119" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Tod_1800s.jpg/300px-Tod_1800s.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Tod_1800s.jpg/400px-Tod_1800s.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1398" data-file-height="832" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tod_1800s.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Todmorden c.1870</div></div></div>
<p>The earliest written record of the area is in the <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> (1086).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Settlement in <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> Todmorden was dispersed. Most people living in scattered farms or in isolated hilltop agricultural settlements. <a href="/wiki/Packhorse" title="Packhorse">Packhorse</a> trails were marked by ancient stones of which many still survive.
</p><p>For hundreds of years streams from the surrounding hills provided water for corn and fulling mills. Todmorden grew to relative prosperity by combining farming with the production of woollen textiles. Some <a href="/wiki/Yeomen" class="mw-redirect" title="Yeomen">yeomen</a> clothiers were able to build fine houses, a few of which still exist today. Increasingly, though, the area's industry turned to cotton. The proximity of Manchester, as a source of material and trade, was undoubtedly a strong factor. Another was that the strong <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennine</a> streams and rivers were able to power the machine <a href="/wiki/Loom" title="Loom">looms</a>. Improvements in textile machinery (by <a href="/wiki/John_Kay_(flying_shuttle)" title="John Kay (flying shuttle)">Kay</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Hargreaves" title="James Hargreaves">Hargreaves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Arkwright" title="Richard Arkwright">Arkwright</a>), along with the development of <a href="/wiki/Turnpike_trust" class="mw-redirect" title="Turnpike trust">turnpike roads</a> (1751–1781), helped to develop the new cotton industry and to increase the local population.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">19th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Todmorden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 19th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1801 most people still lived in the uplands; Todmorden itself could be considered as a mere village.  During the years 1800–1845 great changes took place in the communications and transport of the town which were to have a crucial effect on promoting industrial growth. These included the building of: (1) better roads; (2) the <a href="/wiki/Rochdale_Canal" title="Rochdale Canal">Rochdale Canal</a> (1804); and (3) the main line of the <a href="/wiki/Manchester_and_Leeds_Railway" title="Manchester and Leeds Railway">Manchester and Leeds Railway</a> (1841), which became the <a href="/wiki/Lancashire_and_Yorkshire_Railway" title="Lancashire and Yorkshire Railway">Lancashire and Yorkshire Railway</a> in 1847. This railway line incorporated the (then) longest tunnel in the world, the 2,885-yard (2,638&#160;m) <a href="/wiki/Summit_Tunnel" title="Summit Tunnel">Summit Tunnel</a>.  A second railway, from Todmorden to Burnley, opened as a single line in 1849, being doubled to meet demand in 1860.  A short connecting line, from Stansfield Hall to Hall Royd, completed the "Todmorden Triangle" in 1862, thus enabling trains to travel in all three directions (Manchester, Leeds and Burnley) without reversing.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> caused a concentration of industry and settlement along the valley floor and a switch from woollens to cotton. One family in the area was particularly influential on the town; the Fielden family.  They created a "dynasty" that changed the town forever by establishing several large mills, putting up assorted impressive buildings and bringing about social and educational change.
</p><p>A double murder took place at Christ Church, Todmorden on 2 March 1868. The victims' graves lie in the churchyard.  Miles Weatherhill, a 23-year-old weaver from the town, was forbidden from seeing his housemaid sweetheart, Sarah Bell, by the Reverend Anthony John Plow. Armed with four pistols and an axe, Weatherhill took revenge first on the vicar and then on Jane Smith, another maid who had informed Plow of the secret meetings. Miss Smith died at the scene, while the vicar survived another week before succumbing to his injuries.  Weatherhill also seriously injured the vicar's wife. On 4 April 1868 Weatherhill became the last person to be publicly <a href="/wiki/Hanged" class="mw-redirect" title="Hanged">hanged</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, at the New Bailey prison.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Local legend has it that the face of a young woman is sometimes seen in the window of the vicarage, now in private ownership.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Todmorden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Throughout the first decade of the 20th&#160;century, the population of the Borough of Todmorden remained constant. The ten-yearly UK census returns show figures of 25,418 in 1901 and 25,404 in 1911. Like the rest of the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Calder_Valley" title="Upper Calder Valley">Upper Calder Valley</a>, Todmorden's economy experienced a slow decline from around the end of the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a> onwards, accelerating after the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> until around the late 1970s. During this period there was a painful restructuring of the local economy with the closure of mills and the demise of heavy industry.
</p><p>On 1 January 1907, Todmorden Corporation became only the second municipality in the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a> to operate a motor bus service.  By the end of that year, the fleet had expanded to five double-deck vehicles: two by Critchley-Norris, two by <a href="/wiki/Lancashire_Steam_Motor_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Lancashire Steam Motor Company">Lancashire Steam</a> (predecessor of <a href="/wiki/Leyland_Motors" title="Leyland Motors">Leyland Motors</a>) and one by <a href="/wiki/Ryknield_Motor_Company" title="Ryknield Motor Company">Ryknield</a>. In 1931, the service became jointly operated by the Corporation and the LMS railway under the name "Todmorden Joint Omnibus Committee". At its maximum size in the 1940s and 1950s, the undertaking operated 40 vehicles over 50 route miles (80&#160;km) through the rugged South Pennine terrain.
</p><p>Until 1938, the town was served by no fewer than six railway stations: <a href="/wiki/Todmorden_railway_station" title="Todmorden railway station">Todmorden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stansfield_Hall_railway_station" title="Stansfield Hall railway station">Stansfield Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornholme_railway_station" title="Cornholme railway station">Cornholme</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth_railway_station_(West_Yorkshire)" class="mw-redirect" title="Portsmouth railway station (West Yorkshire)">Portsmouth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walsden_railway_station" title="Walsden railway station">Walsden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastwood_(L%26Y)_railway_station" title="Eastwood (L&amp;Y) railway station">Eastwood</a>. With the exception of Todmorden railway station, all closed during the middle third of the 20th&#160;century although Walsden railway station reopened on 10 September 1990 on a site a few yards north of the original 1845 railway station. In December 1984 a goods train carrying petrol <a href="/wiki/Summit_tunnel_fire" class="mw-redirect" title="Summit tunnel fire">derailed in the Summit Tunnel</a> between Todmorden and <a href="/wiki/Littleborough,_Greater_Manchester" title="Littleborough, Greater Manchester">Littleborough</a> causing what is still considered as one of the biggest underground fires in transport history.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1980, Todmorden found itself at the centre of a celebrated murder enquiry. On 11 June that year police were called to J.W. Parker's coal yard in Todmorden after the discovery of a body, subsequently identified as 56-year-old Zigmund Adamski from <a href="/wiki/Tingley" title="Tingley">Tingley</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a>. The former coal miner had not been seen since setting out on a local shopping trip five days earlier. Although still wearing a suit, his shirt, watch and wallet were missing. A post mortem established that he died of a heart attack earlier that day, and discovered burns on his neck, shoulders and back of his head. These appeared to have been dressed by a green ointment, which toxicology tests were unable to identify.<sup id="cite_ref-adamski_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-adamski-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Adamski's case has never been solved, no suspect was ever arrested and in a television documentary the coroner, James Turnbull, described it as "one of the most puzzling cases I've come across in 25 years".<sup id="cite_ref-adamski_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-adamski-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Among the explanations to gain currency was that Adamski was the victim of <a href="/wiki/Alien_abduction" title="Alien abduction">extraterrestrial abduction</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> After intense media interest, the Todmorden police force were forbidden from talking further to the press about the case.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Todmorden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2008, a group of local residents initiated the <a href="/wiki/Incredible_Edible_Todmorden_project" class="mw-redirect" title="Incredible Edible Todmorden project">Incredible Edible Todmorden project</a> to raise awareness of food issues and in particular local food and food provenance.<sup id="cite_ref-IET_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IET-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The project has been responsible for the planting of 40 public fruit and vegetable gardens throughout the town, with each plot inviting passers-by to help themselves to the <a href="/wiki/Open-source_model" title="Open-source model">open source</a> produce.<sup id="cite_ref-IET_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IET-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  The project has attracted publicity, media attention and visitors and the idea has been replicated in at least fifteen towns and villages in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-IET_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IET-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Todmorden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Todcoatofarms.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/4/4f/Todcoatofarms.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="312" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="200" data-file-height="312" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Todcoatofarms.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Coat_of_Arms" class="mw-redirect" title="Coat of Arms">Coat of Arms</a> of the former Todmorden Borough Council.</div></div></div>
<p>Todmorden has a complex geo-administrative history. It lies along the <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">historic county boundary</a> of <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>.
</p><p>Until the boundary reformation by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1888" title="Local Government Act 1888">Local Government Act 1888</a>, the Lancashire-Yorkshire boundary ran through the centre of Todmorden, following the River Calder to the north-west and the Walsden Water for less than 1&#160;mi (1.6&#160;km) to the south before turning south-eastwards across Langfield Common. The Town Hall, which was presented to Todmorden by the Fielden family and opened in 1875, straddles the Walsden Water; thus, from 1875 to 1888 it was possible to dance in the Town Hall ballroom, forward and back, across two <a href="/wiki/Counties_of_England" title="Counties of England">counties of England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1894" title="Local Government Act 1894">Local Government Act 1894</a>, the Todmorden Local Board became an Urban District Council, comprising the wards of Todmorden, Walsden, Langfield and Stansfield. At the same time, Todmorden Rural District Council, comprising the parishes of Blackshaw, Erringden, Heptonstall and Wadsworth, came into being. Two years later, on 2 June 1896, the town was granted a Charter of Incorporation and the area covered by the Urban District Council became a <a href="/wiki/Municipal_borough" title="Municipal borough">municipal borough</a>. The number of wards was increased from four to six: Central, Walsden, Langfield, Stansfield, Stoodley and Cornholme. Todmorden Rural District was later renamed <a href="/wiki/Hepton_Rural_District" title="Hepton Rural District">Hepton Rural District</a>.  Since the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">local government reforms of 1974</a>, Todmorden has been administered as part of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Borough" class="mw-redirect" title="Metropolitan Borough">Metropolitan Borough</a> of <a href="/wiki/Calderdale" title="Calderdale">Calderdale</a>, within the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_county" title="Metropolitan county">Metropolitan county</a> of <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>. At the local government level, Todmorden, the town, is almost entirely within Todmorden ward<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> although the eastern portion of the town toward Eastwood shares some of adjoining Calder ward<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> with Hebden Bridge.
</p><p>Until 1996, when <a href="/wiki/Postal_counties_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Postal counties of the United Kingdom">postal counties</a> were abolished, Todmorden's official postal county was Lancashire.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Other villages and towns in the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Calder_Valley" title="Upper Calder Valley">Upper Calder Valley</a> include <a href="/wiki/Hebden_Bridge" title="Hebden Bridge">Hebden Bridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mytholmroyd" title="Mytholmroyd">Mytholmroyd</a>. The territory of the <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> of Todmorden also extends to cover <a href="/wiki/Eastwood,_West_Yorkshire" title="Eastwood, West Yorkshire">Eastwood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walsden" title="Walsden">Walsden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cornholme" title="Cornholme">Cornholme</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mankinholes" title="Mankinholes">Mankinholes</a>, Lumbutts, Robinwood, Lydgate, Portsmouth, Shade, Stansfield, Dobroyd, Ferney Lee, Gauxholme and Cross Stone.
</p><p>Medieval Todmorden had consisted of the <a href="/wiki/Township_(England)" title="Township (England)">townships</a> of Langfield and Stansfield in Yorkshire, and Todmorden/Walsden section of the greater township of <a href="/wiki/Hundersfield" title="Hundersfield">Hundersfield</a> in the Ancient Parish of Rochdale, Lancashire.  The township of Todmorden and Walsden was created in 1801 by the union of the older villages of Todmorden and Walsden.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Todmorden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Weaving_shed,_Queen_Street_Mill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_680867.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Weaving_shed%2C_Queen_Street_Mill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_680867.jpg/220px-Weaving_shed%2C_Queen_Street_Mill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_680867.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Weaving_shed%2C_Queen_Street_Mill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_680867.jpg/330px-Weaving_shed%2C_Queen_Street_Mill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_680867.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Weaving_shed%2C_Queen_Street_Mill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_680867.jpg/440px-Weaving_shed%2C_Queen_Street_Mill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_680867.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="429" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Weaving_shed,_Queen_Street_Mill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_680867.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A typical <a href="/wiki/Weaving_shed" title="Weaving shed">weaving shed</a> at <a href="/wiki/Queen_Street_Mill" title="Queen Street Mill">Queen Street Mill</a> Textile Museum, Burnley</div></div></div>
<p>Heavy industry is now part of Todmorden's history, not its present. The industrial chimneys have largely gone and the remaining <a href="/wiki/Mill_(grinding)" title="Mill (grinding)">mills</a> have mostly been converted for other purposes. The town's industrial base is much reduced (at one time Todmorden had the largest weaving shed in the world).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> There has been a great deal of regeneration activity and Todmorden is now increasingly a <a href="/wiki/Commuter_town" title="Commuter town">commuter town</a> for people working in <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a> and smaller towns. Todmorden also services the local rural area and attracts visitors through its <a href="/wiki/Todmorden_Markets" title="Todmorden Markets">market</a> (indoor and outdoor), various events, heritage and the local Pennine countryside. Changing work patterns may have influenced the fact that the town was the first rural telephone exchange in Britain to be broadband-enabled through public demand. Rising house prices over recent years are a particular problem as there is limited land available in the valley for building <a href="/wiki/Affordable_housing" title="Affordable housing">affordable housing</a>. It has for centuries been considered the safest accessible route directly across the Pennines.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Landmarks">Landmarks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Todmorden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Landmarks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Todmorden_Town_Hall.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Todmorden_Town_Hall.jpg/220px-Todmorden_Town_Hall.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Todmorden_Town_Hall.jpg/330px-Todmorden_Town_Hall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Todmorden_Town_Hall.jpg/440px-Todmorden_Town_Hall.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Todmorden_Town_Hall.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Todmorden Town Hall</div></div></div>
<p>Todmorden has a <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">Neo-Classical</a> town hall (built 1866–1875) which dominates the centre of the town. The building straddles the Walsden Water, a tributary of the River Calder, and was situated in both Lancashire and Yorkshire until the administrative county boundary was moved on 1 January 1888. Designed by John Gibson of Westminster, this imposing building has a northern end which is semi-circular. One interesting external feature of the town hall is the pediment to the front elevation, which reflects the fact that it straddled the boundary as it depicts the main industries of the two counties. The fine carved stonework has two central female figures on a pedestal. The left-hand sculpture represents Lancashire (cotton spinning and weaving industries), and the right-hand one Yorkshire (wool manufacturing, engineering and agriculture).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Todmorden_Market_Hall_(29th_August_2010).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Todmorden_Market_Hall_%2829th_August_2010%29.jpg/170px-Todmorden_Market_Hall_%2829th_August_2010%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Todmorden_Market_Hall_%2829th_August_2010%29.jpg/255px-Todmorden_Market_Hall_%2829th_August_2010%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Todmorden_Market_Hall_%2829th_August_2010%29.jpg/340px-Todmorden_Market_Hall_%2829th_August_2010%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1253" data-file-height="1966" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Todmorden_Market_Hall_(29th_August_2010).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Todmorden Market Hall</div></div></div>
<p>Todmorden has the look of a <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian</a> mill town. Other notable buildings include <a href="/wiki/Dobroyd_Castle" title="Dobroyd Castle">Dobroyd Castle</a> (completed in 1869), now used as a residential activity centre for schoolchildren; the <a href="/wiki/Edwardian_era" title="Edwardian era">Edwardian</a> Hippodrome Theatre, and the <a href="/wiki/Grade_I_listed" class="mw-redirect" title="Grade I listed">Grade I listed</a> <a href="/wiki/Todmorden_Unitarian_Church" title="Todmorden Unitarian Church">Todmorden Unitarian Church</a> (built 1865–1869). Dobroyd Castle, the town hall and the <a href="/wiki/Todmorden_Unitarian_Church" title="Todmorden Unitarian Church">Unitarian church</a> were all built at the behest of <a href="/wiki/John_Fielden" title="John Fielden">John Fielden</a> and his sons and designed by <a href="/wiki/John_Gibson_(architect)" title="John Gibson (architect)">John Gibson</a>, who had been a member of <a href="/wiki/Charles_Barry" title="Charles Barry">Charles Barry</a>'s team at the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Houses of Parliament</a>. Pre-Victorian buildings include two 18th&#160;century <a href="/wiki/Pub" title="Pub">pubs</a>; Todmorden Old Hall, a <a href="/wiki/Grade_II*_listed" class="mw-redirect" title="Grade II* listed">Grade II* listed</a> manor house (<a href="/wiki/Elizabethan_architecture" title="Elizabethan architecture">Elizabethan</a>) in the centre of town, and St. Mary's Church which dates from 1476.
</p><p>Todmorden is situated alongside the <a href="/wiki/Pennine_Way" title="Pennine Way">Pennine Way</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennine_Bridleway" title="Pennine Bridleway">Pennine Bridleway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Towneley_Loop" title="Mary Towneley Loop">Mary Towneley Loop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calderdale_Way" title="Calderdale Way">Calderdale Way</a> and is popular for outdoor activities such as <a href="/wiki/Walking" title="Walking">walking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fell_running" title="Fell running">fell running</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mountain_biking" title="Mountain biking">mountain biking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bouldering" title="Bouldering">bouldering</a>. Its attractions include canals and locks, a park containing a sports centre, an outdoor skateboard park, tennis courts, a golf course, an aquarium/reptile house and a cricket ground. There are wooded areas around the town and cafés and restaurants.
The Hippodrome Theatre shows films as well as putting on live performances. The town has a small toy and model museum, a library and a tourist information centre, along with independent retailers. Annual events include a carnival, agricultural show, beer festival, music festival and the traditional Easter <a href="/wiki/Pace_Egg_plays" class="mw-redirect" title="Pace Egg plays">Pace Egg plays</a>.
</p><p>Centre Vale Park in Todmorden is the setting for several pieces of local art, including tree carvings by the sculptor John Adamson,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and an iconic bandstand. The bandstand is unique in construction and is designed in an arc shape because this gives it better acoustics. It opened in 1914 but is set to be demolished.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Also in the park are the reconstructed remains of Centre Vale Mansion, next to <a href="/wiki/Todmorden_War_Memorial" title="Todmorden War Memorial">Todmorden War Memorial</a> in the Garden of Remembrance, and nearby there is a sculpture of a dog. This was sculpted by local sculptor David Wynne<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> in 2005, and was cast in steel at the local Todmorden foundry Weir Minerals. It was donated to the park by the sculptor and the foundry, but installation was delayed for several years due to the extensive flood alleviation works. In 2011, the dog was featured on an episode of <a href="/wiki/Derren_Brown#Derren_Brown:_The_Experiments_(2011)" title="Derren Brown">Derren Brown's</a> <i>The Experiments</i>. Brown spread a rumour that the dog was lucky; it then gained a reputation for bringing luck to anyone that touched it. During the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a> the mansion was used as a military hospital.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Centre Vale Park has hosted a parkrun since 9 March 2019.
</p><p>The 120 feet (37&#160;m) <a href="/wiki/Stoodley_Pike" title="Stoodley Pike">Stoodley Pike</a> monument (built 1814 and rebuilt in 1854) stands atop the 1,300 feet (400&#160;m) hill of the same name. It commemorates the defeat of <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a> and the surrender of <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>. It is a prominent feature of Todmorden's moors, and is a landmark on the <a href="/wiki/Pennine_Way" title="Pennine Way">Pennine Way</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Todmorden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Todmorden has been used as a location for the 1980s BBC TV police drama <i><a href="/wiki/Juliet_Bravo" title="Juliet Bravo">Juliet Bravo</a></i>, Territorial Army series <i><a href="/wiki/All_Quiet_on_the_Preston_Front" title="All Quiet on the Preston Front">All Quiet on the Preston Front</a></i>, parts of <i><a href="/wiki/The_League_of_Gentlemen" title="The League of Gentlemen">The League of Gentlemen</a></i>, BBC TV miniseries <i><a href="/wiki/Oranges_Are_Not_the_Only_Fruit_(TV_serial)" title="Oranges Are Not the Only Fruit (TV serial)">Oranges Are Not the Only Fruit</a></i>, the award-winning BBC1 series <i><a href="/wiki/Life_on_Mars_(UK_TV_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Life on Mars (UK TV series)">Life on Mars</a></i>, a town in the book Spooks Blood and a <a href="/wiki/My_Summer_of_Love" title="My Summer of Love">film adaptation</a> of the novel <i><a href="/wiki/My_Summer_of_Love_(novel)" title="My Summer of Love (novel)">My Summer of Love</a></i>. The BBC One crime drama series <i><a href="/wiki/Happy_Valley_(TV_series)" title="Happy Valley (TV series)">Happy Valley</a></i>, written by <a href="/wiki/Sally_Wainwright" title="Sally Wainwright">Sally Wainwright</a> (who grew up in nearby Sowerby Bridge<sup id="cite_ref-YorkshirePost-ScottBailey-2011_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YorkshirePost-ScottBailey-2011-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>), is filmed in and around the town, amongst other locations. In the 1980s the town was used for two consecutive episodes of BBC fashion series The Clothes Show.
</p><p>Todmorden featured in the ITV paranormal show "Strange But True" which in their pilot episode from May 1993 investigated the UFO claims in the Todmorden area. The programme included a closed surgery in which <a href="/wiki/Harold_Shipman" title="Harold Shipman">Harold Shipman</a> worked for a number of years, as well as the town hall (haunted by a grey lady), and Oddfellows Hall (known as Baxter's bar), which is haunted by a builder who died in the construction of the building in 1811.
</p><p>Before May 2009, the links to Lancashire and the North West were also seen in the media with Todmorden receiving an analogue TV signal from <a href="/wiki/BBC_North_West" title="BBC North West">BBC North West</a>. The local television transmitter relayed <a href="/wiki/BBC_One" title="BBC One">BBC One</a> and <a href="/wiki/BBC_Two" title="BBC Two">BBC Two</a> to the Todmorden area, however <a href="/wiki/ITV_(TV_network)" title="ITV (TV network)">ITV</a> and <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a> was different and has always been relayed from Emley Moor (via Cornholme) which broadcasts <a href="/wiki/ITV_Yorkshire" title="ITV Yorkshire">ITV Yorkshire</a>. Since 2009 the majority of services were relayed via Cornholme although some parts of Walsden retained their television signal from the North West.
</p><p>In February 2010, Todmorden featured in the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> Radio 4 programme "Costing the Earth: The New Diggers".<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Members of a guerrilla gardening group spoke about reclaiming unused land for growing vegetables, how this helps the local community and how it can be a driver for change.
</p><p>In November 2011, Todmorden featured in the <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a> programme <i>The Secret of Luck</i>, in which <a href="/wiki/Derren_Brown" title="Derren Brown">Derren Brown</a> sought to convince the town that the dog statue in Centre Vale Park brought good luck.
</p><p>In September 2010 Todmorden received a visit from <a href="/wiki/Prince_Charles" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince Charles">Prince Charles</a> (his second visit to the town) who came to support Mary Clear's <a href="/wiki/Incredible_Edible_Todmorden_project" class="mw-redirect" title="Incredible Edible Todmorden project">Incredible Edible Todmorden project</a>. This featured on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Yorkshire" title="BBC Yorkshire">BBC Yorkshire</a>.
</p><p>Todmorden's local <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">newspaper</a> is the <i>Todmorden News</i> owned by <a href="/wiki/Johnston_Press" title="Johnston Press">Johnston Press</a>, now merged (since October 2015<sup id="cite_ref-twitter-todnews-merger_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-twitter-todnews-merger-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>) with the <i>Hebden Bridge Times</i> from the neighbouring town by the same publisher.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Singletrack_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Singletrack Magazine">Singletrack Magazine</a></i>, a national <a href="/wiki/Mountain_biking" title="Mountain biking">mountain biking</a> magazine, is based in Todmorden.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics_and_Law">Politics and Law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Todmorden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Politics and Law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Fielden" title="John Fielden">John Fielden</a> (1784–1849), land and factory owner in Todmorden and scion of the town's Fielden family, was a Member of Parliament and national leader of the Ten Hours Campaign for factory reform.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Samuel_Fielden" title="Samuel Fielden">Samuel Fielden</a> (1847–1922), socialist, anarchist and labour activist who was one of the eight convicted in the 1886 <a href="/wiki/Haymarket_affair" title="Haymarket affair">Haymarket affair</a> in Chicago. He was sentenced to death along with six other defendants, but after writing to the Illinois Governor asking for clemency his sentence was commuted to life imprisonment in November 1887. He spent six years in prison before being pardoned, along with two other co-defendants, in 1893. He died in Colorado in 1922 and is buried in La Veta (Pioneer) Cemetery, Huerfano County, Colorado alongside his wife and two children.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Wilfred_Judson" title="Wilfred Judson">Wilfred Judson</a>, a justice of the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Canada" title="Supreme Court of Canada">Supreme Court of Canada</a>, was born in and spent his early youth in Todmorden.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Rebecca_Taylor_(politician)" title="Rebecca Taylor (politician)">Rebecca Taylor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrat</a> <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">MEP</a> for <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_and_the_Humber_(European_Parliament_constituency)" title="Yorkshire and the Humber (European Parliament constituency)">Yorkshire and the Humber</a> from 8 March 2012 to 2 July 2014.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arts_and_culture">Arts and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Todmorden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Arts and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Geoff_Crowther" class="mw-redirect" title="Geoff Crowther">Geoff Crowther</a> (born 1944), independent travel guide writer, founding editor of <a href="/wiki/BIT_(alternative_information_centre)" title="BIT (alternative information centre)">BIT travel guides</a>, London (1972–1980): the first guidebooks to cover the <a href="/wiki/Hippie_trail" title="Hippie trail">Hippie trail</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Crowther went on to be a leading author for <a href="/wiki/Lonely_Planet" title="Lonely Planet">Lonely Planet</a> (1977–1995).<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fred_Lawless" title="Fred Lawless">Fred Lawless</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> born theatre playwright has a house in Todmorden; he was also a writer for the <a href="/wiki/BBC_1" class="mw-redirect" title="BBC 1">BBC 1</a> TV series <i><a href="/wiki/EastEnders" title="EastEnders">EastEnders</a></i>, as well as several other TV and radio programmes.
</p><p>Todmorden actress <a href="/wiki/Claire_Benedict" title="Claire Benedict">Claire Benedict</a> has appeared in UK TV shows <i>Waking The Dead</i>, <i>Prime Suspect</i>, <i>Unforgiven</i>, <i>Holby City</i>, <i>Casualty</i>, <i>Doctors</i>, <i>Grange Hill</i>, <i>The Bill</i> and the <i>Lenny Henry Show</i>. She featured in the films <i>Felicia's Journey</i>, <i>Sea Sick</i> and <i>Mersinias</i>, and has had numerous theatre roles, including work for the National Theatre and Royal Shakespeare Company. On BBC radio she is the voice of <a href="/wiki/Precious_Ramotswe" class="mw-redirect" title="Precious Ramotswe">Precious Ramotswe</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_No.1_Ladies_Detective_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="The No.1 Ladies Detective Agency">The No.1 Ladies Detective Agency</a></i>.
</p><p>Todmorden-born actor <a href="/wiki/Dicken_Ashworth" title="Dicken Ashworth">Dicken Ashworth</a> appeared in <i>Coronation Street</i> and <i>Brookside</i>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Antony_Booth" class="mw-redirect" title="Antony Booth">Antony Booth</a>, actor, father of <a href="/wiki/Cherie_Blair" title="Cherie Blair">Cherie Blair</a> and father-in-law of former Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a>, resided in Todmorden.
</p><p>Manchester-born <a href="/wiki/Becky_Simpson" title="Becky Simpson">Becky Simpson</a> is an award-winning actress. As a 10-year-old child she starred as <a href="/wiki/Spoonface_Steinberg" title="Spoonface Steinberg">Spoonface Steinberg</a> in the BBC production by that name written by writer <a href="/wiki/Lee_Hall_(playwright)" title="Lee Hall (playwright)">Lee Hall</a>, famous for writing <i><a href="/wiki/Billy_Elliot" title="Billy Elliot">Billy Elliot</a></i>. Becky is married to <a href="/wiki/Wes_Paul" title="Wes Paul">Wes Paul</a> notable <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll" class="mw-redirect" title="Rock and Roll">Rock and Roll</a> lead singer with the Wes Paul Band; they are tenants of the Grade-I-listed lodge inside the gates of <a href="/wiki/Todmorden_Unitarian_Church" title="Todmorden Unitarian Church">Todmorden Unitarian Church</a> and are both members of the local management committee.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The Bayes family of artists were prominent in the 19th and 20th&#160;centuries. They were: Alfred Bayes (1832–1909), painter; <a href="/wiki/Walter_Bayes" title="Walter Bayes">Walter Bayes</a> (1869–1856), painter; <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_Bayes" title="Gilbert Bayes">Gilbert Bayes</a> (1872–1952), sculptor; and <a href="/wiki/Jessie_Bayes" title="Jessie Bayes">Jessie Bayes</a> (1876–1970), painter (some of her work can be see at Lumbutts Methodist Church, Lumbutts, Todmorden).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/William_Holt_(writer_and_artist)" class="mw-redirect" title="William Holt (writer and artist)">William Holt</a> (1897–1977) was a writer, painter, political activist, journalist and traveller. William was often seen riding his white horse Trigger around Todmorden and other local areas.
</p><p>Keyboardist <a href="/wiki/Keith_Emerson" title="Keith Emerson">Keith Emerson</a> (1944-2016), founder member of UK prog-rock groups <a href="/wiki/The_Nice" title="The Nice">The Nice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emerson,_Lake_%26_Palmer" title="Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer">Emerson, Lake &amp; Palmer</a>, was born in the town while the family was evacuated from the south coast.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/John_Helliwell" title="John Helliwell">John Helliwell</a>, another Todmorden-born musician, was saxophonist in the band <a href="/wiki/Supertramp" title="Supertramp">Supertramp</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dale_Hibbert" class="mw-redirect" title="Dale Hibbert">Dale Hibbert</a>, original bass player with The Smiths, author of "Boy Interrupted".
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Geoff_Love" title="Geoff Love">Geoff Love</a> (1917–1991), the big band leader, was born in Todmorden.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/John_Kettley" title="John Kettley">John Kettley</a> (born 1952), the former BBC weatherman, grew up in Todmorden.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Tim_Benjamin" title="Tim Benjamin">Tim Benjamin</a> (born 1975), the composer, lives in Todmorden, and the world premiere of his opera <i>Emily</i> was given at the town's Hippodrome Theatre in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport_2">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Todmorden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>England Test cricketers <a href="/wiki/Peter_Lever" title="Peter Lever">Peter Lever</a> (born 1940) and <a href="/wiki/Derek_Shackleton" title="Derek Shackleton">Derek Shackleton</a> (1924–2007) were originally from Todmorden.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Todmorden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Harold_Shipman" title="Harold Shipman">Harold Shipman</a>, the <a href="/wiki/General_Practitioner" class="mw-redirect" title="General Practitioner">General Practitioner</a> who is believed to have killed over 200 patients in the 1970s, 1980s and 1990s, claimed at least one of his victims while working as a doctor at the Abraham Ormerod Medical Centre between March 1974 and September 1975.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> His first known victim, 70-year-old Eva Lyons, lived at Keswick Close in the town. Shipman had initially been charged with 15 murders committed around <a href="/wiki/Hyde,_Greater_Manchester" title="Hyde, Greater Manchester">Hyde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester" title="Greater Manchester">Greater Manchester</a>, between 1995 and 1998 when he went on trial in late 1999, but Lyons was only identified as a victim of Shipman when the inquiry into his crimes was completed in July 2002 by <a href="/wiki/Janet_Smith_(judge)" title="Janet Smith (judge)">Dame Janet Smith</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 29 November 1980 police officer <a href="/wiki/Alan_Godfrey" title="Alan Godfrey">Alan Godfrey</a> allegedly experienced an <a href="/wiki/Alien_abduction" title="Alien abduction">alien abduction</a>, described in <a href="/wiki/Jenny_Randles" title="Jenny Randles">Jenny Randles</a>' 1983 book <i>The Pennine UFO Mystery</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-randles_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-randles-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-forteantimes_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forteantimes-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
