	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Cornish surnames</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>

<b>Cornish surnames</b> are surnames used by <a href="/wiki/Cornish_people" title="Cornish people">Cornish people</a> and often derived from the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a> such as Jago, Trelawney or Enys. Others have strong roots in the region and many in the UK with names such as Eddy, Stark or Rowe are likely to have Cornish origins. Such surnames for the common people emerged in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, although the nobility probably had surnames much earlier on. Not until the later <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> did it become necessary for a common man to have a surname. Most surnames were fully established throughout <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> by the end of the 15th century.<sup id="cite_ref-cornishsurnames1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cornishsurnames1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Today Cornish surnames can be found throughout the world as part of the <a href="/wiki/Cornish_diaspora" title="Cornish diaspora">Cornish diaspora</a>.
</p><p>Due to the linguistic similarity of Cornish, Welsh and Breton, some surnames can derive from any of the three regions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patronymics">Patronymics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Patronymics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most common surnames in Cornwall are derived from <a href="/wiki/Patronymics" class="mw-redirect" title="Patronymics">patronymics</a>, the father’s first name being taken either without alteration, for example 'John', or with the addition of genitive '-s' or, typically Cornish, '-o', e.g. 'Bennetto' or '-y' as in 'Pawley'.<sup id="cite_ref-cornishsurnames1_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cornishsurnames1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cornish_surnames_deriving_from_the_Cornish_language">Cornish surnames deriving from the Cornish language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cornish surnames deriving from the Cornish language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Placenames">Placenames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Placenames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The phrase <b><a href="/wiki/Tre_Pol_and_Pen" class="mw-redirect" title="Tre Pol and Pen">Tre, Pol and Pen</a></b> is used to describe people from, or places in, <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, the United Kingdom. Carew has <i>By Tre, Pol and Pen / You shall know the Cornishmen</i>; however, <a href="/wiki/William_Camden" title="William Camden">Camden</a> records the rhyme as <i>By Tre, Ros, Pol, Lan, Caer and Pen / You may know the most Cornishmen</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>Tre</i> – a settlement or homestead</li>
<li><i>Ros(e)</i> – heath, moor</li>
<li><i>Pol</i> – a pond, lake or well</li>
<li><i>Lan</i> – a religious enclosure</li>
<li><i>Car</i> – either from <i>karn</i> "<a href="/wiki/Tor_(rock_formation)" title="Tor (rock formation)">tor</a>" or <i>ker</i> "hill-fort"</li>
<li><i>Pen(n)</i>, <i>Pedn</i> – a hill or headland</li></ul>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Cornish_people" title="Cornish people">Cornish</a> surnames and place names still retain these words as prefixes, such as the names <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Trelawny</a> or <a href="/wiki/Trevithick" title="Trevithick">Trevithick</a> and the towns of <a href="/wiki/Polperro" title="Polperro">Polperro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polkerris" title="Polkerris">Polkerris</a> and of course <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a>. "Carbines" derives from <i>karn byghan</i> "little <a href="/wiki/Tor_(rock_formation)" title="Tor (rock formation)">tor</a>".
</p><p>Caution should be exercised with the derivation of "Car-" surnames as there seems also to be fusion with names containing the word <i>ker</i> "hill-fort", as in "Carvosso" <i>ker fosow</i> "walled hill-fort", a placename found in <a href="/wiki/Ludgvan" title="Ludgvan">Ludgvan</a>. There is also <i>karrek</i> "rocky". "Rosdew" is <i>ros du</i> "black moor" and "Ros(e)warne" from <i>ros (g)wern</i> "alder heath" or perhaps "heath by an alder-marsh". "Landry" means <i>lan dre</i> "enclosure of farmhouse or church-house".
</p><p>Other examples of place-names used as surnames:
</p>
<ul><li>Gwavas" <i>gwavos</i> "winter residence or <a href="/wiki/Bothy" title="Bothy">bothy</a>"- an area near <a href="/wiki/Penzance" title="Penzance">Penzance</a> and also the surname of a well-known Cornish family (<a href="/wiki/William_Gwavas" title="William Gwavas">William Gwavas</a>)</li>
<li>Hammett: "summer bothy"; from Old Cornish <i>hav bot</i> (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">havos</i>, <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>: <i lang="cy"><a href="/wiki/Hafod" title="Hafod">hafod</a></i>). In East Cornwall, <i>-m-</i> was the Anglo-Saxon representation of the Cornish nasal <i>-v-</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Place-names: Hammett, Hammett Down in the parish of <a href="/wiki/Quethiock" title="Quethiock">Quethiock</a>; Great Hammett, Little Hammett in the parish of <a href="/wiki/St_Neot,_Cornwall" title="St Neot, Cornwall">St Neot, Cornwall</a>.</li>
<li>Tremethyk: <i>tre medhyk</i> "doctor's residence or farm"</li>
<li>Trenowden: <i>Tre Renowden</i> "Renowden's farm"; Renowden is the Cornish form of Renaud or Reginald</li>
<li>Trengrouse: <i>Tre an Grows</i>, "farm at the cross"</li>
<li>Penprase: <i>Penn Pras</i> "head of the meadow or common pasture"</li>
<li>Nan(s)- surnames- there are many Nan(s)- surnames in Cornwall, "nan(s)" meaning valley- for example Nankervis from "nans" and "kervys", meaning "valley of the deer/stag" and <a href="/wiki/Nankivell" title="Nankivell">Nankivell</a>/Nancekivell "nans" "Cyfel", "the valley of Cyfel" (Cyfel being a personal name) or perhaps from the Old Welsh/Cornish word "ceffyl" a "horse" .<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Nanskeval is located on the parish boundaries of <a href="/wiki/St_Mawgan_in_Pydar" class="mw-redirect" title="St Mawgan in Pydar">St Mawgan in Pydar</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Columb_Major" title="St Columb Major">St Columb Major</a>.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nankivell_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Nankivell_2.jpg/220px-Nankivell_2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Nankivell_2.jpg/330px-Nankivell_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Nankivell_2.jpg/440px-Nankivell_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1522" data-file-height="1044" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nankivell_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nanskeval house at <a href="/wiki/St_Mawgan_in_Pydar" class="mw-redirect" title="St Mawgan in Pydar">St Mawgan in Pydar</a>, Cornwall. A possible location whence the surname originated</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trades">Trades</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Trades">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As in many other parts of Europe, names were used to describe the occupation of the head of the family; "Angove" (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">an Gov</i> "the Smith"), for example, being the equivalent to the Irish Gowan, Scottish Gow, Breton "Le Goff", "Legoff", "Legoffic", English "Smith", German "Schmidt" and Italian "Ferrero". Other examples of names derived from trades include "Dyer" (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">tior</i> "thatcher") and "Helyer" (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">helghyer</i> "hunter"), both of which can be found in English too, i.e. "Thatcher" and "Hunter" respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-cornishsurnames1_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cornishsurnames1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other examples:
</p>
<ul><li>Marrak: <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">marghek</i> "horseman, <a href="/wiki/Cavalier" title="Cavalier">cavalier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knight</a>"</li>
<li>Gloyn(e): <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">glowbrenn</i> "charcoal", perhaps from the ancient profession of a charcoal burner</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Animals">Animals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Animals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some surnames were derived from animals which may indicate that the bearer of some of these surnames may have made a living from hunting, examples include Bligh "wolf" (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">blydh</i>) and Coon "hounds" (<a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">keun</i>).
</p><p>Gwinnel: possibly from <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish</a>: <i lang="kw">gwennel</i> "swallow"; it also refers to a <a href="/wiki/Shuttle_(weaving)" title="Shuttle (weaving)">weaver's shuttle</a>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Festivals">Festivals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Festivals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At least one known Cornish surname derives from the name of a festival, namely
"Pascoe" from "Easter".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_characteristics_or_nicknames">Personal characteristics or nicknames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal characteristics or nicknames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another category of surnames is derived from personal characteristics or nicknames/<a href="/wiki/Hypocoristic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypocoristic">hypocoristics</a> . e.g. "Coad" (Cor.coth=old), "Couch" (Cor.cough=red) and "Tallack" (Cor.talek=wide-browed).<sup id="cite_ref-cornishsurnames1_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cornishsurnames1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other examples:
</p>
<ul><li>Teague te(k)g- from Cornish "fair" or "beautiful"</li>
<li>Gwyn(n)- from Cornish "gwy(d)n"  "white"</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_surname_Cornish">The surname Cornish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The surname Cornish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The surname "Cornish" with
variants "Cornysshe", "Cornyshe", "Cornysh", "Cornishe", "Cornisshe" and "Cornis"- standardised as "Cornish"- is to be found throughout Great Britain and Ireland. The name seems to originate from a time when ordinary people were still not using surnames in the modern way. A native Cornishman who had left Cornwall for another part of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a> or Ireland was given the name "Cornish", i.e. the <i>Cornishman</i>. In "A Dictionary of British Surnames", P.H. Reaney (1976), the following entries and dates are to be found:<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Cornish, Cornes, Badekoc Korneys (1296)</li>
<li>John Corneys (1327)</li>
<li>Henry Cornysh (1375)</li></ul>
<p>The first recorded instance is in the National Dictionary in 1547. It is likely that the Adam Corneys recorded in 1300 is identical to Adam Le Cornwalais recorded in 1275. Other related names to Cornish that designate a Cornish origin include "Cornwall", "Cornwell", "Curnow", "Cornu", "Kernew", "Kernow" etc. In previous centuries these names may have alternated along with "Cornwallis" and "Le Cornwalais".
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Welsh.22_names"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Welsh&quot;_names">"Welsh" names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: &quot;Welsh&quot; names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Especially in West Cornwall, many names typically associated with Welsh are also found. In the Cornish language, ultimately a language linked to Welsh and Breton, the prefix 'map' may have been used, as in Welsh, to indicate the relationship of father to son, this later becoming "ap" (as in NW Breton area, Leon dialect, <a href="https://br.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ap" class="extiw" title="br:Ap">Breton WP</a>) and then finally the "p" alone being prefixed to the name, e.g. (m)ap Richard becoming "Pri(t)chard". Another feature of these patronymics was the diminutive suffix  "-kin"  being added the father’s first name e.g. "Tonkin", which may derive from either Anthony or Thomas. 
</p>
<ul><li>Prowse/Prouse from "ma(b)p" "ros" meaning "son of the heath"</li>
<li>Prynne "ma(b)p" "Ryn", "son of Rynne"</li></ul>
<p>Surnames found at high frequencies in both Wales and Cornwall include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Davies" title="Davies">Davies</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Williams_(surname)" title="Williams (surname)">Williams</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Evans_(surname)" title="Evans (surname)">Evans</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jones_(surname)" title="Jones (surname)">Jones</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Morgan_(surname)" title="Morgan (surname)">Morgan</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anglicised_names_and_folk_etymology">Anglicised names and folk etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Anglicised names and folk etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Owing to the gradual language shift in Cornwall from the native <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">Cornish language</a> to English, approximately until the mid-18th century, some Cornish language surnames underwent change through <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">folk etymology</a>. The Cornish meaning of the name was no longer understood and so it was changed into a similar-sounding English word, not necessarily anything to do with the original meaning in Cornish. The same process has been noted in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornish_placenames&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cornish placenames (page does not exist)">Cornish placenames</a> too. One example of this process regarding surnames is the surname "Kneebone" which actually derives from the Cornish "Carn Ebwen" or the "tomb", "carn" of "Ebwen". The change must have occurred at a point when the original "k" at the beginning of the English word was still pronounced and thus suggests an early period in which it was anglicised.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-Cornish_language_surnames_typically_associated_with_Cornwall">Non-Cornish language surnames typically associated with Cornwall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cornish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Non-Cornish language surnames typically associated with Cornwall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are also many names typically found in Cornwall that may have a completely non-Cornish language origin, excluding those names taken from English, yet a strong association with the area. These names reflect the historical connections between Cornwall and Brittany and also the Norman occupation of Cornwall. The <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a> themselves employed Bretons in the administration of Cornwall and thus "imported" Breton names in Cornwall are not unusual.
</p>
<ul><li>Vingoe &#8211; possibly of <a href="/wiki/Norman_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman people">Norman</a> origin from French "vin-gout" "wine taster"</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arundells_of_Lanherne&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Arundells of Lanherne (page does not exist)">Arundell</a> &#8211; the name of an aristocratic <a href="/wiki/Baron_Arundell_of_Trerice" title="Baron Arundell of Trerice">family</a> in Cornwall.</li>
<li>Arscott &#8211; possibly from <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a> "harscoet" meaning "iron shield"</li>
<li>Briton, Brittan etc. &#8211; from "Breton", a name given to a <a href="/wiki/Breton_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Breton people">Breton</a> resident in Cornwall</li>
<li>Ferris &#8211; Of Scots origin, commonly found in the <a href="/wiki/Restormel" title="Restormel">Restormel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carrick,_Cornwall" title="Carrick, Cornwall">Carrick</a> areas.</li>
<li>Orchard</li>
<li>Rowe &#8211; of Norman origin, the name became popular in the region following large-scale Norman settlement. Remains a common surname in Cornwall to this day.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tangye" title="Tangye">Tangye</a> &#8211; of Breton origin.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
