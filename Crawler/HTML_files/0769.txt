<p><b>Lindley</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Suburb" title="Suburb">suburb</a> of <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>, within the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan borough</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kirklees" title="Kirklees">Kirklees</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, England. It is approximately 2 miles (3&#160;km) northwest from Huddersfield town centre.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_Royal_Infirmary" title="Huddersfield Royal Infirmary">Huddersfield Royal Infirmary</a>, Huddersfield's main hospital, is in Lindley. It is in the Central Huddersfield <a href="/wiki/Primary_Care_Trust" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary Care Trust">Primary Care Trust</a>, part of the Calderdale and Huddersfield Trust. In 1951, the scheme for building the new Infirmary was announced, at an initial cost of £5.5 million. Work started in 1957, but progress was slow. The hospital was officially opened only in 1967, by <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister" class="mw-redirect" title="Prime Minister">Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Harold_Wilson" title="Harold Wilson">Harold Wilson</a>, born in Huddersfield.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lindley,_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name for Lindley comes from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Saxon" title="Old Saxon">Saxon</a> for "<a href="/wiki/Flax" title="Flax">flax</a> <a href="/wiki/Meadow" title="Meadow">meadow</a>" or possibly from the Germanic word 'lind' denoting an area of <a href="/wiki/Tilia" title="Tilia">linden</a> (or lime) trees.
</p><p>Probably established by the <a href="/wiki/Angles" title="Angles">Angles</a> in the 7th century as a <a href="/wiki/Farming" class="mw-redirect" title="Farming">farming</a> community, it is mentioned in the <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> under the names "Lilleia". In the reign of <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">Edward the Confessor</a> it was owned by Godwin, and in the reign of <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a> it was being cultivated by Ulchel for <a href="/wiki/De_Lacy" title="De Lacy">Ilbert de Lacy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sheriff#History" title="Sheriff">Sheriff</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire" title="Hertfordshire">Hertfordshire</a> and descendant of the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> noble family from <a href="/wiki/Lassy,_Calvados" title="Lassy, Calvados">Lassy</a>.  At that time, Lindley consisted of two farmsteads totalling "5 quarantens by 2 quarantens".
</p><p>The Lindley Clock Tower is the most prominent landmark in Lindley,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> standing at the junction between Lidget Street and Daisy Lea Lane.  This <a href="/wiki/Art_Nouveau" title="Art Nouveau">Art Nouveau</a> clock tower was designed by the <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> architect <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Wood" title="Edgar Wood">Edgar Wood</a> in 1900 and erected by James Nield Sykes <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_Peace" class="mw-redirect" title="Justice of the Peace">JP</a>, in 1902.  The tower also features four <a href="/wiki/Buttress" title="Buttress">buttress</a> figures, four <a href="/wiki/Gargoyle" title="Gargoyle">gargoyles</a> and four <a href="/wiki/Frieze" title="Frieze">friezes</a>. The top of the tower is accessible via the doorway at its foot.
</p><p>Another key building in Lindley is Lindley Liberal Club, situated adjacent to Lindley Clock Tower. The Club has been in existence since its foundation stone was laid on 9 August 1887. The club came into use when building was completed the following year. This building is actually older than the Clock Tower, which was commissioned in 1902.
</p><p>Lindley appeared top in a survey carried out in 2006 by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Bank_of_Scotland" title="Royal Bank of Scotland">Royal Bank of Scotland</a>. By creating an algorithm factoring aspects such as desirability, return on investment and affordability, the survey results listed the top 10 locations throughout the UK for first time buyers to get on the property ladder.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Band">Band</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lindley,_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Band">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lindley Band was formed in the late 1830s and has been a major band in Huddersfield for most of its history.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It was one of the leading bands in the UK from 1880 to 1910.
The band were featured in a 2008 episode of <i>Life on Mars</i> and also appeared in a 1950s film called <i>Asylum</i>. It is currently in the first section.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Schools">Schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lindley,_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lindley Junior and Infants School is situated on George Street next to a housing estate. It caters for age group 4 to 11, with an enrolment of approximately 500 pupils. Lindley Junior School won the Songs Of Praise School Choir of The Year competition in 2008 and 2016.They have appeared on Look North, and have previously taken first place in the local Mrs Sunderland Music Competition.
</p>
