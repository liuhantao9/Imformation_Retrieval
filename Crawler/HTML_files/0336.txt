<p>The <b>Jewish name</b> has historically varied, encompassing throughout the centuries several different traditions.  This article looks at the <a href="/wiki/Onomastics" title="Onomastics">onomastics practices</a> of the Jewish people, that is, the history of the origin and forms of proper names.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Biblical_Era">Early Biblical Era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early Biblical Era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p>The name conferred upon a person in early Biblical times was generally connected with some circumstance of that person's birth—several of <a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">Jacob</a>'s sons are recorded as having received their names in this manner (<a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Genesis</a> 30). Generally, it was the mother who chose the name, as in the case of Jacob's sons, but there were occasions on which the father chose the child's name, such as in Genesis 16:15, 17:19, and 21:2.  Occasionally, persons other than the parents were the name-givers, as in the cases of <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> (<a href="/wiki/Book_of_Exodus" title="Book of Exodus">Exodus</a> 2:10) and <a href="/wiki/Solomon" title="Solomon">Solomon</a> (<a href="/wiki/II_Samuel" class="mw-redirect" title="II Samuel">II Samuel</a> 12:25).
</p><p>It appears to have been the custom in early Biblical times to confer a name immediately upon birth, but in later periods a name was given to a boy at circumcision (compare Luke 1:59 to 2:21). Before the <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_exile" class="mw-redirect" title="Babylonian exile">Babylonian exile</a>, it was not common practice to name children after their relatives, even in the royal family—none of the twenty-one kings of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Judah" title="Kingdom of Judah">Judah</a> was named after a predecessor, or after <a href="/wiki/David" title="David">David</a>, the founder of the dynasty. On the other hand, a son of <a href="/wiki/David_and_Jonathan" title="David and Jonathan">Jonathan</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Saul_the_King" class="mw-redirect" title="Saul the King">King Saul</a> were each named <a href="/wiki/Meribaal" class="mw-redirect" title="Meribaal">Meribaal</a> (II Samuel 21:7 and following). Instead of repeating the exact name of an ancestor, however, it was customary to make use of one of the elements of the family name. Thus, <a href="/wiki/Ahitub" title="Ahitub">Ahitub</a> has two sons, <a href="/wiki/Ahijah" title="Ahijah">Ahijah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ahimelech" title="Ahimelech">Ahimelech</a>. Three of Saul's family members have the element <i>ba'al</i> (in the altered form <i>bosheth</i>) in their names. As a consequence of this avoidance of repetition, a single name was as a rule sufficient to identify a person. It became traditional to identify a son by his father's name and a chosen name, like <a href="/wiki/Jaazaniah" title="Jaazaniah">Jaazaniah ben Shaphan</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ezekiel" title="Ezekiel">Ezekiel</a> 8:11) only in later years of Hebrew history.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Significance">Significance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Significance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p>It is probable that, as in other ancient societies, a name carried spiritual significance.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> A large majority of the 2,800 personal names found in the Hebrew Bible (shared among about 15,000 persons) convey a specific meaning. The meanings of the remainder have been obscured either through textual corruption or insufficient current understanding of comparative philology.  In addition, a considerable number of these names are probably <a href="/wiki/Eponym" title="Eponym">eponyms</a>. There is little doubt that this applies to the names of the <a href="/wiki/Israelite" class="mw-redirect" title="Israelite">Israelite</a> clans, each of which was assumed to be descended from the descendants of Jacob, described in <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Numbers" title="Book of Numbers">Numbers</a> 26.
</p><p>Names may be derived from the order of birth, as in the cases of <a href="/wiki/Akkub" class="mw-redirect" title="Akkub">Akkub</a> and Jacob, whose names probably mean "posthumous."  <a href="/wiki/Jephthah" title="Jephthah">Jephthah</a> implies "first-born", as does <a href="/w/index.php?title=Becher_(biblical)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Becher (biblical) (page does not exist)">Becher</a>, while names like <a href="/wiki/Manasseh_(tribal_patriarch)" title="Manasseh (tribal patriarch)">Manasseh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nahum" title="Nahum">Nahum</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nehemiah" title="Nehemiah">Nehemiah</a> refer probably to children who have come to take the place of others that have died in childhood. The idea of relationship is expressed in <a href="/wiki/Ahab" title="Ahab">Ahab</a>, probably originally <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ahiab&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ahiab (page does not exist)">Ahiab</a> (Jeremiah 29:21).
</p><p>Personal peculiarities may give rise to a name, as <a href="/wiki/Laban_(Bible)" title="Laban (Bible)">Laban</a> ("white", or "blond"), <a href="/wiki/Gideon" title="Gideon">Gideon</a> ("maimed"), or <a href="/wiki/Harim" class="mw-redirect" title="Harim">Harim</a> ("with pierced nose"). Personality may be referred to, as in the names <a href="/wiki/Job_(Biblical_figure)" class="mw-redirect" title="Job (Biblical figure)">Job</a> ("assailant") and <a href="/wiki/Barak" title="Barak">Barak</a> ("lightning"). There are no occupational names in the Bible corresponding to Anglo-Saxon names like <a href="/wiki/Smith_(surname)" title="Smith (surname)">Smith</a> of England or <a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCller_(surname)" title="Müller (surname)">Müller</a> of Germany, but names taken from objects are found, especially among females. The name <a href="/wiki/Rebecca" title="Rebecca">Rebecca</a> (<i>Rivka</i>) seems to be derived from a sheep-rope, <a href="/wiki/Peninnah" title="Peninnah">Peninnah</a> from <a href="/wiki/Pearl" title="Pearl">pearl</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Keren-happuch" title="Keren-happuch">Keren-happuch</a> from a box of face-paint. Abstract names seem to have been given primarily to women, such as <a href="/wiki/Manoah" title="Manoah">Manoah</a> ("rest") and <a href="/wiki/Michal" title="Michal">Michal</a> ("power").
</p><p>Jacobs gives eighty-four names (applied to 120 different persons) derived from animals and plants.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Leah" title="Leah">Leah</a> is generally known as a word for cow, and <a href="/wiki/Rachel" title="Rachel">Rachel</a> for <a href="/wiki/Domestic_sheep" class="mw-redirect" title="Domestic sheep">ewe</a> (appropriate since both are considered <a href="/wiki/Matriarchs_(Bible)" class="mw-redirect" title="Matriarchs (Bible)">matriarchs</a>). <a href="/wiki/Oreb" class="mw-redirect" title="Oreb">Oreb</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Raven" title="Raven">raven</a>") and <a href="/wiki/Ze%27ev" title="Ze&#39;ev">Ze'ev</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Wolf" title="Wolf">wolf</a>") were princes of the <a href="/wiki/Midianites" class="mw-redirect" title="Midianites">Midianites</a> (although Ze'ev was also an appellation of <a href="/wiki/Benjamin" title="Benjamin">Benjamin</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Caleb" title="Caleb">Caleb</a> ("the dog") was the founder of the chief Judean <a href="/wiki/Tribe" title="Tribe">tribe</a>. <a href="/wiki/Achbor" title="Achbor">Achbor</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">mouse</a>") and <a href="/wiki/Shaphan" title="Shaphan">Shaphan</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">coney</a>") also occur. <a href="/wiki/Jonah" title="Jonah">Jonah</a> is the equivalent of "<a href="/wiki/Dove" class="mw-redirect" title="Dove">dove</a>", <a href="/wiki/Zipporah" title="Zipporah">Zipporah</a> of "bird", and <a href="/wiki/Deborah" title="Deborah">Deborah</a> of "<a href="/wiki/Bee" title="Bee">bee</a>."  <a href="/wiki/Esther" title="Esther">Esther</a>'s Jewish name, <a href="/wiki/Hadassah" class="mw-redirect" title="Hadassah">Hadassah</a>, means "<a href="/wiki/Myrtus" title="Myrtus">myrtle</a>."  Citing these animal-inspired names, Robertson Smith and others have attempted to find evidence of <a href="/wiki/Totemism" class="mw-redirect" title="Totemism">totemism</a> among the ancient <a href="/wiki/Hebrews" title="Hebrews">Hebrews</a>.
</p><p>Other Jewish names taken from animals include Ari (lion), Ariel means ("God is my lion"), Dov (bear), and Tzvi or Zvi (deer).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Compound_names">Compound names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Compound names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p>A distinctive characteristic of Bible onomastics is the frequency of composite names, which form at times even complete sentences, as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Isaiah" title="Isaiah">Isaiah</a>'s son <a href="/wiki/Shear-jashub" class="mw-redirect" title="Shear-jashub">Shear-jashub</a> ("the remnant shall return") and <a href="/wiki/Hephzibah" title="Hephzibah">Hephzibah</a> ("my pleasure is in her"). Sometimes these composites have a preposition as their first element, as Bishlam (= "with peace"; <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Ezra" title="Book of Ezra">Ezra</a> 4:7) and <a href="/wiki/Lemuel_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lemuel (disambiguation)">Lemuel</a> ("belonging to God"; <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Proverbs" title="Book of Proverbs">Proverbs</a> 31:4); but in the majority of cases these composite names are theophorous, referring to, or actually mentioning, the Deity, either by the name of <a href="/wiki/YHWH" class="mw-redirect" title="YHWH">YHWH</a> or by the name of <a href="/wiki/El_(god)" class="mw-redirect" title="El (god)">El</a>. The specific name of the Hebrew God appears at the beginning as <i>Jo-</i> and at the end as <i>-iah</i>; thus, <i>Jonathan</i> is a doublet of <i><a href="/wiki/Elnathan" class="mw-redirect" title="Elnathan">Elnathan</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Joezer" class="mw-redirect" title="Joezer">Joezer</a></i> ("YHWH is help") is the same as <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joazar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Joazar (page does not exist)">Joazar</a></i> ("YHWH has helped"). A whole <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">theology</a> may be deduced from the large number of Biblical names referring to acts, actions, and attributes of the deity; thus: YHWH "gives" (Elnathan, <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_(name)" title="Jonathan (name)">Jonathan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel" title="Nathaniel">Nathaniel</a>); "increases the family" (<a href="/wiki/Eliasaph" class="mw-redirect" title="Eliasaph">Eliasaph</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_(given_name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph (given name)">Joseph</a>); "is gracious" (Elhanan, <a href="/wiki/Hananeel" class="mw-redirect" title="Hananeel">Hananeel</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_(given_name)" title="John (given name)">John</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hananiah_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hananiah (disambiguation)">Hananiah</a>); "has mercy" (<a href="/wiki/Jerahmeel" title="Jerahmeel">Jerahmeel</a>); "blesses" (<a href="/wiki/Barachel_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Barachel (disambiguation)">Barachel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berechiah" title="Berechiah">Berechiah</a>); "loves" (<a href="/wiki/Jedidiah" class="mw-redirect" title="Jedidiah">Jedidiah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eldad" title="Eldad">Eldad</a>); "helps" (<a href="/wiki/Eleazar" title="Eleazar">Eleazar</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Azareel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Azareel (page does not exist)">Azareel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Azariah_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Azariah (disambiguation)">Azariah</a>); "benefits" (<a href="/wiki/Gamaliel" title="Gamaliel">Gamaliel</a>); "holds fast" (<a href="/wiki/Jehoahaz_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Jehoahaz (disambiguation)">Jehoahaz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ahaziah" title="Ahaziah">Ahaziah</a>); "is strong" (<a href="/wiki/Uzziel" title="Uzziel">Uzziel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Azaziah" title="Azaziah">Azaziah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uzziah" title="Uzziah">Uzziah</a>); "delivers" (<a href="/wiki/Elpalet" class="mw-redirect" title="Elpalet">Elpalet</a>, Eliphalet); "comforts" (Nehemiah); "heals" (<a href="/wiki/Rephael" class="mw-redirect" title="Rephael">Rephael</a>); "conceals" (<a href="/wiki/Elzaphan" class="mw-redirect" title="Elzaphan">Elzaphan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zephaniah" title="Zephaniah">Zephaniah</a>); "establishes" (<a href="/wiki/Eliakim_(Bible)" class="mw-redirect" title="Eliakim (Bible)">Eliakim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jehoiakim" title="Jehoiakim">Jehoiakim</a>); "knows" (<a href="/wiki/Eliada" class="mw-redirect" title="Eliada">Eliada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jehoiada" title="Jehoiada">Jehoiada</a>); "remembers" (<a href="/wiki/Zechariah_(given_name)" title="Zechariah (given name)">Zechariah</a>); "sees" (<a href="/wiki/Hazael" title="Hazael">Hazael</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jahaziel" title="Jahaziel">Jahaziel</a>); "hears" (Elishama, Hoshama); "answers" (Anaiah, Ananiah, Janai); "speaks" (<a href="/wiki/Amariah" title="Amariah">Amariah</a>); "is praised" (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Jehaleel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jehaleel (page does not exist)">Jehaleel</a>); "is asked" (<a href="/wiki/Shealtiel" title="Shealtiel">Shealtiel</a>); "comes" (<a href="/wiki/Eliathah" class="mw-redirect" title="Eliathah">Eliathah</a>); "lives" (<a href="/wiki/Jehiel" class="mw-redirect" title="Jehiel">Jehiel</a>); "exalts/uplifts" (<a href="/wiki/Jeremiah" title="Jeremiah">Jeremiah</a>); "thunders" (<a href="/wiki/Raamiah" class="mw-redirect" title="Raamiah">Raamiah</a>; <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Nehemiah" title="Book of Nehemiah">Nehemiah</a> 7:7); "gladdens" (<a href="/wiki/Jahdiel" title="Jahdiel">Jahdiel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jehdeiah" class="mw-redirect" title="Jehdeiah">Jehdeiah</a>); "judges" (<a href="/wiki/Elishaphat" class="mw-redirect" title="Elishaphat">Elishaphat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jehoshaphat" title="Jehoshaphat">Jehoshaphat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shephatiah" class="mw-redirect" title="Shephatiah">Shephatiah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_(biblical_figure)" title="Daniel (biblical figure)">Daniel</a>); "is just" (<a href="/wiki/Jehozadak" title="Jehozadak">Jehozadak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zedekiah" title="Zedekiah">Zedekiah</a>); "is king" (<a href="/wiki/Elimelech" class="mw-redirect" title="Elimelech">Elimelech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malchiel" class="mw-redirect" title="Malchiel">Malchiel</a>); "is lord" (<a href="/wiki/Bealiah" title="Bealiah">Bealiah</a>); "is great" (<a href="/wiki/Gedaliah" title="Gedaliah">Gedaliah</a>); "is perfect" (<a href="/wiki/Jotham" title="Jotham">Jotham</a>); "is high" (<a href="/wiki/Jehoram_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Jehoram (disambiguation)">Jehoram</a>); "is glorious" (<a href="/wiki/Jochebed" title="Jochebed">Jochebed</a>); "is incomparable" (<a href="/wiki/Michael_(archangel)" title="Michael (archangel)">Michael</a>, literally "who is like God?")
</p><p>Besides these distinct names of God other divine names are used, as <i><a href="/wiki/Adonai" class="mw-redirect" title="Adonai">Adoni</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Adoniram" title="Adoniram">Adoniram</a>, and <i>Melech</i> in <a href="/wiki/Nathan-melech" title="Nathan-melech">Nathan-melech</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ebed-melech" class="mw-redirect" title="Ebed-melech">Ebed-melech</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Baal" title="Baal">Baal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Eshbaal" class="mw-redirect" title="Eshbaal">Esh-baal</a> (changed for special reasons to <a href="/wiki/Ishbosheth" class="mw-redirect" title="Ishbosheth">Ishbosheth</a>). In some cases names of relationship seem to be used as applied to the Deity (compare <a href="/wiki/Abiel" title="Abiel">Abiel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abijah" title="Abijah">Abijah</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Abimelech" title="Abimelech">Abimelech</a>, signifying in each case the fatherhood of God), and in this way <a href="/wiki/Abinadab" class="mw-redirect" title="Abinadab">Abinadab</a> would correspond to <a href="/wiki/Jehonadab" title="Jehonadab">Jehonadab</a> and <a href="/wiki/Abiezer" title="Abiezer">Abiezer</a> to <a href="/wiki/Eliezer" title="Eliezer">Eliezer</a>. The same applies to the elements <i>aḥ-</i> ("brother") and <i>amm-</i> ("uncle"). As, however, some of these words are applied to families, not individuals, the whole must be taken as a sentence: <a href="/w/index.php?title=Avihud&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Avihud (page does not exist)">Avihud</a> means "my father is glorious" (referring to God). On the same principle it must be assumed that some verbal names are theophorous, and refer to the action of the Deity, <a href="/wiki/Nathan_(given_name)" title="Nathan (given name)">Nathan</a> being the abbreviation of <a href="/wiki/Elnathan" class="mw-redirect" title="Elnathan">Elnathan</a> ("God gives"), <a href="/wiki/Shaphat" title="Shaphat">Shaphat</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jehoshaphat" title="Jehoshaphat">Jehoshaphat</a> ("God judges"). Thus <a href="/wiki/Ahaz" title="Ahaz">Ahaz</a> appears in a form corresponding to <a href="/wiki/Jehoahaz_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Jehoahaz (disambiguation)">Jehoahaz</a> in an inscription of <a href="/wiki/Tiglath-Pileser_III" title="Tiglath-Pileser III">Tiglath-Pileser III</a>. Many of the theophorous endings are contracted into <i>-a</i>, <i>-i</i>, or <i>-ai</i>, as in <a href="/wiki/Shebna" title="Shebna">Shebna</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hosa_(surname)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hosa (surname) (page does not exist)">Hosa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Talti" class="mw-redirect" title="Talti">Talti</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shemai" class="mw-redirect" title="Shemai">Shemai</a>. A few names are adjectival, and may contain references to the Deity: <a href="/wiki/Baruch_(given_name)" title="Baruch (given name)">Baruch</a> ("blessed"), <a href="/wiki/David" title="David">David</a> ("beloved"), <a href="/wiki/Amos_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Amos (disambiguation)">Amos</a> ("strong"). Some names have grammatical endings which it is difficult to interpret, as <i>-oth</i> in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shelomoth&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Shelomoth (page does not exist)">Shelomoth</a>; the final <i>-i</i> in <a href="/wiki/Omri" title="Omri">Omri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barzilai" title="Barzilai">Barzilai</a> probably refers to a tribal origin. Many names ending in <i>-on</i> are animal-names, as <a href="/wiki/Ephron" title="Ephron">Ephron</a> ("small <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a>"), <a href="/wiki/Nahshon" title="Nahshon">Nahshon</a> ("small <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">serpent</a>"); compare <a href="/wiki/Samson" title="Samson">Samson</a> ("small sun").
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Exilic_names">Post-Exilic names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post-Exilic names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">July 2019</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>After the Exile to Babylon there appeared a tendency toward the use of foreign names, the literal significance of which was disregarded, and this tendency became more and more prominent as time went on. Biblical names ending in <i>-a</i> (as in the books of Ezra and Nehemiah) are <a href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">Aramaic</a>. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shamsherai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Shamsherai (page does not exist)">Shamsherai</a> (<a href="/wiki/I_Chronicles" class="mw-redirect" title="I Chronicles">I Chronicles</a> 8:26), while <a href="/wiki/Mordecai" title="Mordecai">Mordecai</a> is probably derived from <a href="/wiki/Marduk" title="Marduk">Marduk</a>, or may be derived from "pure myrrh"), as are <a href="/wiki/Belteshazzar" class="mw-redirect" title="Belteshazzar">Belteshazzar</a> (Daniel 10:1), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shenazar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Shenazar (page does not exist)">Shenazar</a> (I Chronicles 3:18), and <a href="/wiki/Sheshbazzar" class="mw-redirect" title="Sheshbazzar">Sheshbazzar</a> (Ezra 1:8) from other deities. There is in this period a tendency also toward descriptive and adjectival names with the definite article prefixed, which easily gave rise to such surnames as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hakkaz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hakkaz (page does not exist)">Hakkaz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hakkatan" class="mw-redirect" title="Hakkatan">Hakkatan</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hallosheth&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hallosheth (page does not exist)">Hallosheth</a> (Ezra 2:61; 8:12; Nehemiah 3:12; compare the form <i>ha-Kohelet</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastes" title="Ecclesiastes">Ecclesiastes</a> 12:8, in the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>). In the <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">Hellenistic period</a> <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> names became quite usual among the Jews, especially those of <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">Alexander</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jason" title="Jason">Jason</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Antigonid_dynasty" title="Antigonid dynasty">Antigonus</a>. Even the name of a god like <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">Apollo</a> occurs (<a href="/wiki/Book_of_Acts" class="mw-redirect" title="Book of Acts">Acts</a> 18:24). Other names are <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apollonius_(given_name)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Apollonius (given name) (page does not exist)">Apollonius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hyrcanus" class="mw-redirect" title="Hyrcanus">Hyrcanus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lysimachus" title="Lysimachus">Lysimachus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demetrius" title="Demetrius">Demetrius</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dosa_(name)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dosa (name) (page does not exist)">Dosa</a>, Nicanor, Pappus, <a href="/wiki/Patroclus" title="Patroclus">Patroclus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philo" title="Philo">Philo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sosa_(surname)" title="Sosa (surname)">Sosa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Symmachus_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Symmachus (disambiguation)">Symmachus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tryphon" title="Tryphon">Tryphon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zeno_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Zeno (disambiguation)">Zeno</a>. The same occurs among women, as Alexandra and Priscilla. <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> names also occur, as <a href="/wiki/Antonius" title="Antonius">Antonius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Apella" title="Apella">Apella</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drusus_(cognomen)" title="Drusus (cognomen)">Drusus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Justinus_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Justinus (disambiguation)">Justinus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Justus" title="Justus">Justus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marcus_(name)" title="Marcus (name)">Marcus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rufus_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Rufus (disambiguation)">Rufus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tiberius" title="Tiberius">Tiberius</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Titus" title="Titus">Titus</a>. It was during this period that the practice arose of giving a son the name of his grandfather, as was done in the <a href="/wiki/Kohen_Gadol" class="mw-redirect" title="Kohen Gadol">high-priestly</a> family, the members of which were named alternately <a href="/wiki/Onias_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Onias (disambiguation)">Onias</a> and <a href="/wiki/Simon_(given_name)" title="Simon (given name)">Simon</a> from 332 to 165 BCE. Similarly, a little later, in the family of the <a href="/wiki/Hillel_the_Elder" title="Hillel the Elder">Hillelites</a>, the names <a href="/wiki/Gamaliel" title="Gamaliel">Gamaliel</a> and Judah succeed each other with only an occasional occurrence of Simon and Hillel. Toward the end of the period, owing to the intermixture of foreign languages, the use of double names for the same person began to be adopted, as in the instances of <a href="/wiki/Simon_Peter" class="mw-redirect" title="Simon Peter">Simon Peter</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Mark" title="John Mark">John Mark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Didymus" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Didymus">Thomas Didymus</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Herodes_Agrippa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Herodes Agrippa (page does not exist)">Herodes Agrippa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Salome_Alexandra" title="Salome Alexandra">Salome Alexandra</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Talmudic_period">Talmudic period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Talmudic period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Among the names in the <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">Talmud</a> there is a considerable proportion of Greek ones. A large number also are Aramaic, ending in <i>-a</i> or <i>-ai</i>: Abba, Huna, and Papa are instances of the former. Even Bible names were transformed in this direction——Ḥanina instead of Hananiah, Abuya instead of Abijah; while others were shortened, as <a href="/wiki/Eleazar" title="Eleazar">Lazar</a> (for <a href="/wiki/Eleazar" title="Eleazar">Eleazar</a>). Many Biblical names received renewed popularity owing to the distinction of their bearers, as those of <a href="/wiki/Gamaliel" title="Gamaliel">Gamaliel</a>, Hillel, and <a href="/wiki/Ulla_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Ulla (disambiguation)">Ulla</a>. The tendency toward double names existed here, as Sarah Miriam, Johanan Joseph,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and Mahaliel Judah.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Converts to Judaism, like <a href="/wiki/Aquila_of_Sinope" title="Aquila of Sinope">Aquila</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monobaz_II" title="Monobaz II">Monabaz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Helena_of_Adiabene" title="Helena of Adiabene">Helena</a>, retained their pagan names (as was the custom also in the early <a href="/wiki/Christian_Church" title="Christian Church">Christian Church</a>). There was some objection to foreign names among the Jews of this period,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> yet legend declares that the high priest Simon promised Alexander the Great that all the children of priestly families born in the year following his visit to <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> would be named Alexander, after him.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the adoption of double names during this early period an attempt was made to translate the Hebrew terms into corresponding Greek, as <i>Ariston</i> for <i>Tobi</i>, <i>Boethus</i> for <i>Ezra</i>, <i>Justus</i> for <i><a href="/wiki/Zadok" title="Zadok">Zadok</a></i>, <i>Philo</i> for <i>Jedidah</i>, <i>Theodorus</i> for <i>Nethaneel</i>, and <i>Zosimus</i> for <i>Ḥayyim</i>.
</p><p>It was somewhat rare for the same name to be used by both sexes. In Biblical times this occurs with regard to the names <i>Abigail</i>, <i>Abijah</i>, <i>Athaliah</i>, <i>Chushan</i>, <i>Ephah</i>, <i>Micha</i>, <i>Nahash</i>, <i>Shelomith</i>, <i>Zibiah</i>; in Talmudic times, with regard to <i>Ibu</i>, <i>Johanan</i>, <i>Nehorai</i>, <i>Pasi</i>, <i>Shalom</i>; the only later instances that may be cited are <i>Jeroham</i>, <i>Mazal-Ṭob</i>, <i>Neḥamah</i>, <i>Menuḥah</i>, <i>Simḥah</i>, <i>Tamar</i>, <i>Bongodas</i>, and <i>Bien-li-Viengue</i>. To wear a man's name seemed as objectionable as wearing men's clothes.
</p><p>It was already noticed in Talmudic times that the use of family names had died out.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The name of <a href="/wiki/Rabbi_Meir" title="Rabbi Meir">Rabbi Meir</a> was said to be derived from an experience at school which was regarded as being of good omen.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It is recommended not to name a child after enemies of the Jews, like <a href="/wiki/Sisera" title="Sisera">Sisera</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pharaoh" title="Pharaoh">Pharaoh</a>, but to use the names of the Patriarchs (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Abraham" title="Abraham">Abraham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isaac" title="Isaac">Isaac</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">Jacob</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Talmudic_period">Post-Talmudic period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Post-Talmudic period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the Jews spread throughout the lands bordering the Mediterranean, they drew upon other languages for their personal names while still retaining Biblical ones, and they were especially prone to adopt names ending in <i>-el</i>. These new names became exceptionally popular in Italy. To this source must be traced the new name <i>Ḥushiel</i>, composed on the same plan as the Biblical ones ending in <i>-el</i>. The kings of the <a href="/wiki/Khazars" title="Khazars">Khazars</a>, so far as their names are known, wavered between pure Biblical names, like <a href="/wiki/Obadiah" title="Obadiah">Obadiah</a>, and local names, like <i>Bulan</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Karaite_(Jewish_sect)" class="mw-redirect" title="Karaite (Jewish sect)">Karaites</a> in the same neighborhood adopted <a href="/wiki/Tatar" class="mw-redirect" title="Tatar">Tatar</a> names, one of them being known as <a href="/wiki/Toktamish" class="mw-redirect" title="Toktamish">Toktamish</a>; but elsewhere Karaite names are mostly Arabic and <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>.
</p><p>The custom of calling one of the sons, generally the eldest, after the paternal (sometimes the maternal) grandfather, of which only nine instances are known during the Talmudic period, became more popular, especially in European states. <a href="/wiki/Maimonides" title="Maimonides">Maimonides</a>' grandfather was <i>Joseph ben Isaac ben Joseph ben Obadiah ben Solomon ben Obadiah</i>, for instance, and certain families seem to have similarly confined themselves to a few chosen names. Thus, in the <a href="/wiki/Kalonymus" class="mw-redirect" title="Kalonymus">Kalonymus</a> family there occurs <i>Meshullam b. Moses b. Ithiel b. Moses b. Kalonymus b. Meshullam b. Kalonymus b. Moses b. Kalonymus b. Jekuthiel b. Moses b. Meshullam b. Ithiel b. Meshullam</i>—only five names among fourteen persons throughout three centuries. As a consequence certain names became characteristic of certain districts: <i>Japheth</i> and <i>Caleb</i> in Greece, and hence among the Karaites; <i>Kalonymus</i> in south Italy; <i>Sheshet</i> and <i>Joab</i> in Rome; <i>Sinai</i> and <i>Pesaḥ</i> in Germany. Some of the older names were revived—<i>Meïr</i>, for example, of which only two previous instances before, had been known, the <a href="/wiki/Tanna_Me%C3%AFr" class="mw-redirect" title="Tanna Meïr">tanna Meïr</a> and the Meïr mentioned by <a href="/wiki/Josephus" title="Josephus">Josephus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <i>Samson</i> was never used by Jews before the 11th century.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Kinnuyim"><i>Kinnuyim</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Kinnuyim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kinnuy" class="mw-redirect" title="Kinnuy">kinnuy</a></div>

<p>The most striking tendency of the post-Talmudic period is the general choice of local names by the Jews for their civic relations. This led to the adoption of two names, one for civic purposes, known as the <i><a href="/wiki/Kinnuy" class="mw-redirect" title="Kinnuy">kinnuy</a></i> (probably from the Arabic <i>kunyah</i>), the other (<i>shem ha-ḳodesh</i>) for use in the <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a> and in all Hebrew documents. The latter, the "sacred" name, was as far as possible associated with the former, and was often a translation of a civic one, e.g., <i>Asael</i> for <i>Diofatto</i>, <i>Manoah</i> for <i>Tranquillo</i>, <i>Ḥayyim</i> for <i>Vita</i>; at times the civic name was merely a contraction of the sacred one, e.g., <i>Leser</i> for <i>Eliezer</i>, <i>Sender</i> for <i>Alexander</i>. In other cases mere similarity in sound was sufficient to determine the sacred name, as <i>Mann</i> for <i>Menahem</i>, <i>Kalman</i> for <i>Kalonymus</i>, and the like. Especially noteworthy was the use made of Jacob's blessing to transfer a personal name from the civic to the sacred sphere. Judah being compared to a <a href="/wiki/Lion" title="Lion">lion</a>'s whelp in Jacob's blessing, <i>Judah</i> became <i>Leo</i>, or <i>Löwe</i>, in lay relationship, and <i>Ephraim</i> became <i>Fischlin</i>. Later on these name-equations became so usual that they formed doublets, which were almost invariably found together, as <i>Dov Bär</i>, <i>Naphtali Hirsch</i>, <i>Solomon Zalman</i>, <i>Judah</i> or <i>Aryeh Löb</i>, <i>Binyamin Wolf</i>, and these again gave currency to similar correlative names, as <i>Uri Phoebus</i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Titular_abbreviations">Titular abbreviations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Titular abbreviations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>It was during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> that the somewhat curious custom arose of combining the abbreviation of a title with the initials of a name to form a single personal name. This almost invariably implies frequency of mention, and, therefore, celebrity. The best-known examples are those of <b><a href="/wiki/Rashi" title="Rashi">RaSHI</a></b> and <b><a href="/wiki/Rambam" class="mw-redirect" title="Rambam">RaMBaM</a></b>, who are hardly ever quoted in rabbinical texts except by these names; but there exists a large number of similar contractions.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A somewhat similar use of a title is the combination with <i>Messer</i>, as in the Italian <i>Messer Leon</i>, while in <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">Provence</a> the honorary prefixes <i>en-</i>, for men, and <i>na-</i>, for women, are combined with the name to form <i>Engusek</i> (En-Joseph), <i>Nabona</i>, etc.
</p><p>Apart from these tendencies, the general trend of nomenclature among Jews in the Middle Ages was to adopt that of the countries in which they lived, the given names being often identical with those of the surrounding peoples, and other means of identification being derived mainly from localities or offices. Certain peculiarities of various countries may be taken separately.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mizrahim">Mizrahim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Mizrahim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">July 2019</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Among the <a href="/wiki/Mizrahim" class="mw-redirect" title="Mizrahim">Mizrahim</a> (Arabic-speaking <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> living in the <a href="/wiki/Middle-East" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle-East">Middle-East</a>) it was quite common to replace their <a href="/wiki/Hebrew" class="mw-redirect" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a> names with relevant Arabic ones, whenever they bore a religious note and were deemed sacred to both <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muslims" title="Muslims">Muslims</a>, so <a href="/wiki/Abraham" title="Abraham">Abraham</a> turned into <a href="/wiki/Ibrahim_(name)" title="Ibrahim (name)">Ibrahim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aharon_(given_name)" title="Aharon (given name)">Aharon</a> into <a href="/wiki/Haroun" class="mw-redirect" title="Haroun">Haroun</a>, <a href="/wiki/David" title="David">David</a> into <a href="/wiki/Daoud_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Daoud (disambiguation)">Daoud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moshe" class="mw-redirect" title="Moshe">Moshe</a> into <a href="/wiki/Moussa" title="Moussa">Moussa</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shlomo" title="Shlomo">Shlomo</a> into <a href="/wiki/Soliman_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Soliman (disambiguation)">Soliman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Salman_(name)" title="Salman (name)">Salman</a>.
</p><p>The indication of a family's elevated religious status, which also gained its members the reverence of their non-Jewish neighbors, was expressed in Arabic through their name. One has to note that such names were often given by the surrounding non-Jewish communities, who confused the appropriate religious terms of the <a href="/wiki/Halakhah" class="mw-redirect" title="Halakhah">Halakhah</a>. An example of this is the name <a href="/wiki/Nader_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nader (disambiguation)">Nader</a>, which is the Arabic translation of <a href="/wiki/Nazir_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Nazir (disambiguation)">Nazir</a>: the Hebrew root word <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neizer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Neizer (page does not exist)">neizer</a> (pronounced nay-zeer) means delineation or designation, and refers to the status of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nezirut&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nezirut (page does not exist)">nezirut</a> in which a person makes a vow to isolate himself from certain matters, such as intoxicating beverages, and other material luxuries (the absence of which would deaden his cravings for physical pleasures, as well as enhance his spiritual sensitivity and further his quest to attain spiritual heightenings), as well as things that are considered being <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tamea&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tamea (page does not exist)">tamea</a> (spiritually unclean, such as corpses). Yet in the Arabic environment a family of <a href="/wiki/Cohanim" class="mw-redirect" title="Cohanim">Cohanim</a> (descendants of <a href="/wiki/Aaron" title="Aaron">Aaron</a> and priests of the <a href="/wiki/Beit_haMiqdash" class="mw-redirect" title="Beit haMiqdash">Beit haMiqdash</a>), would often be described as such through the name <a href="/wiki/Nazir_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Nazir (disambiguation)">Nazir</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Nader_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nader (disambiguation)">Nader</a>. The obvious reason behind the wrong usage of the term <a href="/wiki/Nazir_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Nazir (disambiguation)">Nazir</a> and its attribution to <a href="/wiki/Cohanim" class="mw-redirect" title="Cohanim">Cohanim</a>, is the fact that the <a href="/wiki/Mizrahi_Cohanim" class="mw-redirect" title="Mizrahi Cohanim">Mizrahi Cohanim</a> had always strictly adhered to the relevant <a href="/wiki/Halachic_Laws" class="mw-redirect" title="Halachic Laws">Halachic Laws</a> which entail that they avoid coming into contact with corpses, lest they lose their <a href="/w/index.php?title=Status_of_purity&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Status of purity (page does not exist)">status of purity</a>, and which is similar to the rules that govern the status of <a href="/wiki/Nazarites" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazarites">nazarites</a>, as well as their perception amongst their surrounding Arab neighbors, especially the <a href="/wiki/Coptic_Christians" class="mw-redirect" title="Coptic Christians">Coptic Christians</a>, as being consecrated (vowed) to the religious service of the <a href="/wiki/Beit_haMiqdash" class="mw-redirect" title="Beit haMiqdash">Beit haMiqdash</a>.
</p><p>Personal peculiarities also gave rise to a Jew's name in the Arab world, as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abyad&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Abyad (page does not exist)">Abyad</a> referred to fair skin, <a href="/wiki/Afia_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Afia (disambiguation)">Afia</a> possessor of strength, health or well being, <a href="/wiki/Tawil" title="Tawil">Tawil</a> to a tall person.
</p><p>It was also quite usual that Hebrew names were translated into their corresponding meaning in the Arabic language, such as <a href="/wiki/Adin" title="Adin">Adin</a> into <a href="/wiki/Latif_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Latif (disambiguation)">Latif</a>, or <a href="/w/index.php?title=Loutfi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Loutfi (page does not exist)">Loutfi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eleazar" title="Eleazar">Eleazar</a> into <a href="/wiki/Mansour" class="mw-redirect" title="Mansour">Mansour</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gershom" title="Gershom">Gershom</a> into <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ghareeb&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ghareeb (page does not exist)">Ghareeb</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mazliach&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mazliach (page does not exist)">Mazliach</a> into <a href="/wiki/Maimun_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Maimun (disambiguation)">Maimun</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sameah&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sameah (page does not exist)">Sameah</a> into <a href="/wiki/Said" class="mw-redirect" title="Said">Said</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tovia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tovia (page does not exist)">Tovia</a> into <a href="/wiki/Hassan_(surname)" title="Hassan (surname)">Hassan</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Hassoun" title="Hassoun">Hassoun</a>.
</p><p>Common segments of <a href="/wiki/Mizrahi" class="mw-redirect" title="Mizrahi">Mizrahi</a> society adopted local Arabic names, such as <a href="/wiki/Abdallah" class="mw-redirect" title="Abdallah">Abdallah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abdela" class="mw-redirect" title="Abdela">Abdela</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abdo_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Abdo (disambiguation)">Abdo</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aboudi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Aboudi (page does not exist)">Aboudi</a> (all meaning servant of the Lord), <a href="/wiki/Farag" title="Farag">Farag</a> (relief), <a href="/wiki/Massoud" class="mw-redirect" title="Massoud">Massoud</a> (fortunate), <a href="/wiki/Mourad_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mourad (disambiguation)">Mourad</a> (aspired to), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nessim&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nessim (page does not exist)">Nessim</a> (fresh breeze), <a href="/wiki/Sabah" title="Sabah">Sabah</a> (morning), <a href="/wiki/Sahl_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Sahl (disambiguation)">Sahl</a> (ease), <a href="/wiki/Salama_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Salama (disambiguation)">Salama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salem_(name)" title="Salem (name)">Salem</a> and <a href="/wiki/Selim_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Selim (disambiguation)">Selim</a> (all meaning unharmed), <a href="/wiki/Zaki_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Zaki (disambiguation)">Zaki</a> (well-smelling), because of their beautiful meanings, or the good fortune they seemed to evoke.
</p><p>As had been the case within the <a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi" class="mw-redirect" title="Ashkenazi">Ashkenazi</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi" class="mw-redirect" title="Ashkenazi">Central and East European Jewish</a>) cultural environment, <a href="/wiki/Mizrahim" class="mw-redirect" title="Mizrahim">Mizrahim</a> bore names that related to their trades and occupations, such as <a href="/wiki/Albaz" class="mw-redirect" title="Albaz">Albaz</a> (also <a href="/wiki/Elbaz" class="mw-redirect" title="Elbaz">Elbaz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elvas_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Elvas (disambiguation)">Elvas</a> meaning falconer), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aboutboul&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Aboutboul (page does not exist)">Aboutboul</a> (<a href="/wiki/Abitbol" title="Abitbol">Abitbol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Botbol" title="Botbol">Botbol</a> all meaning drummer, drum maker, or seller), <a href="/wiki/Hayek" title="Hayek">Hayek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Khayat" title="Khayat">Khayat</a> (which both mean tailor), <a href="/wiki/Hakim_(title)" title="Hakim (title)">Hakim</a> (physician, or sage), <a href="/wiki/Naggar" title="Naggar">Naggar</a> (carpenter), <a href="/wiki/Sabbagh_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Sabbagh (disambiguation)">Sabbagh</a> (dyer of cloth), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sabban&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sabban (page does not exist)">Sabban</a> (soap maker), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sannoua&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sannoua (page does not exist)">Sannoua</a> (labourer), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sarrouf&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sarrouf (page does not exist)">Sarrouf</a> or <i>assaraf</i> (money changer), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shenhav&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Shenhav (page does not exist)">Shenhav</a> (also <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chenhav&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Chenhav (page does not exist)">Chenhav</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shenhavy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Shenhavy (page does not exist)">Shenhavy</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chenhavy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Chenhavy (page does not exist)">Chenhavy</a>) all referring to ivory and mean ivory craftsman or trader. Yemenite Jewish names were often adopted by place names such as Tzanani (from <a href="/wiki/Sana%27a" class="mw-redirect" title="Sana&#39;a">Sana'a</a>), Taeizi (from <a href="/wiki/Taiz" title="Taiz">Taiz</a>), Harazi (from <a href="/wiki/Jabal_Haraz" title="Jabal Haraz">Jabal Haraz</a>), and Damari (from <a href="/wiki/Dhamar,_Yemen" title="Dhamar, Yemen">Dhamar</a>). Other family names indicate pre-Islamic Jewish origin such as Kahalani (<a href="/wiki/Kahlan" title="Kahlan">Kahlan</a>) and Chorath (<a href="/wiki/Bnei_Chorath" class="mw-redirect" title="Bnei Chorath">Bnei Chorath</a>). Additionally, some Yemenite Jewish was related to their occupation such as Tabib/Taviv (doctor) and Qafih (poet).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mizrahim" class="mw-redirect" title="Mizrahim">Mizrahim</a> sought to avert the <a href="/wiki/Ein_ha_raa" class="mw-redirect" title="Ein ha raa">ein ha raa</a> (evil eye) and envy by never calling the dearest of their children (especially, the firstborn son) by the real name. In their quest to protect a child, parents would even revert to such drastic measures as giving it a very unattractive nickname, such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Garboua&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Garboua (page does not exist)">Garboua</a> (the one who is clad in rags), which has later become a family name.
</p><p>Contrary to <a href="/wiki/Ashkenazim" class="mw-redirect" title="Ashkenazim">Ashkenazim</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mizrahim" class="mw-redirect" title="Mizrahim">Mizrahim</a> applied the rulings of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Talmud_Shabbat_134&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Talmud Shabbat 134 (page does not exist)">Talmud Shabbat 134</a>, which provides that a child be called after a living relative. This led to the creation of another <a href="/wiki/Mizrahi" class="mw-redirect" title="Mizrahi">Mizrahi</a> particularity, where a child could be named <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sarah_bat_Sarah&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sarah bat Sarah (page does not exist)">Sarah bat Sarah</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Abraham_ben_Abraham" title="Abraham ben Abraham">Abraham ben Abraham</a>, which as per <a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi_customs" class="mw-redirect" title="Ashkenazi customs">Ashkenazi customs</a>, would only have been applied to a <a href="/wiki/Convert_to_Judaism" class="mw-redirect" title="Convert to Judaism">ger</a> (<a href="/wiki/Gerim" class="mw-redirect" title="Gerim">gerim</a> being convertites to <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>, and thus, deemed having become <a href="/wiki/Bnai_brith" class="mw-redirect" title="Bnai brith">bnai brith</a>, that is to say <a href="/wiki/Covenant_(biblical)" title="Covenant (biblical)">children of the Covenant</a>, holding the status of children of the <a href="/wiki/Patriarch_Abraham" class="mw-redirect" title="Patriarch Abraham">Patriarch Abraham</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Matriarch_Sarah" class="mw-redirect" title="Matriarch Sarah">Matriarch Sarah</a>).
</p><p>Name giving also served as a social welfare tool, whereby poor parents sought to secure the support of a benefactor for their child, by naming him after a wealthy relative or employer, who would show his acceptance to look after the welfare of the child by presenting it with a first set of clothing, and later on support him with marriage expenses and the like.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Mizrahim" class="mw-redirect" title="Mizrahim">Mizrahim</a> also reverted to this measure, as a means to strengthening the family ties between a married woman and her blood relatives, whereby a newly born child would be named after the elder of the mother's family, or one of its notables, and thus, retain his material benevolence, as well as underline the husband's expressed will to align himself to his spouse's family and gain their goodwill, or at least not to estrange his wife from her roots.
</p><p>A peculiarity of the Arabic onomatology is the <a href="/wiki/Kunyah" class="mw-redirect" title="Kunyah">kunyah</a>, the by-name given to a father after the birth of his son, by which he is named after the latter (Abou, Abo, or Abu followed by the name of his son). It may be added here that <a href="/wiki/Jonah_ibn_Janah" title="Jonah ibn Janah">Abu al-Walid</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Kunyah" class="mw-redirect" title="Kunyah">kunyah</a> or by-name for <a href="/wiki/Jonah" title="Jonah">Jonah</a>. <a href="/wiki/Abu_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Abu (disambiguation)">Abu</a> also forms family names, as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Abudarham" class="mw-redirect" title="Abudarham">Abudarham</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Aboab" title="Aboab">Aboab</a>. Here it has to be noted, that the word <a href="/wiki/Abou" title="Abou">Abou</a> was sometimes used in the sense of "owner", especially when it was followed by an object that was of importance to the manner in which the surrounding community perceived the person in question. The most prominent example for this is <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abou_Hassira&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Abou Hassira (page does not exist)">Abou Hassira</a> (owner of a straw mat), a descendant of a long line of kabbalists and pietists from Morocco (born 1807 in Morocco, died 1880 in Egypt, buried in the village of Demitiouh, near Damanhour, in the Governorate of Beheira), whose real name <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yaccov_Ben_Massoud&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Yaccov Ben Massoud (page does not exist)">Yaccov Ben Massoud</a> has almost been forgotten, as he has become identified as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rabbi_Abou_Hassira&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rabbi Abou Hassira (page does not exist)">Rabbi Abou Hassira</a> since his pilgrimage from his homeland to Jerusalem, during which the boat he was on sank, yet he was miraculously saved because he hung on to his straw mat (hassira), which had been his only possession. For this the Egyptian population venerated him from that day on as a holy man and miracle worker, whose life had been protected by the Lord through a frail mat.
</p><p>Akin to this is the use of the Arabic <a href="/wiki/Patronymic#Arabic" title="Patronymic">Ibn</a> which is relaten to Hebraic <i>ben</i> like in <i>benjamin</i> or ben yamin, (meaning son of, also spelled <a href="/wiki/Aben_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Aben (disambiguation)">Aben</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aven_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Aven (disambiguation)">Aven</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avin_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Avin (disambiguation)">Avin</a>) to form a family name amongst <a href="/wiki/Mizrahim" class="mw-redirect" title="Mizrahim">Mizrahim</a> who lived in the Eastern part of the Arab world. Among the best known of this formation are <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Aknin" class="mw-redirect" title="Ibn Aknin">Ibn Aknin</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ibn_Danan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ibn Danan (page does not exist)">Ibn Danan</a> (hence <a href="/wiki/Abendana" title="Abendana">Abendana</a>), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ibn_Latif&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ibn Latif (page does not exist)">Ibn Latif</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ibn_Migas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ibn Migas (page does not exist)">Ibn Migas</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ibn_Verga&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ibn Verga (page does not exist)">Ibn Verga</a>. Those <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> who lived in the <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> countries (especially Morocco) chose the use of the  word <a href="/w/index.php?title=O_or_sometimes_spelled_%22U%22&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="O or sometimes spelled &quot;U&quot; (page does not exist)">O or sometimes spelled "U"</a> (belonging to), which created the names <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ou%27Hanna&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ou&#39;Hanna (page does not exist)">Ou'Hanna</a>, (son of Hanna, also spelled <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bohana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bohana (page does not exist)">Bohana</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abuhana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Abuhana (page does not exist)">Abuhana</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abuhenna&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Abuhenna (page does not exist)">Abuhenna</a>), <a href="/w/index.php?title=O%27Hayon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="O&#39;Hayon (page does not exist)">O'Hayon</a> (son of Life), <a href="/w/index.php?title=O%27Knine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="O&#39;Knine (page does not exist)">O'Knine</a> (son of Yacob, also spelled <a href="/w/index.php?title=Waknin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Waknin (page does not exist)">Waknin</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ouaknin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ouaknin (page does not exist)">Ouaknin</a>).
</p><p>The Arabic article <i>al</i> appears in quite a number of names, as in <i>Al-Ḥarisi</i>. Other names of interest, given by Steinschneider in a long list of eight hundred Arabic names in the <i>Jewish Quarterly Review</i> (ix. -xiii.), are <i>Ghayyat</i> (in Spanish <i>Gayet</i>), <i>Ibn Danan</i> and <i>Ibn al-Dayyal</i>, <i>Al-Haruni</i> ("the Aaronide", the same as <i>Cohen</i>), <i>Ibn Waḳar</i>, <i>Ibn Zabara</i> and <i>Ibn Zimra</i>, <i>Ḥaji</i> (applied to Karaites who had performed the pilgrimage to Jerusalem), <i>Yaḥya</i> (equivalent to <i>John</i> or <i>Judah</i>). <i>Morel</i> is said to be derived from <i>Samuel</i>; <i>Molko</i> means "royal"; <i>Mas'ud</i> is equivalent to <i>Baruch</i>; <i>Muḳattil</i> ("champion") would be a proper origin for the family name <i>Mocatta</i>; <i>Najar</i> and <i>Najara</i> refer to carpentry; <i>Sasun</i> is merely a transcript of <i><a href="/wiki/Sason" title="Sason">Sason</a></i> ("<a href="/wiki/Lily" class="mw-redirect" title="Lily">lily</a>"). The proper names <i><a href="/wiki/Sa%E2%80%98id" title="Sa‘id">Sa‘id</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Saad" title="Saad">Saad</a></i>, and <i>Sa'dan</i> are equally popular among Jews and Arabs. <i><a href="/wiki/Abbas_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Abbas (disambiguation)">Abbas</a></i> ("lion") corresponds to <i>Judah</i>, as <i>Leo</i> and the like in Europe.
</p><p>Very many <a href="/wiki/Judeo-Arabic" class="mw-redirect" title="Judeo-Arabic">Judeo-Arabic</a> names are compounded of<i> 'abd</i> ("servant"), as <i>Abdallah</i> and '<i>Abd al-Walid</i>. <i>Al-Faraj</i> occurs as the name of the translator at <i>Girgenti</i>, and it is possibly the remote origin of the curious name of Admiral Farragut, whose grandfather came from <a href="/wiki/Menorca" title="Menorca">Menorca</a>. It is considered doubtful whether the name of the <i>Ḳimḥis</i> is Hebrew in that form, or whether it should be pronounced as an Arabic word, <i>Ḳamḥi</i> ("formed of wheat").
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sephardic_Jews">Sephardic Jews</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sephardic Jews">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sephardic Jews in the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> were often known by multiple forms of their names, such as those in Ladino, Turkish, French, Hebrew, Arabic, and/or other European languages. Many were given names from the Bible.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surname">Surname</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Surname">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Jewish_surname" title="Jewish surname">Jewish surname</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hebraization_of_surnames" title="Hebraization of surnames">Hebraization of surnames</a></div>

<p>Jews have historically used Hebrew <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> names. In the Jewish patronymic system the first name is followed by either ben- or bat- ("son of" and "daughter of", respectively), and then the father's name. (Bar-, "son of" in Aramaic, is also seen). Permanent family surnames exist today but only gained popularity among Sephardic Jews in Iberia as early as the 10th or 11th century and did not spread widely to the Ashkenazic Jews of Germany or Eastern Europe until the late 18th and mid 19th century, where the adoption of German surnames was imposed in exchange for Jewish emancipation. 
</p><p>Although Ashkenazi Jews now use European or modern-Hebrew surnames for everyday life, the Hebrew patronymic form is still used in Jewish religious and cultural life, and is common in <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>. It is used in synagogue and in documents in Jewish law such as the ketubah (marriage contract). Many Sephardic Jews used the Arabic ibn instead of bat or ben when it was the norm. The Spanish family Ibn Ezra is one example.
</p><p>Many recent immigrants to Israel have changed their names to Hebrew ones, in a process called <a href="/wiki/Hebraization_of_surnames" title="Hebraization of surnames">hebraization</a>, to erase remnants of Diaspora history still present in family names from other languages. This is especially common among Ashkenazi Jews, because most of their European names do not go back far in history; surnames were imposed by the German and Austro-Hungarian Empires in the 18th century (explaining why many Ashkenazi Jews have German or European-sounding names).  The newly-assumed Hebrew names were sometimes based on phonetic similarity with their former European surname, for example, Golda Meyersohn became <a href="/wiki/Golda_Meir" title="Golda Meir">Golda Meir</a>.
</p><p>A popular form to create a new family name is the false patronymic, using the prefix "ben" or "bar" followed by words who are not the name of a parent. Examples include patriotic themes, such as ben Ami ("son of my people"), or ben Artzi ("son of my country"), and terms related to the Israeli landscape, such as bar Ilan ("son of the trees", also similar phonetically to <a href="/wiki/Meir_Bar-Ilan" title="Meir Bar-Ilan">the bearer</a>'s original family name Berlin).  Another famous person who used a false patronymic is the first Israeli Prime Minister, <a href="/wiki/David_Ben-Gurion" title="David Ben-Gurion">David Ben-Gurion</a>. His former family name was Grün, and he adopted the name "Ben-Gurion" ("son of Gurion"), not "Ben-Avigdor" (his father's name). Gurion was a Jewish leader in the period of the <a href="/wiki/First_Jewish%E2%80%93Roman_War" title="First Jewish–Roman War">First Jewish–Roman War</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Change_of_name">Change of name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Change of name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Change of name was not an unusual occurrence in Biblical times, if one may judge by the instances occurring among the Patriarchs, and it seems to have been not altogether unknown in later times. Thus, Moses Benveniste mentions a certain Obadiah who wandered from Germany to <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> in 1654 and changed his name to Moses because the former name was unusual.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Later in the Middle Ages a person who was dangerously sick would change his name in the hope that the <a href="/wiki/Death_(personification)#Death_(angels)_in_religion" title="Death (personification)">Angel of Death</a>, who summons persons by name, would be baffled thereby. This custom, known as <i>meshanneh shem,</i> is given in the Talmud<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and is mentioned by Judah Ḥasid.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> One of the names thus adopted was the appropriate one of <i>Ḥayyim</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In order to prevent any misunderstanding at the resurrection the <a href="/wiki/Kabbalah" title="Kabbalah">cabalists</a> later recommended persons to learn a <a href="/wiki/Psalm" class="mw-redirect" title="Psalm">psalm</a> the first and last verses of which began and ended with the first and last letters of their names. Particular care is to be taken in the writing of names in legal documents, the slightest error in which invalidates them. Hence there are quite a number of monographs on names, both personal and geographical, the first of which was that written by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sim%E1%B8%A5ah_Cohen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Simḥah Cohen (page does not exist)">Simḥah Cohen</a>; the best known is that of <a href="/wiki/Samuel_ben_Phoebus" class="mw-redirect" title="Samuel ben Phoebus">Samuel ben Phoebus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ephraim_Zalman_Margulies" class="mw-redirect" title="Ephraim Zalman Margulies">Ephraim Zalman Margulies</a> entitled <i>Ṭib Giṭṭin.</i>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Superstitions">Superstitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Superstitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>It was thought that Jews of the same name should not live in the same town or permit their children to marry into each other's families;<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> this seems to have some reference to <a href="/wiki/Exogamy" title="Exogamy">exogamy</a>.  It is even urged that one should not marry a woman of the same name as one's mother; or that she should be required to change it. Even to the present day it is considered unlucky in Russia for a father-in-law to have the same name as the bridegroom. In other parts of Russia it is considered bad luck to name a child after a living relative. When several children have died in a family the next that is born has no name given to it, but is referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Alter_(name)" title="Alter (name)">Alter</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Yiddish_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Yiddish language">Yiddish</a>: <span lang="yi" dir="rtl">אלטער</span>&#8206;, literally "old"), or <i>Alterke</i>, the view being that the Angel of Death, not knowing the name of the child, will not be able to seize it. When such a child attains the marriageable age, a new name, generally that of one of the Patriarchs, is given to it.  For a somewhat similar reason it is considered unlucky in <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> to call an only child by its true name.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_authors_named_by_their_works">Religious authors named by their works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Religious authors named by their works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is customary for well-known authors, beginning with <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> times, to be known by the titles of their works rather than by their own names. Thus, <a href="/wiki/Jacob_ben_Asher" title="Jacob ben Asher">Jacob ben Asher</a> is referred to as the <i>Ṭur</i> or the <i>Ba'al ha-Ṭurim</i>; <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Caro" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph Caro">Joseph Caro</a> is known as the <i>Bet Yosef</i>; and <a href="/wiki/Ezekiel_Landau" class="mw-redirect" title="Ezekiel Landau">Ezekiel Landau</a> as <i>Noda' bi-Yehudah</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Schwab&quot;_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schwab&quot;-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acronyms_used_for_religious_authors">Acronyms used for religious authors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Acronyms used for religious authors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Even more frequently were authors known by contracted forms of their names, with the addition of some honorary prefix, as given above. Among contemporary Hebrew writers this practice is still more widely observed, though no honorary title is prefixed. A list is given by <a href="/wiki/Mo%C3%AFse_Schwab" title="Moïse Schwab">Moïse Schwab</a> in his <i>Repertoire</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Schwab&quot;_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schwab&quot;-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_non-religious_authors">Modern non-religious authors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jewish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Modern non-religious authors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most <a href="/wiki/Yiddish" title="Yiddish">Yiddish</a> writers appear to prefer to write under some pen-name or pseudonym, and their example is at times followed by modern writers of <a href="/wiki/Modern_Hebrew" title="Modern Hebrew">Hebrew</a>, though these, as a rule, prefer to give a name composed of their initials.
</p><p>Of course, other Jewish writers besides the above have adopted pen-names. <a href="/wiki/Israel_Zangwill" title="Israel Zangwill">Israel Zangwill</a> has written under the names "<a href="/wiki/J._Freeman_Bell" class="mw-redirect" title="J. Freeman Bell">J. Freeman Bell</a>" (in collaboration), "Countess von S.", and "Marshallik"; Mrs. Frankau is known as "<a href="/wiki/Frank_Danby" class="mw-redirect" title="Frank Danby">Frank Danby</a>"; and so on; but there is nothing specifically Jewish about this adoption of a pen-name.
</p>
