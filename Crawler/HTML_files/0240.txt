	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Albanian name</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Albanian names</b> are names used in, or originating in, <a href="/wiki/Albania" title="Albania">Albania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kosovo" title="Kosovo">Kosovo</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Macedonia" title="North Macedonia">North Macedonia</a> and the Albanian diaspora. In Albania a complete name usually consists of a <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> (<a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">Albanian</a>: <i lang="sq">emri</i>); the given name of the individual's father (<a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">Albanian</a>: <i lang="sq">atësia</i>), which is seldom included except in official documents; and a (most commonly <a href="/wiki/Patrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrilineal">patrilineal</a>) <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a> or <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> (<a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">Albanian</a>: <i lang="sq">mbiemri</i>). They are invariably given in the <a href="/wiki/Western_name_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Western name order">Western name order</a>, or given name followed by family name.
</p><p>Albanian given names are traditionally original Albanian-meaning names, or religious names (<a href="/wiki/Islam_in_Albania" title="Islam in Albania">Islamic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_Albania" title="Christianity in Albania">Christian</a>). During the Communist regime, based on the theory of the Illyrian origin of Albanians, supposedly Illyrian names were constructed as appropriate names instead of religious ones. The government issued a decree ordering people to change their religious names to "pure Albanian names", while newborns had to receive non-religious names.
</p><p>Albanian names have changed dramatically with more opting for foreign, English or Romance names in recent times than traditional Balkan names. In addition Albanians from Albania tend to focus on names that are <a href="/wiki/Greek_name" title="Greek name">Greek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_name" title="Italian name">Italian</a>, Western European as opposed to those in Kosovo, North Macedonia and elsewhere that are either religious, local, geographic or related to traditionalism.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Given_names">Given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While in Kosovo, Montenegro and North Macedonia, Albanian ("<a href="/wiki/Illyrians" title="Illyrians">Illyrian</a>") and <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_Albania" title="Religion in Albania">religious</a> names are quite common, in Albania proper, Albanian or Muslim names are barely given. This is a result of the high willingness to emigrate and not to be noticed as Albanian in the countries where they want to emigrate to. Also, it's a result of the Communist dictatorship in which the regime wanted to erase the differences between Muslim and Christian Albanians and imposed naming laws. In 2014, among the 20 most commonly used given names for newborn children in Albania, there was not a single Albanian name. Instead, "international" (Christian or English) names, got quite common.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Traditionally, given names in Albania did not have Albanian origins because they were religious names, either Christian or Islamic. In <a href="/wiki/Communist_Albania" class="mw-redirect" title="Communist Albania">Communist Albania</a>, an Illyrian origin of the Albanians (without denying "<i>Pelasgian</i> roots",<sup id="cite_ref-schwandner2_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-schwandner2-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> a theory which has been revitalized today<sup id="cite_ref-European_Cultures_2009_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-European_Cultures_2009-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>) continued to play a significant role in Albanian nationalism, resulting in a revival of given names supposedly of Illyrian origin, at the expense of given names associated with Christianity or Islam. This trend had originated with the 19th century <i><a href="/wiki/Albanian_National_Awakening" title="Albanian National Awakening">Rilindja</a></i>, but it became more common after 1944, when it became the communist regime's declared doctrine to oust Christian or Islamic given names. Ideologically acceptable names were listed in the <i>Fjalor me emra njerëzish</i> (1982). These could be native Albanian words like <i>Flutur</i> ("butterfly"), ideologically communist ones like <i>Marenglen</i> (<a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Marx</a>-<a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Engels</a>-<a href="/wiki/Lenin" class="mw-redirect" title="Lenin">Lenin</a>), or "Illyrian" ones compiled from epigraphy, e.g. from the necropolis at <a href="/wiki/Dyrrhachion" class="mw-redirect" title="Dyrrhachion">Dyrrhachion</a> excavated in 1958-60.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surnames">Surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many surnames in Albania have Islamic and Christian roots.<sup id="cite_ref-Carmichael2003_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carmichael2003-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The long mutual influence of the population resulted in a large number of Albanian surnames ending in <i>ić</i>, <i>ović</i> or <i>ovit</i> even under Ottoman rule.<sup id="cite_ref-Filipović1989_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Filipović1989-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Common last name endings include -aj, as well as common definite Albanian nominative singular endings: hence -i for originally masculine last names except for those previously ending in k, g, h or i, which have -u added; or -a/-ja for feminine names.
</p><p>Many last names were originally surnames, many of these being either Muslim (Ahmeti, Rexhepi, etc.), Bektashi (Bektashi itself as a surname, Dervishi, Shehu, etc.) or Christian (Kristo(ja), Evangjeli, etc.), but a large number are neither and are simply from old Albanian secular names (Zogolli, Dushku, Shkoza etc.). Albanians frequently have surnames that don't match their actual religious identity, often because of recent secularization, religious intermarriage, relatively recent conversion in late Ottoman times (many Muslims have Christian names for this reason, while after the fall of communism some with Muslim ancestry have become practicing Christians and vice versa) or the practice of Ottoman Christians taking Muslim names due to Muslim dominance of society during those times. Names starting with Papa- usually indicate Christian origin but there are cases where it is followed by a Muslim element (i.e. Papazisi, a name held by Albanians of both Christian and Muslim heritage).
</p><p>Another major source of Albanian last names are place names- Albanians sometimes took their hometowns as surnames,<sup id="cite_ref-DiLellio2627_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DiLellio2627-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and especially  when a family moved to another place, they often took their former residence as a surname, leading to somewhat well known last names such as Frashëri, Përmeti, Shkodra, Kelmendi, Shkreli, Delvina, Prishtina, etc.). 
In the North and in Kosovo, clan names are also very prominent, most notably the names of widespread clans such as Krasniqi, Berisha and Gashi. The surnames Gega, Gegprifti, Gegaj etc. probably indicate Northern (Gheg) origins, as Tosku and Toskaj do for Southerners. In addition, many names, even if not explicitly, are strongly identified with certain regions and Albanians can often tell another Albanian's regional origin from their last name. Surnames based on occupation are less common than in other countries but nevertheless the surnames Hoxha (mullah, either Bektashi or Sunni) and Prifti (priest, used by both Catholics and Orthodox) remain very common.
</p><p>Arvanite and pre- modern Albanian surnames are also common. Many Arvanite surnames are found in Albania, in the modern Albanian form. For example, the word in Arvanitika (Arbërisht) for "brave" or "pallikari" (in Greek) being "çanavar" (Turkish canavar meaning "monster") or its shortened form "çavar" was pronounced "tzanavar" or "tzavar" giving birth to Arvanitic family names like "Tzanavaras" and/or "Tzavaras". This is a link between Albanian and Greek names.
The <a href="/wiki/Arvanite" class="mw-redirect" title="Arvanite">Arvanite</a> surname "<a href="/w/index.php?title=%CE%9A%CF%81%CE%B9%CE%B5%CE%B6%CE%AE%CF%82&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Κριεζής (page does not exist)">Κριεζής</a>" (<a href="/wiki/Kriezis" title="Kriezis">Kriezis</a>) is a very common <a href="/wiki/Albanians" title="Albanians">Albanian</a> surname. "<a href="/wiki/Kryezi" title="Kryezi">Kryezi</a>" means "Blackhead" in Albanian (hence same meaning in <a href="/wiki/Arvanitika" title="Arvanitika">Arbërisht</a>/<a href="/wiki/Arvanitika" title="Arvanitika">Arvanitika</a>).
</p><p>Orthodox Christian names tend to be heavily Greek, including last names which have counterparts in the Greek language.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Communist-era_Albania">Communist-era Albania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Communist-era Albania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to a decree issued in 1966, Muslims in Albania had to change their names to Albanian names while newborn Albanians had to receive non-religious names.<sup id="cite_ref-Waardenburg2003_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Waardenburg2003-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In a decree of November 1975, all the citizens of Albania whose names were considered objectionable by the <a href="/wiki/Albanian_Communist_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Albanian Communist Party">Albanian Communist Party</a> were ordered to change their names to "pure Albanian names" by the end of the year.<sup id="cite_ref-Prifti1978_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Prifti1978-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gianaris1996_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gianaris1996-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Situation_in_the_present">Situation in the present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Albanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Situation in the present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Albanians form the largest migrant group in Greece and second largest migrant group in Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-ArmandFeka_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ArmandFeka-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Many modern names are thus Greek or Italian.<sup id="cite_ref-ArmandFeka_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ArmandFeka-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In Greece, and likewise in Italy, many Albanian newcomers change their Albanian names to Greek or Italian ones and their religion, if they are not Christian, from <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> to <a href="/wiki/Christianity_in_Albania" title="Christianity in Albania">Christianity</a>:<sup id="cite_ref-ArmandFeka_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ArmandFeka-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Even before emigration, more and more people in the south of Albania are pretending to be Greeks, and even are changing their Muslim names to Greek ones in order to not being discriminated against in Greece. By this way, they hope to get a visa for Greece.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
