	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Metalworking</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dreher_an_einer_Drehbank.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Dreher_an_einer_Drehbank.jpg/220px-Dreher_an_einer_Drehbank.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Dreher_an_einer_Drehbank.jpg/330px-Dreher_an_einer_Drehbank.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Dreher_an_einer_Drehbank.jpg/440px-Dreher_an_einer_Drehbank.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="1312" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dreher_an_einer_Drehbank.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Turning a bar of metal on a <a href="/wiki/Lathe" title="Lathe">lathe</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Metalworking</b> is the process of working with <a href="/wiki/Metal" title="Metal">metals</a> to create individual parts, assemblies, or large-scale structures. The term covers a wide range of work from large <a href="/wiki/Ship" title="Ship">ships</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bridge" title="Bridge">bridges</a> to precise <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">engine</a> parts and delicate <a href="/wiki/Jewelry" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewelry">jewelry</a>. It therefore includes a correspondingly wide range of skills, processes, and <a href="/wiki/Tool" title="Tool">tools</a>.
</p><p>Metalworking is a science, art,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> hobby, industry and trade. Its historical roots span cultures, civilizations, and millennia. Metalworking has evolved from the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a> various <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ores</a>, producing <a href="/wiki/Malleable" class="mw-redirect" title="Malleable">malleable</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ductile" class="mw-redirect" title="Ductile">ductile</a> metal useful tools and adornments. Modern metalworking processes, though diverse and specialized, can be categorized as forming, cutting, or joining processes. Today's <a href="/wiki/Machine_shop" title="Machine shop">machine shop</a> includes a number of <a href="/wiki/Machine_tool" title="Machine tool">machine tools</a> capable of creating a precise, useful workpiece.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory">Prehistory</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Prehistory">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The oldest archaeological evidence of <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a> and working was the discovery of a copper <a href="/wiki/Pendant" title="Pendant">pendant</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Iraq" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Iraq">northern Iraq</a> from 8,700 BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The earliest substantiated and dated evidence of metalworking in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> was the processing of copper in <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Lake_Michigan" title="Lake Michigan">Lake Michigan</a>. Copper was hammered until brittle then heated so it could be worked some more. This technology is dated to about 4000-5000 BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The oldest <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> artifacts in the world come from the Bulgarian <a href="/wiki/Varna_Necropolis" title="Varna Necropolis">Varna Necropolis</a> and date from 4450 BCE.
</p><p>Not all metal required fire to obtain it or work it. <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">Isaac Asimov</a> speculated that gold was the "first metal".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> His reasoning is that by its <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">chemistry</a> it is found in nature as nuggets of pure gold. In other words, gold, as rare as it is, is sometimes found in nature as the metal that it is.  There are a few other metals that sometimes occur natively, and as a result of <a href="/wiki/Meteor" class="mw-redirect" title="Meteor">meteors</a>. Almost all other metals are found in <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ores</a>, a mineral-bearing <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">rock</a>, that require heat or some other process to liberate the metal. Another feature of gold is that it is workable as it is found, meaning that no technology beyond a stone <a href="/wiki/Hammer" title="Hammer">hammer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anvil" title="Anvil">anvil</a> to work the metal is needed.  This is a result of gold's properties of <a href="/wiki/Malleability" class="mw-redirect" title="Malleability">malleability</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ductility" title="Ductility">ductility</a>. The earliest <a href="/wiki/Tool" title="Tool">tools</a> were stone, <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sinew" class="mw-redirect" title="Sinew">sinew</a>, all of which sufficed to work gold.
</p><p>At some unknown point the connection between heat and the liberation of metals from rock became clear, and rocks rich in copper, <a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">tin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> came into demand. These ores were mined wherever they were recognized.  Remnants of such ancient mines have been found all over <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_Asia" class="mw-redirect" title="Southwestern Asia">Southwestern Asia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Metalworking was being carried out by the <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asian</a> inhabitants of <a href="/wiki/Mehrgarh" title="Mehrgarh">Mehrgarh</a> between 7000–3300 BCE.<sup id="cite_ref-Possehl_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Possehl-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The end of the beginning of metalworking occurs sometime around 6000 BCE when copper <a href="/wiki/Smelting" title="Smelting">smelting</a> became common in Southwestern Asia.
</p><p>Ancient civilisations knew of seven metals. Here they are arranged in order of their <a href="/wiki/Oxidation_potential" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxidation potential">oxidation potential</a> (in <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">volts</a>):
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">Iron</a>     +0.44 V,</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tin" title="Tin">Tin</a>      +0.14 V</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">Lead</a>     +0.13 V</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">Copper</a>   −0.34 V</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mercury_(element)" title="Mercury (element)">Mercury</a>  −0.79 V</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">Silver</a>   −0.80 V</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">Gold</a>     −1.50 V.</li></ul>
<p>The oxidation potential is important because it is one indicator of how tightly bound to the ore the metal is likely to be.  As can be seen, iron is significantly higher than the other six metals while gold is dramatically lower than the six above it. Gold's low oxidation is one of the main reasons that gold is found in nuggets. These nuggets are relatively pure gold and are workable as they are found.
</p><p>Copper ore, being relatively abundant, and tin ore became the next important players in the story of metalworking. Using heat to smelt copper from ore, a great deal of copper was produced. It was used for both <a href="/wiki/Jewelry" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewelry">jewelry</a> and simple tools. However, copper by itself was too soft for tools requiring edges and stiffness. At some point tin was added into the molten copper and <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">bronze</a> was born.  Bronze is an <a href="/wiki/Alloy" title="Alloy">alloy</a> of copper and tin.  Bronze was an important advance because it had the edge-durability and stiffness that pure copper lacked.  Until the advent of iron, bronze was the most advanced metal for tools and <a href="/wiki/Weapon" title="Weapon">weapons</a> in common use (see <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> for more detail).
</p><p>Outside Southwestern Asia, these same advances and materials were being discovered and used around the world. <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> jumped into the use of bronze with little time being devoted to copper. <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> began the use of bronze and <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">iron</a> almost simultaneously. In the Americas things were different.  Although the peoples of the Americas knew of metals, it was not until the <a href="/wiki/European_colonisation_of_the_Americas" class="mw-redirect" title="European colonisation of the Americas">European colonisation</a> that metalworking for tools and weapons became common. Jewelry and <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a> were the principal uses of metals in the Americas prior to European influence.
</p><p>Around 2700 BCE, production of bronze was common in locales where the necessary materials could be assembled for smelting, heating, and working the metal. Iron was beginning to be smelted and began its emergence as an important metal for tools and weapons. The period that followed became known as the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_ferrous_metallurgy" class="mw-redirect" title="History of ferrous metallurgy">History of ferrous metallurgy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WomanFactory1940s.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/WomanFactory1940s.jpg/220px-WomanFactory1940s.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/WomanFactory1940s.jpg/330px-WomanFactory1940s.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/WomanFactory1940s.jpg/440px-WomanFactory1940s.jpg 2x" data-file-width="7296" data-file-height="5656" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WomanFactory1940s.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A turret lathe operator machining parts for transport planes at the Consolidated Aircraft Corporation plant, Fort Worth, Texas, USA in the 1940s</div></div></div>
<p>By the historical periods of the <a href="/wiki/Pharaohs" class="mw-redirect" title="Pharaohs">Pharaohs</a> in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Vedic" class="mw-redirect" title="Vedic">Vedic</a> <a href="/wiki/King" title="King">Kings</a> in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tribes_of_Israel" class="mw-redirect" title="Tribes of Israel">Tribes of Israel</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">Maya civilization</a> in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, among other ancient populations, <a href="/wiki/Precious_metal" title="Precious metal">precious metals</a> began to have value attached to them. In some cases rules for ownership, distribution, and <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trade</a> were created, enforced, and agreed upon by the respective peoples. By the above periods metalworkers were very skilled at creating objects of adornment, religious artifacts, and trade instruments of <a href="/wiki/Precious_metals" class="mw-redirect" title="Precious metals">precious metals</a> (non-ferrous), as well as weaponry usually of ferrous <a href="/wiki/Metals" class="mw-redirect" title="Metals">metals</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Alloys" class="mw-redirect" title="Alloys">alloys</a>.  These skills were finely honed and well executed.  The techniques were  practiced by artisans, <a href="/wiki/Blacksmith" title="Blacksmith">blacksmiths</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atharvaveda" title="Atharvaveda">atharvavedic</a> practitioners, <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemists</a>, and other categories of metalworkers around the globe. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Granulation_(jewellery)" title="Granulation (jewellery)">granulation</a> technique was employed by numerous ancient cultures before the historic record shows people traveled to far regions to share this process.   This and many other ancient techniques are still used by metalsmiths today.
</p><p>As time progressed metal objects became more common, and ever more complex. The need to further acquire and work metals grew in importance. Skills related to extracting metal ores from the earth began to evolve, and metalsmiths became more knowledgeable. Metalsmiths became important members of society. Fates and economies of entire civilizations were greatly affected by the availability of metals and metalsmiths. The metalworker depends on the extraction of precious metals to make <a href="/wiki/Jewelry" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewelry">jewelry</a>, build more efficient <a href="/wiki/Electronics" title="Electronics">electronics</a>, and for <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrial</a> and technological applications from <a href="/wiki/Construction" title="Construction">construction</a> to <a href="/wiki/Shipping" class="mw-redirect" title="Shipping">shipping</a> containers to <a href="/wiki/Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway">rail</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Air_transport" class="mw-redirect" title="Air transport">air transport</a>. Without metals, goods and services would cease to move around the globe on the scale we know today.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_processes">General processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: General processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CombinationSquareSet.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/CombinationSquareSet.jpg/220px-CombinationSquareSet.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="131" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/CombinationSquareSet.jpg/330px-CombinationSquareSet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/CombinationSquareSet.jpg/440px-CombinationSquareSet.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2025" data-file-height="1209" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CombinationSquareSet.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Combination_square" title="Combination square">combination square</a> used for transferring designs.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DigitalCaliperEuro.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/DigitalCaliperEuro.jpg/220px-DigitalCaliperEuro.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/DigitalCaliperEuro.jpg/330px-DigitalCaliperEuro.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/DigitalCaliperEuro.jpg/440px-DigitalCaliperEuro.jpg 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="384" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:DigitalCaliperEuro.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A caliper is used to precisely measure a short length.</div></div></div>
<p>Metalworking generally is divided into the following categories, <i>forming</i>, <i>cutting</i>, and, <i>joining</i>.  Most metal cutting is done by high speed steel tools or carbide tools.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Each of these categories contain various processes.
</p><p>Prior to most operations, the metal must be marked out and/or measured, depending on the desired finished product.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Marking_out" title="Marking out">Marking out</a></i> (also known as layout) is the process of transferring a <a href="/wiki/Design" title="Design">design</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pattern" title="Pattern">pattern</a> to a workpiece and is the first step in the handcraft of metalworking. It is performed in many industries or hobbies, although in industry, the repetition eliminates the need to mark out every individual piece. In the metal trades area, marking out consists of transferring the engineer's <a href="/wiki/Technical_drawing" title="Technical drawing">plan</a> to the workpiece in preparation for the next step, machining or manufacture.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Caliper" class="mw-redirect" title="Caliper">Calipers</a></i> are hand tools designed to precisely measure the distance between two points. Most calipers have two sets of flat, parallel edges used for inner or outer diameter measurements. These calipers can be accurate to within one-thousandth of an inch (25.4 μm). Different types of calipers have different mechanisms for displaying the distance measured. Where larger objects need to be measured with less precision, a <a href="/wiki/Tape_measure" title="Tape measure">tape measure</a> is often used.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forming_processes">Forming processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Forming processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>These <i>forming</i> processes modify metal or workpiece by deforming the object, that is, without removing any material. Forming is done with a system of mechanical forces and, especially for bulk metal forming, with heat.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cutting_processes">Cutting processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cutting processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cnc_plasma_cutting.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Cnc_plasma_cutting.jpg/220px-Cnc_plasma_cutting.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Cnc_plasma_cutting.jpg/330px-Cnc_plasma_cutting.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Cnc_plasma_cutting.jpg/440px-Cnc_plasma_cutting.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2016" data-file-height="1512" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cnc_plasma_cutting.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A CNC <a href="/wiki/Plasma_cutting" title="Plasma cutting">plasma cutting</a> machine.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cutting" title="Cutting">Cutting</a></div>
<p><i>Cutting</i> is a collection of processes wherein material is brought to a specified geometry by removing excess material using various kinds of tooling to leave a finished part that meets specifications.  The net result of cutting is two products, the waste or excess material, and the finished part.  In woodworking, the waste would be sawdust and excess wood.  In cutting metals the waste is chips or <a href="/wiki/Swarf" title="Swarf">swarf</a> and excess metal.
</p><p>Cutting processes fall into one of three major categories:
</p>
<ul><li>Chip producing processes most commonly known as <a href="/wiki/Machining" title="Machining">machining</a></li>
<li>Burning, a set of processes wherein the metal is cut by oxidizing a kerf to separate pieces of metal</li>
<li>Miscellaneous specialty process, not falling easily into either of the above categories</li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Drilling" title="Drilling">Drilling</a> a hole in a metal part is the most common example of a chip producing process.  Using an <a href="/wiki/Oxy-fuel_welding_and_cutting" title="Oxy-fuel welding and cutting">oxy-fuel cutting torch</a> to separate a plate of steel into smaller pieces is an example of burning. Chemical milling is an example of a specialty process that removes excess material by the use of etching chemicals and masking chemicals.
</p><p>There are many technologies available to cut metal, including: 
</p>
<ul><li>Manual technologies: <a href="/wiki/Saw" title="Saw">saw</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chisel" title="Chisel">chisel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tin_snips" class="mw-redirect" title="Tin snips">shear or snips</a></li>
<li>Machine technologies: <a href="/wiki/Turning" title="Turning">turning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milling_(machining)" title="Milling (machining)">milling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drilling" title="Drilling">drilling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grinding_machine" title="Grinding machine">grinding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saw" title="Saw">sawing</a></li>
<li>Welding/burning technologies: burning by <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxy-fuel_welding_and_cutting" title="Oxy-fuel welding and cutting">oxy-fuel burning</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Plasma_cutting" title="Plasma cutting">plasma</a></li>
<li>Erosion technologies: by <a href="/wiki/Water_jet_cutter" title="Water jet cutter">water jet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spark_erosion" class="mw-redirect" title="Spark erosion">electric discharge</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Abrasive_flow_machining" title="Abrasive flow machining">abrasive flow machining</a>.</li>
<li>Chemical technologies: <a href="/wiki/Photochemical_machining" title="Photochemical machining">Photochemical machining</a></li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cutting_fluid" title="Cutting fluid">Cutting fluid</a> or <a href="/wiki/Coolant" title="Coolant">coolant</a> is used where there is significant friction and heat at the cutting interface between a cutter such as a drill or an end mill and the workpiece.  Coolant is generally introduced by a spray across the face of the tool and workpiece to decrease friction and temperature at the cutting tool/workpiece interface to prevent excessive tool wear. In practice there are many methods of delivering coolant.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Milling">Milling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Milling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Makino-S33-MachiningCenter-example.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Makino-S33-MachiningCenter-example.jpg/220px-Makino-S33-MachiningCenter-example.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="187" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Makino-S33-MachiningCenter-example.jpg/330px-Makino-S33-MachiningCenter-example.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Makino-S33-MachiningCenter-example.jpg/440px-Makino-S33-MachiningCenter-example.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1659" data-file-height="1413" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Makino-S33-MachiningCenter-example.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A milling machine in operation, including coolant hoses.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Milling_(machining)" title="Milling (machining)">Milling (machining)</a></div>
<p><b>Milling</b> is the complex shaping of metal or other materials by removing material to form the final shape. It is generally done on a <a href="/wiki/Milling_machine" class="mw-redirect" title="Milling machine">milling machine</a>, a power-driven machine that in its basic form consists of a <a href="/wiki/Milling_cutter" title="Milling cutter">milling cutter</a> that rotates about the spindle axis (like a <a href="/wiki/Drill" title="Drill">drill</a>), and a <a href="/wiki/Worktable" class="mw-redirect" title="Worktable">worktable</a> that can move in multiple directions (usually two dimensions [x and y axis] relative to the workpiece). The spindle usually moves in the z axis.  It is possible to raise the table (where the workpiece rests).  Milling machines may be operated manually or under <a href="/wiki/Computer_numerical_control" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer numerical control">computer numerical control</a> (CNC), and can perform a vast number of complex operations, such as slot cutting, <a href="/wiki/Planer_(metalworking)" title="Planer (metalworking)">planing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drilling_and_threading" class="mw-redirect" title="Drilling and threading">drilling and threading</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rabbet" title="Rabbet">rabbeting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wood_router" class="mw-redirect" title="Wood router">routing</a>, etc. Two common types of mills are the horizontal mill and vertical mill.
</p><p>The pieces produced are usually complex 3D objects that are converted into x, y, and z coordinates that are then fed into the <a href="/wiki/CNC" class="mw-redirect" title="CNC">CNC</a> machine and allow it to complete the tasks required.  The milling machine can produce most parts in 3D, but some require the objects to be rotated around the x, y, or z coordinate axis (depending on the need).  Tolerances come in a variety of standards, depending on the locale. In countries still using the imperial system, this is usually in the thousandths of an inch (Unit known as Thou), depending on the specific machine. In many other European countries, standards following the ISO are used instead.
</p><p>In order to keep both the bit and material cool, a high temperature coolant is used.  In most cases the coolant is sprayed from a hose directly onto the bit and material.  This coolant can either be machine or user controlled, depending on the machine.
</p><p>Materials that can be milled range from <a href="/wiki/Aluminum" class="mw-redirect" title="Aluminum">aluminum</a> to stainless steel and almost everything in between.  Each material requires a different speed on the milling tool and varies in the amount of material that can be removed in one pass of the tool.  Harder materials are usually milled at slower speeds with small amounts of material removed.  Softer materials vary, but usually are milled with a high bit speed.
</p><p>The use of a milling machine adds costs that are factored into the manufacturing process.  Each time the machine is used coolant is also used, which must be periodically added in order to prevent breaking bits.  A milling bit must also be changed as needed in order to prevent damage to the material.  Time is the biggest factor for costs.  Complex parts can require hours to complete, while very simple parts take only minutes.  This in turn varies the production time as well, as each part will require different amounts of time.
</p><p>Safety is key with these machines.  The bits are traveling at high speeds and removing pieces of usually scalding hot metal.  The advantage of having a CNC milling machine is that it protects the machine operator.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turning">Turning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Turning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SchlichtenDrehen.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/SchlichtenDrehen.jpg/220px-SchlichtenDrehen.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/SchlichtenDrehen.jpg/330px-SchlichtenDrehen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/SchlichtenDrehen.jpg/440px-SchlichtenDrehen.jpg 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SchlichtenDrehen.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A lathe cutting material from a workpiece.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Turning" title="Turning">Turning</a></div>
<p>Turning is a metal cutting process for producing a cylindrical surface with a single point tool. The workpiece is rotated on a spindle and the cutting tool is fed into it radially, axially or both. Producing surfaces perpendicular to the workpiece axis is called facing. Producing surfaces using both radial and axial feeds is called profiling.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A <i>lathe</i> is a machine tool which spins a block or cylinder of material so that when <a href="/wiki/Abrasive" title="Abrasive">abrasive</a>, cutting, or <a href="/wiki/Knurling" title="Knurling">deformation tools</a> are applied to the workpiece, it can be shaped to produce an object which has <a href="/wiki/Rotational_symmetry" title="Rotational symmetry">rotational symmetry</a> about an <a href="/wiki/Axis_of_rotation" class="mw-redirect" title="Axis of rotation">axis of rotation</a>. Examples of objects that can be produced on a lathe include <a href="/wiki/Candlestick" title="Candlestick">candlestick</a> holders, <a href="/wiki/Crankshaft" title="Crankshaft">crankshafts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camshaft" title="Camshaft">camshafts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bearing_(mechanical)" title="Bearing (mechanical)">bearing</a> mounts.
</p><p>Lathes have four main components: the bed, the headstock, the carriage, and the tailstock.  The bed is a precise &amp; very strong base which all of the other components rest upon for alignment. The headstock's <a href="/wiki/Spindle_(tool)" title="Spindle (tool)">spindle</a> secures the workpiece with a <a href="/wiki/Chuck_(engineering)" title="Chuck (engineering)">chuck</a>, whose jaws (usually three or four) are tightened around the piece. The spindle rotates at high speed, providing the energy to cut the material. While historically lathes were powered by <a href="/wiki/Belt_(mechanical)" title="Belt (mechanical)">belts</a> from a <a href="/wiki/Line_shaft" title="Line shaft">line shaft</a>, modern examples uses electric motors. The workpiece extends out of the spindle along the axis of rotation above the flat bed. The carriage is a platform that can be moved, precisely and independently parallel and perpendicular to the axis of rotation. A hardened <a href="/wiki/Tool_bit" title="Tool bit">cutting tool</a> is held at the desired height (usually the middle of the workpiece) by the toolpost. The carriage is then moved around the rotating workpiece, and the cutting tool gradually removes material from the workpiece. The tailstock can be slid along the axis of rotation and then locked in place as necessary. It may hold centers to further secure the workpiece, or cutting tools driven into the end of the workpiece.
</p><p>Other operations that can be performed with a single point tool on a lathe are:<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chamfering: Cutting an angle on the corner of a cylinder.<br />
Parting: The tool is fed radially into the workpiece to cut off the end of a part.<br />
<a href="/wiki/Threading_(manufacturing)" title="Threading (manufacturing)">Threading</a>: A tool is fed along and across the outside or inside surface of rotating parts to produce external or internal <a href="/wiki/Screw_thread" title="Screw thread">threads</a>.<br />
<a href="/wiki/Boring_(manufacturing)" title="Boring (manufacturing)">Boring</a>: A single-point tool is fed linearly and parallel to the axis of rotation to create a round hole.<br />
<a href="/wiki/Drilling" title="Drilling">Drilling</a>: Feeding the drill into the workpiece axially.<br />
<a href="/wiki/Knurling" title="Knurling">Knurling</a>: Uses a tool to produce a rough surface texture on the work piece.  Frequently used to allow grip by hand on a metal part.
</p><p>Modern computer numerical control (CNC) lathes and (CNC) machining centres can do secondary operations like milling by using driven tools. When driven tools are used the work piece stops rotating and the driven tool executes the machining operation with a rotating cutting tool.  The CNC machines use x, y, and z coordinates in order to control the turning tools and produce the product. Most modern day CNC lathes are able to produce most turned objects in 3D.
</p><p>Nearly all types of metal can be turned, although more time &amp; specialist cutting tools are needed for <a href="/wiki/Hardness" title="Hardness">harder</a> workpieces.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Threading">Threading</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Threading">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ThreadingTaps.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/ThreadingTaps.jpg/220px-ThreadingTaps.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/ThreadingTaps.jpg/330px-ThreadingTaps.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/ThreadingTaps.jpg/440px-ThreadingTaps.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1452" data-file-height="1032" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ThreadingTaps.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Three different types and sizes of taps.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Threading_(manufacturing)" title="Threading (manufacturing)">Threading (manufacturing)</a></div>
<p>There are many threading processes including: cutting threads with a <a href="/wiki/Tap_and_die" title="Tap and die">tap or die</a>, thread milling, single-point thread cutting, thread rolling, cold root rolling and forming, and thread grinding. A <i>tap</i> is used to cut a female thread on the inside surface of a pre-drilled hole, while a <i>die</i> cuts a male thread on a preformed cylindrical rod.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grinding">Grinding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Grinding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Scheibe_im_eingriff.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Scheibe_im_eingriff.jpg/220px-Scheibe_im_eingriff.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="83" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Scheibe_im_eingriff.jpg/330px-Scheibe_im_eingriff.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Scheibe_im_eingriff.jpg/440px-Scheibe_im_eingriff.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1591" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Scheibe_im_eingriff.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A surface grinder</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Grinding_(abrasive_cutting)" title="Grinding (abrasive cutting)">Grinding (abrasive cutting)</a></div>
<p><i>Grinding</i> uses an abrasive process to remove material from the workpiece.  A <b>grinding machine</b> is a machine tool used for producing very fine finishes, making very light cuts, or high precision forms using an <a href="/wiki/Grinding_wheel" title="Grinding wheel">abrasive wheel</a> as the cutting device. This wheel can be made up of various sizes and types of stones, <a href="/wiki/Diamond" title="Diamond">diamonds</a> or <a href="/wiki/Inorganic" class="mw-redirect" title="Inorganic">inorganic</a> materials.
</p><p>The simplest grinder is a bench grinder or a hand-held angle grinder, for deburring parts or cutting metal with a zip-disc.
</p><p>Grinders have increased in size and complexity with advances in time and technology. From the old days of a manual toolroom grinder sharpening endmills for a production shop, to today's 30000 RPM CNC auto-loading manufacturing cell producing jet turbines, grinding processes vary greatly.
</p><p>Grinders need to be very rigid machines to produce the required finish. Some grinders are even used to produce glass scales for positioning CNC machine axis. The common rule is the machines used to produce scales be 10 times more accurate than the machines the parts are produced for.
</p><p>In the past grinders were used for finishing operations only because of limitations of tooling. Modern grinding wheel materials and the use of industrial diamonds or other man-made coatings (cubic boron nitride) on wheel forms have allowed grinders to achieve excellent results in production environments instead of being relegated to the back of the shop.
</p><p>Modern technology has advanced grinding operations to include CNC controls, high material removal rates with high precision, lending itself well to aerospace applications and high volume production runs of precision components.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Filing">Filing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Filing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Filing_(metalworking)" title="Filing (metalworking)">Filing (metalworking)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Surface_of_a_file.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Surface_of_a_file.jpg/220px-Surface_of_a_file.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="121" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Surface_of_a_file.jpg/330px-Surface_of_a_file.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Surface_of_a_file.jpg/440px-Surface_of_a_file.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3456" data-file-height="1898" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Surface_of_a_file.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A file is an abrasive surface like this one that allows machinists to remove small, imprecise amounts of metal.</div></div></div>
<p><i>Filing</i> is combination of grinding and saw tooth cutting using a <a href="/wiki/File_(tool)" title="File (tool)">file</a>. Prior to the development of modern machining equipment it provided a relatively accurate means for the production of small parts, especially those with flat surfaces.  The skilled use of a file allowed a <a href="/wiki/Machinist" title="Machinist">machinist</a> to work to fine tolerances and was the hallmark of the craft.  Files can vary in shape, coarseness, and whether the teeth or single cut or double cut depending on what application the file is to be used for.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Today filing is rarely used as a production technique in industry, though it remains as a common method of <a href="/wiki/Deburr" class="mw-redirect" title="Deburr">deburring</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Broaching_(metalworking)" title="Broaching (metalworking)">Broaching</a> is a machining operation used to cut <a href="/wiki/Keyway_(engineering)" class="mw-redirect" title="Keyway (engineering)">keyways</a> into shafts. <a href="/wiki/Electron_beam_machining" class="mw-redirect" title="Electron beam machining">Electron beam machining</a> (EBM) is a machining process where high-velocity electrons are directed toward a work piece, creating heat and vaporizing the material. <a href="/wiki/Ultrasonic_machining" title="Ultrasonic machining">Ultrasonic machining</a> uses <a href="/wiki/Ultrasound" title="Ultrasound">ultrasonic</a> vibrations to machine very hard or brittle materials.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Joining_processes">Joining processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Joining processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GMAW.welding.af.ncs.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/GMAW.welding.af.ncs.jpg/220px-GMAW.welding.af.ncs.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="248" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/GMAW.welding.af.ncs.jpg/330px-GMAW.welding.af.ncs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/GMAW.welding.af.ncs.jpg/440px-GMAW.welding.af.ncs.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1960" data-file-height="2213" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GMAW.welding.af.ncs.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mig welding</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Welding">Welding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Welding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">Welding</a></div>
<p><i>Welding</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Fabrication_(metal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fabrication (metal)">fabrication</a> process that joins materials, usually metals or <a href="/wiki/Thermoplastic" title="Thermoplastic">thermoplastics</a>, by causing <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/coalesce" class="extiw" title="wikt:coalesce">coalescence</a>.  This is often done by <a href="/wiki/Melting" title="Melting">melting</a> the workpieces and adding a filler material to form a pool of molten material that cools to become a strong joint, but sometimes pressure is used in conjunction with <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>, or by itself, to produce the weld.
</p><p>Many different energy sources can be used for welding, including a gas <a href="/wiki/Fire" title="Fire">flame</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Electric_arc" title="Electric arc">electric arc</a>, a laser, an <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">electron</a> beam, <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ultrasound" title="Ultrasound">ultrasound</a>.  While often an industrial process, welding can be done in many different environments, including open air, <a href="/wiki/Underwater_welding" class="mw-redirect" title="Underwater welding">underwater</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">space</a>.  Regardless of location, however, welding remains dangerous, and precautions must be taken to avoid burns, <a href="/wiki/Electric_shock" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric shock">electric shock</a>, poisonous fumes, and overexposure to <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_light" class="mw-redirect" title="Ultraviolet light">ultraviolet light</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazing">Brazing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Brazing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Brazing" title="Brazing">Brazing</a></div>
<p><i>Brazing</i> is a joining process in which a filler metal is melted and drawn into a <a href="/wiki/Capillary_action" title="Capillary action">capillary</a> formed by the assembly of two or more work pieces.  The filler metal reacts metallurgically with the workpiece(s) and solidifies in the capillary, forming a strong joint.  Unlike welding, the work piece is not melted.  Brazing is similar to soldering, but occurs at temperatures in excess of 450&#160;°C (842&#160;°F).  Brazing has the advantage of producing less thermal stresses than welding, and brazed assemblies tend to be more ductile than weldments because alloying elements can not segregate and precipitate.
</p><p>Brazing techniques include, flame brazing, resistance brazing, furnace brazing, diffusion brazing, inductive brazing and vacuum brazing.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soldering">Soldering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Soldering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Soldering" title="Soldering">Soldering</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Soldering-PCB-b.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Soldering-PCB-b.jpg/220px-Soldering-PCB-b.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Soldering-PCB-b.jpg/330px-Soldering-PCB-b.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Soldering-PCB-b.jpg/440px-Soldering-PCB-b.jpg 2x" data-file-width="954" data-file-height="716" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Soldering-PCB-b.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Soldering a printed circuit board.</div></div></div>
<p><i>Soldering</i> is a joining process that occurs at temperatures below 450&#160;°C (842&#160;°F). It is similar to brazing in the way that a filler is melted and drawn into a capillary to form a join, although at a lower temperature. Because of this lower temperature and different alloys used as fillers, the metallurgical reaction between filler and work piece is minimal, resulting in a weaker joint.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Riveting">Riveting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Riveting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rivet" title="Rivet">Rivet</a></div>
<p><i>Riveting</i> is one of the most ancient metalwork joining processes. Its use declined markedly during the second half of the 20th century, but it still retains important uses in industry and construction, and in artisan crafts such as <a href="/wiki/Jewellery" title="Jewellery">jewellery</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medieval_armour" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval armour">medieval armouring</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metal_couture" title="Metal couture">metal couture</a> in the early 21st century. The earlier use of rivets is being superseded by improvements in <a href="/wiki/Welding" title="Welding">welding</a> and component <a href="/wiki/Fabrication_(metal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fabrication (metal)">fabrication</a> techniques.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Rivet" title="Rivet">rivet</a> is essentially a two-headed and unthreaded <a href="/wiki/Screw" title="Screw">bolt</a> which holds two other pieces of metal together. Holes are <a href="/wiki/Drill" title="Drill">drilled</a> or <a href="/wiki/Punch_(tool)" title="Punch (tool)">punched</a> through the two pieces of metal to be joined. The holes being aligned, a rivet is passed through the holes and permanent heads are formed onto the ends of the rivet utilizing hammers and forming dies (by either <a href="/wiki/Cold" title="Cold">coldworking</a> or <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hotworking&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hotworking (page does not exist)">hotworking</a>).
Rivets are commonly purchased with one head already formed.
</p><p>When it is necessary to remove rivets, one of the rivet's heads is sheared off with a <a href="/wiki/Chisel" title="Chisel">cold chisel</a>. The rivet is then driven out with a <a href="/wiki/Hammer" title="Hammer">hammer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Punch_(tool)" title="Punch (tool)">punch</a>.
</p>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Associated_processes">Associated processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Associated processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While these processes are not primary metalworking processes, they are often performed before or after metalworking processes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heat_treatment">Heat treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Heat treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Heat_treatment" class="mw-redirect" title="Heat treatment">Heat treatment</a></div>
<p>Metals can be heat treated to alter the properties of strength, ductility, toughness, hardness or resistance to corrosion.  Common heat treatment processes include <a href="/wiki/Annealing_(metallurgy)" title="Annealing (metallurgy)">annealing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_hardening" title="Precipitation hardening">precipitation hardening</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quenching" title="Quenching">quenching</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tempering_(metallurgy)" title="Tempering (metallurgy)">tempering</a>.  The <b>annealing</b> process softens the metal by allowing recovery of cold work and grain growth.  <b>Quenching</b> can be used to harden alloy steels, or in precipitation hardenable alloys, to trap dissolved solute atoms in solution.  <b>Tempering</b> will cause the dissolved alloying elements to precipitate, or in the case of quenched steels, improve impact strength and ductile properties.
</p><p>Often, mechanical and thermal treatments are combined in what is known as thermo-mechanical treatments for better properties and more efficient processing of materials.  These processes are common to high alloy special steels, super alloys and titanium alloys.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plating">Plating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Plating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plating" title="Plating">Plating</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Electroplating" title="Electroplating">Electroplating</a> is a common surface-treatment technique. It involves bonding a thin layer of another metal such as <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chromium" title="Chromium">chromium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">zinc</a> to the surface of the product by hydrolysis. It is used to reduce corrosion, create abrasion resistance and improve the product's aesthetic appearance. Plating can even change the properties of the original part including conductivity, heat dissipation or structural integrity. There are four main electroplating methods to ensure proper coating and cost effectiveness per product: mass plating, rack plating, continuous plating and line plating.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thermal_spraying">Thermal spraying</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metalworking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Thermal spraying">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Thermal_spraying" title="Thermal spraying">Thermal spraying</a></div>
<p>Thermal spraying techniques are another popular finishing option, and often have better high temperature properties than electroplated coatings due to the thicker coating. The four main thermal spray processes include electric wire arc spray, flame (oxy acetylene combustion) spray, plasma spray and high velocity oxy fuel (HVOF) spray.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
