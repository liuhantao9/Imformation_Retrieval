<p><b>Indian names</b> are based on a variety of systems and <a href="/wiki/Naming_conventions" class="mw-redirect" title="Naming conventions">naming conventions</a>, which vary from region to region. Names are also influenced by religion and <a href="/wiki/Caste" title="Caste">caste</a> and may come from <a href="/wiki/Indian_epic_poetry" title="Indian epic poetry">epics</a>. <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>'s population speaks a <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_India" title="Languages of India">wide variety</a> of languages and nearly every major <a href="/wiki/Religions_of_the_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Religions of the world">religion</a> in the world has a following in India. This variety makes for subtle, often confusing, differences in names and naming styles. Due to <a href="/wiki/Greater_India" title="Greater India">historical Indian cultural influences</a>, several names across <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> are influenced or adaptations of Indian names or words.
</p><p>For some Indians, their <b>birth name</b> is different from their <b>official name</b>; the birth name starts with a randomly selected name from the <a href="/wiki/Hindu_astrology" title="Hindu astrology">person's horoscope</a> (based on the <a href="/wiki/Nakshatra" title="Nakshatra">nakshatra</a> or lunar mansion corresponding to the person's birth).
</p><p>Many children are given three names, sometimes as a part of religious teaching.
</p>

<h2><span id="Pronunciation.5Bneeds_IPA.5D"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Pronunciation[needs_IPA]">Pronunciation<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Pronunciation" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Pronunciation"><span title="IPA transcription needed for this word. Although some languages, like Kannada or Tamil may have different vowel sounds, the ones used in most major Indian languages are represented in this table along with typical English transcriptions.

<p>Thus 'Ekamresh'  is pronounced /eka:mresh/ The vowels /æ/ and short 'o' of American English are absent in Indian languages and their use can often result in mispronunciation of Indian names.
</p><p>Furthermore, the letters used in English for the retroflex stops /ʈ/ and /ɖ/ are also used to sound dental stops (as in tenginkai or rohit), especially when they occur in the beginning of a word. As an example, the India name 'Dev' would not have its first consonant pronounced as in the American name 'Dave'. Similarly the name 'Tarun' would not have its first consonant sounded as in 'Tom'.
</p><p>The letter 'h' is used to represent aspirated consonants. So, in the names 'Khare', 'Ghanshyam', 'Kaccha', 'Jhumki', 'Vitthal', 'Ranchodh', 'Thimmayya', 'Uddhav', 'Phaneesh', and 'Bhanu,' the 'h' means the sound before it should be pronounced with a strong outward breath (see <a href="/wiki/Aspirated_consonant" title="Aspirated consonant">Aspirated consonant</a> for more on this). These names are more likely to be found in places that speak an Indo-Aryan language like Bhojpuri or Gujarati.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_by_states">Names by states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Names by states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bengali_names">Bengali names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bengali names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bengali_Brahmin" class="mw-redirect" title="Bengali Brahmin">Bengali Brahmin</a> surnames include <i><a href="/wiki/Banerjee" title="Banerjee">Banerjee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chatterjee" title="Chatterjee">Chatterjee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ganguly_(surname)" title="Ganguly (surname)">Ganguly</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ghoshal_(surname)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ghoshal (surname) (page does not exist)">Ghoshal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goswami" title="Goswami">Goswami</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mukherjee" title="Mukherjee">Mukherjee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nath" title="Nath">Nath</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sanyal" title="Sanyal">Sanyal</a></i>, etc. A Brahmin name is often the name of the clan or gotra, but can be an honorific, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Chakraborty" title="Chakraborty">Chakraborty</a> or <a href="/wiki/Bhattacharya" class="mw-redirect" title="Bhattacharya">Bhattacharya</a></i>.
</p><p>Common <a href="/wiki/Baidya" title="Baidya">Baidya</a> surnames are <i><a href="/wiki/Sengupta" title="Sengupta">Sengupta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dasgupta" title="Dasgupta">Dasgupta</a>, Duttagupta, <a href="/wiki/Gupta" title="Gupta">Gupta</a>, Sen-Sharma</i>, etc.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bengali_Kayastha" title="Bengali Kayastha">Bengali Kayastha</a> surnames include <i><a href="/wiki/Bose_(surname)" title="Bose (surname)">Basu, Bose</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dutta" title="Dutta">Dutta</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ghosh" title="Ghosh">Ghosh</a>, Choudhury, <a href="/wiki/Guha_(surname)" title="Guha (surname)">Guha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gain_(surname)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gain (surname)">Gain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mitra_(surname)" title="Mitra (surname)">Mitra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singh" title="Singh">Singh</a>/<a href="/wiki/Sinha" title="Sinha">Sinha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sen_(surname)" title="Sen (surname)">Sen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pal_(surname)" title="Pal (surname)">Pal</a>, <a href="/wiki/De_(surname)" title="De (surname)">De/Dey/Deb/Dev</a>, Palit, Chanda/Chandra, <a href="/wiki/Das_(surname)" title="Das (surname)">Das</a>, Dam, Kar, Nandi, Sarkar,Nag, Som</i> etc.<sup id="cite_ref-sharma2005discovery1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sharma2005discovery1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Karnataka">Karnataka</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Karnataka">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>North Karnataka surnames are drawn from the name of the place, food items, dresses, temples, type of people, platforms, cities and profession and so on.<sup id="cite_ref-Names_have_interesting_surnames_in_north_Karnataka_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Names_have_interesting_surnames_in_north_Karnataka-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Surnames are drawn from many other sources. 
</p><p><i>Katti</i> as a suffix is used for soldiers while <i>Karadis</i> is related to local folk art. Surnames according to trade or what they traditionally farm include <i>Vastrad</i> (piece of cloth), <i>Kubasad</i> (blouse), <i>Menasinkai</i> (chilly), <i>Ullagaddi</i> (onion), <i>Limbekai</i>, <i>Ballolli</i> (garlic), <i>Tenginkai</i> (coconut), <i>Byali</i> (pulse) and <i>Akki</i> (rice). Surnames based on house include <i>Doddamani</i> (big house), <i>Hadimani</i> (house next to the road), <i>Kattimani</i> (house with a platform in its front), <i>Bevinmarad</i> (person having a big <a href="/wiki/Neem" class="mw-redirect" title="Neem">neem</a> tree near his house) and <i>Hunasimarad</i> (person having a big <a href="/wiki/Tamarind" title="Tamarind">tamarind</a> tree near his house). 
</p><p>A carpenter will have <i>Badigar</i> as a surname while <i>Mirjankar</i>, <i>Belagavi</i>, <i>Hublikar</i> and <i>Jamkhandi</i> are surnames drawn from places. <i>Angadi</i> (shop), <i>Amavasya</i> (new moon day), <i>Kage</i> (crow), <i>Bandi</i> (bullock cart), <i>Kuri</i> (sheep), <i>Kudari</i> (horse), <i>Toppige</i> (cap), <i>Beegadkai</i> (key), <i>Pyati</i> (market), <i>Hanagi</i> (comb) and <i>Rotti</i> (bread) are some other surnames.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kashmir">Kashmir</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Kashmir">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kashmiri_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Kashmiri people">Kashmiri</a> names often have the following format: <b>first name</b>, <b>middle name</b> (optional), <b>family name</b>. (For example: <a href="/wiki/Jawahar_Lal_Nehru" class="mw-redirect" title="Jawahar Lal Nehru">Jawahar Lal Nehru</a>)
</p><p>Nicknames often replace family names. Hence, some family names like Razdan and Nehru may very well be derived originally from the Kaul family tree.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Goa">Goa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Goa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Konkani_people" title="Konkani people">Konkani people</a> inhabiting Goa, and also Konkan regions of Karnataka and Maharashtra, are traditionally patriarchal. Many of the originally Hindu residents were converted to Catholicism by the Portuguese. Generally, the first name is followed by the father's name, though this is now mostly observed by Hindus.<sup id="cite_ref-pat_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pat-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Village names were used only after the arrival of the Portuguese, when the people migrated from their ancestral villages. A suffix <i>kar</i> or <i>hailing from</i> was attached to the village name.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Almost all the Konkani Catholics have Portuguese surnames like <i>Rodrigues, Fernandes, Pereira and D'Souza</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-catholic_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-catholic-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Catholic families belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Brahmin" title="Roman Catholic Brahmin">Roman Catholic Brahmin</a> (Bamonn) caste use lusophonised versions of Hindu surnames like <i>Prabhu, Bhat, etc</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Porbu_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Porbu-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tamil_Nadu">Tamil Nadu<span id="Tamil_names"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tamil Nadu">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Usually, Tamil names follow this pattern: <b>Initial</b> (Village name), <b>Initial</b> (Father's name), <b>First Name</b>, <b>Caste name</b> (Example: E.V. Ramasamy, where E stands for Erode, and V stands for the father's name).There is a widespread usage of a <a href="/wiki/Patronym" class="mw-redirect" title="Patronym">patronym</a> (use of the father's first name as the second name). This means that the first name of one generation becomes the second name of the next. In many cases, the father's name appear as an initial and thus the first name may be presented as a second name. When written in full (for example, on a passport<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>), the initial is expanded as last name. For example, a name like "R. Ramesh" or "Ramesh R.", will be written in full as "Ramesh Ramaiah", and refers to "Ramesh son of Ramaiah". If Ramesh then has a son named Ashwin, then his name would be "R. Ashwin" or "Ashwin Ramesh" as it would be in the West. There is also a general custom for Tamil women to adopt their husband's first name as their second name. Saravanan Sunitha (Sunitha daughter of Saravanan) might change her name to Ram Kumar Sunitha (Sunitha wife of Ram Kumar) after marriage. However, these customs vary from family to family and are normally never carried on over successive generations.
</p><p>More common among women, making the patronym or husband name the last name is a custom adopted by people migrating to the West who want to be called by their first names without having to explain Indian naming conventions. In earlier times a caste name or village name was used by the Tamils as their last name, but the present day generation is wary to do so. However, people influenced by northern India or western civilization frequently adopt their father's or husband's name and take it for successive generations.
</p><p>The various Tamil caste names include <i><a href="/wiki/Paraiyar" title="Paraiyar">Paraiyar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vishwakarma_(caste)" title="Vishwakarma (caste)">Vishwakarma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vishwakarma_(caste)" title="Vishwakarma (caste)">Aachari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Konar_(caste)" title="Konar (caste)">Konar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Konar_(caste)" title="Konar (caste)">Idaiyar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reddiar" title="Reddiar">Reddiar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Udayar_(caste)" title="Udayar (caste)">Udayar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yadav" title="Yadav">Yadhavar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iyer" title="Iyer">Iyer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iyengar" title="Iyengar">Iyengar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pillai_(surname)" title="Pillai (surname)">Pillai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mudaliar" title="Mudaliar">Mudaliar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thevar" class="mw-redirect" title="Thevar">Thevar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nadar_(caste)" title="Nadar (caste)">Nadar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chettiar" title="Chettiar">Chettiar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gounder" title="Gounder">Gounder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naicker" class="mw-redirect" title="Naicker">Naicker</a></i> etc. The naming is therefore done in the fashion: Sunitha Ram Kumar Iyer. Hindus in Tamil Nadu view the practice of adding the full family name to an individual's name to be a heretic practice, as according to their beliefs, the individual's heritage does not trump his or her own identity. And hence they are known to only use initials besides their name except for when caste names are given more preference by certain families rather than the family name itself.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indexing">Indexing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Indexing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Manual_of_Style" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago Manual of Style">Chicago Manual of Style</a>, Indian names are usually <a href="/wiki/Index_(publishing)" title="Index (publishing)">indexed</a> by the family name, with the family name separated from the other names by a comma, but indexing may differ according to the local usage and the preferences of the individual.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

Nehru Says: <br />
<i>We were Kashmiris. Over two hundred years ago, early in the eighteenth century, our ancestor came down from that mountain valley to seek fame and fortune in the rich plains below. Those were the days of the decline of the Moghal Empire. <br /> Raj Kaul was the name of that ancestor of ours, and he had gained eminence as a Sanskrit and Persian scholar. He attracted the notice of the Emperor and, probably at his instance, the family migrated to Delhi, the imperial capital, about the year 1716. A jagir with a house situated on the banks of a canal had been granted to Raj Kaul, and, from the fact of this residence, "Nehru" (from nahar, a canal) came to be attached to his name. Kaul had been the family name; in later years, this dropped out and we became simply Nehrus.</i></span>
