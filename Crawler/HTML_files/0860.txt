	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">D. R. Thorpe</h1>

<b>D. R. (Richard) Thorpe</b> (born 1943) is a historian and biographer who has written biographies of three British Prime Ministers of the mid 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Sir_Anthony_Eden" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Anthony Eden">Sir Anthony Eden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sir_Alec_Douglas-Home" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Alec Douglas-Home">Sir Alec Douglas-Home</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harold_Macmillan" title="Harold Macmillan">Harold Macmillan</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_academic_career">Education and academic career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D._R._Thorpe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education and academic career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thorpe was educated at <a href="/wiki/Fettes_College" title="Fettes College">Fettes College</a>, Edinburgh and <a href="/wiki/Selwyn_College,_Cambridge" title="Selwyn College, Cambridge">Selwyn College, Cambridge</a>. He taught history at <a href="/wiki/Charterhouse_School" title="Charterhouse School">Charterhouse</a>, a public school in <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a>, for over 30 years. Among other academic appointments, he was Archives Fellow of <a href="/wiki/Churchill_College,_Cambridge" title="Churchill College, Cambridge">Churchill College, Cambridge</a>, and a Fellow of <a href="/wiki/St_Antony%27s_College,_Oxford" title="St Antony&#39;s College, Oxford">St Antony's College, Oxford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brasenose_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Brasenose College">Brasenose College</a>, Oxford.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Published_works">Published works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D._R._Thorpe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Published works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thorpe's first book, <i>The Uncrowned Prime Ministers</i> (1980), examined the careers of <a href="/wiki/Austen_Chamberlain" title="Austen Chamberlain">Austen Chamberlain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lord_Curzon" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Curzon">Lord Curzon</a> and <a href="/wiki/R._A._Butler" class="mw-redirect" title="R. A. Butler">R. A. Butler</a>, three men who came close to reaching the "top of the greasy pole" (<a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Disraeli" title="Benjamin Disraeli">Disraeli</a>'s phrase,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> applied by Thorpe to Home's ascent to the premiership<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>). He was subsequently the official biographer of the former Foreign Secretary, Chancellor of the Exchequer and <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="Speaker of the British House of Commons">Speaker</a> of the House of Commons <a href="/wiki/Selwyn_Lloyd" title="Selwyn Lloyd">Selwyn Lloyd</a> (1989) and of Sir Alec Douglas-Home (1996) and Sir Anthony Eden (2003).
</p>
<h3><span id="Selwyn_Lloyd.2C_Douglas-Home_and_Anthony_Eden"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Selwyn_Lloyd,_Douglas-Home_and_Anthony_Eden">Selwyn Lloyd, Douglas-Home and Anthony Eden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=D._R._Thorpe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Selwyn Lloyd, Douglas-Home and Anthony Eden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As with Sir <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rhodes_James" title="Robert Rhodes James">Robert Rhodes James</a>'s earlier official biography of Eden (1986), Thorpe's, which was undertaken at the invitation of Eden's widow, <a href="/wiki/Clarissa_Eden,_Countess_of_Avon" class="mw-redirect" title="Clarissa Eden, Countess of Avon">Clarissa</a>, in 1991,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> did much to restore Eden's reputation, which had suffered considerably in the aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/Suez_Crisis" title="Suez Crisis">Suez Crisis</a> of 1956. Indeed, a feature of Thorpe’s biographical trilogy was that all three subjects, though having each held two of the three great offices of the British state (Eden and Home, in addition to being Prime Minister, both served more than one term as Foreign Secretary), tended, in retrospect, to be underestimated.  Lloyd was remembered as Eden’s compliant Foreign Secretary at the time of Suez who, as Chancellor, was dismissed ignominiously by Harold Macmillan in a major Cabinet reshuffle (the so-called "<a href="/wiki/Night_of_the_Long_Knives_(1962)" title="Night of the Long Knives (1962)">Night of the Long Knives</a>") in 1962; Home, then an <a href="/wiki/Hereditary_peer" title="Hereditary peer">hereditary peer</a>, seemed to many an unlikely choice to succeed Macmillan as Prime Minister in 1963 and narrowly lost a general election less than a year later; and Eden, though widely admired for his work at the Foreign Office, attracted, after his short, but momentous premiership, the famous judgement of <a href="/wiki/Tacitus" title="Tacitus">Tacitus</a> on the Roman Emperor <a href="/wiki/Galba" title="Galba">Galba</a>, <i>Omnium consensu capax imperii nisi imperasset</i><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> ("All would have pronounced him worthy of empire if he had never been emperor").
</p><p>After completing <i>Eden</i>, Thorpe began work on a biography of Harold Macmillan. This was published as <i>Supermac: The Life of Harold Macmillan</i> by Chatto &amp; Windus on 9 September 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  It is described by Vernon Bogdanor, Professor of Government at Oxford University, as 'the best biography of a post-war British Prime Minister yet written.'  The book was one of the shortlisted six books (from a record 213 entries) for the prestigious <a href="/wiki/Orwell_Prize" title="Orwell Prize">Orwell Prize</a> for political writing 2010.   In August 2011 the book was one of the shortlisted six books (from over  100 entries) for the English Speaking Union Marsh Biography award, to be presented in November 2011.
On 22 November <i>Supermac</i> was awarded the Biennial English Speaking Union Marsh Biography Award at a dinner at Dartmouth House, Headquarters of the English Speaking Union in London.
</p><p>Thorpe is now editing the six million word Journals of the late Kenneth Rose for publication.  Volume One will be published with the title "Who's In Who's Out: The Journals of Kenneth Rose, Volume 1, 1944-1979" by Weidenfeld &amp; Nicolson on 1 November 2018.  Volume 2 "Who Loses Who Wins: The Journals of Kenneth Rose, Volume 2, 1979-2014" will follow in July 2019.
</p>
