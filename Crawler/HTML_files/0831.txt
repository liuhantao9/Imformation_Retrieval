<p><b>Irish republicanism</b> (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">poblachtánachas Éireannach</i>) is the <a href="/wiki/Political_movement" title="Political movement">political movement</a> for the <a href="/wiki/United_Ireland" title="United Ireland">unity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Independence" title="Independence">independence</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. The development of <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">nationalist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democratic</a> sentiment throughout Europe in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries was reflected in Ireland in the emergence of republicanism, in opposition to <a href="/wiki/History_of_Ireland_(1801%E2%80%931923)" title="History of Ireland (1801–1923)">British rule</a>. This followed hundreds of years of British conquest and Irish resistance through rebellion.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Discrimination against <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church_in_Ireland" title="Catholic Church in Ireland">Catholics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nonconformist" title="Nonconformist">nonconformists</a>, attempts by the British administration to suppress <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Ireland" title="Culture of Ireland">Irish culture</a>, and the belief that Ireland was economically disadvantaged as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1800" title="Acts of Union 1800">Acts of Union</a> were among the specific factors leading to such opposition.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Society_of_United_Irishmen" title="Society of United Irishmen">Society of United Irishmen</a>, formed in 1791 and led primarily by liberal Protestants,<sup id="cite_ref-Connolly_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Connolly-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> evolved into a revolutionary republican organisation, inspired by the American Revolution and allied with Revolutionary France. It launched the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Rebellion_of_1798" title="Irish Rebellion of 1798">1798 Rebellion</a> with the help of French troops. The rebellion had some success, especially in <a href="/wiki/County_Wexford" title="County Wexford">County Wexford</a>, before it was suppressed. A second rising in 1803, led by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Emmet" title="Robert Emmet">Robert Emmet</a>, was quickly put down, and Emmet was hanged. The <a href="/wiki/Young_Ireland" title="Young Ireland">Young Ireland</a> movement, formed in the 1830s, was initially a part of the <a href="/wiki/Repeal_Association" title="Repeal Association">Repeal Association</a> of <a href="/wiki/Daniel_O%27Connell" title="Daniel O&#39;Connell">Daniel O'Connell</a>, but broke with O'Connell on the issue of the legitimacy of the use of violence. Primarily a political and cultural organisation, some members of Young Ireland staged an abortive rising, the <a href="/wiki/Young_Irelander_Rebellion_of_1848" title="Young Irelander Rebellion of 1848">Young Irelander Rebellion of 1848</a>. Its leaders were <a href="/wiki/Penal_transportation" title="Penal transportation">transported</a> to <a href="/wiki/Van_Diemen%27s_Land" title="Van Diemen&#39;s Land">Van Diemen's Land</a>. Some of these escaped to the United States, where they linked up with other Irish exiles to form the <a href="/wiki/Fenian_Brotherhood" title="Fenian Brotherhood">Fenian Brotherhood</a>. Together with the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Brotherhood" title="Irish Republican Brotherhood">Irish Republican Brotherhood</a>, founded in Ireland by <a href="/wiki/James_Stephens_(Fenian)" title="James Stephens (Fenian)">James Stephens</a> and others in 1858, they made up a movement commonly known as "<a href="/wiki/Fenian" title="Fenian">Fenians</a>" which was dedicated to the overthrow of British imperial rule in Ireland. They staged another rising, the <a href="/wiki/Fenian_Rising" title="Fenian Rising">Fenian Rising</a>, in 1867, and a <a href="/wiki/Fenian_dynamite_campaign" title="Fenian dynamite campaign">dynamite campaign</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> in the 1880s.
</p><p>In the early 20th century IRB members, in particular <a href="/wiki/Tom_Clarke_(Irish_republican)" title="Tom Clarke (Irish republican)">Tom Clarke</a> and <a href="/wiki/Se%C3%A1n_Mac_Diarmada" title="Seán Mac Diarmada">Seán MacDermott</a>, began planning another rising. The <a href="/wiki/Easter_Rising" title="Easter Rising">Easter Rising</a> took place from 24 to 30 April 1916, when members of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Volunteers" title="Irish Volunteers">Irish Volunteers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irish_Citizen_Army" title="Irish Citizen Army">Irish Citizen Army</a> seized the centre of <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Proclamation_of_the_Irish_Republic" title="Proclamation of the Irish Republic">proclaimed</a> a republic and held off British forces for almost a week. The execution of the Rising's leaders, including Clarke, MacDermott, <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Pearse" title="Patrick Pearse">Patrick Pearse</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Connolly" title="James Connolly">James Connolly</a>, led to a surge of support for republicanism in Ireland. In 1917 the <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a> party stated as its aim the "securing the international recognition of Ireland as an independent Irish Republic", and in the <a href="/wiki/1918_Irish_general_election" title="1918 Irish general election">general election of 1918</a> Sinn Féin took 73 of the 105 Irish seats in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">British House of Commons</a>. The elected members did not take their seats but instead set up the <a href="/wiki/First_D%C3%A1il" title="First Dáil">First Dáil</a>. Between 1919 and 1921 the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army_(1919%E2%80%931922)" title="Irish Republican Army (1919–1922)">Irish Republican Army</a> (IRA), who were loyal to the Dáil, fought the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> and <a href="/wiki/Royal_Irish_Constabulary" title="Royal Irish Constabulary">Royal Irish Constabulary</a> (RIC), a predominantly Roman Catholic force, in the <a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence" title="Irish War of Independence">Irish War of Independence</a>. Talks between the British and Irish in late 1921 led to a treaty by which the British conceded, not a 32-county Irish Republic, but a 26-county <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a> with <a href="/wiki/Dominion" title="Dominion">Dominion</a> status. This led to the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Civil_War" title="Irish Civil War">Irish Civil War</a>, in which the republicans were defeated by their former comrades.
</p><p>The Free State became an independent <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a> following the <a href="/wiki/Balfour_Declaration_of_1926" title="Balfour Declaration of 1926">Balfour Declaration of 1926</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Westminster_1931" title="Statute of Westminster 1931">Statute of Westminster 1931</a> and formally became a republic with the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland_Act_1948" title="Republic of Ireland Act 1948">Republic of Ireland Act 1948</a>. That same year, the republican movement took the decision to focus on <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> thereafter. The <a href="/wiki/Border_Campaign_(Irish_Republican_Army)" class="mw-redirect" title="Border Campaign (Irish Republican Army)">Border Campaign</a>, which lasted from 1956 to 1962, involved bombings and attacks on <a href="/wiki/Royal_Ulster_Constabulary" title="Royal Ulster Constabulary">Royal Ulster Constabulary</a> barracks. The failure of this campaign led the republican leadership to concentrate on political action, and to move to the left. Following the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">The Troubles</a> in 1968-9, the movement split between <a href="/wiki/Official_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Official Irish Republican Army">Officials</a> (leftists) and <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Provisional Irish Republican Army">Provisionals</a> (traditionalists) at the beginning of 1970. Both sides were initially involved in an armed campaign against the British state, but the Officials gradually moved into mainstream politics after the Official IRA ceasefire of 1972; the associated "Official Sinn Féin" eventually renamed itself the <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_Party_of_Ireland" title="Workers&#39; Party of Ireland">Workers' Party</a>. The Provisional IRA, except during brief ceasefires in 1972 and 1975, kept up a campaign of violence for nearly thirty years, directed against security forces and civilian targets (especially businesses). While the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_and_Labour_Party" title="Social Democratic and Labour Party">Social Democratic and Labour Party</a> (SDLP) represented the nationalists of Northern Ireland in initiatives such as the 1973 <a href="/wiki/Sunningdale_Agreement" title="Sunningdale Agreement">Sunningdale Agreement</a>, republicans took no part in these, believing that a withdrawal of British troops and a commitment to a united Ireland was a necessary precondition of any settlement. This began to change with a landmark speech by <a href="/wiki/Danny_Morrison_(Irish_republican)" title="Danny Morrison (Irish republican)">Danny Morrison</a> in 1981, advocating what became known as the <a href="/wiki/Armalite_and_ballot_box_strategy" title="Armalite and ballot box strategy">Armalite and ballot box strategy</a>. Under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Adams" title="Gerry Adams">Gerry Adams</a>, Sinn Féin began to focus on the search for a political settlement. When the party voted in 1986 to take seats in legislative bodies within Ireland, there was a walk-out of die-hard republicans, who set up <a href="/wiki/Republican_Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Republican Sinn Féin">Republican Sinn Féin</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Continuity_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Continuity Irish Republican Army">Continuity IRA</a>. Following the <a href="/wiki/Hume%E2%80%93Adams_dialogue" title="Hume–Adams dialogue">Hume–Adams dialogue</a>, Sinn Féin took part in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_peace_process" title="Northern Ireland peace process">Northern Ireland peace process</a> which led to the IRA ceasefires of 1994 and 1997 and the <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a> of 1998. After elections to the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a>, republicans sat in government in Northern Ireland for the first time when <a href="/wiki/Martin_McGuinness" title="Martin McGuinness">Martin McGuinness</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bairbre_de_Br%C3%BAn" title="Bairbre de Brún">Bairbre de Brún</a> were elected to the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Executive" title="Northern Ireland Executive">Northern Ireland Executive</a>. However, another split occurred, with anti-Agreement republicans setting up the <a href="/wiki/32_County_Sovereignty_Movement" title="32 County Sovereignty Movement">32 County Sovereignty Movement</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Real_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Real Irish Republican Army">Real IRA</a>. Today, Irish republicanism is divided between those who support the institutions set up under the Good Friday Agreement and the later <a href="/wiki/St_Andrews_Agreement" title="St Andrews Agreement">St Andrews Agreement</a>, and those who oppose them. The latter are often referred to as "dissident" republicans.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_republicanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_of_English_rule_in_Ireland">Background of English rule in Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_republicanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background of English rule in Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Ireland_in_1609.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Map_of_Ireland_in_1609.gif/200px-Map_of_Ireland_in_1609.gif" decoding="async" width="200" height="250" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Map_of_Ireland_in_1609.gif/300px-Map_of_Ireland_in_1609.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Map_of_Ireland_in_1609.gif/400px-Map_of_Ireland_in_1609.gif 2x" data-file-width="466" data-file-height="582" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Ireland_in_1609.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of Ireland in 1609 showing the major <a href="/wiki/Plantations_of_Ireland" title="Plantations of Ireland">Plantations of Ireland</a></div></div></div>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Norman_invasion_of_Ireland" title="Norman invasion of Ireland">Norman invasion of Ireland</a> in the 12th century, Ireland, or parts of it, had experienced alternating degrees of rule from England. While some of the native <a href="/wiki/Gaels" title="Gaels">Gaelic</a> population attempted to resist this occupation,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> a single, unified political goal did not exist amongst the independent lordships that existed throughout the island. The <a href="/wiki/Tudor_conquest_of_Ireland" title="Tudor conquest of Ireland">Tudor conquest of Ireland</a> took place in the 16th century. This included the <a href="/wiki/Plantations_of_Ireland" title="Plantations of Ireland">Plantations of Ireland</a>, in which the lands held by Gaelic <a href="/wiki/Irish_clans" title="Irish clans">Irish clans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Normans_in_Ireland" title="Normans in Ireland">Hiberno-Norman</a> dynasties were confiscated and given to Protestant settlers ("Planters") from England and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Plantation_of_Ulster" title="Plantation of Ulster">Plantation of Ulster</a> began in 1609, and the <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">province</a> was heavily <a href="/wiki/Plantation_(settlement_or_colony)" title="Plantation (settlement or colony)">colonised</a> with English and Scottish settlers.<sup id="cite_ref-Kee_Robert_1972_p._12_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kee_Robert_1972_p._12-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Campaigns against English presence on the island had occurred prior to the emergence of the Irish republican ideology. In the 1590s, resistance was led by <a href="/wiki/Hugh_O%27Neill,_Earl_of_Tyrone" title="Hugh O&#39;Neill, Earl of Tyrone">Hugh O'Neill</a> (see the <a href="/wiki/Nine_Years%27_War_(Ireland)" title="Nine Years&#39; War (Ireland)">Nine Years' War</a>). The Irish chieftains were ultimately defeated, leading to their exile (the '<a href="/wiki/Flight_of_the_Earls" title="Flight of the Earls">Flight of the Earls</a>') and the aforementioned Plantation of Ulster in 1609.<sup id="cite_ref-Kee_Robert_1972_p._12_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kee_Robert_1972_p._12-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years&#39; War">Thirty Years' War</a>, Irish exiles in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> petitioned <a href="/wiki/Philip_IV_of_Spain" title="Philip IV of Spain">Philip IV</a> to launch an invasion of Ireland and so in December 1627 a document was drafted by Philip's ministers in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> containing among other things the first proposal for an Irish republic with the intention of preventing a conflict between the <a href="/wiki/Shane_O%27Neill_(son_of_Hugh)" class="mw-redirect" title="Shane O&#39;Neill (son of Hugh)">Earls of Tyrone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hugh_O%27Donnell,_2nd_Earl_of_Tyrconnell" title="Hugh O&#39;Donnell, 2nd Earl of Tyrconnell">Tyrconnell</a> over the crown of Ireland. Ultimately however the invasion did not go ahead.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A decade later, the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Rebellion_of_1641" title="Irish Rebellion of 1641">Irish Rebellion of 1641</a> began. This consisted of a coalition between the Irish and the <a href="/wiki/Normans_in_Ireland" title="Normans in Ireland">Old English</a> (descendants of the English/Norman settlers who settled during the Norman Invasion) rebelling against the English rulers. Beginning as a <i>coup d'état</i> with the aim of restoring lost lands in the north of Ireland and defending Catholic religious and property rights,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> (which had been suppressed by the <a href="/wiki/Puritans" title="Puritans">Puritan</a> <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">Parliament of England</a>) it evolved into the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Confederate_Wars" title="Irish Confederate Wars">Irish Confederate Wars</a>. In the summer of 1642, the Catholic upper classes formed the <a href="/wiki/Confederate_Ireland" title="Confederate Ireland">Catholic Confederation</a>, which essentially became the de facto government of Ireland for a brief period until 1649, when the forces of the English Parliament carried out the <a href="/wiki/Cromwellian_conquest_of_Ireland" title="Cromwellian conquest of Ireland">Cromwellian conquest of Ireland</a> and the old Catholic landowners were permanently dispossessed of their lands.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_of_United_Irishmen_and_the_Irish_Rebellion_of_1798">Society of United Irishmen and the Irish Rebellion of 1798</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_republicanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Society of United Irishmen and the Irish Rebellion of 1798">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Society_of_United_Irishmen" title="Society of United Irishmen">Society of United Irishmen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irish_Rebellion_of_1798" title="Irish Rebellion of 1798">Irish Rebellion of 1798</a></div>
<p>Irish republicanism has its origins in the ideals of the American and French revolutions in the late 18th century. In Ireland these ideals were taken up by the United Irishmen, founded in 1791. Originally they sought reform of the Irish parliament, such as an end to sectarian discrimination against <a href="/wiki/Dissenter" title="Dissenter">Dissenters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irish_Catholics" title="Irish Catholics">Catholics</a>, which was enshrined in the <a href="/wiki/Penal_Laws_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Penal Laws (Ireland)">Penal Laws</a>. Eventually they became a revolutionary group advocating an Irish republic free from British control.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Theobald_Wolfe_Tone_-_Project_Gutenberg_13112.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Theobald_Wolfe_Tone_-_Project_Gutenberg_13112.png/170px-Theobald_Wolfe_Tone_-_Project_Gutenberg_13112.png" decoding="async" width="170" height="265" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Theobald_Wolfe_Tone_-_Project_Gutenberg_13112.png/255px-Theobald_Wolfe_Tone_-_Project_Gutenberg_13112.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Theobald_Wolfe_Tone_-_Project_Gutenberg_13112.png/340px-Theobald_Wolfe_Tone_-_Project_Gutenberg_13112.png 2x" data-file-width="350" data-file-height="545" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Theobald_Wolfe_Tone_-_Project_Gutenberg_13112.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Wolfe_Tone" title="Wolfe Tone">Wolfe Tone</a> circa 1794. Tone is considered by many as the father of Irish Republicanism</div></div></div>
<p>At this stage, the movement was led primarily by liberal Protestants,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> particularly <a href="/wiki/Presbyterianism" title="Presbyterianism">Presbyterians</a> from the province of Ulster.The Founding members of the United Irishmen were mainly Southern Irish Protestant aristocrats. The key founders included <a href="/wiki/Wolfe_Tone" title="Wolfe Tone">Wolfe Tone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Russell_(rebel)" title="Thomas Russell (rebel)">Thomas Russell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Joy_McCracken" title="Henry Joy McCracken">Henry Joy McCracken</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Napper_Tandy" title="James Napper Tandy">James Napper Tandy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Neilson" title="Samuel Neilson">Samuel Neilson</a>. By 1797, the Society of United Irishmen had around 100,000 members. Crossing the religious divide in Ireland, it had a mixed membership of Catholics, Presbyterians, and even <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglicans</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Ascendancy" title="Protestant Ascendancy">Protestant Ascendancy</a>. It also attracted support and membership from Catholic <a href="/wiki/Agrarianism" title="Agrarianism">agrarian</a> resistance groups, such as the <a href="/wiki/Defenders_(Ireland)" title="Defenders (Ireland)">Defenders</a> organisation, who were eventually incorporated into the Society.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Battle_of_Killala_1798.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Battle_of_Killala_1798.JPG/220px-Battle_of_Killala_1798.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Battle_of_Killala_1798.JPG/330px-Battle_of_Killala_1798.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Battle_of_Killala_1798.JPG/440px-Battle_of_Killala_1798.JPG 2x" data-file-width="896" data-file-height="589" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Battle_of_Killala_1798.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Killala" title="Battle of Killala">Battle of Killala</a> marked the end of <a href="/wiki/Irish_Rebellion_of_1798" title="Irish Rebellion of 1798">the rising</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Irish_Rebellion_of_1798" title="Irish Rebellion of 1798">Irish Rebellion of 1798</a> began on 23 May, with the first clashes taking place in <a href="/wiki/County_Kildare" title="County Kildare">County Kildare</a> on 24 May, before spreading throughout <a href="/wiki/Leinster" title="Leinster">Leinster</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/County_Antrim" title="County Antrim">County Antrim</a> and other areas of the country. French soldiers landed in <a href="/wiki/Killala" title="Killala">Killala</a> on 22 August and participated in the fighting on the rebels' side.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Even though they had considerable success against British forces in <a href="/wiki/County_Wexford" title="County Wexford">County Wexford</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> rebel forces were eventually defeated. Key figures in the organisation were arrested and executed.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acts_of_Union">Acts of Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_republicanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Acts of Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1800" title="Acts of Union 1800">Acts of Union 1800</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Ireland" title="Parliament of Ireland">Parliament of Ireland</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dwyer.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Dwyer.jpg/170px-Dwyer.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="250" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Dwyer.jpg/255px-Dwyer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Dwyer.jpg/340px-Dwyer.jpg 2x" data-file-width="374" data-file-height="550" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dwyer.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Michael_Dwyer" title="Michael Dwyer">Michael Dwyer</a></div></div></div>
<p>Though the Rebellion of 1798 was eventually crushed, small republican <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_warfare" title="Guerrilla warfare">guerrilla campaigns</a> against the British Army continued for a short time afterward in the <a href="/wiki/Wicklow_Mountains" title="Wicklow Mountains">Wicklow Mountains</a> under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Michael_Dwyer" title="Michael Dwyer">Michael Dwyer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Holt_(rebel)" title="Joseph Holt (rebel)">Joseph Holt</a>, involving attacks on small parties of <a href="/wiki/Yeoman" title="Yeoman">yeomen</a>. These activities were perceived by some to be merely "the dying echoes of an old convulsion",<sup id="cite_ref-Kee_Robert_1972_p._149_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kee_Robert_1972_p._149-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> but others feared further large-scale uprisings, due to the United Irishmen continuing to attract large numbers of Catholics in rural areas of the country and arms raids being carried out on a nightly basis.<sup id="cite_ref-Kee_Robert_1972_p._149_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kee_Robert_1972_p._149-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> It was also feared that rebels would again seek military aid from French troops, and another rising was expected take place by 10 April.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This perceived threat of further rebellion resulted in the Parliamentary Union between the Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland. After some uncertainty, the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Ireland" title="Parliament of Ireland">Irish Parliament</a> voted to abolish itself in the Acts of Union 1800, forming the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland, by a vote of 158 to 115.<sup id="cite_ref-Webster,_Hollis_2001_p._83_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Webster,_Hollis_2001_p._83-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> A number of tactics were used to achieve this end. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stewart,_Viscount_Castlereagh" title="Robert Stewart, Viscount Castlereagh">Lord Castlereagh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Cornwallis,_1st_Marquess_Cornwallis" title="Charles Cornwallis, 1st Marquess Cornwallis">Charles Cornwallis</a> were known to use bribery extensively. In all, a total of sixteen Irish borough-owners were granted British <a href="/wiki/Peerage" title="Peerage">peerages</a>. A further twenty-eight new <a href="/wiki/Peerage_of_Ireland" title="Peerage of Ireland">Irish peerages</a> were created, while twenty existing Irish peerages increased in rank.<sup id="cite_ref-Kee_Robert_1972_p._158_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kee_Robert_1972_p._158-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Furthermore, the government of Great Britain sought to replace Irish politicians in the Irish parliament with pro-Union politicians, and rewards were granted to those that vacated their seats, with the result being that in the eighteen months prior to the decision in 1800, one-fifth of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_House_of_Commons" title="Irish House of Commons">Irish House of Commons</a> changed its representation due to these activities and other factors such as death.<sup id="cite_ref-Kee_Robert_1972_p._158_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kee_Robert_1972_p._158-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It was also promised by Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/William_Pitt_the_Younger" title="William Pitt the Younger">William Pitt the Younger</a> that he would bring about <a href="/wiki/Catholic_emancipation" title="Catholic emancipation">Catholic emancipation</a>, though after the Acts of Union were successfully voted through, <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="George III of the United Kingdom">King George III</a> saw that this pledge was never realised,<sup id="cite_ref-Webster,_Hollis_2001_p._83_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Webster,_Hollis_2001_p._83-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and as such Catholics were not granted the rights that had been promised prior to the Acts.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Robert_Emmet">Robert Emmet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_republicanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Robert Emmet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A second attempt at forming an independent Irish republic occurred under <a href="/wiki/Robert_Emmet" title="Robert Emmet">Robert Emmet</a> in 1803. Emmet had previously been expelled from <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College_Dublin" title="Trinity College Dublin">Trinity College</a>, Dublin for his political views.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Like those who had led the 1798 rebellion, Emmet was a member of the United Irishmen, as was his brother <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Addis_Emmet" title="Thomas Addis Emmet">Thomas Addis Emmet</a>, who had been imprisoned for membership in the organisation.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Robert_Emmet_-_Trial.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Robert_Emmet_-_Trial.jpg/220px-Robert_Emmet_-_Trial.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="168" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Robert_Emmet_-_Trial.jpg/330px-Robert_Emmet_-_Trial.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Robert_Emmet_-_Trial.jpg/440px-Robert_Emmet_-_Trial.jpg 2x" data-file-width="634" data-file-height="485" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Robert_Emmet_-_Trial.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Depiction of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Emmet" title="Robert Emmet">Robert Emmet</a>'s trial</div></div></div>
<p>Emmet and his followers had planned to seize <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Castle" title="Dublin Castle">Dublin Castle</a> by force, manufacturing weaponry and explosives at a number of locations in Dublin.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Unlike those of 1798, preparations for the uprising were successfully concealed from the government and law enforcement, and though a premature explosion at an arms depot attracted the attention of police, they were unaware of the United Irishmen activities at the time and did not have any information regarding the planned rebellion. Emmet had hoped to avoid the complications of the previous rebellion and chose not to organise the county outside of Dublin to a large extent. It was expected that the areas surrounding Dublin were sufficiently prepared for an uprising should one be announced, and Thomas Russell had been sent to northern areas of the country to prepare republicans there.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A proclamation of independence, addressed from 'The Provisional Government' to 'The People of Ireland' was produced by Emmet, echoing the republican sentiments expressed during the previous rebellion:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>You are now called on to show to the world that you are competent to take your place among nations, that you have a right to claim their recognisance of you, as an independent country ... We therefore solemnly declare, that our object is to establish a free and independent republic in Ireland: that the pursuit of this object we will relinquish only with our lives ... We war against no religious sect ... We war against English dominion.
<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite>Robert Emmet, <i>Proclamation of the Provisional Government</i></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>However, failed communications and arrangements produced a considerably smaller force than had been anticipated. Nonetheless, the rebellion began in Dublin on the evening of 23 July. Emmet's forces were unable to take Dublin Castle, and the rising broke down into rioting, which ensued sporadically throughout the night. Emmet escaped and hid for some time in the Wicklow Mountains and <a href="/wiki/Harold%27s_Cross" title="Harold&#39;s Cross">Harold's Cross</a>, but was captured on 25 August and hanged on 20 September 1803, at which point the Society of United Irishmen was effectively finished.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Young_Ireland_and_the_Irish_Confederation">Young Ireland and the Irish Confederation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_republicanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Young Ireland and the Irish Confederation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Young_Ireland" title="Young Ireland">Young Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Young_Irelander_Rebellion_of_1848" title="Young Irelander Rebellion of 1848">Young Irelander Rebellion of 1848</a>, <a href="/wiki/Repeal_Association" title="Repeal Association">Repeal Association</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Irish_Confederation" title="Irish Confederation">Irish Confederation</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Young_Ireland" title="Young Ireland">Young Ireland</a> movement began in the late 1830s. The term 'Young Ireland' was originally a derogatory one, coined by the press in Britain to describe members of the <a href="/wiki/Repeal_Association" title="Repeal Association">Repeal Association</a> (a group campaigning for the repeal of the Acts of Union 1800 which joined the Kingdom of Ireland and the Kingdom of Great Britain) who were involved with the Irish nationalist newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nation_(Irish_newspaper)" title="The Nation (Irish newspaper)">The Nation</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Duffy,_Charles_Gavan_1880_p._291_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duffy,_Charles_Gavan_1880_p._291-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Encouraging the repeal of the Acts of Union, members of the Young Ireland movement advocated the removal of British authority from Ireland and the re-establishment of the Irish Parliament in Dublin.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The group had cultural aims also, and encouraged the study of Irish history and the revival of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish language</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Influential Young Irelanders included <a href="/wiki/Charles_Gavan_Duffy" title="Charles Gavan Duffy">Charles Gavan Duffy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Davis_(Young_Irelander)" title="Thomas Davis (Young Irelander)">Thomas Davis</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Blake_Dillon" title="John Blake Dillon">John Blake Dillon</a>, the three founders of <i>The Nation</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Duffy,_Charles_Gavan_1880_p._291_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Duffy,_Charles_Gavan_1880_p._291-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Smith_O%27Brien.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/William_Smith_O%27Brien.jpg/170px-William_Smith_O%27Brien.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="201" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/William_Smith_O%27Brien.jpg/255px-William_Smith_O%27Brien.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/William_Smith_O%27Brien.jpg/340px-William_Smith_O%27Brien.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1095" data-file-height="1295" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:William_Smith_O%27Brien.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/William_Smith_O%27Brien" title="William Smith O&#39;Brien">William Smith O'Brien</a>, leader of the <a href="/wiki/Young_Ireland" title="Young Ireland">Young Ireland</a> movement</div></div></div>
<p>The Young Irelanders eventually seceded from the Repeal Association. The leader of the Repeal Association, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_O%27Connell" title="Daniel O&#39;Connell">Daniel O'Connell</a>, opposed the use of physical force to enact repeal, and passed 'peace resolutions' declaring that violence and force were not to be employed.<sup id="cite_ref-doheny105_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-doheny105-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Though the Young Irelanders did not support the use of violence, the writers of <i>The Nation</i> maintained that the introduction of these peace resolutions was poorly timed, and that to declare outright that physical force would never be used was 'to deliver themselves bound hand and foot to the <a href="/wiki/Whigs_(British_political_party)" title="Whigs (British political party)">Whigs</a>.'<sup id="cite_ref-Kee,_Robert_1972_p._253_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kee,_Robert_1972_p._253-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_Smith_O%27Brien" title="William Smith O&#39;Brien">William Smith O'Brien</a>, who had previously worked to achieve compromise between O'Connell and <i>The Nation</i> group, was also concerned, and claimed that he feared these resolutions were an attempt to exclude the Young Irelanders from the Association altogether.<sup id="cite_ref-Kee,_Robert_1972_p._253_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kee,_Robert_1972_p._253-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> At an Association meeting held in July 1846 at <a href="/wiki/Tivoli_Variety_Theatre" title="Tivoli Variety Theatre">Conciliation Hall</a>, the meeting place of the Association, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Francis_Meagher" title="Thomas Francis Meagher">Thomas Francis Meagher</a>, a Young Irelander, addressing the peace resolutions, delivered his 'Sword Speech', in which he stated, "I do not abhor the use of arms in the vindication of national rights ... Be it for the defence, or be it for the assertion of a nation's liberty, I look upon the sword as a sacred weapon."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_O%27Connell_(MP)" title="John O&#39;Connell (MP)">John O'Connell</a>, Daniel O'Connell's son, was present at the proceedings and interrupted Meagher's speech, claiming that Meagher could no longer be part of the same association as O'Connell and his supporters. After some protest, the Young Irelanders left Conciliation Hall and the Repeal Association forever, founding the Irish Confederation 13 January 1847 after negotiations for a reunion had failed.
</p><p>The Young Ireland movement culminated in a failed uprising (see <a href="/wiki/Young_Irelander_Rebellion_of_1848" title="Young Irelander Rebellion of 1848">Young Irelander Rebellion of 1848</a>), which, influenced by the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution_of_1848" title="French Revolution of 1848">French Revolution of 1848</a> and further provoked by government inaction during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Famine_(Ireland)" title="Great Famine (Ireland)">Great Famine</a> and the suspension of <i><a href="/wiki/Habeas_corpus" title="Habeas corpus">habeas corpus</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> which allowed the government to imprison Young Irelanders and other political opponents without trial, was hastily planned and quickly suppressed. Following the abortive uprising, several rebel leaders were arrested and convicted of sedition. Originally sentenced to death, Smith O'Brien and other members of the Irish Confederation were <a href="/wiki/Penal_transportation" title="Penal transportation">transported</a> to <a href="/wiki/Van_Diemen%27s_Land" title="Van Diemen&#39;s Land">Van Diemen's Land</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fenian_movement">Fenian movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_republicanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fenian movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Irish_Fenian_Executive.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/The_Irish_Fenian_Executive.jpg/170px-The_Irish_Fenian_Executive.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="270" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/The_Irish_Fenian_Executive.jpg/255px-The_Irish_Fenian_Executive.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/The_Irish_Fenian_Executive.jpg/340px-The_Irish_Fenian_Executive.jpg 2x" data-file-width="378" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Irish_Fenian_Executive.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Some of the founding members of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Brotherhood" title="Irish Republican Brotherhood">Irish Republican Brotherhood</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fenian" title="Fenian">Fenian</a> movement consisted of the <a href="/wiki/Fenian_Brotherhood" title="Fenian Brotherhood">Fenian Brotherhood</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Brotherhood" title="Irish Republican Brotherhood">Irish Republican Brotherhood</a> (IRB), fraternal organisations founded in the United States and Ireland respectively with the aim of establishing an independent republic in Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The IRB was founded on <a href="/wiki/Saint_Patrick%27s_Day" title="Saint Patrick&#39;s Day">Saint Patrick's Day</a> 1858 in Dublin.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Members present at the first meeting were <a href="/wiki/James_Stephens_(Fenian)" title="James Stephens (Fenian)">James Stephens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Clarke_Luby" title="Thomas Clarke Luby">Thomas Clarke Luby</a>, Peter Langan, Joseph Denieffe, Garrett O'Shaughnessy, and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Kickham" title="Charles Kickham">Charles Kickham</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Stephens had previously spent time exiled in Paris, along with <a href="/wiki/John_O%27Mahony" title="John O&#39;Mahony">John O'Mahony</a>, having taken part in the uprising of 1848 and fleeing to avoid capture. O'Mahony left France for America in the mid-1850s and founded the <a href="/wiki/Emmet_Monument_Association" title="Emmet Monument Association">Emmet Monument Association</a> with <a href="/wiki/Michael_Doheny" title="Michael Doheny">Michael Doheny</a>. Stephens returned to Ireland in 1856.
</p><p>The original oath of the society, drawn up by Luby under Stephens' direction, read:
</p>
<blockquote><p>I, AB., do solemnly swear, in the presence of Almighty God, that I will do my utmost, at every risk, while life lasts, to make [other versions, according to Luby, establish in'] Ireland an independent Democratic Republic; that I will yield implicit obedience, in all things not contrary to the law of God [ 'laws of morality'] to the commands of my superior officers; and that I shall preserve inviolable secrecy regarding all the transactions [ 'affairs'] of this secret society that may be confided in me. So help me God! Amen.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>The Fenian Brotherhood was the IRB's counterpart organisation, formed in the same year in the United States by O'Mahony and Doheny.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The Fenian Brotherhood's main purpose was to supply weapons and funds for its Irish counterpart and raise support for the Irish republican movement in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The term "Fenian" was coined by O'Mahony, who named the American wing of the movement after the <i><a href="/wiki/Fianna" title="Fianna">Fianna</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> — a class of warriors that existed in <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_Ireland" title="Gaelic Ireland">Gaelic Ireland</a>. The term became popular and is still in use, especially in Northern Ireland and Scotland, where it has expanded to refer to all Irish nationalists and republicans, as well as being a pejorative term for Irish Catholics.
</p><p>Public support for the Fenian movement in Ireland grew in November 1861 with the funeral of <a href="/wiki/Terence_MacManus" title="Terence MacManus">Terence MacManus</a>, a member of the Irish Confederation, which Stephens and the Fenians had organised and which was attended by between twenty thousand and thirty thousand people.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Following this, Stephens (accompanied by Luby) undertook a series of organisational tours throughout the island.
</p><p>In 1865 the Fenian Brotherhood in America had split into two factions. One was led by O'Mahony with Stephens' support. The other, which was more powerful, was led by <a href="/wiki/William_R._Roberts" title="William R. Roberts">William R. Roberts</a>. The Fenians had always planned an armed rebellion, but there was now disagreement as to how and where this rebellion might be carried out. Roberts' faction preferred focusing all military efforts on <a href="/wiki/Canada_under_British_rule" title="Canada under British rule">British Canada</a> (Roberts and his supporters theorised that victory for the American Fenians in nearby Canada would propel the Irish republican movement as a whole to success).<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The other, headed by O'Mahony, proposed that a rising in Ireland be planned for 1866.<sup id="cite_ref-Kee_Robert_1972_p._325_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kee_Robert_1972_p._325-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> In spite of this, the O'Mahony wing of the movement itself tried and failed to capture <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">New Brunswick</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Campobello_Island" title="Campobello Island">Campobello Island</a> in April 1866.<sup id="cite_ref-Kee_Robert_1972_p._325_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kee_Robert_1972_p._325-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Following this failure, the Roberts faction of the Fenian Brotherhood carried out its own, occupying the village of <a href="/wiki/Fort_Erie,_Ontario" title="Fort Erie, Ontario">Fort Erie, Ontario</a> on 31 May 1866 and engaging Canadian troops at the battles of <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ridgeway" title="Battle of Ridgeway">Ridgeway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fort_Erie_(1866)" title="Battle of Fort Erie (1866)">Fort Erie</a> on 2 June.<sup id="cite_ref-Kee_Robert_1972_p._325_37-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kee_Robert_1972_p._325-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> It was in reference to Fenians fighting in this battle that the name "Irish Republican Army" was first used.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> These attacks (and those that followed) in Canada are collectively known as the "<a href="/wiki/Fenian_raids" title="Fenian raids">Fenian raids</a>".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nineteenth_century_onward">Nineteenth century onward</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_republicanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nineteenth century onward">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence" title="Irish War of Independence">Irish War of Independence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_force_Irish_republicanism" title="Physical force Irish republicanism">Physical force Irish republicanism</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Birth_of_the_Irish_Republic.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Birth_of_the_Irish_Republic.jpg/220px-Birth_of_the_Irish_Republic.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="137" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Birth_of_the_Irish_Republic.jpg/330px-Birth_of_the_Irish_Republic.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Birth_of_the_Irish_Republic.jpg/440px-Birth_of_the_Irish_Republic.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2163" data-file-height="1347" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Birth_of_the_Irish_Republic.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A depiction of the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Rising" title="Easter Rising">Easter Rising</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hogan%27s_Flying_Column.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Hogan%27s_Flying_Column.gif/220px-Hogan%27s_Flying_Column.gif" decoding="async" width="220" height="121" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Hogan%27s_Flying_Column.gif/330px-Hogan%27s_Flying_Column.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Hogan%27s_Flying_Column.gif/440px-Hogan%27s_Flying_Column.gif 2x" data-file-width="2057" data-file-height="1133" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hogan%27s_Flying_Column.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Se%C3%A1n_Hogan" title="Seán Hogan">Seán Hogan</a>'s IRA <a href="/wiki/Flying_column" title="Flying column">flying column</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Irish_War_of_Independence" title="Irish War of Independence">Irish War of Independence</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1800" title="Acts of Union 1800">Act of Union in 1801</a> merging Ireland with Britain into the United Kingdom, Irish independence movements were suppressed by the British authorities. Nationalist rebellions against British rule in 1803, by Robert Emmet, 1848 (by the Young Irelanders) and 1865 and 1867 (by the Fenians) were followed by harsh reprisals by British forces.
</p><p>In 1916 the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Rising" title="Easter Rising">Easter Rising</a>, organised by the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Brotherhood" title="Irish Republican Brotherhood">Irish Republican Brotherhood</a>, was launched in Dublin and the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republic" title="Irish Republic">Irish Republic</a> was proclaimed, albeit without significant popular support.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The Rising was suppressed after six days, and most of its leaders were executed by the British authorities. This was a turning point in Irish history, leading to the War of Independence and the end of British rule in most of Ireland.
</p><p>From 1919–1921 the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army_(1919%E2%80%931922)" title="Irish Republican Army (1919–1922)">Irish Republican Army</a> (IRA) was organised as a guerilla army, led by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Mulcahy" title="Richard Mulcahy">Richard Mulcahy</a> and with <a href="/wiki/Michael_Collins_(Irish_leader)" title="Michael Collins (Irish leader)">Michael Collins</a> as Director of Intelligence and fought against British forces. During the Anglo-Irish War (or War of Independence), the British sent paramilitary police, the "<a href="/wiki/Black_and_Tans" title="Black and Tans">Black and Tans</a>" and the <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_Division" title="Auxiliary Division">Auxiliary Division</a>, to help the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> and <a href="/wiki/Royal_Irish_Constabulary" title="Royal Irish Constabulary">Royal Irish Constabulary</a>. These groups committed atrocities which included killing captured POWs and any Irish civilians they viewed as being sympathetic to the IRA. Among the most infamous of their actions were the <a href="/wiki/Burning_of_Cork" title="Burning of Cork">burning of half the city</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cork_(city)" title="Cork (city)">Cork</a> in 1920 and the <a href="/wiki/Bloody_Sunday_(1920)" title="Bloody Sunday (1920)">Bloody Sunday</a> massacre of 1920. These atrocities, together with the popularity of the republican ideal, and British repression of republican political expression, led to widespread support across Ireland for the Irish rebels.
</p><p>In 1921 the British government led by <a href="/wiki/David_Lloyd_George" title="David Lloyd George">David Lloyd George</a> negotiated the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a> with <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty#Negotiators" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">republican leaders</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Griffith" title="Arthur Griffith">Arthur Griffith</a> who had been delegated as <a href="/wiki/Plenipotentiary" title="Plenipotentiary">plenipotentiaries</a> on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/Second_D%C3%A1il" title="Second Dáil">Second Dáil</a>, thus ending the conflict.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Irish_Free_State_and_Republic_of_Ireland">Irish Free State and Republic of Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_republicanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Irish Free State and Republic of Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Irish_Civil_War" title="Irish Civil War">Irish Civil War</a></div>
<p>Though many across the country were unhappy with the Anglo-Irish Treaty (since, during the war, the IRA had fought for independence for all Ireland and for a republic, not a partitioned <a href="/wiki/Dominion" title="Dominion">dominion</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Monarchy of the United Kingdom">British crown</a>), some republicans were satisfied that the Treaty was the best that could be achieved at the time. However, a substantial number opposed it. <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann" title="Dáil Éireann">Dáil Éireann</a>, the Irish parliament, voted by 64 votes to 57 to ratify it,<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> the majority believing that the treaty created a new base from which to move forward. <a href="/wiki/%C3%89amon_de_Valera" title="Éamon de Valera">Éamon de Valera</a>, who had served as <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Irish_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="President of the Irish Republic">President of the Irish Republic</a> during the war, refused to accept the decision of the Dáil and led the opponents of the treaty out of the House. The pro-Treaty republicans organised themselves into the <a href="/wiki/Cumann_na_nGaedheal" title="Cumann na nGaedheal">Cumann na nGaedheal</a> party, while the anti-Treaty republicans retained the Sinn Féin name. The IRA itself split between pro-Treaty and anti-Treaty elements, with the former forming the nucleus of the new <a href="/wiki/National_Army_(Ireland)" title="National Army (Ireland)">Irish National Army</a>.
</p><p>Michael Collins became Commander-in-Chief of the National Army. Shortly afterwards, some dissidents, apparently without the authorisation of the anti-Treaty IRA Army Executive, occupied the <a href="/wiki/Four_Courts" title="Four Courts">Four Courts</a> in Dublin and kidnapped a pro-Treaty general. The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of Ireland">government</a>, responding to this provocation and to intensified British pressure following the assassination by an IRA unit in London of <a href="/wiki/Sir_Henry_Wilson,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir Henry Wilson, 1st Baronet">Sir Henry Wilson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dublin" title="Battle of Dublin">ordered the regular army to take the Four Courts</a>, thereby beginning the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Civil_War" title="Irish Civil War">Irish Civil War</a>. It is believed that Collins continued to fund and supply the IRA in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> throughout the civil war, but, after his death, <a href="/wiki/W._T._Cosgrave" title="W. T. Cosgrave">W. T. Cosgrave</a> (the new <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Executive_Council_of_the_Irish_Free_State" title="President of the Executive Council of the Irish Free State">President of the Executive Council</a>, or prime minister) discontinued this support.
</p><p>By May 1923, the war (which had claimed more lives than the War of Independence) ended in the call by the IRA to dump arms. However, the harsh measures adopted by both sides, including assassinations of politicians by the Republicans and <a href="/wiki/Executions_during_the_Irish_Civil_War" title="Executions during the Irish Civil War">executions and atrocities</a> by the Free State side, left a bitter legacy in Irish politics for decades to follow.
</p><p>De Valera, who had strongly supported the Republican side in the Civil War, reconsidered his views while in jail and came to accept the ideas of political activity under the terms of the Free State constitution. Rather than abstaining from Free State politics entirely, he now sought to republicanise it from within. However, he and his supporters – which included most Sinn Féin TDs – failed to convince a majority of the anti-treaty Sinn Féin of these views and the movement split again. In 1926, he formed a new party called <a href="/wiki/Fianna_F%C3%A1il" title="Fianna Fáil">Fianna Fáil</a> (Soldiers of Destiny), taking most of Sinn Féin's TDs with him. In 1931, following the enactment of the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Westminster_1931" title="Statute of Westminster 1931">Statute of Westminster</a>, the country became a sovereign state along with the other Dominions and the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The following year, De Valera was appointed President of the Executive Council of the Free State and began a slow process of turning the country from a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a> to a constitutional republic, thus fulfilling Collins's prediction of "the freedom to achieve freedom".<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By then, the IRA was engaged in confrontations with the <a href="/wiki/Blueshirts" title="Blueshirts">Blueshirts</a>, a quasi-fascist group led by a former War of Independence and pro-Treaty leader, <a href="/wiki/Eoin_O%27Duffy" title="Eoin O&#39;Duffy">Eoin O'Duffy</a>. O'Duffy looked to <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy" title="Kingdom of Italy">Fascist Italy</a> as an example for Ireland to follow. Several hundred supporters of O'Duffy briefly went to Spain to volunteer on the <a href="/wiki/Nationalist_faction_(Spanish_Civil_War)" title="Nationalist faction (Spanish Civil War)">Nationalist</a> side in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a>, and a smaller number of ex-IRA members, communists and others participated on the <a href="/wiki/Republican_faction_(Spanish_Civil_War)" title="Republican faction (Spanish Civil War)">Republican</a> side.
</p><p>In 1937, the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Constitution of Ireland">Constitution of Ireland</a> was drafted by the de Valera government and <a href="/wiki/Adoption_of_the_Constitution_of_Ireland" title="Adoption of the Constitution of Ireland">approved via referendum</a> by the majority of the population of the Free State. The constitution changed the name of the state to <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%89ire" title="Éire">Éire</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> in the Irish language (<i>Ireland</i> in English) and asserted its national territory as the whole of Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> The new state was headed by a <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a> elected by universal suffrage, (though the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_in_the_Irish_Free_State" title="Monarchy in the Irish Free State">constitutional Monarch of the State</a> (by then <a href="/wiki/George_VI" title="George VI">George VI</a>) remained, albeit with a role diminished solely to functions in relation to accreditation of diplomats. He is believed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (June 2018)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> to have been left with those residual functions as a concession to <a href="/wiki/Unionism_in_Ireland" title="Unionism in Ireland">Unionist</a> opinion.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>) The new state had the objective characteristics of a republic and was referred to as such by de Valera himself, but, it remained within the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">British Commonwealth</a> and was regarded by the British as a Dominion, like Canada, Australia, New Zealand, and South Africa. Furthermore, the claim to the whole of the island did not reflect practical reality and inflamed anti-Dublin sentiment among northern Protestants.
</p><p>In 1948, Fianna Fáil went out of office for the first time in sixteen years. <a href="/wiki/John_A._Costello" title="John A. Costello">John A. Costello</a>, leader of the coalition government, announced his intention to declare Ireland a republic.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland_Act_1948" title="Republic of Ireland Act 1948">Republic of Ireland Act 1948</a>, which "described" the state as the Republic of Ireland (without changing its name), led the British government to pass the <a href="/wiki/Ireland_Act_1949" title="Ireland Act 1949">Ireland Act 1949</a>, which declared that Northern Ireland would continue as part of the United Kingdom unless the Parliament of Northern Ireland consented to leave;<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> and Ireland ceased to be a member of the Commonwealth. As a result of this—and also because continuing struggle against the Dublin government was futile—the republican movement took the decision to focus on Northern Ireland from then on. The decision was announced by the IRA in its Easter statement of 1949.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Republicanism_in_Northern_Ireland">Republicanism in Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_republicanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Republicanism in Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="1921.E2.80.9366"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1921–66">1921–66</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_republicanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 1921–66">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1921, Ireland was partitioned. Most of the country became part of the independent <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a>. However, six out of the nine counties of <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a> remained part of the United Kingdom as <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. In the 1921 elections<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> in Northern Ireland,	
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/County_Antrim" title="County Antrim">Antrim</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Down" title="County Down">Down</a> and the borough of <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a> had Unionist majorities of over 25%.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/County_Londonderry" title="County Londonderry">County Londonderry</a>, the breakdown in that election was 56.2% Unionist / 43.8% Nationalist.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/County_Armagh" title="County Armagh">Armagh</a>, the ratio was 55.3% Unionist / 44.7% Nationalist.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/County_Fermanagh" title="County Fermanagh">Fermanagh</a>–<a href="/wiki/County_Tyrone" title="County Tyrone">Tyrone</a> (which was a single constituency), the ratio 54.7% Nationalist / 45.3% Unionist. (Tyrone was 55.4% Catholic in the 1911 census and 55.5% in the 1926 census, though of course only adults had votes on the other hand religious and national affiliations while closely linked are not as absolute as commonly assumed.) Within most of these counties there were large pockets which predominantly nationalist or Unionist (South Armagh, West Tyrone West Londonderry and parts of North Antrim were largely nationalist whereas much of North Armagh, East Londonderry, East Tyrone and most of Antrim were/are largely Unionist).</li></ul>
<p>This territory of Northern Ireland, as established by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland_Act_1920" title="Government of Ireland Act 1920">Government of Ireland Act 1920</a>, had its own <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Government of Northern Ireland">provincial government</a> which was controlled for 50 years until 1972 by the conservative <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Unionist_Party" title="Ulster Unionist Party">Ulster Unionist Party</a> (UUP). The tendency to vote on <a href="/wiki/Sectarianism" title="Sectarianism">sectarian</a> lines and the proportions of each religious denomination ensured that there would never be a change of government. In local government, constituency boundaries were drawn to divide nationalist communities into two or even three constituencies and so weaken their effect (see <a href="/wiki/Gerrymandering" title="Gerrymandering">Gerrymandering</a>).
</p><p>The (mainly Catholic) Nationalist population in Northern Ireland, besides feeling politically alienated, was also economically alienated, often with worse living standards compared to their Protestant (mainly Unionist) neighbours, with fewer job opportunities, and living in ghettos in <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>, Derry, <a href="/wiki/Armagh" title="Armagh">Armagh</a> and other places. Many Catholics considered the Unionist government was undemocratic, bigoted and favoured Protestants. Emigration for economic reasons kept the nationalist population from growing, despite its higher birth rate. Although poverty, (e)migration and unemployment were fairly widespread (albeit not to the same extent) among Protestants as well, on the other hand the economic situation in Northern Ireland (even for Catholics) was for a long time arguably still better than in the Republic of Ireland.
</p><p>During the 1930s the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army_(1922%E2%80%931969)" title="Irish Republican Army (1922–1969)">IRA</a> launched minor attacks against the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Ulster_Constabulary" title="Royal Ulster Constabulary">Royal Ulster Constabulary</a> (RUC) and <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> in Northern Ireland. The IRA began another armed campaign in Britain in 1939. During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> the IRA leadership hoped for support from Germany, and chief of staff <a href="/wiki/Se%C3%A1n_Russell" title="Seán Russell">Seán Russell</a> travelled there in 1940; he died later that year after falling ill on a U-boat that was bringing him back to Ireland (possibly with a view to starting a German sponsored revolution in Ireland). Suspected republicans were interned on both sides of the border, for different reasons.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Border_Campaign_(Irish_Republican_Army)" class="mw-redirect" title="Border Campaign (Irish Republican Army)">Border Campaign</a> in the mid-50s was the last attempt at traditional military action and was an abject failure. The Movement needed to reconsider its strategy.
</p>
<h3><span id="1966.E2.80.9369"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1966–69">1966–69</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_republicanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 1966–69">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the late 1960s, Irish political activists groups found parallels with their struggle against religious discrimination in the <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights_movement" title="Civil rights movement">civil rights</a> campaign of <a href="/wiki/African_Americans" title="African Americans">African Americans</a> the US against racial discrimination. Student leaders such a <a href="/wiki/Bernadette_Devlin_McAliskey" title="Bernadette Devlin McAliskey">Bernadette Devlin McAliskey</a> and Nationalist politicians such as <a href="/wiki/Austin_Currie" title="Austin Currie">Austin Currie</a> tried to use <a href="/wiki/Nonviolence" title="Nonviolence">non-violent</a> <a href="/wiki/Direct_action" title="Direct action">direct action</a> to draw attention to the blatant discrimination. By 1968, Europe as a whole was engulfed in a struggle between radicalism and conservativism. In Sinn Féin, the same debate raged. The dominant analysis was that Protestant Irishmen and women would never be bombed into a <a href="/wiki/United_Ireland" title="United Ireland">united Ireland</a>. The only way forward was to have both sides embrace socialism and forget their sectarian hatreds. They resolved to no longer to be drawn into inter-communal violence.
</p><p>As a response to the civil rights campaign militant loyalist paramilitary groups started to emerge in the Protestant community. The <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Volunteer_Force" title="Ulster Volunteer Force">Ulster Volunteer Force</a> (UVF) was the first. The UVF had originally existed among <a href="/wiki/Loyalism" title="Loyalism">loyalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Protestants" title="Ulster Protestants">Ulster Protestants</a> before <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> to oppose <a href="/wiki/Irish_Home_Rule_movement" title="Irish Home Rule movement">Home Rule</a>. In the 1960s it was relaunched by militant loyalists, encouraged by certain politicians, to oppose any attempt to reunite Northern Ireland with the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, which is how they saw any change in their status vis-a-vis Catholics.
</p><p>By mid-1969 the violence in Northern Ireland exploded. Consistent with their new political ideology, the IRA declined to intervene. By late August, the British government had to intervene and declare a <a href="/wiki/State_of_emergency" title="State of emergency">state of emergency</a>, sending a large number of troops into Northern Ireland to stop the intercommunal violence. Initially welcomed by some Catholics as protectors, later events such as Bloody Sunday and the Falls Road curfew turned many against the British Army.
</p>
<h3><span id="1970.E2.80.9385"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1970–85">1970–85</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_republicanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 1970–85">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Divisions began to emerge in the Republican movement between leftists and conservatives. The leader of the IRA, <a href="/wiki/Cathal_Goulding" title="Cathal Goulding">Cathal Goulding</a> believed that the IRA could not beat the British with military tactics and should turn into a workers' revolutionary movement that would overthrow both governments to achieve a 32-county socialist republic through the will of the people (after WWII the IRA no longer engaged in any actions against the Republic). Goulding also drove the IRA into an ideologically <a href="/wiki/Marxism%E2%80%93Leninism" title="Marxism–Leninism">Marxist-Leninist</a> direction which attracted idealistic young supporters in the Republic, but alienated and angered many of the IRA's core supporters in the North. In particular, his decision to regard the UVF as deluded rather than as the enemy, was anathema to traditionalists and those who were its potential victims.
</p><p>The argument led to a split in 1970, between the <a href="/wiki/Official_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Official Irish Republican Army">Official IRA</a> (supporters of Goulding's Marxist line) and the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Provisional Irish Republican Army">Provisional IRA</a> (also called Provos, traditional nationalist republicans). The Provos were led by <a href="/wiki/Se%C3%A1n_Mac_St%C3%ADof%C3%A1in" title="Seán Mac Stíofáin">Seán Mac Stíofáin</a> and immediately began a <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Irish_Republican_Army_campaign" title="Provisional Irish Republican Army campaign">large scale campaign</a> against British state forces and economic targets in Northern Ireland. The Official IRA were also initially drawn into an armed campaign by the escalating communal violence. In 1972, the Official IRA declared a cease-fire, which, apart from feuds with other republican groups, has been maintained to date. Nowadays the term 'Irish Republican Army' almost always denotes the Provisional IRA.
</p><p>Throughout the 1970s and 1980s the conflict continued claiming thousands of lives, with the UVF (and other loyalist groups) extending attacks into the Republic of Ireland and the IRA launching attacks on targets in England. However some things slowly began to change. In the 1980s Provisional Sinn Féin (the Provisional IRA's political wing) began contesting elections and by the mid-1990s was representing the republican position at peace negotiations. In the loyalist movement splits occurred, the Ulster Unionist Party made tentative attempts to reform itself and attract Catholics into supporting the union with Britain, while the radical <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Unionist_Party" title="Democratic Unionist Party">Democratic Unionist Party</a> (DUP) led by <a href="/wiki/Ian_Paisley" title="Ian Paisley">Rev. Ian Paisley</a> began attracting working class Protestant loyalists who felt alienated by the UUP's overtures towards Catholics.
</p>
<h3><span id="1986.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1986–present">1986–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_republicanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 1986–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Funeral_of_Martin_McGuinness_(5).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Funeral_of_Martin_McGuinness_%285%29.jpg/220px-Funeral_of_Martin_McGuinness_%285%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="161" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Funeral_of_Martin_McGuinness_%285%29.jpg/330px-Funeral_of_Martin_McGuinness_%285%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Funeral_of_Martin_McGuinness_%285%29.jpg/440px-Funeral_of_Martin_McGuinness_%285%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2906" data-file-height="2124" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Funeral_of_Martin_McGuinness_(5).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The funeral procession of Irish republican politician <a href="/wiki/Martin_McGuinness" title="Martin McGuinness">Martin McGuinness</a>, Derry, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a></div></div></div>
<p>At the 1986 <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a> <a href="/wiki/Ardfheis" title="Ardfheis">Ardfheis</a>, a motion declaring the end of the policy of <a href="/wiki/Abstentionism" title="Abstentionism">abstentionism</a> (refusing to take seats in the Republic of Ireland's parliament), was passed. This motion caused a split in the movement creating <a href="/wiki/Republican_Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Republican Sinn Féin">Republican Sinn Féin</a>, a party committed to the 1970s "provisional" Sinn Féin vision of a 32 County federal republic. It was led by former Sinn Féin President <a href="/wiki/Ruair%C3%AD_%C3%93_Br%C3%A1daigh" title="Ruairí Ó Brádaigh">Ruairí Ó Brádaigh</a> (who had previously led "provisional" Sinn Féin to split from Official Sinn Féin). The policy of participation in Dáil elections became known as "the <a href="/wiki/ArmaLite_AR-18" title="ArmaLite AR-18">Armalite</a> and the ballot box".
</p><p>In 1994 the leaders of Northern Ireland's two largest nationalist parties, <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Adams" title="Gerry Adams">Gerry Adams</a>, the leader of Sinn Féin and <a href="/wiki/John_Hume" title="John Hume">John Hume</a>, the leader of the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_and_Labour_Party" title="Social Democratic and Labour Party">Social Democratic and Labour Party</a> (SDLP) entered into peace negotiations with Unionist leaders like <a href="/wiki/David_Trimble" title="David Trimble">David Trimble</a> of the UUP and the British government. At the table most of the paramilitary groups (including the IRA and UVF) had representatives. In 1998 when the IRA endorsed the <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a> between nationalist and unionist parties and both governments, another small group split from the IRA to form the <a href="/wiki/Real_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Real Irish Republican Army">Real IRA</a> (RIRA). The Continuity and Real IRA have both engaged in attacks not only against the British and loyalists, but even against their fellow nationalists (members of Sinn Féin, the SDLP and IRA).
</p><p>Since 1998, the IRA and UVF have adhered to a ceasefire.
</p><p>Today the republican movement can be divided into moderates who wish to reunite with the Republic through peaceful means and radicals who wish to continue an armed campaign.
</p><p>In late July 2005, the IRA announced that the armed conflict was over and that their weapons were to be put out of use. A large stock of weapons was reportedly "decommissioned" later that year. Some Unionists disputed the claim that this represented the entire stock of IRA weaponry.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">Variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_republicanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Irish_Socialist_Republicanism">Irish Socialist Republicanism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_republicanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Irish Socialist Republicanism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Socialism has traditionally been part of the Irish republican movement since the early 20th century, when <a href="/wiki/James_Connolly" title="James Connolly">James Connolly</a>, an Irish <a href="/wiki/Marxism" title="Marxism">Marxist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syndicalism" title="Syndicalism">Syndicalist</a> theorist, took part in the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Rising" title="Easter Rising">Easter Rising</a> of 1916. Today, most Irish nationalist and Republican organizations located in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> advocate some form of socialism, both Marxist and non-Marxist. The <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_and_Labour_Party" title="Social Democratic and Labour Party">Social Democratic and Labour Party</a>, which until recently was the largest nationalist party in Northern Ireland, promotes <a href="/wiki/Social_democracy" title="Social democracy">social democracy</a>, while militant republican parties such as <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%89ir%C3%ADg%C3%AD" title="Éirígí">Éirígí</a>, <a href="/wiki/Republican_Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Republican Sinn Féin">Republican Sinn Féin</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/32_County_Sovereignty_Movement" title="32 County Sovereignty Movement">32 County Sovereignty Movement</a> all promote their own varieties of <a href="/wiki/Democratic_socialism" title="Democratic socialism">democratic socialism</a> intended to re-distribute wealth on an all-island basis once a <a href="/wiki/United_Ireland" title="United Ireland">united Ireland</a> has been achieved. The <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Socialist_Movement" title="Irish Republican Socialist Movement">Irish Republican Socialist Movement</a>, encompassing the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Socialist_Party" title="Irish Republican Socialist Party">Irish Republican Socialist Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irish_National_Liberation_Army" title="Irish National Liberation Army">Irish National Liberation Army</a>, as well as the defunct <a href="/wiki/Official_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Official Irish Republican Army">Official Irish Republican Army</a> and Irish National Liberation Front, are known for promoting an ideology which combines <a href="/wiki/Marxism%E2%80%93Leninism" title="Marxism–Leninism">Marxism–Leninism</a> with traditional revolutionary militant republicanism and is claimed by its adherents to be the most direct fulfilment of Connolly's legacy.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_parties">Political parties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_republicanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Political parties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following are active republican parties in Ireland.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a><sup id="cite_ref-Horgan164_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Horgan164-48">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> is a Republican party in Ireland. Throughout the <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">Northern Ireland troubles</a>, it was closely allied with the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Provisional Irish Republican Army">Provisional Irish Republican Army</a>, publicly arguing for the validity of its armed campaign. Its policy platform combines civic nationalism with socialist views on economic and social issues. It is led by <a href="/wiki/Mary_Lou_McDonald" title="Mary Lou McDonald">Mary Lou McDonald</a> and organises in both the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> and Northern Ireland. The Party was also known as "<i>Provisional</i>" Sinn Féin by the media and commentators, having split from what later became known as the "<i>Official</i>" Sinn Féin (later the <a href="/wiki/Workers%27_Party_of_Ireland" title="Workers&#39; Party of Ireland">Workers' Party</a>) in 1970, because the latter had voted to enter a 'partitionist parliament'.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> In 1986, it reversed its original policy of not taking seats in <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann" title="Dáil Éireann">Dáil Éireann</a>, prompting another split, when <a href="/wiki/Republican_Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Republican Sinn Féin">Republican Sinn Féin</a> was formed. By the early 21st century it had replaced the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_and_Labour_Party" title="Social Democratic and Labour Party">Social Democratic and Labour Party</a> (SDLP) as Northern Ireland's largest nationalist party. As of 2017, it holds seven seats in the British parliament, twenty-three seats in the Dáil, seven in the <a href="/wiki/Seanad_%C3%89ireann" title="Seanad Éireann">Seanad</a> and 27 in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a>. Sinn Féin members contest elections to the British parliament on an <a href="/wiki/Abstentionism" title="Abstentionism">abstentionist</a> basis, that is, they refuse to take their seats in that parliament as they refuse to accept the right of that body to rule in any part of Ireland.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/%C3%89ir%C3%ADg%C3%AD" title="Éirígí">Éirígí</a><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> is a Socialist Republican political party that formed by a small group of community and political activists who had left Sinn Féin, in <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> in April 2006 as a political campaigns group, and became a full-fledged political party at the party's first <a href="/wiki/Ardfheis" title="Ardfheis">Ardfheis</a> (conference) in May 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/Independent_Monitoring_Commission" title="Independent Monitoring Commission">Independent Monitoring Commission</a> report said the group was "a small political grouping based on revolutionary socialist principles". While it continues to be a political association, albeit, with aggressive protest activities, it was not seen as paramilitary in nature.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Republican_Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Republican Sinn Féin">Republican Sinn Féin</a><sup id="cite_ref-Horgan164_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Horgan164-48">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> was formed in 1986 by former Sinn Féin leader <a href="/wiki/Ruair%C3%AD_%C3%93_Br%C3%A1daigh" title="Ruairí Ó Brádaigh">Ruairí Ó Brádaigh</a> who led traditional Republicans in a break with Sinn Féin over the ending of the policy of abstention in relation to elections to <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1il_%C3%89ireann" title="Dáil Éireann">Dáil Éireann</a>. The party continues to operate on an abstentionist basis: it would not take seats in the assemblies of either the Republic of Ireland or Northern Ireland because it views neither as legitimate. It is linked to the <a href="/wiki/Continuity_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Continuity Irish Republican Army">Continuity IRA</a>, whose goals are the overthrow of British rule in Northern Ireland and the unification of the island to form an independent country. In November 2009, <a href="/wiki/Des_Dalton" title="Des Dalton">Des Dalton</a> replaced Ó Brádaigh as leader of Republican Sinn Féin.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Socialist_Party" title="Irish Republican Socialist Party">Irish Republican Socialist Party</a><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> (IRSP) was founded in 1974 by former <a href="/wiki/Official_Irish_Republican_Army" title="Official Irish Republican Army">Official IRA</a> militant <a href="/wiki/Seamus_Costello" title="Seamus Costello">Seamus Costello</a>, who possibly had an eye towards <a href="/wiki/James_Connolly" title="James Connolly">James Connolly</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Irish_Socialist_Republican_Party" title="Irish Socialist Republican Party">Irish Socialist Republican Party</a> of the late 19th/early 20th century when coining the party's name. Costello led other former Official IRA members dissatisfied with <a href="/wiki/Cathal_Goulding" title="Cathal Goulding">Cathal Goulding</a>'s policies and tactics. The party quickly organised a paramilitary wing called the <a href="/wiki/Irish_National_Liberation_Army" title="Irish National Liberation Army">Irish National Liberation Army</a> (INLA) which has decommissioned recently. It claims to follow the principles of republican socialism as set out by the 1916 rebellion leader Connolly and radical 20th-century trade unionist <a href="/wiki/James_Larkin" title="James Larkin">James Larkin</a>.</li></ul>
