<p>An <b>emergency department</b> (<b>ED</b>), also known as an <b>accident &amp; emergency department</b> (<b>A&amp;E</b>), <b>emergency room</b> (<b>ER</b>), <b>emergency ward</b> (<b>EW</b>) or <b>casualty department</b>, is a medical treatment facility specializing in <a href="/wiki/Emergency_medicine" title="Emergency medicine">emergency medicine</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Acute_(medicine)" title="Acute (medicine)">acute</a> care of patients who present without prior appointment; either by their own means or by that of an <a href="/wiki/Ambulance" title="Ambulance">ambulance</a>. The emergency department is usually found in a <a href="/wiki/Hospital" title="Hospital">hospital</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Primary_care" title="Primary care">primary care</a> center.
</p><p>Due to the unplanned nature of patient attendance, the department must provide initial treatment for a broad spectrum of illnesses and injuries, some of which may be <a href="/wiki/Medical_emergency" title="Medical emergency">life-threatening</a> and require immediate attention. In some countries, emergency departments have become important entry points for those without other means of access to medical care.
</p><p>The emergency departments of most hospitals operate 24 hours a day, although staffing levels may be varied in an attempt to reflect patient volume.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Accident services were already provided by workmen's compensation plans, railway companies, and municipalities in Europe and the United States by the late mid-nineteenth century, but the first specialized trauma care center in the world was opened in 1911 in the United States at the University of Louisville Hospital in <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville, Kentucky</a>, and was developed by surgeon Arnold Griswold during the 1930s. Griswold also equipped police and fire vehicles with medical supplies and trained officers to give emergency care while en route to the hospital.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Today, a typical hospital has its emergency department in its own section of the ground floor of the grounds, with its own dedicated entrance. As patients can present at any time and with any complaint, a key part of the operation of an emergency department is the prioritization of cases based on clinical need.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  This process is called <a href="/wiki/Triage" title="Triage">triage</a>.
</p><p>Triage is normally the first stage the patient passes through, and consists of a brief assessment, including a set of <a href="/wiki/Vital_signs" title="Vital signs">vital signs</a>, and the assignment of a "chief complaint" (e.g. chest pain, abdominal pain, difficulty breathing, etc.). Most emergency departments have a dedicated area for this process to take place, and may have staff dedicated to performing nothing but a triage role. In most departments, this role is fulfilled by a triage <a href="/wiki/Nurse" class="mw-redirect" title="Nurse">nurse</a>, although dependent on training levels in the country and area, other health care professionals may perform the triage sorting, including <a href="/wiki/Paramedic" title="Paramedic">paramedics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physician" title="Physician">physicians</a>. Triage is typically conducted face-to-face when the patient presents, or a form of triage may be conducted via radio with an ambulance crew; in this method, the paramedics will call the hospital's triage center with a short update about an incoming patient, who will then be triaged to the appropriate level of care.
</p><p>Most patients will be initially assessed at triage and then passed to another area of the department, or another area of the hospital, with their waiting time determined by their clinical need.  However, some patients may complete their treatment at the triage stage, for instance if the condition is very minor and can be treated quickly, if only advice is required, or if the emergency department is not a suitable point of care for the patient. Conversely, patients with evidently serious conditions, such as cardiac arrest, will bypass triage altogether and move straight to the appropriate part of the department.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cardiopulmonary_resuscitation" title="Cardiopulmonary resuscitation">resuscitation</a> area, commonly referred to as "Trauma" or "Resus", is a key area in most departments. The most seriously ill or injured patients will be dealt with in this area, as it contains the equipment and staff required for dealing with immediately life-threatening illnesses and injuries. Typical resuscitation staffing involves at least one attending physician, and at least one and usually two nurses with trauma and <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Cardiac_Life_Support" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced Cardiac Life Support">Advanced Cardiac Life Support</a> training. These personnel may be assigned to the resuscitation area for the entirety of the shift, or may be "on call" for resuscitation coverage (i.e. if a critical case presents via walk-in triage or ambulance, the team will be paged to the resuscitation area to deal with the case immediately). Resuscitation cases may also be attended by <a href="/wiki/Resident_physician" class="mw-redirect" title="Resident physician">residents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radiographer" title="Radiographer">radiographers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emergency_medical_services" title="Emergency medical services">ambulance personnel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_therapists" class="mw-redirect" title="Respiratory therapists">respiratory therapists</a>, hospital <a href="/wiki/Pharmacists" class="mw-redirect" title="Pharmacists">pharmacists</a> and students of any of these professions depending upon the skill mix needed for any given case and whether or not the hospital provides teaching services.
</p><p>Patients who exhibit signs of being seriously ill but are not in immediate danger of life or limb will be triaged to "acute care" or "majors", where they will be seen by a physician and receive a more thorough assessment and treatment. Examples of "majors" include chest pain, difficulty breathing, abdominal pain and neurological complaints. Advanced diagnostic testing may be conducted at this stage, including laboratory testing of blood and/or urine, <a href="/wiki/Ultrasonography" class="mw-redirect" title="Ultrasonography">ultrasonography</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computed_tomography" class="mw-redirect" title="Computed tomography">CT</a> or <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging" title="Magnetic resonance imaging">MRI</a> scanning. Medications appropriate to manage the patient's condition will also be given. Depending on underlying causes of the patient's chief complaint, he or she may be discharged home from this area or admitted to the hospital for further treatment.
</p><p>Patients whose condition is not immediately life-threatening will be sent to an area suitable to deal with them, and these areas might typically be termed as a <i>prompt care</i> or <i>minors</i> area. Such patients may still have been found to have significant problems, including <a href="/wiki/Fracture_(bone)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fracture (bone)">fractures</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dislocation_(medicine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dislocation (medicine)">dislocations</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wound" title="Wound">lacerations</a> requiring <a href="/wiki/Surgical_suture" title="Surgical suture">suturing</a>.
</p><p>Children can present particular challenges in treatment. Some departments have dedicated <a href="/wiki/Pediatrics" title="Pediatrics">pediatrics</a> areas, and some departments employ a <a href="/wiki/Play_therapy" title="Play therapy">play therapist</a> whose job is to put children at ease to reduce the anxiety caused by visiting the emergency department, as well as provide distraction therapy for simple procedures.
</p><p>Many hospitals have a separate area for evaluation of <a href="/wiki/Mental_illness" class="mw-redirect" title="Mental illness">psychiatric problems</a>. These are often staffed by <a href="/wiki/Psychiatrist" title="Psychiatrist">psychiatrists</a> and mental health nurses and <a href="/wiki/Social_worker" class="mw-redirect" title="Social worker">social workers</a>. There is typically at least one room for people who are actively a risk to themselves or others (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicidal</a>).
</p><p>Fast decisions on life-and-death cases are critical in hospital emergency departments. As a result, doctors face great pressures to overtest and overtreat. The fear of missing something often leads to extra blood tests and imaging scans for what may be harmless chest pains, run-of-the-mill head bumps, and non-threatening stomach aches, with a high cost on the health care system.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature_in_English">Nomenclature in English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nomenclature in English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Emergency department</i> became commonly used when <a href="/wiki/Emergency_medicine" title="Emergency medicine">emergency medicine</a> was recognised as a medical specialty, and hospitals and medical centres developed departments of emergency medicine to provide services. Other common variations include 'emergency ward,' 'emergency centre' or 'emergency unit'.
</p><p>'Accident and Emergency' or 'A&amp;E' is still the accepted term in the United Kingdom,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and some <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> countries,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> as are earlier terms such as 'Casualty' or 'casualty ward', which continue to be used informally.  The same applies to 'emergency room' or 'ER' in North America, originating when emergency facilities were provided in a single room of the hospital by the department of surgery.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signage">Signage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Signage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Regardless of naming convention, there is a widespread usage of directional signage in white text on a red background across the world, which indicates the location of the emergency department, or a hospital with such facilities.
</p><p>Signs on emergency departments may contain additional information.  In some American states there is close regulation of the design and content of such signs.  For example, California requires wording such as "Comprehensive Emergency Medical Service" and "Physician On Duty",<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> to prevent persons in need of critical care from presenting to facilities that are not fully equipped and staffed.
</p><p>In some countries, including the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and Canada, a smaller facility that may provide assistance in medical emergencies is known as a <a href="/wiki/Clinic" title="Clinic">clinic</a>. Larger communities often have walk-in clinics where people with medical problems that would not be considered serious enough to warrant an emergency department visit can be seen. These clinics often do not operate on a 24-hour basis. Very large clinics may operate as "free-standing emergency centres", which are open 24 hours and can manage a very large number of conditions. However, if a patient presents to a free-standing clinic with a condition requiring hospital admission, he or she must be transferred to an actual hospital, as these facilities do not have the capability to provide inpatient care.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Medicare_and_Medicaid_Services" title="Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services">Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services</a> (CMS) classified emergency departments into two types: Type A, the majority, which are open 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, 365 days a year; and those who are not, Type B. Many US emergency departments are exceedingly busy.  A study found that in 2009, there were an estimated 128,885,040 ED encounters in US hospitals. Approximately one-fifth of ED visits in 2010 were for patients under the age of 18 years.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  In 2009–2010, a total of 19.6 million emergency department visits in the United States were made by persons aged 65
and over.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  Most encounters (82.8 percent) resulted in treatment and release; 17.2 percent were admitted to inpatient care.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1986 <a href="/wiki/Emergency_Medical_Treatment_and_Active_Labor_Act" title="Emergency Medical Treatment and Active Labor Act">Emergency Medical Treatment and Active Labor Act</a> is an act of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a>, that requires emergency departments, if the associated hospital receives payments from <a href="/wiki/Medicare_(United_States)" title="Medicare (United States)">Medicare</a>, to provide appropriate medical examination and emergency treatment to all individuals seeking treatment for a medical condition, regardless of citizenship, legal status, or ability to pay. Like an <a href="/wiki/Unfunded_mandate" title="Unfunded mandate">unfunded mandate</a>, there are no reimbursement provisions.
</p><p>Rates of ED visits rose between 2006 and 2011 for almost every patient characteristic and location.  The total rate of ED visits increased 4.5% in that time.  However, the rate of visits for patients under one year of age declined 8.3%.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A survey of New York area doctors in February 2007 found that injuries and even deaths have been caused by excessive waits for hospital beds by ED patients.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  A 2005 patient survey found an average ED wait time from 2.3 hours in Iowa to 5.0 hours in Arizona.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One inspection of Los Angeles area hospitals by Congressional staff found the EDs operating at an average of 116% of capacity (meaning there were more patients than available treatment spaces) with insufficient beds to accommodate victims of a terrorist attack the size of the <a href="/wiki/2004_Madrid_train_bombings" title="2004 Madrid train bombings">2004 Madrid train bombings</a>. Three of the five Level I trauma centres were on "diversion", meaning ambulances with all but the most severely injured patients were being directed elsewhere because the ED could not safely accommodate any more patients.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  This controversial practice was banned in Massachusetts (except for major incidents, such as a fire in the ED), effective 1 January 2009; in response, hospitals have devoted more staff to the ED at peak times and moved some elective procedures to non-peak times.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009, there were 1,800 EDs in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  In 2011, about 421 out of every 1,000 people in the United States visited the emergency department; five times as many were discharged as were admitted.<sup id="cite_ref-Weiss_AJ,_Wier_LM,_Stocks_C,_Blanchard_J_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weiss_AJ,_Wier_LM,_Stocks_C,_Blanchard_J-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Rural areas are the highest rate of ED visits (502 per 1,000 population) and large metro counties had the lowest (319 visits per 1,000 population).  By region, the Midwest had the highest rate of ED visits (460 per 1,000 population) and Western States had the lowest (321 visits per 1,000 population).<sup id="cite_ref-Weiss_AJ,_Wier_LM,_Stocks_C,_Blanchard_J_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weiss_AJ,_Wier_LM,_Stocks_C,_Blanchard_J-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Most Common Reasons for Discharged Emergency Department Visits in the United States, 2011</b><sup id="cite_ref-Weiss_AJ,_Wier_LM,_Stocks_C,_Blanchard_J_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weiss_AJ,_Wier_LM,_Stocks_C,_Blanchard_J-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Freestanding">Freestanding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Freestanding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to the normal hospital based emergency departments a trend has developed in some states (including Texas and Colorado) of emergency departments not attached to hospitals. These new emergency departments are referred to as free standing emergency departments. The rationale for these operations is the ability to operate outside of hospital policies that may lead to increased wait times and reduced patient satisfaction.
</p><p>These departments have attracted controversy due to consumer confusion around their prices and insurance coverage. In 2017, the largest operator, <a href="/wiki/Adeptus_Health" title="Adeptus Health">Adeptus Health</a>, declared bankruptcy.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:EdinburghRoyalInfirmary.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/EdinburghRoyalInfirmary.jpg/220px-EdinburghRoyalInfirmary.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/EdinburghRoyalInfirmary.jpg/330px-EdinburghRoyalInfirmary.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/EdinburghRoyalInfirmary.jpg/440px-EdinburghRoyalInfirmary.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:EdinburghRoyalInfirmary.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The emergency department at <a href="/wiki/The_Royal_Infirmary_of_Edinburgh" class="mw-redirect" title="The Royal Infirmary of Edinburgh">The Royal Infirmary of Edinburgh</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Accident_and_Emergency_(sign).png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Accident_and_Emergency_%28sign%29.png/250px-Accident_and_Emergency_%28sign%29.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="39" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Accident_and_Emergency_%28sign%29.png/375px-Accident_and_Emergency_%28sign%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Accident_and_Emergency_%28sign%29.png/500px-Accident_and_Emergency_%28sign%29.png 2x" data-file-width="1010" data-file-height="156" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Accident_and_Emergency_(sign).png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A&amp;E sign common in the UK.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:UK_traffic_sign_827.2.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/UK_traffic_sign_827.2.svg/150px-UK_traffic_sign_827.2.svg.png" decoding="async" width="150" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/UK_traffic_sign_827.2.svg/225px-UK_traffic_sign_827.2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/UK_traffic_sign_827.2.svg/300px-UK_traffic_sign_827.2.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="661" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:UK_traffic_sign_827.2.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>UK road sign to a hospital with A&amp;E</div></div></div>
<p>All A&amp;E departments throughout the United Kingdom are financed and managed publicly by the <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">NHS</a> of each constituent country (<a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service_(England)" title="National Health Service (England)">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/NHS_Scotland" title="NHS Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/NHS_Wales" title="NHS Wales">Wales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Health_and_Social_Care_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Health and Social Care in Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>). As with most other <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">NHS</a> services, emergency care is provided to all, both resident citizens and those not ordinarily resident in the UK, free at the point of need and regardless of any ability to pay.
</p><p>In England departments are divided into three categories:
</p>
<ul><li>Type 1 A&amp;E department – major A&amp;E, providing a consultant-led 24 hour service with full resuscitation facilities</li>
<li>Type 2 A&amp;E department – single specialty A&amp;E service (e.g. ophthalmology, dentistry)</li>
<li>Type 3 A&amp;E department – other A&amp;E/ minor injury unit/ walk-in centre, treating minor injuries and illnesses <sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Historically, waits for assessment in A&amp;E were very long in some areas of the UK. In October 2002, the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Health_(United_Kingdom)" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Health (United Kingdom)">Department of Health</a> introduced a <a href="/wiki/Four-hour_target_in_emergency_departments" class="mw-redirect" title="Four-hour target in emergency departments">four-hour target in emergency departments</a> that required departments in England to assess and treat patients within four hours of arrival, with referral and assessment by other departments if deemed necessary. It was expected that the patients would have physically left the department within the four hours. Present policy is that 95% of all patient cases do not "breach" this four-hour wait. The busiest departments in the UK outside London include <a href="/wiki/University_Hospital_of_Wales" title="University Hospital of Wales">University Hospital of Wales</a> in Cardiff, The <a href="/wiki/Wrexham_Maelor_Hospital" title="Wrexham Maelor Hospital">North Wales Regional Hospital</a> in Wrexham, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Infirmary_of_Edinburgh" title="Royal Infirmary of Edinburgh">Royal Infirmary of Edinburgh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Queen_Alexandra_Hospital" title="Queen Alexandra Hospital">Queen Alexandra Hospital</a> in Portsmouth.
</p><p>In July 2014, the QualityWatch research programme published in-depth analysis which tracked 41 million A&amp;E attendances from 2010 to 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> This showed that the number of patients in a department at any one time was closely linked to waiting times, and that crowding in A&amp;E had increased as a result of a growing and ageing population, compounded by the freezing or reduction of A&amp;E capacity. Between 2010/11 and 2012/13 crowding increased by 8%, despite a rise of just 3% in A&amp;E visits, and this trend looks set to continue. Other influential factors identified by the report included temperature (with both hotter and colder weather pushing up A&amp;E visits), staffing and inpatient bed numbers.
</p><p>A&amp;E services in the UK are often the focus of a great deal of media and political interest, and data on A&amp;E performance is published weekly.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> However, this is only one part of a complex urgent and emergency care system. Reducing A&amp;E waiting times therefore requires a comprehensive, coordinated strategy across a range of related services.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many A&amp;E departments are crowded and confusing. Many of those attending are understandably anxious, and some are mentally ill, and especially at night are under the influence of alcohol or other substances. Pearson Lloyd's redesign – 'A Better A&amp;E' – is claimed to have reduced aggression against hospital staff in the departments by 50 per cent. A system of environmental signage provides location-specific information for patients. Screens provide live information about how many cases are being handled and the current status of the A&amp;E department.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  Waiting times for patients to be seen at A&amp;E have been rising.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_conditions_handled">Critical conditions handled</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Critical conditions handled">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cardiac_arrest">Cardiac arrest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cardiac arrest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cardiac_arrest" title="Cardiac arrest">Cardiac arrest</a> may occur in the ED/A&amp;E or a patient may be transported by ambulance to the emergency department already in this state. Treatment is basic <a href="/wiki/Life_support" title="Life support">life support</a> and advanced life support as taught in advanced <a href="/wiki/Life_support" title="Life support">life support</a> and <a href="/wiki/Advanced_cardiac_life_support" title="Advanced cardiac life support">advanced cardiac life support</a> courses.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heart_attack">Heart attack</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Heart attack">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">Myocardial infarction</a></div>
<p>Patients arriving to the emergency department with a myocardial infarction (heart attack) are likely to be triaged to the resuscitation area. They will receive oxygen and monitoring and have an early <a href="/wiki/Electrocardiogram" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrocardiogram">ECG</a>; aspirin will be given if not <a href="/wiki/Contraindication" title="Contraindication">contraindicated</a> or not already administered by the ambulance team; morphine or diamorphine will be given for pain; sub lingual (under the tongue) or buccal (between cheek and upper gum) glyceryl trinitrate (<a href="/wiki/Nitroglycerin_(drug)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nitroglycerin (drug)">nitroglycerin</a>) (GTN or NTG) will be given, unless contraindicated by the presence of other drugs.
</p><p>An <a href="/wiki/Electrocardiogram" class="mw-redirect" title="Electrocardiogram">ECG</a> that reveals ST segment elevation suggests complete blockage of one of the main coronary arteries. These patients require immediate reperfusion (re-opening) of the occluded vessel. This can be achieved in two ways: <a href="/wiki/Thrombolysis" title="Thrombolysis">thrombolysis</a> (clot-busting medication) or <a href="/wiki/Angioplasty" title="Angioplasty">percutaneous transluminal coronary angioplasty</a> (PTCA). Both of these are effective in reducing significantly the mortality of myocardial infarction. Many centers are now moving to the use of PTCA as it is somewhat more effective than thrombolysis if it can be administered early. This may involve transfer to a nearby facility with facilities for <a href="/wiki/Angioplasty" title="Angioplasty">angioplasty</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trauma">Trauma</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Trauma">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Physical_trauma" class="mw-redirect" title="Physical trauma">Physical trauma</a></div>
<p>Major trauma, the term for patients with multiple injuries, often from a <a href="/wiki/Traffic_accident" class="mw-redirect" title="Traffic accident">motor vehicle crash</a> or a major fall, is initially handled in the Emergency Department.  However, trauma is a separate (surgical) specialty from emergency medicine (which is itself a medical specialty, and has certifications in the United States from the American Board of Emergency Medicine).
</p><p>Trauma is treated by a <a href="/wiki/Trauma_team" title="Trauma team">trauma team</a> who have been trained using the principles taught in the internationally recognized <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Trauma_Life_Support" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced Trauma Life Support">Advanced Trauma Life Support</a> (ATLS) course of the <a href="/wiki/American_College_of_Surgeons" title="American College of Surgeons">American College of Surgeons</a>. Some other international training bodies have started to run similar courses based on the same principles.
</p><p>The services that are provided in an emergency department can range from <a href="/wiki/Medical_radiography" class="mw-redirect" title="Medical radiography">x-rays</a> and the setting of broken bones to those of a full-scale <a href="/wiki/Trauma_centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Trauma centre">trauma centre</a>. A patient's chance of survival is greatly improved if the patient receives definitive treatment (i.e. surgery or reperfusion) within one hour of an accident (such as a car accident) or onset of acute illness (such as a heart attack). This critical time frame is commonly known as the "<a href="/wiki/Golden_hour_(medicine)" title="Golden hour (medicine)">golden hour</a>".
</p><p>Some emergency departments in smaller hospitals are located near a helipad which is used by helicopters to transport a patient to a trauma centre. This inter-hospital transfer is often done when a patient requires advanced medical care unavailable at the local facility. In such cases the emergency department can only <a href="/wiki/Stabilization_(medical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stabilization (medical)">stabilize</a> the patient for transport.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mental_illness">Mental illness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Mental illness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some patients arrive at an emergency department for a complaint of mental illness. In many jurisdictions (including many US states), patients who appear to be mentally ill and to present a danger to themselves or others may be brought against their will to an emergency department by law enforcement officers for psychiatric examination. The emergency department conducts medical clearance rather than treats acute behavioral disorders. From the emergency department, patients with significant mental illness may be transferred to a psychiatric unit (in many cases involuntarily).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asthma_and_COPD">Asthma and COPD</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Asthma and COPD">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Acute exacerbations of chronic respiratory diseases, mainly <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chronic_obstructive_pulmonary_disease" title="Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease">chronic obstructive pulmonary disease</a> (COPD), are assessed as emergencies and treated with <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_therapy" title="Oxygen therapy">oxygen therapy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bronchodilator" title="Bronchodilator">bronchodilators</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glucocorticoid" title="Glucocorticoid">steroids</a> or <a href="/wiki/Theophylline" title="Theophylline">theophylline</a>, have an urgent <a href="/wiki/Chest_X-ray" class="mw-redirect" title="Chest X-ray">chest X-ray</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arterial_blood_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Arterial blood gas">arterial blood gases</a> and are referred for <a href="/wiki/Intensive_care" class="mw-redirect" title="Intensive care">intensive care</a> if necessary. <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_ventilation" title="Mechanical ventilation">Noninvasive ventilation</a> in the ED has reduced the requirement for <a href="/wiki/Tracheal_intubation" title="Tracheal intubation">tracheal intubation</a> in many cases of severe exacerbations of COPD.
</p>
<h2><span id="Special_facilities.2C_training.2C_and_equipment"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Special_facilities,_training,_and_equipment">Special facilities, training, and equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Special facilities, training, and equipment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An ED requires different equipment and different approaches than most other hospital divisions. Patients frequently arrive with unstable conditions, and so must be treated quickly. They may be unconscious, and information such as their medical history, allergies, and blood type may be unavailable. ED staff are trained to work quickly and effectively even with minimal information.
</p><p>ED staff must also interact efficiently with pre-hospital care providers such as <a href="/wiki/EMTs" class="mw-redirect" title="EMTs">EMTs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paramedics" class="mw-redirect" title="Paramedics">paramedics</a>, and others who are occasionally based in an ED. The pre-hospital providers may use equipment unfamiliar to the average physician, but ED physicians must be expert in using (and safely removing) specialized equipment, since devices such as <a href="/wiki/Military_anti-shock_trousers" title="Military anti-shock trousers">military anti-shock trousers</a> ("MAST") and <a href="/wiki/Traction_splint" title="Traction splint">traction splints</a> require special procedures. Among other reasons, given that they must be able to handle specialized equipment, physicians can now specialize in emergency medicine, and EDs employ many such specialists.
</p><p>ED staff have much in common with ambulance and fire crews, <a href="/wiki/Combat_medic" title="Combat medic">combat medics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Search_and_rescue" title="Search and rescue">search and rescue</a> teams, and <a href="/wiki/Disaster_response" title="Disaster response">disaster response</a> teams. Often, joint training and practice drills are organized to improve the coordination of this complex response system. Busy EDs exchange a great deal of equipment with ambulance crews, and both must provide for replacing, returning, or reimbursing for costly items.
</p><p>Cardiac arrest and major trauma are relatively common in EDs, so <a href="/wiki/Defibrillator" class="mw-redirect" title="Defibrillator">defibrillators</a>, automatic ventilation and <a href="/wiki/Cardiopulmonary_resuscitation" title="Cardiopulmonary resuscitation">CPR</a> machines, and bleeding control dressings are used heavily. Survival in such cases is greatly enhanced by shortening the wait for key interventions, and in recent years some of this specialized equipment has spread to pre-hospital settings. The best-known example is defibrillators, which spread first to ambulances, then in an <a href="/wiki/Automated_external_defibrillator" title="Automated external defibrillator">automatic version</a> to police cars and fire apparatus, and most recently to public spaces such as airports, office buildings, hotels, and even shopping malls.
</p><p>Because time is such an essential factor in emergency treatment, EDs typically have their own diagnostic equipment to avoid waiting for equipment installed elsewhere in the hospital. Nearly all have radiographic examination rooms staffed by dedicated <a href="/wiki/Radiographer" title="Radiographer">Radiographer</a>, and many now have full radiology facilities including CT scanners and ultrasonography equipment. Laboratory services may be handled on a priority basis by the hospital lab, or the ED may have its own "STAT Lab" for basic labs (blood counts, blood typing, toxicology screens, etc.) that must be returned very rapidly.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-emergency_use">Non-emergency use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Non-emergency use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Metrics applicable to the ED can be grouped into three main categories, volume, cycle time, and patient satisfaction. Volume metrics including arrivals per hour, percentage of ED beds occupied and age of patients are understood at a basic level at all hospitals as an indication for staffing requirements. Cycle time metrics are the mainstays of the evaluation and tracking of process efficiency and are less widespread since an active effort is needed to collect and analyze this data. Patient satisfaction metrics, already commonly collected by nursing groups, physician groups and hospitals, are useful in demonstrating the impact of changes in patient perception of care over time. Since patient satisfaction metrics are derivative and subjective, they are less useful in primary process improvement. Health information exchanges can reduce nonurgent ED visits by supplying current data about <a href="/wiki/Admission,_discharge,_and_transfer_system" title="Admission, discharge, and transfer system">admissions, discharges, and transfers</a> to health plans and accountable care organizations, allowing them to shift ED use to primary care settings.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In all <a href="/wiki/NHS_primary_care_trust" title="NHS primary care trust">Primary Care Trusts</a> there are out of hours medical consultations provided by <a href="/wiki/General_practitioner" title="General practitioner">general practitioners</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nurse_practitioner" title="Nurse practitioner">nurse practitioners</a>.
</p><p>In the United States, high costs are incurred by non-emergency use of the emergency room. The National Hospital Ambulatory Medical Care Survey looked ath the ten most common symptoms for which giving rise to emergency room visits (cough, sore throat, back pain, fever, headache, abdominal pain, chest pain, other pain, shortness of breath, vomiting) and made suggestions as to which would be the most cost-effective choice among <a href="/w/index.php?title=Virtual_care&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Virtual care (page does not exist)">virtual care</a>, <a href="/wiki/Retail_clinic" class="mw-redirect" title="Retail clinic">retail clinic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urgent_care" class="mw-redirect" title="Urgent care">urgent care</a> or <a href="/wiki/Emergency_room" class="mw-redirect" title="Emergency room">emergency room</a>. Notably, certain complaints may also be addressed by a telephone call to a person's <a href="/wiki/Primary_care" title="Primary care">primary care</a> provider.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, and many other countries, hospitals are beginning to create areas in their emergency rooms for people with minor injuries. These are commonly referred as <i>Fast Track</i> or <i>Minor Care</i> units. These units are for people with non-life-threatening injuries. The use of these units within a department have been shown to significantly improve the flow of patients through a department and to reduce waiting times. <a href="/wiki/Urgent_care" class="mw-redirect" title="Urgent care">Urgent care</a> clinics are another alternative, where patients can go to receive immediate care for non-life-threatening conditions. To reduce the strain on limited ED resources, <a href="/wiki/American_Medical_Response" title="American Medical Response">American Medical Response</a> created a checklist that allows <a href="/wiki/EMTs" class="mw-redirect" title="EMTs">EMTs</a> to identify intoxicated individuals who can be safely sent to detoxification facilities instead.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overcrowding">Overcrowding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Overcrowding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Emergency department overcrowding is when function of a department is hindered by an inability to treat all patients in an adequate manner. This is a common occurrence in emergency departments worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-triage_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-triage-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Overcrowding causes inadequate patient care which leads to poorer patient outcomes.<sup id="cite_ref-triage_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-triage-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-overcrowding_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-overcrowding-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> To address this problem, escalation policies are used by emergency departments when responding to an increase in demand (e.g., a sudden inflow of patients) or a reduction in capacity (e.g., a lack of beds to admit patients). The policies aim to maintain the ability to deliver patient care, without compromising safety, by modifying ‘normal’ processes.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emergency_department_waiting_times">Emergency department waiting times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Emergency department waiting times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Emergency department (ED) waiting times have a serious impact on patient mortality, morbidity with <a href="/wiki/Hospital_Readmission" class="mw-redirect" title="Hospital Readmission">readmission</a> in less than 30 days, length of stay, and patient satisfaction. A review of the literature bears out the logical premise that since the outcome of treatment for all diseases and injuries is time-sensitive, the sooner treatment is rendered, the better the outcome.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Various studies reported significant associations between waiting times and higher mortality and morbidity among those who survived.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> It is clear from the literature that untimely hospital deaths and morbidity can be reduced by reductions in ED waiting times.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exit_block">Exit block</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Exit block">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While a significant proportion of people attending emergency departments are discharged home after treatment, many require admission for ongoing observation or treatment, or to ensure adequate social care before discharge is possible. If people requiring admission are not able to be moved to inpatient beds swiftly, "exit block" or "access block" occurs. This often leads to crowding and impairs flow to the point that it can lead to delays in appropriate treatment for newly presenting cases ("arrival access block").<sup id="cite_ref-Mason2017_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason2017-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> This phenomenon is more common in densely populated areas, and affects pediatric departments less than adults ones.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason2017_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason2017-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Exit block can lead to delays in care both in the people awaiting inpatient beds ("boarding") and those who newly present to an exit blocked department. Various solutions have been proposed, such as changes in staffing or increasing inpatient capacity.<sup id="cite_ref-Mason2017_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mason2017-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Frequent_presenters">Frequent presenters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Frequent presenters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Frequent presenters are persons who will present themselves at a hospital multiple times, usually those with complex medical requirements or with psychological issues complicating medical management.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid22171720_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid22171720-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> These persons contribute to overcrowding and typically require more hospital resources although they do not account for a significant number of visits.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid10905642_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid10905642-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> To help prevent inappropriate emergency department use and return visits, some hospitals offer care coordination and support services such as at-home and in-shelter transitional primary care for frequent presenters and short-term housing for homeless patients recovering after discharge.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_military">In the military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: In the military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Emergency departments in the military benefit from the added support of enlisted personnel who are capable of performing a wide variety of tasks they have been trained for through specialized military schooling. For example, in United States Military Hospitals, Air Force Aerospace Medical Technicians and Navy Hospital Corpsmen perform tasks that fall under the scope of practice of both doctors (i.e. sutures, staples and incision and drainages) and nurses (i.e. medication administration, foley catheter insertion, and obtaining intravenous access) and also perform splinting of injured extremities, nasogastric tube insertion, intubation, wound cauterizing, eye irrigation, and much more. Often, some civilian education and/or certification will be required such as an EMT certification, in case of the need to provide care outside the base where the member is stationed. The presence of highly trained enlisted personnel in an Emergency Departments drastically reduces the workload on nurses and doctors.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Violence_against_health_care_workers">Violence against health care workers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Violence against health care workers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to a survey at an urban inner-city tertiary care centre in <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Fernandes1999_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandes1999-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> 57% of health care workers were physically assaulted in 1996. 73% were afraid of patients as a result of violence, almost half, 49%, hid their identities from patients, 74% had reduced job satisfaction. Over one-quarter of the respondents took days off because of violence. Of respondents no longer working in the emergency department, 67% reported that they had left the job at least partly owing to violence. Twenty-four-hour security and a workshop on violence prevention strategies were felt to be the most useful potential interventions. Physical exercise, sleep and the company of family and friends were the most frequent coping strategies cited by those surveyed.<sup id="cite_ref-Fernandes1999_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fernandes1999-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medication_errors">Medication errors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Medication errors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dartmouth_General_Hospital_emergency_department.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Dartmouth_General_Hospital_emergency_department.jpg/220px-Dartmouth_General_Hospital_emergency_department.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Dartmouth_General_Hospital_emergency_department.jpg/330px-Dartmouth_General_Hospital_emergency_department.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Dartmouth_General_Hospital_emergency_department.jpg/440px-Dartmouth_General_Hospital_emergency_department.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3212" data-file-height="2408" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dartmouth_General_Hospital_emergency_department.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Emergency Department of Dartmouth General Hospital</div></div></div><p><a href="/wiki/Medication_error" class="mw-redirect" title="Medication error">Medication errors</a> are issues that lead to incorrect medication distribution or potential for patient harm.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> As of 2014, around 3% of all hospital-related adverse effects were due to medication errors in the emergency department (ED); between 4% and 14% of medications given to patients in the ED were incorrect and children were particularly at risk.<sup id="cite_ref-Weant_2014_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weant_2014-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Errors can arise if the doctor prescribes the wrong medication, if the prescription intended by the doctor is not the one actually communicated to the <a href="/wiki/Pharmacy" title="Pharmacy">pharmacy</a> due to an illegibly-written <a href="/wiki/Medical_prescription" title="Medical prescription">prescription</a> or misheard verbal order, if the pharmacy dispenses the wrong medication, or if the medication is then given to the wrong person.<sup id="cite_ref-Weant_2014_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weant_2014-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The ED is a riskier environment than other areas of the hospital due to medical practitioners not knowing the patient as well as they know longer term hospital patients, due to time pressure caused by overcrowding, and due to the emergency-driven nature of the medicine that is practiced there.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid12954674_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid12954674-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
