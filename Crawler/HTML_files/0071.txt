	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">English name</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">&#160;&#160;(Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=English_surname&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="English surname">English surname</a>)</span></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><dl><dd><i>For English names in biology, see <a href="/wiki/Common_name" title="Common name">Common name</a></i></dd></dl>
<p><b>English names</b> are names used in, or originating in, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.
In England as elsewhere in the <a href="/wiki/English-speaking_world" title="English-speaking world">English-speaking world</a>, a complete name usually consists of a <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>, commonly referred to as a first name or Christian name, and a (most commonly <a href="/wiki/Patrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrilineal">patrilineal</a>) <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a> or <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a>, also referred to as a last name. There can be several given names, some of these being often referred to as a second name, or middle name(s).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Given_names">Given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=English_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most given names used in England do not have English derivation. Most traditional names are <a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions" title="Roman naming conventions">Latin or Romance</a>,  <a href="/wiki/List_of_biblical_names" title="List of biblical names">Christian or Biblical names</a>/<a href="/wiki/Hebrew" class="mw-redirect" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a> (Daniel, David, John, Michael), <a href="/wiki/Greek_name" title="Greek name">Greek</a> (Nicholas, Peter, Paul) or <a href="/wiki/Germanic_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Germanic names">Germanic names</a> adopted via the transmission of <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a>/<a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">Norman</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Robert" title="Robert">Robert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard" title="Richard">Richard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henry_(given_name)" title="Henry (given name)">Henry</a> or <a href="/wiki/William_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="William (name)">William</a>.
There remains a limited set of given names which have an actual English derivation (see  <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon names">Anglo-Saxon names</a>); examples include  <a href="/wiki/Alfred_(name)" title="Alfred (name)">Alfred</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edgar" title="Edgar">Edgar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edmund" title="Edmund">Edmund</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward" title="Edward">Edward</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edwin" title="Edwin">Edwin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harold_(given_name)" title="Harold (given name)">Harold</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oswald_(given_name)" title="Oswald (given name)">Oswald</a>. A distinctive feature of Anglophone names is the surnames of important families used as given names, originally to indicate political support or patronage. Many examples have now become normal names chosen because parents like them, and any political sense lost. Most are male names like Cecil, Gerald, Howard, Percy, Montague, Stanley or Gordon, though some have female versions like Cecilia or Geraldine. Other languages have few equivalents, although the saint's surname Xavier is often used by Roman Catholics.
</p><p>During most of the 19th century, the most popular given names were <a href="/wiki/Mary_(given_name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mary (given name)">Mary</a> and either <a href="/wiki/John_(given_name)" title="John (given name)">John</a> or <a href="/wiki/William_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="William (name)">William</a> for girls and boys, respectively. Throughout the Early Modern period, the variation of given names was comparatively small; the three most frequent male given names accounted for close to 50% of male population throughout this period. For example, of the boys born in London in the year 1510, 24.4% were named <a href="/wiki/John_(given_name)" title="John (given name)">John</a>, 13.3% were named <a href="/wiki/Thomas_(name)" title="Thomas (name)">Thomas</a> and 11.7% were named William.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> A trend towards more diversity in given names began in the mid-19th century, and by 1900, 22.9% of the newborn boys, and 16.2% of the newborn girls in the UK shared the top three given names. The trend continued during the 20th century, and by 1994, these figures had fallen to 11% and 8.6%, respectively. This trend is a result of a combination of greater <a href="/wiki/Individualism" title="Individualism">individualism</a> in the choice of names, and the increasing <a href="/wiki/Immigration_to_the_United_Kingdom_since_1922" class="mw-redirect" title="Immigration to the United Kingdom since 1922">ethnic heterogeneity</a> of UK population, which led to a wider range of frequent given names from non-European traditions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surnames">Surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=English_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Christopher Daniell, in <i>From Norman Conquest to Magna Carta</i>, 1140 marked what might be the first recorded use of a modern surname, inherited by multiple generations.  The sons of a Norman named Robert used a modern inheritable surname, <i><a href="/wiki/FitzGerald_(surname)" title="FitzGerald (surname)">FitzGerald</a></i>, in honour of an earlier relative, named <i>Gerald</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-From_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-From-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The introduction of parish registers in 1538 contributed significantly to the stabilization of the surname system, but it was not until the late 17th century that fixed surnames were introduced throughout England.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Office_for_National_Statistics" title="Office for National Statistics">Office for National Statistics</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the top ten most frequent surnames in England during the 1990s were:

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Compound_surnames">Compound surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=English_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Compound surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Double-barrelled_name" title="Double-barrelled name">Double-barrelled name</a></div>
<p>Double-barrelled names may be formed for a variety of reasons, including combining of spouses' surnames upon marriage or, more commonly in the past, adding another family's surname as a condition of inheritance.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Compound surnames in English feature two words, often joined by a hyphen or hyphens, for example <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hepburne-Scott,_10th_Lord_Polwarth" title="Henry Hepburne-Scott, 10th Lord Polwarth">Henry Hepburne-Scott</a>, with some families having as many as three or four words making up their surname, such as <a href="/wiki/Charles_Hepburn-Stuart-Forbes-Trefusis,_21st_Baron_Clinton" title="Charles Hepburn-Stuart-Forbes-Trefusis, 21st Baron Clinton">Charles Hepburn-Stuart-Forbes-Trefusis, 21st Baron Clinton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Charles_Robert_Vane-Tempest-Stewart,_9th_Marquess_of_Londonderry" class="mw-redirect" title="Alexander Charles Robert Vane-Tempest-Stewart, 9th Marquess of Londonderry">Alexander Charles Robert Vane-Tempest-Stewart, 9th Marquess of Londonderry</a>.  However, it is not unusual for compound surnames to be composed of separate words not linked by a hyphen, for example <a href="/wiki/Iain_Duncan_Smith" title="Iain Duncan Smith">Iain Duncan Smith</a>, a former leader of the <a href="/wiki/British_Conservative_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="British Conservative Party">Conservative Party</a>, whose surname is "Duncan Smith".
</p>
