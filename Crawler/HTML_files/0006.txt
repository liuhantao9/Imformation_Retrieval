<p>The <b>Department of Computer Science and Technology</b>, formerly the <b>Computer Laboratory</b>, is the <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">computer science</a> department of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a>. As of 2007<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Department_of_Computer_Science_and_Technology,_University_of_Cambridge&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> it employed 35 academic staff, 25 support staff, 35 affiliated research staff, and about 155 research students. The current head of department is Professor <a href="/wiki/Ann_Copestake" title="Ann Copestake">Ann Copestake</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Department_of_Computer_Science_and_Technology,_University_of_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Department was founded as the <b>Mathematical Laboratory</b> under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/John_Lennard-Jones" title="John Lennard-Jones">John Lennard-Jones</a> on 14 May 1937, though it did not get properly established until after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. The new laboratory was housed in the North Wing of the former Anatomy School, on the <a href="/wiki/New_Museums_Site" title="New Museums Site">New Museums Site</a>. Upon its foundation, it was intended <i>to provide a computing service for general use, and to be a centre for the development of computational techniques in the University</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Diploma_in_Computer_Science" title="Cambridge Diploma in Computer Science">Cambridge Diploma in Computer Science</a> was the world's first postgraduate taught course in computing, starting in 1953.
</p><p>In October 1946, work began under <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Wilkes" title="Maurice Wilkes">Maurice Wilkes</a> on <a href="/wiki/EDSAC" title="EDSAC">EDSAC</a> (<i>Electronic Delay Storage Automatic Calculator</i>), which subsequently became the world's first fully operational and practical <a href="/wiki/Stored_program" class="mw-redirect" title="Stored program">stored program</a> computer when it ran its first program on 6 May 1949. It inspired the world's first business computer, <a href="/wiki/LEO_computer" class="mw-redirect" title="LEO computer">LEO</a>. It was replaced by <a href="/wiki/EDSAC_2" title="EDSAC 2">EDSAC 2</a>, the first microcoded and bitsliced computer, in 1958.
</p><p>In 1961, <a href="/wiki/David_Hartley_(computer_scientist)" title="David Hartley (computer scientist)">David Hartley</a> developed <a href="/wiki/Autocode" title="Autocode">Autocode</a>, one of the first <a href="/wiki/High-level_programming_language" title="High-level programming language">high-level programming languages</a>, for <a href="/wiki/EDSAC_2" title="EDSAC 2">EDSAC 2</a>. Also in that year, proposals for <a href="/wiki/Titan_(1963_computer)" title="Titan (1963 computer)">Titan</a>, based on the <a href="/wiki/Atlas_Computer_(Manchester)" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlas Computer (Manchester)">Ferranti Atlas</a> machine, were developed. Titan became fully operational in 1964 and EDSAC 2 was retired the following year. In 1967, a full ('24/7') multi-user time-shared service for up to 64 users was inaugurated on Titan.
</p><p>In 1953, the Mathematical Laboratory offered the world's first postgraduate taught course in computer science. 
</p><p>In 1970, the Mathematical Laboratory was renamed the <b>Computer Laboratory</b>, with separate departments for Teaching and Research and the Computing Service, providing computing services to the university and its colleges. The two did not fully separate until 2001, when the Computer Laboratory moved out to the new William Gates building in <a href="/wiki/West_Cambridge" title="West Cambridge">West Cambridge</a>, off <a href="/wiki/Madingley_Road" title="Madingley Road">Madingley Road</a>, leaving behind an independent <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge_Computing_Service" title="University of Cambridge Computing Service">Computing Service</a>.
</p><p>In 2002, the Computer Laboratory launched the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Computer_Lab_Ring" title="Cambridge Computer Lab Ring">Cambridge Computer Lab Ring</a>, a graduate society named after the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Ring_(computer_network)" title="Cambridge Ring (computer network)">Cambridge Ring</a> network.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current">Current</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Department_of_Computer_Science_and_Technology,_University_of_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Current">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 30 June 2017, the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University_Reporter" title="Cambridge University Reporter">Cambridge University Reporter</a> announced that the Computer Laboratory would change its name to the Department of Computer Science and Technology from 1 October 2017, to reflect the broadened scope of its purpose and activities.<sup id="cite_ref-namechange_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-namechange-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Department currently offers a 3-year undergraduate course and a 1-year masters course (with an optional natural language processing theme). Recent research has focused on <a href="/wiki/Platform_virtualization" class="mw-redirect" title="Platform virtualization">virtualisation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_security" title="Information security">security</a>, <a href="/wiki/Usability" title="Usability">usability</a>, <a href="/wiki/Formal_verification" title="Formal verification">formal verification</a>, <a href="/wiki/Formal_semantics_of_programming_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Formal semantics of programming languages">formal semantics of programming languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Computer_architecture" title="Computer architecture">computer architecture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natural_language_processing" title="Natural language processing">natural language processing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wireless_networking" class="mw-redirect" title="Wireless networking">wireless networking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biometric_identification" class="mw-redirect" title="Biometric identification">biometric identification</a>, <a href="/wiki/Routing" title="Routing">routing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Positioning_system" title="Positioning system">positioning systems</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> (<i>"Computing for the future of the planet"</i>). Members have been involved in the creation of many successful UK <a href="/wiki/Information_technology" title="Information technology">IT</a> companies such as <a href="/wiki/Acorn_Computers" title="Acorn Computers">Acorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/ARM_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="ARM Limited">ARM</a>, nCipher and <a href="/wiki/XenSource" class="mw-redirect" title="XenSource">XenSource</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Achievements_and_innovations">Achievements and innovations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Department_of_Computer_Science_and_Technology,_University_of_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Achievements and innovations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Members have made impact in computers, Turing machines,
microprogramming, subroutines, computer networks, mobile protocols, security, 
programming languages, kernels, OS, security, virtualisation, location badge
systems, etc. Below is a list.
</p>
<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 35em; -webkit-column-width: 35em; column-width: 35em;">
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/EDSAC" title="EDSAC">EDSAC</a> – world's first practical stored program electronic computer (1949–1958)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Subroutine" title="Subroutine">Subroutine</a> (1951)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/OXO" title="OXO">OXO</a> – world's first video game (1952)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/EDSAC_2" title="EDSAC 2">EDSAC 2</a> (1958–1965)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Autocode" title="Autocode">Autocode</a> – one of the first <a href="/wiki/High-level_programming_language" title="High-level programming language">high-level programming languages</a> (1961)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Titan_(1963_computer)" title="Titan (1963 computer)">Titan</a> – early multi-user time-share computer (1964–1973)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Phoenix_(computer)" title="Phoenix (computer)">Phoenix</a> – IBM 370 with locally developed OS and hardware extensions (1973–1995)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/TRIPOS" title="TRIPOS">TRIPOS</a> operating system – became later the basis for <a href="/wiki/AmigaDOS" title="AmigaDOS">AmigaDOS</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/BCPL" title="BCPL">BCPL</a> programming language – ancestor of <a href="/wiki/C_(programming_language)" title="C (programming language)">C</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/CAP_computer" title="CAP computer">CAP computer</a> – hardware support for <a href="/wiki/Capability-based_security" title="Capability-based security">capability-based security</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Ring_(computer_network)" title="Cambridge Ring (computer network)">Cambridge Ring</a> – an early <a href="/wiki/Local_area_network" title="Local area network">local area network</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Distributed_Computing_System" title="Cambridge Distributed Computing System">Cambridge Distributed Computing System</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Trojan_Room_coffee_pot" title="Trojan Room coffee pot">Trojan Room coffee pot</a> – the world's first <a href="/wiki/Webcam" title="Webcam">webcam</a> (1993)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Iris_recognition" title="Iris recognition">Iris recognition</a> – <a href="/wiki/Biometric_identification" class="mw-redirect" title="Biometric identification">biometric identification</a> with vanishingly small false-accept rate</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nemesis_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nemesis (computing)">Nemesis</a> – real-time microkernel OS</li>
<li>Active Badge System –<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Active_Bat" title="Active Bat">Active Bat</a> – ultrasonic indoor positioning system</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Xen_(virtual_machine_monitor)" class="mw-redirect" title="Xen (virtual machine monitor)">Xen</a> – virtual machine monitor (2003–<i>present</i>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Isabelle_(proof_assistant)" title="Isabelle (proof assistant)">Isabelle</a> and <a href="/wiki/HOL_(proof_assistant)" title="HOL (proof assistant)">HOL</a> – interactive theorem provers</li></ul>
</div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_business_enterprise">Impact on business enterprise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Department_of_Computer_Science_and_Technology,_University_of_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Impact on business enterprise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of companies have been founded by staff and graduates. Their names were featured in the new entrance in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-businessweekly_2012_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-businessweekly_2012-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Some cited examples of successful companies are <a href="/wiki/ARM_Holdings" class="mw-redirect" title="ARM Holdings">ARM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Autonomy_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Autonomy Corporation">Autonomy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aveva" title="Aveva">Aveva</a>, <a href="/wiki/CSR_plc" class="mw-redirect" title="CSR plc">CSR</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Domino_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Domino (company) (page does not exist)">Domino</a>. One common factor they share is that key staff or founder members are "drenched in university training and research".<sup id="cite_ref-cabume_vc_fund_2012_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cabume_vc_fund_2012-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Computer_Lab_Ring" title="Cambridge Computer Lab Ring">Cambridge Computer Lab Ring</a> was praised for its "tireless work" by <a href="/wiki/Andy_Hopper" title="Andy Hopper">Andy Hopper</a> in 2012, at its tenth anniversary dinner.<sup id="cite_ref-businessweekly_quested_2012_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-businessweekly_quested_2012-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
