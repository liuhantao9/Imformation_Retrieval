<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="English_Canadian_names">English Canadian names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: English Canadian names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Expand_section plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg" class="image"><img alt="[icon]" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" decoding="async" width="20" height="14" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="44" data-file-height="31" /></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Canadian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">January 2011</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>In English Canada, names follow much the same convention as they do in the United States and United Kingdom. Usually the "first name" (as described in e.g. birth certificates) is what a child goes by, although a middle name (if any) may be preferred—both also known as "given names." The "last name" is usually taken from a child's parents, which may be from either or both (joined by hyphenation).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Live_Birth_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Live_Birth-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Outside Quebec (with distinct civil law system), either spouse has the right to assume the otherʼs last name, as long as it is not intended for the purposes of fraud. Getting married does not result in a legal change of name nor automatically change identification records.<sup id="cite_ref-Taking_spouse_name_Ontario_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taking_spouse_name_Ontario-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Proof_of_name_Canada_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Proof_of_name_Canada-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;note 2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is not uncommon to see names that follow patterns differing from the English and French naming conventions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="French_Canadian_names">French Canadian names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: French Canadian names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Given_names_in_Quebec">Given names in Quebec</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Given names in Quebec">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/French_Canada" class="mw-redirect" title="French Canada">French Canada</a>, up until the late 1960s, children of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> origin were given three names at birth (usually not hyphenated): the first, Marie or Joseph, usually indicated the gender of the child. The second was usually the name of the <a href="/wiki/Godparent" title="Godparent">godfather</a> or <a href="/wiki/Godparent" title="Godparent">godmother</a>, while the third and last given name was the name used in everyday situations.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="reliable source needed for the whole proposition (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Thus, a child prenamed <i>Joseph Bruno Jean</i> on his birth or baptismal certificate would indicate the baby was a boy, the godfather's first name was <i>Bruno</i> and that the child would be called <i>Jean</i> (and not <i>Joseph</i>) for all intents and purposes of everyday life. A real-life example of this naming convention was that of Canadian prime minister <a href="/wiki/Jean_Chr%C3%A9tien" title="Jean Chrétien">Jean Chrétien</a>, who was born <i>Joseph Jacques Jean Chrétien</i>.
</p><p>Although it is still permitted to list multiple names on a birth, it is uncommon for people to even be aware of their other given names, and the practice of using religious names became increasingly rarer after the <a href="/wiki/Quiet_Revolution" title="Quiet Revolution">Quiet Revolution</a> as baptism ceremonies gradually became less common. The Quebec government officially recommends not using more than four given names.<sup id="cite_ref-Given_Name_rules_Quebec_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Given_Name_rules_Quebec-6">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surnames_in_Quebec">Surnames in Quebec</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Surnames in Quebec">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Currently, most couples give the child the surname of the father, though the <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> civil code allows a couple to combine at most two of their surnames, with or without hyphens.<sup id="cite_ref-Naming_rules_Quebec_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Naming_rules_Quebec-7">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Thus a couple named <i>Joseph Bouchard-Tremblay</i> and <i>Marie Dion-Roy</i> could give to their children the surnames <i>Bouchard</i>, <i>Tremblay</i>, <i>Dion</i>, <i>Roy</i>, <i>Bouchard-Tremblay</i>, <i>Dion-Roy</i>, <i>Bouchard-Dion</i>, <i>Bouchard-Roy</i>, and so on.
</p><p>Following traditional French custom, Quebec women did not change their legal names upon marriage, but were referred to by their husband's surname in common speech. This latter practice fell out of favour following the <a href="/wiki/Quiet_Revolution" title="Quiet Revolution">Quiet Revolution</a>, and spouses now retain their surnames after marriage in all contexts. A name change for marriage is difficult or impossible to do if desired, as requests to adopt a husband's name after marriage are typically denied.<sup id="cite_ref-Marriage_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marriage-8">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Change_Name_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Change_Name-9">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-News_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-News-10">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="The_.22nom-dit.22_tradition"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_&quot;nom-dit&quot;_tradition">The "nom-dit" tradition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The &quot;nom-dit&quot; tradition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Dit_name" title="Dit name">Dit name</a></div>
<p>Until the late 19th century, several families also had a <i>nom-dit</i> tradition.  This was a family nickname (literally a "said name").<sup id="cite_ref-Canadian_Genealogy_Centre_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canadian_Genealogy_Centre-11">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The origins of the <i>noms-dits</i> were various.  Some <i>noms-dits</i> were the war-name of the first settler, while he was a soldier: <i>Hébert dit Jolicœur</i> (Pretty Heart, cf. Braveheart), <i>Thomas dit Tranchemontagne</i> (mountain chopper).  Some denoted the place of origin of the first settler: <i>Langevin</i> (<a href="/wiki/Anjou" title="Anjou">Anjou</a>), <i>Barbeau dit Poitevin</i> (<a href="/wiki/Poitou" title="Poitou">Poitou</a>).  Others denoted a characteristic of the person or of his dwelling: <i>Lacourse</i>, <i>Lépine</i>, <i>Larivière</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indigenous_Canadian_names">Indigenous Canadian names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Indigenous Canadian names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many, if not most, Indigenous Canadians (primarily in this First Nations and Métis people, but also Inuit to an extent) carry European surnames, and most of those are French names, either because of intermarriage with French Canadian and Métis men and indigenous women or because a surname was assigned to an indigenous person by a French-speaking Christian missionary.  The most notable example is the <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_(name)" title="Cardinal (name)">Cardinal</a> family, which started with a few French Canadians moving to the West and now includes thousands of Cree and Metis people in Alberta, Saskatchewan, and beyond.  Other examples would <a href="/wiki/Belcourt_(surname)" title="Belcourt (surname)">Belcourt</a>.  Other European groups have contributed notably to indigenous, and especially Métis names, such as <a href="/wiki/Isbister" title="Isbister">Isbister</a> (Scottish), <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Hardisty" title="Sarah Hardisty">Hardisty</a> (English), and so on.
</p><p>On the Great Plains, many First Nations people have surnames that are direct English translations of an ancestor's given name, often these are multiple words long; examples would be <a href="/wiki/Milton_Born_With_A_Tooth" title="Milton Born With A Tooth">Born with a Tooth</a> (Blackfoot), <a href="/wiki/Byron_Chief-Moon" title="Byron Chief-Moon">Chief-Moon</a> (Blood), or <a href="/wiki/Massey_Whiteknife" title="Massey Whiteknife">Whiteknife</a> (Cree).
</p><p>For others, a name in their ancestral language is rendered in English or French spelling such as <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Tootoosis" title="Gordon Tootoosis">Tootoosis</a> (Plains Cree), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Newashish&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Newashish (page does not exist)">Newashish</a> (Atikamekw), or <a href="/w/index.php?title=Yahgulanaas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Yahgulanaas (page does not exist)">Yahgulanaas</a> (Haida).
</p><p>Some people have a legal or baptismal name in English or French and separate name in their ancestral language, for example <a href="/wiki/Oronhyatekha" title="Oronhyatekha">Oronhyatekha</a> (Mohawk) who was also called Peter Martin.
</p><p>For an overview of common Inuit names in Canada see <a href="/wiki/List_of_Canadian_Inuit" title="List of Canadian Inuit">List of Canadian Inuit</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_titles_in_Canadian_English">Use of titles in Canadian English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Use of titles in Canadian English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Canadian_honorifics" title="Canadian honorifics">Canadian honorifics</a></div>
<p>Courtesy titles may be used on official documents in Canada, and are frequently used in formal situations such as business correspondence or meetings.
</p><p>As in other English-speaking countries, the following courtesy titles are used by default or can be chosen by the bearer when completing a form or application:
</p>
<ul><li>Mr. (Mister) (referencing any male)</li>
<li>Mrs. (Missus) (referencing a married, widowed, or divorced female)</li>
<li>Miss (Miss) (referencing a single young female)</li>
<li>Ms. (Miz) (referencing a female of any age without specifying her marital status).</li></ul>
<p>When referring to a woman whose marital status that one does not know, <a href="/wiki/Ms." title="Ms.">it is common to write Ms.</a>, but no specific rules apply. Using a courtesy title in a non-formal situation may be construed as wrong and awkward.
</p><p>Although each person has one of the above titles in their name, other titles may be acquired through various ways. The titles of Dr. (doctor) and Prof. (Professor) are acquired through schooling. Often the titles for professors and medical doctors are used instead of the Given name of the individual when being said by one of the individuals students or patients. This does not apply, however, for friends and family and is strictly prohibited to those using the service of the individual and does not know the individual well enough. An example would be for a medical doctor named Mark. His friends would say, "Hello, Mark," but his patients would say, "Hello, doctor."
</p><p>The parliamentary titles Hon. (honourable) or Rt. Hon. (right honourable) are given to serving cabinet ministers, either federal or provincial/territorial and in some cases are retained for life; for example in the case of retired prime ministers. Senators are designated with the prenominal title Sen. (Senator) while in office.
</p><p>Foreign titles, such as Sir and Count, are not used; the acceptance of foreign honours by Canadian citizens is not permitted under the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_titles_debate" title="Canadian titles debate">Nickle resolution and subsequent Acts of Parliament</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Suffixes">Suffixes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Suffixes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Suffixes always come after the family name, and are also acquired for various reasons.
Suffixes are only used in oral communication when necessary, and are also rarely written on the wish of the individual bearing the suffix.
</p><p>The most common suffixes are Sr. (Senior) and Jr. (Junior). When an individual names their child after themselves or another family member (not necessarily immediate), that individual then bears the suffix Sr. and the offspring bears the suffix Jr., which may also be replaced with II (the second). The suffix "III" is used after either Jr or II and like subsequent numeric suffixes, does not need to happen in one family line. For example, if John and Bob Gruber are brothers and if Bob has a son before John, he will call his son John, II. If John now has a son, his son is John, Jr. As time passes, the III suffix goes to the first born of either John Jr or John II. This is how it is possible and correct for a Jr. to father a IV. Such suffixes are rare amongst French-Canadians due the strongly generational nature of most French given names.
</p><p>Other completely accepted suffixes include:
</p>
<ul><li>M.D. (Medical Doctor)</li>
<li>Ph.D. (Doctor of Philosophy)</li>
<li>MP (Member of Parliament), for federal elected politicians</li>
<li>Abbreviations for provincially elected politicians:
<ul><li>MLA (Member of the Legislative Assembly)</li>
<li>MPP (Member of Provincial Parliament, Ontario only)</li>
<li>MNA (Member of the National Assembly, Quebec only)</li>
<li>MHA (Member of the House of Assembly, Newfoundland and Labrador only)</li></ul></li></ul>
<p>MP and its provincial or territorial equivalents may be followed by the name of the constituency in which that member was elected. For example: Buddy Johnson, M.P. London North-Centre. However, the constituency name or similar designations are not shown of official documents outside of parliament and is never used in casual communication.
</p><p>Postnominals granted under the Canadian honour system include PC (<a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Privy_Council_for_Canada" title="Queen&#39;s Privy Council for Canada">Privy Councillor</a>, granted to federal cabinet ministers and some other senior politicians including leaders of opposition parties); CM (Member of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Canada" title="Order of Canada">Order of Canada</a>); OC (Officer of the Order of Canada); and CC (Companion of the Order of Canada, the highest rank of that order). For a list of other postnominals, including professional titles and orders, provincial honours, and honours in gift of the Crown, see <a href="/wiki/List_of_post-nominal_letters_in_Canada" title="List of post-nominal letters in Canada">List of post-nominal letters in Canada</a>.
</p>
