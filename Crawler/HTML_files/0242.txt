	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">African-American names</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>

		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>African-American names</b> are an integral part of the traditions of the <a href="/wiki/African-American" class="mw-redirect" title="African-American">African-American</a> community. While many Black Americans use names that are popular with wider <a href="/wiki/American_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="American culture">American culture</a>, a number of specific naming trends have emerged within <a href="/wiki/African-American_culture" title="African-American culture">African-American culture</a>. Sources include <a href="/wiki/French_names" class="mw-redirect" title="French names">French names</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arabic_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic names">Arabic names</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muslim_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim names">Muslim names</a>, as well as other European and <a href="/wiki/Biblical_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Biblical name">Biblical names</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African-American_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is widely held that prior to the 1950s and 1960s, most African-American names closely resembled those used within European-American culture.<sup id="cite_ref-Baby_Name_Wizard_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baby_Name_Wizard-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Even within the White-American population, most babies of that era were given a few very common names, with children given <a href="/wiki/Nicknames" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicknames">nicknames</a> to distinguish the various people with the same name.<sup id="cite_ref-individuality_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-individuality-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It was also quite common for immigrants and cultural minorities to choose baby names or change their names to fit in within the wider American culture. This applied to both given names and surnames.<sup id="cite_ref-individuality_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-individuality-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although most consider distinctively black names only a recent phenomenon, recent research by Cook et al. has documented the use of distinctive names by blacks in the late 19th and early 20th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The percentage of black people with such names was similar to that in the 21st century. However, those early names are no longer used by blacks. In fact, Paustian has argued that black names display the same themes and patterns as those in West Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With the rise of 1960s <a href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil Rights Movement">Civil Rights Movement</a>, there was a dramatic rise in African-American names of various origins. <a href="/wiki/San_Diego_State_University" title="San Diego State University">San Diego State University</a> professor Jean Twenge believes that the shift toward unique Black-American baby names is also the result of the cultural shift in America that values individuality over conformity.<sup id="cite_ref-individuality_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-individuality-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2004, Fryer et al. examined the rapid change in naming practices in the early 1970s, with the rapid adoption of distinctively black names, especially in low-income, racially isolated neighborhoods.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> They favor an explanatory model which attributes a change in black perceptions of their identity to the Black Power Movement.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences_and_conventions">Influences and conventions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African-American_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Influences and conventions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lieberson and Mikelson of <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a> analyzed black names, finding that the recent innovative naming practices follow American linguistic conventions even if they are independent of organizations or institutions.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="French_names">French names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African-American_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: French names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many names of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> origin entered the picture during the 1950s and 1960s. Opinions on the origins of the French influence vary, but historically French names such as <a href="/wiki/Monique" title="Monique">Monique</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chantal_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Chantal (disambiguation)">Chantal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9" title="André">André</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Antoine" title="Antoine">Antoine</a> became so common within African-American culture that many white Americans began to think of them solely as "Black names". These names are often seen with spelling variations such as Antwan (Antoine) or Shauntelle (Chantal).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Afrocentric_and_inventive_names">Afrocentric and inventive names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African-American_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Afrocentric and inventive names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Afrocentrism" title="Afrocentrism">Afrocentrism</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lipofsky_Shaquille_O%27Neal.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Lipofsky_Shaquille_O%27Neal.jpg/170px-Lipofsky_Shaquille_O%27Neal.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="256" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/65/Lipofsky_Shaquille_O%27Neal.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="199" data-file-height="300" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lipofsky_Shaquille_O%27Neal.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Basketball player <a href="/wiki/Shaquille_O%27Neal" title="Shaquille O&#39;Neal">Shaquille O'Neal</a>. Shaquille, shortened to "Shaq", is an example of an invented African-American spelling of the name <a href="/wiki/Shakil" class="mw-redirect" title="Shakil">Shakil</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Afrocentrism" title="Afrocentrism">Afrocentrism</a> movement that grew in popularity during the 1970s saw the advent of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">African</a> names among African-Americans, as well as names imagined to be African sounding. Names such as Ashanti have African origins.<sup id="cite_ref-Baby_Name_Wizard_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baby_Name_Wizard-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Black_Power" title="Black Power">Black Power</a> movement inspired many to show pride in their heritage. Harvard University sociologist <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Lieberson" title="Stanley Lieberson">Stanley Lieberson</a> noted that in 1977, the name "<a href="/wiki/Kizzy_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Kizzy (disambiguation)">Kizzy</a>" rose dramatically in popularity following the use of the name in the book and television series <i><a href="/wiki/Roots:_The_Saga_of_an_American_Family" title="Roots: The Saga of an American Family">Roots</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Baby_Name_Wizard_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baby_Name_Wizard-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-salon_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salon-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the 1970s and 1980s, it had become common within African-American culture to invent new names. Many of the invented names took elements from popular existing names. <a href="/wiki/Prefix" title="Prefix">Prefixes</a> such as La/Le, Da/De, Ra/Re, or Ja/Je and <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffixes</a> such as -ique/iqua, -isha, -ari, and -aun/-awn are common, as well as inventive spellings for common names. The book <i>Baby Names Now: From Classic to Cool—The Very Last Word on First Names</i> places the origins of "La" names in African-American culture in <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rosenkrantz_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rosenkrantz-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The name LaKeisha is typically considered American in origin but has elements drawn from both French and African roots. Other names—for example, LaTanisha, DeShawn, JaMarcus, DeAndre, and Shaniqua—were created in the same way. Punctuation marks are seen more often within African-American names than other American names, such as the names Mo'nique and D'Andre.<sup id="cite_ref-Baby_Name_Wizard_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baby_Name_Wizard-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In his dictionary of black names, Cenoura asserts that in the early 21st century, black names are "unique names that come from combinations of two or more names, names constructed with common prefixes and suffixes...'conjugated' with a formula..."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> "Da", "La", and related sounds may originate from the French spoken in Louisiana. Attached to a common name such as Sean and spelled phonetically, one obtains "DaShawn". Diminutive suffixes from French, Spanish and Scottish such as "ita" may be combined directly with prefixes or to a name, as is often found in white naming or nicknaming. Conventions followed usually make the persons gender easily identifiable. Following Spanish, masculine names often end in "o", e.g. "Carmello", while feminine names end with "a", e.g. "Jeretta". Following Irish, French and Italian, apostrophes may be used, e.g. "D'Andre" and "Rene'e". Parents' names may be blended, e.g. the son of "Raymond" and "Yvonne" might be named "Rayvon".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Muslim_names">Muslim names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African-American_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Muslim names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Muhammad_Ali_NYWTS.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Muhammad_Ali_NYWTS.jpg/170px-Muhammad_Ali_NYWTS.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="213" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Muhammad_Ali_NYWTS.jpg/255px-Muhammad_Ali_NYWTS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Muhammad_Ali_NYWTS.jpg/340px-Muhammad_Ali_NYWTS.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2932" data-file-height="3669" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Muhammad_Ali_NYWTS.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ali" title="Muhammad Ali">Muhammad Ali</a>'s name change from Cassius Clay in 1964 helped inspire the popularity of Muslim names within African-American culture.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> has been an influence upon African-American names. <a href="/wiki/Islamic_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic names">Islamic names</a> entered African-American culture with the rise of The <a href="/wiki/Nation_of_Islam" title="Nation of Islam">Nation of Islam</a> among Black Americans with its focus upon Black advocacy. The popular names <a href="/wiki/A%27isha_(name)" title="A&#39;isha (name)">Aisha</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Baby_Name_Wizard_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baby_Name_Wizard-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aliya" title="Aliya">Aaliyah</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-BehindTheName_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BehindTheName-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and others are also examples of names derived from Islam.
</p><p>A number of African-American celebrities began adopting Muslim names, including <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ali" title="Muhammad Ali">Muhammad Ali</a>, who changed his name in 1964 from Cassius Marcellus Clay, Jr. Other celebrities adopting Muslim names include <a href="/wiki/Kareem_Abdul-Jabbar" title="Kareem Abdul-Jabbar">Kareem Abdul-Jabbar</a> (formerly Lew Alcindor) and <a href="/wiki/Amiri_Baraka" title="Amiri Baraka">Amiri Baraka</a> (formerly LeRoi Jones).<sup id="cite_ref-salon_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salon-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Despite the Muslim origin of these names and the place of the <a href="/wiki/Nation_of_Islam" title="Nation of Islam">Nation of Islam</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil Rights Movement">Civil Rights Movement</a>, many Muslim names such as <a href="/wiki/Jamal" title="Jamal">Jamal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malik" title="Malik">Malik</a> entered popular usage among Black Americans simply because they were fashionable, and many Islamic names are now commonly used by African Americans regardless of religion.<sup id="cite_ref-Baby_Name_Wizard_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baby_Name_Wizard-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-salon_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salon-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_and_Biblical_names">European and Biblical names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=African-American_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: European and Biblical names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Even with the rise of created names, it is also still common for African Americans to use biblical, historic, or European names. <a href="/wiki/Daniel" title="Daniel">Daniel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christopher" title="Christopher">Christopher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael" title="Michael">Michael</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_(name)" title="David (name)">David</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_(name)" title="James (name)">James</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph" title="Joseph">Joseph</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Matthew_(given_name)" title="Matthew (given name)">Matthew</a> were among the most common names for African-American boys in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-Baby_Name_Wizard_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baby_Name_Wizard-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Conley_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conley-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
