<p><b>Ultravox</b> (earlier stylized as <b>Ultravox!</b>) were a British <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">new wave</a> band, formed in London in 1973 as <b>Tiger Lily</b>. Between 1980–86, they scored seven Top Ten albums and seventeen Top 40 singles in the UK, the most successful of which was their 1981 hit "<a href="/wiki/Vienna_(Ultravox_song)" title="Vienna (Ultravox song)">Vienna</a>".
</p><p>From 1974 until 1979, singer <a href="/wiki/John_Foxx" title="John Foxx">John Foxx</a> was frontman and the main driving force behind Ultravox. Foxx left the band in March 1979 to embark on a solo career and, following his departure, <a href="/wiki/Midge_Ure" title="Midge Ure">Midge Ure</a> took over as lead singer, guitarist and frontman in October 1979 after he and keyboardist <a href="/wiki/Billy_Currie" title="Billy Currie">Billy Currie</a> worked in the studio project <a href="/wiki/Visage_(band)" title="Visage (band)">Visage</a>. Ure revitalised the band and steered it to commercial chart success lasting until 1988, at which time the group disbanded.
</p><p>A new line-up, led by Currie, was formed in 1992, but achieved limited success, with two albums failing to chart and one solitary single reaching 90 in the UK Singles Chart.
</p><p>The band's best-known line-up of Currie, Ure, bassist Chris Cross and drummer Warren Cann reformed in 2008 and performed a series of reunion shows in 2009 and 2010 before releasing a new studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Brilliant_(album)" title="Brilliant (album)">Brill!ant</a></i>, in May 2012 which reached 21 in the British Album Charts. In November 2013, Ultravox performed as special guests on a four date UK arena tour with <a href="/wiki/Simple_Minds" title="Simple Minds">Simple Minds</a>. These shows proved to be Ultravox's last, as in 2017 both Currie and Ure indicated that Ultravox had run its course.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultravox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Early_years_.E2.80.93_Tiger_Lily:_1973.E2.80.931976"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years_–_Tiger_Lily:_1973–1976">Early years – Tiger Lily: 1973–1976</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultravox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years – Tiger Lily: 1973–1976">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tiger_Lily_(UK_band)" title="Tiger Lily (UK band)">Tiger Lily (UK band)</a></div>
<p>The band was formed in 1973 on the initiative of vocalist and songwriter <a href="/wiki/John_Foxx" title="John Foxx">Dennis Leigh</a>, a then <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Art" title="Royal College of Art">Royal College of Art</a> student, and was originally known as <a href="/wiki/Tiger_Lily_(UK_band)" title="Tiger Lily (UK band)">Tiger Lily</a>. An initial but incomplete line-up comprised Leigh plus <a href="/wiki/Chris_Cross" title="Chris Cross">Chris Allen</a> (bass guitar) and <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Shears" title="Stevie Shears">Stevie Shears</a> (guitar),<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> with <a href="/wiki/Warren_Cann" title="Warren Cann">Warren Cann</a> (drums) and <a href="/wiki/Billy_Currie" title="Billy Currie">Billy Currie</a> (violin) joining in May and October 1974 respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-Discog_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Discog-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The group released one single in 1975, "<a href="/wiki/Ain%27t_Misbehavin%27_(song)#Tiger_Lily_version" title="Ain&#39;t Misbehavin&#39; (song)">Ain't Misbehavin'</a>", a cover of the <a href="/wiki/Fats_Waller" title="Fats Waller">Fats Waller</a> song which featured in a soft porn film. Later, the band went through a series of name changes including The Zips, Fire of London, London Soundtrack, and The Damned, using this last name for a few weeks before discovering that <a href="/wiki/The_Damned_(band)" title="The Damned (band)">another band</a> had already taken it.<sup id="cite_ref-Discog_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Discog-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Experimental_years:_1976.E2.80.931979"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimental_years:_1976–1979">Experimental years: 1976–1979</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultravox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Experimental years: 1976–1979">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="Ultravox.21"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ultravox!">Ultravox!</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultravox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ultravox!">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On the strength of their live act, the band signed to <a href="/wiki/Island_Records" title="Island Records">Island Records</a> in 1976. The group had still not finalised their band name, wanting to make a good and lasting choice. In July 1976, while working on the late stages of their debut album, the band conceived the name Ultravox! (the exclamation mark was a reference to <a href="/wiki/Krautrock" title="Krautrock">krautrock</a> band <a href="/wiki/Neu!" title="Neu!">Neu!</a>, produced by <a href="/wiki/Conny_Plank" title="Conny Plank">Conny Plank</a>, who later produced three Ultravox albums). At the same time, Leigh chose <a href="/wiki/John_Foxx" title="John Foxx">John Foxx</a> and Allen chose <a href="/wiki/Chris_Cross" title="Chris Cross">Chris Cross</a> as their respective stage names.<sup id="cite_ref-Discog_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Discog-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In February 1977, Island released their eponymous debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Ultravox!_(album)" title="Ultravox! (album)">Ultravox!</a></i>.
</p><p>Like many other bands that formed Britain's <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">new wave</a> movements, Ultravox! drew inspiration from the art-school side of <a href="/wiki/Glam_rock" title="Glam rock">glam rock</a>. Musically, Ultravox were heavily influenced by <a href="/wiki/Roxy_Music" title="Roxy Music">Roxy Music</a>, the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Dolls" title="New York Dolls">New York Dolls</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kraftwerk" title="Kraftwerk">Kraftwerk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Their debut was co-produced by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Lillywhite" title="Steve Lillywhite">Steve Lillywhite</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brian_Eno" title="Brian Eno">Brian Eno</a> (who next co-wrote and performed with Bowie on <i><a href="/wiki/Low_(David_Bowie_album)" title="Low (David Bowie album)">Low</a></i>). <i><a href="/wiki/Ultravox!_(album)" title="Ultravox! (album)">Ultravox!</a></i>'s sales were disappointing, and neither the album nor the associated single "Dangerous Rhythm" managed to enter the UK charts. Relations within the band were on an occasionally tenuous footing during this time as Foxx declared that he intended to live without emotions, a sentiment he wrote into the début album track "I Want to Be a Machine".
</p><p>Ultravox returned later in 1977 with the punkier <i><a href="/wiki/Ha!-Ha!-Ha!" title="Ha!-Ha!-Ha!">Ha!-Ha!-Ha!</a></i>. Sales of both the album and its lead single, "<a href="/wiki/ROckWrok" title="ROckWrok">ROckWrok</a>", were poor, both failing to register on the UK charts. "ROckWrok" had a punk-lyric chorus, with the words "Come on, let's tangle in the dark/Fuck like a dog, bite like a shark". (Despite this, it got airplay on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_1" title="BBC Radio 1">BBC Radio 1</a>.) Although <i>Ha!-Ha!-Ha!</i> was dominated by guitars and <a href="/wiki/Electric_violin" title="Electric violin">electric violin</a>, the final track, "Hiroshima Mon Amour", was a prototypical <a href="/wiki/Synthpop" class="mw-redirect" title="Synthpop">synthpop</a> song. One of the first tracks by a British band to feature a drum machine (a <a href="/wiki/Roland_Rhythm_77" class="mw-redirect" title="Roland Rhythm 77">Roland Rhythm 77</a> with preset patterns), the song signalled a new direction for Ultravox. The energy, anger and popular appeal of punk was fading in 1978,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and the more creative UK punk genre talent sought new directions—calling themselves <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">British new wave</a> instead of <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a> artists. "Hiroshima Mon Amour" remains a critics' and fans' favourite from the group's initial period. Ultravox! also performed it on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Old_Grey_Whistle_Test" title="The Old Grey Whistle Test">The Old Grey Whistle Test</a></i> later in 1978.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Becoming_Ultravox">Becoming Ultravox</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultravox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Becoming Ultravox">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In early March 1978, Stevie Shears, whose style of guitar playing was considered a limiting factor, was sacked from the band after they toured England<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and joined <a href="/wiki/Cowboys_International" title="Cowboys International">Cowboys International</a> in 1980. He was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Robin_Simon" title="Robin Simon">Robert Simon</a> (ex-member of <a href="/wiki/Ian_North" title="Ian North">Ian North</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Neo_(UK_band)" class="mw-redirect" title="Neo (UK band)">Neo</a>), who during his first days with the band changed his performance-name to Robin Simon.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Some time in 1978, the group also dropped the exclamation mark, becoming simply "Ultravox". The new line-up performed live at the <a href="/wiki/Reading_and_Leeds_Festivals" title="Reading and Leeds Festivals">Reading Festival</a> along with <a href="/wiki/Radio_Stars" title="Radio Stars">Radio Stars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Penetration_(band)" title="Penetration (band)">Penetration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sham_69" title="Sham 69">Sham 69</a>, The Pirates and <a href="/wiki/The_Jam" title="The Jam">The Jam</a>, playing early versions of "Slow Motion" and "Quiet Men" on 27 August 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Their third album, 1978's <i><a href="/wiki/Systems_of_Romance" title="Systems of Romance">Systems of Romance</a></i>, was recorded with producer <a href="/wiki/Conny_Plank" title="Conny Plank">Conny Plank</a> (the producer of German electronic outfit <a href="/wiki/Kraftwerk" title="Kraftwerk">Kraftwerk</a>) and engineer Dave Hutchins at Plank's Studio in rural Germany. Musically, the album was markedly different from Ultravox's earlier work, bringing synthesisers to the forefront of the group's sound. Despite praise from some critics, the album was a commercial failure. Since none of the albums to date had generated much income, tensions within the band—particularly between Currie and Foxx—threatened the band's viability.
</p><p>Island dropped the band on 31 December 1978 after an attempt to market the album in the United States failed to generate sales. That appeared to be the final nail in their coffin, but Ultravox undertook a self-financed US tour in early 1979. Splitting after their final gig near San Francisco in March 1979, Foxx declared his intention to go solo. Simon remained in the US and briefly joined The Futants, an American punk band from New York. He later returned to England and teamed up with <a href="/wiki/Howard_Devoto" title="Howard Devoto">Howard Devoto</a> to replace guitarist <a href="/wiki/John_McGeoch" title="John McGeoch">John McGeoch</a> in the band <a href="/wiki/Magazine_(band)" title="Magazine (band)">Magazine</a>. The remaining members made their way back to a Britain in the midst of a "winter of discontent". <a href="/wiki/Island_Records" title="Island Records">Island</a> dropped the three Ultravox albums from its catalogue, and released a compilation of highlights from the group's first three albums in 1980, called <i><a href="/wiki/Three_into_One" title="Three into One">Three into One</a></i>.
</p><p>Foxx subsequently signed to Virgin Records and released his album <i><a href="/wiki/Metamatic" title="Metamatic">Metamatic</a></i> in January 1980. By this time, Billy Currie had been recruited by the rising star <a href="/wiki/Gary_Numan" title="Gary Numan">Gary Numan</a> in 1979 to do a performance at the <i>Old Grey Whistle Test</i> show with his band <a href="/wiki/Tubeway_Army" title="Tubeway Army">Tubeway Army</a>. Numan had been a fan of Ultravox and Currie was also asked to play on Numan's début solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pleasure_Principle_(Gary_Numan_album)" title="The Pleasure Principle (Gary Numan album)">The Pleasure Principle</a></i>, and its subsequent tour. Warren Cann went to work for <a href="/wiki/Zaine_Griff" title="Zaine Griff">Zaine Griff</a>, while Chris Cross did some shows with <a href="/wiki/James_Honeyman-Scott" title="James Honeyman-Scott">James Honeyman-Scott</a> (of <a href="/wiki/The_Pretenders" title="The Pretenders">The Pretenders</a>) and Barrie Masters (from <a href="/wiki/Eddie_and_the_Hot_Rods" title="Eddie and the Hot Rods">Eddie and the Hot Rods</a>).
</p>
<h3><span id="The_Midge_Ure_years:_1979.E2.80.931988"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Midge_Ure_years:_1979–1988">The Midge Ure years: 1979–1988</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultravox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Midge Ure years: 1979–1988">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the band seemingly over, Ultravox were then revitalised by <a href="/wiki/Midge_Ure" title="Midge Ure">Midge Ure</a>, who joined the band as vocalist, guitarist and keyboardist. He had already achieved minor success with semi-glam outfit <a href="/wiki/Slik" title="Slik">Slik</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glen_Matlock" title="Glen Matlock">Glen Matlock</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Rich_Kids" class="mw-redirect" title="The Rich Kids">The Rich Kids</a>, and in 1979 he was temporarily playing with hard rock band <a href="/wiki/Thin_Lizzy" title="Thin Lizzy">Thin Lizzy</a>. Ure and Billy Currie had met while collaborating on <a href="/wiki/Visage_(band)" title="Visage (band)">Visage</a>, a studio-based band fronted by <a href="/wiki/New_Romantic" title="New Romantic">New Romantic</a> icon and nightclub impresario <a href="/wiki/Steve_Strange" title="Steve Strange">Steve Strange</a>.
</p><p>Encouraged by Visage drummer and mutual friend <a href="/wiki/Rusty_Egan" title="Rusty Egan">Rusty Egan</a>, Currie asked Ure to join Ultravox. Ure filled both John Foxx's and Robin Simon's posts for Ultravox's next album, <i><a href="/wiki/Vienna_(album)" title="Vienna (album)">Vienna</a></i>, which heralded a major change of direction and would become their most successful to date, far surpassing any of the previous Ultravox (or Foxx's) albums. As with <i>Systems of Romance</i>, it was produced in Germany by Conny Plank. Ure knew of Ultravox's past, being a fan of <i>Systems</i> to the point where the new four-piece outfit (Ultravox mk. III) played songs from that album on tours with Ure singing Foxx's lyrics. Released on <a href="/wiki/Chrysalis_Records" title="Chrysalis Records">Chrysalis Records</a> in June 1980, the <i>Vienna</i> album produced the band's first UK Top 40 hit with "<a href="/wiki/Sleepwalk_(song)" title="Sleepwalk (song)">Sleepwalk</a>" reaching No. 29, while the album itself initially peaked at No.&#160;14. A second single, "<a href="/wiki/Passing_Strangers" title="Passing Strangers">Passing Strangers</a>", failed to reach the Top 40, only reaching No. 57, but the band achieved a substantial hit with the third single, the album's <a href="/wiki/Vienna_(Ultravox_song)" title="Vienna (Ultravox song)">title track</a> (inspired by <a href="/wiki/Carol_Reed" title="Carol Reed">Carol Reed</a>'s 1949 film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Third_Man" title="The Third Man">The Third Man</a></i>). Accompanied by a highly distinctive video, the single became Ultravox's biggest ever hit, released in January 1981 and peaking at Number 2 (kept off the top spot by John Lennon's "Woman" and then <a href="/wiki/Joe_Dolce" title="Joe Dolce">Joe Dolce</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Shaddap_You_Face" title="Shaddap You Face">Shaddap You Face</a>"). On the strength of the single, the album then re-entered the chart and reached No. 3 in early 1981. A fourth single from the album, "<a href="/wiki/All_Stood_Still" title="All Stood Still">All Stood Still</a>", peaked at No. 8. in 1981, and "<a href="/wiki/Systems_of_Romance" title="Systems of Romance">Slow Motion</a>" from <i>Systems of Romance</i> was also re-issued, reaching No. 33 the same year.
</p><p>This was soon followed by <i><a href="/wiki/Rage_in_Eden" title="Rage in Eden">Rage in Eden</a></i> (1981), with the band returning to Conny Plank's studio for what turned out to be a difficult recording session. Whereas the <i>Vienna</i> material had been performed live a great deal prior to a three-week recording process, <i>Rage in Eden</i> took over three months. The album featured a long track in three parts on the second side. The album peaked at No.&#160;4 in the UK, while two singles from the album, "<a href="/wiki/The_Thin_Wall" title="The Thin Wall">The Thin Wall</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/The_Voice_(Ultravox_song)" title="The Voice (Ultravox song)">The Voice</a>", both made the UK Top 20, reaching No.&#160;14 and No.&#160;16 respectively.
</p><p>Ultravox teamed up with producer <a href="/wiki/George_Martin" title="George Martin">George Martin</a> for 1982's <i><a href="/wiki/Quartet_(Ultravox_album)" title="Quartet (Ultravox album)">Quartet</a></i>, which peaked at No.&#160;6 in the UK and contained four Top 20 hit singles; "<a href="/wiki/Reap_the_Wild_Wind_(song)" title="Reap the Wild Wind (song)">Reap the Wild Wind</a>" reaching No.&#160;12 and "<a href="/wiki/Hymn_(Ultravox_song)" title="Hymn (Ultravox song)">Hymn</a>" No.&#160;11 both in 1982, and "<a href="/wiki/Visions_in_Blue" title="Visions in Blue">Visions in Blue</a>" and in 1983 "<a href="/wiki/We_Came_to_Dance" title="We Came to Dance">We Came to Dance</a>" charting at No. 15 and No.&#160;18 respectively. It was their most successful album in the US, peaking at No.&#160;61.
</p><p>The band undertook a major world tour known as the Monument Tour, which was recorded and released as <a href="/wiki/Monument_(Ultravox_album)" title="Monument (Ultravox album)">a live LP and video</a> in 1983, which also reached the UK top ten.
</p><p>1984's <i><a href="/wiki/Lament_(Ultravox_album)" title="Lament (Ultravox album)">Lament</a></i> continued the band's run of top ten albums and produced three top 40 hit singles, including the international hit "<a href="/wiki/Dancing_with_Tears_in_My_Eyes" title="Dancing with Tears in My Eyes">Dancing with Tears in My Eyes</a>" (UK No.&#160;3), "<a href="/wiki/Lament" title="Lament">Lament</a>" (No.&#160;22) and "<a href="/wiki/One_Small_Day" title="One Small Day">One Small Day</a>" peaking at No.&#160;27.
</p><p>Towards the end of 1984, a "greatest hits" compilation spanning the band's 1980s output was released entitled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Collection_(Ultravox_album)" title="The Collection (Ultravox album)">The Collection</a></i>. It was preceded by a new single, "<a href="/wiki/Love%27s_Great_Adventure" title="Love&#39;s Great Adventure">Love's Great Adventure</a>", which enjoyed massive radio airplay that autumn and eventually peaked at No.&#160;12 in the UK, accompanied by a popular <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones" title="Indiana Jones">Indiana Jones</a>-style spoof video. <i>The Collection</i> went triple Platinum and reached No.&#160;2 in the UK album chart, the band's highest ever peak.
</p><p>At this time, Ure also co-wrote and helped produce the 1984 <a href="/wiki/Band_Aid_(band)" title="Band Aid (band)">Band Aid</a> single "<a href="/wiki/Do_They_Know_It%27s_Christmas%3F" title="Do They Know It&#39;s Christmas?">Do They Know It's Christmas?</a>". The group also appeared at <a href="/wiki/Live_Aid" title="Live Aid">Live Aid</a> the following year and played four of their hit singles ("Vienna", "Reap the Wild Wind", "Dancing with Tears in My Eyes" and "<a href="/wiki/One_Small_Day" title="One Small Day">One Small Day</a>"). Later in 1985, Ure scored a No.&#160;1 solo hit with "If I Was" and his solo album <i>The Gift</i> reached No. 2 in the UK.
</p><p>Ultravox reconvened in 1986, but <a href="/wiki/Warren_Cann" title="Warren Cann">Warren Cann</a> was sacked from the band at the beginning of sessions for their <i><a href="/wiki/U-Vox" title="U-Vox">U-Vox</a></i> album. Cann emigrated to the US and retired from music to become an actor. <a href="/wiki/Big_Country" title="Big Country">Big Country</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mark_Brzezicki" title="Mark Brzezicki">Mark Brzezicki</a> took his place. <i>U-Vox</i> was later described by Currie and Ure as "unfocused". Although it continued their string of top ten albums in the UK, the declining performance of its singles prompted both Ure and Cross to leave the band, "<a href="/wiki/Same_Old_Story" class="mw-redirect" title="Same Old Story">Same Old Story</a>" peaking at No.&#160;31, and "All Fall Down" at No.&#160;30 in 1986, with "<a href="/wiki/All_in_One_Day" title="All in One Day">All in One Day</a>" reaching only No. 88 in 1987. In 1987 Ultravox decided not to continue after the <i>U-Vox</i> tour early that year, and split up in 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Ure subsequently concentrated on his solo career with varying levels of success, and Cross retired from music altogether and returned to his former career as a psychotherapist. Billy Currie and Robin Simon reunited in 1989 as the short-lived Humania, performing live shows but never making a release until 2006, the album <i>Sinews of the Soul</i>.
</p><p>Since 1979, Ure and Currie had also been part of the <a href="/wiki/Visage_(band)" title="Visage (band)">Visage</a> ensemble on a part-time basis while simultaneously being in Ultravox. During Ure and Currie's tenure, Visage had released two successful albums and had a string of hit singles (the most notable being "<a href="/wiki/Fade_to_Grey_(Visage_song)" title="Fade to Grey (Visage song)">Fade to Grey</a>"), but Ure decided to leave in 1982 to concentrate solely on Ultravox. Currie remained with Visage for a while longer, but he too had left them by 1984.
</p>

<h3><span id="Post-Midge_Ure_years:_1992.E2.80.931996"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-Midge_Ure_years:_1992–1996">Post-Midge Ure years: 1992–1996</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultravox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Post-Midge Ure years: 1992–1996">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Without any other original members, Currie reformed Ultravox again in 1992 with vocalist <a href="/wiki/Tony_Fenelle" title="Tony Fenelle">Tony Fenelle</a> to record <i><a href="/wiki/Revelation_(Ultravox_album)" title="Revelation (Ultravox album)">Revelation</a></i>, and later <a href="/wiki/Sam_Blue" title="Sam Blue">Sam Blue</a> replaced Fenelle in a new five-piece Ultravox line-up, lending his voice to another release, <i><a href="/wiki/Ingenuity_(album)" title="Ingenuity (album)">Ingenuity</a></i> (1994). These line-ups had little commercial success and the band dissolved again in 1996.
</p>
<h3><span id="Classic_line-up_reunion:_2008.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Classic_line-up_reunion:_2008–present">Classic line-up reunion: 2008–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultravox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Classic line-up reunion: 2008–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Warren Cann, Chris Cross, Billy Currie and Midge Ure reformed Ultravox again in 2008 for a UK tour in April 2009, entitled "Return to Eden".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> This would be the first time the classic line-up of the band have performed together since <a href="/wiki/Live_Aid" title="Live Aid">Live Aid</a> in 1985. The tour played at venues across the UK. Since success from touring the UK they have extended the tour to Germany and Belgium.
</p><p>In September 2008, both <i>Vienna</i> and <i>Rage in Eden</i> were released in digitally remastered two-disc format with the second disc containing previously unreleased material. <i>Quartet</i> and <i>Monument</i> were released in February 2009, also in two-disc editions. The second disc in the <i>Monument</i> package is a DVD containing the concert video that was previously only available on VHS tape. The other Chrysalis-era releases were planned for similar future releases. A live CD and DVD of Ultravox's sell-out Roundhouse show was released on 5 April 2010. The DVD features a documentary filmed on the buildup to and during the reunion tour, much of it filmed by Midge Ure.
</p><p>An anthology of unreleased Ultravox material was planned in mid-2008, but has not been scheduled for release.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Speaking to the <i>Sunday Mail</i>, Midge Ure stated that the reunion was only a one-off, and there would be no new material from the group. He said: "We're not trying to recapture our youth and won't be writing new songs or recording another album. This is about celebrating our music and our anniversary."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Ross" title="Jonathan Ross">Jonathan Ross</a>'s show on BBC's Radio 2 on 21 February 2009, Ure discussed the tour, with all four members of the most successful incarnation of the band on board. He mentioned that this reunion was not planned in light of the previously mentioned events, but rather was spontaneous and happened very quickly with the band members talking and communicating online (using email and Skype). The interview did not reveal whether or not new material would be recorded as a result of the reunion tour.  Ultravox returned to performing live with their Return to Eden II tour during 2010.
</p><p>On 20 January 2011, via their official website, Ultravox.org.uk, the band announced that a new studio album was nearing completion.<sup id="cite_ref-Ultravox_News_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ultravox_News-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The band were to release a fan store exclusive superdeluxe EP on CD and 7&#160;inch single via Townsend Records on 14 March. The EP, called <i>Moments from Eden</i>, featured four new live tracks and was finally released in May 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-Ultravox_News_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ultravox_News-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ultravox's 11th studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Brilliant_(album)" title="Brilliant (album)">Brill!ant</a></i>, was released on 28 May 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Following this release the band embarked on the 'Brilliant Tour' performing shows across the UK and Europe in late 2012. In November 2013 Ultravox performed their last shows as special guests on a four date UK arena tour with <a href="/wiki/Simple_Minds" title="Simple Minds">Simple Minds</a>.
</p><p>On 4 January 2017 Billy Currie announced on his official website that he had decided that his time as a member of Ultravox had come to an end after 43 years.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In December 2017 Midge Ure revealed in an interview with the <a href="/wiki/Daily_Express" title="Daily Express">Daily Express</a> that a further reformation by the band was unlikely.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ultravox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Along with acts like <a href="/wiki/Visage_(band)" title="Visage (band)">Visage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gary_Numan" title="Gary Numan">Gary Numan</a>, Ultravox was one of the early influences on <a href="/wiki/Techno" title="Techno">techno</a>. In his book <i>Energy Flash</i>, <a href="/wiki/Simon_Reynolds" title="Simon Reynolds">Simon Reynolds</a> quotes Adam Lee Miller of <a href="/wiki/Adult_(band)" title="Adult (band)">Adult</a>: "I always get a kick when people say the first 'techno' record was <a href="/wiki/Cybotron_(American_band)" title="Cybotron (American band)">Cybotron</a>'s 'Alleys of Your Mind'... To me, it was just a <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">new wave</a> record. It sounds particularly close to 'Mr X' by Ultravox."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
