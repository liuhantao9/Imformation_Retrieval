<p><i><b>Jamaica Inn</b></i> is a British drama television series that was first broadcast on <a href="/wiki/BBC_One" title="BBC One">BBC One</a> for three consecutive nights from 21 to 23 April 2014. The three-part series, written by <a href="/wiki/Emma_Frost_(screenwriter)" title="Emma Frost (screenwriter)">Emma Frost</a>, is an adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Daphne_du_Maurier" title="Daphne du Maurier">Daphne du Maurier</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Gothic_fiction" title="Gothic fiction">gothic novel</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Jamaica_Inn_(novel)" title="Jamaica Inn (novel)">Jamaica Inn</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jamaica_Inn_(2014_TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Jamaica Inn</i> is set in 1821. It tells the story of Mary Yellan (<a href="/wiki/Jessica_Brown_Findlay" title="Jessica Brown Findlay">Jessica Brown Findlay</a>) who is uprooted to live with her Aunt Patience (<a href="/wiki/Joanne_Whalley" title="Joanne Whalley">Joanne Whalley</a>) after her mother dies. Mary finds Aunt Patience under the spell of her husband, Joss Merlyn (<a href="/wiki/Sean_Harris" title="Sean Harris">Sean Harris</a>) after she arrives at <a href="/wiki/Jamaica_Inn" title="Jamaica Inn">Jamaica Inn</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Coaching_inn" title="Coaching inn">coaching inn</a> he owns in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>. Mary soon realizes that the inn has no guests and is being used as the hub of Joss' 'free' trade. Mary becomes attracted to Jem Merlyn (<a href="/wiki/Matthew_McNulty" title="Matthew McNulty">Matthew McNulty</a>), Joss' younger brother who is a petty thief. Mary meets Francis Davey (<a href="/wiki/Ben_Daniels" title="Ben Daniels">Ben Daniels</a>), the parish vicar, and his sister Hannah (<a href="/wiki/Shirley_Henderson" title="Shirley Henderson">Shirley Henderson</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-BBC1_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jamaica_Inn_(2014_TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Filming began in September 2013 in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kirkby_Lonsdale" title="Kirkby Lonsdale">Kirkby Lonsdale</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-BBC1_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-DS_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DS-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It was originally decided that the series would be filmed in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> An investment from Screen Yorkshire was provided for the series.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC1_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The three-part series was commissioned by <a href="/wiki/Ben_Stephenson" title="Ben Stephenson">Ben Stephenson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Danny_Cohen_(television_executive)" title="Danny Cohen (television executive)">Danny Cohen</a>, both from the BBC.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC1_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jamaica_Inn_(2014_TV_series)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The transmission of the first episode brought about a major debate on social media sites about the sound quality and inaudible dialogue, culminating in over 2,000 complaints being received by the end of the series.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Reviews were mixed to negative. Terry Ramsey of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i> claimed "Daphne du Maurier's story is a classic, but this hard-to-watch (and hear) version is unlikely to have had people gripped."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> David Stephenson of <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Express" title="Daily Express">Daily Express</a></i> agreed: "disappointing BBC drama with mumbling dialogue and absent plot."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sean_Harris" title="Sean Harris">Sean Harris</a> later addressed his reaction to the mumbling controversy in an interview after his BAFTA Award for Best Actor in a Drama Series for <i><a href="/wiki/Southcliffe" title="Southcliffe">Southcliffe</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
