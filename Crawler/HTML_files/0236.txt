	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Heteronym (literature)</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>

		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Heteronym_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Heteronym (disambiguation)">Heteronym (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pessoacopo.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Pessoacopo.jpg/200px-Pessoacopo.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="284" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Pessoacopo.jpg/300px-Pessoacopo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Pessoacopo.jpg/400px-Pessoacopo.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1298" data-file-height="1844" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pessoacopo.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pessoa at Abel Pereira da Fonseca wine house, Fanqueiros Street, Lisbon, 1928.  <br /> Photo by Manuel Martins da Hora.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">literary</a> concept of the <b>heteronym</b> refers to one or more imaginary character(s) created by a writer to write in different styles. Heteronyms differ from <a href="/wiki/Pen_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Pen names">pen names</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonyms</a>, from the Greek words for "false" and "name") in that the latter are just false names, while the former are characters that have their own supposed physiques, biographies, and writing styles.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Heteronyms were named and developed by the <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> writer and poet <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Pessoa" title="Fernando Pessoa">Fernando Pessoa</a> in the early 20th Century, but they were thoroughly explored by the Danish philosopher <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B8ren_Kierkegaard" title="Søren Kierkegaard">Kierkegaard</a> in the 19th century and have also been used by other writers.
</p>

<h2><span id="Pessoa.27s_heteronyms"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Pessoa's_heteronyms">Pessoa's heteronyms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heteronym_(literature)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Pessoa&#039;s heteronyms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Heteronyms_Almada_1958.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ec/Heteronyms_Almada_1958.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="242" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="250" data-file-height="242" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Heteronyms_Almada_1958.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Alberto_Caeiro" class="mw-redirect" title="Alberto Caeiro">Alberto Caeiro</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%81lvaro_de_Campos" title="Álvaro de Campos">Álvaro de Campos</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ricardo_Reis" title="Ricardo Reis">Ricardo Reis</a></i>.<br /> <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Pessoa" title="Fernando Pessoa">Pessoa's</a> Heteronyms by <a href="/wiki/Almada_Negreiros" class="mw-redirect" title="Almada Negreiros">Almada Negreiros</a>, 1958.</div></div></div>
<p>In Pessoa's case, there are at least 70 heteronyms (according to the latest count by Pessoa's editor Teresa Rita Lopes). Some of them are relatives or know each other; they criticise and translate each other's works. Pessoa's three chief heteronyms are Alberto Caeiro, Ricardo Reis and <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lvaro_de_Campos" title="Álvaro de Campos">Álvaro de Campos</a>; the latter two consider the former their master. There are also two whom Pessoa called <i>semi-heteronyms</i>, <a href="/wiki/Bernardo_Soares" class="mw-redirect" title="Bernardo Soares">Bernardo Soares</a> and the Baron of Teive, who are semi-autobiographical characters who write in prose, "a mere mutilation" of the Pessoa personality. There is, lastly, an <i>orthonym</i>, <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Pessoa" title="Fernando Pessoa">Fernando Pessoa</a>, the namesake of the author, who also considers Caeiro his master.
</p><p>The heteronyms dialogue with each other and even with Pessoa in what he calls "the theatre of being" or "drama in people". They sometimes intervened in Pessoa's social life: during Pessoa's only attested romance, a jealous Campos wrote letters to the girl, who enjoyed the game and wrote back.
</p><p>Pessoa, also an amateur astrologer, created in 1915 the heteronym Raphael Baldaya, a long bearded astrologer. He elaborated horoscopes of his main heteronyms in order to determine their personalities.
</p><p><b>Fernando Pessoa on the heteronyms</b>
</p>
<blockquote><p>How do I write in the name of these three? Caeiro, through sheer and unexpected inspiration, without knowing or even suspecting that I’m going to write in his name. Ricardo Reis, after an abstract meditation, which suddenly takes concrete shape in an ode. Campos, when I feel a sudden impulse to write and don’t know what. (My semi-heteronym Bernardo Soares, who in many ways resembles Álvaro de Campos, always appears when I'm sleepy or drowsy, so that my qualities of inhibition and rational thought are suspended; his prose is an endless reverie. He’s a semi-heteronym because his personality, although not my own, doesn’t differ from my own but is a mere mutilation of it. He’s me without my rationalism and emotions. His prose is the same as mine, except for certain formal restraint that reason imposes on my own writing, and his Portuguese is exactly the same – whereas Caeiro writes bad Portuguese, Campos writes it reasonably well but with mistakes such as "me myself" instead of "I myself", etc.., and Reis writes better than I, but with a purism I find excessive...)</p></blockquote>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd>Fernando Pessoa, Letter to Adolfo Casais Monteiro, 13.01.1935, in <i>The Book of Disquiet</i>, tr. Richard Zenith, Penguin Classics, 2002, p. 474.</dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p><b>George Steiner on the heteronyms</b>
</p>
<blockquote><p>Pseudonymous writing is not rare in literature or philosophy (Kierkegaard provides a celebrated instance). 'Heteronyms', as Pessoa called and defined them, are something different and exceedingly strange. For each of his 'voices', Pessoa conceived a highly distinctive poetic idiom and technique, a complex biography, a context of literary influence and polemics and, most arrestingly of all, subtle interrelations and reciprocities of awareness. Octavio Paz defines Caeiro as 'everything that Pessoa is not and more'.
He is a man magnificently at home in nature, a virtuoso of pre-Christian innocence, almost a Portuguese teacher of Zen. Reis is a stoic Horatian, a pagan believer in fate, a player with classical myths less original than Caeiro, but more representative of modern symbolism. De Campos emerges as a Whitmanesque futurist, a dreamer in drunkenness, the Dionysian singer of what is oceanic and windswept in Lisbon. None of this triad resembles the metaphysical solitude, the sense of being an occultist medium which characterise Pessoa's 'own' intimate verse.</p></blockquote>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd>George Steiner, "A man of many parts", in <i>The Observer</i>, Sunday, 3 June 2001.</dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p><b>Richard Zenith on the heteronyms</b>
</p>
<blockquote><p>Álvaro de Campos, the poet-persona who grew old with Pessoa and held a privileged place in his inventor’s hearts. Soares, the assistant bookkeeper and Campos, the naval engineer never met in the pen-and-paper drama of Pessoa’s heteronyms, who were frequently pitted against one other, but the two writer-characters were spiritual brothers, even if their worldly occupations were at odds. Campos wrote prose, as well as poetry, and much of it reads at it came, so to speak, from the hand of Soares. Pessoa was often unsure who was writing when he wrote, and it’s curious that the very first item among the more than 25,000 pieces that make up his archives in the National Library of Lisbon bears the heading  <i>A. de C. (?) or B. de D.  (or something else).</i></p></blockquote>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd>Richard Zenith, introduction to <i>The Book of Disquiet</i>, Penguin Classics, 2002, p. XI.</dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<h2><span id=".C3.81lvaro_de_Campos"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Álvaro_de_Campos">Álvaro de Campos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heteronym_(literature)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Álvaro de Campos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This heteronym was created by <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Pessoa" title="Fernando Pessoa">Fernando Pessoa</a> as an <i><a href="/wiki/Alter_ego" title="Alter ego">alter ego</a></i> who inherited his role from <b>Alexander Search</b> and this one from <b>Charles Robert Anon</b>. The latter was created when Pessoa lived in <a href="/wiki/Durban" title="Durban">Durban</a>, while Search was created in 1906, when Pessoa was a student at Lisbon's University, in search of his Portuguese cultural identity, after his return from <a href="/wiki/Durban" title="Durban">Durban</a>. 
</p><p>Anon was supposedly English, while Search, although English, was born in Lisbon. After the Portuguese republican revolution, in 1910, and consequent patriotic atmosphere, Pessoa dropped his English heteronyms and <b>Álvaro de Campos</b> was created as a Portuguese <i><a href="/wiki/Alter_ego" title="Alter ego">alter ego</a></i>. Álvaro de Campos, born in 1890, was supposedly a Portuguese naval engineer graduated in <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>. 
</p><p>Campos sailed to the Orient, living experiences that he describes in his poem "Opiarium". He worked in London (1915), Barrow on Furness and Newcastle (1922), but became unemployed, and returned to Lisbon in 1926, the year of the military putsch that installed dictatorship. He also wrote "Lisbon Revisited (1923)" and "Lisbon Revisited (1926)".
</p><p>Campos was a <a href="/wiki/Decadentism" title="Decadentism">decadent</a> poet, but he embraced <a href="/wiki/Futurism" title="Futurism">Futurism</a>; his poetry was strongly influenced by <a href="/wiki/Walt_Whitman" title="Walt Whitman">Walt Whitman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marinetti" class="mw-redirect" title="Marinetti">Marinetti</a>. He wrote the "Ode Triumphal" and "Ode Maritime", published in the literary journal <i><a href="/wiki/Gera%C3%A7%C3%A3o_de_Orpheu" title="Geração de Orpheu">Orpheu</a></i>, in 1915, and other unfinished. 
</p><p>while unemployed in Lisbon, he became depressed, returning to <a href="/wiki/Decadentism" title="Decadentism">Decadentism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pessimism" title="Pessimism">Pessimism</a>. 
Then he wrote his master work, "Tobacco Shop", published in 1933, in the literary journal <i><a href="/wiki/Presen%C3%A7a" title="Presença">Presença</a></i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alberto_Caeiro">Alberto Caeiro</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heteronym_(literature)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Alberto Caeiro">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pessoa created this heteronym as "Master" of the other heteronyms and even Pessoa himself. 
This fictional character was born in 1889 and died in 1915, at 26, the same age of Pessoa's best friend <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1rio_de_S%C3%A1-Carneiro" title="Mário de Sá-Carneiro">Mário de Sá-Carneiro</a>, who killed himself in Paris, in 1916. Thus, Sá-Carneiro seems to have inspired, at least partially, Alberto Caeiro.
</p><p>Caeiro was a humble man of poor education, but a great poet "naif", he was born in Lisbon, but lives almost his life in the countryside, Ribatejo, near Lisbon, where he died. However, his poetry is full of philosophy. He wrote "Poemas Inconjuntos" (Disconnected Poems) and "O Guardador de Rebanhos" (The Keeper of Sheep), published by <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Pessoa" title="Fernando Pessoa">Fernando Pessoa</a> in his "Art Journal" <i>Athena</i> in 1924-25.
</p><p>In a famous letter to the literary critic Adolfo Casais Monteiro, dated January 13, 1935, Pessoa describes his "triumphal day", March 8, 1914, when Caeiro "appeared", making him write down all the poetry of "The Keeper of Sheep" at once. Caeiro influenced the Neopaganism of Pessoa, and of the heteronyms António Mora and Ricardo Reis. Poetically, he influenced mainly the Neoclassicism of Reis, which is connected to Paganism.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ricardo_Reis">Ricardo Reis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heteronym_(literature)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ricardo Reis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carta_astral_ricardo_reis.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Carta_astral_ricardo_reis.jpg/300px-Carta_astral_ricardo_reis.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="226" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f1/Carta_astral_ricardo_reis.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="437" data-file-height="329" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Carta_astral_ricardo_reis.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Astrological chart of the heteronym <i>Ricardo Reis</i> by Fernando Pessoa.</div></div></div>
<p>This heteronym was created by <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Pessoa" title="Fernando Pessoa">Pessoa</a> as a Portuguese doctor born in <a href="/wiki/Oporto" class="mw-redirect" title="Oporto">Oporto</a>, in September 19, 1887. Reis supposedly studied at a boarding school run by <a href="/wiki/Jesuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesuit">Jesuits</a> in which he received a classical education. He was an amateur <a href="/wiki/Latinist" class="mw-redirect" title="Latinist">latinist</a> and poet; politically a monarchist, he went into exile to Brazil after the defeat of a monarchical rebellion against the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="Portuguese Republic">Portuguese Republic</a> in 1919. Ricardo Reis reveals his <a href="/wiki/Epicureanism" title="Epicureanism">Epicureanism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stoicism" title="Stoicism">Stoicism</a> in the "Odes by Ricardo Reis", published by <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Pessoa" title="Fernando Pessoa">Pessoa</a> in 1924, in his literary journal <i>Athena</i>.
</p><p>Since <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Pessoa" title="Fernando Pessoa">Pessoa</a> didn't determine the death of Reis, one can assume that he survived his author who died in 1935. In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Year_of_the_Death_of_Ricardo_Reis" title="The Year of the Death of Ricardo Reis">The Year of the Death of Ricardo Reis</a></i> (1984), Portuguese <a href="/wiki/Nobel_prize" class="mw-redirect" title="Nobel prize">nobel prize</a> <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Saramago" title="José Saramago">José Saramago</a> rebuilds, in his own personal outlook, the literary world of this heteronym after 1935, creating a dialog between Ricardo Reis and the ghost of his author.
</p>

In his neoclassical odes he ponders on the briefness of life, the inevitability of death and the helplessness of  the human condition. This “sad epicure” tries to find some contentment through the acceptance of fate and self-discipline lives by Horatio’s a motto: carpe diem (seize the day).

He feels a foreigner wherever he is. Suffocated by a tedious and monotonous existence, failing to see the meaning of life, he looks for new sensations and travels to the Far East. When he comes back he is a renewed man. He embraces the futuristic movement and worships industrialisation and scientific and technological progress, with its hectic relentless rhythm and continuous change. There is an ecstasy of the senses, his relationship with machines and charcoal and steel is almost erotic. But in the background of this modern world lurk the shadows of the assembly line, the pollution, the emptiness of material things. The darkness of what is to come —eventually Campos tires. In his third phase, profoundly disenchanted with the present, he evokes the long-gone days of his perfectly happy childhood.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_writers_and_their_heteronyms">Other writers and their heteronyms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heteronym_(literature)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other writers and their heteronyms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/S%C3%B8ren_Kierkegaard" title="Søren Kierkegaard">Søren Kierkegaard</a> - Had more than a dozen heteronyms with distinct biographies and personalities.</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bitty_Navarro&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bitty Navarro (page does not exist)">Bitty Navarro</a> - Mexican-Spanish poet, asexual activist, and <a href="/wiki/Buzzfeed" class="mw-redirect" title="Buzzfeed">Buzzfeed</a> contributor, has explored heteronyms in poetry and microblogging.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/David_Solway" title="David Solway">David Solway</a>, Canadian writer, writing as <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Karavis" title="Andreas Karavis">Andreas Karavis</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kent_Johnson" title="Kent Johnson">Kent Johnson</a>, American scholar and poet writing as <a href="/wiki/Araki_Yasusada" title="Araki Yasusada">Araki Yasusada</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Robin_Skelton" title="Robin Skelton">Robin Skelton</a>, Canadian poet and occultist, writing as French <a href="/wiki/Surrealist" class="mw-redirect" title="Surrealist">surrealist</a> Georges Zuk.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Hill" title="Geoffrey Hill">Geoffrey Hill</a>. English poet, translated <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Songbook_Of_Sebastian_Arrurruz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Songbook Of Sebastian Arrurruz (page does not exist)">The Songbook Of Sebastian Arrurruz</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/B.H._Fairchild" class="mw-redirect" title="B.H. Fairchild">B.H. Fairchild</a>, American poet, writing as Roy Eldridge Garcia.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Daniel_Handler" title="Daniel Handler">Daniel Handler</a>, American writer, writing as <a href="/wiki/Lemony_Snicket" title="Lemony Snicket">Lemony Snicket</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Romain_Gary" title="Romain Gary">Romain Gary</a>, French writer, writing as Émile Ajar, Fosco Sinibaldi, Shatan Bogat.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Antoine_Volodine" title="Antoine Volodine">Antoine Volodine</a>, French writer, writing as Elli Kronauer, Manuela Draeger, Lutz Bassman.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Brian_O%27Nolan" title="Brian O&#39;Nolan">Brian O'Nolan</a> writing as Flann O'Brien and Myles na gCopaleen.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Arnon_Grunberg" title="Arnon Grunberg">Arnon Grunberg</a> writing as Marek van der Jagt.</li>
<li><a href="https://hy.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D4%B5%D5%B2%D5%AB%D5%A1_%D5%8F%D5%A5%D5%B4%D5%AB%D6%80%D5%B3%D5%AB%D5%BA%D5%A1%D5%B7%D5%B5%D5%A1%D5%B6" class="extiw" title="hy:Եղիա Տեմիրճիպաշյան">Eghia Temirchipashyan</a>, Armenian writer, writing as Graser Atom, Devanio, Melania, Nurania, Graser's Cat ...</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Raymond_A._Palmer" title="Raymond A. Palmer">Raymond A. Palmer</a> (1910-1977), American editor and writer; published stories under his own name and multiple pseudonyms, several of which were attributed different personalities and biographies (e.g., "A.E. Steber" was a Russian military officer who engaged in espionage against Nazi Germany before relocating to the US). <sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Internet personality MsScribe - Had a similar number and variety of heteronyms to Kierkegaard, used to manipulate the <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_fandom" title="Harry Potter fandom">Harry Potter fandom</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Laura_Albert" title="Laura Albert">Laura Albert</a> (born 1955) wrote under the name <a href="/wiki/JT_LeRoy" title="JT LeRoy">JT LeRoy</a>, an adolescent male persona she described as an "avatar" that allowed her to write things that she was unable to write as herself.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jane_Holland" title="Jane Holland">Jane Holland</a>, poet and novellist, also writes as Victoria Lamb, Elizabeth Moss, Beth Good and Hannah Coates.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
