<p>The <b>City of Wakefield</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_England" title="Local government in England">local government district</a> in West Yorkshire, England, with the status of a <a href="/wiki/City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="City status in the United Kingdom">city</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan borough</a>. <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a> is the district's <a href="/wiki/Administrative_centre" title="Administrative centre">administrative centre</a>.  The population of the City of Wakefield at the 2011 Census was 325,837.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The district includes the ‘Five Towns’ of <a href="/wiki/Normanton,_West_Yorkshire" title="Normanton, West Yorkshire">Normanton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pontefract" title="Pontefract">Pontefract</a>, <a href="/wiki/Featherstone" title="Featherstone">Featherstone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Castleford" title="Castleford">Castleford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Knottingley" title="Knottingley">Knottingley</a>. Other towns include <a href="/wiki/Ossett" title="Ossett">Ossett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemsworth" title="Hemsworth">Hemsworth</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Kirkby_and_Moorthorpe" title="South Kirkby and Moorthorpe">South Kirkby and Moorthorpe</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Elmsall" title="South Elmsall">South Elmsall</a>. The City and borough are governed by <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Metropolitan_District_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Wakefield Metropolitan District Council">Wakefield Metropolitan District Council</a>. Wakefield lies between <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barnsley" title="Barnsley">Barnsley</a> <sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2010, Wakefield was named as the UK's third most musical city by <a href="/wiki/PRS_for_Music" title="PRS for Music">PRS for Music</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mirror.co.uk_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mirror.co.uk-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Wakefield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In recent years,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (May 2019)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the economic and physical condition of several of the former mining towns and villages in Wakefield District have started to improve due to the booming economy of <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> – and an increase in numbers of commuters to the city from the sub-region – and a recognition of undeveloped assets. For instance <a href="/wiki/Castleford" title="Castleford">Castleford</a>, to the North East of Wakefield is seeing extensive development and investment because of the natural asset of its outlook on to the <a href="/wiki/River_Aire" title="River Aire">River Aire</a>, its easy access to the national motorway network and the availability of former mining land for house-building. In Ossett, house prices have risen from an average of £50,000 in 1998 to £130,000 in 2003.
</p><p>Although unemployment was amongst the highest in the country for most of the 1980s, and 1990s, Wakefield District now has below-average unemployment. The "Wakefield East" ward had 4.7% unemployment in May 2005 (source: <a href="/wiki/Office_for_National_Statistics" title="Office for National Statistics">Office for National Statistics</a>)—which was more than 1% higher than any other ward.  The eastern half of the district remains considerably less prosperous than the western half, with several deprived wards
</p><p>The district is mainly made out of old <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">coal-mining</a> towns, although other industries include <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chemicals" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemicals">chemicals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Machine_tools" class="mw-redirect" title="Machine tools">machine tools</a>, glass and other forms of manufacturing.  <a href="/wiki/Horbury" title="Horbury">Horbury</a> is something of an anomaly in having had an <a href="/wiki/Iron_works" class="mw-redirect" title="Iron works">iron works</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> When <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> came to power in 1979 there were 21 pits in the district. By the time the <a href="/wiki/UK_miners%27_strike_(1984%E2%80%931985)" class="mw-redirect" title="UK miners&#39; strike (1984–1985)">1984 Strike</a> began this had decreased to 15, however it still had more collieries than any other district in the country. At the time of <a href="/wiki/Privatisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Privatisation">privatisation</a> in November 1994, only two remained: the <i>Prince of Wales</i> at Pontefract, which closed in 2002, and <i>Kellingley</i> at Knottingley which closed in 2015 ending the industry that once dominated the district.  Most of the district's pits had been very hardline during the 1984 strike.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Wakefield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The former Borough of Wakefield was raised to <a href="/wiki/City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="City status in the United Kingdom">city status</a> by <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">letters patent</a> in 1888.  It became a <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county borough</a> in 1913, taking it out of the jurisdiction of the West Riding County Council. The present boundaries were set in 1974 by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a>, when the <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county borough</a> of Wakefield merged with the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding</a> <a href="/wiki/Municipal_borough" title="Municipal borough">municipal boroughs</a> of <a href="/wiki/Castleford" title="Castleford">Castleford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ossett" title="Ossett">Ossett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pontefract" title="Pontefract">Pontefract</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Urban_district_(Great_Britain_and_Ireland)" title="Urban district (Great Britain and Ireland)">urban districts</a> of <a href="/wiki/Featherstone" title="Featherstone">Featherstone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hemsworth" title="Hemsworth">Hemsworth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horbury" title="Horbury">Horbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Knottingley" title="Knottingley">Knottingley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Normanton,_West_Yorkshire" title="Normanton, West Yorkshire">Normanton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stanley,_West_Yorkshire" title="Stanley, West Yorkshire">Stanley</a>, along with Wakefield Rural District and parts of <a href="/wiki/Hemsworth_Rural_District" title="Hemsworth Rural District">Hemsworth Rural District</a> and <a href="/wiki/Osgoldcross_Rural_District" title="Osgoldcross Rural District">Osgoldcross Rural District</a>. The new <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_district" class="mw-redirect" title="Metropolitan district">metropolitan district</a>'s city status was reconfirmed by letters patent in 1974. The Council's headquarters is <a href="/wiki/County_Hall,_Wakefield" title="County Hall, Wakefield">County Hall</a>, originally built for the West Riding County Council and acquired by Wakefield in 1989.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Wakefield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_belt">Green belt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Wakefield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Green belt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/South_and_West_Yorkshire_Green_Belt" title="South and West Yorkshire Green Belt">South and West Yorkshire Green Belt</a></div>
<p>The district is within a <a href="/wiki/Green_belt_(United_Kingdom)" title="Green belt (United Kingdom)">green belt</a> region that extends into the wider surrounding counties, and is in place to reduce <a href="/wiki/Urban_sprawl" title="Urban sprawl">urban sprawl</a>, prevent the cities and towns in the <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire_Urban_Area" title="West Yorkshire Urban Area">West Yorkshire Urban Area</a> conurbation from further convergence, protect the identity of outlying communities, encourage <a href="/wiki/Brownfield" class="mw-redirect" title="Brownfield">brownfield</a> reuse, and preserve nearby countryside. This is achieved by restricting inappropriate development within the designated areas, and imposing stricter conditions on permitted building.<sup id="cite_ref-belt1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-belt1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The green belt surrounds the Wakefield built up area, and stretches into the wider borough, with larger outlying towns and villages such as Hollingthorpe, <a href="/wiki/Walton,_Wakefield" title="Walton, Wakefield">Walton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherton,_Wakefield" title="Netherton, Wakefield">Netherton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Castleford,_West_Yorkshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Castleford, West Yorkshire">Castleford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Knottingley" title="Knottingley">Knottingley</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pontefract" title="Pontefract">Pontefract</a> exempted from it. However, smaller villages, hamlets and rural areas such as <a href="/wiki/Warmfield_cum_Heath" title="Warmfield cum Heath">Warmfield and Heath</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Ferry" class="mw-redirect" title="Stanley Ferry">Stanley Ferry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newmillerdam" title="Newmillerdam">Newmillerdam</a>, Snydale, <a href="/wiki/Wintersett" title="Wintersett">Wintersett</a>, and Chapelthorpe are 'washed over' by the designation. The green belt was first adopted in 1987,<sup id="cite_ref-belt1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-belt1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and the size in the borough in 2017 amounted to some 23,500 hectares (235&#160;km<sup>2</sup>; 91&#160;sq&#160;mi).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A subsidiary aim of the green belt is to encourage recreation and leisure interests,<sup id="cite_ref-belt1_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-belt1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> with rural landscape features, greenfield areas and facilities including the <a href="/wiki/River_Calder,_West_Yorkshire" title="River Calder, West Yorkshire">River Calder</a> and valleys, the <a href="/wiki/Aire_and_Calder_navigation" class="mw-redirect" title="Aire and Calder navigation">Aire and Calder navigation</a> canal, <a href="/wiki/Barnsley_Canal" title="Barnsley Canal">Barnsley Canal</a>, Walton nature reserve, Brandy Carr hill, Pugney's country park and lakes, <a href="/wiki/Sandal_Castle" title="Sandal Castle">Sandal Castle</a>, Crigglestone Cemetery War Memorial, Crigglestone rugby club, Altofts and Stanley sports centres, several golf courses, Church of St Peter the Apostle at Kirkthorpe, Fitzwilliam Country Park, and the Stanley Ferry marina.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Wakefield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Metropolitan_District_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Wakefield Metropolitan District Council">Wakefield Metropolitan District Council</a></div>
<p>The district is divided into 21 <a href="/wiki/Ward_(politics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ward (politics)">wards</a>, with each ward represented on the district's <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Metropolitan_District_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Wakefield Metropolitan District Council">Wakefield Metropolitan District Council</a> by three councillors. Each councillor is elected on a <a href="/wiki/Plurality_voting_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Plurality voting system">first past the post</a> basis, normally for a four-year period which is staggered annually with the other councillors of that ward so that only one councillor per ward is up for election at any one time. Exceptions to this include by-elections and ward boundary changes.
</p>

<p>The city was the safest Labour council in England in 2003, but there was a short-lived swing against Labour in recent years.  After the <a href="/wiki/2008_Wakefield_Council_election" class="mw-redirect" title="2008 Wakefield Council election">2008 election</a> results the Labour Party had a majority of just one. However the death of Labour councillor Graham Phelps meant that the authority was for a time in <a href="/wiki/No_overall_control" title="No overall control">no overall control</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Labour did however, hold the seat in the by-election in January 2010 restoring their majority.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the May 2010 local elections Labour held all of their seats and made a net gain of one seat from the Independents increasing Labour's majority on the Council to three. Following the defection of an Independent to Labour, Labour's majority was increased to 5.<sup id="cite_ref-wakefield1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wakefield1-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In January 2011 a Conservative Councillor defected to become an independent Councillor, leaving the Tories with 23 seats.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2011 Labour increased their majority on the council to 15 making 5 gains, taking 3 seats from the Conservatives (Horbury and South Ossett, Pontefract South and Wrenthorpe and Outwood West), and 2 from the Independents (Featherstone and South Elmsall and South Kirkby). The Conservatives gained Ossett from the Liberal Democrats, which means there are no Liberal Democrat councillors in Wakefield.
</p><p>The current Council make up is Labour 54, Conservatives 6, UKIP 2 and 1 Independent following the 2014 local elections held in May 2014.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Wakefield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The city district is home to three professional <a href="/wiki/Rugby_league" title="Rugby league">rugby league</a> clubs, the <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Trinity" title="Wakefield Trinity">Wakefield Trinity</a>, <a href="/wiki/Castleford_Tigers" title="Castleford Tigers">Castleford Tigers</a> who both play in the <a href="/wiki/Super_League" title="Super League">Super League</a> and <a href="/wiki/Featherstone_Rovers" title="Featherstone Rovers">Featherstone Rovers</a> who play in the <a href="/wiki/Kingstone_Press_Championship" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingstone Press Championship">Kingstone Press Championship</a>. All three have had periods of success. The city also has several amateur <a href="/wiki/Rugby_league" title="Rugby league">rugby league</a> clubs including <a href="/wiki/Featherstone_Lions" title="Featherstone Lions">Featherstone Lions</a> and <a href="/wiki/Normanton_(rugby_league)" title="Normanton (rugby league)">Normanton Knights</a>. Current England rugby league internationals from the area include; <a href="/wiki/Tom_Briscoe" title="Tom Briscoe">Tom Briscoe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rob_Burrow" title="Rob Burrow">Rob Burrow</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zak_Hardaker" title="Zak Hardaker">Zak Hardaker</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Brett_Ferres" title="Brett Ferres">Brett Ferres</a>. Wakefield is also home to <a href="/wiki/Frickley_Athletic_F.C." title="Frickley Athletic F.C.">Frickley Athletic</a> association football club who play in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Premier_League" title="Northern Premier League">Northern Premier League</a> Premier Division, the highest level football club in the Wakefield region, based in South Elmsall.
</p><p>The district has a strong heritage of cricket with former Yorkshire and England captain <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Boycott" title="Geoffrey Boycott">Geoffrey Boycott</a> born in <a href="/wiki/Fitzwilliam,_West_Yorkshire" title="Fitzwilliam, West Yorkshire">Fitzwilliam</a> and current Yorkshire and England cricketer <a href="/wiki/Tim_Bresnan" title="Tim Bresnan">Tim Bresnan</a> from <a href="/wiki/Pontefract" title="Pontefract">Pontefract</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_aspects">Social aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=City_of_Wakefield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Social aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:County_Hall_(1898),_Wood_Street,_Wakefield.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/County_Hall_%281898%29%2C_Wood_Street%2C_Wakefield.jpg/220px-County_Hall_%281898%29%2C_Wood_Street%2C_Wakefield.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="294" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/County_Hall_%281898%29%2C_Wood_Street%2C_Wakefield.jpg/330px-County_Hall_%281898%29%2C_Wood_Street%2C_Wakefield.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/County_Hall_%281898%29%2C_Wood_Street%2C_Wakefield.jpg/440px-County_Hall_%281898%29%2C_Wood_Street%2C_Wakefield.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1712" data-file-height="2288" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:County_Hall_(1898),_Wood_Street,_Wakefield.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The City of Wakefield MDC's <a href="/wiki/Queen_Anne_style_architecture" title="Queen Anne style architecture">Queen Anne style</a> administrative HQ, County Hall (1898), <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a></div></div></div>
<p>A decision was made, in 2004, to transfer the district's extensive <a href="/wiki/Council_house" title="Council house">council housing</a> to Wakefield and District Housing (WDH), an 'independent' housing association, who would be more efficient with repairs and maintaining decent accommodation; as council housing represented almost 30% of the district, this was the second-largest stock transfer in British history.  WDH are investing over £700&#160;million to regenerate the District and working with partners, such as WMDC, are investing in new housing within the District.
</p>
