<p><b>Longwood</b> is a village and suburb of the town of <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a> in the <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> county of <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>. It is situated some 2.5 miles (4.0&#160;km) west of Huddersfield town centre, in the valley of the Longwood Brook, a tributary of the <a href="/wiki/River_Colne,_West_Yorkshire" title="River Colne, West Yorkshire">River Colne</a>.
</p><p>The village is the home of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Longwood_Sing&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Longwood Sing (page does not exist)">Longwood Sing</a>, an annual event, where <a href="/wiki/Choirs" class="mw-redirect" title="Choirs">choirs</a> from around the area gather to sing <a href="/wiki/Hymns" class="mw-redirect" title="Hymns">hymns</a> and songs. Near the amphitheatre, where the sing takes place, the <a href="/wiki/Folly" title="Folly">folly</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nab_End_Tower&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nab End Tower (page does not exist)">Nab End Tower</a> can be found. The tower was built in 1861 by unemployed local woollen <a href="/wiki/Mill_(factory)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mill (factory)">mill</a> workers. In 2008 it was restored by <a href="/wiki/Kirklees" title="Kirklees">Kirklees Metropolitan Borough Council</a> at a cost of <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">£</a>200,000.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are also three reservoirs that supply Huddersfield. They were built around 1840, although strengthening work in the 1930s makes them appear newer. One now covered and the lower reservoir looking more like a natural lake that is called a compensation reservoir - 'Compensation' as it was set up to compensate the mill owner for water lost from natural sources that now went to the main reservoirs to be distributed by the water corporation under 'rates' systems - although the mill owner was most likely the owner of the main reservoir too.
</p><p>During the 1840s, Longwood was the home to the Hanson family of Ballroyd. Mary Hanson set up a carrying business to supplement her husband's income from farming and soon the Hanson wagons were seen taking the Great North Road to London. <a href="/wiki/James_Hanson,_Baron_Hanson" title="James Hanson, Baron Hanson">James Hanson</a>, founder and former chairman of <a href="/wiki/Hanson_plc" class="mw-redirect" title="Hanson plc">Hanson plc</a>, is a direct descendant of the Ballroyd family. When he was made a <a href="/wiki/Life_peer" title="Life peer">life peer</a>, he took the title <i>Baron Hanson of Edgerton</i> after the nearby suburb of <a href="/wiki/Edgerton,_Huddersfield" title="Edgerton, Huddersfield">Edgerton</a>.
</p><p>Longwood was built up around the woollen industry and its mills, most of which have now been demolished and replaced by housing. As a result of this the village has recently seen an influx of new inhabitants.
</p><p>In 2005, a campaign was mounted to save the Longwood <a href="/wiki/Allotment_(gardening)" title="Allotment (gardening)">allotment plots</a> (situated behind the <a href="/wiki/Mechanics%27_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Mechanics&#39; Institute">Mechanics' Institute</a>) from being developed into housing.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Longwood Church is dedicated to <a href="/wiki/St._Mark" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Mark">St. Mark</a>, and is in the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_parish" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecclesiastical parish">parish</a> of Huddersfield, being a <a href="/wiki/Perpetual_curacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Perpetual curacy">perpetual curacy</a>. Longwood is also home to the <a href="/wiki/The_Scout_Association" title="The Scout Association">27th Longwood Scout Group</a>.
</p><p>The name Longwood derives from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>Lang wudu</i>, meaning "The Long Wood".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
