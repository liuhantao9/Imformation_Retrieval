<p>A <b>unisex name</b> (also known as an <b>epicene name</b>, a <b>gender-neutral name</b> or an <b>androgynous name</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> that can be used by a person regardless of their sex. Unisex names are common in the English speaking world, especially in the United States. By contrast, some countries have <a href="/wiki/Naming_laws" class="mw-redirect" title="Naming laws">laws</a> preventing unisex names, requiring parents to give their children sex-specific names.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In other countries, unisex names are sometimes avoided for social reasons such as potential <a href="/wiki/Discrimination" title="Discrimination">discrimination</a>, ridicule, and <a href="/wiki/Psychological_abuse" title="Psychological abuse">psychological abuse</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Names may have different gender connotations from country to country or language to language. For example, the Italian <a href="/wiki/Male_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Male name">male name</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Andrea" title="Andrea">Andrea</a></i> (derived from Greek <i>Andreas</i>) is understood as a <a href="/wiki/Female_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Female name">female name</a> in many languages, such as English, German, Hungarian, Czech, and Spanish.
</p><p>Parents may name their child in honor of a person of another sex, which – if done widely – can result in the name becoming unisex. For example, Christians, particularly <a href="/wiki/Catholics" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholics">Catholics</a>, may give a child a second/middle name of the opposite sex, e.g. name a son Marie or Maria in honor of the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Mary" class="mw-redirect" title="Virgin Mary">Virgin Mary</a> or formerly <a href="/wiki/Anne" title="Anne">Anne</a> for <a href="/wiki/Saint_Anne" title="Saint Anne">Saint Anne</a>; or name a daughter José in honor of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Joseph" title="Saint Joseph">Saint Joseph</a> or Jean in honor of <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a>. This practice is rare in English-speaking countries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Some masculine and feminine names are <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">homophones</a>, pronounced the same for both sexes but spelled differently. For example, <a href="/wiki/Yves_(given_name)" title="Yves (given name)">Yves</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eve_(name)" title="Eve (name)">Eve</a> and (for some speakers) <a href="/wiki/Artemus_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Artemus (disambiguation)">Artemus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Artemis_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Artemis (disambiguation)">Artemis</a>. These names are not strictly unisex names.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unisex_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Unisex names can be used as a source of humor, such as <a href="/wiki/Julia_Sweeney" title="Julia Sweeney">Julia Sweeney</a>'s sexually ambiguous character "<a href="/wiki/Pat_(Saturday_Night_Live)" title="Pat (Saturday Night Live)">Pat</a>" on <i><a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Live" title="Saturday Night Live">Saturday Night Live</a></i>. A running joke on the TV show <i><a href="/wiki/Scrubs_(TV_series)" title="Scrubs (TV series)">Scrubs</a></i> is that almost every woman J.D. sleeps with has a unisex name: Jordan, Alex, Danni, Elliot, Jamie, Kim, etc. Similarly, the sex of the baby Jamie in <i><a href="/wiki/Malcolm_in_the_Middle" title="Malcolm in the Middle">Malcolm in the Middle</a></i> was purposely kept ambiguous when first introduced at the end of the show's fourth season to build suspense. In <i><a href="/wiki/Gilmore_Girls" title="Gilmore Girls">Gilmore Girls</a></i>, Rory is bothered by the discovery that her boyfriend Logan's workmate Bobby, is female. Rory had assumed Bobby was male and it is only upon their first meeting that Rory discovers Bobby's gender.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The name "Rory" was historically a male name until <i>Gilmore Girls</i> reached popularity, at which point the name reached rough gender parity.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Japanese <a href="/wiki/Dramas" class="mw-redirect" title="Dramas">dramas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manga" title="Manga">manga</a>, a unisex name may be given to an <a href="/wiki/Androgynous" class="mw-redirect" title="Androgynous">androgynous</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gender-bending" class="mw-redirect" title="Gender-bending">gender-bending</a> character as part of a plot twist to aid in presenting the character as one sex when they are actually another.
</p><p>In mystery fiction, unisex names have been used to tease readers into trying to solve the mystery of a character's sex. The novels of <a href="/wiki/Sarah_Caudwell" title="Sarah Caudwell">Sarah Caudwell</a> feature a narrator named Hilary Tamar, a law professor who is never identified as either male or female.
</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwean_names" title="Zimbabwean names">Zimbabwean names</a></div>
<p>Shona, a Bantu group in Zimbabwe, have unisex names<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> which may indicate the circumstances of the baby or the family during the time of the birth. All Shona names have a meaning, some also celebrate virtue or worship God. Popular unisex names in the Shona ethnic group<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> are:

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_languages">Indian languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unisex_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Indian languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Indian_family_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian family names">Indian family names</a></div>
<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_India" title="Languages of India">Indian</a> names become unisex when written with Latin characters because of the limitations of <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">transliteration</a>. The spellings <i>Chandra</i> and <i>Krishna</i>, for example, are transliterations of both the masculine and feminine versions of those names. In Indian languages<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Cleanup" title="Wikipedia:Cleanup"><span title="Wikipedia:Cleanup">in which alphabets?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, the final <i>a</i> in each of these names are different letters with different pronunciations, so there is no ambiguity. However, when they are seen (and usually, spoken) by someone unfamiliar with Indian languages, they become sexually ambiguous. Other Indian names, such as <i>Ananda</i>, are exclusively or nearly exclusively masculine in India, but because of their <i>a</i> ending, are assumed to be feminine in Anglophone societies. 
</p><p>Many unisex names in India are obvious and ridiculed. For instance <a href="/wiki/Nehal" title="Nehal">Nehal</a>, Sonal, Snehal, Niral, Pranjal and Anmol are used commonly to name baby boys or girls in western states of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">Gujarat</a>. Similarly, names like Kajal, Sujal, Viral, Harshal, Deepal, Bobby, Mrinal, Jyoti, Shakti, <a href="/wiki/Neelam_(given_name)" title="Neelam (given name)">Nilam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kiran_(given_name)" title="Kiran (given name)">Kiran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lucky_(name)" title="Lucky (name)">Lucky</a>, Ashwini, Shashi, Malhar, Umang, Shubham and Anupam are also very common sex-neutral names or unisex names in India.  Most <a href="/wiki/Punjabi_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Punjabi people">Punjabi</a> <a href="/wiki/Sikh" class="mw-redirect" title="Sikh">Sikh</a> first names such as "Sandeep, Gurdeep, Kuldeep, Mandeep", "Surjeet, Gurjeet, Kuljeet, Harjeet, Manjeet", "Harpreet, Gurpreet, Jaspreet, Kulpreet, Manpreet", "Prabhjot, Harjot, Gurjot, Jasjot" and "Sukhjinder, Bhupinder, Jasbinder, Parminder, Kulvinder, Harjinder, Ranjodh, Sheeraz, Hardeep, Kirandeep, Sukhdeep, Govindpal, Encarl, Rajan" are unisex names and equally commonly given to either sex.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Also, names derived from Dari Persian and Arabic, but not used among native speakers of those languages, are common among South Asian Muslims. Since Persian does not assign genders to inanimate nouns, some of these names are gender-neutral, for example Roshan, Parveen, and Insaaf.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkish">Turkish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unisex_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Turkish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Czech">Czech</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unisex_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Czech">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Czech Registry Act forbids to give male names or surnames to females or female names or surnames to males, but doesn't restrict neutral names and surnames. For the period of <a href="/wiki/Transitioning_(transgender)" title="Transitioning (transgender)">transitioning</a>, the act explicitly enables to use gender-neutral given name and surname.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>A Czech trans people web enumerates given names which are most popular as gender-neutral names. Most of them are originally <a href="/wiki/Hypocorism" title="Hypocorism">hypocorisms</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanwords</a>. The most natural of them are names ending with -a, falling under <a href="/wiki/Czech_declension#Masculine_animate" title="Czech declension">male</a> <a href="/wiki/Inflectional_paradigm" class="mw-redirect" title="Inflectional paradigm">inflectional paradigm</a> "předseda" and/or <a href="/wiki/Czech_declension#Feminine" title="Czech declension">female paradigm</a> "žena". Generally, <a href="/wiki/Hypocorism" title="Hypocorism">hypocorisms</a> are not allowed to be used in  official registers, but in case of trans people, they are tolerated. 
</p><p>The most popular neutral names are <a href="/wiki/Sa%C5%A1a" title="Saša">Saša</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nikola" title="Nikola">Nikola</a>, both of them having a bit Russian (or East-Slavic) connotation. Other names of that paradigm are Áda, Jarka, Jára, Jindra, Jirča, Jirka, Kája, Mára, Míla, Mira, Míša, Míťa, Nikola, Péťa, Saša, Stáňa, Sváťa, Štěpa, Vlasta, Zbyňa, Zdena. They are unisex <a href="/wiki/Hypocorism" title="Hypocorism">hypocorisms</a> of complementary male and female names, e.g. Péťa belong to male "Petr" as well as female "Petra". Ilja, Issa, Bronia or Andrea are felt as original foreign names. Maria is felt as a female name (this form is reserved for mother of Jesus, the common Czech female form is Marie, the male form is Marián), but some historical men are known under that name (<a href="/wiki/Clement_Mary_Hofbauer" title="Clement Mary Hofbauer">Klement Maria Hofbauer</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Vianney" title="John Vianney">Jan Maria Vianney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rainer_Maria_Rilke" title="Rainer Maria Rilke">Rainer Maria Rilke</a>).   
</p><p>Other mentioned names are mostly felt as foreign names
</p>
<ul><li>Alex, Aliz, Janis, Jannis (really, this form is felt rather as male names)</li>
<li>Dan, Jean, Kim, Robin, Vivian (really, this form is felt rather as male names)</li>
<li>Andy, Deny, Lenny, Romy, Viky, Marti, Riki, Niki, Robbie</li>
<li>Ivo (really, it's felt as male name)</li>
<li>René (really, this form is felt rather as male name)</li>
<li>Martine, Michele.</li>
<li>Nikol</li></ul>
<p>The page indicates some other names which can be usable as unisex but are not tried by that community yet.
</p>

<p>In the United States, most of the male names are now largely female, while in Britain, some (notably Charlie, Hilary, Sidney, and Robin) remain largely male. Sometimes different spellings have different sex distributions (Francis is less likely female than Frances), but these are rarely definitive.<sup id="cite_ref-nigs_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nigs-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> For example, in the US, as of 2016, both Skylar and Skyler are more common for females, but Skylar is most strongly associated with females (the 42nd most common name for females and the 761 most common for males born in 2016)<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> than Skyler (the 359 most common name for females and the 414 most common for males born in 2016).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Social_Security_Administration" title="Social Security Administration">Social Security Administration</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jayden" title="Jayden">Jayden</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> has been the most popular unisex name for boys since 2008 and <a href="/wiki/Madison_(name)" title="Madison (name)">Madison</a><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> has been the most popular unisex name for girls since 2000 in the United States.
Prior to Jayden, Logan<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> was the most popular unisex name for boys and prior to Madison, Alexis<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> was the most popular unisex name for girls.

<p>Many of these names are rare, foreign or neologism, established names tend to be strongly sex-specific. Notably, a class of names that are derived from nature can be often used for either sex, for example: <b>Aalto</b> (wave), <b>Halla</b> (frost), <b>Lumi</b> (snow), <b>Paju</b> (willow), <b>Ruska</b> (fall colors), and <b>Valo</b> (light). Similarly, there are some (sometimes archaic) adjectives which carry no strong gender connotations, like <b>Kaino</b> (timid), <b>Vieno</b> (calm) or <b>Lahja</b> (a gift). Certain names can have unisex diminutives, such as Alex, which can be short for Aleksandra or Aleksanteri (or variants thereof).
</p>

<p>There are also pairs of masculine and feminine names that have slightly different spelling but identical pronunciation, such as André / Andrée, Frédéric / Frédérique and Gabriel / Gabrielle.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and French-speaking countries, it can happen for people to have a combination of both masculine and feminine given names, but most of these include "Marie", such as Jean-Marie, Marie-Jean, Marie-Pierre.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Marie_(given_name)" title="Marie (given name)">Marie</a> was a unisex name in medieval times; it is nowadays only female except for its presence in compound names. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Icelandic">Icelandic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unisex_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Icelandic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_name" title="Icelandic name">Icelandic name</a></div>
<p>In June, 2019, Iceland's Parliament, the <a href="/wiki/Althing" title="Althing">Althing</a>, passed a new gender autonomy act which will recognize all approved Icelandic names as unisex.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-icelandreview_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-icelandreview-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Previously, unisex names were in general illegal in Iceland. The <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_Naming_Committee" title="Icelandic Naming Committee">Icelandic Naming Committee</a> (Icelandic: <i>Mannanafnanefnd</i>) maintained preapproved lists of male and female names, with names not on the list - or on a different gender's list - typically denied. Earlier court cases had carved out exceptions, such as the names <i>Blær</i> (approved for women after a 2013 court case),<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-icelandreview_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-icelandreview-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> <i>Auður</i> (approved for men later in 2013),<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and <i>Alex</i> (denied for women in 2013 but approved in 2018).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-icelandreview_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-icelandreview-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Additionally, the new gender autonomy act makes changes to the traditional patronymics/matronymics used as <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_name" title="Icelandic name">Icelandic surnames</a>. Before the bill, <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_name" title="Icelandic name">Icelandic last names</a> (by law) could not be unisex: the suffix <i>-dóttir</i> ("daughter") was attached to a parent's name for women and the suffix <i>-son</i> ("son") was used for men. The new law will allow adults who have officially changed their gender marker to "X", a <a href="/wiki/Non-binary_gender" title="Non-binary gender">non-binary gender</a> marker, to also change their patronymic/matronymic suffix to <i>-bur</i> ("child").<sup id="cite_ref-icelandreview_27-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-icelandreview-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Newborns cannot be assigned a non-binary gender marker at this time, and will continue to receive a patronymic/matronymic suffix in keeping with their assigned sex at birth.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Italian">Italian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unisex_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Italian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Italian_name" title="Italian name">Italian name</a></div>
<p>In Italy, unisex names are very rare. There are basically male names like i.e. <a href="/wiki/Andrea" title="Andrea">Andrea</a> (which is female i.e. in English, Spanish, German or French), Elia or Mattia that can also be given to females. Names like Celeste, Amabile, <a href="/wiki/Fiore_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Fiore (disambiguation)">Fiore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Loreto_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Loreto (disambiguation)">Loreto</a>, or Diamante are, as opposite, female names that occasionally can be given to males.
</p><p>Sometimes "Maria" is used as a middle male name (such as Antonio Maria).
</p><p>"Rosario" (feminine: "Rosaria") is a male name in Italian whereas in Spanish is female.
</p><p>There are also unisex nicknames, i.e. Giusi or Giusy that can stand either for Giuseppe or Giuseppina, respectively "Joseph" and "Josephine"; Dani or Dany which stand for Daniele (male) or Daniela (female); Ale that can stand for Alessandro (male) as well as for Alessandra (female); Fede that can stand either for Federico or Federica.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazilian">Brazilian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unisex_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Brazilian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Names that end with an <i>i</i> are considered unisex in Brazil. They tend to be Native Brazilian <a href="/wiki/Tupi_people" title="Tupi people">Indian</a> names in origin, such as Araci, Jaci, Darci, Ubirani, but names from other cultures are now being absorbed, such as Remy, Wendy, and Eddy. Names that end with <i>ir</i> and <i>mar</i> tend to be unisex also, such as Nadir, Aldenir, Dagmar and Niomar – though in these cases there are some exceptions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russian">Russian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unisex_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Russian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Slavic_naming_customs" title="Eastern Slavic naming customs">Eastern Slavic naming customs</a></div>
<p>Diminutive forms of names in Russian language can be unisex, such as Sasha/Shura (Alexandr or Alexandra), Zhenya (Yevgeniy or Yevgeniya), Valya (Valentin or Valentina), Valera (Valeriy or Valeriya), <a href="/wiki/Slava_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Slava (disambiguation)">Slava</a> (for names ending with -slav or -slava).
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish">Spanish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unisex_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Spanish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_naming_customs" title="Spanish naming customs">Spanish naming customs</a></div>
<p>In Spain, unisex names are extremely rare. María, an originally feminine name is used in Spanish for males as second name, very commonly after José (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mar%C3%ADa" title="José María">José María</a>). José is used for females preceded by María (<a href="/wiki/Mar%C3%ADa_Jos%C3%A9" title="María José">María José</a>). Also <a href="/wiki/Guadalupe_(name)" title="Guadalupe (name)">Guadalupe</a>, a feminine name is sometimes used as masculine after José (José Guadalupe). More names given to both genders include <a href="/wiki/Carmen_(given_name)" title="Carmen (given name)">Carmen</a>, Inés, <a href="/wiki/Reno_(given_name)" title="Reno (given name)">Reno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Trinidad (disambiguation)">Trinidad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nazaret" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazaret">Nazaret</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reyes_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Reyes (disambiguation)">Reyes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Celes_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Celes (disambiguation)">Celes</a>. 
</p><p>Like in English, some common nicknames are unisex such as Álex (<a href="/wiki/Alejandro" title="Alejandro">Alejandro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alejandra" title="Alejandra">Alejandra</a>), Cris (<a href="/wiki/Cristina_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Cristina (disambiguation)">Cristina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cristiano_(given_name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Cristiano (given name)">Cristian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crist%C3%B3bal_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Cristóbal (disambiguation)">Cristóbal</a>), Dani (<a href="/wiki/Daniel" title="Daniel">Daniel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniela" class="mw-redirect" title="Daniela">Daniela</a>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anomalous_Unisex_Names">Anomalous Unisex Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unisex_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Anomalous Unisex Names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some names are masculine in one culture and feminine in another, so that when these cultures mix in a third location, the same name appears unisex. Some examples are:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Adel_(name)" title="Adel (name)">Adel</a>, a masculine name in Arabic and Persian-speaking countries, and a female name in Jewish countries or Hungarian as Adél</li>
<li>Aki, a male name in Finland and Nigeria, and unisex in Japan</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ami_(given_name)" title="Ami (given name)">Ami</a>, a feminine name in Japan and English-speaking countries but unisex in Hebrew-speaking countries and India</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Andrea" title="Andrea">Andrea</a>, a female name in most European countries, but male in Italy</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dayan_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Dayan (disambiguation)">Dayan</a>, masculine in <a href="/wiki/Bashkir_language" title="Bashkir language">Bashkir</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yakut_language" title="Yakut language">Yakut</a> but unisex in Jewish</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eli_(name)" title="Eli (name)">Eli</a>, a masculine name of Hebrew origin in English and Jewish-speaking countries and a feminine name in Spanish, Norwegian, and Danish-speaking countries</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jules" title="Jules">Jules</a>, masculine in French but unisex in English-speaking countries</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Karen_(name)" title="Karen (name)">Karen</a>, a female name in America, parts of Europe and Asia, and masculine in Armenia, Kurdistan, and Iran (The actual pronunciation is karēn.)</li>
<li>Kiernan, masculine in Irish but unisex in English</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kiko_(given_name)" title="Kiko (given name)">Kiko</a>, masculine in Spanish, Portuguese, and South America and feminine in Japan</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kiran_(given_name)" title="Kiran (given name)">Kiran</a>, unisex in India, feminine in Korea and Japan, and masculine in Russia and English-speaking countries</li>
<li>Lennox, masculine in Scotland but unisex in English-speaking countries</li>
<li>Lin, unisex in Chinese but masculine in <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>, French, Russian, Ukrainian and <a href="/wiki/Burmese_language" title="Burmese language">Burmese</a>, and feminine in English, <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">Faroese</a> and Icelandic
<ul><li>Lyn, a masculine name in Welsh-speaking countries but feminine in Danish and English</li></ul></li>
<li>Luan, a masculine name in Albanian and <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">Brazilian Portuguese</a> but a feminine name in Chinese and English</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Luca_(given_name)" title="Luca (given name)">Luca</a>, a masculine name in <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Latin languages</a>, feminine name in Hungarian and Croatian
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Luca_(given_name)" title="Luca (given name)">Luka</a>, masculine name in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">Balkans</a>, unisex name in Hawaii (used as the Hawaiian form of both <a href="/wiki/Luke_(name)" title="Luke (name)">Luke</a> for boys and <a href="/wiki/Ruth_(given_name)" title="Ruth (given name)">Ruth</a> for girls)</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Manu_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Manu (disambiguation)">Manu</a>, masculine in Finland and parts of India, and unisex in parts of Europe such as France and Germany</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mao_(given_name)" title="Mao (given name)">Mao</a>, a feminine name in Japan and a masculine name in <a href="/wiki/Limburg_(Belgium)" title="Limburg (Belgium)">Limburg</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Miho" title="Miho">Miho</a>, female in Japan and male in Croatia</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mika" title="Mika">Mika</a>, a male name in Finland and a female name in Japan</li>
<li>Moe, masculine in English-speaking countries and feminine in Japan</li>
<li>Naomi, a feminine name in America, Europe and Hebrew-speaking countries, and unisex in Japan</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nikita_(given_name)" title="Nikita (given name)">Nikita</a>, masculine in Russia and feminine in India</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nikola" title="Nikola">Nikola</a>, masculine name in <a href="/wiki/South_Slavs" title="South Slavs">South Slavic</a> countries, and feminine name in <a href="/wiki/West_Slavs" title="West Slavs">West Slavic</a> countries</li>
<li>Nima, masculine in Persia but unisex in Arabic</li>
<li>Noah, masculine in English but feminine in Hebrew</li>
<li>Olie, a unisex name in English-speaking countries, and a feminine name in Indonesia</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rani" title="Rani">Rani</a>, masculine in Hebrew and Arabic, feminine in Indian, <a href="/wiki/Telugu_language" title="Telugu language">Telugu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Marathi_language" title="Marathi language">Marathi</a>, and unisex in Faroese and Old Danish</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ren_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Ren (disambiguation)">Ren</a>, feminine in Taiwan and unisex in Japanese and English-speaking countries</li>
<li>Tyrann, feminine in English but masculine in <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Creole" title="Louisiana Creole">Louisiana Creole</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yury" title="Yury">Yuri</a>, a male name in Russia.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Yuri_(Japanese_name)" title="Yuri (Japanese name)">Yuri</a>, a female name in Japan</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yu-ri_(Korean_name)" title="Yu-ri (Korean name)">Yu-ri</a>, a Formal=Unisex name in Korea</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Zan_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Zan (disambiguation)">Zan</a>, a unisex name in both Chinese and English</li></ul>
