<p><b>Equestrianism</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">equester</i>, <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">equestr-</i>, <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">equus</i>, 'horseman', 'horse'),<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> more often known as <b>horse riding</b> (<a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>) or <b>horseback riding</b> (<a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> refers to the skill and sport of riding, <a href="/wiki/Driving_(horse)" title="Driving (horse)">driving</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steeplechase_(horse_racing)" title="Steeplechase (horse racing)">steeplechasing</a> or <a href="/wiki/Equestrian_vaulting" title="Equestrian vaulting">vaulting</a> with <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horses</a>. This broad description includes the use of horses for practical <a href="/wiki/Working_animal" title="Working animal">working</a> purposes, transportation, recreational activities, artistic or cultural exercises, and <a href="/wiki/Animals_in_sport" title="Animals in sport">competitive sport</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview_of_equestrian_activities">Overview of equestrian activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview of equestrian activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tibetan_equestrian.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Tibetan_equestrian.JPG/220px-Tibetan_equestrian.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Tibetan_equestrian.JPG/330px-Tibetan_equestrian.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Tibetan_equestrian.JPG/440px-Tibetan_equestrian.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tibetan_equestrian.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A young <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibetan</a> rider. Horse riding is an essential means of transportation in parts of the world where the landscape does not permit other means.</div></div></div>
<p>Horses are <a href="/wiki/Horse_training" title="Horse training">trained</a> and ridden for practical working purposes, such as in <a href="/wiki/Mounted_police" title="Mounted police">police work</a> or for controlling herd animals on a <a href="/wiki/Ranch" title="Ranch">ranch</a>. They are also used in <a href="/wiki/Horse#Sport" title="Horse">competitive sports</a> including <a href="/wiki/Dressage" title="Dressage">dressage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Endurance_riding" title="Endurance riding">endurance riding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eventing" title="Eventing">eventing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reining" title="Reining">reining</a>, <a href="/wiki/Show_jumping" title="Show jumping">show jumping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tent_pegging" title="Tent pegging">tent pegging</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equestrian_vaulting" title="Equestrian vaulting">vaulting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polo" title="Polo">polo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horse_racing" title="Horse racing">horse racing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Driving_(horse)" title="Driving (horse)">driving</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rodeo" title="Rodeo">rodeo</a> (see additional equestrian sports listed later in this article for more examples).  Some popular forms of competition are grouped together at <a href="/wiki/Horse_show" title="Horse show">horse shows</a> where horses perform in a wide variety of disciplines. Horses (and other <a href="/wiki/Equid" class="mw-redirect" title="Equid">equids</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Mule" title="Mule">mules</a>) are used for non-competitive recreational riding such as <a href="/wiki/Fox_hunting" title="Fox hunting">fox hunting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trail_riding" title="Trail riding">trail riding</a>, or hacking. There is public access to horse trails in almost every part of the world; many parks, <a href="/wiki/Ranch" title="Ranch">ranches</a>, and public <a href="/wiki/Stable" title="Stable">stables</a> offer both guided and independent riding. Horses are also used for <a href="/wiki/Therapeutic_horseback_riding" class="mw-redirect" title="Therapeutic horseback riding">therapeutic</a> purposes both in specialized para-equestrian competition as well as non-competitive riding to improve human health and emotional development.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Horses" class="mw-redirect" title="Horses">Horses</a> are also <a href="/wiki/Driving_(horse)" title="Driving (horse)">driven</a> in <a href="/wiki/Harness_racing" title="Harness racing">harness racing</a>, at <a href="/wiki/Horse_show" title="Horse show">horse shows,</a> and in other types of exhibition such as <a href="/wiki/Historical_reenactment" title="Historical reenactment">historical reenactment</a> or ceremony, often pulling <a href="/wiki/Carriage" title="Carriage">carriages</a>. In some parts of the world, they are still used for practical purposes such as <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farming</a>.
</p><p>Horses continue to be used in public service, in traditional ceremonies (parades, funerals, weddings), <a href="/wiki/Police_horse" class="mw-redirect" title="Police horse">police</a> and volunteer mounted patrols and for <a href="/wiki/Mounted_search_and_rescue" title="Mounted search and rescue">mounted search and rescue</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Riding_hall" title="Riding hall">Riding halls</a> enable the training of horse and rider in all weathers as well as indoor competition riding.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_horse_use">History of horse use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of horse use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cave_painting_in_Doushe_cave,_Lorstan,_Iran,_8th_millennium_BC.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Cave_painting_in_Doushe_cave%2C_Lorstan%2C_Iran%2C_8th_millennium_BC.JPG/220px-Cave_painting_in_Doushe_cave%2C_Lorstan%2C_Iran%2C_8th_millennium_BC.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="149" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Cave_painting_in_Doushe_cave%2C_Lorstan%2C_Iran%2C_8th_millennium_BC.JPG/330px-Cave_painting_in_Doushe_cave%2C_Lorstan%2C_Iran%2C_8th_millennium_BC.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Cave_painting_in_Doushe_cave%2C_Lorstan%2C_Iran%2C_8th_millennium_BC.JPG/440px-Cave_painting_in_Doushe_cave%2C_Lorstan%2C_Iran%2C_8th_millennium_BC.JPG 2x" data-file-width="486" data-file-height="330" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cave_painting_in_Doushe_cave,_Lorstan,_Iran,_8th_millennium_BC.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Prehistoric cave painting, depicting a horse and rider</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Domestication_of_the_horse" title="Domestication of the horse">Domestication of the horse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Horses_in_warfare" title="Horses in warfare">Horses in warfare</a></div>
<p>Though there is controversy over the exact date horses were <a href="/wiki/Domestication" title="Domestication">domesticated</a> and when they were first ridden, the best estimate is that horses first were ridden approximately 3500 BC. Indirect evidence suggests that horses were ridden long before they were driven. There is some evidence that about 3,000 BC, near the <a href="/wiki/Dnieper_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Dnieper River">Dnieper River</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Don_River_(Russia)" class="mw-redirect" title="Don River (Russia)">Don River</a>, people were using <a href="/wiki/Bit_(horse)" title="Bit (horse)">bits</a> on horses, as a stallion that was buried there shows teeth wear consistent with using a bit.<sup id="cite_ref-Chamberlin69_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chamberlin69-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However, the most unequivocal early <a href="/wiki/Archeology" class="mw-redirect" title="Archeology">archaeological</a> evidence of equines put to working use was of horses being driven. <a href="/wiki/Chariot" title="Chariot">Chariot</a> burials about 2500 BC present the most direct hard evidence of horses used as <a href="/wiki/Working_animal" title="Working animal">working animals</a>. In ancient times chariot warfare was followed by the use of <a href="/wiki/Horses_in_warfare" title="Horses in warfare">war horses</a> as light and heavy <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">cavalry</a>. The horse played an important role throughout human history all over the world, both in warfare and in peaceful pursuits such as <a href="/wiki/Transportation" class="mw-redirect" title="Transportation">transportation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trade" title="Trade">trade</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>. Horses lived in North America, but died out at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Ice_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Ice Age">Ice Age</a>. Horses were brought back to North America by European explorers, beginning with the second voyage of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Columbus</a> in 1493.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Equestrianism was introduced in the <a href="/wiki/1900_Summer_Olympics" title="1900 Summer Olympics">1900 Summer Olympics</a> as an Olympic sport with jumping events.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Horse_racing">Horse racing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Horse racing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Horse_racing" title="Horse racing">Horse racing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harness_racing" title="Harness racing">Harness racing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Parimutuel_gambling" class="mw-redirect" title="Parimutuel gambling">Parimutuel gambling</a></div>
<p>Humans appear to have long expressed a desire to know which horse or horses were the fastest, and <a href="/wiki/Horse_racing" title="Horse racing">horse racing</a> has ancient roots. <a href="/wiki/Gambling" title="Gambling">Gambling</a> on horse races appears to go hand-in hand with racing and has a long history as well. <a href="/wiki/Thoroughbred" title="Thoroughbred">Thoroughbreds</a> have the pre-eminent reputation as a racing breed, but other breeds also race.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_horse_racing">Types of horse racing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Types of horse racing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Under saddle
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Thoroughbred_horse_racing" class="mw-redirect" title="Thoroughbred horse racing">Thoroughbred horse racing</a> is the most popular form worldwide. In the UK, it is known as <a href="/wiki/Flat_racing" class="mw-redirect" title="Flat racing">flat racing</a> and is governed by the <a href="/wiki/Jockey_Club" title="Jockey Club">Jockey Club</a> in the United Kingdom. In the US, horse racing is governed by <a href="/wiki/The_Jockey_Club" class="mw-redirect" title="The Jockey Club">The Jockey Club</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Steeplechase_(horse_racing)" title="Steeplechase (horse racing)">Steeplechasing</a> involves racing on a track where the horses also jump over obstacles. It is most common in the UK, where it is also called <a href="/wiki/National_Hunt" class="mw-redirect" title="National Hunt">National Hunt</a> racing.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/American_Quarter_Horse" title="American Quarter Horse">American Quarter Horse</a> racing—races over distances of approximately a quarter-mile. Seen mostly in the United States, sanctioned by the <a href="/wiki/American_Quarter_Horse_Association" title="American Quarter Horse Association">American Quarter Horse Association</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Arabian_horse" title="Arabian horse">Arabian horses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Akhal-Teke" title="Akhal-Teke">Akhal-Teke</a>, <a href="/wiki/Appaloosa" title="Appaloosa">Appaloosas</a>, <a href="/wiki/American_Paint_Horse" title="American Paint Horse">American Paint Horses</a> and other light breeds are also raced worldwide.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Endurance_riding" title="Endurance riding">Endurance riding</a>, a sport in which the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_horse" title="Arabian horse">Arabian horse</a> dominates at the top levels, has become very popular in the United States and in Europe. The <a href="/wiki/Federation_Equestre_International" class="mw-redirect" title="Federation Equestre International">Federation Equestre International</a> (FEI) governs international races, and the American Endurance Ride Conference (AERC) organizes the sport in North America. Endurance races take place over a given, measured distance and the horses have an even start. Races are usually 50 to 100 miles (80 to 161&#160;km), over mountainous or other natural terrain, with scheduled stops to take the horses' vital signs, check soundness and verify that the horse is fit to continue. The first horse to finish and be confirmed by the veterinarian as fit to continue is the winner. Additional awards are usually given to the best-conditioned horses who finish in the top 10. Limited distance rides of about 25–20 miles (40–32&#160;km) are offered to newcomers.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ride_and_Tie" title="Ride and Tie">Ride and Tie</a> (in North America, organized by Ride and Tie Association). Ride and Tie involves three equal partners: two humans and one horse. The humans alternately run and ride.</li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Show_jumping" title="Show jumping">Show jumping</a>:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Show_jumping" title="Show jumping">Show jumping</a> is when a horse carries a rider over an obstacle commonly known as a jump. There are multiple jumps in a show, and if the horse hits or refuses a jump, points will be deducted from the rider score. This is also a timed event, and the rider is expected to complete the course in a certain amount of time, without error. There are also the hunter divisions. In the hunters, riders have to make their horses look good whilst they ride their courses. The judges look at the quality of the course, and if there are two or more riders who had put down amazing courses, then the judge or judges looks at how the horse looks and acts with the rider.</li></ul>
<p>In harness:
</p>
<ul><li>Both light and heavy breeds as well as ponies are raced in harness with a <a href="/wiki/Sulky" title="Sulky">sulky</a> or racing bike. The <a href="/wiki/Standardbred" title="Standardbred">Standardbred</a> dominates the sport in both <a href="/wiki/Trot_(horse_gait)" class="mw-redirect" title="Trot (horse gait)">trotting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pacing_(horse_gait)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacing (horse gait)">pacing</a> varieties.</li>
<li>The United States Trotting Association organizes <a href="/wiki/Harness_racing" title="Harness racing">harness racing</a> in the United States.</li>
<li>Harness racing is also found throughout Europe, New Zealand and Australia.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_and_Olympic_disciplines">International and Olympic disciplines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: International and Olympic disciplines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Equestrian_at_the_Olympics" class="mw-redirect" title="Equestrian at the Olympics">Equestrian at the Olympics</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_Federation_for_Equestrian_Sports" title="International Federation for Equestrian Sports">International Federation for Equestrian Sports</a></div>
<p>Equestrian events were first included in the modern <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a> in 1900. By 1912, all three Olympic disciplines still seen today were part of the games. The following forms of competition are recognized worldwide and are a part of the <a href="/wiki/Equestrian_at_the_Summer_Olympics" title="Equestrian at the Summer Olympics">equestrian events</a> at the Olympics. They are governed by the rules of the <a href="/wiki/International_Federation_for_Equestrian_Sports" title="International Federation for Equestrian Sports">International Federation for Equestrian Sports</a> (FEI).
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Dressage" title="Dressage">Dressage</a> ("training" in <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>) involves the progressive training of the horse to a high level of <a href="/wiki/Impulsion" title="Impulsion">impulsion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Collection_(horse)" title="Collection (horse)">collection</a> and obedience. Competitive dressage has the goal of showing the horse carrying out, on request, the natural movements that it performs without thinking while running loose.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Show_jumping" title="Show jumping">Show jumping</a> comprises a timed event judged on the ability of the horse and rider to jump over a series of obstacles, in a given order and with the fewest refusals or knockdowns of portions of the obstacles.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eventing" title="Eventing">Eventing</a>, also called <i>combined training</i>, <i>horse trials</i>, the <i>three-day event</i>, <i>the Military</i> or <i>the complete test</i>, puts together the obedience of dressage with the athletic ability of show jumping, the fitness demands the <a href="/wiki/Cross-country_equestrianism" class="mw-redirect" title="Cross-country equestrianism">cross-country jumping</a> phase. In the last-named, the horses jump over fixed obstacles, such as logs, stone walls, <a href="/wiki/Bank_(obstacle)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bank (obstacle)">banks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ditch_(obstacle)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ditch (obstacle)">ditches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Water_(obstacle)" class="mw-redirect" title="Water (obstacle)">water</a>, trying to finish the course under the "optimum time." There was also the 'Steeple Chase' Phase, which is now excluded from most major competitions to bring them in line with the Olympic standard.</li></ul>
<p>The additional internationally sanctioned but non-Olympic disciplines governed by the <a href="/wiki/International_Federation_for_Equestrian_Sports" title="International Federation for Equestrian Sports">FEI</a> are: <a href="/wiki/Combined_driving" title="Combined driving">combined driving</a>; <a href="/wiki/Endurance_riding" title="Endurance riding">endurance</a>; <a href="/wiki/Reining" title="Reining">reining</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Equestrian_vaulting" title="Equestrian vaulting">vaulting</a>. These disciplines are part of the <a href="/wiki/FEI_World_Equestrian_Games" title="FEI World Equestrian Games">FEI World Equestrian Games</a> every four years and may hold their own individual World Championships in other years. The FEI also recognizes <a href="/wiki/Horseball" title="Horseball">horseball</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tent_pegging" title="Tent pegging">tent pegging</a> as its two regional disciplines.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Para-equestrian_disciplines">Para-equestrian disciplines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Para-equestrian disciplines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Para-equestrian" title="Para-equestrian">Para-equestrian</a> competition at the international level, including the <a href="/wiki/Equestrian_at_the_Summer_Paralympics" title="Equestrian at the Summer Paralympics">Paralympics</a>, are also governed by the FEI and offer the following competition events:
</p>
<ul><li>Para-Equestrian Dressage is conducted under the same rules as conventional Dressage, but with riders divided into different competition grades based on their functional abilities.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Para-Equestrian Driving places competitors in grades based on their skill.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span id="Haute_.C3.89cole"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Haute_École">Haute École</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Haute École">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Classical_dressage" title="Classical dressage">Classical dressage</a></div>
<p>The <i>haute école</i> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">F.</a> "high school"), an advanced component of <a href="/wiki/Classical_dressage" title="Classical dressage">Classical dressage</a>, is a highly refined set of skills seldom used in competition but often seen in demonstration performances.
</p><p>The "Big Four" leading haute ecole demonstration teams include:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Cadre_Noir" title="Cadre Noir">Cadre Noir</a> in <a href="/wiki/Saumur" title="Saumur">Saumur</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Riding_School" title="Spanish Riding School">Spanish Riding School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_School_of_Equestrian_Art" title="Portuguese School of Equestrian Art">Portuguese School of Equestrian Art</a> at <a href="/wiki/Queluz_National_Palace" class="mw-redirect" title="Queluz National Palace">Queluz National Palace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_Andalusian_School_of_Equestrian_Art" title="Royal Andalusian School of Equestrian Art">Royal Andalusian School of Equestrian Art</a> in <a href="/wiki/Jerez_de_la_Frontera" title="Jerez de la Frontera">Jerez de la Frontera</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Other major classical teams include the <a href="/wiki/South_African_Lipizzaners" title="South African Lipizzaners">South African Lipizzaners</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hollandsche_Manege" title="Hollandsche Manege">Hollandsche Manege</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Horse_shows">Horse shows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Horse shows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Horse_show" title="Horse show">Horse show</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Horse_show" title="Horse show">Horse shows</a> are held throughout the world with a tremendous variety of possible events, equipment, attire and judging standards used. However, most forms of horse show competition can be broken into the following broad categories:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Equitation" title="Equitation">Equitation</a>, sometimes called <i>seat and hands</i> or <i>horsemanship</i>, refers to events where the rider is judged on form, style and ability.</li>
<li><i>Pleasure, flat</i> or <i>under saddle</i> classes feature horses who are ridden on the flat (not jumped) and judged on manners, performance, movement, style and quality.</li>
<li><i>Halter, in-hand breeding</i> or <i>conformation</i> classes, where the horse is led by a handler on the ground and judged on conformation and suitability as a breeding animal.</li>
<li><i>Harness</i> classes, where the horse is <a href="/wiki/Driving_(horse)" title="Driving (horse)">driven</a> rather than ridden, but still judged on manners, performance and quality.</li>
<li><i>Jumping</i> or <i>Over Fences</i> refers broadly to both <a href="/wiki/Show_jumping" title="Show jumping">show jumping</a> and <a href="/wiki/Show_hunter" title="Show hunter">show hunter</a>, where horses and riders must jump obstacles.</li></ul>

<h2><span id=".22English.22_riding"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;English&quot;_riding">"English" riding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: &quot;English&quot; riding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/English_riding" title="English riding">English riding</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Hunt_seat" title="Hunt seat">Hunt seat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Equitation" title="Equitation">Equitation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Show_jumping" title="Show jumping">Show jumping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dressage" title="Dressage">Dressage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Riding_Horse" class="mw-redirect" title="Riding Horse">Riding Horse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saddle_seat" title="Saddle seat">Saddle seat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jumpers" title="Jumpers">Jumpers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hunters" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunters">Hunters</a></div>
<p>In addition to the classical Olympic events, the following forms of competition are seen. In North America they are referred to as "<a href="/wiki/English_riding" title="English riding">English riding</a>" in contrast with western riding; elsewhere in the world, if a distinction is necessary, they are usually described as "classic riding":
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Hunt_seat" title="Hunt seat">Hunt seat</a> or <a href="/wiki/Show_hunter" title="Show hunter">Hunter</a> classes judge the movement and the form of horses suitable for work over fences. A typical <a href="/wiki/Show_hunter" title="Show hunter">show hunter</a> division would include classes over fences as well as "Hunter under Saddle" or "flat" classes (sometimes called "hack" classes), in which the horse is judged on its performance, manners and movement without having to jump. Hunters have a long, flat-kneed trot, sometimes called "daisy cutter" movement, a phrase suggesting a good hunter could slice daisies in a field when it reaches its stride out. The over fences classes in <a href="/wiki/Show_hunter" title="Show hunter">show hunter</a> competition are judged on the form of the horse, its manners and the smoothness of the course. A horse with good jumping form snaps its knees up and jumps with a good <a href="/wiki/Bascule_(horse)" title="Bascule (horse)">bascule</a>. It should also be able to canter or gallop with control while having a stride long enough to make a proper number of strides over a given distance between fences. Hunter classes differ from jumper classes, in which they are not timed, and equitation classes, in which the rider's performance is the focus. Hunter style is based on fox hunting, so jumps in the hunter division are usually more natural colors than the jumps in a jumper division.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eventing" title="Eventing">Eventing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Show_jumping" title="Show jumping">show jumping</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dressage" title="Dressage">dressage</a>, described under "Olympic disciplines," above are all "English" riding disciplines that in North America sometimes are loosely classified within the "hunt seat" category.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Saddle_seat" title="Saddle seat">Saddle seat</a>, is a primarily American discipline, though has recently become somewhat popular in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, was created to show to best advantage the animated movement of high-stepping and gaited breeds such as the <a href="/wiki/American_Saddlebred" title="American Saddlebred">American Saddlebred</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Walking_Horse" title="Tennessee Walking Horse">Tennessee Walker</a>. Many <a href="/wiki/Arabian_horse" title="Arabian horse">Arabians</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morgan_horse" title="Morgan horse">Morgans</a> are also shown saddle seat in the United States. There are usually three basic divisions. <i>Park</i> divisions are for the horses with the highest action. <i>Pleasure</i> divisions still emphasis animated action, but to a lesser degree, with manners ranking over animation. <i>Plantation</i> or <i>Country</i> divisions have the least amount of animation (in some breeds, the horses are flat-shod) and the greatest emphasis on manners.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Show_hack" title="Show hack">Show hack</a> is a competition seen primarily in the United Kingdom, Australia and other nations influenced by British traditions, featuring horses of elegant appearance, with excellent way of going and self-carriage. A related event is <a href="/wiki/Riding_Horse" class="mw-redirect" title="Riding Horse">riding Horse</a>.</li></ul>
<h2><span id=".22Western.22_riding"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Western&quot;_riding">"Western" riding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: &quot;Western&quot; riding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Western_riding" title="Western riding">Western riding</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Reining" title="Reining">Reining</a>, <a href="/wiki/Western_pleasure" title="Western pleasure">Western pleasure</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rodeo" title="Rodeo">Rodeo</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cutting_(sport)" title="Cutting (sport)">Cutting (sport)</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Western_riding" title="Western riding">Western riding</a> evolved from the cattle-working and warfare traditions brought to the Americas by the <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> <a href="/wiki/Conquistadors" class="mw-redirect" title="Conquistadors">Conquistadors</a>, and both equipment and riding style evolved to meet the working needs of the <a href="/wiki/Cowboy" title="Cowboy">cowboy</a> on <a href="/wiki/Ranch" title="Ranch">ranches</a> in the <a href="/wiki/American_West" class="mw-redirect" title="American West">American West</a>.
</p><p>Though the differences between English and Western riding appear dramatic, there are many similarities. Both styles require riders to have a solid seat, with the hips and shoulders balanced over the feet, with hands independent of the seat so as to avoid disturbing the balance of the horse and interfering with its performance.
</p><p>The most noticeable feature of western style riding is in the saddle, which has a substantial tree that provides greater support to horse and rider when working long hours in the saddle. The western saddle features a prominent pommel topped by a horn (a knob used for dallying a <a href="/wiki/Lasso" title="Lasso">lariat</a> after roping an animal), a deep seat and a high cantle. The stirrups are wider and the saddle has rings and ties that allow objects to be attached to the saddle.
</p><p>Western horses are asked to perform with a loose rein, controlled by one hand. The standard western bridle lacks a <a href="/wiki/Noseband" title="Noseband">noseband</a> and usually consists of a single set of reins attached to a <a href="/wiki/Curb_bit" title="Curb bit">curb bit</a> that has somewhat longer and looser <a href="/wiki/Bit_shank" title="Bit shank">shanks</a> than the curb of an English <a href="/wiki/Double_bridle" title="Double bridle">Weymouth</a> bridle or a <a href="/wiki/Pelham_bit" title="Pelham bit">pelham bit</a>. Two styles of Western <a href="/wiki/Rein" title="Rein">reins</a> developed: The long split reins of the Texas tradition, which are completely separated, or the closed-end "<a href="/wiki/Romal" title="Romal">Romal</a>" reins of the California tradition, which have a long single attachment on the ends that can be used as a <a href="/wiki/Quirt" title="Quirt">quirt</a>. Modern <a href="/wiki/Rodeo" title="Rodeo">rodeo</a> competitors in timed events sometimes use a closed rein without a <i>romal</i>.
</p><p>Western riders wear a long-sleeved shirt, denim jeans, boots, and a wide-brimmed <a href="/wiki/Cowboy_hat" title="Cowboy hat">cowboy hat</a>. <a href="/wiki/Cowboy_boot" title="Cowboy boot">Cowboy boots</a>, which have pointed toes and higher heels than a traditional riding boot, are designed to prevent the rider's foot from slipping through the stirrup during a fall, preventing the rider from being dragged—most western saddles have no safety bars for the leathers or automatic stirrup release mechanism. A rider may wear protective leather leggings called <a href="/wiki/Chaps" title="Chaps">chaps</a>. Clean, well-fitting work clothing is the usual outfit seen in <a href="/wiki/Rodeo" title="Rodeo">rodeo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cutting_(sport)" title="Cutting (sport)">cutting</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reining" title="Reining">reining</a> competitions, especially for men, though sometimes both men and women wear brighter colors or finer fabrics for competition than for work.
</p><p>Show events such as <a href="/wiki/Western_pleasure" title="Western pleasure">Western pleasure</a> use much flashier equipment, unlike the English traditions where clothing and tack is quiet and unobtrusive. Saddles, bits and bridles are ornamented with substantial amounts of silver. The rider may add a jacket or vest, and women's clothing in particular features vivid colors and even rhinestones or sequins.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Western horses are asked to have a brisk, ground-covering walk, but a slow, relaxed jog <a href="/wiki/Trot_(horse_gait)" class="mw-redirect" title="Trot (horse gait)">trot</a> that allows the rider to sit the saddle and not post. The Western version of the <a href="/wiki/Canter" class="mw-redirect" title="Canter">canter</a> is called a <i>lope</i> and while collected and balanced, is expected to be slow and relaxed. Working western horses seldom use a sustained hand gallop, but must be able to accelerate quickly to high speed when chasing cattle or competing in <a href="/wiki/Rodeo" title="Rodeo">rodeo</a> speed events, must be able to stop quickly from a dead run and "turn on a dime."
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Harness">Harness</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Harness">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Welsh-A-Buggy.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Welsh-A-Buggy.jpg/220px-Welsh-A-Buggy.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Welsh-A-Buggy.jpg/330px-Welsh-A-Buggy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Welsh-A-Buggy.jpg/440px-Welsh-A-Buggy.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2436" data-file-height="1827" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Welsh-A-Buggy.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Welsh pony in fine harness competition</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Driving_(horse)" title="Driving (horse)">Driving (horse)</a></div>
<p>Horses, <a href="/wiki/Mule" title="Mule">mules</a> and <a href="/wiki/Donkey" title="Donkey">donkeys</a> are driven in <a href="/wiki/Horse_harness" title="Horse harness">harness</a> in many different ways. For working purposes, they can pull a <a href="/wiki/Plow" class="mw-redirect" title="Plow">plow</a> or other <a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farm</a> equipment designed to be pulled by animals. In many parts of the world they still pull <a href="/wiki/Wagon" title="Wagon">wagons</a> for basic hauling and transportation. They may draw <a href="/wiki/Carriage" title="Carriage">carriages</a> at ceremonies, in parades or for tourist rides.
</p><p>As noted in "horse racing" <i>above</i>, horses can race in harness, pulling a very lightweight cart known as a <a href="/wiki/Sulky" title="Sulky">sulky</a>. At the other end of the spectrum, some <a href="/wiki/Draft_horse" title="Draft horse">draft horses</a> compete in <a href="/wiki/Horse_pulling" title="Horse pulling">horse pulling</a> competitions, where single or teams of horses and their drivers vie to determine who can pull the most weight for a short distance.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Horse_show" title="Horse show">horse show</a> competition, the following general categories of competition are seen:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Combined_driving" title="Combined driving">Combined driving</a>, an internationally recognized competition where horses perform an arena-based "dressage" class where precision and control are emphasized, a cross-country "marathon" section that emphasizes fitness and endurance, and a "stadium" or "cones" obstacle course.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Draft_horse_showing" title="Draft horse showing">Draft horse showing</a>: Most <a href="/wiki/Draft_horse" title="Draft horse">draft horse</a> performance competition is done in harness.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pleasure_driving" title="Pleasure driving">Pleasure driving</a>: Horses and ponies are usually hitched to a light cart shown at a walk and two speeds of trot, with an emphasis on manners.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fine_harness" title="Fine harness">Fine harness</a>: Also called "Formal driving," Horses are hitched to a light four-wheeled cart and shown in a manner that emphasizes flashy action and dramatic performance.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Roadster_(horse)" title="Roadster (horse)">Roadster</a>: A horse show competition where exhibitors wear <a href="/wiki/Racing_silks" class="mw-redirect" title="Racing silks">racing silks</a> and ride in a sulky in a style akin to <a href="/wiki/Harness_racing" title="Harness racing">harness racing</a>, only without actually racing, but rather focusing on manners and performance.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Carriage" title="Carriage">Carriage</a> driving, using somewhat larger two or four wheeled carriages, often restored <a href="/wiki/Antique" title="Antique">antiques</a>, judged on the turnout/neatness or suitability of horse and carriage.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rodeo">Rodeo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Rodeo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rodeo" title="Rodeo">Rodeo</a></div>
<p>Rodeo events include the following forms of competition:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Timed_events">Timed events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Timed events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Barrel_racing" title="Barrel racing">Barrel racing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pole_bending" title="Pole bending">pole bending</a> – the timed speed and agility events seen in <a href="/wiki/Rodeo" title="Rodeo">rodeo</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Gymkhana_(Equestrian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gymkhana (Equestrian)">gymkhana</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gymkhana_(Equestrian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gymkhana (Equestrian)">O-Mok-See</a> competition. Both men and women compete in speed events at gymkhanas or O-Mok-Sees; however, at most professional, sanctioned rodeos, barrel racing is an exclusively women's sport. In a barrel race, horse and rider gallop around a cloverleaf pattern of barrels, making agile turns without knocking the barrels over. In pole bending, horse and rider run the length of a line of six upright poles, turn sharply and weave through the poles, turn again and weave back, then return to the start.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Steer_wrestling" title="Steer wrestling">Steer wrestling</a> – Also known as "Bulldogging," this is a rodeo event where the rider jumps off his horse onto a steer and 'wrestles' it to the ground by grabbing it by the horns. This is probably the single most physically dangerous event in rodeo for the cowboy, who runs a high risk of jumping off a running horse head first and missing the steer or of having the thrown steer land on top of him, sometimes horns first.</li>
<li>Goat tying – usually an event for women or pre-teen girls and boys, a goat is staked out while a mounted rider runs to the goat, dismounts, grabs the goat, throws it to the ground and ties it in the same manner as a calf. This event was designed to teach smaller or younger riders the basics of calf roping without the more complex need to also lasso the animal.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roping">Roping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Roping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Roping includes a number of timed events that are based on the real-life tasks of a working cowboy, who often had to capture calves and adult <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">cattle</a> for <a href="/wiki/Livestock_branding" title="Livestock branding">branding</a>, medical treatment and other purposes. A lasso or <a href="/wiki/Lasso" title="Lasso">lariat</a> is thrown over the head of a <a href="/wiki/Calf_(animal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Calf (animal)">calf</a> or the horns of adult cattle, and the animal is secured in a fashion dictated by its size and age.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Calf_roping" title="Calf roping">Calf roping</a>, also called "tie-down roping," is an event where a calf is roped around the neck by a <a href="/wiki/Lasso" title="Lasso">lariat</a>, the horse stops and sets back on the rope while the cowboy dismounts, runs to the calf, throws it to the ground and ties three feet together. (If the horse throws the calf, the cowboy must lose time waiting for the calf to get back to its feet so that the cowboy can do the work. The job of the horse is to hold the calf steady on the rope) This activity is still practiced on modern working ranches for <a href="/wiki/Brand" title="Brand">branding</a>, medical treatment, and so on.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Team_roping" title="Team roping">Team roping</a>, also called "heading and heeling," is the only rodeo event where men and women riders may compete together. Two people capture and restrain a full-grown steer. One horse and rider, the "header," lassos a running steer's horns, while the other horse and rider, the "heeler," lassos the steer's two hind legs. Once the animal is captured, the riders face each other and lightly pull the steer between them, so that it loses its balance, thus in the real world allowing restraint for treatment.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Breakaway_roping" title="Breakaway roping">Breakaway roping</a> – an easier form of calf roping where a very short lariat is used, tied lightly to the saddle horn with string and a flag. When the calf is roped, the horse stops, allowing the calf to run on, flagging the end of time when the string and flag breaks from the saddle. In the United States, this event is primarily for women of all ages and boys under 12, while in some nations where traditional calf roping is frowned upon, riders of both genders compete.</li></ul>
<h3><span id=".22Rough_Stock.22_competition"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Rough_Stock&quot;_competition">"Rough Stock" competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: &quot;Rough Stock&quot; competition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Horses_abreast_IMG_5342.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Horses_abreast_IMG_5342.jpg/220px-Horses_abreast_IMG_5342.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Horses_abreast_IMG_5342.jpg/330px-Horses_abreast_IMG_5342.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Horses_abreast_IMG_5342.jpg/440px-Horses_abreast_IMG_5342.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3647" data-file-height="2799" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Horses_abreast_IMG_5342.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Small herd of rough stock in Texas.</div></div></div>
<p>In spite of popular myth, most modern "broncs" are not in fact wild horses, but are more commonly spoiled riding horses<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> or horses bred specifically as bucking stock.
</p>
<ul><li>Bronc riding – there are two divisions in rodeo, <a href="/wiki/Bareback_bronc" class="mw-redirect" title="Bareback bronc">bareback bronc</a> riding, where the rider rides a bucking horse holding onto a leather <a href="/wiki/Surcingle" title="Surcingle">surcingle</a> or rigging with only one hand, and <a href="/wiki/Saddle_bronc" class="mw-redirect" title="Saddle bronc">saddle bronc</a> riding, where the rider rides a modified western saddle without a horn (for safety) while holding onto a braided lead rope attached to the horse's halter.</li>
<li>Bull Riding – though technically not an equestrian event, as the cowboys ride full-grown bulls instead of horses, skills similar to bareback bronc riding are required.</li></ul>

<p>There are many other forms of equestrian activity and sports seen worldwide. There are both competitive events and <a href="/wiki/Pleasure_riding" title="Pleasure riding">pleasure riding</a> disciplines available.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arena_sports">Arena sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Arena sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Arena <a href="/wiki/Polo" title="Polo">polo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cowboy_polo" title="Cowboy polo">Cowboy polo</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Pato" title="Pato">Pato</a> (Argentina's national sport)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Charreada" title="Charreada">Charreada</a>, the highest form of Mexican horsemanship based on a mixture of Spanish and Native traditions.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Equestrian_vaulting" title="Equestrian vaulting">Equestrian vaulting</a>: In vaulting, a <a href="/wiki/Surcingle" title="Surcingle">surcingle</a> with two hoops at the top is attached around a horse's <a href="/wiki/Horse_anatomy" class="mw-redirect" title="Horse anatomy">barrel</a>. The horse also wears a <a href="/wiki/Bridle" title="Bridle">bridle</a> with <a href="/wiki/Side_reins" title="Side reins">side reins</a>. The vaulter is <a href="/wiki/Longeing" title="Longeing">longed</a> on the horse, and performs <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics" title="Gymnastics">gymnastic</a> movements while the horse walks, trots, and canters.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gymkhana_(Equestrian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gymkhana (Equestrian)">Gymkhana</a>, also known as <i>O-Mok-See</i> in the western United States.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Reining" title="Reining">Reining</a> is a part of western riding where the horses and rider perform fast circles, galloping right into a halt, and all of the while, the riders barely pull on the horses mouth.</li></ul>

<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Buzkashi" title="Buzkashi">Buzkashi</a>, a sport originating on the <a href="/wiki/Steppes" class="mw-redirect" title="Steppes">steppes</a> of central <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, now the national sport of <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kyrgyzstan" title="Kyrgyzstan">Kyrgyzstan</a>.</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cross-country_sports">Cross-country sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Cross-country sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Competitive_Mounted_Orienteering" class="mw-redirect" title="Competitive Mounted Orienteering">Competitive Mounted Orienteering</a>, a form of orienteering on horses (but unrelated to <a href="/wiki/Orienteering" title="Orienteering">orienteering</a>) – consists of three stages: following a precise route marked on a map, negotiation of obstacles and control of paces.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Techniques_de_Randonn%C3%A9e_%C3%89questre_de_Comp%C3%A9tition" title="Techniques de Randonnée Équestre de Compétition">Le Trec</a>, which comprises three phases – trail riding, with jumping and correct basic flatwork. Le Trec, which is very popular in Europe, tests the partnership's ability to cope with an all-day ride across varied terrain, route finding, negotiating natural obstacles and hazards, while considering the welfare of the horse, respecting the countryside and enjoying all it has to offer.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Competitive_trail_riding" title="Competitive trail riding">Competitive trail riding</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Pace_race" title="Pace race">pace race</a> held across terrain similar to <a href="/wiki/Endurance_riding" title="Endurance riding">endurance riding</a>, but shorter in length (25 – 35 miles (56&#160;km), depending on class). Being a form of <a href="/wiki/Pace_race" title="Pace race">pace race</a>, the objective is not to finish in the least time. Instead, as in other forms of <a href="/wiki/Judged_trail_ride" title="Judged trail ride">judged trail riding</a>, each competitor is graded on everything including physical condition, campsite and horse management. Horsemanship also is considered, including how the rider handles the trail and how horse is handled and presented to the judge and vet throughout the ride. The horse is graded on performance, manners, etc. "Pulse and respiration" stops check the horse's recovery ability. The judges also set up obstacles along the trail and the horse and rider are graded on how well they perform as a team. The whole point is the partnership between the horse and rider.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cross_Country_Jumping" class="mw-redirect" title="Cross Country Jumping">Cross Country Jumping</a>, a jumping course that contains logs and natural obstacles mostly. The common clothes worn are usually brighter colors and less conservative.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Endurance_riding" title="Endurance riding">Endurance riding</a>, a competition usually of 50 to 100 miles (160&#160;km) or more, over mountainous or other natural terrain, with scheduled stops to take the horses' vital signs, check soundness and verify that the horse is fit to continue. The first horse to finish and be confirmed by the veterinarian as fit to continue is the winner. Additional awards are usually given to the best-conditioned horses who finish in the top 10.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fox_hunting" title="Fox hunting">Fox hunting</a></li>
<li>Hacking, or <a href="/wiki/Pleasure_riding" title="Pleasure riding">pleasure riding</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hunter_Pacing" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunter Pacing">Hunter Pacing</a> is a sport where a horse and rider team travel a trail at speeds based the ideal conditions for the horse, with competitors seeking to ride closest to that perfect time. Hunter paces are usually held in a series. Hunter paces are usually a few miles long and covered mostly at a canter or gallop. The horsemanship and management skills of the rider are also considered in the scoring, and periodic stops are required for veterinarians to check the vital signs and overall soundness of the horses.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ride_and_Tie" title="Ride and Tie">Ride and Tie</a> is a form of <a href="/wiki/Endurance_riding" title="Endurance riding">endurance riding</a> in which teams of 3 (two humans and one horse) alternate running and riding.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Steeplechase_(horse_racing)" title="Steeplechase (horse racing)">Steeplechase</a>, a distance horse race with diverse fence and ditch obstacles.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Trail_Riding" class="mw-redirect" title="Trail Riding">Trail Riding</a>, pleasure riding any breed horse, any style across the land.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_issues">Health issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Health issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Expand_section plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg" class="image"><img alt="[icon]" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" decoding="async" width="20" height="14" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="44" data-file-height="31" /></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">January 2009</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Handling, riding and driving horses has a number of health risks. Because horses are 1200 pound beasts, and they are animals with minds of their own, they are still capable of hurting people. Should the human give the highly trained horses a reason to want to hurt the human, they absolutely will, and there is absolutely no way that the human can stop the horse. However, this is a rare occurrence, as horses are such forgiving animals, and it is almost always the persons fault.
</p><p>Riding has some inherent risks, as when mounted, the rider's head may be up to 4&#160;m (13&#160;ft) from the ground, and the horse may travel at a speed of up to 65&#160;km/h (40&#160;mph).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The injuries observed range from very minor injuries to fatalities.
</p><p>A study in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> reported that the <a href="/wiki/Relative_risk" title="Relative risk">relative risk</a> of injury from riding a horse, compared to riding a bicycle, was 9 times higher for <a href="/wiki/Adolescent" class="mw-redirect" title="Adolescent">adolescents</a> and 5.6 times higher for younger <a href="/wiki/Child" title="Child">children</a>, but that riding a horse was less risky than riding a <a href="/wiki/Moped" title="Moped">moped</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid17426559_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17426559-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Victoria,_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Victoria, Australia">Victoria, Australia</a>, a search of state records found that equestrian sports had the third highest incidence of serious injury, after <a href="/wiki/Motor_sport" class="mw-redirect" title="Motor sport">motor sports</a> and <a href="/wiki/Power_boat" class="mw-redirect" title="Power boat">power boating</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid16046347_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid16046347-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, an analysis of a national registry estimated the incidence of equestrian injury to be 21 per 100,000 person-years for farming and equestrian sports combined, and 160 times higher for <a href="/wiki/Horse_racing" title="Horse racing">horse racing</a> personnel. Other findings noted that helmets likely prevent traumatic brain injuries.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid15128138_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15128138-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> each year an estimated 30 million people ride horses, resulting in 50,000 <a href="/wiki/Emergency_department" title="Emergency department">emergency department</a> visits (1 visit per 600 riders per year).<sup id="cite_ref-pmid17461318_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17461318-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> A survey of 679 equestrians in Oregon, Washington and Idaho estimated that at some time in their equestrian career one in five will be seriously injured, resulting in hospitalization, surgery or long-term disability.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid17414356_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17414356-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Among survey respondents, novice equestrians had an incidence of any injury that was threefold over intermediates, fivefold over advanced equestrians, and nearly eightfold over professionals. Approximately 100 hours of experience are required to achieve a substantial decline in the risk of injury. The survey authors conclude that efforts to prevent equestrian injury should focus on novice equestrians.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mechanisms_of_injury">Mechanisms of injury</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Mechanisms of injury">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most common injury is falling from the horse, followed by being kicked, trampled and bitten. About 3 out of 4 injuries are due to falling, broadly defined.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> A broad definition of falling often includes being crushed and being thrown from the horse, but when reported separately each of these mechanisms may be more common than being kicked.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid18695484_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18695484-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pmid18424291_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18424291-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_and_severity_of_injury">Types and severity of injury</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Types and severity of injury">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, a 10-year study of trauma center patients injured while riding reported that although 48% had suffered head injuries, only 9% of these riders had been wearing helmets at the time of their accident. Other injuries involved the chest (54%), abdomen (22%) and extremities (17%).<sup id="cite_ref-ball-2007_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ball-2007-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> A German study reported that injuries in horse riding are rare compared to other sports, but when they occur they are severe. Specifically, they found that 40% of horse riding injuries were fractures, and only 15% were sprains. Furthermore, the study noted that in Germany, one quarter of all sport related fatalities are caused by horse riding.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid1793946_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid1793946-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
Most horse related injuries are a result of falling from a horse, which is the cause of 60–80% of all such reported injuries.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Another common cause of injury is being kicked by a horse, which may cause skull fractures or severe trauma to the <a href="/wiki/Internal_organs" class="mw-redirect" title="Internal organs">internal organs</a>.  Some possible injuries resulting from horse riding, with the percent indicating the amounts in relation to all injuries as reported by a <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> study,<sup id="cite_ref-pmid14581953_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid14581953-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> include:
</p>
<ul><li>Arm fracture or dislocation (31%)</li>
<li>Head injury (21%)</li>
<li>Leg fracture or dislocation (15%)</li>
<li>Chest injury (33%)</li></ul>
<p>Among 36 members and employees of the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Jockey_Club" title="Hong Kong Jockey Club">Hong Kong Jockey Club</a> who were seen in a trauma center during a period of 5 years, 24 fell from horses and 11 were kicked by the horse. Injuries comprised: 18 torso; 11 head, face or neck; and 11 limb.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid17049524_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17049524-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The authors of this study recommend that helmets, face shields and body protectors be worn when riding or handling horses.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, a study of equestrians seen at one hospital over a 6-year period found that 81% were wearing a helmet at the time of injury, and that helmet use both increased over time and was correlated with a lower rate of admission.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid12887517_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid12887517-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In the second half of the study period, of the equestrians seen at a hospital, only 14% were admitted. In contrast, a study of child equestrians seen at a hospital emergency department in <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">Adelaide</a> reported that 60% were admitted.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid18782209_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18782209-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, an analysis of <a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Electronic_Injury_Surveillance_System&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="National Electronic Injury Surveillance System (page does not exist)">National Electronic Injury Surveillance System</a> (NEISS) data performed by the Equestrian Medical Safety Association studied 78,279 horse-related injuries in 2007: "The most common injuries included fractures (28.5%); contusions/abrasions (28.3%); strain/sprain (14.5%); internal injury (8.1%); lacerations (5.7%); concussions (4.6%); dislocations (1.9%); and hematomas (1.2%). Most frequent injury sites are the lower trunk (19.6%); head (15.0%); upper trunk (13.4%); shoulder (8.2%); and wrist (6.8%). Within this study patients were treated and released (86.2%), were hospitalized (8.7%), were transferred (3.6%), left without being treated (0.8%), remained for observation (0.6%) and arrived at the hospital deceased (0.1%)."<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Head_injuries">Head injuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Head injuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Horseback riding is one of the most dangerous sports, especially in relation to head injury. Statistics from the United States, for example, indicate that about 30 million people ride horses annually.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> On average, about 67,000 people are admitted to the hospital each year from injuries sustained while working with horses.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> 15,000 of those admittances are from traumatic brain injuries. Of those, about 60 die each year from their brain injuries.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Studies have found horseback riding to be more dangerous than several sports, including skiing, auto racing and football.<sup id="cite_ref-ball-2007_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ball-2007-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Horseback riding has a higher hospital admittance rate per hours of riding than motorcycle racing, at 0.49 per thousand hours of riding and 0.14 accidents per thousand hours, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-ball-2007_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ball-2007-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Head injuries are especially traumatic in horseback riding. About two-thirds of all riders requiring hospitalization after a fall have sustained a traumatic brain injury.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Falling from a horse without wearing a helmet is comparable to being struck by a car.<sup id="cite_ref-Nelson,_M.A._1992_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nelson,_M.A._1992-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Most falling deaths are caused by head injury.<sup id="cite_ref-Nelson,_M.A._1992_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nelson,_M.A._1992-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The use of riding helmets substantially decreases the likelihood and severity of head injuries. When a rider falls with a helmet, he or she is five times less likely to experience a traumatic brain injury than a rider who falls without a helmet.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Helmets work by crushing on impact and extending the length of time it takes the head to stop moving.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Despite this, helmet usage rates in North America are estimated to be between eight and twenty percent.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Once a helmet has sustained an impact from falling, that part of the helmet is structurally weakened, even if no visible damage is present.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Helmet manufacturers recommend that a helmet that has undergone impact from a fall be replaced immediately. In addition, helmets should be replaced every three to five years; specific recommendations vary by manufacturer.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rules_on_helmet_use_in_competition">Rules on helmet use in competition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Rules on helmet use in competition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many organizations mandate helmet use in competition or on show grounds, and rules have continually moved in the direction of requiring helmet use. In 2011, the United States Equestrian Federation passed a rule making helmet use mandatory while mounted on competition grounds at U.S. nationally rated eventing competitions.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Also in 2011, the United States Dressage Federation made helmet use in competition mandatory for all riders under 18 and all riders who are riding any test at Fourth Level and below.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> If a rider competing at Prix St. Georges and above is also riding a test at Fourth Level or below, he or she must also wear a helmet at all times while mounted.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Riding_astride">Riding astride</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Riding astride">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The idea that riding a horse astride could injure a woman's sex organs is a historic, but sometimes popular even today, misunderstanding or misconception, particularly that riding astride can damage the <a href="/wiki/Hymen" title="Hymen">hymen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid12346860_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid12346860-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Evidence of injury to any female sex organs is scant. In female high-level athletes, trauma to the <a href="/wiki/Perineum" title="Perineum">perineum</a> is rare and is associated with certain sports (see <a href="/wiki/Pelvic_floor#Clinical_significance" title="Pelvic floor">Pelvic floor#Clinical significance</a>). The type of trauma associated with <a href="/wiki/Equestrian_sports" class="mw-redirect" title="Equestrian sports">equestrian sports</a> has been termed "horse riders' perineum".<sup id="cite_ref-pmid17450681_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17450681-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Case_series" title="Case series">case series</a> of 4 female mountain bike riders and 2 female horse riders found both patient-reported perineal pain and evidence of sub-clinical changes in the <a href="/wiki/Clitoris" title="Clitoris">clitoris</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> the relevance of these findings to horse riding is unknown.
</p><p>In men, sports-related injuries are among the major causes of testicular trauma. In a small <a href="/wiki/Scientific_control" title="Scientific control">controlled</a> but <a href="/wiki/Blind_experiment" class="mw-redirect" title="Blind experiment">unblinded</a> study of 52 men, <a href="/wiki/Varicocele" title="Varicocele">varicocele</a> was significantly more common in equestrians than in non-equestrians.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid15972705_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15972705-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The difference between these two groups was small, however, compared to differences reported between extreme <a href="/wiki/Mountain_bike" title="Mountain bike">mountain bike</a> riders and non-riders,<sup id="cite_ref-pmid11323467_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid11323467-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> and also between mountain bike riders and on-road bicycle riders.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid18185039_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid18185039-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Horse-riding injuries to the <a href="/wiki/Scrotum" title="Scrotum">scrotum</a> (<a href="/wiki/Contusion" class="mw-redirect" title="Contusion">contusions</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Testes" class="mw-redirect" title="Testes">testes</a> (<a href="/wiki/Blunt_trauma" title="Blunt trauma">blunt trauma</a>) were well known to surgeons in the 19th century and early 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-KeenDaCosta1908_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KeenDaCosta1908-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Injuries from collision with the pommel of a saddle are mentioned specifically.<sup id="cite_ref-KeenDaCosta1908_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KeenDaCosta1908-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_horses_in_sport">Criticism of horses in sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Criticism of horses in sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Horse_racing" title="Horse racing">Horse racing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rodeo" title="Rodeo">Rodeo</a></div>
<p>Organized welfare groups, such as the <a href="/wiki/Humane_Society_of_the_United_States" title="Humane Society of the United States">Humane Society of the United States</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Animal_rights" title="Animal rights">animal rights</a> groups such as <a href="/wiki/People_for_the_Ethical_Treatment_of_Animals" title="People for the Ethical Treatment of Animals">People for the Ethical Treatment of Animals</a>, have been known to criticise some horse sports with claims of <a href="/wiki/Animal_cruelty" class="mw-redirect" title="Animal cruelty">animal cruelty</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Horse_racing" title="Horse racing">Horse racing</a> is a popular equestrian sport which is practiced in many nations around the world. It is inextricably associated with <a href="/wiki/Gambling" title="Gambling">gambling</a>, where in certain events, stakes can become very high. Despite its illegality in most competitions, these conditions of extreme competitiveness can lead to the use of performing-enhancing drugs and extreme training techniques, which can result in negative side effects for the horses' well-being.
The races themselves have also proved dangerous to the horses – especially <a href="/wiki/Steeplechase_(horse_racing)" title="Steeplechase (horse racing)">steeplechasing</a>, which requires the horse to jump hurdles whilst galloping at full speed. This can result in injury or death to the horse, as well as the jockey.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> A study by animal welfare group <a href="/wiki/Animal_Aid" title="Animal Aid">Animal Aid</a> revealed that approximately 375 racehorses die yearly, with 30% of these either during or as a result of injuries from a race.<sup id="cite_ref-animalaid_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-animalaid-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The report also highlighted the increasing frequency of race-related illnesses, including bleeding lungs (exercise-induced pulmonary hemorrhage) and gastric ulcers.<sup id="cite_ref-animalaid_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-animalaid-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Animal rights groups are also primarily concerned that certain sports or training exercises may cause unnecessary pain or injuries to horse athletes. Some specific training or showing practices are so widely condemned that they have been made illegal at the national level and violations can incur criminal penalties. The most well known is <i>soring,</i> a practice of applying a caustic ointment just above the hooves of a <a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Walking_Horse" title="Tennessee Walking Horse">Tennessee Walking Horse</a> to make it pick up its feet higher. However, in spite of a federal law in the United States prohibiting this practice and routine inspections of horse shows by inspectors from the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">United States Department of Agriculture</a>, soring is still widespread and difficult to eliminate.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Some events themselves are also considered so abusive that they are banned in many countries. Among these are horse-tripping, a sport where riders chase and rope a loose-running horse by its front legs, throwing it to the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Secondary effects of racing have also recently been uncovered. A 2006 investigation by <a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a> in the UK found that each year 6,000–10,000 horses are slaughtered for consumption abroad, a significant proportion of which are horses bred for racing.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> A boom in the number of foals bred has meant that there are not adequate resources to care for unwanted horses. Demand has increased for this massive breeding programme to be scaled back.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Despite over 1000 foals being produced annually by the Thoroughbred <a href="/wiki/Horse_industry" title="Horse industry">horse industry</a>, 66% of those bred for such a purpose were never entered into a race, and despite a life expectancy of 30 years, many are killed before their fifth birthday.<sup id="cite_ref-guardian_49-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Horse_riding_on_coinage">Horse riding on coinage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equestrianism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Horse riding on coinage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Horse riding events have been selected as a main motif in numerous collectors' coins. One of the recent samples is the €10 Greek <a href="/wiki/Euro_gold_and_silver_commemorative_coins_(Greece)#2003_coinage" title="Euro gold and silver commemorative coins (Greece)">Horse Riding commemorative coin</a>, minted in 2003 to commemorate the <a href="/wiki/2004_Summer_Olympics" title="2004 Summer Olympics">2004 Summer Olympics</a>. On the composition of the obverse of this coin, the modern horseman is pictured as he jumps over an obstacle, while in the background the ancient horseman is inspired by a representation on a black-figure vase of the 5th century BC.
</p><p>For the 2012 Olympics, the Royal Mint has produced a 50p coin showing a horse jumping a fence.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A mounted rider from the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Canadian_Mounted_Police" title="Royal Canadian Mounted Police">Royal Canadian Mounted Police</a> is featured on a 1973 Canadian quarter dollar coin.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
