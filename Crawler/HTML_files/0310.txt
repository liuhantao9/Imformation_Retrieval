<p>A <i><b>cognomen</b></i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/ɒ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;body&#39;">ɒ</span><span title="/ɡ/: &#39;g&#39; in &#39;guy&#39;">ɡ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/oʊ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;code&#39;">oʊ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <small>Classical Latin:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Latin" title="Help:IPA/Latin">[koːŋˈnoːmen]</a></span>; Latin plural <i><b>cognomina</b></i>; from <i>con-</i> "together with" and <i>(g)nomen</i> "name") was the third name of a citizen of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Rome</a>, under <a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions" title="Roman naming conventions">Roman naming conventions</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Initially, it was a <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nickname</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> but lost that purpose when it became hereditary.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Hereditary <i>cognomina</i> were used to augment the second name, the <i><a href="/wiki/Gens" title="Gens">gens</a></i> (the <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a>, or clan name), in order to identify a particular branch within a family or family within a clan. The term has also taken on other contemporary meanings.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_names">Roman names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cognomen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Roman names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions" title="Roman naming conventions">Roman naming conventions</a></div>
<p>Because of the limited nature of the Latin <i><a href="/wiki/Praenomen" title="Praenomen">praenomen</a></i>, the <i>cognomen</i> developed to distinguish branches of the family from one another, and occasionally, to highlight an individual's achievement, typically in warfare. One example of this is <a href="/wiki/Pompey" title="Pompey">Gnaeus Pompeius Magnus</a>, whose cognomen <i>Magnus</i> was earned after his military victories under <a href="/wiki/Sulla" title="Sulla">Sulla</a>'s dictatorship. The <i>cognomen</i> was a form of distinguishing people who made important feats, and those who already bore a <i>cognomen</i> were awarded another exclusive name, the <a href="/wiki/Agnomen" title="Agnomen">agnomen</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Scipio_Africanus" title="Scipio Africanus">Publius Cornelius Scipio</a> received the agnomen <i>Africanus</i> after his victory over the Carthaginian general <a href="/wiki/Hannibal" title="Hannibal">Hannibal</a> at Zama, Africa (<i>Africanus</i> here means "of Africa" in the sense that his fame derives from Africa, rather than being born in Africa, which would have been <i>Afer</i>); and the same procedure occurred in the names of <a href="/wiki/Quintus_Caecilius_Metellus_Numidicus" title="Quintus Caecilius Metellus Numidicus">Quintus Caecilius Metellus <i>Numidicus</i></a> (conqueror of Numidia) and <a href="/wiki/Quintus_Caecilius_Metellus_Macedonicus" title="Quintus Caecilius Metellus Macedonicus">Quintus Caecilius Metellus <i>Macedonicus</i></a>.
</p><p>In contrast to the honorary <i>cognomina</i> adopted by successful generals, most <i>cognomina</i> were based on a physical or personality quirk; for example, <i>Rufus</i> meaning "<a href="/wiki/Red_hair" title="Red hair">red-haired</a>" or <i><a href="/wiki/Mucia_(gens)" title="Mucia (gens)">Scaevola</a></i> meaning "<a href="/wiki/Left-handedness" class="mw-redirect" title="Left-handedness">left-handed</a>". Some cognomina were hereditary (such as <a href="/wiki/Caesar_(title)" title="Caesar (title)">Caesar</a> among a branch of the <a href="/wiki/Julia_(gens)" title="Julia (gens)">Julii</a>, Brutus and Silanus among the <a href="/wiki/Junia_(gens)" title="Junia (gens)">Junii</a>, or Pilius and <a href="/wiki/Caecilii_Metelli_family_tree" title="Caecilii Metelli family tree">Metellus</a> among the <a href="/wiki/Caecilia_(gens)" title="Caecilia (gens)">Caecilii</a>): others tended to be individual. And some names appear to have been used both as <i>praenomen</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Agnomen" title="Agnomen">agnomen</a></i>, or non-hereditary <i>cognomen</i>. For instance, <i>Vopiscus</i> was used as both <i>praenomen</i> and <i>cognomen</i> in the Julii Caesares; likewise <i>Nero</i> among the early imperial <a href="/wiki/Claudia_(gens)" title="Claudia (gens)">Claudii</a>, several of whom used the traditional hereditary Claudian cognomen as a praenomen.
</p><p>The upper-class usually used the <i>cognomen</i> to refer to one another.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Today, we refer to many prominent ancient Romans by only their <i>cognomen</i>; for example, <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a> (from <i>cicer</i> "<a href="/wiki/Chickpea" title="Chickpea">chickpea</a>") serves as a shorthand for Marcus Tullius Cicero, and <a href="/wiki/Etymology_of_the_name_of_Julius_Caesar" class="mw-redirect" title="Etymology of the name of Julius Caesar">Caesar</a> for <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Gaius Julius Caesar</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_a_contemporary_term">As a contemporary term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cognomen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: As a contemporary term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nickname</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epithet" title="Epithet">epithet</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Clan_name_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Clan name (disambiguation)">clan name</a></div>
<p>The term "cognomen" (sometimes pluralized "cognomens") has come into use as an English noun used outside the context of Ancient Rome. According to the 2012 edition of the <i><a href="/wiki/Random_House_Dictionary" class="mw-redirect" title="Random House Dictionary">Random House Dictionary</a></i>, cognomen can mean a "surname" or "any name, especially a nickname".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The basic sense in English is "how one is well known". For example <a href="/wiki/Alfred_the_Great" title="Alfred the Great">Alfred the Great</a>. (This is more similar to the Roman use of <a href="/wiki/Agnomen" title="Agnomen">agnomen</a> than their use of cognomen.)
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">Catalan</a> <i>cognom</i> and <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> <i>cognome,</i> derived from the Latin <i>cognomen</i>, mean "family name". <a href="/wiki/Maltese_language" title="Maltese language">Maltese</a> <i>kunjom</i> is derived from the Italian version, retaining the same meaning.
</p><p>The term "cognomen" can also be applied to cultures with a clan structure and naming conventions comparable to those of Ancient Rome; thus, hereditary "cognomina" have been described as in use among the  <a href="/wiki/Xhosa_people" title="Xhosa people">Xhosa</a> (<a href="/wiki/Iziduko" class="mw-redirect" title="Iziduko">Iziduko</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Igbo_people" title="Igbo people">Igbo</a> (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Aha_Otutu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Aha Otutu (page does not exist)">Aha Otutu</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Yoruba_people" title="Yoruba people">Yoruba</a> (<a href="/wiki/Oriki" class="mw-redirect" title="Oriki">Oriki</a>), or the <a href="/wiki/Zulu_people" title="Zulu people">Zulu</a> (<a href="/wiki/Isibongo" title="Isibongo">Isibongo</a>).
</p>
