<p>The <b>Bibliothèque nationale de France</b> (<b>BnF</b>, English: National Library of France; <small>French:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[bi.bli.jɔ.tɛk na.sjɔ.nal də fʁɑ̃s]</a></span>) is the <a href="/wiki/National_library" title="National library">national library</a> of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, located in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>. It is the national repository of all that is published in France and also holds extensive historical collections.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biblioth%C3%A8que_nationale_de_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The National Library of France traces its origin to the royal library founded at the <a href="/wiki/Louvre_Palace" title="Louvre Palace">Louvre Palace</a> by <a href="/wiki/Charles_V_of_France" title="Charles V of France">Charles V</a> in 1368. Charles had received a collection of manuscripts from his predecessor, <a href="/wiki/John_II_of_France" title="John II of France">John II</a>, and transferred them to the Louvre from the <a href="/wiki/Palais_de_la_Cit%C3%A9" title="Palais de la Cité">Palais de la Cité</a>. The first librarian of record was Claude Mallet, the king's valet de chambre, who made a sort of catalogue, <i>Inventoire des Livres du Roy nostre Seigneur estans au Chastel du Louvre</i>. Jean Blanchet made another list in 1380 and Jean de Bégue one in 1411 and another in 1424. Charles V was a patron of learning and encouraged the making and collection of books. It is known that he employed <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Oresme" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicholas Oresme">Nicholas Oresme</a>, Raoul de Presle and others to transcribe ancient texts. At the death of <a href="/wiki/Charles_VI_of_France" title="Charles VI of France">Charles VI</a>, this first collection was unilaterally bought by the English regent of France, the <a href="/wiki/John_of_Lancaster,_1st_Duke_of_Bedford" title="John of Lancaster, 1st Duke of Bedford">Duke of Bedford</a>, who transferred it to England in 1424. It was apparently dispersed at his death in 1435.<sup id="cite_ref-priebe_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-priebe-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ea_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ea-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Charles_VII_of_France" title="Charles VII of France">Charles VII</a> did little to repair the loss of these books, but the invention of printing resulted in the starting of another collection in the Louvre inherited by <a href="/wiki/Louis_XI_of_France" title="Louis XI of France">Louis XI</a> in 1461. <a href="/wiki/Charles_VIII_of_France" title="Charles VIII of France">Charles VIII</a> seized a part of the collection of the kings of <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">Aragon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-staikos2012_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-staikos2012-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Louis_XII_of_France" title="Louis XII of France">Louis XII</a>, who had inherited the library at <a href="/wiki/Blois" title="Blois">Blois</a>, incorporated the latter into the <i>Bibliothèque du Roi</i> and further enriched it with the <a href="/wiki/Louis_de_Gruuthuse" title="Louis de Gruuthuse">Gruthuyse</a> collection and with plunder from <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">Milan</a>. <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">Francis I</a> transferred the collection in 1534 to <a href="/wiki/Fontainebleau" title="Fontainebleau">Fontainebleau</a> and merged it with his private library. During his reign, fine bindings became the craze and many of the books added by him and <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_France" title="Henry II of France">Henry II</a> are masterpieces of the binder's art.<sup id="cite_ref-ea_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ea-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Under librarianship of <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Amyot" title="Jacques Amyot">Amyot</a>, the collection was transferred to Paris during which process many treasures were lost. <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_France" title="Henry IV of France">Henry IV</a> again moved it to the <a href="/wiki/Coll%C3%A8ge_de_Clermont" class="mw-redirect" title="Collège de Clermont">Collège de Clermont</a> and in 1604 it was housed in the <a href="/wiki/Rue_de_la_Harpe" title="Rue de la Harpe">Rue de la Harpe</a>. The appointment of <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Auguste_de_Thou" title="Jacques Auguste de Thou">Jacques Auguste de Thou</a> as librarian initiated a period of development that made it the largest and richest collection of books in the world. He was succeeded by his son who was replaced, when executed for treason, by <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Bignon" title="Jérôme Bignon">Jérôme Bignon</a>, the first of a line of librarians of the same name. Under de Thou, the library was enriched by the collections of Queen <a href="/wiki/Catherine_de_Medici" class="mw-redirect" title="Catherine de Medici">Catherine de Medici</a>. The library grew rapidly during the reigns of <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIII_of_France" title="Louis XIII of France">Louis XIII</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV</a>, due in great part to the interest of the Minister of Finance, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Colbert" title="Jean-Baptiste Colbert">Colbert</a>, an indefatigable collectors of books.<sup id="cite_ref-ea_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ea-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The quarters in the Rue de la Harpe becoming inadequate, the library was again moved, in 1666, to a more spacious house in Rue Vivienne. The minister <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Michel_le_Tellier,_Marquis_de_Louvois" title="François-Michel le Tellier, Marquis de Louvois">Louvois</a> took quite as much interest in the library as Colbert and during his administration a magnificent building to be erected in the Place Vendôme was planned. The death of Louvois, however, prevented the realization of this plan. Louvois employed <a href="/wiki/Jean_Mabillon" title="Jean Mabillon">Mabillon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melchis%C3%A9dech_Th%C3%A9venot" title="Melchisédech Thévenot">Thévenot</a> and others to procure books from every source. In 1688, a catalogue in eight volumes was compiled.<sup id="cite_ref-ea_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ea-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The library opened to the public in 1692, under the administration of <a href="/wiki/Camille_le_Tellier_de_Louvois" title="Camille le Tellier de Louvois">Abbé Louvois</a>, Minister Louvois's son. Abbé Louvois was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Paul_Bignon" title="Jean-Paul Bignon">Jean-Paul Bignon</a>, who instituted a complete reform of the library's system. Catalogues were made which appeared from 1739 to 1753 in 11 volumes. The collections increased steadily by purchase and gift to the outbreak of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>, at which time it was in grave danger of partial or total destruction, but owing to the activities of <a href="/wiki/Antoine-Augustin_Renouard" title="Antoine-Augustin Renouard">Antoine-Augustin Renouard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Van_Praet" title="Joseph Van Praet">Joseph Van Praet</a> it suffered no injury.<sup id="cite_ref-ea_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ea-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The library's collections swelled to over 300,000 volumes during the <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_(France)" class="mw-redirect" title="Legislative Assembly (France)">radical phase</a> of the French Revolution when the private libraries of aristocrats and clergy were seized. After the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/French_First_Republic" title="French First Republic">French First Republic</a> in September 1792, "the Assembly declared the <i>Bibliotheque du Roi</i> to be national property and the institution was renamed the <i>Bibliothèque Nationale</i>. After four centuries of control by the Crown, this great library now became the property of the French people."<sup id="cite_ref-priebe_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-priebe-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:France,_Paris,_Biblioth%C3%A8que_nationale_de_France,_site_Richelieu,_salle_ovale.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/France%2C_Paris%2C_Biblioth%C3%A8que_nationale_de_France%2C_site_Richelieu%2C_salle_ovale.jpg/220px-France%2C_Paris%2C_Biblioth%C3%A8que_nationale_de_France%2C_site_Richelieu%2C_salle_ovale.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/France%2C_Paris%2C_Biblioth%C3%A8que_nationale_de_France%2C_site_Richelieu%2C_salle_ovale.jpg/330px-France%2C_Paris%2C_Biblioth%C3%A8que_nationale_de_France%2C_site_Richelieu%2C_salle_ovale.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/France%2C_Paris%2C_Biblioth%C3%A8que_nationale_de_France%2C_site_Richelieu%2C_salle_ovale.jpg/440px-France%2C_Paris%2C_Biblioth%C3%A8que_nationale_de_France%2C_site_Richelieu%2C_salle_ovale.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4368" data-file-height="2912" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:France,_Paris,_Biblioth%C3%A8que_nationale_de_France,_site_Richelieu,_salle_ovale.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reading room, Richelieu site</div></div></div>
<p>A new administrative organization was established. <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a> took great interest in the library and among other things issued an order that all books in provincial libraries not possessed by the <i>Bibliothèque Nationale</i> should be forwarded to it, subject to replacement by exchanges of equal value from the duplicate collections, making it possible, as Napoleon said, to find a copy of any book in France in the National Library. Napoleon furthermore increased the collections by spoil from his conquests. A considerable number of these books was restored after his downfall. During the period from 1800 to 1836, the library was virtually under the control of Joseph Van Praet. At his death it contained more than 650,000 printed books and some 80,000 manuscripts.<sup id="cite_ref-ea_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ea-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following a series of regime changes in France, it became the <b>Imperial National Library</b> and in 1868 was moved to newly constructed buildings on the <a href="/wiki/Rue_de_Richelieu" title="Rue de Richelieu">Rue de Richelieu</a> designed by <a href="/wiki/Henri_Labrouste" title="Henri Labrouste">Henri Labrouste</a>. Upon Labrouste's death in 1875 the library was further expanded, including the grand staircase and the Oval Room, by academic architect <a href="/wiki/Jean-Louis_Pascal" title="Jean-Louis Pascal">Jean-Louis Pascal</a>. In 1896, the library was still the largest repository of books in the world, although it has since been surpassed by other libraries for that title.<sup id="cite_ref-world_and_its_people_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-world_and_its_people-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
By 1920, the library's collection had grown to 4,050,000 volumes and 11,000 manuscripts.<sup id="cite_ref-ea_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ea-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>M. Henri Lemaître, a vice-president of the French Library Association and formerly librarian of the Bibliothèque Nationale&#160;... outlined the story of French libraries and librarians during the German occupation, a record of destruction and racial discrimination. During 1940–1945, more than two million books were lost through the ravages of war, many of them forming the irreplaceable local collections in which France abounded. Many thousands of books, including complete libraries, were seized by the Germans. Yet French librarians stood firm against all threats, and continued to serve their readers to the best of their abilities. In their private lives and in their professional occupations they were in the van of the struggle against the Nazis, and many suffered imprisonment and death for their devotion. Despite <a href="/wiki/Nazi" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> opposition they maintained a supply of books to French <a href="/wiki/Prisoners_of_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Prisoners of war">prisoners of war</a>. They continued to supply books on various proscribed lists to trustworthy readers; and when liberation came, they were ready with their plans for rehabilitation with the creation of new book centres for the French people on lines of the English county library system.<sup id="cite_ref-Nature_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nature-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_buildings">New buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biblioth%C3%A8que_nationale_de_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: New buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Paris_13e_BNF_Site_Fran%C3%A7ois-Mitterrand_746.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Paris_13e_BNF_Site_Fran%C3%A7ois-Mitterrand_746.jpg/220px-Paris_13e_BNF_Site_Fran%C3%A7ois-Mitterrand_746.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Paris_13e_BNF_Site_Fran%C3%A7ois-Mitterrand_746.jpg/330px-Paris_13e_BNF_Site_Fran%C3%A7ois-Mitterrand_746.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Paris_13e_BNF_Site_Fran%C3%A7ois-Mitterrand_746.jpg/440px-Paris_13e_BNF_Site_Fran%C3%A7ois-Mitterrand_746.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4896" data-file-height="3264" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Paris_13e_BNF_Site_Fran%C3%A7ois-Mitterrand_746.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View of the Bibliothèque nationale de France, François-Mitterrand site</div></div></div>
<p>On 14 July 1988, <a href="/wiki/President_of_France" title="President of France">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Mitterrand" title="François Mitterrand">François Mitterrand</a> announced "the construction and the expansion of one of the largest and most modern libraries in the world, intended to cover all fields of knowledge, and designed to be accessible to all, using the most modern data transfer technologies, which could be consulted from a distance, and which would collaborate with other European libraries". Book and media <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">logistics</a> inside the whole library was planned with an automated 6.6&#160;km (4.1&#160;mi) <a href="/wiki/Telelift" title="Telelift">Telelift</a> system. Only with this high level of <a href="/wiki/Automation" title="Automation">automation</a>, the library can comply with all demands fully in time. Due to initial trade union opposition, a wireless network was fully installed only in August 2016.
</p><p>In July 1989, the services of the architectural firm of <a href="/wiki/Dominique_Perrault" title="Dominique Perrault">Dominique Perrault</a> were retained. The design was recognized with the <a href="/wiki/European_Union_Prize_for_Contemporary_Architecture" title="European Union Prize for Contemporary Architecture">European Union Prize for Contemporary Architecture</a> in 1996. The construction was carried out by <a href="/wiki/Bouygues" title="Bouygues">Bouygues</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
Construction of the library ran into huge cost overruns and technical difficulties related to its high-rise design, so much so that it was referred to as the "TGB" or "<i>Très Grande Bibliothèque</i>" (i.e. "Very Large Library," a sarcastic allusion to France's successful high-speed rail system, the <a href="/wiki/TGV" title="TGV">TGV</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-VisionOutdate_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VisionOutdate-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  After the move of the major collections from the <a href="/wiki/Rue_de_Richelieu" title="Rue de Richelieu">Rue de Richelieu</a>, the National Library of France was inaugurated on 15 December 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-Ramsay2003_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ramsay2003-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biblioth%C3%A8que_nationale_de_France&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, the BnF contained roughly 14 million books at its four Parisian sites (Tolbiac, Richelieu, Arsenal, Opéra) as well as printed documents, manuscripts, prints, photographs, maps and plans, scores, coins, medals, sound documents, video and multimedia documents, scenery elements..."<sup id="cite_ref-BnF_main_page_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BnF_main_page-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The library retains the use of the Rue de Richelieu complex for some of its collections.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mission">Mission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biblioth%C3%A8que_nationale_de_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The National Library of France is a public establishment under the supervision of the <a href="/wiki/French_Ministry_of_Culture" class="mw-redirect" title="French Ministry of Culture">Ministry of Culture</a>. Its mission is to constitute collections, especially the copies of works published in France that must, by law, be deposited there, conserve them, and make them available to the public. It produces a reference catalogue, cooperates with other national and international establishments, and participates in research programs.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manuscript_collection">Manuscript collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biblioth%C3%A8que_nationale_de_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Manuscript collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="Manuscripts"></span>
The  Manuscripts department houses the largest collection of medieval and modern manuscripts worldwide.
The collection includes medieval  <a href="/wiki/Chansons_de_geste" class="mw-redirect" title="Chansons de geste">chansons de geste</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chivalric_romance" title="Chivalric romance">chivalric romances</a>,  eastern literature, eastern and western religions, ancient history, scientific history, and literary manuscripts by Pascal, Diderot, Apollinaire, Proust, Colette, Sartre, etc. 
The collection is organised:
</p>
<ul><li>according to language (Ancient Greek, Latin, French and other European languages, Arabic, Coptic, Ethiopian, Hebrew, Persian, Turkish, Near- and Middle-Eastern languages, Chinese, Japanese, Tibetan, Sanskrit, Indian languages, Vietnamese, etc.)
<ul><li>The library holds about 5,000 Ancient Greek manuscripts, which are divided into three <i>fonds</i>: <i>Ancien fonds grec</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Fonds_Coislin" title="Fonds Coislin">fonds Coislin</a></i>, and <i>Fonds du Supplément grec</i>.</li></ul></li>
<li>according to content: learned and bibliophilic, collections of learned materials, Library Archives, genealogical collections, French provinces, Masonic collection, etc.</li></ul>
