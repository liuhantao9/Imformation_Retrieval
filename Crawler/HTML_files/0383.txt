<p>A <b>signature</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="/ɡ/: &#39;g&#39; in &#39;guy&#39;">ɡ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="/tʃ/: &#39;ch&#39; in &#39;China&#39;">tʃ</span><span title="/ər/: &#39;er&#39; in &#39;letter&#39;">ər</span></span>/</a></span></span>; from <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">signare</i>, "to sign") is a handwritten (and often stylized) depiction of someone's name, nickname, or even a simple "X" or other mark that a person writes on documents as a proof of identity and intent. The writer of a signature is a <b>signatory</b> or <b>signer</b>. Similar to a handwritten signature, a <b>signature work</b> describes the work as readily identifying its creator. A signature may be confused with an <a href="/wiki/Autograph" title="Autograph">autograph</a>, which is chiefly an artistic signature. This can lead to confusion when people have both an autograph and signature and as such some people in the public eye keep their signatures private whilst fully publishing their autograph.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function_and_types">Function and types</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Autograph_of_Benjamin_Franklin.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Autograph_of_Benjamin_Franklin.svg/220px-Autograph_of_Benjamin_Franklin.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="107" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Autograph_of_Benjamin_Franklin.svg/330px-Autograph_of_Benjamin_Franklin.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Autograph_of_Benjamin_Franklin.svg/440px-Autograph_of_Benjamin_Franklin.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="860" data-file-height="419" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Autograph_of_Benjamin_Franklin.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Signature of <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a>.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Farah_Pahlavi_signature.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Farah_Pahlavi_signature.svg/180px-Farah_Pahlavi_signature.svg.png" decoding="async" width="180" height="119" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Farah_Pahlavi_signature.svg/270px-Farah_Pahlavi_signature.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Farah_Pahlavi_signature.svg/360px-Farah_Pahlavi_signature.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="621" data-file-height="410" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Farah_Pahlavi_signature.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Signature of <a href="/wiki/Shahbanu" title="Shahbanu">Empress</a> <a href="/wiki/Farah_Pahlavi" title="Farah Pahlavi">Farah Pahlavi</a> of <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> in <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian Handwriting</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The traditional function of a signature is to permanently affix to a document a person’s uniquely personal, undeniable self-identification as physical evidence of that person's personal witness and certification of the content of all, or a specified part, of the document.  For example, the role of a signature in many consumer contracts is not solely to provide evidence of the identity of the contracting party, but also to provide evidence of deliberation and informed consent. In many countries, signatures may be witnessed and recorded in the presence of a <a href="/wiki/Notary_public" title="Notary public">notary public</a> to carry additional legal force. On legal documents, an illiterate signatory can make a "mark" (often an "X" but occasionally a personalized symbol), so long as the document is countersigned by a literate witness. In some countries, illiterate people place a thumbprint on legal documents in lieu of a written signature.
</p><p>In the United States, signatures encompass marks and actions of all sorts that are indicative of identity and intent. The legal rule is that unless a statute specifically prescribes a particular method of making a signature it may be made in any number of ways. These include by a mechanical or rubber stamp facsimile. A signature may be made by the purported signatory; alternatively someone else duly authorized by the signatory, acting in the signer's presence and at the signatory's direction, may make the signature.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many individuals have much more fanciful signatures than their normal <a href="/wiki/Cursive" title="Cursive">cursive</a> writing, including elaborate <a href="/wiki/Ascender_(typography)" title="Ascender (typography)">ascenders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Descender" title="Descender">descenders</a> and exotic <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/flourish" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:flourish">flourishes</a>, much as one would find in <a href="/wiki/Calligraphy" title="Calligraphy">calligraphic</a> writing. As an example, the final "k" in <a href="/wiki/John_Hancock" title="John Hancock">John Hancock</a>'s famous signature on the US Declaration of Independence loops back to underline his name. This kind of flourish is also known as a <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/paraph" class="extiw" title="wikt:paraph">paraph</a></i>.
</p><p><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/paraphe" class="extiw" title="wikt:paraphe">Paraphe</a> is a term meaning flourish, initial or signature in <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The paraph is used in <a href="/wiki/Graphology" title="Graphology">graphology</a> analyses.
</p><p>Several cultures whose languages use writing systems other than alphabets do not share the Western notion of signatures per se: the "signing" of one's name results in a written product no different from the result of "writing" one's name in the standard way. For these languages, to write or to sign involves the same written characters. Also see <a href="/wiki/Calligraphy" title="Calligraphy">Calligraphy</a>.
</p>

<p>Special signature machines, called <a href="/wiki/Autopen" title="Autopen">autopens</a>, are capable of automatically reproducing an individual's signature.  These are typically used by people required to sign a lot of printed matter, such as celebrities, heads of state or CEOs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> More recently, <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Congress" title="Member of Congress">Members of Congress</a> in the United States have begun having their signature made into a <a href="/wiki/TrueType" title="TrueType">TrueType</a> font file. This allows staff members in the Congressman's office to easily reproduce it on correspondence, legislation, and official documents.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>In the East Asian languages of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>, people traditionally use stamp-like objects known as <i>name-<a href="/wiki/Seal_(East_Asia)" title="Seal (East Asia)">seals</a></i> with the name carved in <i>tensho</i> script (<i><a href="/wiki/Seal_script" title="Seal script">seal script</a></i>) in lieu of a handwritten signature.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wet_signatures">Wet signatures</span></h3>
<p>Some government agencies require that professional persons or official reviewers sign originals and all copies of originals to authenticate that they personally viewed the content. In the United States this is prevalent with architectural and construction plans. Its intent is to prevent mistakes or fraud but the practice is not known to be effective.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Online_usage">Online usage</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Signature_block" title="Signature block">Signature block</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/E-mail" class="mw-redirect" title="E-mail">e-mail</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newsgroup" class="mw-redirect" title="Newsgroup">newsgroup</a> usage, another type of signature exists which is independent of one's language. Users can set one or more lines of custom text known as a <a href="/wiki/Signature_block" title="Signature block">signature block</a> to be automatically appended to their messages. This text usually includes a name, contact information, and sometimes quotations and <a href="/wiki/ASCII_art" title="ASCII art">ASCII art</a>. A shortened form of a signature block, only including one's name, often with some distinguishing prefix, can be used to simply indicate the end of a <a href="/wiki/Posting_style" title="Posting style">post</a> or response. Some web sites also allow graphics to be used. Note, however, that this type of signature is not related to <a href="/wiki/Electronic_signature" title="Electronic signature">electronic signatures</a> or <a href="/wiki/Digital_signature" title="Digital signature">digital signatures</a>, which are more technical in nature and not directly understandable by humans. On <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">Wikipedia</a>, an online <a href="/wiki/Wiki" title="Wiki">wiki</a>-based encyclopedia edited by volunteers, the contributors "sign" their comments on talk pages with their username (only the username holder has the right to digitally affix their signature).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span></h2>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Signature&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Vermeer_autograph.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Vermeer_autograph.svg/220px-Vermeer_autograph.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="119" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Vermeer_autograph.svg/330px-Vermeer_autograph.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Vermeer_autograph.svg/440px-Vermeer_autograph.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="180" data-file-height="97" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vermeer_autograph.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Vermeer" class="mw-redirect" title="Vermeer">Vermeer</a>'s signature</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>signature</b> on a painting or other work of art has always been an important item in the assessment of art. Fake signatures are sometimes added to enhance the value of a painting, or are added to a fake painting to support its authenticity. A notorious case was the signature of <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Vermeer" title="Johannes Vermeer">Johannes Vermeer</a> on the fake "Supper at Emmaus" made by the art-forger <a href="/wiki/Han_van_Meegeren" title="Han van Meegeren">Han van Meegeren</a>. However, the fact that painters' signatures often vary over time (particularly in the modern and contemporary periods) might complicate the issue. The signatures of some painters take on an artistic form that may be of less value in determining forgeries.
</p><p>The term "signature" is also used to mean the characteristics that give an object, or a piece of information, its identity—for example, the shape of a <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">Coca-Cola</a> bottle. In <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock music</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">heavy metal music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electric_guitar" title="Electric guitar">electric guitarists</a> develop a unique tone and sound using particular settings on their <a href="/wiki/Guitar_amp" class="mw-redirect" title="Guitar amp">guitar amp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Effects_unit" title="Effects unit">effects units</a> and modifications to their <a href="/wiki/Pickup_(music_technology)" title="Pickup (music technology)">guitar pickups</a> that is called their "signature sound". In wrestling such as <a href="/wiki/WWE" title="WWE">WWE</a>, wrestlers are known for distinctive "signature" finishing moves. <span id="hole"></span>In <a href="/wiki/Golf_course" title="Golf course">golf courses</a>, a "signature hole" is the most aesthetically pleasing and photogenic hole, which makes a particular course unique.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> By analogy, the word "signature" may be used to refer to the characteristic expression of a process or thing. For example, the climate phenomenon known as <a href="/wiki/ENSO" class="mw-redirect" title="ENSO">ENSO</a> or El Niño has characteristic modes in different ocean basins which are often referred to as the "signature" of Icie collier. A <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/signatory" class="extiw" title="wikt:signatory">signatory</a> indicates a party to an agreement, especially an international treaty or convention, <i>e.g.</i> <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> is a signatory to the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Biological_Diversity" title="Convention on Biological Diversity">Convention on Biological Diversity</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Copyright">Copyright</span></h2>
<p>Under British law, the appearance of signatures (not the names themselves) may be protected under <a href="/wiki/Copyright_law_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Copyright law of the United Kingdom">copyright law</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Spilsbury_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spilsbury-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Under <a href="/wiki/Copyright_law_of_the_United_States" title="Copyright law of the United States">United States copyright law</a>, "titles, names [I c...]; mere variations of typographic ornamentation, lettering, or coloring" are not eligible for copyright;<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> however, the appearance of signatures (not the names themselves) may be protected under copyright law.<sup id="cite_ref-Spilsbury_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spilsbury-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uniform_Commercial_Code">Uniform Commercial Code</span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Uniform_Commercial_Code" title="Uniform Commercial Code">Uniform Commercial Code</a> §1-201(37) of the United States generally defines <b>signed</b> as "using any symbol executed or adopted with present intention to adopt or accept a writing." The Uniform Commercial Code  §3-401(b) for <a href="/wiki/Negotiable_instruments" class="mw-redirect" title="Negotiable instruments">negotiable instruments</a> states "A signature may be made (i) manually or by means of a device or machine, and (ii) by the use of any name, including a trade or assumed name, or by a word, mark, or symbol executed or adopted by a person with present intention to authenticate a writing."
</p>
