<p><b>WHSmith PLC</b> (also known as <b>WHS</b> or colloquially as <b>Smith's</b>, and formerly <b>W. H. Smith &amp; Son</b>) is a British retailer, headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Swindon" title="Swindon">Swindon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a>, which operates a chain of <a href="/wiki/High_street" class="mw-redirect" title="High street">high street</a>, railway station, airport, port, hospital and motorway service station shops selling books, stationery, magazines, newspapers, entertainment products and confectionery.
</p><p>The company was formed by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Walton_Smith" title="Henry Walton Smith">Henry Walton Smith</a> and his wife Anna in 1792 as a news vendor in London. It remained under the ownership of the Smith family for many years and saw large-scale expansion during the 1970s as the company began to diversify into other markets. Following a rejected private equity takeover in 2004, the company began to focus on its core retail business. It was the first <a href="/wiki/Retail_chain" class="mw-redirect" title="Retail chain">retail chain</a> in the world, and was responsible for the creation of the <a href="/wiki/ISBN" class="mw-redirect" title="ISBN">ISBN</a> book identifier.
</p><p>WHSmith is listed on the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a> and is a constituent of the <a href="/wiki/FTSE_250_Index" title="FTSE 250 Index">FTSE 250 Index</a>. It celebrated its 225th anniversary in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHSmith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHSmith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WHSmith1.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/3c/WHSmith1.png/220px-WHSmith1.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="29" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/3/3c/WHSmith1.png 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="40" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WHSmith1.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The WHSmith logo until the early 1990s, featuring the then-familiar cube of letters, revived in the mid-2010s</div></div></div>
<p>In 1792, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Walton_Smith" title="Henry Walton Smith">Henry Walton Smith</a> and his wife Anna established the business as a news vendor in Little Grosvenor Street, London.<sup id="cite_ref-history3_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history3-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> After their deaths, the business&#8212;valued in 1812 at £1,280 (equivalent to £83,741&#32;in 2018)&#8212;was taken over by their youngest son <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Smith_(1792%E2%80%931865)" title="William Henry Smith (1792–1865)">William Henry Smith</a>, and in 1846 the firm became <b>W. H. Smith &amp; Son</b> when his only son, also <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Smith_(1825%E2%80%931891)" title="William Henry Smith (1825–1891)">William Henry</a>, became a partner.<sup id="cite_ref-history1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The firm took advantage of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_rail_transport_in_Great_Britain#1830–1922:_Early_development" title="History of rail transport in Great Britain">railway boom</a> by opening news-stands on <a href="/wiki/Railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway station">railway stations</a>, beginning with <a href="/wiki/Euston_Station" class="mw-redirect" title="Euston Station">Euston</a> in 1848.<sup id="cite_ref-history1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 1850, the firm opened depots in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-history1_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It also ran a <a href="/wiki/Lending_library" title="Lending library">circulating library</a> service for a century, from 1860 to 1961.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The younger W. H. Smith also used the success of the firm as a springboard into politics, becoming an <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> in 1868<sup id="cite_ref-history1_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and serving as a minister in several <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> governments.<sup id="cite_ref-history1_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the death of W. H. Smith the younger in 1891, his widow was created <a href="/wiki/Viscount_Hambleden" title="Viscount Hambleden">Viscountess Hambleden</a> in her own right;<sup id="cite_ref-history1_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> their son inherited the business from his father and the <a href="/wiki/Viscount" title="Viscount">Viscountcy</a> from his mother. After the death of the second Viscount in 1928, the business was reconstituted as a <a href="/wiki/Limited_company" title="Limited company">limited company</a>, in which his son, the third Viscount, owned all the ordinary shares.<sup id="cite_ref-history3_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history3-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> On the death of the third Viscount in 1948, the death duties were so severe that a public holding company had to be formed and shares sold to WHSmith staff and the public.<sup id="cite_ref-history3_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history3-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> A younger brother of the third Viscount remained chairman until 1972, but the Smith family's control slipped away, and the last family member left the board in 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-history3_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history3-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In 1966, WHSmith originated a 9-digit code for uniquely referencing books, called Standard Book Numbering or SBN. It was adopted as international standard ISO 2108 in 1970, and was used until 1974, when it became the <a href="/wiki/ISBN" class="mw-redirect" title="ISBN">ISBN</a> scheme.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion">Expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHSmith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From the 1970s, WHSmith began to expand into other retail sectors. WHSmith Travel operated from 1973<sup id="cite_ref-history3_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history3-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> to 1991. The <a href="/wiki/Do_It_All" title="Do It All">Do It All</a> chain of DIY shops originated with an acquisition in 1979,<sup id="cite_ref-history3_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history3-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> becoming a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Boots_UK" title="Boots UK">Boots</a> in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-history3_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history3-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Boots acquired WHSmith's share in June 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-history3_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history3-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The bookshop chain <a href="/wiki/Waterstone%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="Waterstone&#39;s">Waterstone's</a>, founded by former WHSmith executive <a href="/wiki/Tim_Waterstone" title="Tim Waterstone">Tim Waterstone</a> in 1982, was bought in 1989<sup id="cite_ref-history3_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history3-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and sold in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-history3_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history3-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1986, WHSmith bought a 75% controlling share of the <a href="/wiki/Our_Price" title="Our Price">Our Price</a> music chain;<sup id="cite_ref-history3_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history3-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> in the 1990s it also bought other music retailers including the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Group" title="Virgin Group">Virgin Group</a>'s smaller (non-<a href="/wiki/Virgin_Megastore" class="mw-redirect" title="Virgin Megastore">Megastore</a>) shops. The 75% share of Virgin Our Price was sold to Virgin Retail Group Ltd in July 1998 for £145m.<sup id="cite_ref-history3_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history3-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> WHSmith also owned the American record chain The Wall,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> which was sold to <a href="/wiki/Camelot_Music" title="Camelot Music">Camelot Music</a> in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 1998, the company acquired <a href="/wiki/John_Menzies" title="John Menzies">John Menzies</a>' retail outlets for £68m, which for many years were the main rival to the company's railway-station outlets. This purchase also cleared the way for WHSmith's retail expansion into Scotland. Prior to the takeover, Menzies' larger Scottish shops (carrying a very similar range of products to High Street WHSmith shops elsewhere) dominated the market, and the latter's presence was minimal.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Restructuring">Restructuring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHSmith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Restructuring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For several years, the company's retail side had difficulties competing with specialist book and music chains on one side and large supermarkets on the other. This led to poor financial performance, and a takeover bid in 2004 by <a href="/wiki/Permira" title="Permira">Permira</a>, which fell through.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> It reacted to this by disposing of its overseas subsidiaries<sup id="cite_ref-overseas_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-overseas-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and its publishing business <a href="/wiki/Hodder_Headline" class="mw-redirect" title="Hodder Headline">Hodder Headline</a>, in order to concentrate on reforming its core businesses.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In August 2006 the company demerged the retail and news distribution arms of the business into two separate companies: WHSmith PLC (retail) and <a href="/wiki/Smiths_News" class="mw-redirect" title="Smiths News">Smiths News PLC</a> (newspaper and magazine distribution).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In September 2010 WHSmith bought <a href="/wiki/The_Gadget_Shop" class="mw-redirect" title="The Gadget Shop">The Gadget Shop</a> from <a href="/wiki/The_Entertainer_(retailer)" title="The Entertainer (retailer)">The Entertainer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> That year, it also bought online greeting card retailer Funky Pigeon.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Since_2011">Since 2011</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHSmith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Since 2011">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Funky_Pigeon.com_shop,_Leeds_railway_station_(19th_July_2014).JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Funky_Pigeon.com_shop%2C_Leeds_railway_station_%2819th_July_2014%29.JPG/170px-Funky_Pigeon.com_shop%2C_Leeds_railway_station_%2819th_July_2014%29.JPG" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Funky_Pigeon.com_shop%2C_Leeds_railway_station_%2819th_July_2014%29.JPG/255px-Funky_Pigeon.com_shop%2C_Leeds_railway_station_%2819th_July_2014%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Funky_Pigeon.com_shop%2C_Leeds_railway_station_%2819th_July_2014%29.JPG/340px-Funky_Pigeon.com_shop%2C_Leeds_railway_station_%2819th_July_2014%29.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3456" data-file-height="4608" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Funky_Pigeon.com_shop,_Leeds_railway_station_(19th_July_2014).JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A WHSmith owned Funky Pigeon shop at <a href="/wiki/Leeds_railway_station" title="Leeds railway station">Leeds railway station</a></div></div></div>
<p>In April 2011, WHSmith agreed a deal with the legal services provider <a href="/wiki/QualitySolicitors" title="QualitySolicitors">QualitySolicitors</a> under which QualitySolicitors is to place representatives in up to 500 of its UK branches.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Past_Times" title="Past Times">Past Times</a> went into administration in January 2012, and the brand name was bought by WHSmith in March 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2013, WHSmith announced that it had bought the <a href="/wiki/ModelZone" class="mw-redirect" title="ModelZone">ModelZone</a> brand and will sell products under this brand through existing WHSmith shops.<sup id="cite_ref-WHSmith-profit_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHSmith-profit-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WHSmith-Clarke_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHSmith-Clarke-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-telegraph-WHSmith_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-telegraph-WHSmith-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> WHSmith subsequently announced through the ModelZone <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> page in November 2013 that 10 shops were to carry products under the ModelZone brand name by 23 November 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-twitter_13/11/13_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-twitter_13/11/13-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In October 2014, WHSmith announced as part of its preliminary statement that it was planning on extending its greetings card offering by launching the value focussed brand <i>Cardmarket</i> on a trial basis. According to the statement, these trial shops will be in low rent areas and will be let to WHSmith under short term leases.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The company announced in late 2018 that the trial of Cardmarket would be wound up, with the closure of the Cardmarket stores. This was in addition to the announcement of the closure for at least 6 WHSmith stores which were deemed economically unviable following a strategic business review. <sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Late in 2017 the Company purchased Cult Pens, a UK based retailer of stationery items, for an as yet undisclosed figure.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHSmith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>WHSmith founded one of the UK's earliest cable television channels, <a href="/wiki/Lifestyle_(British_TV_channel)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lifestyle (British TV channel)">Lifestyle</a>, which was carried on almost every cable system in the UK and Ireland prior to the start of <a href="/wiki/Sky_Television_plc" title="Sky Television plc">Sky Television</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-history3_4-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history3-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> By late 1984, the company had bought a 15% stake in <a href="/wiki/Screensport" class="mw-redirect" title="Screensport">Screensport</a> and from January 1986 took over the operations and management when <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a> and R Kennedy pulled out.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHSmith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHSmith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_old_Lion_and_Lamb_Public_House,_High_Street,_Brentwood._-_geograph.org.uk_-_50636.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/The_old_Lion_and_Lamb_Public_House%2C_High_Street%2C_Brentwood._-_geograph.org.uk_-_50636.jpg/170px-The_old_Lion_and_Lamb_Public_House%2C_High_Street%2C_Brentwood._-_geograph.org.uk_-_50636.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/The_old_Lion_and_Lamb_Public_House%2C_High_Street%2C_Brentwood._-_geograph.org.uk_-_50636.jpg/255px-The_old_Lion_and_Lamb_Public_House%2C_High_Street%2C_Brentwood._-_geograph.org.uk_-_50636.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/The_old_Lion_and_Lamb_Public_House%2C_High_Street%2C_Brentwood._-_geograph.org.uk_-_50636.jpg/340px-The_old_Lion_and_Lamb_Public_House%2C_High_Street%2C_Brentwood._-_geograph.org.uk_-_50636.jpg 2x" data-file-width="519" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_old_Lion_and_Lamb_Public_House,_High_Street,_Brentwood._-_geograph.org.uk_-_50636.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Brentwood,_Essex" title="Brentwood, Essex">Brentwood</a> High Street branch</div></div></div>
<p>Since 2007, the company has taken on a number of <a href="/wiki/Post_Office_Ltd" title="Post Office Ltd">Post Office</a> branches, mainly within its high street shops.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> By April 2016, this had reached 107, including former Crown Post Offices, with plans for an additional 61.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>WHSmith also operate a number of shops within hospitals, following its acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>-based newsagent chain United News in March 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to its existing joint ventures and franchise shops, the company trialed the smaller format, convenience-based WHSmith Local concept during 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Targeted at independent newsagents and post office business owners,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> a total of 40 such stores were trading and a further 40 planned by the time the 2015 annual report had been published.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since 2011, the company has also opened shops under its Funky Pigeon brand, offering stationery and personalised greetings cards.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International">International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHSmith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: International">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stansted_Airport_Shops_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1135179.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Stansted_Airport_Shops_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1135179.jpg/220px-Stansted_Airport_Shops_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1135179.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Stansted_Airport_Shops_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1135179.jpg/330px-Stansted_Airport_Shops_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1135179.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Stansted_Airport_Shops_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1135179.jpg/440px-Stansted_Airport_Shops_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1135179.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="492" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stansted_Airport_Shops_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1135179.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Stansted_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Stansted Airport">Stansted Airport</a> branch</div></div></div>
<p>Canadian operations began in 1950 and continued until 1989, when they were sold to domestic owners and renamed SmithBooks. SmithBooks later merged with <a href="/wiki/Coles_(bookstore)" title="Coles (bookstore)">Coles</a>, forming <a href="/wiki/Chapters_(bookstore)" title="Chapters (bookstore)">Chapters</a>, which retained the Coles and SmithBooks names and locations while also opening new namesake <a href="/wiki/Superstore" class="mw-redirect" title="Superstore">superstores</a>. Many SmithBooks locations were eventually closed or converted to Coles; a few locations still retain the name as of 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>WHSmith operated shops in the United States from 1985 until 2003, primarily in airports. The company acquired Australian and New Zealand subsidiaries in 2001 which were subsequently disposed of, along with those in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_International_Airport" title="Hong Kong International Airport">Hong Kong International Airport</a> (now as <a href="/wiki/Page_One_(bookstore)" title="Page One (bookstore)">Page One</a>) and in Singapore at <a href="/wiki/Changi_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Changi Airport">Changi Airport</a>, in 2004 (now Times Travel under the <a href="/wiki/Times_Bookstore" class="mw-redirect" title="Times Bookstore">Times Bookstore</a> banner).<sup id="cite_ref-overseas_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-overseas-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>WHSmith restarted its Australian operations in March 2011 following the collapse of <a href="/wiki/Borders_(Asia_Pacific)" title="Borders (Asia Pacific)">Angus &amp; Robertson/Borders</a> who held the naming rights in Australia. The first new shop was opened at <a href="/wiki/Melbourne_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Melbourne International Airport">Melbourne International Airport</a>, International Departures Terminal.  There are now three outlets at Melbourne Airport, three at Southern Cross Railway Station and one within the Melbourne Central Shopping Mall.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>WHSmith has opened shops in major Indian airports. WHSmith is currently in the process of planning 30 kiosk shops in China.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
Currently, WHSmith sponsor the IPL cricket team (<a href="/wiki/Sunrisers_Hyderabad" title="Sunrisers Hyderabad">Sunrisers Hyderabad</a>) (SRH) in India.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The company retains one shop on <a href="/wiki/Rue_de_Rivoli" title="Rue de Rivoli">Rue de Rivoli</a> in the centre of <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, France.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> In October 2008, WHSmith, together with SSP, opened five branches within <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen_Airport" title="Copenhagen Airport">Copenhagen Airport</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> and in April 2009 opened a branch in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm-Arlanda_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Stockholm-Arlanda Airport">Stockholm-Arlanda Airport</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009, WHSmith opened two shops in <a href="/wiki/Shannon_Airport" title="Shannon Airport">Shannon Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Clare" title="County Clare">County Clare</a>, Ireland. A further three shops are operated in Dublin Airport's Terminal Two, which opened in November 2010 and 5 shops in <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Airport" title="Dublin Airport">Dublin Airport</a>'s Terminal One, which opened in 2013. The chain promised when winning this latter contract to hire a full-time Irish book buyer; however, the appointment of an Australian, based in London and not in Dublin, drew adverse criticism.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, it opened an additional four shops at Dublin Airport's Terminal 1. Eason's, currently at T1 in Dublin, asked the airport operator to tender for a new contract one year earlier as the retailer blamed a fall in sales on the success of Terminal 2 at Dublin, which carried the majority of long haul traffic and long haul passengers tend to spend more on books.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>WHSmith opened four branches in <a href="/wiki/Helsinki_Airport" title="Helsinki Airport">Helsinki Airport</a>, Finland in late 2016 and early 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=WHSmith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 19 June 2009, WHSmith apologised after promoting a book on cellar rapist <a href="/wiki/Fritzl_case" title="Fritzl case">Josef Fritzl</a> as one of the "Top 50 Books for Dad" as a <a href="/wiki/Father%27s_Day" title="Father&#39;s Day">Father's Day</a> gift.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2012, WHSmith faced criticism from shooters after the sale of shooting magazines to children under 14 was banned, although it is legal for children under 14 to go shooting. The decision appeared to follow a campaign by animal rights activists. The British Association for Shooting and Conservation (BASC) campaigned against the ban, including a 12,000+ signature petition. In mid-November it emerged that the restrictions had been removed from all UK shooting magazines.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 14 October 2013, WHSmith took their website offline because "unacceptable titles were appearing on their website". These were e-books with themes of abuse.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The chain was criticised in 2014 for the condition of its shops, with both analysts and customers accusing the chain of under-investing in its estate.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015 an investigation by <i><a href="/wiki/The_Independent" title="The Independent">The Independent</a></i> revealed that WHSmith and other airport retailers were charging VAT to shoppers travelling outside the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, then claiming the <a href="/wiki/Value-added_tax" title="Value-added tax">VAT</a> back from the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">government</a> and not passing the refund on to customers.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> This was made possible by the practice of scanning customer's boarding passes at the till point - solely for the benefit of the company - which made the passengers unwitting accomplices in their own deception. After a public outcry, a customer revolt in which many refused to hand over their boarding passes, and an intervention by <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament</a>, the company confirmed in March 2017 that it would pass on the VAT reduction to customers spending over £6, who were travelling outside the EU.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, the company was also criticised for the prices charged in its branches in hospitals, after media investigations found some items to be on sale at significantly higher prices than in high street branches.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> In May 2018, WHSmith apologised after it was revealed that it has made more than £700 by selling single tubes of toothpaste for £7.99 through its branch in <a href="/wiki/Pinderfields_Hospital" title="Pinderfields Hospital">Pinderfields Hospital</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> The price was described as an 'error' and WHSmith promised that the proceeds from the sales would be donated to a local charity. The price was restored to £2.49, still more than three times the price of 80p charged in a nearby <a href="/wiki/Tesco" title="Tesco">Tesco</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
