	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Toponymic surname</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>toponymic surname</b> or <b>topographic surname</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> derived from a <a href="/wiki/Place_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Place name">place name</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kedar_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kedar-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> This can include specific locations, such as the individual's place of origin, residence, or of lands that they held, or can be more generic, derived from topographic features.<sup id="cite_ref-Shagir_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shagir-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Toponymic surnames originated as non-hereditary personal <a href="/wiki/By-name" class="mw-redirect" title="By-name">by-names</a>, and only subsequently came to be family names.  The origins of toponymic by-names have been attributed to two non-mutually exclusive trends.  One was to link the nobility to their places of origin and their feudal holdings and provide a marker of their status, while the other relates to the growth of the <a href="/wiki/Burgher_(title)" title="Burgher (title)">burgher class</a> in the cities, partly via migration from the countryside.  In London in the 13th century, toponymic surnames came to predominate. Also linked to this process was the increased popularity of the names of saints, leading to a reduction in the pool of given names used and the need or personal desire for by-names to distinguish increasing numbers of like-named individuals.<sup id="cite_ref-Shagir_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shagir-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some forms originally included a <a href="/wiki/Preposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Preposition">preposition</a>, such as <i>at</i>, <i>by</i>, <i>in</i>, or <i>of</i> (<i>de</i> in French and Spanish), subsequently dropped, as in "de Guzmán" (of Guzman) becoming simply Guzmán.  While the disappearance of the preposition has been linked to toponymic by-names becoming inherited family names, it predates this trend.  In England, this can be seen as early as the 11th century, and although there is some regional variation, a significant shift away from preposition usage can be seen to have occurred during the 14th century.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In some cases, the preposition has coalesced into the name,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> such as Atwood (at wood)<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and Daubney (originating as de Albigni, from <a href="/wiki/Saint-Martin-d%27Aubigny" title="Saint-Martin-d&#39;Aubigny">Saint-Martin-d'Aubigny</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">aristocratic</a> societies of Europe, both <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobiliary</a> and non-nobiliary forms of toponymic surnames exist, and in some languages a degree of differentiation evolved in their treatment. For nobles, the preposition evolved into a <a href="/wiki/Nobiliary_particle" title="Nobiliary particle">nobiliary particle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nobiliary_particle#France" title="Nobiliary particle">in French, for example,</a> a trend evolved in which non-nobiliary forms tended to fuse the preposition whereas nobiliary forms retained it as the discrete particle, although this was never an invariable practice. 
</p><p>Issues such as local pronunciation can cause toponymic surnames to take a form that varies significantly from the toponym that gave rise to them.  Examples include Wyndham, derived from <a href="/wiki/Wymondham" title="Wymondham">Wymondham</a>, Anster from <a href="/wiki/Anstruther" title="Anstruther">Anstruther</a>, and Badgerly from <a href="/wiki/Badgworthy_Water" title="Badgworthy Water">Badgworthy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One must be cautious to interpret a surname as toponymic based on its spelling alone, without knowing its history. A notable example is the name of <a href="/wiki/Jeanne_d%27Arc" class="mw-redirect" title="Jeanne d&#39;Arc">Jeanne d'Arc</a>, which is not related to a place called <i>Arc</i> but instead is a distorted <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> (see "<a href="/wiki/Name_of_Joan_of_Arc" title="Name of Joan of Arc">Name of Joan of Arc</a>").  Likewise, it has been suggested that a toponymic cannot be assumed to be a place of residence or origin: merchants could have adopted a toponymic by-name to associate themselves with a place where they never resided.<sup id="cite_ref-kedar_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kedar-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Polish, a toponymic surname may be created by adding "(w)ski" or "cki" at the end. For example, <a href="/wiki/Maliszewski" title="Maliszewski">Maliszewski</a> is a toponymic surname associated with one of the places in Poland named <a href="/wiki/Maliszew" title="Maliszew">Maliszew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maliszewo" title="Maliszewo">Maliszewo</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Malisz%C3%B3w" title="Maliszów">Maliszów</a>.  <sup id="cite_ref-DAFN_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAFN-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><br />
</p>
