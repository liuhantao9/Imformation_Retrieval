<p>A <b>Portuguese name</b> is typically composed of one or two <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given names</a>, and a number of <a href="/wiki/Family_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Family names">family names</a> (rarely one, but often two or three, seldom more). The first additional names are usually the mother's family surname(s) and the father's family surname(s). For practicality, usually only the last surname (<a href="#The_particle_&#39;de&#39;">excluding prepositions</a>) is used in formal greetings.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Portuguese naming system is quite flexible. Portuguese law establishes the need for a child to have at least one given name and one last name (surname) from one of the parents. The law also establishes the maximum number of names allowed: up to two given names and four surnames.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  This restriction is generally not enforced and it is not uncommon to have more than four surnames.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Usually, the maternal surnames precede the paternal ones, but the opposite is also possible.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  If the father is unknown, or he has not acknowledged the child, only the mother's family name(s) is/are used. A child can receive surnames from their parents' ancestors, even if those surnames are not part of the parents' names, provided that the parents prove those names were used by their ancestors.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most Portuguese-speaking people use only their last surname (usually the paternal one) in their daily and professional life. The regular usage of a middle surname or of a combination of two surnames is also widespread.
</p><p>Some Portuguese family names are made of two words, most often not hyphenated, but are not composite names, as they were not the result of combining two family names in past generations; instead, the words constitute a single logical unit. These include toponyms (e.g. Castelo Branco), religious references (e.g. Espírito Santo, Santa Rita), or other expressions (e.g. Corte Real, Mil-Homens). In this case both words must be cited (e.g. writer Camilo Castelo Branco is <i>never</i> referred to as Camilo Branco).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Number_of_names">Number of names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Number of names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is not uncommon in <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> that a married woman has two given names and six surnames, two from her mother's family, two from her father's family, and the last two coming from her husband. In addition, some of these names may be made of more than one word, so that a full feminine name can have more than 12 words. For instance, the name "<i>Maria do Carmo Mão de Ferro e Cunha de Almeida Santa Rita Santos Abreu</i>" would not be surprising in a married woman. <b>Mão de Ferro</b> (iron hand) and <b>Santa Rita</b> (after <a href="/wiki/Rita_of_Cascia" title="Rita of Cascia">Saint Rita of Cascia</a>) count only as one surname each. In this case, <b>Santos Abreu</b> would probably have come from this woman's husband.
</p><p>In Portugal, the custom of giving a child four last names is becoming popular, since this way a child can have each of their grandparents' last names. In Portugal and Brazil, some people view this as a sign of <a href="/wiki/Snobbery" class="mw-redirect" title="Snobbery">snobbery</a>, since it used to be the noble families who had a large number of given names. For instance, the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Pedro_I_of_Brazil" title="Pedro I of Brazil">Pedro I of Brazil</a> (also known as <a href="/wiki/Pedro_IV_of_Portugal" class="mw-redirect" title="Pedro IV of Portugal">Pedro IV of Portugal</a>) (1798–1834) had the full name of <i>Pedro de Alcântara Francisco Antônio João Carlos Xavier de Paula Miguel Rafael Joaquim José Gonzaga Pascoal Cipriano Serafim de <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">Bourbon</a> e <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bragan%C3%A7a" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Bragança">Bragança</a></i>, and his son, the Emperor <a href="/wiki/Pedro_II_of_Brazil" title="Pedro II of Brazil">Pedro II of Brazil</a>, had the full name of <i>Pedro de Alcântara João Carlos Leopoldo Salvador Bibiano Francisco Xavier de Paula Leocádio Miguel Gabriel Rafael Gonzaga de <a href="/wiki/House_of_Habsburg-Lothringen" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Habsburg-Lothringen">Habsburgo-Lorena</a> e <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bragan%C3%A7a" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Bragança">Bragança</a></i>. For the sake of simplicity, most Portuguese people have two surnames.
</p><p>For example, if <i>José Santos Almeida</i> and <i>Maria Abreu Melo</i> had a daughter, her name could simply be <i>Joana Melo Almeida</i> (given name + mother's last name + father's last name). However, they could give her two given names, for example <i>Joana Gabriela</i>, and combine their surnames in various ways, such has <i>Joana Gabriela Melo Almeida</i>, <i>Joana Gabriela Abreu Melo Almeida</i> (two surnames from the mother, one from the father), <i>Joana Gabriela Abreu Santos Almeida</i> (one name from the mother, two from the father), or even <i>Joana Gabriela Abreu Melo Santos Almeida</i> (two names from each parent). It would also be possible to use surnames that are not part of either parent's legal name, but which the parents would be entitled to use, i.e., a surname from a grandparent or a great-grandparent that was not transmitted to the father or the mother. This child would probably become known by her final surname, <i>Joana Almeida</i>. However, her parents could decide to change the order of surnames and name her <i>Joana Almeida Melo</i>, etc. In this case she would probably be known as <i>Joana Melo</i>.
</p><p>In Portugal, having only one surname is rare, and it usually happens when both the parents have the same last name, to avoid repetitive combinations such as <i>António Santos Santos</i> (which would, however, be an acceptable legal name).<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  In Brazil, having only one surname is common in areas with large communities of non-Portuguese immigrants.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spelling">Spelling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spelling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Portuguese names have a standard spelling, since names are considered as regular <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">nouns</a>, and are thus subject to the orthographical rules of the Portuguese language. The spelling of many names has evolved through times and with orthography reforms; at the same time, archaic forms of names survive, though they are considered misspellings by current spelling rules. The <i>Acordo Ortográfico</i> ("Orthographic Agreement"), valid in Brazil and Portugal, states on Section XI (<a href="/wiki/Proper_noun" title="Proper noun">Proper Nouns</a>): <i>Os nomes próprios personativos, locativos e de qualquer natureza, sendo portugueses ou aportuguesados, serão sujeitos às mesmas regras estabelecidas para os nomes comuns.</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Anthroponymy" title="Anthroponymy">Anthroponymic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">toponymic</a> proper nouns, if Portuguese or incorporated to the Portuguese language, are subject to the same spelling rules established to regular nouns.").<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Portugal, given names have a standard spelling that is considered the norm (even for non-contemporary figures) and the rules are enforced by law. The 'Instituto dos Registos e do Notariado', under the jurisdiction of the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="Portuguese Republic">Portuguese Republic</a>, has rules about given names. There is a defined list of allowed names;<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> misspelt and archaic forms (e.g., Luiz is the archaic form of Luís; Felipe is a misspelling of Filipe), and names containing foreign letters – k, y, w – are usually not allowed. However, older people who were registered with archaic forms have continued to use them (examples include <a href="/wiki/Manoel_de_Oliveira" title="Manoel de Oliveira">Manoel de Oliveira</a> – the modern spelling would be Manuel). Regarding surnames, there are no legal restrictions, and as such many people continue to use archaic spellings of family names, as in Athayde (modern form Ataíde), Telles (modern form Teles).
</p><p>In Brazil, there are no laws concerning names, and only obscene or ridiculous names are forbidden when parents <a href="/wiki/Name_at_birth" class="mw-redirect" title="Name at birth">report the birth</a> of a child to the local <i>cartório de registro civil</i> (<a href="/wiki/Civil_registry" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil registry">Civil registry</a>). Many archaic spellings coexist with the orthographically correct, and even with fancy orthographies (Filipe [the only correct form under the current orthography], Philippe [archaic and traditional], Fellype [fancy]).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Names of international inspiration are common, bringing with them the unusual characters "k", "w", and "y" (Katya, William), diacritics that do not match the Brazilian pronunciation (Desirée, pronounced Desirrê) or  do not exist in Portuguese (Thaïs), double letters that retain their foreign pronunciation (Roosevelt) or not (Giovanni), silent letters (as in the formerly mentioned Desirée and Thaïs), and letters that are intended to sound differently from the orthographic norms (Juan, if intended to sound as in Spanish, Hannah, if the initial "h" is intended as an aspiration). Parents can make up any type of name, and suffixes with an English or French "flavour" are often used to give foreign allure to their offspring's names, such as "-son" for boys and "-elly" for girls (Deividson, Jéferson, Joeldson, Maiksson, Andrielly, Marcelly, Nadrielly, Nathyelly, etc.). This phenomenon can be easily seen in Brazilian football players' names.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Names of <a href="/wiki/Historical_figure" title="Historical figure">historical figures</a> must be spelled following the current orthographic rules: <a href="/wiki/Lu%C3%ADs_de_Cam%C3%B5es" title="Luís de Camões">Luís de Camões</a> (not <i>Luiz de Camoens</i>), <a href="/wiki/Venceslau_Br%C3%A1s" title="Venceslau Brás">Venceslau Brás</a> (not <i>Wenceslau Braz</i>), <a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1s_Ant%C3%B4nio_Gonzaga" class="mw-redirect" title="Tomás Antônio Gonzaga">Tomás Antônio Gonzaga</a> (not <i>Thomaz Antonio Gonzaga</i>) etc.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="The_particle_.27de.27"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_particle_'de'">The particle 'de'</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The particle &#039;de&#039;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Adposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Adposition">Prepositions</a> that can be used in Portuguese surnames are <b>da</b>, <b>das</b>, <b>do</b>, <b>dos</b> and <b>de</b>, such as in Maria da Cunha, José das Neves, Joana do Rosário, Luís dos Santos, Gabriela de Sousa, etc. and mean "from" or "of." <i>Da</i>, <i>dos</i>, etc. are contractions of the preposition <i>de</i> and a <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">definite article</a> (<i>o</i>, <i>as</i>, etc.), meaning "from the" or "of the." The current convention in Portuguese is that they be written in lower case<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. Different from in Italian surnames, these conjunctives are usually not part of a composite name, i.e., "Sousa" is not different from "de Sousa," and both are ordered under 'S' in an alphabetical list. Therefore, one should not refer to <i>Luiz Pereira da Silva</i> as <i>Mr. Da Silva</i> but rather <i>Mr. Silva</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjunction" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammatical conjunction">conjunction</a> <b>"e"</b> (and) is also common, e.g. "Maria Costa e Silva." Most commonly this would be a composite surname; in this case the person should be addressed as "Ms. Costa e Silva", and not as Ms. Silva.
</p><p>The most well-known exception to this norm is Angolan president <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Eduardo_dos_Santos" title="José Eduardo dos Santos">José Eduardo dos Santos</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> who is frequently referred to as President Dos Santos, even among Portuguese-speaking people and in Portuguese-language media (although, in Portugal, the forms "Presidente José Eduardo dos Santos" or "Presidente Eduardo dos Santos" are still more common). Likewise, the Anglophone media often ignores this rule when referring to <a href="/wiki/Luiz_In%C3%A1cio_Lula_da_Silva" title="Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva">Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva</a> as <i>Mr. Da Silva</i>, instead of the appropriate <i>Mr. Silva</i>, while he is mostly called <i>Lula</i> in Portuguese-speaking media.
</p>
<h2><span id="The_name_.27Maria.27"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_name_'Maria'">The name 'Maria'</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The name &#039;Maria&#039;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The given name <b>Maria</b> (like <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Mary_(given_name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mary (given name)">Mary</a></i>, from <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> <i>Miryam</i>, via <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>Maria</i>) is extremely common as a feminine given name and even combined with masculine names. In Portugal, it has always been common.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Since the turn of the 21st century, a new wave of traditional given names has resulted in an increase in its popularity. In 2014, it was the most common girl name in Portugal, more than twice the second-rated <i>Matilde</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Traditionally Maria is more common as the first part of a double first name combination; these may be formed by several different elements.
</p><p><b>Religious predicates</b> (often honouring one of the Virgin Mary's denominations):
</p>
<ul><li>Catholic devotion festivities: <i>Maria da Conceição</i> (referring to Our Lady of Conception), <i>Maria das Dores</i> (Our Lady of Sorrows), <i>Maria da Assunção</i> (<a href="/wiki/Assumption_of_Mary" title="Assumption of Mary">Assumption of Mary</a>), <i>Maria da Natividade</i> (<a href="/wiki/Nativity_of_Mary" title="Nativity of Mary">Nativity of Mary</a>).</li>
<li>A place of a <a href="/wiki/Marian_apparitions" class="mw-redirect" title="Marian apparitions">Marian apparition</a>: <i>Maria de Fátima</i> (<a href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of_F%C3%A1tima" title="Our Lady of Fátima">Fátima</a>), <i>Maria de Lurdes</i> (<a href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of_Lourdes" title="Our Lady of Lourdes">Lourdes</a>), <i>Maria de la Salete</i> (<a href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of_La_Salette" title="Our Lady of La Salette">La Salette</a>), <i>Maria Aparecida</i> (common in Brazil, after <a href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of_Aparecida" title="Our Lady of Aparecida">Aparecida</a>), <i>Maria Nazaré</i> (<a href="/wiki/Nazareth" title="Nazareth">Nazareth</a>).</li>
<li>A virtue or a nature element (many of which have lost religious associations nowadays): <i>Maria do Céu</i> (<a href="/wiki/Heaven" title="Heaven">Heaven</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sky" title="Sky">Sky</a>), <i>Maria da Luz</i> (<a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">Light</a>), <i>Maria do Mar</i> (<a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">Sea</a>), <i>Maria da Graça</i> (<a href="/wiki/Divine_grace" title="Divine grace">Grace</a>).</li>
<li>The name of a saint: <i>Maria de São José</i> (after <a href="/wiki/Saint_Joseph" title="Saint Joseph">Saint Joseph</a>).</li></ul>
<p><b>Other types of combinations</b>:
</p>
<ul><li>Maria paired with a different feminine given name: <i>Maria Madalena</i>, <i>Maria Teresa</i>, <i>Maria Antónia (or Antônia, in Brazil), Maria Gabriela, Maria Beatriz, Maria Eduarda, Maria Luíza, Maria Fernanda, Maria Alice</i>, <i>Maria Carolina</i>, <i>Maria Dulce</i></li>
<li>Maria paired with a masculine given name: <i>Maria João</i>, <i>Maria José</i>, <i>Maria Manuel</i>, <i>Maria Luís</i>, <i>José Maria </i> (which is often abbreviated as JM).</li></ul>
<p>Many names that are <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymologically</a> related to Maria are also used. The most common is the name Mariana, a contraction of Maria and Ana. Other international aglutinations of Maria combinations have been introduced in more recent times. These include <b>Marisa</b>, <b>Marlene</b>, <b>Marília</b> and Míriam (from <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>).
</p><p>As Maria is so widely used, women are most likely to be addressed by just the second element of their name: <i>Conceição</i> (Conception), <i>Dores</i> (Sorrows), <i>Céu</i> (sky/heaven), <i>Luz</i> (light), Lurdes (<a href="/wiki/Lourdes" title="Lourdes">Lourdes</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/F%C3%A1tima,_Portugal" title="Fátima, Portugal">Fátima</a></i>, <i>Salete</i>, <i>Aparecida</i> (appeared one), <i>Madalena</i>, <i>Antónia</i>, <i>Teresa</i>, <i>Glória</i> (glory), <i>Prazeres</i> (pleasures) etc. A woman named Maria de Jesus would be addressed as <i>Jesus</i>, even though the second name is masculine.
</p><p>A similar thing happens with the name <i><a href="/wiki/Anna_(given_name)" title="Anna (given name)">Ana</a></i> (English <a href="/wiki/Anne" title="Anne">Anne</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hannah_(given_name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hannah (given name)">Hannah</a>), also very common in double-name combinations such as <i>Ana Paula</i> and <i>Ana Carolina</i>, especially in the younger generations. A woman called Ana Paula would be usually called 'Paula', Ana Carolina would be 'Carolina' and so on.
</p><p>A similar procedure occurs with masculine names, but using a reverse order. It is not unusual to find masculine names such as <i>João Maria</i>, <i>José Maria</i>, <i>Manuel Maria</i>, <i>Luís Maria</i> etc. In this case, Maria would always be the second given name, in honour of the <a href="/wiki/Mary_(mother_of_Jesus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mary (mother of Jesus)">Virgin Mary</a>, and the first name would be a masculine name. This custom is <a href="/wiki/Fashionable" class="mw-redirect" title="Fashionable">fashionable</a> among the Portuguese and Brazilian nobility and the upper classes.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surname_and_marriage">Surname and marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Surname and marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The custom of a woman adopting a different surname through marriage is not a Portuguese-Brazilian tradition.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It spread in the late 19th century in the upper classes, under French influence.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> After the 1940s, it became almost socially obligatory. Not doing so was seen as evidence of concubinage, particularly until the 1970s. There is no longer a distinct pattern, with men and women being allowed to choose whether to change or not their surname(s).
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, a person may adopt his or her spouse's surname(s), but each always keeps the original birth surnames. For example, if <i>Maria Abreu Melo</i> marries <i>José Santos Almeida</i>, she could choose to become <i>Maria Abreu Melo Almeida</i> or <i>Maria Abreu Melo Santos Almeida</i>.
</p><p>In Brazil, a woman may adopt her husband's surname(s) and choose to keep or exclude her birth names. For example, when <i>Maria Abreu Melo</i> marries <i>José Santos Almeida</i>, she could choose to become <i>Maria Abreu Melo Almeida</i>, <i>Maria Abreu Melo Santos Almeida</i>, <i>Maria Santos Almeida</i>, <i>Maria Almeida</i>, etc. Usually, in these cases, a woman keeps part of her birth name and uses part of her husband's surname, in order to avoid having long names. So, the most used combination from the above example would be <i>Maria Melo Almeida</i>.
</p><p>The mandatory adoption of the new name led to unusual combinations, as in the (not uncommon) case of both spouses having the same surname, when the woman's surname was kept. Another confusing situation was, for example when a woman named <i>Ana Lima Silva</i> married a man named <i>João Lima</i>, her name could legally become <i>Ana Lima Silva Lima</i>.
</p><p>In Portugal, since 1977, and in <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, since the 1970s, a woman has the option of whether or not to change her name after marriage. In Portugal, since 1977, and in Brazil, since 2002, a husband can also adopt his wife's surname. In Portugal, when this happens, usually both spouses change their name after marriage (for example, <i>José Santos Almeida</i> and <i>Maria Abreu Melo</i> could become <i>José Santos Melo Almeida</i> and <i>Maria Abreu Melo Almeida</i> or even "José Santos Almeida Melo" and "Maria Abreu Melo Almeida"). In Brazil, there is not yet a perceived pattern.
</p><p>This custom of name change has been fading since the 1970s and nowadays it is rarely found,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> due to the cumbersome need to update registries, documents, etc., after the name change and back again in the event of divorce.
</p><p>In 2012, a circuit of the <a href="/wiki/Superior_Court_of_Justice_(Brazil)" title="Superior Court of Justice (Brazil)">Superior Court of Justice</a> in Brazil allowed a woman to adopt her male partner's surname while in a <a href="/wiki/Civil_union" title="Civil union">civil union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collation">Collation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Collation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When producing alphabetized lists of Portuguese names, generally the <i>full name</i> is used. This occurs mainly in schools or official documents, and it is usually done because many people prefer to use multiple surname combinations in their daily life, or do not use the last surname at all.  It is therefore difficult to order people by the surnames they use. A typical alphabetized list:
</p>
<ul><li>António Borges Santos</li>
<li>António Silva Abreu Melo</li>
<li>Leonor Soares Henriques Pais</li>
<li>Sofia Matilde Almeida Pais</li></ul>
<p>However, in areas such as a telephone directory or bibliography, the practice of using the (last) surname is preferred:
</p>
<ul><li>Santos, António Borges (or Antônio, used in Brazil)</li>
<li>Melo, António Silva Abreu</li>
<li>Pais, Leonor Soares Henriques</li>
<li>Pais, Sofia Matilde Almeida</li></ul>
<p>The conjunctives and affixes preceding or following it, such as "da" and "Filho", are not used. When a full composite surname is known, it is alphabetized according to the first name even if not separated by a hyphen. When it is not known, the last name should be used. Because of this, many errors are committed in the alphabetization of Portuguese surnames, such as in a telephone directory. For example:
</p>
<ul><li>Chagas Filho, Carlos</li>
<li>Siqueira Campos, Luís Pereira</li>
<li>Sousa, Luís de</li></ul>
<p>These rules may change if the Portuguese name has been absorbed into a different culture, as in English-speaking countries. In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, for example, where many Portuguese have emigrated since the 18th century to <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>, alphabetising rules use "da" and "de" as part of the surname. The <a href="/wiki/Portuguese-American" class="mw-redirect" title="Portuguese-American">Portuguese-American</a> author <a href="/wiki/John_Dos_Passos" title="John Dos Passos">John Dos Passos</a>, who is referred to as having the surname Dos Passos, is a good example.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nicknames">Nicknames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nicknames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Portuguese nicknames are usually formed by inserting the diminutive infix -<i>inh</i> or -<i>it</i> before the final vowel in the name. For example, <a href="/wiki/Teresa" title="Teresa">Teresa</a> becomes <i>Teresinha</i> (meaning "little Teresa"), and <a href="/wiki/Carlos_(given_name)" title="Carlos (given name)">Carlos</a> becomes <i>Carlinhos</i> ("little Carlos"). In some cases, a nickname is formed by adding <i>zinho(a)</i> or <i>-zito(a)</i> – to the actual name. For example, <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o" title="João">João</a> becomes <i>Joãozinho</i> ("little João") or <a href="/wiki/Sofia" title="Sofia">Sofia</a> becomes <i>Sofiazinha</i> ("little Sofia").
</p><p>Augmentative suffixes may be used as well, with "Marcos" becoming "Marcão" ("Big Mark"), for example.
</p><p>Other practices include the repetition of a syllable (<i>Nonô</i> from <a href="/wiki/Eleanor" title="Eleanor">Leonor</a>, <i>Zezé</i> from <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9" title="José">José</a>), a simple shortening of the name (<i>Fred</i> from Frederico, <i>Bea</i> or <i>Bia</i> from Beatriz), the contraction of the name (Manel or Mané from Manuel), or of a fraction of it (<i>Beto</i> from Alberto or Roberto, <i>Mila</i> from Emília or Camila). A mix of shortening and adding a suffix may also occur (<i>Leco</i> from Leonardo). Sometimes, a foreign-language nickname is used for the corresponding Portuguese name ("<i>Rick"</i> for Ricardo, "Maggie" from Margarida). Most given names have one or more standard diminutives.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazilian-specific_patterns">Brazilian-specific patterns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Brazilian-specific patterns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Children_of_immigrants">Children of immigrants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Children of immigrants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Brazil, recent immigrants – especially Italians, Germans, Jews and Japanese – usually give their sons only the father's family surname. Although there is no legal restriction on this practice, the pattern in succeeding generations changes to the traditional Portuguese pattern, usually because of assimilation.
</p><p>Today one can find people who use two Italian surnames (like <i>"Gardi Bianchini</i>") or two Japanese surnames (like <i>"Sugahara Uemura"</i>), a  practice that is unusual in Italy and nonexistent in Japan. Having two surnames from different non-Portuguese origin is also not uncommon, such as the Brazilian celebrity "<i><a href="/wiki/Sabrina_Sato" title="Sabrina Sato">Sabrina Sato Rahal</a></i>", a Japanese and an Arab surname, respectively. Particularly common are German-Italian combinations (Becker Bianchini, for instance), especially in Rio Grande do Sul.
</p><p>The Spanish pattern is to use both the father's and mother's family surnames, but in reverse order compared to Portuguese pattern. Almost all of the first Spanish-Brazilian born generation were named in order of the family surnames of the Portuguese pattern.
</p>
<h3><span id="S.C3.A3o_Paulo_State_area"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="São_Paulo_State_area">São Paulo State area</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: São Paulo State area">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A specific pattern developed among the descendants of 20th-century immigrants: they use only their father's surname and two given names, the first is a Portuguese given name and the second one is a given name from their father's original country.
</p><p>This pattern is most used among <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syrian</a>-<a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanese</a> immigrants sons and grandsons. So one can find names like <i>"<a href="/wiki/Paulo_Salim_Maluf" class="mw-redirect" title="Paulo Salim Maluf">Paulo Salim Maluf</a>"</i> where <i>Paulo</i> is a Portuguese given name, <i>Salim</i> is an Arabian given name, and <i>Maluf</i> is his father's surname; or <i>"Maria Heiko Sugahara"</i> where <i>Maria</i> is a Portuguese given name, <i>Heiko</i> a Japanese given name and <i>Sugahara</i> is her father's surname. This practice allows the person to be recognized as <i>"Paulo Maluf</i>" or <i>"Maria Sugahara"</i> (in the large Brazilian society) or as <i>"Salim Maluf"</i> or <i>"Heiko Sugahara"</i> (in the immigrant's social community).
</p><p>This pattern used to be quite common in <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo_(state)" title="São Paulo (state)">São Paulo</a>. Intermarriage has reduced this practice, but it is commonly used when both father and mother belong to the same ethnicity. Younger generations tend to use both the father's and the mother's family name, thus giving four names to their sons (like <i>"Paulo Salim Lutfalla Maluf"</i> or <i>"Maria Heiko Sugahara Uemura"</i>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_Portuguese_surnames">Origin of Portuguese surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Origin of Portuguese surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before Romans entered the territory of present-day Portugal, the native people identified themselves by a single name, or that name followed by a patronym. The names could be <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a> (Mantaus), <a href="/wiki/Lusitania" title="Lusitania">Lusitanian</a> (Casae), Iberian (Sunua) or <a href="/wiki/Conii" class="mw-redirect" title="Conii">Conii</a> (Alainus). The names were clearly ethnic and some typical of a tribe or region. A slow adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions" title="Roman naming conventions">Roman onomastic</a> occurred after the end of the first century AD, with the adoption of a <a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions" title="Roman naming conventions">Roman name</a> or of the tria nomina: praenomen (given name), nomen (gentile) and <a href="/wiki/Cognomen" title="Cognomen">cognomen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most Portuguese surnames have a patronymical, locative or religious origin.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surnames_originating_from_patronymics">Surnames originating from patronymics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Surnames originating from patronymics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Patronymics</b> are names derived from the father's given name that, many centuries ago, began to be used as surnames. They are a common form of surnames in the lands where Portuguese is spoken and also have developed in many other languages.
</p><p>In Portuguese, patronymics are surnames such as <i><a href="/wiki/Henriques_(surname)" title="Henriques (surname)">Henriques</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pires_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Pires (disambiguation)">Pires</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rodrigues_(surname)" title="Rodrigues (surname)">Rodrigues</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lopes" title="Lopes">Lopes</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Nunes" title="Nunes">Nunes</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mendes" title="Mendes">Mendes</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Fernandes" title="Fernandes">Fernandes</a></i>, <i>Gonçalves</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Arantes" title="Arantes">Arantes</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Esteves" title="Esteves">Esteves</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%81lvares" title="Álvares">Álvares</a>,</i> where the ending -es- means (<i>son of</i>).
</p><p>Some surnames that originated in this way do not end in <i>es</i>; instead they end in <i>iz</i>, like <i>Muniz</i> (<i>son of Monio</i>) and Ruiz, (<i>son of Ruy</i>), or <i>ins</i>, like <i>Martins</i> (<i>son of Martim</i>).
</p><p>Although most Portuguese surnames ending in <i>-es</i> are former patronymics, some family names with -es- endings are not patronymics, but toponymics, such as Tavares, Cortês and Chaves.
</p><p>Some surnames are equal to given names, such as <i>Joana Fernando</i>, or <i>André João</i>, in which "Fernando" and "João" are surnames, not given names. It is rather improbable that those are patronymics; more likely they originated with people with no surnames, who were given two names for the sake of enhanced individuality. One can find today in Portugal and Brazil people who still use surnames that for other people are just given names, although they were passed from parents to sons for generations, such as Valentim, Alexandre, Fernando, Afonso (note the family name <i>de Melo Afonso</i>) and Antonio (note <i>de Melo Antonio</i>). Names like <i>Dinis</i>, <i>Duarte</i>, <i>Garcia</i> and <i>Godinho</i> were originally given names, but today they are used in Brazil almost exclusively as surnames, although Duarte and Dinis are still common given names in Portugal.
</p><p>Matronymics (surnames derived from female given names) are not used in Portuguese. Surnames such as "Catarino" (from <a href="/wiki/Santa_Catarina_(Caldas_da_Rainha)" title="Santa Catarina (Caldas da Rainha)">Catarina</a>) and "Mariano" (meaning related to <i><a href="/wiki/Mary_(mother_of_Jesus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mary (mother of Jesus)">Maria</a></i>) are rather references to Catholic saints (probably originating with the practice of giving a child the name of the saint of the day in which he or she was born).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Some former patronymics are not easily recognized, for two main reasons. Sometimes the given name that was the basis of the patronymic became archaic, such as  <i>Lopo</i> (the basis of <i>Lopes</i>), <i>Mendo</i> or <i>Mem</i> (<i>Mendes</i>), <i>Vasco</i> (<i>Vasques</i>), Soeiro (<i>Soares</i>), Munio (<i>Muniz</i>), <i>Sancho</i> (<i>Sanches</i>). Also, often the given names or the related patronymic changed through centuries, although always some resemblance can still be noted – such as <i>Antunes</i> (son of <i>Antão</i> or <i>Antonio</i>), <i>Peres</i> (son of <i>Pero</i>, archaic form of <i>Pedro</i>), <i>Alves</i> (from <i>Alvares</i>, son of <i>Álvaro</i>), and <i>Eanes</i> (from mediaeval Iohannes, son of <i>João</i>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Locative_surnames">Locative surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Locative surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A large number of surnames are <b>locative</b>, related to the geographical origin of a person, such as the name of a village, town, city, land, river. Such surnames like <i>Almeida</i>, <i>Andrada</i> or <i>Andrade</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Barcelos,_Portugal" title="Barcelos, Portugal">Barcelos</a></i>, <i>Barros</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bastos" title="Bastos">Bastos</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Braga" title="Braga">Braga</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Beira_(Portugal)" title="Beira (Portugal)">Beira</a> </i>(edge), <i><a href="/wiki/Castelo_Branco_Municipality" class="mw-redirect" title="Castelo Branco Municipality">Castelo Branco</a></i>, <i>Cintra</i> (from <a href="/wiki/Sintra" title="Sintra">Sintra</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Coimbra" title="Coimbra">Coimbra</a></i>, <i>Faria</i>, <i>Gouveia</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Guimar%C3%A3es" title="Guimarães">Guimarães</a></i>, <i>Lima</i> (the name of a river, not meaning <a href="/wiki/Lime_(fruit)" title="Lime (fruit)">lime</a>), <i>Lisboa</i> (<a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>), <i>Maia</i>, <i>Pacheco</i> (from village of Pacheca), <i>Porto</i> (<a href="/wiki/Oporto" class="mw-redirect" title="Oporto">Oporto</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Serpa" title="Serpa">Serpa</a></i>, etc., fit this rule. A surname such as <i><a href="/wiki/Le%C3%A3o" class="mw-redirect" title="Leão">Leão</a></i> (lion) may mean that an ancestor came from the old Iberian kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Kingdom of León">León</a> (Northwestern Spain) or the French city of <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Not all villages and towns that were the basis of surnames still exist, have kept the same name, or are inhabited today.
</p><p>Some names specify a location of the family's house within the village: <i>Fonte</i> (by the fountain), <i>Fontoira</i> (golden font), <i>Azenha</i> (by the water-mill), <i>Eira</i> (by the threshing-floor), <i>Tanque</i> (by the community cistern), <i>Fundo</i> (on the lower part of the village), <i>Cimo/Cima</i> (on the upper part of the village), <i>Cabo</i> (on the far end of the village),<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <i>Cabral</i> (near the field where the goats graze). In some cases, the family name may not be a locative, but an indication of ownership.
</p><p>Surnames were also derived from geological or geographical forms, such as <i>Pedroso</i> (stony or full of pebbles land), <i>Rocha</i> (rock), <i>Souza</i>/<i>Sousa</i> (from Latin saxa, a place with seixos, i.e. pebbles, or the name of a Portuguese river), <i>Vale</i> (valley, dale), <i>Bierzo</i> (mountain), <i>Ribeiro</i>/<i>Rivero</i> (little river, creek, brook), <i>Siqueira</i> or <i>Sequeira</i> (a non-irrigated land), <i>Castro</i> (castle or ruins of ancient buildings, fortress or fortification of the ancient Roman Iberia), <i>Dantas</i> (from d'Antas, a place with antas, i.e. prehistoric stone monuments or dolmens), <i>Costa</i> (coast of the sea), <i>Pedreira</i> (quarry), <i>Barreira</i> (clay quarry), <i>Couto</i> (site fenced), <i>Outeiro</i> (high place). The name <i>Ferreira</i>/<i>Ferreyra</i>, i.e. it's a place where iron ore is found (ferro), <i>Vilar/Villar</i> (from Latin <i>villare</i> or <i>villagio</i>, i.e. farm or ranch), <i>Seixo</i>/<i>Seijo</i> (stone), <i>Veiga</i>/<i>Vega</i> (banks of a river), <i>Cordoba/Cordova</i> (hill near the river), <i>Padron</i> (rock or stone), <i>Celanova</i> (barn or reservoir).
</p><p>Names of trees or plantations are also locative surnames, originally related to identifying a person who lived near or inside a plantation, an orchard or a place with a characteristic kind of vegetation. Names such as <i>Silva</i> and <i>Matos</i> (woods, forest), <i>Campos</i> (grass fields, prairie), <i>Teixeira</i> (a place covered with yew trees), <i>Queirós</i> (a kind of grass), Cardoso (a place covered with <i>cardos</i>, i.e. with cardoons or thistles), Correia (a place covered with <i>corriolas</i> or <i>correas</i>, a kind of plant), <i>Macedo</i> (an apple tree garden), and <i>Azevedo</i> (a forest of azevinho, i.e. a holly wood) fit this pattern.
</p><p>Tree names are very common locative surnames – <i><a href="/wiki/Oliveira_(surname)" title="Oliveira (surname)">Oliveira</a></i>/<i>Olivera</i> (olive tree), <i>Carvalho</i> (<a href="/wiki/Oak_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Oak tree">oak tree</a>), <i>Servia</i> (from <i>serba</i>, i.e. a sort of <a href="/wiki/Sorbus" title="Sorbus">sorbus</a> or serbal tree), <i>Pinheiro</i> (<a href="/wiki/Pine_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Pine tree">pine tree</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Pereira_(surname)" title="Pereira (surname)">Pereira</a>/<a href="/wiki/Pereyra" title="Pereyra">Pereyra</a></i> (pear orchard or a kind of <a href="/wiki/Pear_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Pear tree">pear tree</a>), Pêro/Pero (wild <a href="/wiki/Apple_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Apple tree">apple tree</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Pereiro_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Pereiro (disambiguation)">Pereiro</a></i>/<i>Do Pereyro</i> (<a href="/wiki/Apple_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Apple tree">apple tree</a>), <i>Aciveiro</i> (<a href="/wiki/Holly" title="Holly">holly tree</a>), <i>Moreira</i> (from amoreira, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Mulberry_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Mulberry tree">mulberry tree</a>), <i>Maceda</i>/<i>Macieira</i> (from <i>maçã</i> or <i>macieira</i>, i.e. a kind of <a href="/wiki/Apple_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Apple tree">apple tree</a>), <i>Filgueira</i>/<i>Figueira</i> (fern tree or <a href="/wiki/Cyatheales" title="Cyatheales">cyatheales</a>), <i>Loureiro</i>/<i>Laureiro</i> (<a href="/wiki/Laurus_nobilis" title="Laurus nobilis">laurel tree</a>), <i>Parreira</i> (grape tree). There is the case of <i>Pereira</i>/<i>Pereyra</i> which is not only a tree. In the old documentations of the Portuguese language also appears as a variant of Pedreira or Pedreiro and this is "site covered with stones".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_surnames">Religious surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Religious surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Surnames with <b>religious meanings</b> or connotations are common. It is possible that some of these originated from an ancestor who converted to Catholicism and intended or needed to demonstrate his new faith. Another possible source of religious names were orphans who were abandoned in the churches and raised in Catholic orphanages by priests and nuns. They were usually baptized with a name related to the date near when they were found or baptized. Another possible source is when previous religious given names (expressing a special devotion by the parents or the god-parents, or the child's birth date) were adopted as family names.
</p><p>Religious names includes <i>de <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a></i> (of Jesus), <i> dos Reis</i> (of the kings, from the day of the Epiphany of the Lord, the Day of the Wise Kings), <i>Ramos</i> (branches, from Palm Sunday, the Sunday before Easter), <i>Pascoal</i> (of Easter), <i>da Assunção</i> (of the Assumption of the Virgin Mary), <i>do Nascimento</i> (of the Nativity of the Virgin Mary or the Nativity of Jesus – Christmas), <i>da Visitação</i> (of the Visitation of the Virgin Mary), <i>da Anunciação</i> (of the Annunciation of the Virgin Mary), <i>da Conceição</i> (of the Immaculate Conception of the Virgin Mary), <i>Trindade</i> (from Trinity Sunday), <i>do Espírito Santo</i> (of the Holy Ghost, from the Feast of the Holy Ghost), <i>das Chagas</i> (of wounds, from the Feast of the Five Wounds of Christ), <i>Graça</i> (grace, from Our Lady of Grace), <i>Patrocínio</i> (patronage, from Our Lady of Patronage), <i>Paz</i> (peace, from Our Lady Mediatrix of Peace), <i>Luz</i> (light, from Our Lady of the Divine Light), <i>Neves</i> (snows, from Our Lady of the Snows), <i>Penha</i> (cliff, bluff, from Our Lady of the Bluff of France, that in Spanish is called Nuestra Señora de Peñafrancia), <i>das Dores</i> (of sorrows, from Our Lady of Sorrows), <i>Bonfim</i> (good end, from Our Lord of Good Death), <i>das Virgens</i> (of the virgins martyrs), <i>dos Anjos</i> (of angels, from the Archangels Michael, Raphael, and Gabriel day), <i>São João</i> (Saint John), <i>Santana</i> (Saint Ann), <i>Santos</i> (from 'Todos os Santos', i.e. from All Hallows or All Saints day and the Latin language exist the spelling <i>sanctus</i> gives rise to other variants as <i>Sanctius</i>, <i>Santious</i>, <i>Sancti</i>, <i>Santis</i>, <i>Santi</i>, <i>Sante</i> or <i>Santé</i>, <i>Santiz</i>, <i>Santiso</i> or <i>Santizo</i> and <i>Santotis</i>) and <i>Cruz</i> (Cross, the most common surname among the <a href="/wiki/Belmonte_Jews" class="mw-redirect" title="Belmonte Jews">Belmonte Jews</a>).
</p><p>An orphan with unknown parents or a converted (<a href="/wiki/Jew" class="mw-redirect" title="Jew">Jew</a>, African slave, or <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Brazil" title="Indigenous peoples in Brazil">Native Brazilian</a>) person was frequently baptized with the name of a saint, such as <i>João Baptista</i> (from Saint John Baptist), <i>João Evangelista</i> (from Saint John the Evangelist), <i>João de Deus</i> (from Saint John of God), <i>Antônio de Pádua</i> (from Saint Anthony of Padova), <i>João Nepomuceno</i> (from Saint John of Nepomuk), <i>Francisco de Assis (</i>from Saint Francis of Assisi), <i>Francisco de Paula</i> (from Saint Francis of Paola), <i>Francisco de Salles</i> (from Saint Francis de Salles), <i>Inácio de Loiola</i> (from Saint Ignatius of Loyola), <i>Tomás Aquino</i> (from Saint Thomas Aquinas), <i>José de Calanzans</i> (from Saint Joseph of Calasanz), or <i>José de Cupertino</i> (from Saint Joseph of Cupertino). After that, they usually passed only the second given name (<i>Batista, Evangelista, de Deus, Pádua, Nepomuceno, Assis, de Paula, Sales, Loiola, Aquino, Calanzans</i> or <i>Cupertino</i>) to their sons as a surname.
</p><p>A surname such as <i>Xavier</i> could have originated from someone baptized after Saint Francis Xavier or from the old Portuguese family <i>Xavier</i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Descriptive_surnames">Descriptive surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Descriptive surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some surnames are possible <b>descriptions</b> of a peculiar characteristic of an ancestor, originating from nicknames.
</p><p>These include names like <i>Peixoto</i> ("little fish", applied to a nobleman who used a fish to trick his enemies during a siege<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>), <i>Peixe</i> (fish, i.e. swimmer, or also fisherman or fishmonger<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>), <i>Veloso</i> (wooly, i.e. hairy), <i>Ramalho</i> (full of tree branches, bushy, i.e. with a thick beard<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>), <i>Barroso</i> (clay covered, i.e. with pimples<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>), Lobo (wolf, i.e. fierce, savage<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>), <i>Lobato</i> (little wolf, wolf cub), <i>Raposo</i> (fox, i.e. smart<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>), <i>Pinto</i> (chick, i.e. gentle and kind<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>), <i>Tourinho</i> (little bull, i.e. stout, strong<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>), <i>Vergueiro</i> (that bends, i.e., weak), <i>Medrado</i> (grown-up, i.e. tall), <i>Tinoco</i> (short, small<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>), <i>Porciúncula</i> (small part, small piece), <i>Magro</i> (thin), <i>Magriço</i> (skinny), <i>Gago</i> (stutterer, stammerer), <i>Galhardo</i> (gallant, chivalrous), <i>Terrível</i> (terrible), <i>Penteado</i> (hairdressing, the nickname of a branch of the German Werneck family whose members used to wear wigs), <i>Romero</i> (from romeiro, pilgrim, i.e. someone who had made a religious voyage to <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Compostela" title="Santiago de Compostela">Santiago de Compostela</a> or <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>). <i>Verdugo/Berdugo</i> ("Branch of a Tree" or 'Executioner")
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Profession_and_occupation_surnames">Profession and occupation surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Profession and occupation surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Portuguese surnames that originated from <b>professions</b> or <b>occupations</b> are few, such as <i>Serrador</i> (sawman), <i>Monteiro</i> (hunter of the hills or woods guard), <i>Guerreiro</i> (warrior), <i>Caldeira</i> (cauldron, i.e. cauldron maker), <i>Cubas</i> (wooden barrels, i.e., barrel maker or cooper), <i>Carneiro</i> (sheep, for a shepherd), <i>Peixe</i> (fish, for a fisherman or a fishmonger).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign-origin_surnames">Foreign-origin surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Foreign-origin surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some Portuguese names originated from foreigners who came to live in <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> or Brazil many centuries ago. They are so ancient that, despite their known foreign origin, they are an integrated part of Portuguese and Brazilian cultures.
</p><p>Most of these names are Spanish, such as <i>Toledo</i> (a city in Spain), <i>Ávila</i> or <i>Dávila</i> (a city in Spain) and <i>Padilha</i>. Other common "foreign" surnames are Bettencourt or Bittencourt (from <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9thencourt" title="Béthencourt">Béthencourt</a>, French), <i>Goulart, Goulard</i> or <i>Gullar</i> (French, original meaning is glutton), <i>Fontenele</i> or <i>Fontenelle</i> (French, from fountain), <i>Rubim</i> (from Robin, French), <i>Alencastro</i>, <i>Lencastre</i> (from Lancaster, English), <i>Drummond</i> (Scottish), <i>Werneck, Vernek</i> or <i>Berneque</i> (southern German, the name of the <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavarian</a> city <a href="/wiki/Werneck" title="Werneck">Werneck</a>), <i>Wanderley</i> (from <i>van der Ley</i>, Flemish), <i>Dutra</i> (from <i>De Ultra</i>, a Latin name meaning "from beyond" assumed by the Flemish family <i>Van Hurtere</i>), <i>Brum</i> (from <i>Bruyn</i>, Flemish), Bulcão (from <i>Bulcamp</i>, Flemish), <i>Dulmo</i> (from <i>van Olm</i>, Flemish),<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Acioli (Italian), <i>Doria</i> (Italian), <i>Cavalcanti</i> (Italian), <i>Netto</i> or <i>Neto</i> (Italian, not to be confused with the name suffix "Neto" ("grandson") that is used in Portuguese to distinguish a grandson and grandfather who bear the same names).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_question_of_Portuguese_Jewish_surnames">The question of Portuguese Jewish surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: The question of Portuguese Jewish surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is a popular belief<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> that the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Portugal" title="History of the Jews in Portugal">Jews living in Portugal</a> up to 1497, when they were forced to choose between conversion or expulsion, substituted their surnames with the names of trees that do not bear edible fruits, such as <i>Carvalho</i> (oak tree) and <i>Junqueira</i> (reed, bulrush, junk). Others say that they usually chose animal <i>Lion</i> (Leao); plant/vegetable <i>Pimentel</i> (pepper); fruit such as <i><a href="/wiki/Piccio" title="Piccio">Figo</a></i> and <i>Moreira</i> (berry); and tree  names such as <i>Pereira</i> (pear tree)  or <i>Oliveira</i> (olive tree), in this case trees that bear edible fruits. However, even these names were already used by Christians during the Middle Ages, these surnames were mostly used by the converted Jews (conversos, new Christians) during the inquisition.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Another family name usually pointed out<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> as denoting Jewish ancestry is <i>Espírito Santo</i> (Holy Spirit or Holy Ghost) and Verdugo/Berdugo (Branch of a Tree). The rationale is that Jews would adopt as a family name an (apparently) Christian concept as a deception. In fact, they were choosing the most incorporeal <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinity</a> person, that is, the one that offended least their (secret) Jewish faith. This theory is not totally unfounded, as there is evidence<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> that the cult around the Holy Spirit flourished after 1496, especially among <a href="/wiki/New_Christians" class="mw-redirect" title="New Christians">New Christians</a>. This does not rule out that "Espírito Santo" was also adopted by faithful Christians, following the rationale of other religious surnames.
</p><p>The Portuguese Jews living in Portugal up to 1497 bore given names that could distinguish them from the Christian population.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Most of these names are Portuguese versions of older semitic (Arabian, Hebrew, Aramaic) names like <i>Abenazo, Aboab, Abravanel, Albarrux, Azenha, Benafull, Benafaçom, Benazo, Caçez, Cachado, Çaçom/Saçom, Carraf, Carilho, Cide/Cid, Çoleima, Faquim, Faracho, Faravom, Fayham/Fayam, Focem, Çacam/Sacam, Famiz, Gadim, Gedelha, Labymda, Latam/Latão, Loquem, Lozora, Maalom, Maçon, Maconde, Mocatel, Mollaão, Montam, Motaal, Rondim, Rosall, Samaia/Çamaya, Sanamel, Saraya, Tarraz, Tavy/Tovy, Toby, Varmar, Verdugo/Berdugo, Zaaboca, Zabocas, Zaquim, Zaquem</i>. Some were locative names, not necessarily specific to Jewish populations, like <i>Catelaão/Catalão </i>(Catalan)<i>, Castelão/Castelhão </i>(Castilian)<i>, Crescente </i>(crescent, from Turkey)<i>, Medina </i>(<a href="/wiki/Medina" title="Medina">Medinah</a>)<i>, Romano </i>(Roman)<i>, Romão, Romeiro, Tolledam/Toledano </i>(from <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Spain" title="Toledo, Spain">Toledo</a>)<i>, Vallency</i> (<a href="/wiki/Valencia,_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Valencia, Spain">Valencia</a>)<i> and Vascos </i>(Basque)<i>; some were patronymics from Biblical names like Abraão </i>(Abraham)<i>, Lázaro </i>(Lazar)<i>, Barnabé, Benjamim, Gabril </i>(Gabriel)<i>, Muça </i>(Moses)<i> and Natam </i>(Nathan); some are profession names such as <i> Caldeirão </i>(cauldron)<i>, Martelo </i>(hammer)<i>, Pexeiro </i>(fishmonger)<i>, Chaveirol </i>(locksmith)<i> and Prateiro </i>(silversmith); some are nicknames such as Calvo<i> (bald), Dourado</i> (golden, like the German Goldfarb), <i>Ruivo</i> (red-headed), <i>Crespo</i> (curly),<i> Querido</i> (beloved) and<i> Parente</i> (family relative). A few names are not distinct from old Portuguese surnames like <i>Camarinha, Castro, Crespim</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-nomesjudeus_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nomesjudeus-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some scholars proved<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> that the converted Portuguese Jews usually chose a patronymic as their new surname and, when the conversion was not forced, they would choose to bear the surname of their godfather.<sup id="cite_ref-nomesjudeus_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nomesjudeus-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Jewish-Portuguese community that flourished in the Netherlands and Hamburg, Germany, after their expulsion from Portugal used surnames such as Camargo, Costa, Fonseca, Dias, Pinto, and Silveira.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Some of the most famous descendants of Portuguese Jews who lived outside Portugal are the philosopher <a href="/wiki/Baruch_Spinoza" title="Baruch Spinoza">Baruch Spinoza</a> (in Portuguese <i>Bento de Espinosa</i>), the British Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Disraeli" title="Benjamin Disraeli">Benjamin Disraeli</a><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>  and the classical economist <a href="/wiki/David_Ricardo" title="David Ricardo">David Ricardo</a>. Other famous members of the Portuguese Synagogue of Amsterdam bore names such as <a href="/wiki/Uriel_da_Costa" title="Uriel da Costa">Uriel da Costa</a> (or <i>Uriel Acosta</i>), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Abraham_Pimentel_-_Rabbi_of_the_Portuguese_synagogue_of_Amsterdam&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Abraham Pimentel - Rabbi of the Portuguese synagogue of Amsterdam (page does not exist)">Abraham Pimentel - Rabbi of the Portuguese synagogue of Amsterdam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Aboab_da_Fonseca" title="Isaac Aboab da Fonseca">Isaac Aboab da Fonseca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_de_Pinto" title="Isaac de Pinto">Isaac de Pinto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Menasseh_ben_Israel" class="mw-redirect" title="Menasseh ben Israel">Menasseh ben Israel</a> (whose original surname was Soeiro).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Belmonte_Jews" class="mw-redirect" title="Belmonte Jews">Belmonte Jews</a> (crypto-Jews from the <a href="/wiki/Belmonte_Municipality" class="mw-redirect" title="Belmonte Municipality">Belmonte</a> region in Portugal) also bear surnames that cannot be used to distinguish them from the older Catholic Portuguese families. Using tree names as surnames was not a common practice among converted or non-converted Portuguese Jews, before or after their <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Portugal" title="History of the Jews in Portugal">expulsion</a> in 1497.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazilian_surnames">Brazilian surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Brazilian surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Giving_Portuguese_surnames_to_Afro-Brazilians_and_native_Brazilians">Giving Portuguese surnames to Afro-Brazilians and native Brazilians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Giving Portuguese surnames to Afro-Brazilians and native Brazilians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Until abolition of slavery, slaves did not have surnames, only given names.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> They were even forbidden to use their distinct African or <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Brazil" title="Indigenous peoples in Brazil">Native Brazilian</a> names and were christened with a Portuguese given name. While slavery persisted, slaves needed to have distinct names only within the plantation (<i>fazenda</i> or <i>engenho</i>) to which they belonged.
</p><p>It was a common practice to name free slaves after their former owners, so all their descendants have the Portuguese surnames of their former owner.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Indigenous people who were not slaves also chose to use their godparents' surnames as their own.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Religious names are also more common among people with African or native Brazilian ancestors than among people with only European ancestors. A slave who had just a given name like <i>Francisco de Assis</i> (from Saint <a href="/wiki/Francis_of_Assisi" title="Francis of Assisi">Francis of Assisi</a>) could use the partial name <i>de Assis</i> as a surname, since the connective – <i>de</i> – gives the appearance of surname.
</p><p>The practice of naming <a href="/wiki/Afro-Brazilians" title="Afro-Brazilians">Afro-Brazilians</a> with religious surnames was proved even by some indirect approaches. Medical researchers demonstrated that there is a statistical correlation between a religious name and genetic diseases related to African ancestry such as the <a href="/wiki/Sickle-cell_disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Sickle-cell disease">sickle-cell disease</a>. Due to <a href="/wiki/Miscegenation" title="Miscegenation">miscegenation</a>, the correlation exists even among white people that have religious surnames.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>It was also common to name indigenous people and freed slaves with surnames which were already very common such as <i>Silva</i> or <i>Costa</i>. That is why<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <i>Silva</i> is the most common surname in Brazil.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Surnames_originated_from_Native_Brazilian_words">Surnames originated from Native Brazilian words</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Surnames originated from Native Brazilian words">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the years following Brazil's independence, some old Brazilians families changed their surnames to surnames derived from <a href="/wiki/Tupian_languages" title="Tupian languages">Tupian languages</a> as a patriotic way to emphasize the new Fatherland. Some of these names are still spelled with <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> old <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a>, but some are spelled according to the new rules. These names, following the old <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a>, include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Brazil" title="Indigenous peoples in Brazil">Native Brazilian</a> nations or tribes: <i><a href="/wiki/Tupinamb%C3%A1_people" title="Tupinambá people">Tupinambá</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tabajara" title="Tabajara">Tabajara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carij%C3%B3" class="mw-redirect" title="Carijó">Carijó</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goytacaz_people&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Goytacaz people (page does not exist)">Goytacaz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guarani_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Guarani people">Guarany</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tamoyo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tamoyo (page does not exist)">Tamoyo</a></i> (the name of a confederation of many tribes that fought the first Portuguese settlers);</li>
<li>Brazilian trees: <i><a href="/wiki/Hymenaea_courbaril" title="Hymenaea courbaril">Jatobá</a></i>, <i>Mangabeira</i> (<a href="/wiki/Mangaba" class="mw-redirect" title="Mangaba">mangaba</a> tree), <i>Pitangui</i> (<a href="/wiki/Surinam_Cherry" class="mw-redirect" title="Surinam Cherry">pitanga</a> tree), <i>Sarahyba</i>, <i>Palmeira</i> (<a href="/wiki/Palm_(plant)" class="mw-redirect" title="Palm (plant)">palm</a> tree), <i>Goiabeira</i> (<a href="/wiki/Guava" title="Guava">guava</a> tree);</li>
<li>Typical Brazilian fruits: <i><a href="/wiki/Surinam_Cherry" class="mw-redirect" title="Surinam Cherry">Pitanga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muricy_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Muricy (disambiguation)">Muricy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guaran%C3%A1" class="mw-redirect" title="Guaraná">Guaraná</a></i> (a Brazilian family with Dutch ancestors changed their surname from <i>Van Ness</i> to <i>Guaraná</i>);</li>
<li>Famous <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Brazil" title="Indigenous peoples in Brazil">Native Brazilian</a> chiefs: <i>Cayubi</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tibiri%C3%A7%C3%A1" title="Tibiriçá">Tibiriçá</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Paragua%C3%A7u" title="Paraguaçu">Paraguaçu</a></i> (big river, sea, in <a href="/wiki/Tupian_languages" title="Tupian languages">Tupian language</a>), <i>Piragibe</i> (fish's arm, in <a href="/wiki/Tupian_languages" title="Tupian languages">Tupian language</a>).</li></ul>
<p>Due to emigration, nowadays one can find these surnames even in <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazilian_locative_surnames">Brazilian locative surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Brazilian locative surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some Brazilian surnames, like some old Portuguese surnames, are <b>locative surnames</b> that denote the original place where the ancestor who first used it was born or lived. Like surnames that originated from words, this practice started during the patriotic years that followed Brazil's Independence.
</p><p>These are surnames like <i>Brasil</i>, (Brazil), <i>Brasiliense</i> (Brazilian), <i>Brasileiro</i> (also Brazilian), <i>América</i>, <i>Americano</i> (American), <i>Bahiense</i> (from Bahia city, today called <a href="/wiki/Salvador,_Bahia" title="Salvador, Bahia">Salvador</a>), <i>Cearense</i> (from <a href="/wiki/Cear%C3%A1" title="Ceará">Ceará</a> State) and Maranhão<i> (from <a href="/wiki/Maranh%C3%A3o" title="Maranhão">Maranhão</a> State)</i>
</p><p>Some of these are <a href="/wiki/Toponyms" class="mw-redirect" title="Toponyms">toponyms</a> derived from <a href="/wiki/Tupian_languages" title="Tupian languages">Tupian languages</a> such as:
</p>
<ul><li>Brazilian rivers: <i><a href="/wiki/Capibaribe_River" title="Capibaribe River">Capibaribe</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Capibara" class="mw-redirect" title="Capibara">Capibaras</a>' river in <a href="/wiki/Tupian_languages" title="Tupian languages">Tupian language</a>), 'Parahyba<i> (from <a href="/wiki/Para%C3%ADba_do_Sul" title="Paraíba do Sul">Paraíba do Sul</a> river, not related to the northern  <a href="/wiki/Para%C3%ADba" title="Paraíba">Paraíba</a> river, <a href="/wiki/Para%C3%ADba" title="Paraíba">Paraíba</a> State, or Paraíba city, today called <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_Pessoa,_Para%C3%ADba" title="João Pessoa, Paraíba">João Pessoa</a>);</i></li>
<li>Brazilian places: <i><a href="/wiki/Pirassununga" title="Pirassununga">Pirassununga</a></i> (snoring fish, in <a href="/wiki/Tupian_languages" title="Tupian languages">Tupian language</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Piratininga" title="Piratininga">Piratininga</a></i> (dried fish, in <a href="/wiki/Tupian_languages" title="Tupian languages">Tupian language</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Carioca" title="Carioca">Carioca</a></i> (from Rio de Janeiro city, originally meant <i>white man house</i> in <a href="/wiki/Tupian_languages" title="Tupian languages">Tupian language</a>).</li></ul>
<p>Due to immigration, nowadays one can find these surnames even in <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>.
</p><p>Some locative surnames derived indirectly as the result of its incorporation by the family after the Imperial nobility title of an ancestor. During the times of Emperor Pedro II, non-hereditary nobilities titles would be granted to notable persons, generally statesmen. The title (but no lordship) would be granted and named after a location, as in Europe, generally owned by the notable. At their death, the family in order to maintain the reference to the  title would adopt them, to the point that many Brazilians still believe these are hereditary.
</p><p>Thus surnames like:
Rio Branco (from Barão de <a href="/wiki/Baron_of_Rio_Branco" class="mw-redirect" title="Baron of Rio Branco">Rio Branco</a>, i.e., José Maria da Silva Paranhos), Jaguaribe (from Barão de <a href="/wiki/Jaguaribe" title="Jaguaribe">Jaguaribe</a>), Ouro Preto (from Visconde de <a href="/wiki/Ouro_Preto" title="Ouro Preto">Ouro Preto</a>), Paranaguá (from the various Marqueses de <a href="/wiki/Paranagu%C3%A1" title="Paranaguá">Paranaguá</a> as the title would be granted to more than one notable), Araripe (Barão de <a href="/wiki/Araripe" title="Araripe">Araripe</a>), Suassuna (Barão de Suassuna), etc...
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-Portuguese_surnames_in_Brazil">Non-Portuguese surnames in Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Non-Portuguese surnames in Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Despite the lesser variation in Portuguese surnames, immigration from other countries (mainly from <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/UK" class="mw-redirect" title="UK">UK</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and more recently <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">Hispanic America</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>) increased the diversity of surnames in Brazil.
</p><p>Some foreign surnames were misspelled after many generations and today cannot be recognized in their original country<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> (the French-Swiss family name <i>Magnan</i> changed to <i>Manhães</i> after some decades). Some misspelled foreign surnames are hardly recognized by speakers of the original language such as <i><a href="/wiki/Fernando_Collor_de_Mello" title="Fernando Collor de Mello">Collor</a></i> (from German <i>Koeller</i>), <i>Chamareli</i> (from Italian <i>Sciammarelli</i>) and <i>Branquini</i> (from Italian <i>Bianchini</i>). Sometimes, different rules of romanization were applied to <a href="/wiki/Japanese_name" title="Japanese name">Japanese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabic_name" title="Arabic name">Arabic names</a> (like <i>Nacamura</i> and <i>Nakamura</i>, <i>Yamaguchi</i> and <i>Iamaguti</i>, <i>Sabag</i> and <i>Sappak</i>, <i>Bukhalil</i> and <i>Bucalil</i>).
</p><p>Thus there are extensively adapted or misspelled foreign surnames used by Brazilian descendants of non-Portuguese immigrants. Due to emigration, nowadays one can find these misspelled surnames even in their original country.
</p>
<h4><span id="Immigrants.27_surnames"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Immigrants'_surnames">Immigrants' surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Immigrants&#039; surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Although not so widely used as in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, immigrants used to change their surname to show assimilation or to avoid social discrimination in Brazil.
</p><p>This practice was most used during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> by Italian immigrants because Italy was an enemy country for a few years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> As Italians are Catholics and were easily assimilated in the larger Brazilian society, the practice is not perceived and almost forgot after a single generation.
</p><p>The new Portuguese surname was generally chosen based on the original meaning of the foreign surname (<i>Olivetto</i>, <i>Olivetti</i> or <i>Oliva</i> sometimes changed to <i>Oliveira</i>). Sometimes the new surname had only a phonetic resemblance with the foreign one (the Italian surnames <i>Livieiro</i> and <i>Salviani</i> sometimes were changed to <i>Oliveira</i> and <i> Silva</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Respectful_treatment_using_hypocoristics">Respectful treatment using hypocoristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Respectful treatment using hypocoristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Brazil, until the first half of the 20th century, very important people could be called in a very respectful – but not formal – way using a social or military title and a childish <a href="/wiki/Hypocoristic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypocoristic">hypocoristics</a> of their given name, such as <i>"Coronel Tonico"</i> (Colonel Tony), <i>"Comendador Paulinho"</i> (Commander Little Paul), <i>"Dona Chica"</i> (Lady Little Frances"), <i>Sinhá Mariquinha</i> (Mrs. Little Mary, <i>sinhá</i> is a popular pronunciation of senhora, i.e. Mrs.). Although an American president could be called <i>Bill</i> (Clinton) or <i>Jimmy</i> (Carter) by the press, this practice was used in Brazil as a much more respectful treatment and never in a formal way.
</p><p>Some sociologists<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2010)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> have suggested that members of the Brazilian upper classes were often raised by slave women who called them using a hypocoristics, and that childish name continued to be used, but in a respectful way, when they grew up.
</p><p>Today, this practice is not so widespread, but one can find people informally, but respectfully, called <i>"Seu Zé"</i> (Mr Joe, <i>Seu</i> is a short Mister) or <i>"Dona Ritinha"</i> (Lady Little Rita).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Adding_given_names_to_surnames">Adding given names to surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Adding given names to surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Brazil, descendants of famous people sometimes use a surname composed of both the given name and the surname of their ancestor, like <i><a href="/wiki/Ruy_Barbosa" title="Ruy Barbosa">Ruy Barbosa</a></i>, <i><a href="https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vital_Brasil" class="extiw" title="pt:Vital Brasil">Vital Brasil</a></i>, <i><a href="https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miguel_da_Silva_Pereira" class="extiw" title="pt:Miguel da Silva Pereira">Miguel Pereira</a></i> and <i><a href="https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lafayette_Rodrigues_Pereira" class="extiw" title="pt:Lafayette Rodrigues Pereira">Lafayette Rodrigues</a></i> families.Such practice allows them to be easily recognized by other people as descendants of their famous ancestor. Such a pattern is rare.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Given_names">Given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Given_names_of_foreign_origin">Given names of foreign origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Given names of foreign origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, newborn children can only be named from a list of given names<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> permitted by Civil Law. Names are required to be spelt according to the rules of <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_orthography" title="Portuguese orthography">Portuguese orthography</a> and to be a part of Portuguese-language <a href="/wiki/Onomastic" class="mw-redirect" title="Onomastic">onomastic</a> (traditionally names in Portugal were based on the <a href="/wiki/Calendar_of_saints" title="Calendar of saints">calendar of saints</a>). Thus in Portugal the given names show little variation, as traditional names are favoured over modern ones. Examples of popular Portuguese names are <i>António, João, José, Francisco, Pedro or Manuel</i> (for men) and <i>Maria, Ana, Isabel, Teresa or Joana</i> (for women). In recent decades there has been a popularity rise for ancient historical names such as <i>Gonçalo, Bernardo, Vasco, Afonso, Leonor, Catarina or Beatriz</i>. If one of the parents is not Portuguese or has double citizenship, foreign names are allowed, as long as the parents present a document proving the requested name is allowed in their country of origin. In the past, immigrant children who were born abroad were required to adopt a Portuguese name in order to become Portuguese citizens –  an example is tennis player <a href="/wiki/Michelle_de_Brito" class="mw-redirect" title="Michelle de Brito">Michelle de Brito</a>, whose legal name is <i>Micaela</i>. This practise no longer applies.
</p><p>In Brazil, there is no legal restriction on naming a newborn child, unless the given name has a meaning that can humiliate or embarrass those who bear it.
</p><p>Brazilians living far from the big cities or lower-class people are prone to create new given names, joining together the given names of the parents or classical given names, changing the spelling of foreign names or even using foreign suffixes that – they may believe – give a sophisticated or modern sound to the new name (e.g. Maurren – from Maureen -, Deivid – from David, Robisson).
</p><p>Foreign surnames are also widely used as given names such as <i><a href="/wiki/Richard_Wagner" title="Richard Wagner">Wagner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mozart" class="mw-redirect" title="Mozart">Mozart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donizetti" class="mw-redirect" title="Donizetti">Donizetti</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lamartine" class="mw-redirect" title="Lamartine">Lamartine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georges_Danton" title="Georges Danton">Danton</a>, Anderson, <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Waldo_Emerson" title="Ralph Waldo Emerson">Emerson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Edison</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Franklin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Nelson,_1st_Viscount_Nelson" title="Horatio Nelson, 1st Viscount Nelson">Nelson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Wilson</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Jefferson" title="Thomas Jefferson">Jefferson</a>, Jensen, <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">Kennedy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Lenin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Newton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Nobel" title="Alfred Nobel">Nobel</a></i>, <i>Rosenberg</i>, <i>Alextricia</i> (combination of <i><a href="/wiki/Alexander" title="Alexander">Alexander</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Patricia" title="Patricia">Patricia</a></i>) and <i>Ocirema</i> (<i>Americo</i> in reverse).<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Originally these names showed the political, artistic or scientific admiration of the parents who first used them to name their sons. (See also <a href="#Spelling">Spelling</a> section of this article).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Given_names_originating_from_Native_Brazilian_names">Given names originating from Native Brazilian names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Given names originating from Native Brazilian names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the reign of the second Emperor, <a href="/wiki/Dom_Pedro_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Dom Pedro II">Dom Pedro II</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Brazil" title="Indigenous peoples in Brazil">Native Brazilian</a> was used as the symbol of the Empire. At this time, Brazilian people started to use <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Brazil" title="Indigenous peoples in Brazil">Native Brazilian</a> names as given names. Some are among the most popular until nowadays.
</p><p>These are names like <i>Araci, Caubi, Guaraci, Iara, Iberê, Ioná, Jaci, Janaína, Jandira, Juçara, Juraci, Jurema, Maiara, Moacir, Moema, Ubiratã, Ceci, Iracema, Peri</i> and <i>Ubirajara</i> (the last four taken from <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_de_Alencar" title="José de Alencar">José de Alencar</a>'s works).
</p><p>Recently, Brazilians have started to use other given names of <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Brazil" title="Indigenous peoples in Brazil">Native Brazilian</a> origin like <i>Rudá</i> (love), <i>Cauã</i> and <i>Cauê</i> (sun), although these are now very rare and their use connotes the hippie culture.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indexing">Indexing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Indexing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Manual_of_Style" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago Manual of Style">Chicago Manual of Style</a>, Portuguese and Lusophone names are <a href="/wiki/Subject_indexing" title="Subject indexing">indexed</a> by the final element of the name, and that this practice differs from the indexing of Spanish and Hispanophone names.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
