<p><b>Heckmondwike</b> is a town and <a href="/wiki/Ward_(politics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ward (politics)">electoral ward</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan borough</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kirklees" title="Kirklees">Kirklees</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, England, 9 miles (14&#160;km) south west of <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>. <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">Historically</a> part of the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>, it is close to <a href="/wiki/Cleckheaton" title="Cleckheaton">Cleckheaton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liversedge" title="Liversedge">Liversedge</a>. It is mostly the <a href="/wiki/Batley_and_Spen_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Batley and Spen (UK Parliament constituency)">Batley and Spen</a> parliamentary constituency, and had an estimated population of 17,066 in March 2001,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> reducing to 16,986 at the 2011 Census.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponymy">Toponymy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heckmondwike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Toponymy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origins of Heckmondwike are in <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a>. First recorded as <i>Hedmundewic</i> &#32;&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>&#93; in the <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> of 1086, <i>Hedmu</i>n<i>dewic</i> in 1166, and as <i>Hecmundewik</i> sometime in the 13th century, the name seems to be from *<i>Hēahmundes wīc</i>, or 'Heahmund's dairy-farm'.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heckmondwike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During <a href="/wiki/Saxons" title="Saxons">Saxon</a> times Heckmondwike was a "berewick" or independent village in the manor of <a href="/wiki/Gomersal" title="Gomersal">Gomersal</a>, which, before 1066, was held by Dunstan and Gamel. After the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman conquest</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William</a> confiscated the land and divided it amongst his followers, one of which, <a href="/wiki/Ilbert_de_Lacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Ilbert de Lacy">Ilbert de Lacy</a>, was made Baron of <a href="/wiki/Pontefract" title="Pontefract">Pontefract</a> and over-lord of vast stretches of land, including the Spen Valley.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The Poll Tax of 1379 records there were seven families in Heckmondwike, about 35 people including one named Thomas of Stubly. Most lived in isolated farmsteads such as Stubley Farm, on high ground overlooking the marshy Spen Valley floor.
</p><p>In 1684 there were around 250 people in the township, occupying 50 houses. The town became famous for manufacturing blankets and by 1811 the Blanket Hall was built for trade in the town's primary manufacture. It was replaced by a second hall erected in 1839, on Blanket Hall Street in the town centre. <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Gaskell" title="Elizabeth Gaskell">Elizabeth Gaskell</a>'s biography of <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Bront%C3%AB" title="Charlotte Brontë">Charlotte Brontë</a> in 1857 described the inhabitants of Heckmondwike as "a chapel-going people, very critical of their sermons, tyrannical to their ministers and violent radicals".
</p><p>The town ceased generating electricity in 1924. The Power Company buildings survive in part on Bath Road.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
The remains of the first Blanket Hall were demolished in spring 2008, along with a number of other old buildings including some former <a href="/wiki/Co-op" class="mw-redirect" title="Co-op">Co-op</a> buildings that had been used as the post office and former <i>"George" <a href="/wiki/Public_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Public house">public house</a></i>.
</p><p>The Heckmondwike footwear company, Goliath, or Co-op Boot Company, made football boots for <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Matthews" title="Stanley Matthews">Sir Stanley Matthews</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The Brunswick Mill site is being redeveloped for housing. The development is called "Brunswick Place".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>A health centre was opened in July 2010 housing two former doctors' practices.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heckmondwike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_James%27_Church_-_Church_Street_-_geograph.org.uk_-_706270.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/St_James%27_Church_-_Church_Street_-_geograph.org.uk_-_706270.jpg/220px-St_James%27_Church_-_Church_Street_-_geograph.org.uk_-_706270.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="307" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/St_James%27_Church_-_Church_Street_-_geograph.org.uk_-_706270.jpg/330px-St_James%27_Church_-_Church_Street_-_geograph.org.uk_-_706270.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/St_James%27_Church_-_Church_Street_-_geograph.org.uk_-_706270.jpg/440px-St_James%27_Church_-_Church_Street_-_geograph.org.uk_-_706270.jpg 2x" data-file-width="459" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_James%27_Church_-_Church_Street_-_geograph.org.uk_-_706270.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>St James's Church (Church of England)</div></div></div>
<p>Located at the edge of the <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennine</a> hills, the land rises to the north, east and south of the town centre. The town covers an area of one square mile (640 acres),<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> the town boundary is not the same as the ward boundary.
</p><p>Heckmondwike has a telephone exchange, north of High Street in the <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a> 01924 dialling area. The exchange covers <a href="/wiki/Liversedge" title="Liversedge">Liversedge</a>, and parts of <a href="/wiki/Dewsbury_Moor" title="Dewsbury Moor">Dewsbury Moor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Staincliffe" title="Staincliffe">Staincliffe</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heckmondwike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1894 Heckmondwike <a href="/wiki/Urban_District_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban District Council">Urban District Council</a> was established and was incorporated into Kirklees in the local government reorganisation of 1974. The Heckmondwike electoral ward includes Millbridge, Flush, and Norristhorpe in <a href="/wiki/Liversedge" title="Liversedge">Liversedge</a> south of the <a href="/wiki/A62_road" title="A62 road">A62 road</a>. Heckmondwike ward is represented on <a href="/wiki/Kirklees_Council" title="Kirklees Council">Kirklees Council</a> by three Labour councillors.
</p><p>In 2003 the ward elected David Exley of the <a href="/wiki/British_National_Party" title="British National Party">British National Party</a>, after the serving councillor left the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> to run as an independent. Exley was re-elected in 2004, and in 2006 a second BNP member, Roger Roberts, was elected. Roberts had previously served as councillor for the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In May 2007 David Sheard (Labour), was returned. In May 2008 Exley lost his seat to Labour candidate Steve Hall. In May 2010 Roberts lost his seat to Labour candidate Viv Kendrick. Sheard was re-elected in 2011, and Hall was re-elected in 2012.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heckmondwike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Heckmondwike is the home of Heckmondwike Pėtanque Club who currently play in the West Yorkshire Petanque League. Heckmondwike Petanque Club also currently hosts the International tournament, La British Open<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>. 2018 was the tournament's inaugural year and proceedings were opened by local MP, <a href="/wiki/Tracy_Brabin" title="Tracy Brabin">Tracy Brabin</a>. 32 teams took part with players travelling from Belgium, France, Slovakia as well as from across the UK to take part. The 2018 final was won by players from the Chiltern region and was live streamed to over 10,000 people via Facebook.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Newspapers">Newspapers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Heckmondwike&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Newspapers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The weekly newspaper was the <i>Heckmondwike Herald</i> until Friday 15 August 2008 after which the title was merged into the <i>Spenborough Guardian</i> incorporating the Heckmondwike Herald. It covers the former <a href="/wiki/Spenborough" title="Spenborough">Spenborough Urban District</a> and Heckmondwike.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
