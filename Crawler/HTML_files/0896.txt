<p><b>Halifax</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;h&#39; in &#39;hi&#39;">h</span><span title="/æ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">æ</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;f&#39; in &#39;find&#39;">f</span><span title="/æ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">æ</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Minster_(church)" title="Minster (church)">minster town</a><sup id="cite_ref-halifaxcourier.co.uk_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-halifaxcourier.co.uk-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> in the <a href="/wiki/Calderdale" title="Calderdale">Metropolitan Borough of Calderdale</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, England. Historically in the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>, the town has been a centre of <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">woollen</a> manufacture from the 15th&#160;century onward, originally dealing through the <a href="/wiki/Piece_Hall" title="Piece Hall">Piece Hall</a>. Halifax is known for <a href="/wiki/Mackintosh%27s" title="Mackintosh&#39;s">Mackintosh's</a> <a href="/wiki/Chocolate" title="Chocolate">chocolate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toffee" title="Toffee">toffee</a> products including <a href="/wiki/Rolo" title="Rolo">Rolo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quality_Street_(confectionery)" title="Quality Street (confectionery)">Quality Street</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Halifax_(bank)" title="Halifax (bank)">Halifax Bank</a> was also founded and is still headquartered in Halifax. <a href="/wiki/Dean_Clough" title="Dean Clough">Dean Clough</a>, one of the largest textile factories in the world at more than <span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span> mile (800&#160;m) long, was in the north of the town. The premises have since been converted for office and retail use including a gym, theatre, <a href="/wiki/Travelodge_UK" title="Travelodge UK">Travelodge</a> and radio station.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponymy">Toponymy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halifax,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Toponymy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2ndEarlofHalifaxByJoshuaReynoldsNSArtGallery.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/2ndEarlofHalifaxByJoshuaReynoldsNSArtGallery.jpg/180px-2ndEarlofHalifaxByJoshuaReynoldsNSArtGallery.jpg" decoding="async" width="180" height="218" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/2ndEarlofHalifaxByJoshuaReynoldsNSArtGallery.jpg/270px-2ndEarlofHalifaxByJoshuaReynoldsNSArtGallery.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/2ndEarlofHalifaxByJoshuaReynoldsNSArtGallery.jpg/360px-2ndEarlofHalifaxByJoshuaReynoldsNSArtGallery.jpg 2x" data-file-width="795" data-file-height="961" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2ndEarlofHalifaxByJoshuaReynoldsNSArtGallery.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/George_Montagu-Dunk,_2nd_Earl_of_Halifax" title="George Montagu-Dunk, 2nd Earl of Halifax">George Montagu-Dunk, 2nd Earl of Halifax</a></div></div></div>
<p>The town's name was recorded in about 1091 as <i>Halyfax</i>, from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old English language">Old English</a> <i>halh-gefeaxe</i>, meaning "area of coarse grass in the nook of land".<sup id="cite_ref-cambridge_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cambridge-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  This explanation is preferred to derivations from the Old English <i>halig</i> (holy), in <i>hālig feax</i> or "holy hair", proposed by 16th-century antiquarians.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  The incorrect interpretation gave rise to two legends. One concerned a maiden killed by a lustful priest whose advances she spurned. Another held that the head of <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a> was buried here after his execution.<sup id="cite_ref-cambridge_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cambridge-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The legend is almost certainly medieval rather than ancient, although the town's <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a> carries an image of the saint. Another explanation is a corruption of the Old English <i>hay</i> and <i>ley</i> a clearing or meadow. This etymology is based on Haley Hill, the nearby hamlet of <a href="/wiki/Healey,_Kirklees" title="Healey, Kirklees">Healey</a> (another corruption), and the common occurrence of the surnames Hayley/Haley around Halifax.<sup id="cite_ref-haliflax_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-haliflax-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The erroneous derivation from <i>halig</i> has given rise to the <a href="/wiki/Demonym" title="Demonym">demonym</a> Haligonian, which is of recent origin and not in universal use.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Halifax" title="Earl of Halifax">Earldom of Halifax</a> took the name of the town. Its first creation<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2017)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, in the <a href="/wiki/Peerage_of_England" title="Peerage of England">Peerage of England</a> in 1677, was for <a href="/wiki/George_Savile,_1st_Marquess_of_Halifax" title="George Savile, 1st Marquess of Halifax">William Savile</a>, who was created Baron Savile of Eland and Viscount Halifax in 1668 and later became the <a href="/wiki/Marquess_of_Halifax" title="Marquess of Halifax">Marquess of Halifax</a> (this creation of the earldom became extinct in 1700). <a href="/wiki/George_Montagu-Dunk,_2nd_Earl_of_Halifax" title="George Montagu-Dunk, 2nd Earl of Halifax">George Montagu-Dunk, 2nd Earl of Halifax</a>, (2nd order of the 3rd creation) became the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Board_of_Trade" title="President of the Board of Trade">President of the Board of Trade</a> in 1748. In 1749 the city of <a href="/wiki/Halifax,_Nova_Scotia" title="Halifax, Nova Scotia">Halifax</a>, the capital of <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>, Canada, was named in his honour.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Halifax_River" title="Halifax River">Halifax River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Central_Florida" title="Central Florida">Central Florida</a>, United States, was also named after him.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halifax,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Town_of_Halifax,Yorkshire,FromPennyMagazineMarch15,1834.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Town_of_Halifax%2CYorkshire%2CFromPennyMagazineMarch15%2C1834.PNG/240px-Town_of_Halifax%2CYorkshire%2CFromPennyMagazineMarch15%2C1834.PNG" decoding="async" width="240" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Town_of_Halifax%2CYorkshire%2CFromPennyMagazineMarch15%2C1834.PNG/360px-Town_of_Halifax%2CYorkshire%2CFromPennyMagazineMarch15%2C1834.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Town_of_Halifax%2CYorkshire%2CFromPennyMagazineMarch15%2C1834.PNG/480px-Town_of_Halifax%2CYorkshire%2CFromPennyMagazineMarch15%2C1834.PNG 2x" data-file-width="951" data-file-height="676" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Town_of_Halifax,Yorkshire,FromPennyMagazineMarch15,1834.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Halifax in 1834</div></div></div>
<p>Halifax is not mentioned in the <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i>, and evidence of the early settlement is indefinite.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> By the 12th&#160;century the township had become the religious centre of the vast parish of Halifax, which extended from <a href="/wiki/Brighouse" title="Brighouse">Brighouse</a> in the east to <a href="/wiki/Heptonstall" title="Heptonstall">Heptonstall</a> in the west.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Halifax_Minster" title="Halifax Minster">Halifax Minster</a>, parts of which date from the 12th&#160;century is dedicated to St John the Baptist. The minster's first organist, in 1766, was <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">William Herschel</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> who discovered the planet <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">Uranus</a>. The coat of arms of Halifax include the chequers from the original coat of arms of the Earls Warenne, who held the town during <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a> times.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Halifax was notorious for its <a href="/wiki/Halifax_Gibbet" title="Halifax Gibbet">gibbet</a>, an early form of <a href="/wiki/Guillotine" title="Guillotine">guillotine</a> used to <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">execute</a> criminals by <a href="/wiki/Decapitation" title="Decapitation">decapitation</a>, that was last used in 1650. A replica has been erected on the original site in Gibbet Street. Its original blade is on display at <a href="/wiki/Bankfield_Museum" title="Bankfield Museum">Bankfield Museum</a>. Punishment in Halifax was notoriously harsh, as remembered in the <i>Beggar's Litany</i><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/John_Taylor_(poet)" title="John Taylor (poet)">John Taylor</a> (1580–1654), a prayer whose text included "From <a href="/wiki/Kingston_upon_Hull" title="Kingston upon Hull">Hull</a>, from Halifax, from <a href="/wiki/Hell" title="Hell">Hell</a>, ‘tis thus, From all these three, Good Lord deliver us.".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The town's 19th century wealth came from the cotton, wool and carpet industries and like most other Yorkshire towns, it had a large number of <a href="/wiki/Weaving" title="Weaving">weaving</a> <a href="/wiki/Textile_mill" class="mw-redirect" title="Textile mill">mills</a> many of which have been lost or converted to alternate use.
</p><p>In November 1938, in an incident of <a href="/wiki/Mass_hysteria" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass hysteria">mass hysteria</a>, many residents believed a serial killer, the <a href="/wiki/Halifax_Slasher" title="Halifax Slasher">Halifax Slasher</a>, was on the loose. <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Yard" title="Scotland Yard">Scotland Yard</a> concluded there were no attacks after several locals admitted they had inflicted wounds on themselves.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Halifax_(bank)" title="Halifax (bank)">Halifax plc</a> started as a <a href="/wiki/Building_society" title="Building society">building society</a>, the Halifax Permanent Benefit Building and Investment Society, in the town in 1853. Today the bank operates as a trading name of <a href="/wiki/HBOS" title="HBOS">HBOS</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Lloyds_Banking_Group" title="Lloyds Banking Group">Lloyds Banking Group</a>. <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Bank" title="Yorkshire Bank">Yorkshire Bank</a>, based in <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> and known as the <i><a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding</a> Penny Savings Bank</i>, was established on 1 May 1859 by Colonel <a href="/wiki/Edward_Akroyd" title="Edward Akroyd">Edward Akroyd</a> of Halifax. Halifax is <a href="/wiki/Town_twinning" class="mw-redirect" title="Town twinning">twinned</a> with <a href="/wiki/Aachen" title="Aachen">Aachen</a> in Germany. The <a href="/wiki/A58_road" title="A58 road">A58</a> has a stretch called Aachen Way.
</p><p>Halifax has benefited from Single Regeneration Budget, European URBAN II and the Home Office’s Community Cohesion Fund money through Action Halifax <sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> who have a vision for "a prosperous, vibrant and safe centre where all sections of the community can access opportunities to enhance their quality of life."
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halifax,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Halifax_Town_Hall.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Halifax_Town_Hall.jpg/220px-Halifax_Town_Hall.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Halifax_Town_Hall.jpg/330px-Halifax_Town_Hall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Halifax_Town_Hall.jpg/440px-Halifax_Town_Hall.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="547" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Halifax_Town_Hall.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Halifax_Town_Hall" title="Halifax Town Hall">Halifax Town Hall</a></div></div></div>
<p>The ancient parish of Halifax was divided into a large number of civil parishes in the 19th&#160;century. In Halifax, a body of <a href="/wiki/Improvement_commissioners" title="Improvement commissioners">improvement commissioners</a> or town trustees was created between 1762 and 1823,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and the town became a <a href="/wiki/Halifax_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Halifax (UK Parliament constituency)">borough constituency</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Great_Reform_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Reform Act">Great Reform Act</a> of 1832. Halifax was incorporated as a <a href="/wiki/Municipal_borough" title="Municipal borough">municipal borough</a> in 1848 under the <a href="/wiki/Municipal_Corporations_Act_1835" title="Municipal Corporations Act 1835">Municipal Corporations Act 1835</a>, and, with the passing of the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1888" title="Local Government Act 1888">Local Government Act 1888</a>, became a <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county borough</a> in 1889. Since 1974, Halifax has been the <a href="/wiki/Administrative_centre" title="Administrative centre">administrative centre</a> of the Metropolitan Borough of <a href="/wiki/Calderdale" title="Calderdale">Calderdale</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halifax,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Paul%27s_Church_-_Queens_Road,_Halifax_-_geograph.org.uk_-_698428.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/St_Paul%27s_Church_-_Queens_Road%2C_Halifax_-_geograph.org.uk_-_698428.jpg/220px-St_Paul%27s_Church_-_Queens_Road%2C_Halifax_-_geograph.org.uk_-_698428.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="279" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/St_Paul%27s_Church_-_Queens_Road%2C_Halifax_-_geograph.org.uk_-_698428.jpg/330px-St_Paul%27s_Church_-_Queens_Road%2C_Halifax_-_geograph.org.uk_-_698428.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/St_Paul%27s_Church_-_Queens_Road%2C_Halifax_-_geograph.org.uk_-_698428.jpg/440px-St_Paul%27s_Church_-_Queens_Road%2C_Halifax_-_geograph.org.uk_-_698428.jpg 2x" data-file-width="504" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Paul%27s_Church_-_Queens_Road,_Halifax_-_geograph.org.uk_-_698428.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>St Paul's Church</div></div></div>
<p>Topographically, Halifax is located in the south-eastern corner of the moorland region called the <a href="/wiki/South_Pennines" title="South Pennines">South Pennines</a>. Halifax is situated about 4 miles (6&#160;km) from the <a href="/wiki/M62_motorway" title="M62 motorway">M62 motorway</a>, close to <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Tees-Exe_line" title="Tees-Exe line">Tees-Exe line</a> passes through the <a href="/wiki/A641_road" class="mw-redirect" title="A641 road">A641 road</a>, which links <a href="/wiki/Brighouse" title="Brighouse">Brighouse</a> with Bradford and Huddersfield, The town lies 65 miles (105&#160;km) from <a href="/wiki/Kingston_upon_Hull" title="Kingston upon Hull">Hull</a> and <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>, and about 170 miles (270&#160;km) from the cities of London, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cardiff" title="Cardiff">Cardiff</a> as the crow flies. The <a href="/wiki/Hebble_Brook" title="Hebble Brook">Hebble Brook</a> joins the <a href="/wiki/River_Calder,_West_Yorkshire" title="River Calder, West Yorkshire">River Calder</a> at <a href="/wiki/Salterhebble" title="Salterhebble">Salterhebble</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demography">Demography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halifax,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Demography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2004<sup id="cite_ref-ward_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Calderdale had a population of 192,405, of which 82,500 live in the Halifax <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban area</a>. The main ethnic group in Halifax is <a href="/wiki/Caucasian_race" title="Caucasian race">White</a> (87%), followed by <a href="/wiki/Pakistani_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Pakistani people">Pakistani</a> (10%). Over 90% of people aged 16–74 were employed, mostly full-time. 64% of residents had qualifications.
Halifax is home to a large <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asian</a> community mainly of <a href="/wiki/British_Pakistanis" title="British Pakistanis">British Pakistanis</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Kashmir_region" class="mw-redirect" title="Kashmir region">Kashmir region</a>, which originally moved to the area for employment in the textile industry. The majority of the community lives in the west central Halifax region of the town, which was previously home to immigrant <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish</a> communities who have since moved to the outer suburbs. The Illingworth and Mixenden areas, in contrast to west central Halifax, consists mostly of white, <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> residents. In the 2001 census,<sup id="cite_ref-ward_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ward-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> 5% stated they were <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslim</a>, 16.3% of no religion, and 63.8% of <a href="/wiki/Christians" title="Christians">Christian</a> background. 12.8% did not disclose their religion. The population density of the Halifax urban area is 530/km<sup>2</sup>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halifax,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Joseph_Crossley%27s_Almshouses.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c0/Joseph_Crossley%27s_Almshouses.jpg/220px-Joseph_Crossley%27s_Almshouses.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c0/Joseph_Crossley%27s_Almshouses.jpg/330px-Joseph_Crossley%27s_Almshouses.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/c/c0/Joseph_Crossley%27s_Almshouses.jpg/440px-Joseph_Crossley%27s_Almshouses.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Joseph_Crossley%27s_Almshouses.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Joseph Crossley's Almshouses</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HBOS_Halifax_310805.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/HBOS_Halifax_310805.JPG/220px-HBOS_Halifax_310805.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/HBOS_Halifax_310805.JPG/330px-HBOS_Halifax_310805.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/HBOS_Halifax_310805.JPG/440px-HBOS_Halifax_310805.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HBOS_Halifax_310805.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The former <a href="/wiki/Halifax_(United_Kingdom_bank)" class="mw-redirect" title="Halifax (United Kingdom bank)">Halifax Bank</a> headquarters on Trinity Road</div></div></div>
<p>As well as the significance of the bank <a href="/wiki/Halifax_(United_Kingdom_bank)" class="mw-redirect" title="Halifax (United Kingdom bank)">Halifax plc</a> which, since 2008, is part of the <a href="/wiki/Lloyds_Banking_Group" title="Lloyds Banking Group">Lloyds Banking Group</a>, the town has strong associations with confectionery.
</p><p>John Mackintosh and his wife, Violet, opened a <a href="/wiki/Toffee" title="Toffee">toffee</a> shop in <a href="/wiki/King_Cross" title="King Cross">King Cross</a> Lane in 1890. Violet formulated the toffee's recipe. John became known as "The Toffee King". A factory was opened on Queens Road in 1898. A new factory at Albion Mill, at the current site near the railway station, opened in 1909. John died in 1920, and his son Harold not only continued the business but took it to the present size and range of confectionery it has today. Their famous brands, including <a href="/wiki/Rolo" title="Rolo">Rolo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toffee_Crisp" class="mw-redirect" title="Toffee Crisp">Toffee Crisp</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quality_Street_(confection)" class="mw-redirect" title="Quality Street (confection)">Quality Street</a> of chocolate and confectionery are not just popular in the UK, but around the world including the USA.
</p><p>In 1969 <a href="/wiki/Mackintosh%27s" title="Mackintosh&#39;s">John Mackintosh &amp; Co Limited</a> merged with the <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>-based <a href="/wiki/Rowntree%27s" title="Rowntree&#39;s">Rowntree Limited</a> to form <a href="/wiki/Rowntree_Mackintosh" class="mw-redirect" title="Rowntree Mackintosh">Rowntree Mackintosh</a>. This was, in turn, purchased by <a href="/wiki/Nestl%C3%A9" title="Nestlé">Nestlé</a> in 1988.
</p><p>Halifax was a busy industrial town, dealing in and producing wool, carpets, machine tools and beer. The Crossley family began carpet manufacture in modest premises at <a href="/wiki/Dean_Clough" title="Dean Clough">Dean Clough</a>, on the banks of <a href="/wiki/Hebble_Brook" title="Hebble Brook">Hebble Brook</a>. The family was philanthropic and Joseph and <a href="/wiki/Francis_Crossley" title="Francis Crossley">Sir Francis Crossley</a> built and endowed <a href="/wiki/Almshouse" title="Almshouse">almshouses</a> for their workers, which exist to this day and are run by volunteer trustees. Halifax is also home to <a href="/wiki/Suma_(co-operative)" title="Suma (co-operative)">Suma Wholefoods</a>, which was established in 1975 and is the largest <a href="/wiki/Worker_cooperative" title="Worker cooperative">workers' co-operative</a> in the UK.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halifax,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Halifaxmap1954.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Halifaxmap1954.png/220px-Halifaxmap1954.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="286" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Halifaxmap1954.png/330px-Halifaxmap1954.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Halifaxmap1954.png/440px-Halifaxmap1954.png 2x" data-file-width="2125" data-file-height="2761" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Halifaxmap1954.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A map of Halifax from 1954</div></div></div>
<p>Public bus and train transportation in Halifax is managed and subsidised by the <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire_Passenger_Transport_Executive" class="mw-redirect" title="West Yorkshire Passenger Transport Executive">West Yorkshire Passenger Transport Executive</a>. It was announced in January 2009 that Halifax was to have a direct rail link to London after a long campaign backed by many, including the local paper the <i>Courier</i>; the service began to run on 23 May 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bus">Bus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halifax,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Bus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most of the bus services in Halifax operate from the town's <a href="/wiki/Halifax_bus_station" title="Halifax bus station">bus station</a>.<a href="/wiki/First_Calderdale_%26_Huddersfield" title="First Calderdale &amp; Huddersfield">First Calderdale &amp; Huddersfield</a> operate most of the town's services, while <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Tiger" title="Yorkshire Tiger">Yorkshire Tiger</a> operate many of the south Calderdale services.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Arriva_Yorkshire" title="Arriva Yorkshire">Arriva Yorkshire</a> operate services that link Halifax with the West Yorkshire towns and cities of <a href="/wiki/Dewsbury" title="Dewsbury">Dewsbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a>. First operate bus services from Halifax to the town of Huddersfield and the nearby cities of Bradford and Leeds. First also run services into other counties, <a href="/wiki/Rochdale" title="Rochdale">Rochdale</a> in <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester" title="Greater Manchester">Greater Manchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Burnley" title="Burnley">Burnley</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>. Other bus operators in the town include T.J. Walsh (also known as the Halifax Bus Company) and <a href="/wiki/Halifax_Joint_Committee" title="Halifax Joint Committee">Halifax Joint Committee</a> which use the livery of the old Halifax Corporation buses, used on the town's buses until 1974.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rail">Rail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halifax,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Rail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Halifax_railway_station_(West_Yorkshire)" class="mw-redirect" title="Halifax railway station (West Yorkshire)">Halifax railway station (West Yorkshire)</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Halifax_railway_station_(West_Yorkshire)" class="mw-redirect" title="Halifax railway station (West Yorkshire)">Halifax railway station</a> is on the <a href="/wiki/Caldervale_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Caldervale Line">Caldervale Line</a>, with services to <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Victoria_station" title="Manchester Victoria station">Manchester Victoria</a>, <a href="/wiki/York_railway_station" title="York railway station">York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Selby_railway_station" title="Selby railway station">Selby</a> via <a href="/wiki/Bradford_Interchange" title="Bradford Interchange">Bradford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leeds_railway_station" title="Leeds railway station">Leeds</a>; <a href="/wiki/Blackpool_North_railway_station" title="Blackpool North railway station">Blackpool North</a>; via <a href="/wiki/Brighouse_railway_station" title="Brighouse railway station">Brighouse</a> to <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_railway_station" title="Huddersfield railway station">Huddersfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Westgate_railway_station" title="Wakefield Westgate railway station">Wakefield Westgate</a> and to <a href="/wiki/London_King%27s_Cross_railway_station" title="London King&#39;s Cross railway station">London King's Cross</a> via <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Kirkgate_railway_station" title="Wakefield Kirkgate railway station">Wakefield Kirkgate</a>. All but the London service are operated by <a href="/wiki/Northern_(train_operating_company)" title="Northern (train operating company)">Northern</a>.
</p><p>Rail passenger representation is organised by the local users' group, the Halifax and District Rail Action Group (HADRAG).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The rail line leading from Halifax due north towards <a href="/wiki/Keighley" title="Keighley">Keighley</a> (and thus towards <a href="/wiki/Skipton" title="Skipton">Skipton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morecambe" title="Morecambe">Morecambe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carlisle_railway_station" title="Carlisle railway station">Carlisle</a>) with a further branch to Bradford via <a href="/wiki/Queensbury,_West_Yorkshire" title="Queensbury, West Yorkshire">Queensbury</a> saw its last through services in May 1955, although parts of the route, which was extremely heavily engineered with long tunnels and high, spectacular <a href="/wiki/Viaduct" title="Viaduct">viaducts</a>, have now been repaired and revived by Sustrans as a walking and cycle route. In 2018 a campaign was launched to save and restore the 2,501 yards (2,287&#160;m) <a href="/wiki/Queensbury_Tunnel" title="Queensbury Tunnel">Queensbury Tunnel</a> and add it to the walking and cycling network.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> A branch from Holmfield, on the Halifax to Queensbury section of the lines to Keighley and Bradford, served the west side of Halifax. It terminated at St Pauls station. This short branch closed to passengers in January 1927 and to all traffic in June 1960.
</p><p>Halifax is also served by <a href="/wiki/Sowerby_Bridge_railway_station" title="Sowerby Bridge railway station">Sowerby Bridge railway station</a> on the southwest edge of the town, just to the south of the <a href="/wiki/River_Calder,_West_Yorkshire" title="River Calder, West Yorkshire">River Calder</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halifax,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Calderdale's BBC local radio station is <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_Leeds" title="BBC Radio Leeds">BBC Radio Leeds</a>.
Calderdale's local radio station, <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Radio_96.7_FM_(Calderdale)" class="mw-redirect" title="Phoenix Radio 96.7 FM (Calderdale)">Phoenix Radio 96.7 FM</a> has its studios in Halifax, and the <i>Courier</i>, Calderdale's local weekly newspaper, has its offices in the town.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halifax,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Halifax area is home to two selective state schools, which are <a href="/wiki/The_Crossley_Heath_School" title="The Crossley Heath School">the Crossley Heath School</a> in Savile Park<sup id="cite_ref-chs_selective_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chs_selective-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/North_Halifax_Grammar_School" title="North Halifax Grammar School">North Halifax Grammar School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Illingworth,_West_Yorkshire" title="Illingworth, West Yorkshire">Illingworth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nhgs_selective_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhgs_selective-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>  Both schools achieve excellent <a href="/wiki/General_Certificate_of_Secondary_Education" title="General Certificate of Secondary Education">GCSE</a> and <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Level_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced Level (UK)">A-level</a> results with both schools achieving a large proportion of A*to C grades at GCSE level. In 2005, the Crossley Heath School was the highest ranking co-educational school in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">North of England</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Calderdale_College" title="Calderdale College">Calderdale College</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Further_education_college" class="mw-redirect" title="Further education college">further education college</a> located on Francis Street, just off King Cross Road, in the west of the town. <a href="/wiki/The_Maltings_College" title="The Maltings College">The Maltings College</a> opened in 2013 and offers a range of vocational <a href="/wiki/Sixth_form" title="Sixth form">sixth form</a> courses.
</p><p>In December 2006 it was announced that Calderdale College, in partnership with <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Beckett_University" title="Leeds Beckett University">Leeds Beckett University</a>, opened a new higher education institution in January 2007 called 'University Centre Calderdale'.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Wellington%27s_Regiment" title="Duke of Wellington&#39;s Regiment">Duke of Wellington's Regiment (West Riding)</a> Regimental Association, previously based at Wellesley Park, on the junction of Gibbet Street and Spring Hall Road, in the former <a href="/wiki/Wellesley_Barracks" title="Wellesley Barracks">Wellesley Barracks</a> is located within the <a href="/wiki/Bankfield_Museum" title="Bankfield Museum">Bankfield Museum</a> on Boothtown Road. The former <a href="/wiki/Barracks" title="Barracks">barracks</a> was converted into an educational school in 2005.
</p><p>Former <a href="/wiki/Colours,_standards_and_guidons" class="mw-redirect" title="Colours, standards and guidons">regimental colours</a> of the 'Duke's' are laid up in the Halifax Minster. These include the stand used by the 33rd Regiment between 1761 and 1771, which is one of the oldest in existence in England, plus those carried by the regiment during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Waterloo" title="Battle of Waterloo">Battle of Waterloo</a> and the Crimea.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The 1981 stand of colours, was taken out of service in 2002. They were marched through the town from the <a href="/wiki/Halifax_Town_Hall" title="Halifax Town Hall">town hall</a> to the minster, which at that time was still a parish church, accompanied by two escorts of 40 troops, the Regimental Drums and the Heavy Cavalry and Cambrai Band on Sunday 31 March 2007. The troops were then inspected by the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant_of_West_Yorkshire" title="Lord Lieutenant of West Yorkshire">Lord Lieutenant of West Yorkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ingrid_Roscoe" title="Ingrid Roscoe">Dr Ingrid Roscoe BA, PhD, FSA</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">Mayor</a> of Halifax Cllr Colin Stout making a total of eight stands of colours within the Regimental Chapel. The regiment was presented with the "Freedom of Halifax" on 18 June 1945.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Eureka!_(museum)" title="Eureka! (museum)">Eureka! The National Children's Museum</a> was inspired and opened by <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">Prince Charles</a> in the summer of 1992 and is in part of the railway station. Once the home of the diarist <a href="/wiki/Anne_Lister" title="Anne Lister">Anne Lister</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shibden_Hall" title="Shibden Hall">Shibden Hall</a> is just outside Halifax in the neighbouring Shibden Valley. <a href="/wiki/Dean_Clough" title="Dean Clough">Dean Clough</a>, a refurbished <a href="/wiki/Worsted" title="Worsted">worsted</a> spinning mill, is the home of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Broadsides" title="Northern Broadsides">Northern Broadsides</a> Theatre Company and the IOU theatre company as well as providing space for eight art galleries. The Artworks is a collection of artists studios, gallery space and an art school housed in an old mill complex just to the south of the town centre.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A recent survey showed Halifax to have above average levels of drink-related violence and associated issues.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Landmarks">Landmarks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halifax,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Landmarks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Piece_Hall,_Halifax_(36239750242).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Piece_Hall%2C_Halifax_%2836239750242%29.jpg/220px-Piece_Hall%2C_Halifax_%2836239750242%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Piece_Hall%2C_Halifax_%2836239750242%29.jpg/330px-Piece_Hall%2C_Halifax_%2836239750242%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Piece_Hall%2C_Halifax_%2836239750242%29.jpg/440px-Piece_Hall%2C_Halifax_%2836239750242%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5636" data-file-height="3881" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Piece_Hall,_Halifax_(36239750242).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Piece_Hall" title="Piece Hall">Piece Hall</a></div></div></div>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Piece_Hall" title="Piece Hall">Piece Hall</a> is the former cloth hall, where pieces of woollen cloth were traded. Opened on 1 January 1779, trading took place for two hours on a Saturday morning in a total of 315 merchant trading rooms. After the <a href="/wiki/Mechanization" title="Mechanization">mechanisation</a> of the cloth industry, the Piece Hall became a public market. Piece Hall is host to many arts, crafts and independent shops. The Piece Hall has recently undergone a £19 million conservation and transformation programme. The works were completed in July 2017, with the building fully reopened in the August (on Yorkshire day) with shops, cafes and events.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dean_Clough" title="Dean Clough">Dean Clough Mill</a> located beside the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a> <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival_architecture" title="Gothic Revival architecture">Gothic Revival</a> <a href="/wiki/North_Bridge,_Halifax" title="North Bridge, Halifax">North Bridge</a> was built in the 1840s–'60s for Crossley's Carpets, owned by John Crossley and was once the largest carpet factory in the world. It was converted to a business park in the 1980s.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Halifax_Town_Hall" title="Halifax Town Hall">Halifax Town Hall</a> was designed by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Barry" title="Charles Barry">Charles Barry</a>, who also designed the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Houses of Parliament</a>, in 1863.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Borough_Market,_Halifax" title="Borough Market, Halifax">Borough Market</a> is a Victorian covered market-place in the town centre.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wainhouse_Tower" title="Wainhouse Tower">Wainhouse Tower</a>, at <a href="/wiki/King_Cross" title="King Cross">King Cross</a>, is a late Victorian <a href="/wiki/Folly" title="Folly">folly</a> constructed between 1871 and 1875. Originally intended to be the chimney for a dye works, it became a folly after the dye works was sold in 1874 and the new owner refused to pay for its completion. It is the tallest folly in the world and the tallest structure in <a href="/wiki/Calderdale" title="Calderdale">Calderdale</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/People%27s_Park_(Halifax)" class="mw-redirect" title="People&#39;s Park (Halifax)">People's Park</a> is a public park originally designed by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Paxton" title="Joseph Paxton">Joseph Paxton</a>, given to the people of Halifax in 1857 by Sir <a href="/wiki/Francis_Crossley" title="Francis Crossley">Francis Crossley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Prescott_Street_drill_hall,_Halifax" title="Prescott Street drill hall, Halifax">Prescott Street drill hall</a> designed by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Coad" title="Richard Coad">Richard Coad</a> and completed in 1870.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halifax,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Shay.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/The_Shay.jpg/220px-The_Shay.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="125" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/The_Shay.jpg/330px-The_Shay.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/The_Shay.jpg/440px-The_Shay.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="729" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Shay.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/The_Shay" title="The Shay">The Shay</a>, the town's football and rugby league stadium.</div></div></div>
<p>The town has relatively successful sports clubs. Its <a href="/wiki/Rugby_league" title="Rugby league">rugby league</a> club, <a href="/wiki/Halifax_RLFC" class="mw-redirect" title="Halifax RLFC">Halifax RLFC</a>, plays in the <a href="/wiki/Betfred_Championship" class="mw-redirect" title="Betfred Championship">Betfred Championship</a>. The town's <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football team</a>, <a href="/wiki/F.C._Halifax_Town" title="F.C. Halifax Town">F.C. Halifax Town</a> participates in the <a href="/wiki/National_League_(division)" title="National League (division)">National League</a>, the fifth tier of English football.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Football">Football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halifax,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_Shay" title="The Shay">The Shay</a> football ground has been the home of the town's football club since 1921. The ground was substantially redeveloped in the late 20th and early 21st&#160;centuries, with money provided by the <a href="/wiki/Football_Foundation" title="Football Foundation">Football Foundation</a> and funds raised or provided by the local community and Calderdale Council.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rugby_league">Rugby league</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halifax,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Rugby league">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Halifax is home to the professional rugby league academy, Halifax Elite Rugby Academy, which has a partnership with <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_Giants" title="Huddersfield Giants">Huddersfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Castleford_Tigers" title="Castleford Tigers">Castleford</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Halifax_RLFC" class="mw-redirect" title="Halifax RLFC">Halifax RLFC</a> is one of the most historic rugby league clubs in the game, formed over a century ago, in 1873. Amateur clubs Boothtown Terriers, Greetland All Rounders, Illingworth, King Cross Park, Ovenden, <a href="/wiki/Siddal_A.R.L.F.C." title="Siddal A.R.L.F.C.">Siddal</a> and Stainland Stags are based in or near the town. The Siddal club is a leading member of amateur rugby league's flagship <a href="/wiki/National_Conference_League" title="National Conference League">National Conference League</a>. Greetland All Rounders and Ovenden are former members.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Speedway">Speedway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halifax,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Speedway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_speedway" title="Motorcycle speedway">Motorcycle speedway</a> racing has been staged at two venues in Halifax. In the pioneering days of 1928–1930 a track operated at <a href="/wiki/Thrum_Hall" title="Thrum Hall">Thrum Hall</a>. A Halifax team took part in the <a href="/wiki/Speedway_English_Dirt_Track_League" class="mw-redirect" title="Speedway English Dirt Track League">English Dirt Track League</a> of 1929.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Speedway returned to Halifax at the Shay Stadium in 1949 and operated until 1951.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The team operated as the Halifax Nomads in 1948 racing three away fixtures. The Halifax Dukes, the name they took once the Shay was opened, operated in the National League Third Division in 1949 before moving up to the Second Division in 1950. Riders including Arthur Forrest, moved on to <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a>. The Dukes re-emerged in 1965 as founder members of the British League and operated there for many years before the team moved en bloc to Odsal Stadium, Bradford. The steeply banked bends of the track at the Shay have been buried under stands at either end when the spectator facilities were squared off.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_buildings">Religious buildings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Halifax,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Religious buildings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:All_Souls_church,_Halifax.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/All_Souls_church%2C_Halifax.JPG/220px-All_Souls_church%2C_Halifax.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/All_Souls_church%2C_Halifax.JPG/330px-All_Souls_church%2C_Halifax.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/All_Souls_church%2C_Halifax.JPG/440px-All_Souls_church%2C_Halifax.JPG 2x" data-file-width="960" data-file-height="1280" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:All_Souls_church,_Halifax.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/All_Souls_Church,_Halifax" class="mw-redirect" title="All Souls Church, Halifax">All Souls' Church</a> (now redundant) and statue of <a href="/wiki/Edward_Akroyd" title="Edward Akroyd">Edward Akroyd</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Assembly_Rooms_and_Trinity_Church_in_Halifax_from_A_Complete_History_of_the_County_of_York_by_Thomas_Allen_(1828-30).JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/48/The_Assembly_Rooms_and_Trinity_Church_in_Halifax_from_A_Complete_History_of_the_County_of_York_by_Thomas_Allen_%281828-30%29.JPG/220px-The_Assembly_Rooms_and_Trinity_Church_in_Halifax_from_A_Complete_History_of_the_County_of_York_by_Thomas_Allen_%281828-30%29.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="132" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/48/The_Assembly_Rooms_and_Trinity_Church_in_Halifax_from_A_Complete_History_of_the_County_of_York_by_Thomas_Allen_%281828-30%29.JPG/330px-The_Assembly_Rooms_and_Trinity_Church_in_Halifax_from_A_Complete_History_of_the_County_of_York_by_Thomas_Allen_%281828-30%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/48/The_Assembly_Rooms_and_Trinity_Church_in_Halifax_from_A_Complete_History_of_the_County_of_York_by_Thomas_Allen_%281828-30%29.JPG/440px-The_Assembly_Rooms_and_Trinity_Church_in_Halifax_from_A_Complete_History_of_the_County_of_York_by_Thomas_Allen_%281828-30%29.JPG 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="301" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Assembly_Rooms_and_Trinity_Church_in_Halifax_from_A_Complete_History_of_the_County_of_York_by_Thomas_Allen_(1828-30).JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Assembly Rooms and Trinity Church in Halifax from <i>A Complete History of the County of York</i> by Thomas Allen (1828–30)</div></div></div>
<p>The 15th-century Minster dedicated to <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">St. John the Baptist</a> did not achieve <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">cathedral</a> status when a new diocese was being considered for the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding</a> (<a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a> Parish Church became the cathedral in 1888 and was extensively altered and enlarged). <a href="/wiki/Minster_(church)" title="Minster (church)">Minster</a> Status was conferred on the Parish Church in a ceremony on 22 November 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-halifaxcourier.co.uk_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-halifaxcourier.co.uk-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> There is a collection of rare <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth" title="Commonwealth">Commonwealth</a> white glass as well as a series of <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian</a> windows. Another feature is the complete array of <a href="/wiki/Jacobean_era" title="Jacobean era">Jacobean</a> <a href="/wiki/Box_pew" title="Box pew">box pews</a>. The pair of <a href="/wiki/Gothic_art" title="Gothic art">Gothic</a> organ cases by John Oldrid Scott now house the four-manual instrument by Harrison &amp; Harrison. The belfry holds fourteen bells and an <a href="/wiki/Angelus" title="Angelus">Angelus</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/St_Mary%27s_Church,_Halifax" title="St Mary&#39;s Church, Halifax">St Mary's Roman Catholic Church</a> is located on the corner of Gibbet Street and Clarence Street, was originally built in 1839, rebuilt in 1864 and extended in 1924.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Serbian_Orthodox_Church" title="Serbian Orthodox Church">Serbian Orthodox Church</a> dedicated to <a href="/wiki/St_John_the_Baptist" class="mw-redirect" title="St John the Baptist">St John the Baptist</a>, in the Boothtown area, formerly the Mount Carmel Methodist Chapel, was acquired in 1956 and after extensive refurbishment was opened in the 1965 by the town's <a href="/wiki/Serbs" title="Serbs">Serbian</a> community. In 2015 the church celebrated its Golden Jubilee.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The currently <a href="/wiki/Mothball" title="Mothball">mothballed</a> mid-<a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a> <a href="/wiki/All_Souls_Church,_Halifax" class="mw-redirect" title="All Souls Church, Halifax">All Souls' Church</a> by Sir <a href="/wiki/George_Gilbert_Scott" title="George Gilbert Scott">George Gilbert Scott</a> standing part way up Haley Hill to the north of the main town centre is now vested in the <a href="/wiki/Historic_Churches_Preservation_Trust" class="mw-redirect" title="Historic Churches Preservation Trust">Historic Churches Preservation Trust</a>. Its lofty 236-foot (72&#160;m) spire and white magnesian limestone exterior stand as a very personal statement in 13th-century French style of the mill owner Colonel <a href="/wiki/Edward_Akroyd" title="Edward Akroyd">Edward Akroyd</a>, who paid solely for its construction as the centre-piece of a purpose-built model village ("Akroydon"). <a href="/wiki/All_Souls%27_Day" title="All Souls&#39; Day">All Souls'</a> boasts an unusually complete sequence of windows by the leading artists of the 1850s, including William Wailes, John Hardman and Clayton &amp; Bell. The large organ by Forster &amp; Andrews inserted in 1868, ten years after the building was completed, is currently unplayable and many of its surviving parts are in storage awaiting restoration. The tower houses a ring of eight bells.
</p><p>Other churches include the <a href="/wiki/Georgian_architecture" title="Georgian architecture">Georgian</a> <a href="/wiki/Holy_Trinity" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Trinity">Holy Trinity</a> Church (now converted to office use) and the late <a href="/wiki/Neo-Gothic" class="mw-redirect" title="Neo-Gothic">neo-Gothic</a> (1911) <a href="/wiki/Saint_Paul_(apostle)" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Paul (apostle)">St. Paul's</a>, <a href="/wiki/King_Cross" title="King Cross">King Cross</a>, by Sir Charles Nicholson. St Paul's is notable not only for its fine <a href="/wiki/Acoustics" title="Acoustics">acoustics</a> but also for an unusual and highly colourful west window, specified by Nicholson, showing the apocalyptic vision of the Holy City descending upon the smoky mills and railway viaducts of Halifax as it was before the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/William_Swinden_Barber#Church_of_St_Jude,_Savile_Park,_1888" title="William Swinden Barber">Church of St Jude</a> in Savile Park, designed by local architect <a href="/wiki/William_Swinden_Barber" title="William Swinden Barber">William Swinden Barber</a> in 1888, is easily identified by the four large pinnacles on its tower.<sup id="cite_ref-EH_1254029_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EH_1254029-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> There is also a more modern <a href="/wiki/Christadelphian" class="mw-redirect" title="Christadelphian">Christadelphian</a> church, located on Balmoral Place.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The spire of the Square Church, not far from the minster at the bottom of the town, paid for by the carpet manufacturing Crossley family, is all that remains of the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival_architecture" title="Gothic Revival architecture">Gothic</a> <a href="/wiki/Congregational_church" title="Congregational church">Congregational church</a> built by Joseph James in 1856–58 as a rival design to All Souls', Haley Hill.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The building was closed in 1969 and arsonists caused severe damage to the building two years later leading to its partial demolition. The rather comic story of the rival spires runs that the two buildings' towers were nearing completion simultaneously; the architects were ordered to stop work within a few feet of the top of the spires to see who would finish first.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> After some time, the Crossleys lost patience and finished their spire at 235-foot (72&#160;m), prompting the immediate completion of the rival building one foot higher. The neighbouring and earlier (Georgian) <a href="/wiki/Square_Chapel" title="Square Chapel">Square Chapel</a> (1772) survived a hundred years of use as a church hall and <a href="/wiki/Sunday_school" title="Sunday school">Sunday school</a> for the larger church: it is currently an arts centre.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
