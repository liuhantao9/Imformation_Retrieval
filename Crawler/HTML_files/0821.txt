<p><b>David Brown Engineering Limited</b> is an English engineering company, principally engaged in the manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Gear" title="Gear">gears</a> and <a href="/wiki/Transmission_(mechanics)" title="Transmission (mechanics)">gearboxes</a>. Their major gear manufacturing plant is in Swan Lane, <a href="/wiki/Lockwood,_West_Yorkshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Lockwood, West Yorkshire">Lockwood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>, adjacent to <a href="/wiki/Lockwood_railway_station" title="Lockwood railway station">Lockwood railway station</a>. It is named after the company's founder, David Brown, though it is more closely associated with his grandson, <a href="/wiki/David_Brown_(entrepreneur)" title="David Brown (entrepreneur)">Sir David Brown</a> (1904–1993).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Brown_Ltd.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="David_Brown">David Brown</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Brown_Ltd.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: David Brown">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Founded in 1860 as a pattern manufacturing company by 1873 David Brown had begun to concentrate on gear systems and by 1898 was specialising in machine-cut gears. 
</p><p>The company moved in 1902 to Park Works at Huddersfield, where the firm is based today. 
</p>
<h3><span id="David_Brown_.26_Sons.2C_Huddersfield_.28the_Huddersfield_group.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="David_Brown_&amp;_Sons,_Huddersfield_(the_Huddersfield_group)">David Brown &amp; Sons, Huddersfield (the Huddersfield group)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Brown_Ltd.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: David Brown &amp; Sons, Huddersfield (the Huddersfield group)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When David Brown died in 1903, his sons Percy and Frank took over and began the manufacture of gears, complete gear units, gear cutting machines, tools and equipment, bearings and shafts and worm drive gears. Its foundry makes steel and non-ferrous castings.
Including motor vehicles, aircraft, ships as well as a wide range of British industry.<sup id="cite_ref-TT51947_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TT51947-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1951 the Huddersfield and Tractor groups freehold land and buildings at Huddersfield, Penistone and Meltham were on sites covering about  150 acres. Another 260,000 square feet of floor space were held under lease.<sup id="cite_ref-TT51947_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TT51947-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Gearing manufactured by David Brown Ltd. and powered by electric motors manufactured by Brook Crompton (Electric) Motors, whose factory was in <a href="/wiki/Brockholes" title="Brockholes">Brockholes</a> are used to rotate the top of the <a href="/wiki/BT_Tower" title="BT Tower">BT Tower</a> in London.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1908 to 1915 David Brown and Sons designed and developed and made the <a href="/wiki/Valveless" title="Valveless">Valveless</a> car under engineer Frederick Tasker Burgess (1879-1929) later chief engineer at <a href="/wiki/Humber" title="Humber">Humber</a> and later still one of the team that developed the first <a href="/wiki/Bentley_3_Litre" title="Bentley 3 Litre">3-litre Bentley</a> engine.
</p><p>In 1913 they established a joint venture in America with <a href="/wiki/Timken_Company" title="Timken Company">Timken</a> for Radicon worm drive units. By the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> the workforce had increased from 200 to 1000 as they started building propulsion units for warships, and drive mechanisms for armaments. By 1921 the company was the largest worm gear manufacturer in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-davidbrown.com_history_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-davidbrown.com_history-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1930 the company took over P.R. Jackson Ltd, another local firm of gear manufacturers and steel founders. Percy's eldest son (Sir David Brown) became managing director in 1931 following Percy's death in June that year. W S Roe was appointed joint managing director with David but he died in April 1933. Percy was appointed chairman. The firm formed another overseas joint venture with Richardson Gears (Pty) Ltd of Footscray, <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>, Australia in 1934. In 1934 the company moved into an old Silk Mill on a site at <a href="/wiki/Meltham" title="Meltham">Meltham</a>, on the south side of Huddersfield. Brown started building tractors with <a href="/wiki/Harry_Ferguson" title="Harry Ferguson">Harry Ferguson</a> there in 1936. 
</p><p>The company obtained a patent for a tank transmission using controlled differential steering system, known as the <b>Merritt-Brown</b> system, devised by Dr. H. E. Merritt, Director of Tank Design at <a href="/wiki/Royal_Arsenal" title="Royal Arsenal">Woolwich Arsenal</a>, in 1935.<sup id="cite_ref-davidbrown.com_history_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-davidbrown.com_history-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The first vehicle to use this system was the <a href="/wiki/Churchill_tank" title="Churchill tank">Churchill tank</a>, and it was subsequently used on the <a href="/wiki/Centurion_tank" class="mw-redirect" title="Centurion tank">Centurion tank</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Conqueror_tank" class="mw-redirect" title="Conqueror tank">Conqueror tank</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Tortoise_heavy_assault_tank" title="Tortoise heavy assault tank">Tortoise heavy tank</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="David_Brown_Tractors_Group">David Brown Tractors Group</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Brown_Ltd.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: David Brown Tractors Group">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:David_Brown_Tractors,_Meltham_Mills_(RLH).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/David_Brown_Tractors%2C_Meltham_Mills_%28RLH%29.jpg/220px-David_Brown_Tractors%2C_Meltham_Mills_%28RLH%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="117" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/David_Brown_Tractors%2C_Meltham_Mills_%28RLH%29.jpg/330px-David_Brown_Tractors%2C_Meltham_Mills_%28RLH%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/David_Brown_Tractors%2C_Meltham_Mills_%28RLH%29.jpg/440px-David_Brown_Tractors%2C_Meltham_Mills_%28RLH%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1480" data-file-height="789" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:David_Brown_Tractors,_Meltham_Mills_(RLH).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>David Brown Tractor Factory Meltham Mills 1981</div></div></div>
<p>Personally controlled since its inception by David Brown  (1904–1993) the first venture into tractor production was in a joint project with <a href="/wiki/Harry_Ferguson" title="Harry Ferguson">Harry Ferguson</a> in 1936 building the <b>Ferguson-Brown</b> tractor. David Brown became one of the biggest British tractor manufactures in the post war period, with a major manufacturing plant at Meltham, <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a> England. The company broke new ground which others were only to follow later, but being a pioneering company ultimately led to its downfall. The Ferguson-Brown had many innovative features, including the use of cast alloy for many the components, which was light but prone to damage. The Ferguson-Brown used a <a href="/wiki/Coventry_Climax" title="Coventry Climax">Coventry Climax</a> engine for the first 350 tractors. Browns developed their own engine which was fitted to subsequent production. Total production was 1350 + 1 built from parts in 1940 after production finished.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1971_David_Brown_990.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/1971_David_Brown_990.jpg/220px-1971_David_Brown_990.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="202" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/1971_David_Brown_990.jpg/330px-1971_David_Brown_990.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/1971_David_Brown_990.jpg/440px-1971_David_Brown_990.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3679" data-file-height="3383" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1971_David_Brown_990.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1971 David Brown 990 tractor</div></div></div>
<p>Brown and Ferguson disagreed over tractor design details in the late 30s, which led David Brown to design his own version, the <i>VAK1</i>, in secret. This was launched at the 1939 Royal Show.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Ferguson split away from Brown and joined up with <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ford" title="Henry Ford">Henry Ford</a> in 1938, after a 'handshake' agreement, to allow his 'Ferguson System' <a href="/wiki/Three-point_hitch" title="Three-point hitch">three-point linkage</a> to be used on the <a href="/wiki/Ford_N_Series_Tractors" class="mw-redirect" title="Ford N Series Tractors">Fordson N</a> tractors. That agreement was eventually terminated by Ford's grandson in 1947 and Ferguson again split away to form <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ferguson_Tractor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ferguson Tractor (page does not exist)">Ferguson Tractors</a> in 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> Brown's new heavier tractor, the VAK1, was produced, with over 7,700 units eventually sold, making Brown a wealthy man. It is said the David Brown Tractor is the only one to be built onto a sturdy cast iron chassis where other makers bolt components together to form a chassis-less construction which is weaker. Brown also built aircraft tugs (VIG) for the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">Royal Air Force</a> and for pulling the bomb trolleys used to re-arm aircraft. These tugs are distinctive, with truck like tyres, wrap round body work and HD bumpers front and rear, some being fitted with winches. In 1942 Brown started building a tracklayer version, the DB4. The DB4 was built for the army engineers and solved some of the problems found with the VTK, and got round an embargo on imported machines for military use. It was powered by a 38 h.p. <a href="/wiki/W.H.Dorman_%26_Co" title="W.H.Dorman &amp; Co">Dorman</a> diesel and a five speed gearbox. The DB4 was replaced in 1950 by the Trackmaster 30.
</p>

<p>The tractors division took over the Lancashire firm of Harrison, McGregor &amp; Guest Ltd, who produced the Albion brand of agricultural machinery to complement the tractor product line. After the takeover the company's badge was modified to incorporate the white rose of Yorkshire and the red rose of Lancashire. The Tractors division had ten subsidiaries around the world. At one stage 80% of production was exported. Sales were handled by 2,508 agents in 100 countries.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> A worldwide recession saw tractor sales slump, and after braving the storm and with the debt of a brand new building and production line to finance, it was inevitable that the company was put up for sale, bought by Tenneco who also owned ji case company, all hope to see the factory prosper was dashed when it was announced that survival was a competition between Huddersfield and the international Tractor plant in Doncaster, with the odds stacked in the latter's favour, especially with access to the motorway network on the doorstep. The Meltham factory ended production and a respected British name was erased.
</p>
<h4><span id="Tractor_Group.27s_Lagonda_and_Aston_Martin"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Tractor_Group's_Lagonda_and_Aston_Martin">Tractor Group's Lagonda and Aston Martin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Brown_Ltd.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tractor Group&#039;s Lagonda and Aston Martin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1947, Brown saw a <a href="/wiki/Classified_advertisement" class="mw-redirect" title="Classified advertisement">classified advertisement</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i>, offering for sale a <i>High Class Motor Business</i>. Brown acquired <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin" title="Aston Martin">Aston Martin</a> for £20,500 and, in the following year, <a href="/wiki/Lagonda" title="Lagonda">Lagonda</a> for £52,500, followed by the coachbuilder <a href="/wiki/Tickford" title="Tickford">Tickford</a> in 1955. He subsequently concentrated all the Aston Martin manufacturing at the Tickford premises in <a href="/wiki/Newport_Pagnell" title="Newport Pagnell">Newport Pagnell</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin#The_David_Brown_era" title="Aston Martin">David Brown years</a> saw production of the legendary DB series of Aston Martins, which were featured in some <a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">James Bond</a> films.  
</p><p>David Brown also had connections with <a href="/wiki/Vosper_%26_Company" title="Vosper &amp; Company">Vosper</a> shipbuilding, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Delapina&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Delapina (page does not exist)">Delapina</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Radyne&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Radyne (page does not exist)">Radyne</a> machinery.
</p><p>Both car companies were sold in 1972 to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Company_Developments_Limited&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Company Developments Limited (page does not exist)">Company Developments Limited</a>, when Aston Martin was in financial trouble, for a nominal £100.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sale_of_David_Brown_Tractors_to_Case_1972">Sale of David Brown Tractors to Case 1972</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Brown_Ltd.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sale of David Brown Tractors to Case 1972">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1972 the tractor operations were sold to <a href="/wiki/Tenneco" title="Tenneco">Tenneco Inc.</a> of America, who owned the <a href="/wiki/Case_Corporation" title="Case Corporation">J.I. Case</a> tractor company. The sale was due to a combination of a reduction in the UK tractor market, increased product development costs, the need to meet new regulations on health and safety, and increased competition from imported machinery.  Case applied the David Brown name and branding to some of its own tractor models in the UK market until the early 1980s before abandoning it in favour of the Case IH brand.
</p>
<h3><span id="Sale_by_David_Brown_family_.E2.80.93_management_buyout_1990"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Sale_by_David_Brown_family_–_management_buyout_1990">Sale by David Brown family – management buyout 1990</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Brown_Ltd.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sale by David Brown family – management buyout 1990">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1990 the David Brown family disposed of its stake to the business's management. It then floated David Brown as a public company in 1993. David Brown was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Textron" title="Textron">Textron Inc.</a> in October 1998.  
</p><p>This business, trading as David Brown Engineering Ltd and headquartered in Huddersfield, remains a supplier of heavy transmission systems for industrial, defense, railway and marine applications. These include transmissions for the British <a href="/wiki/Challenger_2_tank" class="mw-redirect" title="Challenger 2 tank">Challenger 2 tanks</a> and American <a href="/wiki/Bradley_Fighting_Vehicle" title="Bradley Fighting Vehicle">Bradley Fighting Vehicles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Railway transmissions are produced for their Chinese branch 'David Brown China', in a joint partnership called 'Jiangsu Shinri David Brown Gear Systems' at a factory in <a href="/wiki/Changzhou" title="Changzhou">Changzhou</a> near Shanghai.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sale_by_Textron_2008">Sale by Textron 2008</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Brown_Ltd.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sale by Textron 2008">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September 2008 it was announced that David Brown Gear systems and associated companies, David Brown Hydraulics based in Poole in Dorset, Maag Pumps of Switzerland, and Union Pumps of the USA were to be sold to <a href="/wiki/Clyde_Blowers" class="mw-redirect" title="Clyde Blowers">Clyde Blowers</a> of Scotland – owned by entrepreneur <a href="/wiki/Jim_McColl" title="Jim McColl">Jim McColl</a> – in a £368&#160;million deal.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>in 2016 David Brown was merged with Santasalo to form <b>David Brown Santasalo</b>. The joint company remains in the hands of <a href="/wiki/Clyde_Blowers_Capital" title="Clyde Blowers Capital">Clyde Blowers Capital</a>.
</p>
