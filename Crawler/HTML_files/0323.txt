</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_conventions_of_ancient_Tamil_country&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Cankam literature is thematically divided into <a href="/wiki/Akam_(poetry)" title="Akam (poetry)">akam</a> and <a href="/wiki/Puram" title="Puram">puram</a>. The poets worked under a convention of anonymity, not exposing the names of the heroes, heroines and friends in their poems, particularly in the former, in order to underline the universality of the feelings expressed in their poetry.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tolkappiyam_classifications"><i>Tolkappiyam</i> classifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_conventions_of_ancient_Tamil_country&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tolkappiyam classifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Tolk%C4%81ppiyam" title="Tolkāppiyam">Tolkāppiyam</a></i> identifies ten categories of names. An̲n̲i Mirutalakumāri Tāmacu claims to have completely revealed the basis on which the Tamils adopted their names.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETāmacu20034_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETāmacu20034-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wilden_classifications"><i>Wilden</i> classifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_conventions_of_ancient_Tamil_country&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wilden classifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eva Wildern identifies four types of names found in the <a href="/wiki/Sangam_literature" title="Sangam literature">Sangam literature</a>: proper names, proper names connected with a name of a place or a dynasty, proper names with epithets and imagery names.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This convention was codified in the <i> Tolkāppiyam</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tantaipeyar"><i>Tantaipeyar</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_conventions_of_ancient_Tamil_country&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tantaipeyar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This tradition involves firstly having the father's name followed by one's own name. This system was carried even into the medieval period.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Examples include <i>cēramān</i>, meaning "son of <a href="/wiki/Chera_dynasty" title="Chera dynasty">Chera</a>" composed of <i>cēra</i> and <i>makan</i>, or <i>vēlmān</i>, meaning "son of Vel" composed of <i>vēl</i> and <i>makan</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fives_parts_of_a_name">Fives parts of a name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_conventions_of_ancient_Tamil_country&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fives parts of a name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The convention is remains that dynastic name is followed by the proper name. However, in the case of distinguished persons, there may be up to five parts to it.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Parimelazhagar" class="mw-redirect" title="Parimelazhagar">Parimelazhagar</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> (c. 13th century) codified the Classical-era conventions in his explanation of the correct name of <a href="/wiki/Yanaikatchai_Mantaran_Cheral_Irumporai" title="Yanaikatchai Mantaran Cheral Irumporai">Yanaikatchai Mantaran Cheral Irumporai</a> as <i>Kōccēramān Yāṇaikkaṭcēi Māntarañcēral Irumpoṛai</i>. <i>Kō</i> meaning 'king' signifying his official title; <i>Cēramān</i> is the dynastic name; <i>Yāṇaikkat</i> 'Elephant-eyed' is a distinctive feature; <i>Cēi</i> is the <i>iyatpeyar</i>, or proper name; and <i>Irumporai</i> is an appellate.
</p><p>Another example is <i>Malayamān Cōzhiya Ēnati Tirukkaṇṇan</i>. <i>Malayamān</i> is the dynastic name; <i>Cōzhiya</i>, "of the <a href="/wiki/Chola_Nadu" title="Chola Nadu">Chola Nadu</a>; 'Ēnati" is the title of conferred by the King to the chief of the army in a ceremony known as <i>Ēnatippatiyam</i>;<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and <i>Tirukkaṇṇan</i> is his <i>iyatpeyar</i>, or proper name.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynastic_name">Dynastic name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_conventions_of_ancient_Tamil_country&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dynastic name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Poets spoke to kings about the need for children for carrying on the family or dynastic name.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The dynastic or family name acts as a prefix and occurs before the actual name.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Titles">Titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_conventions_of_ancient_Tamil_country&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some titles such as 'Kō' meaning 'king' were acquired, while others were conferred by the King. Three titles particularly renowned were known as Etti, Enati and Kaviti.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  Talaikkoli, Peraiyan and Marayan are a few of the other titles bestowed to eminent personalities in consideration to their talents in particular fields.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
