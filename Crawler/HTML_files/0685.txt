<p><b>James 'Jamie' Whitham</b> (born James Michael Whitham, 6 September 1966),<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is an <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English</a> former professional motorcycle <a href="/wiki/Road_racing" title="Road racing">road racer</a>. He raced in most major British and international championships, winning the British championship twice. The readers of <a href="/wiki/Motor_Cycle_News" title="Motor Cycle News">Motorcycle News</a> voted him 'Man of the Year' in both 1991 and 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After retiring from racing he works as a television motorcycle <a href="/wiki/Color_commentator" title="Color commentator">race commentator</a>, runs road-race tuition track days, and operates a small private airstrip near Huddersfield.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Whitham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>, England, Whitam's first <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle" title="Motorcycle">motorcycle</a> as a child was a <a href="/wiki/Raleigh_Bicycle_Company#Motor_vehicles" title="Raleigh Bicycle Company">Raleigh Wisp</a>, a small-wheeled <a href="/wiki/Moped" title="Moped">moped</a>, and his favourite early road-going machine was a <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_FS1-E" class="mw-redirect" title="Yamaha FS1-E">Yamaha FS1-E</a>, but he admitted to <a href="/wiki/Motor_Cycle_News" title="Motor Cycle News">Motorcycle News</a> in 1994 that he was more embarrassed at taking his <a href="/wiki/Driving_test" title="Driving test">learner-test</a> with a Suzuki X-5 200&#160;cc <a href="/wiki/Sidecar" title="Sidecar">sidecar outfit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MCN_1994_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCN_1994-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He won the 1986 British 80&#160;cc Championship, the 1988 1300&#160;cc Production British Championship, the 1991 MCN TT Superbike Challenge, with <a href="/wiki/Suzuki" title="Suzuki">Suzuki</a>; and for <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_Motor_Company" title="Yamaha Motor Company">Yamaha</a> in 1993 both the <a href="/wiki/British_Superbike_Championship" title="British Superbike Championship">British Superbike Championship</a> Supercup and the <a href="/wiki/Auto-Cycle_Union" title="Auto-Cycle Union">ACU</a> TT Superbike British Championship. He confirmed to Motor Cycle News in 1994 prior to his World Superbike career that his best biking moment was winning the 750&#160;cc double British championship for Yamaha, and that doing 185&#160;mph at <a href="/wiki/Daytona_International_Speedway" title="Daytona International Speedway">Daytona Speedbowl banking</a> was exhilarating.<sup id="cite_ref-MCN_1994_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCN_1994-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He competed in the 1992 French Grand Prix at <a href="/wiki/Magny_Cours" class="mw-redirect" title="Magny Cours">Magny Cours</a>. He also competed on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> - running in the Manx Grand Prix in 1985, and the TT from 1986 to 1989, with three top six results; and he won British championship races at Superstock, Seniorstock, Supersport 600 and TT Formula One levels.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_racing_and_Health_Problems">International racing and Health Problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Whitham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: International racing and Health Problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/1994_Superbike_World_Championship_season" class="mw-redirect" title="1994 Superbike World Championship season">1994</a> he raced full-time in the <a href="/wiki/Superbike_World_Championship" title="Superbike World Championship">Superbike World Championship</a> for <a href="/wiki/Ducati" class="mw-redirect" title="Ducati">Ducati</a> with team-mate <a href="/wiki/Carl_Fogarty" title="Carl Fogarty">Carl Fogarty</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-MCN_1993_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MCN_1993-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> taking a race win at <a href="/wiki/Sentul_International_Circuit" title="Sentul International Circuit">Sentul</a> and finishing 7th overall. He returned to the British series in 1995, leading the standings and taking a podium as a World Superbike Championship wild-card at <a href="/wiki/Brands_Hatch" title="Brands Hatch">Brands Hatch</a>, but contracted <a href="/wiki/Hodgkin%27s_Disease" class="mw-redirect" title="Hodgkin&#39;s Disease">Hodgkin's Disease</a> mid-season. He recovered in time to be British Superbike Championship runner-up in 1996 on a Yamaha, despite not scoring at the first round <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> - he won ten races, twice the number of champion Niall Mackenzie.
</p><p>He raced for the Harris Suzuki team in the World Superbike Championship in <a href="/wiki/1997_Superbike_World_Championship_season" class="mw-redirect" title="1997 Superbike World Championship season">1997</a> and <a href="/wiki/1998_Superbike_World_Championship_season" class="mw-redirect" title="1998 Superbike World Championship season">1998</a>, finishing 8th in the championship both seasons with a total of 3 podiums,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> but for <a href="/wiki/1999_Superbike_World_Championship_season" class="mw-redirect" title="1999 Superbike World Championship season">1999</a> the factory contract changed hands, and the new owners hired <a href="/wiki/Pierfrancesco_Chili" title="Pierfrancesco Chili">Pierfrancesco Chili</a> and <a href="/wiki/Katsuaki_Fujiwara" title="Katsuaki Fujiwara">Katsuaki Fujiwara</a>. Worse was to follow when he was hired by <a href="/wiki/Kenny_Roberts" title="Kenny Roberts">Kenny Roberts</a>' uncompetitive <a href="/wiki/Modenas" title="Modenas">Modenas</a> <a href="/wiki/Grand_Prix_motorcycle_racing" title="Grand Prix motorcycle racing">500cc World Championship</a> team: he crashed heavily at <a href="/wiki/Masaryk_Circuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Masaryk Circuit">Brno</a>, breaking his pelvis.
</p><p>Following his win at Donington during 1999 in a one-off appearance, he competed in the <a href="/wiki/Supersport_World_Championship" title="Supersport World Championship">Supersport World Championship</a> from 2000 to 2002. He won his very first race in 2000 before a string of crashes (including while leading at <a href="/wiki/Misano_World_Circuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Misano World Circuit">Misano</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> knocked his confidence and relegated him to 8th overall in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He was 4th overall in 2001, forced to support team-mate <a href="/wiki/Paolo_Casoli" title="Paolo Casoli">Paolo Casoli</a>'s bid for the championship.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In 2002 he won in the wet at Silverstone,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> before <a href="/wiki/Glaucoma" title="Glaucoma">glaucoma</a>, an eye-condition most likely a consequence of past <a href="/wiki/Chemotherapy" title="Chemotherapy">chemotherapy</a>, caused him to retire at the end of the year. In total he took 4 World Supersport victories <sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and 12 podiums. He held the Supersport lap record at <a href="/wiki/Donington_Park" title="Donington Park">Donington Park</a> until <a href="/wiki/Kenan_Sofuoglu" class="mw-redirect" title="Kenan Sofuoglu">Kenan Sofuoglu</a> beat it in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Whitham confirmed at his own website in May 2017 that his previous cancer problems had recurred, requiring urgent and ongoing chemotherapy and other treatments.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> This was further updated in December.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Whitham was unable to present television coverage of the Isle of Man <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man_TT" title="Isle of Man TT">TT races</a> in May/June 2018 due to intensive treatments as an in-patient at <a href="/wiki/Jimmy%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="Jimmy&#39;s">Jimmy's</a> hospital, in the city of <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post_racing">Post racing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Whitham&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post racing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Whitham now operates <a href="/wiki/Crosland_Moor_airfield" class="mw-redirect" title="Crosland Moor airfield">Crosland Moor airfield</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and also runs race-tuition track days,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> previously with business partner Paul Shoesmith under the defunct company <i>Speed Freak</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He established a second career as a popular TV analyst for <a href="/wiki/Eurosport" title="Eurosport">Eurosport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a> and <a href="/wiki/ITV_Network" class="mw-redirect" title="ITV Network">ITV</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Whitham provides commentary and analysis for the Isle of Man TT coverage by <a href="/wiki/ITV4" title="ITV4">ITV4</a> alongside former racer <a href="/wiki/Steve_Parrish" title="Steve Parrish">Steve Parrish</a>, which is shown on <a href="/wiki/Velocity_(TV_channel)" class="mw-redirect" title="Velocity (TV channel)">Velocity Channel</a> in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Whitham also works as a road tester for the British motorcycle magazine Visordown, and has also worked with the young riders of the Virgin Media Cup.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In 2008 he launched his autobiography, 'What A Good Do!'.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> From January 2013 he hosted and presented <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Superbike_Show&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Superbike Show (page does not exist)">The Superbike Show</a> on <a href="/wiki/Loaded_TV" title="Loaded TV">Loaded TV</a>.
</p><p>Whitham was awarded an honorary Doctorate qualification by Huddersfield University in July 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
