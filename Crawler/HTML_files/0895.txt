<p><b>Featherstone</b> is a town and <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Wakefield" title="City of Wakefield">City of Wakefield</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, two miles south-west of <a href="/wiki/Pontefract" title="Pontefract">Pontefract</a>. <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">Historically</a> part of the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>, in 2011, it had a population of 15,244.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Featherstone_railway_station" title="Featherstone railway station">Featherstone railway station</a> is on the <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Pontefract Line">Pontefract Line</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Featherstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite most population growth taking place around the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, Featherstone traces its history back much further than this. The Domesday Book (1086) records "In Ferestane [Featherstone] and Prestone [Purston] and Arduwic [Hardwick] and Osele [Nostell], Ligulf had 16 carucates of land for geld, and 6 ploughs may be there."<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is thought that a local public house, the Traveller's Rest, can trace its origins to the 17th century whilst the Jubilee Hotel is a <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a> which once provided a resting place for wealthy Victorians and their horses.
</p><p>The original village is now known as North Featherstone (at the junction of the B6134 and the B6421 today), set around All Saints' Church and joined to today's centre by Featherstone Lane.<sup id="cite_ref-Mee_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mee-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Featherstone family lived at Ackton Hall (now demolished), in the hamlet of <a href="/wiki/Ackton" title="Ackton">Ackton</a> about a mile to the west.<sup id="cite_ref-Mee_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mee-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> What is now known as Featherstone was a later development (originally called South Featherstone) near to the railway station and the village of <a href="/wiki/Purston_Jaglin" class="mw-redirect" title="Purston Jaglin">Purston Jaglin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-GENUKI_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GENUKI-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Like many surrounding areas, Featherstone grew around <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">coal mining</a>. Coal had been mined at Featherstone since the 13th century and remains of <a href="/wiki/Bell_pit" title="Bell pit">bell pits</a> can still be seen to the north of Park Lane at North Featherstone. In 1848, the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pontefract" title="Pontefract">Pontefract</a> and <a href="/wiki/Goole" title="Goole">Goole</a> railway line through Featherstone provided the basis for large scale coal mining in Featherstone, by opening up new markets in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_England" title="Southern England">South of England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. Featherstone Main Colliery was opened in 1866, followed by Ackton Hall Colliery in 1873.<sup id="cite_ref-Senior_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Senior-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  These were closer to what is now the main part of Featherstone, which consequently expanded.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carlton_Street_-_Featherstone_Lane_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1193563.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Carlton_Street_-_Featherstone_Lane_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1193563.jpg/220px-Carlton_Street_-_Featherstone_Lane_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1193563.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Carlton_Street_-_Featherstone_Lane_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1193563.jpg/330px-Carlton_Street_-_Featherstone_Lane_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1193563.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Carlton_Street_-_Featherstone_Lane_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1193563.jpg/440px-Carlton_Street_-_Featherstone_Lane_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1193563.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Carlton_Street_-_Featherstone_Lane_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1193563.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Terraced Housing on Carlton street</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Jubilee_-_Wakefield_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1194869.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/The_Jubilee_-_Wakefield_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1194869.jpg/220px-The_Jubilee_-_Wakefield_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1194869.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="152" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/The_Jubilee_-_Wakefield_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1194869.jpg/330px-The_Jubilee_-_Wakefield_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1194869.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/The_Jubilee_-_Wakefield_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1194869.jpg/440px-The_Jubilee_-_Wakefield_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1194869.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="441" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Jubilee_-_Wakefield_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1194869.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Jubilee Pub</div></div></div>
<p>The town came to national attention during a national "lockout" of mine workers in 1893 due to low coal prices and <a href="/wiki/Overproduction" title="Overproduction">overproduction</a>. Soldiers fired on a crowd who were demonstrating at the colliery gates, killing two instantly.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  (The Guardian claimed that a third man, a Mr. Tomlinson from Normanton, died the following day from injuries from being shot but only two dead are named on the town's sculpture.)<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  A distinctive sculpture marking the centenary of the Featherstone Massacre stands in the shopping precinct and a large mural depicting the town's heritage can be seen at the town's main crossroads. Ackton Hall Colliery was the first pit to close following the end of the miners' strike and this could not be contested as geological difficulties had made it impossible for the pit to continue production.
</p><p>Featherstone is the subject of a study, <i>Coal is Our Life, by</i> the sociologist <a href="/wiki/Norman_Dennis" title="Norman Dennis">Norman Dennis</a>, published in 1956.
</p><p>Opened in the 1950s, Purston Park takes up a large area of space and offers a lake and a children's play area. There was also previously a <a href="/wiki/Bowling_green" title="Bowling green">bowling green</a>, until being changed to a rose garden in 2004. It has been made out of the grounds of what was originally a private residence and a country estate, with the stately home formerly acting as the <a href="/wiki/Town_hall" class="mw-redirect" title="Town hall">town hall</a>. This building was sold to developers in 2007 and has since been converted into luxury flats.
</p><p>Featherstone is undergoing continual change and as part of this a new, state-of-the-art £2.5-million <a href="/wiki/Community_centre" title="Community centre">community centre</a> has been built in Station Lane. The "Pit Houses", the houses constituting a council estate which formerly belonged to the <a href="/wiki/National_Coal_Board" title="National Coal Board">National Coal Board</a>, have been demolished to make room for further developments.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponymy">Toponymy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Featherstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Toponymy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like many place-names in the area, 'Featherstone' derives from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a>. The name is formed of two elements: <i lang="ang" title="Old English language text">feother</i>, meaning 'four', and <i>stān</i>, meaning 'stone'. Therefore, the names means "(place at) the four stones". These 'four stones' are likely to have been some waymarker or monument by a road or other well-used route through the town. The settlement was recorded in the <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> of 1086 as <i>Fredestan</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Featherstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Featherstone village historically formed a <a href="/wiki/Township" title="Township">township</a> within the much larger (ecclesiastical) <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parish</a> of Featherstone; the parish also included <a href="/wiki/Purston_Jaglin" class="mw-redirect" title="Purston Jaglin">Purston Jaglin</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Whitwood&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Whitwood (page does not exist)">Whitwood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ackton" title="Ackton">Ackton</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In 1894, Featherstone <a href="/wiki/Urban_district" title="Urban district">urban district</a> (UD) was formed from the <a href="/wiki/Civil_parish" title="Civil parish">civil parishes</a> of Featherstone, Purston Jaglin, Ackton and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Snydale&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Snydale (page does not exist)">Snydale</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In 1974 the town became part of the metropolitan borough of Wakefield; currently the borough's Featherstone ward comprises the former UD area plus <a href="/wiki/Sharlston" title="Sharlston">Sharlston</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Featherstone is currently a Labour stronghold. It is part of the <a href="/wiki/Hemsworth_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Hemsworth (UK Parliament constituency)">Hemsworth constituency</a>, and is represented by Labour MP <a href="/wiki/Jon_Trickett" title="Jon Trickett">Jon Trickett</a> in the House of Commons and by three Labour Councillors on <a href="/wiki/City_of_Wakefield" title="City of Wakefield">Wakefield</a> Council: Richard "Dick" Taylor, Graham Isherwood and Maureen Tennant-King<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Featherstone Town Council is also controlled by <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour.</a>, Cllr Steve Vickers is the Town Mayor for 2016/17
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fighting_decline">Fighting decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Featherstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fighting decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Starting in the mid-1970s to the mid-1980s, the area went into an era of sharp decline in the residents' quality of life. Historians and social scientists have put forward many factors. The gradual loss of the coal industry coupled with poor housing and education. This has begun to improve in recent years with new housing developments, better schools and plans to breathe life back into the local business community via various climate friendly projects.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<p>Featherstone has a <a href="/wiki/Featherstone_railway_station" title="Featherstone railway station">railway station</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Pontefract Line">Pontefract Line</a>. </p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Featherstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town has two <a href="/wiki/Secondary_school" title="Secondary school">secondary schools</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Featherstone_Academy" title="The Featherstone Academy">the Featherstone Academy</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Wilfrids_Catholic_High_School,_North_Featherstone" class="mw-redirect" title="St Wilfrids Catholic High School, North Featherstone">St Wilfrid's Catholic High School</a>.
</p><p>Primary and infant schools include St Thomas' Junior, Girnhill Infants', Purston Infants', North Featherstone Junior and Infants' and All Saints Junior and Infants'.
</p>

<p>Featherstone has a number of churches: St Thomas's Church (<a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Anglican</a>)  – built from traditional Yorkshire sandstone, St Thomas's Church and the adjacent <a href="/wiki/Vicarage" class="mw-redirect" title="Vicarage">vicarage</a> were built in the 1870s. Due to a lack of funding the church has no bell tower, and instead the <a href="/wiki/Church_bell" title="Church bell">bell</a> hangs outside on the church’s south wall. The original vicarage is now a private residence. All Saints' Church (Anglican), the <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodist church</a>, and the South Featherstone <a href="/wiki/Gospel_Hall" class="mw-redirect" title="Gospel Hall">Gospel Hall</a> are also still active churches.
</p><p>A former <a href="/wiki/Methodist" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodist">Methodist</a> chapel on Wakefield Road has since been turned into an antiques salesroom and the North Featherstone Gospel Hall has been converted into a private dwelling. St Gerard's Roman Catholic Church was closed in the summer of 2008 – meaning <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholics</a> now have to travel to <a href="/wiki/Pontefract" title="Pontefract">Pontefract</a> to attend services.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Featherstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Featherstone_Rovers">Featherstone Rovers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Featherstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Featherstone Rovers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Featherstone_Rovers_Rugby_Ground,_Featherstone_-_geograph.org.uk_-_223813.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Featherstone_Rovers_Rugby_Ground%2C_Featherstone_-_geograph.org.uk_-_223813.jpg/220px-Featherstone_Rovers_Rugby_Ground%2C_Featherstone_-_geograph.org.uk_-_223813.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Featherstone_Rovers_Rugby_Ground%2C_Featherstone_-_geograph.org.uk_-_223813.jpg/330px-Featherstone_Rovers_Rugby_Ground%2C_Featherstone_-_geograph.org.uk_-_223813.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Featherstone_Rovers_Rugby_Ground%2C_Featherstone_-_geograph.org.uk_-_223813.jpg/440px-Featherstone_Rovers_Rugby_Ground%2C_Featherstone_-_geograph.org.uk_-_223813.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="423" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Featherstone_Rovers_Rugby_Ground,_Featherstone_-_geograph.org.uk_-_223813.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Post_Office_Road" title="Post Office Road">Post Office Road</a></div></div></div>
<p>The town's sport scene is dominated by its local <a href="/wiki/Rugby_league" title="Rugby league">rugby league</a> club, <a href="/wiki/Featherstone_Rovers" title="Featherstone Rovers">Featherstone Rovers</a>, who have won the <a href="/wiki/Challenge_Cup" title="Challenge Cup">Challenge Cup</a> on three occasions, most recently on 7 May 1983, and won the League Championship in 1976–77. Originally made up of local miners, the club was formed in the Railway Hotel in 1902, then re-formed in 1906.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> They are currently in the <a href="/wiki/Co-operative_Championship" class="mw-redirect" title="Co-operative Championship">Championship</a>, after being promoted in 2007, beating <a href="/wiki/Oldham_R.L.F.C." title="Oldham R.L.F.C.">Oldham</a> in the Play-Offs final. In the 2010 season, Rovers finished first in the league table with a 100% away record and claimed the League Leader's Shield. They reached the Championship Grand Final by beating <a href="/wiki/Halifax_R.L.F.C." title="Halifax R.L.F.C.">Halifax</a> 46–16 in the Semi-Final, only to be defeated by Halifax in the final on a golden point (22–23).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> They went one better in 2012 beating <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_Eagles" title="Sheffield Eagles">Sheffield Eagles</a> 44–4 in the Grand Final to become Champions.
</p><p>Featherstone Rovers' stadium (historically known as <a href="/wiki/Post_Office_Road" title="Post Office Road">Post Office Road</a>) was renamed the LD Nutrition Stadium in 2017, following a sponsorship deal with a local health-food dealer of the same name.
The Stadium was previously known as the Bigfellas Stadium (as named in 2010) due to sponsorship at the time being from the Pontefract nightclub of the same name.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Featherstone_Weightlifting_Club">Featherstone Weightlifting Club</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Featherstone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Featherstone Weightlifting Club">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Formed in 1958 by Allan Whitworth, (who once deadlifted 600lbs!) The club amalgamated in 1959 with Pontefract Weightlifting club ran by Jack Garbutt a future World Masters champion and his brother Len Garbutt who would later become a Featherstone Mayor. This club had existed since the 1940's and make Featherstone Weightlifting club the second oldest members club in Britain. The club has had many international champions and medallists in masters competitions including Jack Garbutt, Tony Flood, Les D'Arcy, Martyn Riley, Alan Lomax, Andrew Philips and Justine Davis. In recent times the club has developed a great reputation for coaching local children and the Featherstone Raptors, coached by the team of Founder Martyn Riley and recently Sharon Tepper, Justine Davis, Robert Jackson, Stefanie Carter, Thomas Smith and Si Walker  as they often have more entrants, medallists and winners than the much bigger city clubs at events. This includes 4 time British Development champion Kobain Riley and multiple time regional and national champions Lea Saka Fajic, Olivia Walker, Jack Hawkins, Wesley Riley, Len Fajic, Kara Swailes, Oliver Tepper, Mackallan Whiteley, Lilly Tepper, Blake Rowlands, Chloe Hutchinson and Casey Swailes still training at the club. Recently the club also had more entrants, medallists and champions at the English senior, masters and development Championships 2019 in Milton Keynes and was awarded for there work in the community at the WMDC love where you live awards.
</p>
