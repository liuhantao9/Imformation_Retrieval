<p><b>Eveline Widmer-Schlumpf</b> (born 16 March 1956) is a <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Swiss</a>  politician and lawyer, and former member of the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Federal_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Swiss Federal Council">Swiss Federal Council</a> from 2008 to 2015.  She was the head of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Department_of_Finance" title="Federal Department of Finance">Federal Department of Finance</a> (the Swiss <a href="/wiki/Finance_minister" title="Finance minister">finance minister</a>). She served as <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Swiss_Confederation" title="President of the Swiss Confederation">President of the Swiss Confederation</a> for the year 2012.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span></h2>
<p>Widmer-Schlumpf is married and has three children. She is the daughter of former federal councillor <a href="/wiki/Leon_Schlumpf" title="Leon Schlumpf">Leon Schlumpf</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> She is the second federal councillor whose father had held the same office, after <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Ruffy" title="Eugène Ruffy">Eugène Ruffy</a>, and the sixth woman to be elected to the Swiss Federal Council. Eveline Widmer-Schlumpf is also patron of the project <a href="/wiki/SAFFA#Saffa_2020" class="mw-redirect" title="SAFFA"><i>SAFFA 2020</i></a>, alongside the federal councillors (<i>Bundesrat</i>) <a href="/wiki/Doris_Leuthard" title="Doris Leuthard">Doris Leuthard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Simonetta_Sommaruga" title="Simonetta Sommaruga">Simonetta Sommaruga</a> and the former councillor (<i>aBR</i>) <a href="/wiki/Micheline_Calmy-Rey" title="Micheline Calmy-Rey">Micheline Calmy-Rey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2020-patronat_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2020-patronat-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career_in_Grisons">Career in Grisons</span></h2>
<p>Widmer-Schlumpf received her degree in law at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Z%C3%BCrich" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Zürich">University of Zürich</a> in 1981 and her LLD in 1990. She worked as a lawyer from 1987 to 1998. She was elected to the district court of <a href="/wiki/Trin" title="Trin">Trin</a> in 1985, presiding from 1991 to 1997. As a member of the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_People%27s_Party" title="Swiss People&#39;s Party">Swiss People's Party</a>, she was the <a href="/wiki/List_of_legislative_and_executive_councils_of_the_Cantons_of_Switzerland" class="mw-redirect" title="List of legislative and executive councils of the Cantons of Switzerland">cantonal legislative</a> of Grisons 1994 to 1998, and in 1998 was elected to the cantonal government as the first woman, acting as president in 2001 and <a href="/wiki/List_of_2005_office-holders_in_Switzerland" class="mw-redirect" title="List of 2005 office-holders in Switzerland">2005</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Swiss_Federal_Council">Swiss Federal Council</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Election">Election</span></h3>
<p>Widmer-Schlumpf was named as an alternative candidate to <a href="/wiki/Christoph_Blocher" title="Christoph Blocher">Christoph Blocher</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_People%27s_Party_of_Switzerland" title="Christian Democratic People&#39;s Party of Switzerland">Christian Democrat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Switzerland" title="Social Democratic Party of Switzerland">Social Democrat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Green_Party_of_Switzerland" title="Green Party of Switzerland">Green</a> factions in the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Federal_Council_elections_of_12_December_2007" class="mw-redirect" title="Swiss Federal Council elections of 12 December 2007">Swiss Federal Council elections of 12 December 2007</a>. In the first round, she received 116 votes, compared to 111 votes for Blocher. In the second round, she was elected <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Federal_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Swiss Federal Council">federal councillor</a> with 125 votes, 115 votes going to Blocher and 6 spurious, empty or invalid. She accepted her election on 13 December 2007.  She assumed Blocher's old portfolio as head of the Department of Justice and Police.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expulsion_from_the_SVP">Expulsion from the SVP</span></h3>
<p>After her election, Widmer-Schlumpf was intensely opposed by the national leadership of the Swiss People's Party, who denounced her as a traitor to her party for accepting an election that she won without the support of the party. Immediately after her election, she was excluded from the SVP <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_group" title="Parliamentary group">party group</a>'s meetings, as was her colleague <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Schmid" title="Samuel Schmid">Samuel Schmid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In another unprecedented development in Swiss politics, on 2 April 2008 the national party leadership called upon Widmer-Schlumpf to resign from the Federal Council at once and to leave the party. When Widmer-Schlumpf refused to do so, the SVP demanded that its <a href="/wiki/Grisons" title="Grisons">Grisons</a> section expel her. Since Swiss parties are legally federations of cantonal parties, the SVP could not directly expel her. The Grisons branch stood by Widmer-Schlumpf, prompting its expulsion from the national party on 1 June. In response, the former SVP Grisons section formed the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Democratic_Party_of_Switzerland" title="Conservative Democratic Party of Switzerland">Conservative Democratic Party of Switzerland</a>.  The SVP's Bern section, of which Schmid is a member, also joined the new party.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_service">Subsequent service</span></h3>
<p>Following a reshuffle of portfolios after the <a href="/wiki/2010_Swiss_Federal_Council_election" title="2010 Swiss Federal Council election">by-election of two new councilors in 2010</a>, Widmer-Schlumpf replaced outgoing <a href="/wiki/Hans-Rudolf_Merz" title="Hans-Rudolf Merz">Hans-Rudolf Merz</a> as the head of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Department_of_Finance" title="Federal Department of Finance">Federal Department of Finance</a> which she presided over since then.<sup id="cite_ref-ATS-2010_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATS-2010-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Widmer-Schlumpf was elected <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Swiss_Confederation" title="President of the Swiss Confederation">Vice President of the Confederation</a> for 2011, alongside President <a href="/wiki/Micheline_Calmy-Rey" title="Micheline Calmy-Rey">Micheline Calmy-Rey</a>. On 14 December 2011 she was elected President of the Confederation for 2012—the fourth woman to hold the post (after <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Dreifuss" title="Ruth Dreifuss">Ruth Dreifuss</a> in 1999, Calmy-Rey in 2007 and 2011 and <a href="/wiki/Doris_Leuthard" title="Doris Leuthard">Doris Leuthard</a> in 2010), and the third woman in a row.  Due to a large amount of turnover on the Federal Council, she was the longest-serving member to have not yet served as President.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span></h3>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_People%27s_Party" title="Swiss People&#39;s Party">Swiss People's Party</a> (SVP) won a record vote of over 29% in the <a href="/wiki/2015_Swiss_federal_election" title="2015 Swiss federal election">2015 general elections</a>, Widmer-Schlumpf announced she would not run for re-election to the Federal Council on 28 October 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10vr10-20151028_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10vr10-20151028-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> She was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Guy_Parmelin" title="Guy Parmelin">Guy Parmelin</a>.
