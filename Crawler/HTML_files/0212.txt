	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Teknonymy</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Teknonymy</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">τέκνον</span>, "child" and <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">ὄνομα</span>, "name"),<sup id="cite_ref-Reflections_on_Japanese_Language_and_Culture_1987_p._65_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reflections_on_Japanese_Language_and_Culture_1987_p._65-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> more often known as a <b>paedonymic</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> is the practice of referring to <a href="/wiki/Parent" title="Parent">parents</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Name" title="Name">names</a> of their <a href="/wiki/Children" class="mw-redirect" title="Children">children</a>. This practice can be found in many different cultures around the world. The term was coined by anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Edward_Burnett_Tylor" title="Edward Burnett Tylor">Edward Burnett Tylor</a> in an 1889 paper.<sup id="cite_ref-Lee1973_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lee1973-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Teknonymy can be found in:
</p>
<ul><li>Various <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_peoples" title="Austronesian peoples">Austronesian peoples</a>:
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Cocos_Malays" title="Cocos Malays">Cocos Malays</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cocos_(Keeling)_Islands" title="Cocos (Keeling) Islands">Cocos (Keeling) Islands</a>, where parents are known by the name of their first-born child.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> For instance, a man named Hashim and his wife, Anisa, have a daughter named Sheila. Hashim is now known as "Pak Sheila" (literally, "Sheila's Father") and Anisa is now known as "Mak Sheila" (literally, "Sheila's Mother").</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Balinese_people" title="Balinese people">Balinese people</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Betsileo_people" title="Betsileo people">Betsileo people</a> of Madagascar, in particular the <a href="/wiki/Zafimaniry" title="Zafimaniry">Zafimaniry</a> subgroup<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>the language of the <a href="/wiki/Madurese_people" title="Madurese people">Madurese people</a> of Indonesia</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Mentawai_people" title="Mentawai people">Mentawai people</a> of Indonesia<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tao_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Tao people">Tao people</a> of Taiwan<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean language</a>; for example, if a Korean woman has a son named <a href="/wiki/Soo-min" title="Soo-min">Su-min</a>, she might be called <i>Su-min Eomma</i> (meaning "mother of Su-min")<sup id="cite_ref-Lee1973_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lee1973-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Arab_world" title="Arab world">Arab world</a>; for example, if a Saudi man named Hasan has a child named Zayn, Hasan will now be known as <i>Abu Zayn</i> (literally, "Father of Zayn"). Similarly, <i>Umm Malik</i> is "Mother of Malik". This is known as a <i><a href="/wiki/Kunya_(Arabic)" title="Kunya (Arabic)">kunya</a></i> in Arabic and is used as a sign of respect for others.</li>
<li>Amazonia<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Zuni_language" title="Zuni language">Zuni language</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Swahili_language" title="Swahili language">Swahili</a>, as spoken in Tanzania and Kenya<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>; for example, if a woman has a son named Musa, the woman would be known as <i>Mama Musa</i>. Musa's father would be known as <i>Baba Musa</i>.</li>
<li>to some extent, <a href="/wiki/Habesha_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Habesha people">Habesha people</a> in the Horn of Africa</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Yoruba_language" title="Yoruba language">Yoruba</a> language of Western Africa; for example, if a woman has a son named <a href="/wiki/Femi" title="Femi">Femi</a>, will now be known as <i>iya Femi</i> (meaning mother of Femi) and her husband <i>baba Femi</i> (meaning father of Femi).</li></ul>
