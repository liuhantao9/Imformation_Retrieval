<p><a href="/wiki/Style_(manner_of_address)" title="Style (manner of address)">Styles</a> represent the fashion by which monarchs and noblemen are properly addressed. Throughout history, many different styles were used, with little standardization. This page will detail the various styles used by royalty and nobility in Europe, in the final form arrived at in the nineteenth century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The reason for this is unclear. </p>

<h2><span id="Imperial.2C_royal.2C_and_princely_styles"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Imperial,_royal,_and_princely_styles">Imperial, royal, and princely styles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial,_royal,_and_noble_styles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Imperial, royal, and princely styles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Only those classified within the social class of <a href="/wiki/Royal_family" title="Royal family">royalty</a> and upper nobility have a style of "Highness" attached before their titles. Reigning bearers of forms of Highness included grand princes, grand dukes, reigning princes, reigning dukes, and princely counts, their families, and the agnatic (of the male bloodline) descendants of emperors and kings. Royalty (usually emperors to princely counts) are all considered <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_prince" class="mw-redirect" title="Sovereign prince">sovereign princes</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Fürsten</i>).
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Emperor" title="Emperor">Emperors</a> and empresses held the style of <b>Imperial Majesty</b> (<b>HIM</b>).</li>
<li>Members of imperial families generally hold the style of <b>Imperial Highness</b> (<b>HIH</b>).</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austrian Empire</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Austria" title="Emperor of Austria">Emperor</a> was also the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Hungary" title="King of Hungary">King of Hungary</a>, and thus bore the style of <b>Imperial and Royal Majesty</b>. Subsequently, members of the imperial family, who were also members of the royal family of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">Hungary</a>, held the style of <b>Imperial and Royal Highness</b> (<b>HI&amp;RH</b>). Abbreviation to Imperial Highness is common and accepted.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German Empire</a>, the other "heir" to the Holy Roman Empire, the emperor and empress were also addressed as Imperial and Royal Majesty, as they ruled over both the German Empire and Kingdom of Prussia. Similarly, the crown prince of the Empire and Prussia was styled Imperial and Royal Highness. Other members of the House of Prussia, having no constitutional place in the Empire as such, were only entitled to the style of Royal Highness.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Russia" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Russia">Imperial Russia</a>, children and male-line grandchildren of the Emperor bore the style of Imperial Highness. Male-line great-grandchildren held the style of Highness; also, the eldest son of any person who held the style of Highness also held the style of Highness. All other male-line descendants held the style of <b>Serenity</b>, often translated as "Serene Highness". Some Russian noble princes also hold the style of Serenity; all others and Russian princely counts hold the style of <b>Illustriousness</b>, often translated as "Illustrious Highness".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">Kings and queens</a> have the style of His/Her <b>Majesty</b>.</li>
<li>Members of royal families (princes and princesses) generally have the style of <b><a href="/wiki/Royal_Highness" title="Royal Highness">Royal Highness</a></b>, although in some royal families (for instance, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>), more junior princes and princesses bear the style of Highness.</li>
<li>Reigning <a href="/wiki/Grand_duke" title="Grand duke">grand dukes</a> and grand duchesses hold the style of Royal Highness.</li>
<li>The styles of members of grand ducal families have been inconsistent. In <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a>, more senior members of the family have also been Royal Highnesses, but only due to their status as <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon-Parma" title="House of Bourbon-Parma">Princes of Bourbon-Parma</a> (itself an inconsistency as Parma was only ducal, but this family has male-line descent from kings of <a href="/wiki/Etruria" title="Etruria">Etruria</a>, Spain and France). In <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Baden" title="Grand Duchy of Baden">Baden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Hesse" title="Grand Duchy of Hesse">Hesse and by Rhine</a>, junior members held the style of <b>Grand Ducal Highness</b>.  Members of other grand ducal families (for instance, <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Oldenburg" title="Grand Duchy of Oldenburg">Oldenburg</a>) generally held the style of Highness.</li>
<li>Reigning <a href="/wiki/Duke" title="Duke">dukes</a> and duchesses bore the style of <b>Highness</b>, as did other members of ducal families. Junior members of some ducal families bore the style of <b>Ducal Serene Highness</b>, although it fell out of fashion.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">elector</a> of <a href="/wiki/Electorate_of_Hesse" title="Electorate of Hesse">Hesse-Kassel</a> also bore the style of Highness, as did other members of the Hesse-Kassel family.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Principality" title="Principality">Reigning princes</a> bear the style of <b>Serene Highness</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Durchlaucht</i>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Son Altesse Sérénissime</i>), as do other members of princely families. <a href="/wiki/Mediatized" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediatized">Mediatized</a> dukes and princes also bear the style of Serene Highness.</li>
<li>Mediatized princely <a href="/wiki/Count" title="Count">counts</a> and countesses bear the style of <b>Illustrious Highness</b> (<b>HIllH</b>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Erlaucht</i>).</li></ul>
<p>In addition to their national royal styles, many <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarchs</a> had "<a href="/wiki/List_of_Treaty_Titles_for_Monarchs" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Treaty Titles for Monarchs">treaty styles</a>" to distinguish one monarch from another in international settings. For example, the sovereign of the United Kingdom was customarily referred to as "Britannic Majesty", of France as "Most Christian Majesty", of Spain as "Catholic Majesty", of Hungary as "Apostolic Majesty", of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire_of_the_German_Nation" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Roman Empire of the German Nation">Holy Roman Empire of the German Nation</a> as "<a href="/wiki/Augustus_(honorific)" class="mw-redirect" title="Augustus (honorific)">August</a> Majesty", etc. Monarchs also typically had a longer style than other princely members within the same royal house. For example, the monarch of the United Kingdom has a much longer style than that of other members of the British royal family. The full style of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth II of the United Kingdom">Elizabeth II</a> in the United Kingdom is, "Elizabeth the Second, by the Grace of God, of the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland and of Her other Realms and Territories Queen, Head of the Commonwealth, Defender of the Faith".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_and_noble_styles_in_France">Royal and noble styles in France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial,_royal,_and_noble_styles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Royal and noble styles in France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Before the French Revolution, and from 1814 to 1830 (apart from a brief period in 1815), the King of France used the formal style of <b>Most Christian Majesty</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Majesté Très Chrétienne</i>). For general usage, however, until the final end of the Bourbon monarchy in 1830, kings and queens of France were styled <b>Majesty</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Majesté</i>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fils_de_France" title="Fils de France">Children and male-line grandchildren</a> of the King used the style of <b>Most High, Most Potent and Excellent Prince</b> or <b>Princess</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Très Haut, Très Puissant et Excellent Prince</i>) or <b>Royal Highness</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Altesse Royale</i>) and <b><a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Monseigneur</i></b> followed by their main title.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Louis,_Grand_Dauphin" title="Louis, Grand Dauphin">Louis, Grand Dauphin</a> was referred to by the title <b>Monseigneur</b> only, while retaining the style of <b>Royal Highness</b></li>
<li>The eldest unmarried daughter of the King was referred to by the title <b>Madame</b> only, while retaining the style of <b>Royal Highness</b></li>
<li>Younger unmarried daughters of the King were referred to by the title <b>Madame</b> followed by their first name, while retaining the style of <b>Royal Highness</b></li>
<li>The oldest brother of the King was referred to by the title <b>Monsieur</b> only, while retaining the style of <b>Royal Highness</b>
<ul><li>His eldest unmarried daughter was referred to by the title <b>Mademoiselle</b> only, while retaining the style of <b>Royal Highness</b>.</li>
<li>Younger unmarried daughters of Monsieur were referred to by the title <b>Mademoiselle</b> followed by their main title, while retaining the style of <b>Royal Highness</b></li></ul></li>
<li>Younger brothers of the King used only the style of <b>Monseigneur</b>, followed by their main title.</li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Prince_of_the_Blood" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince of the Blood">Princes of the Blood</a> used the style of <b>Most High, Most Potent and Excellent Prince</b> or <b>Princess</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Très Haut, Très Puissant et Excellent Prince</i>) or <b>Serene Highness</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Altesse Sérénissime</i>) and <b>Monseigneur</b> or <b>Mademoiselle</b> followed by their main title.
<ul><li>Until 1707, the First Prince of the Blood (head of the House of Condé) was referred to by the title <b>Monsieur le Prince</b> only, while retaining the style of <b>Serene Highness</b></li>
<li>After 1707, the head of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Cond%C3%A9" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Condé">House of Condé</a> was referred to by the title <b>Monsieur le Duc</b> only, while retaining the style of <b>Serene Highness</b></li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Foreign_prince" class="mw-redirect" title="Foreign prince">Foreign</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prince_du_sang#Legitimised_royal_offspring" title="Prince du sang">legitimized princes</a> used the title of <b>High and Potent Prince</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Haut et Puissant Prince</i>) and claimed the right to use the style of <b>Highness</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Altesse</i>) and <b>Monseigneur</b> followed by their main title.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Peerage_of_France" title="Peerage of France">Dukes and Peers</a> used the style of <b>Most High and Most Potent Lord</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Très-Haut et Très-Puissant Seigneur</i>), but in the 18th century, that style was used by lesser-ranked nobles</li>
<li>Other titled nobility used the style of <b>Most High and Potent Lord</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Très-Haut et Puissant Seigneur</i>) or <b>High and Potent Lord</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Haut et Puissant Seigneur</i>)</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Noble_styles_in_the_United_Kingdom">Noble styles in the United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial,_royal,_and_noble_styles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Noble styles in the United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Forms_of_address_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Forms of address in the United Kingdom">Forms of address in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<ul><li>Dukes and duchesses in the peerages of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> (who are not royalty of highness) bear the style of <b>Grace</b> (e.g. "His Grace", "Her Grace", or "Your Grace").<sup id="cite_ref-Maunder_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maunder-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> They also hold the style of <b>Most High, Potent, and Noble Prince</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> but even in the most formal situations, this is usually shortened to <b>The Most Noble</b>, which is still considered to be very formal.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marquess" title="Marquess">Marquesses</a> and marchionesses bear the styles of <b>The Most Honourable</b> and <b>Lordship</b> (e.g. "His Lordship", "Her Ladyship", "Your Lordship", or "Your Ladyship").<sup id="cite_ref-Maunder_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maunder-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Burke1852_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burke1852-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> They also hold the style of <b>Most Noble, Most Honourable, and Potent Prince</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-Crabb1823_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crabb1823-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> but even in the most formal situations, this style is rarely used.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">Earls</a>, countesses, <a href="/wiki/Viscount" title="Viscount">viscounts</a>, viscountesses, <a href="/wiki/Baron" title="Baron">barons</a>, and baronesses bear the styles of <b>The Right Honourable</b> and <b>Lordship</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-Album_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Album-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Maunder_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maunder-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Barons_in_Scotland" title="Barons in Scotland">Scottish barons</a> bear the style of <b>The Much Honoured</b>.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Belgium">Belgium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial,_royal,_and_noble_styles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Belgium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Archdukes are always styled Your Royal and Imperial Highness.</li>
<li>Princes of noble blood, Your Serene Highness, and addressed monseigneur.</li>
<li>Dukes are addressed as Monseigneur.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Noble_styles_in_Germany">Noble styles in Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial,_royal,_and_noble_styles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Noble styles in Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mediatized_nobility">Mediatized nobility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial,_royal,_and_noble_styles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mediatized nobility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Mediatization" class="mw-redirect" title="Mediatization">Mediatized</a> <a href="/wiki/Herzog" title="Herzog">dukes</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Reichsherzöge</i>) and <a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrst" title="Fürst">princes</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Reichsfürsten</i>) in Germany bore the style of  <b>Serene Highness</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Durchlaucht" class="mw-redirect" title="Durchlaucht">Durchlaucht</a></i>) or, in the case of dukes, Ducal Serene Highness. With regard to dukes, this fell out of use in the 19th century, at least for the reigning members (who are styled as Highness).</li>
<li>Mediatized <a href="/wiki/Imperial_count" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial count">counts</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Reichsgrafen</i>) in Germany bore the style of <b>Illustrious Highness</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Erlaucht" class="mw-redirect" title="Erlaucht">Erlaucht</a></i>).</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-mediatized_nobility">Non-mediatized nobility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial,_royal,_and_noble_styles&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Non-mediatized nobility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Non-mediatized noble <a href="/wiki/Herzog" title="Herzog">dukes</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Herzöge</i>) and <a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrst" title="Fürst">princes</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Fürsten</i>) used to bear the title of <b>Ducal/Princely Grace</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">herzogliche/fürstliche Gnaden</i>). They were rare, though, and at the beginning of the 20th century, they were altogether granted the style of Serene Highness by <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Franz_Joseph_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Emperor Franz Joseph I">Emperor Franz Joseph I</a>.</li>
<li>Non-mediatized <a href="/wiki/Graf" title="Graf">counts</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Grafen</i>) in Germany bore the style of <b>High-born</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Hochgeboren" title="Hochgeboren">Hochgeboren</a></i>).</li>
<li>Other German nobles below the rank of count bore the style of <b>High Well-born</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Hochwohlgeboren" title="Hochwohlgeboren">Hochwohlgeboren</a></i>). Another style was <b>Well-born</b> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de"><a href="/wiki/Wohlgeboren" title="Wohlgeboren">Wohlgeboren</a></i>), which ranked below High Well-born but was not used for proper nobility and therefore fell out of use.</li></ul>
