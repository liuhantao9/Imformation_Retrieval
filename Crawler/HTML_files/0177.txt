<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p><p>When a person (traditionally the wife in many cultures) assumes the <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a> of their <a href="/wiki/Spouse" title="Spouse">spouse</a>, that name replaces the person's birth <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a>, which in the case of the wife is called the <b>maiden name</b> (<b>birth name</b> is also used as a gender-neutral or masculine substitute for maiden name), whereas a <b>married name</b> is a family name or surname adopted by a person upon marriage.  In Scotland it is legal and not unusual for a woman to retain her maiden name after marriage. In point of fact if a woman's family was more 'influential' than the groom then he sometimes took his bride's family name.
</p><p>In some jurisdictions, <a href="/wiki/Name_change" title="Name change">changing one's name</a> requires a legal procedure. Nevertheless, in some jurisdictions anyone who either marries or divorces may change their name. Due to increasing security and identification needs, even where it is legal, the common law<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Wikipedia_and_sources_that_mirror_or_use_it" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="This claim cites another Wikipedia article. Articles need references to reliable third-party sources. (March 2019)">circular reference</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> method<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> is now rarely accepted except at marriage (especially for women)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. Traditionally, in the <a href="/wiki/English-speaking_world" title="English-speaking world">Anglophone</a> <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">West</a> only women change their names on marriage, but in some instances men may change their last names upon marriage as well, including <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex couples</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-taking_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taking-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, only eight states have an official name change for a man as part of their marriage process, and in others a man may petition a court or—where not prohibited—use the common law method (though government agencies sometimes do not recognize this procedure for men). Due to the widespread practice of women changing their names at marriage, they encounter little difficulty using the common law method at marriage in those jurisdictions that permit it.<sup id="cite_ref-taking_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taking-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-name_change_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-name_change-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the remainder of this article, birth name, family name, surname, married name and maiden name refer to <a href="/wiki/Patrilineal_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrilineal surname">patrilineal surnames</a> unless explicitly described as referring to <a href="/wiki/Matrilineal_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="Matrilineal surname">matrilineal surnames</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Canadian_name" title="Canadian name">Canadian name</a></div>
<p>In most of Canada, either partner may informally assume the spouse's surname after marriage, so long as it is not for the purposes of fraud. The same is true for people in <a href="/wiki/Common-law_marriage" title="Common-law marriage">common-law relationships</a>, in some provinces. This is not considered a legal name change in most provinces, excluding British Columbia.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> For federal purposes, such as a Canadian <a href="/wiki/Passport" title="Passport">passport</a>, Canadians may also assume their partner's surname if they are in a common-law relationship.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In the province of British Columbia, people have to undergo a legal name change if they want to use a combined surname after marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Their marriage certificate is considered proof of their new name.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The custom in Québec was similar to the one <a href="/wiki/Married_and_maiden_names#France" class="mw-redirect" title="Married and maiden names">in France</a> until 1981. Women would traditionally go by their husband's surname in daily life, but their maiden name remained their legal name.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Since the passage of a 1981 provincial law intended to promote gender equality, as outlined in the <a href="/wiki/Qu%C3%A9bec_Charter_of_Rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Québec Charter of Rights">Québec Charter of Rights</a>, no change may be made to a person's name without the authorization of the registrar of civil status or the authorization of the court. Newlyweds who wish to change their names upon marriage must therefore go through the same procedure as those changing their names for other reasons. The registrar of civil status may refuse to authorize a name change if:
</p>
<ol><li>the name the person generally uses does not correspond to the name on their birth certificate,</li>
<li>the name is of foreign origin or too difficult to pronounce or write in its original form, or</li>
<li>the name invites ridicule or has become infamous.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>This law does not make it legal for a woman to change her name immediately upon marriage, as marriage is not listed among the reasons for a name change.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="English-speaking_world">English-speaking world</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: English-speaking world">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This process is expedited for newly married persons in that their <a href="/wiki/Marriage_certificate" title="Marriage certificate">marriage certificate</a>, in combination with <a href="/wiki/Identity_document" title="Identity document">identification</a> using their married name, is usually accepted as evidence of the change, due to the widespread custom, but the process still requires approaching every contact who uses the old name and asking them to use the new. Unless the statutes where the marriage occurred specify that a name change may occur at marriage (in which case the marriage certificate indicates the new name), the courts have officially recognized that such a change is a result of the <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> right of a person (man, woman, and sometimes child) to change their name. However, men encounter more difficulties in changing their last names.<sup id="cite_ref-lawsuit_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lawsuit-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> There were some early cases in the USA which held that under <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a>, a woman was required to take her husband's name,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> but newer cases overturned that (see "Retain the birth name" below).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Currently, American women do not have to change their names by law.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Lindon_v._First_National_Bank" title="Lindon v. First National Bank">Lindon v. First National Bank</a></i>, 10 F. 894 (W.D. Pa. 1882), is one of the very earliest precedent-setting <a href="/wiki/Federal_judiciary_of_the_United_States" title="Federal judiciary of the United States">US federal court</a> cases involving <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> <a href="/wiki/Name_change" title="Name change">name change</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> A woman who had changed her last name to one that was not her husband's original surname was trying to claim control over her <a href="/wiki/Inheritance" title="Inheritance">inheritance</a>. The court ruled in her favor. This set forth many things. By common law, one may lawfully change their name and be "known and recognized" by that new name. Also, one may enter into any kinds of contracts in their new adopted name. Contracts include employment (see <i><a href="/wiki/Coppage_v._Kansas" title="Coppage v. Kansas">Coppage v. Kansas</a></i> 236 U.S. 1), and one can be recognized legally in court in their new name. In 1967 in <i>Erie Exchange v. Lane</i>, 246 Md. 55 (1967) the Maryland Court of Appeals held that a married woman can lawfully adopt an assumed name, even if it is not her birth name or the name of her lawful husband, without legal proceedings.<sup id="cite_ref-justia1976_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-justia1976-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_options">Common options</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Common options">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl><dt>Use husband's family name</dt></dl>
<p>In the past, a woman in England would usually assume her new husband's family name (or surname) after marriage; often she was compelled to do so under <a href="/wiki/Coverture" title="Coverture">coverture</a> laws. Assuming the husband's surname remains common practice today in the United Kingdom (although there is no law that states the name must be changed) and in other countries such as Australia, New Zealand, Pakistan, Gibraltar, Falkland Islands, Ireland, India, Philippines, the English-speaking provinces of Canada and the United States. 
</p><p>In some communities in India, spouses and children take the father's first name or proper name<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. 
</p><p>Often there are interesting variations of name adoption, including family name adoption. In Massachusetts, for instance, a <a href="/wiki/Claudia_Goldin" title="Claudia Goldin">Harvard study</a> in 2004 found that about 87% of college-educated women take their husbands' name on marriage, down from a peak before 1975 of over 90%, but up from about 80% in 1990. The same study found women with a college degree were "two to four times (depending on age) more likely to retain their surname" than those without a college degree.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the lowlands of Scotland in the 16th century, married women did not change their surnames, but today it is common practice to do so.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Usually, the children of these marriages are given their father's surname. Some families (mainly in the USA) have a custom of using the mother's maiden name as a <a href="/wiki/Middle_name" title="Middle name">middle name</a> for one of the children—<a href="/wiki/Franklin_Delano_Roosevelt" class="mw-redirect" title="Franklin Delano Roosevelt">Franklin Delano Roosevelt</a> received his middle name in this way<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> or even as a first name. <a href="/wiki/Spessard_Holland" title="Spessard Holland">Spessard Holland</a>, a former Governor of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> and former Senator, whose mother's maiden name was Virginia Spessard, received his first name in this way.
</p>
<dl><dt>Retain the birth name</dt></dl>
<p>Women who keep their own surname after marriage may do so for a number of reasons:
</p>
<ul><li>Objection to the perceived inequality of this tradition.<sup id="cite_ref-made_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-made-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Being the last member of their family with that surname.</li>
<li>To avoid the hassle of paperwork related to their change of name.</li>
<li>Wishing to retain their identity</li>
<li>Preferring their last name to their spouse's last name</li>
<li>Fear of professional ramifications.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The American suffragist and abolitionist <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Stone" title="Lucy Stone">Lucy Stone</a> (1818–1893) made a national issue of a married woman's right to keep her own surname (as she herself did upon marriage) as part of her efforts for women's rights in the U.S. Women who choose not to use their husbands' surnames have been called "Lucy Stoners".<sup id="cite_ref-Roiphe2004_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roiphe2004-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In 1879, when Boston women were granted the franchise in school elections, Stone registered to vote. But officials would not allow her to vote unless she added "Blackwell", her husband's last name, to her signature. This she refused to do, and so she was not able to vote. She did not challenge the action in a court of law.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Stone_League" title="Lucy Stone League">Lucy Stone League</a>, named after her, was founded in 1921 by <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Hale_(feminist)" title="Ruth Hale (feminist)">Ruth Hale</a>; it was the first group to fight for women to be allowed to keep their maiden name after marriage&#8212;and to use it legally.<sup id="cite_ref-Ch15_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ch15-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Ruth Hale challenged in federal court any<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> government edict that would not recognize a married woman (such as herself) by the name she chose to use.<sup id="cite_ref-Ch15_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ch15-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In May 1921 Hale obtained a <a href="/wiki/Real_estate" title="Real estate">real estate</a> deed issued in her birth name rather than her married name, Mrs. Heywood Broun.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1925 <a href="/wiki/Doris_Fleischman" title="Doris Fleischman">Doris Fleischman</a> became the first married woman in the United States to receive a passport in her own name.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> But by the early 1930s the <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Stone_League" title="Lucy Stone League">Lucy Stone League</a> was inactive.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>In <i>People ex rel. Rago v. Lipsky</i>, 63 N.E.2d 642 (Ill. 1945), the Appellate Court of Illinois, First District did not allow a married woman to stay registered to vote under her birth name, due to "the long-established custom, policy and rule of the common law among English-speaking peoples whereby a woman's name is changed by marriage and her husband's surname becomes as a matter of law her surname."<sup id="cite_ref-courtlistener1_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-courtlistener1-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1950, <a href="/wiki/Jane_Grant" title="Jane Grant">Jane Grant</a> and 22 former members restarted the Lucy Stone League; its first meeting was on 22 Mar 1950 in New York City. Grant promptly won the <a href="/wiki/Census_Bureau" class="mw-redirect" title="Census Bureau">Census Bureau</a>'s agreement that a married woman could use her birth surname as her official or real name in the census. (<i>The New York Times</i>, 10 Apr 1950).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>In the 1950s and 1960s, the League widened its focus to include all discrimination against women in the USA; the League was a forerunner of the <a href="/wiki/National_Organization_for_Women" title="National Organization for Women">National Organization for Women</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <i>State ex rel. Krupa v. Green</i>, 177 N.E.2d 616 (Ohio 1961), the Ohio appellate court allowed a married woman to register to vote in her birth name which she had openly and solely used, and been well-known to use, before her marriage, and held that she could use that name as a candidate for public office.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-courtlistener1_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-courtlistener1-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1972 in <i>Stuart v. Board of Elections</i>, 266 Md. 440, 446, on the question of whether a wife could register to vote in her birth name rather than her husband's last name, the Maryland Court of Appeals held, "[A] married woman's surname does not become that of her husband where, as here, she evidences a clear intent to consistently and nonfraudulently use her birth given name subsequent to her marriage."<sup id="cite_ref-justia1976_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-justia1976-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1970s the Olympia Brown League was founded to help women's name rights in Milwaukee, in response to a court decision against women seeking to keep their maiden names upon marriage; <a href="/wiki/Olympia_Brown" title="Olympia Brown">Olympia Brown</a> had kept hers upon her marriage in 1873.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Specifically, the case with that court decision was <i>Kruzel v. Podell</i> (1975), in which the Supreme Court of Wisconsin decided that a woman upon marriage adopts the last name of her husband by customarily using that name after marriage, but also stated that no law required her to.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1975 in <i>Dunn v. Palermo</i>, the Supreme Court of Tennessee held that "in this jurisdiction a woman, upon marriage, has a freedom of choice. She may elect to retain her own surname or she may adopt the surname of her husband. The choice is hers. We hold that a person's legal name is that given at birth, or as <i>voluntarily</i> changed by either spouse at the time of marriage, or as changed by affirmative acts as provided under the Constitution and laws of the State of Tennessee. So long as a person's name remains constant and consistent, and unless and until changed in the prescribed manner, and absent any fraudulent or legally impermissible intent, the State has no legitimate concern."<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A new version of the Lucy Stone League was started in 1997, again focused on name equality.<sup id="cite_ref-history_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The American laws and cases noted above do not include all the relevant American laws and cases regarding maiden names. Currently, American women do not have to change their names by law.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Join both names (hyphenation)</dt></dl>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Double-barrelled_name" title="Double-barrelled name">Double-barrelled name</a></div>
<p>It is less common for women, especially in the USA and Canada, to add their spouse's name and their own birth name.<sup id="cite_ref-taking_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-taking-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Name blending</dt></dl>
<p>Although less common than name joining, a growing trend is the <a href="/wiki/Name_blending" title="Name blending">blending of two surnames</a> upon marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> This means adding <i>parts</i> of the two names. An example is <a href="/wiki/Dawn_O%27Porter" title="Dawn O&#39;Porter">Dawn O'Porter</a>.
</p>
<dl><dt>Birth name as middle name</dt></dl>
<p>Examples are <a href="/wiki/Hillary_Clinton" title="Hillary Clinton">Hillary Rodham Clinton</a><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Kim_Kardashian_West" class="mw-redirect" title="Kim Kardashian West">Kim Kardashian West</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Children">Children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United States, some states or areas have laws that restrict what surname a child may have. For example, <a href="/wiki/Tennessee" title="Tennessee">Tennessee</a> allows a child to be given a surname that does not include that of the father only upon "the concurrent submission of a sworn application to that effect signed by both parents."<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_status_of_male_name_changes_at_marriage">Legal status of male name changes at marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legal status of male name changes at marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2007, Michael Buday and Diana Bijon enlisted the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_Liberties_Union" title="American Civil Liberties Union">American Civil Liberties Union</a> and filed a discrimination lawsuit against the state of <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. According to the ACLU, the obstacles facing a husband who wishes to adopt his wife's last name violated the equal protection clause provided by the 14th Amendment of the Constitution.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> At the time of the lawsuit, only the states of <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iowa" title="Iowa">Iowa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a> explicitly allowed a man to change his name through marriage with the same ease as a woman. As a result of the lawsuit, the Name Equality Act of 2007 was passed to allow either spouse to change their name, using their marriage license as the means of the change; the law took effect in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-lawsuit_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lawsuit-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NameEqualityAct_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NameEqualityAct-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, a Florida man successfully forced the Florida Department of Motor Vehicles to accept his decision to take his wife's last name.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Feminism_and_preserving_one.27s_personal_name"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Feminism_and_preserving_one's_personal_name">Feminism and preserving one's personal name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Feminism and preserving one&#039;s personal name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>(Also see "Retain the birth name" above.)<br />
</p><p>The feminist <a href="/wiki/Lucy_Stone" title="Lucy Stone">Lucy Stone</a> (1818–1893) made a national issue of a married woman's right to keep her own surname (as she herself did upon marriage) as part of her efforts for women's rights in the U.S. Because of her, women who choose not to use their husbands' surnames have been called "Lucy Stoners".<sup id="cite_ref-Roiphe2004_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Roiphe2004-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The feminist <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Cady_Stanton" title="Elizabeth Cady Stanton">Elizabeth Cady Stanton</a> took her husband's surname as part of her own, signing herself Elizabeth Cady Stanton or E. Cady Stanton, but she refused to be addressed as Mrs. Henry B. Stanton. She wrote in 1847 that "the custom of calling women Mrs. John This and Mrs. Tom That and colored men <a href="/wiki/Sambo_(racial_term)" title="Sambo (racial term)">Sambo</a> and Zip <a href="/wiki/List_of_ethnic_slurs" title="List of ethnic slurs">Coon</a>, is founded on the principle that white men are lords of all."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Olsen1994_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen1994-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Later, when addressing the judiciary committee of the state legislature of New York in 1860 in a speech called "A Slave's Appeal", she stated in part, "The negro [slave] has no name. He is Cuffy Douglas or Cuffy Brooks, just whose Cuffy he may chance to be. The woman has no name. She is Mrs. Richard Roe or Mrs. John Doe, just whose Mrs. she may chance to be."<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The feminist <a href="/wiki/Jane_Grant" title="Jane Grant">Jane Grant</a>, co-founder of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i>, wrote in 1943 of her efforts to keep her name despite her marriage, as well as other women's experiences with their maiden names regarding <a href="/wiki/Military" title="Military">military</a> service, passports, <a href="/wiki/Voting" title="Voting">voting</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Outline_of_business" title="Outline of business">business</a>. 
</p><p>More recently, the feminist <a href="/wiki/Jill_Filipovic" title="Jill Filipovic">Jill Filipovic</a>'s opposition to name change for women who marry was published in <i>The Guardian</i> in 2013 as “Why should married women change their names? Let men change theirs”, and cited as recommended reading on the <a href="/wiki/Social_construction_of_gender" title="Social construction of gender">social construction of gender</a> in <i>Critical Encounters in Secondary English: Teaching Literacy Theory to Adolescents</i> by Deborah Appleman (2014).<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Filipovic married Ty Lohrer McCormick in 2018, and kept her last name upon marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-marriage_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marriage-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-name_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-name-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_as_security_question">Use as security question</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Use as security question">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One's mother's maiden name has been a common <a href="/wiki/Security_question" title="Security question">security question</a> in American banking since at least the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-Levin_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levin-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish-speaking_world">Spanish-speaking world</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Spanish-speaking world">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Spouses keep their original surnames. Following <a href="/wiki/Spanish_naming_customs" title="Spanish naming customs">Spanish naming customs</a>, a person's name consists of a <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> (simple or composite) followed by two family names (surnames), the father's and the mother's. Any children whom a couple have together, take both first-surnames, so if "José Gómez Hevia" and "María Reyes García" had a child named "Andrés", the resulting name would be "Andrés Gómez Reyes". In <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, a 1995 reform in the law allows the parents to choose whether the father's or the mother's surname goes first, although this order must be the same for all their children. For instance, the name of the son of the couple in the example above could be settled whether "Andrés Gómez Reyes" or "Andrés Reyes Gómez".<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In some Spanish-American countries it is customary for women to unofficially add the husband's first surname after her own, for social purposes such as invitation letters or event announcements. The couple above may introduce themselves as José Gómez Hevia and María Reyes de Gómez. It is also common to name, in formal settings, the wife of a man as "señora de ", followed by her husband's first surname.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Portuguese-speaking_world">Portuguese-speaking world</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Portuguese-speaking world">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wives usually assume the family name of their spouse, although there is a recent trend of women keeping their maiden names<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>. Following <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_name" title="Portuguese name">Portuguese naming customs</a>, a person's name consists of a <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> (simple or composite) followed by two family names (surnames), the mother's and the father's. Any children whom a couple have together, take both second-surnames.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_European_countries">Other European countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other European countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Austria">Austria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Austria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, since 1 April 2013, marriage does not automatically change a woman's name; therefore a name change can only take place upon legal application. Before that date, the default was for a married woman's name to be changed to that of her husband, unless she legally applied to opt out of this.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, by executive decision since 2011<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> and by law since 2013<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>, any married person may officially use their spouse's name as a common name by substituting or compounding it to their own. Before this it was common for married women to use their husband's name in everyday life but this had no legal recognition.
</p><p>A common name does not replace a person's family name as written on their birth certificate.
</p><p>From 4 March 2002 to 4 December 2009, children given both parents' names had to have them separated by a double dash (ex: Dupont--Clairemont). On 4 December 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_State_(France)" class="mw-redirect" title="Council of State (France)">Conseil d'État</a> ruled that a space can be used instead of the double dash. As a result, forms asking for the family name (<i>nom de famille</i>) do so on two lines ("<i>1ère partie</i>: ..... ", "<i>2e partie</i>: ....")<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, since 1977, a woman may adopt her husband's surname or a man may adopt his wife's surname. One of them may use a name combined from both surnames. The remaining single name is the "family name" (<i>Ehename</i>), which will be the surname of the children. If a man and woman both decide to keep and use their birth names after the wedding (no combined name), they have to declare one of those names the "family name". A combined name is not possible as a family name, but, since 2005, it has been possible to have a double name as a family name if one already had a double name, and the partner adopts that name. Double names then must be hyphenated. All family members must use that double name.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greece">Greece</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Greece">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 1983, when <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> adopted a new <a href="/wiki/Marriage_law" title="Marriage law">marriage law</a> which guaranteed <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a> between the spouses,<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> women in Greece are required to <i>keep</i> their birth names for their whole life.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Italy">Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Spouses keep their original surnames. According to the Italian Civil Code (article 143 bis), a woman who marries keeps her surname and has the option of adding her husband's surname after hers. Non-Italian citizens getting married in Italy will not have their surname changed in Italy. However, brides or grooms can request their surname change in their home country.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Netherlands">Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, persons who have been married in the Netherlands or entered into a registered partnership will remain registered under their birth name. They are, however, permitted to use their partner's last name for social purposes or join both names. Upon marriage or registered partnership, one may also indicate how one would like to be addressed by registering one's choice at the Municipal Basis Administration (<i>Gemeentelijke Basis Administratie</i>) (although the birth name does not change). One may choose to be called by one's own name, one's partner's name, one's own name followed by one's partner's name (hyphenated), or one's partner's name followed by their own name (hyphenated). Both men and women may make this choice upon registering to get married or entering into a registered partnership. If the marriage or registered partnership ends, one may continue to use the ex-partner's last surname unless the ex-partner disagrees and requests the court to forbid the use of the ex-partner's surname.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, while name changes due to marriages performed in the Netherlands cannot be processed, it is certainly possible in the Netherlands to process name changes due to marriages performed outside the Netherlands, provided certain conditions are met: the marriage must be registered abroad, the application for name change abroad must be requested on the same date as the marriage date, the changed name must be recorded abroad on a certificate in accordance with the local rules of the foreign country, and the marriage and name change as well as proof of application as of the date of the marriage must be legalized/<a href="/wiki/Apostille_convention" class="mw-redirect" title="Apostille convention">apostilled</a> and provided to the Dutch consulate or Dutch municipality upon return to the Netherlands.
</p><p>The reason is that international marriages are not necessarily governed by Dutch Law but by Private International Law which is codified in the Netherlands in the "Commoner's Law Book" (<i>Burgerlijke Wetboek</i>) Book No. 10, Private International Law, Title 2 - The Name, Article 24.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Before the birth or adoption of a first child, married parents may choose the child's surname (mother's or father's but not both). If no choice is made, the child automatically bears the father's surname. Any further children will also go by this name. If the parents are not married, the children will automatically have their mother's name unless otherwise indicated.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is a widespread, though not universal, custom for a newly married wife to adopt the husband's family name. However, as Russia is not a common law country, any name change requires a formal procedure including an official application to the <a href="/wiki/Civil_registry" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil registry">civil acts registrar</a>. As the same registrar also records marriages, for the convenience sake it is often done during the marriage proceedings, as governed by the Federal Law #143-FZ "On Civil State Acts", and the couple's marriage certificate has an option of having one common family name, or both spouses going by their original surname. However, the law is entirely gender neutral, and the couple may adopt either of their surnames (a husband adopting his wife's family name is an uncommon but by no means unheard-of practice, which is generally accepted and carries little to no social stigma), or even a completely different one. The law also recognizes the couple's right to use the combined family name, and for the either of the spouses to reclaim their original surname in the case the marriage is dissolved.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Asian_countries">Asian countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Asian countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traditionally, a married woman keeps her name unchanged, without adopting her husband's surname.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> A child would inherit the father's surname. This is still the norm in <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">mainland China</a>, though the marriage law explicitly states that a child may use either parent's surname. It is also common for two children born to the same parents to take different surnames, one after the father and the other after the mother. It is also possible, though far less common, for a child to combine both parents' surnames.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> Due to Western influence, a very small portion of people in some areas of <a href="/wiki/Greater_China" title="Greater China">greater China</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a>, have also adopted the tradition of women changing their English last name, or prepending their husband’s Chinese surname to her own in official occasions or business cards but rarely on resident identification or travel documents. Amongst the Chinese diaspora overseas, especially in Southeast Asia, women rarely legally adopt their spouse's surname.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iran">Iran</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Iran">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_system_of_Iran" title="Judicial system of Iran">Judicial system of Iran</a>, until 1976, it was the man who had the right to choose the surname of his family, including his wife. However, since then, all people are allowed to choose their family names, and there has been no more imposition on behalf of the husband regarding his wife's change in surname.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> law does not recognize married couples who have different surnames as lawful husband and wife, which means that 96% of married Japanese women take their husband's surname.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> In 2015, the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese Supreme Court">Japanese Supreme Court</a> upheld the name-change law, ruling that it was not unconstitutional, noting that women could use informally their maiden names, and stating that it was the parliamentarians who should decide on whether to pass new legislation on separate spousal names.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Korea">Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traditionally, Korean women keep their family names after their marriage, but their children take the father's surname. In the premodern,people were extremely conscious of familial values and their own family identities. Korean women keep their surnames after marriage based on traditional reasoning that it is what they inherited from their parents and ancestors.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines">Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Filipino_name#Maiden_and_married_names" title="Filipino name">Filipino name §&#160;Maiden and married names</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Civil_Code_of_the_Philippines" title="Civil Code of the Philippines">Civil Code</a> provides several options for married women on what surname to take upon marriage:
</p>
<ul><li>keep her middle name (maternal surname) and add her husband's surname to the maiden name (e.g. <i>Maria Isabella Flores Garcia-Dimaculangan</i>/<i>Ma. Isabella F. Garcia-Dimaculangan</i>);</li>
<li>take the husband's surname and make her maiden name the middle name (<i>Maria Isabella Garcia Dimaculangan</i>/<i>Ma. Isabella G. Dimaculangan</i>);</li>
<li>take the husband's full name, with a prefix to indicate that she is his wife (e.g. <i>Mrs.</i>/<i>Ms. Dimaculangan</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>A woman may also keep her maiden name, as Philippine law does not require a woman to take her husband's surname at marriage. This is commonly done for professional reasons, as a woman may want to retain her name among her business contacts or audience.
</p><p>The Civil Code also states that children as the result of the marriage will take the mother's middle name (maiden surname) and the father's surname. To illustrate this, the children of a married couple named <i>Maria Josefa Lopez Mañego-Luansing</i> and <i>Juan Candido Luansing</i> will take the middle name <i>Mañego</i> and the surname <i>Luansing</i>, so, one daughter with a given name of <i>Juliana</i> will be named <i>Juliana Mañego Luansing</i>.
</p><p>Married women in professional circles (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Macapagal-Arroyo" class="mw-redirect" title="Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo">Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korina_Sanchez-Roxas" class="mw-redirect" title="Korina Sanchez-Roxas">Korina Sanchez-Roxas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vilma_Santos-Recto" class="mw-redirect" title="Vilma Santos-Recto">Vilma Santos-Recto</a>) typically join their maiden and married surnames in both professional and legal use (e.g. <i>Maria Isabella Flores Garcia-Dimaculangan</i>/<i>Ma. Isabella F. Garcia-Dimaculangan</i>). This allows them to be identified as married, and keep track of their professional achievements without being confused for being two different individual (e.g. <i>Maria Isabella Flores Garcia</i>/<i>Ma. Isabella F. Garcia, as against </i>Maria Isabella Garcia Dimaculangan<i>/</i>Ma. Isabella G. Dimaculangan)
</p><p>An older scheme based on <a href="/wiki/Spanish_naming_customs" title="Spanish naming customs">Spanish naming customs</a> add the particle <i>de</i> ("of") between the maiden and married surnames (e.g. <i>Maria Isabella Garcia de Dimaculangan</i> or <i>Ma. Isabella G. de Dimaculangan</i>). This tradition is no longer common.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Taiwan">Taiwan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Taiwan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Taiwanese women generally keep their surnames after marriage while their children may inherit either the father's or the mother's. It is, however, legal to take the spouse’s surname.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> Some older women have the husband's surname added to theirs, as was common in the early to mid-<a href="/wiki/20th_century" title="20th century">20th century</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thailand">Thailand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Thailand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A Thai wife who adopted her husband's surname due to the old law requiring it, can also change back to her original surname.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkey">Turkey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Turkey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 2014, women in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> are allowed to keep their birth names alone for their whole life instead of using their husbands' names.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> Previously, the Turkish Code of Civil Law, Article 187, required a married woman to use her husband's surname; or else to use her birth name in front of her husband's name by giving a written application to the marriage officer or the civil registry office. In 2014, the Constitutional Court ruled that prohibiting married women from retaining only maiden names is a violation of their rights.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genealogy">Genealogy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maiden_and_married_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Genealogy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Genealogists often also make note of all surnames used by a person during their lifetime (such as those acquired from birth parents, those assigned at birth when the father is unknown or not acknowledged, those acquired at marriage, and those acquired at a remarriage). For example, an illegitimate male child abandoned at birth in Italy or in other European countries will receive no surname from either of his birth parents but, instead, will be assigned a surname—often invented from one of the three kingdoms of nature, e.g., mineral ("Pietra"), vegetable ("Rosa") or animal ("Leoni"), or otherwise according to custom within a locality, such as "Esposito" (meaning "abandoned") or "Casa Grande" (referring to the "Domo Magna," e.g., the <i>ospizio</i> [hospital] where abandoned).<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
