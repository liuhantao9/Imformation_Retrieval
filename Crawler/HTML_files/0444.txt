<p><b>Sir John Betjeman</b> <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Commander of the Order of the British Empire">CBE</a></span> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;b&#39; in &#39;buy&#39;">b</span><span title="/ɛ/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">ɛ</span><span title="/tʃ/: &#39;ch&#39; in &#39;China&#39;">tʃ</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ən/: &#39;on&#39; in &#39;button&#39;">ən</span></span>/</a></span></span>; 28 August 1906&#160;&#8211;&#32;19 May 1984) was an English poet, writer, and broadcaster who described himself in <i><a href="/wiki/Who%27s_Who_(UK)" title="Who&#39;s Who (UK)">Who's Who</a></i> as a "poet and <a href="/wiki/Hack_writer" title="Hack writer">hack</a>".  He was <a href="/wiki/Poet_Laureate_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom">Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom</a> from 1972 until his death.
</p><p>He was a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="Victorian Society">Victorian Society</a> and a passionate defender of <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian architecture</a>. He began his career as a journalist and ended it as one of the most popular British Poets Laureate and a much-loved figure on British television. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Betjeman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Betjeman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Betjeman was born John Betjemann. He was the son of a prosperous silverware maker of Dutch descent. His parents, Mabel (<i>née</i> Dawson) and Ernest Betjemann, had a family firm at 34–42 <a href="/wiki/Pentonville_Road" title="Pentonville Road">Pentonville Road</a> which manufactured the kind of ornamental household furniture and gadgets distinctive to <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorians</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the First World War the family name was changed to the less German-looking Betjeman. His father's forebears had actually come from the present day <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> more than a century earlier, setting up their home and business in <a href="/wiki/Islington" title="Islington">Islington</a>, London, and during the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Anglo-Dutch_War" title="Fourth Anglo-Dutch War">Fourth Anglo-Dutch War</a> had, ironically, added the extra "-n" to avoid the anti-Dutch sentiment existing at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-Mowl2011_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mowl2011-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Betjeman was baptised at St Anne's Church, Highgate Rise, a 19th-century church at the foot of Highgate West Hill.  The family lived at <a href="/wiki/Parliament_Hill,_London" title="Parliament Hill, London">Parliament Hill</a> Mansions in the <a href="/wiki/Lissenden_Gardens" title="Lissenden Gardens">Lissenden Gardens</a> private estate in <a href="/wiki/Gospel_Oak" title="Gospel Oak">Gospel Oak</a> in north London.
</p><p>In 1909, the Betjemanns moved half a mile north to more opulent <a href="/wiki/Highgate" title="Highgate">Highgate</a>. From West Hill they lived in the reflected glory of the <a href="/wiki/Holly_Lodge_Estate" title="Holly Lodge Estate">Burdett-Coutts estate</a>:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><div class="poem">
<p>Here from my eyrie, as the sun went down,<br />
I heard the old <a href="/wiki/North_London_Railway" title="North London Railway">North London</a> puff and shunt,<br />
Glad that I did not live in Gospel Oak.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBetjeman1960_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBetjeman1960-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<p>Betjeman's early schooling was at the local Byron House and <a href="/wiki/Highgate_School" title="Highgate School">Highgate School</a>, where he was taught by poet <a href="/wiki/T._S._Eliot" title="T. S. Eliot">T. S. Eliot</a>. After this, he boarded at the <a href="/wiki/Dragon_School" title="Dragon School">Dragon School</a> <a href="/wiki/Preparatory_school_(England)" class="mw-redirect" title="Preparatory school (England)">preparatory school</a> in North Oxford and <a href="/wiki/Marlborough_College" title="Marlborough College">Marlborough College</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Independent_school_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent school (UK)">public school</a> in Wiltshire. In his penultimate year, he joined the secret Society of Amici<sup id="cite_ref-Hinde1992_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hinde1992-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> in which he was a contemporary of both <a href="/wiki/Louis_MacNeice" title="Louis MacNeice">Louis MacNeice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Graham_Shepard" title="Graham Shepard">Graham Shepard</a>. He founded <i><a href="/wiki/The_Heretick" title="The Heretick">The Heretick</a></i>, a satirical magazine that lampooned Marlborough's obsession with sport. While at school, his exposure to the works of <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Machen" title="Arthur Machen">Arthur Machen</a> won him over to <a href="/wiki/High_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="High Church">High Church</a> <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglicanism</a>, a conversion of importance to his later writing and conception of the arts.<sup id="cite_ref-Gardner2006_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardner2006-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Betjeman left Marlborough in July 1925.<sup id="cite_ref-register_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-register-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Magdalen_College.2C_Oxford"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Magdalen_College,_Oxford">Magdalen College, Oxford</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Betjeman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Magdalen College, Oxford">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Betjeman entered the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">University of Oxford</a> with difficulty, having failed the mathematics portion of the university's matriculation exam, <a href="/wiki/Responsions" title="Responsions">Responsions</a>. He was, however, admitted as a commoner (i.e. a non-scholarship student) at <a href="/wiki/Magdalen_College,_Oxford" title="Magdalen College, Oxford">Magdalen College</a> and entered the newly created School of English Language and Literature. At Oxford, Betjeman made little use of the academic opportunities. His tutor, a young <a href="/wiki/C._S._Lewis" title="C. S. Lewis">C. S. Lewis</a>, regarded him as an "idle prig" and Betjeman in turn considered Lewis unfriendly, demanding, and uninspiring as a teacher.<sup id="cite_ref-OxfordToday_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OxfordToday-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Betjeman particularly disliked the coursework's emphasis on linguistics, and dedicated most of his time to cultivating his social life and his interest in <a href="/wiki/Church_architecture_of_England" title="Church architecture of England">English ecclesiastical architecture</a>, and to private literary pursuits.
</p><p>At Oxford he was a friend of <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Bowra" title="Maurice Bowra">Maurice Bowra</a>, later (1938 to 1970) to be Warden of <a href="/wiki/Wadham_College,_Oxford" title="Wadham College, Oxford">Wadham</a>. Betjeman had a poem published in <i><a href="/wiki/Isis_(magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Isis (magazine)">Isis</a></i>, the university magazine, and served as editor of the <i><a href="/wiki/Cherwell_(newspaper)" title="Cherwell (newspaper)">Cherwell</a></i> student newspaper during 1927. His first book of poems was privately printed with the help of fellow student <a href="/wiki/Edward_James" title="Edward James">Edward James</a>. He famously brought his teddy bear <a href="/wiki/Archibald_Ormsby-Gore" title="Archibald Ormsby-Gore">Archibald Ormsby-Gore</a> up to Magdalen with him, the memory of which inspired his Oxford contemporary <a href="/wiki/Evelyn_Waugh" title="Evelyn Waugh">Evelyn Waugh</a> to include <a href="/wiki/Sebastian_Flyte" class="mw-redirect" title="Sebastian Flyte">Sebastian Flyte</a>'s teddy <a href="/wiki/Aloysius_(Waugh)" title="Aloysius (Waugh)">Aloysius</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Brideshead_Revisited" title="Brideshead Revisited">Brideshead Revisited</a></i>. Much of this period of his life is recorded in his <a href="/wiki/Blank_verse" title="Blank verse">blank verse</a> autobiography <i><a href="/wiki/Summoned_by_Bells" title="Summoned by Bells">Summoned by Bells</a></i> published in 1960 and made into a television film in 1976.<sup id="cite_ref-Peterson2006_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peterson2006-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is a common misapprehension, cultivated by Betjeman himself, that he did not complete his degree because he failed to pass the compulsory holy scripture examination, <a href="/wiki/Oxford_er" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford er">known colloquially as "Divvers"</a>, short for "Divinity". In <a href="/wiki/Hilary_term" title="Hilary term">Hilary term</a> 1928, Betjeman failed Divinity for the second time. He had to leave the university for the <a href="/wiki/Trinity_term" title="Trinity term">Trinity term</a> to prepare for a retake of the exam; he was then allowed to return in October. Betjeman then wrote to the Secretary of the Tutorial Board at <a href="/wiki/Magdalen_College,_Oxford" title="Magdalen College, Oxford">Magdalen</a>, G. C. Lee, asking to be entered for the Pass School, a set of examinations taken on rare occasions by undergraduates who are deemed unlikely to achieve an <a href="/wiki/British_undergraduate_degree_classification" title="British undergraduate degree classification">honours degree</a>. In <i><a href="/wiki/Summoned_by_Bells" title="Summoned by Bells">Summoned by Bells</a></i> Betjeman claims that his tutor, C. S. Lewis, said "You'd have only got a third"&#160;– but he had informed the tutorial board that he thought Betjeman would not achieve an honours degree of any class.<sup id="cite_ref-OxfordToday_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OxfordToday-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Permission to sit the Pass School was granted. Betjeman famously decided to offer a paper in Welsh. <a href="/wiki/Osbert_Lancaster" title="Osbert Lancaster">Osbert Lancaster</a> tells the story that a tutor came by train twice a week (first class) from <a href="/wiki/Aberystwyth" title="Aberystwyth">Aberystwyth</a> to teach Betjeman. However, <a href="/wiki/Jesus_College,_Oxford" title="Jesus College, Oxford">Jesus College</a> had a number of Welsh tutors who more probably would have taught him. Betjeman finally had to leave at the end of the <a href="/wiki/Michaelmas_term" title="Michaelmas term">Michaelmas term</a>, 1928.<sup id="cite_ref-Hillier2003_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hillier2003-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Betjeman did pass his Divinity examination on his third try but was 'sent down' after failing the Pass School. He had achieved a satisfactory result in only one of the three required papers (on <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a> and other English authors).<sup id="cite_ref-OxfordToday_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OxfordToday-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Betjeman's academic failure at Oxford rankled with him for the rest of his life and he was never reconciled with C.S. Lewis, towards whom he nursed a bitter detestation. This situation was perhaps complicated by his enduring love of Oxford, from which he accepted an honorary <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Letters" title="Doctor of Letters">doctorate of letters</a> in 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-OxfordToday_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OxfordToday-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_university">After university</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Betjeman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: After university">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wadebridge,_The_John_Betjeman_Centre_Memorabilia_Room_-_geograph.org.uk_-_211005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Wadebridge%2C_The_John_Betjeman_Centre_Memorabilia_Room_-_geograph.org.uk_-_211005.jpg/220px-Wadebridge%2C_The_John_Betjeman_Centre_Memorabilia_Room_-_geograph.org.uk_-_211005.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Wadebridge%2C_The_John_Betjeman_Centre_Memorabilia_Room_-_geograph.org.uk_-_211005.jpg/330px-Wadebridge%2C_The_John_Betjeman_Centre_Memorabilia_Room_-_geograph.org.uk_-_211005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Wadebridge%2C_The_John_Betjeman_Centre_Memorabilia_Room_-_geograph.org.uk_-_211005.jpg/440px-Wadebridge%2C_The_John_Betjeman_Centre_Memorabilia_Room_-_geograph.org.uk_-_211005.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wadebridge,_The_John_Betjeman_Centre_Memorabilia_Room_-_geograph.org.uk_-_211005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The John Betjeman Centre Memorabilia Room showing the office from his home in <a href="/wiki/Trebetherick" title="Trebetherick">Trebetherick</a></div></div></div>
<p>Betjeman left Oxford without a degree. Whilst there, however, he had made the acquaintance of people who would later influence his work, including <a href="/wiki/Louis_MacNeice" title="Louis MacNeice">Louis MacNeice</a> and <a href="/wiki/W._H._Auden" title="W. H. Auden">W. H. Auden</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Taylor-Martin1983_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-Martin1983-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He worked briefly as a private secretary, school teacher and film critic for the <i><a href="/wiki/Evening_Standard" title="Evening Standard">Evening Standard</a></i>, where he also wrote for their high-society gossip column, the <a href="/wiki/Londoner%27s_Diary" title="Londoner&#39;s Diary">Londoner's Diary</a>. He was employed by the <i><a href="/wiki/Architectural_Review" title="Architectural Review">Architectural Review</a></i> between 1930 and 1935, as a full-time assistant editor, following their publishing of some of his freelance work. <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Mowl" title="Timothy Mowl">Timothy Mowl</a> (2000) says, "His years at the <i><a href="/wiki/Architectural_Review" title="Architectural Review">Architectural Review</a></i> were to be his true university".<sup id="cite_ref-Mowl2011_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mowl2011-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> At this time, while his prose style matured, he joined the <a href="/wiki/MARS_Group" title="MARS Group">MARS Group</a>, an organisation of young modernist architects and architectural critics in Britain.
</p><p>Betjeman's sexuality can best be described as <a href="/wiki/Bisexual" class="mw-redirect" title="Bisexual">bisexual</a>, and his longest and best documented relationships were with women, and a fairer analysis of his sexuality may be that he was "the hatcher of a lifetime of schoolboy crushes – both gay and straight", most of which progressed no further.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, he has been considered "temperamentally gay", and even became a penpal of <a href="/wiki/Lord_Alfred_Douglas" title="Lord Alfred Douglas">Lord Alfred 'Bosie' Douglas</a> of <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Wilde" title="Oscar Wilde">Oscar Wilde</a> fame.<sup id="cite_ref-Gowers2008_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gowers2008-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> On 29 July 1933 Betjeman married the Hon. <a href="/wiki/Penelope_Chetwode" title="Penelope Chetwode">Penelope Chetwode</a>, the daughter of <a href="/wiki/Field_Marshal" class="mw-redirect" title="Field Marshal">Field Marshal</a> <a href="/wiki/Philip_Chetwode,_1st_Baron_Chetwode" title="Philip Chetwode, 1st Baron Chetwode">Lord Chetwode</a>. The couple lived in <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a> and had a son, Paul, in 1937, and a daughter, <a href="/wiki/Candida_Lycett_Green" title="Candida Lycett Green">Candida</a>, in 1942.
</p><p>In 1937, Betjeman was a <a href="/wiki/Churchwarden" title="Churchwarden">churchwarden</a> at <a href="/wiki/Uffington,_Oxfordshire" title="Uffington, Oxfordshire">Uffington</a>, the Berkshire village (in <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a> since boundary changes of 1974) where he lived. That year, he paid for the cleaning of the church's royal arms and later presided over the conversion of the church's <a href="/wiki/Oil_lamp" title="Oil lamp">oil lamps</a> to electricity.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Shell_Guides" title="Shell Guides">Shell Guides</a></i> were developed by Betjeman and <a href="/wiki/Jack_Beddington" title="Jack Beddington">Jack Beddington</a>, a friend who was publicity manager with <a href="/wiki/Shell-Mex_and_BP_Ltd" class="mw-redirect" title="Shell-Mex and BP Ltd">Shell-Mex Ltd</a>, to guide Britain's growing number of motorists around the counties of Britain and their historical sites. They were published by the Architectural Press and financed by <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell" title="Royal Dutch Shell">Shell</a>. By the start of World War II 13 had been published, of which <i><a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a></i> (1934) and <i><a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a></i> (1936) were written by Betjeman. A third, <i><a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a></i>, was written with and designed by his good friend <a href="/wiki/John_Piper_(artist)" title="John Piper (artist)">John Piper</a> in 1951.
</p><p>In 1939, Betjeman was rejected for active service in World War II but found war work with the films division of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Information_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Information (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Information</a>. In 1941 he became British press attaché in neutral <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>, Ireland, working with <a href="/wiki/John_Maffey,_1st_Baron_Rugby" title="John Maffey, 1st Baron Rugby">Sir John Maffey</a>. His secretary in Dublin was the journalist and writer <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shevawn_Lynam&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Shevawn Lynam (page does not exist)">Shevawn Lynam</a>, the author of the novel <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Spirit_and_the_Clay&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Spirit and the Clay (page does not exist)">The Spirit and the Clay</a></i>, about the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Resistance_to_Francoism&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Resistance to Francoism (page does not exist)">resistance to Francoism</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Basque_Country" title="Southern Basque Country">Basque Country</a>. Betjeman may have been involved with the gathering of <a href="/wiki/Military_intelligence" title="Military intelligence">intelligence</a>. He is reported to have been selected for murder by the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army_(1922%E2%80%931969)" title="Irish Republican Army (1922–1969)">IRA</a>. The order was rescinded after a meeting with an unnamed Old IRA man who was impressed by his works.
</p><p>Betjeman wrote a number of poems based on his experiences in <a href="/wiki/The_Emergency_(Ireland)" title="The Emergency (Ireland)">"Emergency" World War II Ireland</a> including "The Irish Unionist's Farewell to Greta Hellstrom in 1922" (written during the war) which contained the refrain "Dungarvan in the rain". The object of his affections, "Greta", has remained a mystery until recently revealed to have been a member of a well-known <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Ascendancy" title="Protestant Ascendancy">Anglo-Irish</a> family of Western <a href="/wiki/County_Waterford" title="County Waterford">county Waterford</a>. His official brief included establishing friendly contacts with leading figures in the Dublin literary scene: he befriended <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Kavanagh" title="Patrick Kavanagh">Patrick Kavanagh</a>, then at the very start of his career. Kavanagh celebrated the birth of Betjeman's daughter with a poem "Candida"; another well-known poem contains the line <i>Let John Betjeman call for me in a car</i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_the_Second_World_War">After the Second World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Betjeman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: After the Second World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Betjeman%27s_house_on_Cloth_Fair.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/John_Betjeman%27s_house_on_Cloth_Fair.jpg/220px-John_Betjeman%27s_house_on_Cloth_Fair.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/John_Betjeman%27s_house_on_Cloth_Fair.jpg/330px-John_Betjeman%27s_house_on_Cloth_Fair.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/John_Betjeman%27s_house_on_Cloth_Fair.jpg/440px-John_Betjeman%27s_house_on_Cloth_Fair.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Betjeman%27s_house_on_Cloth_Fair.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Betjeman's house on <a href="/wiki/Cloth_Fair" title="Cloth Fair">Cloth Fair</a> in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a></div></div></div>
<p>Betjeman's wife Penelope became a Roman Catholic in 1948. The couple drifted apart and in 1951 he met <a href="/wiki/Lady_Elizabeth_Cavendish" title="Lady Elizabeth Cavendish">Lady Elizabeth Cavendish</a>, with whom he developed an immediate and lifelong friendship.
</p><p>By 1948 Betjeman had published more than a dozen books. Five of these were verse collections, including one in the USA. Sales of his <i>Collected Poems</i> in 1958 reached 100,000.<sup id="cite_ref-FandF_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FandF-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The popularity of the book prompted <a href="/wiki/Ken_Russell" title="Ken Russell">Ken Russell</a> to make a film about him, <i>John Betjeman: A Poet in London</i> (1959). Filmed in 35mm and running 11 minutes and 35 seconds, it was first shown on <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Monitor_(UK_TV_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Monitor (UK TV series)">Monitor</a></i> programme.<sup id="cite_ref-BFI2014_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BFI2014-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In 1953 his address was the Old Rectory, <a href="/wiki/Farnborough,_Berkshire" title="Farnborough, Berkshire">Farnborough</a>, Wantage, Berkshire.<sup id="cite_ref-register_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-register-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He continued writing guidebooks and works on architecture during the 1960s and 1970s and started broadcasting. He was a founder member of the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="Victorian Society">Victorian Society</a> (1958). Betjeman was closely associated with the culture and spirit of <a href="/wiki/Metro-land" title="Metro-land">Metro-land</a>, as outer reaches of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Railway" title="Metropolitan Railway">Metropolitan Railway</a> were known before the war.
</p><p>In 1973 he made a widely acclaimed television documentary for the BBC called <i><a href="/wiki/Metro-Land_(TV_film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Metro-Land (TV film)">Metro-Land</a></i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Mirzoeff" title="Edward Mirzoeff">Edward Mirzoeff</a>. On the centenary of Betjeman's birth in 2006, his daughter led two celebratory railway trips: from London to <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, and through <a href="/wiki/Metro-land" title="Metro-land">Metro-land</a>, to <a href="/wiki/Quainton_Road_railway_station" title="Quainton Road railway station">Quainton Road</a>. In 1974, Betjeman and Mirzoeff followed up <i>Metro-Land</i> with <i><a href="/wiki/A_Passion_for_Churches" title="A Passion for Churches">A Passion for Churches</a></i>, a celebration of Betjeman's beloved Church of England, filmed entirely in the Diocese of Norwich. In 1975, he proposed that the Fine Rooms of <a href="/wiki/Somerset_House" title="Somerset House">Somerset House</a> should house the Turner Bequest, so helping to scupper the plan of the <a href="/wiki/Minister_for_the_Arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Minister for the Arts">Minister for the Arts</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Theatre_Museum" title="Theatre Museum">Theatre Museum</a> to be housed there. In 1977 the BBC broadcast "The Queen's Realm: A Prospect of England", an aerial anthology of English landscape, music and poetry, selected by Betjeman and produced by Edward Mirzoeff, in celebration of the Queen's Silver Jubilee.
</p><p>Betjeman was fond of the ghost stories of <a href="/wiki/M.R._James" class="mw-redirect" title="M.R. James">M.R. James</a> and supplied an introduction to <a href="/wiki/Peter_Haining_(author)" title="Peter Haining (author)">Peter Haining</a>'s book <i>M.R. James&#160;– Book of the Supernatural</i>. He was susceptible to the supernatural. <a href="/wiki/Diana_Mitford" title="Diana Mitford">Diana Mitford</a> tells the story of Betjeman staying at her country home, <a href="/wiki/Biddesden_House" title="Biddesden House">Biddesden House</a>, in the 1920s. She says, "he had a terrifying dream, that he was handed a card with wide black edges, and on it his name was engraved, and a date. He knew this was the date of his death".<sup id="cite_ref-Mitford2008_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mitford2008-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Betjeman_memorial.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Betjeman_memorial.JPG/220px-Betjeman_memorial.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Betjeman_memorial.JPG/330px-Betjeman_memorial.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Betjeman_memorial.JPG/440px-Betjeman_memorial.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2112" data-file-height="2816" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Betjeman_memorial.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>John Betjeman's grave</div></div></div>
<p>For the last decade of his life Betjeman suffered increasingly from <a href="/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease" title="Parkinson&#39;s disease">Parkinson's disease</a>. He died at his home in <a href="/wiki/Trebetherick" title="Trebetherick">Trebetherick</a>, Cornwall, on 19 May 1984, aged 77, and is buried nearby at <a href="/wiki/St_Enodoc%27s_Church,_Trebetherick" title="St Enodoc&#39;s Church, Trebetherick">St Enodoc's Church</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ODNB_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ODNB-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Poetry">Poetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Betjeman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Poetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Betjeman's poems are often humorous, and in broadcasting he exploited his bumbling and fogeyish image. His wryly comic verse is accessible and has attracted a great following for its satirical and observant grace. Auden said in his introduction to <i>Slick But Not Streamlined</i>, "so at home with the provincial gaslit towns, the seaside lodgings, the bicycle, the <a href="/wiki/Pump_organ" title="Pump organ">harmonium</a>." His poetry is similarly redolent of time and place, continually seeking out intimations of the eternal in the manifestly ordinary. There are constant evocations of the physical chaff and clutter that accumulates in everyday life, the miscellanea of an England now gone but not beyond the reach of living memory.
</p><p>He talks of <a href="/wiki/Ovaltine" title="Ovaltine">Ovaltine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sturmey-Archer" title="Sturmey-Archer">Sturmey-Archer</a> bicycle gears. "Oh! Fuller's angel cake, <a href="/wiki/Robertson%27s" title="Robertson&#39;s">Robertson's marmalade</a>," he writes, "<a href="/wiki/Liberty_(department_store)" title="Liberty (department store)">Liberty</a> lampshades, come shine on us all."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBetjeman194050Myfanwy_at_Oxford_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBetjeman194050Myfanwy_at_Oxford-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In a 1962 radio interview he told teenage questioners that he could not write about 'abstract things', preferring places, and faces.<sup id="cite_ref-bufvc.ac.uk_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bufvc.ac.uk-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Philip_Larkin" title="Philip Larkin">Philip Larkin</a> wrote of his work, "how much more interesting &amp; worth writing about Betjeman's subjects are than most other modern poets, I mean, whether so-and-so achieves some metaphysical inner unity is not really so interesting to us as the overbuilding of rural <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">Middlesex</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Larkin2010_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Larkin2010-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Betjeman was an <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Anglican</a> and his religious beliefs come through in some of his poems. In a letter written on Christmas Day 1947, he said: “Also my view of the world is that man is born to fulfil the purposes of his Creator i.e. to Praise his Creator, to stand in awe of Him and to dread Him. In this way I differ from most modern poets, who are agnostics and have an idea that Man is the centre of the Universe or is a helpless bubble blown about by uncontrolled forces."<sup id="cite_ref-patr_Angl_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-patr_Angl-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> He combined piety with a nagging uncertainty about the truth of Christianity. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardy" title="Thomas Hardy">Thomas Hardy</a>, who disbelieved in the truth of the Christmas story while hoping it might be so, Betjeman affirms his belief even while fearing it might be false.<sup id="cite_ref-Gardner2006_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gardner2006-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In the poem "Christmas", one of his most openly religious pieces, the last three stanzas that proclaim the wonder of Christ's birth do so in the form of a question "And is it true...?" His views on Christianity were expressed in his poem "The Conversion of St. Paul", a response to a radio broadcast by humanist <a href="/wiki/Margaret_K._Knight" title="Margaret K. Knight">Margaret Knight</a>:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><div class="poem">
<p>But most of us turn slow to see<br />
The figure hanging on a tree<br />
And stumble on and blindly grope<br />
Upheld by intermittent hope,<br />
God grant before we die we all<br />
May see the light as did St. Paul.
</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<p>Betjeman became <a href="/wiki/Poet_Laureate_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom">Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom</a> in 1972, the first <a href="/wiki/Knight_Bachelor" title="Knight Bachelor">Knight Bachelor</a> to be appointed (the only other, Sir <a href="/wiki/William_Davenant" title="William Davenant">William Davenant</a>, was knighted after his appointment). This role, combined with his popularity as a television performer, ensured that his poetry reached an audience enormous by the standards of the time. Similarly to <a href="/wiki/Alfred,_Lord_Tennyson" title="Alfred, Lord Tennyson">Tennyson</a>, he appealed to a wide public and managed to voice the thoughts and aspirations of many ordinary people while retaining the respect of many of his fellow poets. This is partly because of the apparently simple traditional metrical structures and rhymes he uses.<sup id="cite_ref-poetryarchive.org_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-poetryarchive.org-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In the early 1970s, he began a recording career of four albums on <a href="/wiki/Charisma_Records" title="Charisma Records">Charisma Records</a> which included <i>Betjeman's Banana Blush</i> (1974) and <i>Late Flowering Love</i> (1974), where his poetry reading is set to music with overdubbing by leading musicians of the time.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> His recording catalogue extends to nine albums, four singles and two compilations.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Betjeman_and_Slough">Betjeman and Slough</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Betjeman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Betjeman and Slough">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Slough_(poem)" title="Slough (poem)">Slough (poem)</a></div>
<p>Prompted by the rapid development of the <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a> town before <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, Betjeman wrote the ten-stanza poem <i><a href="/wiki/Slough_(poem)" title="Slough (poem)">Slough</a></i> to express his dismay at the industrialisation of Britain. He later came to regret having written it. The poem was first included in his 1937 collection <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Continual_Dew&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Continual Dew (page does not exist)">Continual Dew</a></i>.
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><div class="poem">
<p>Come, friendly bombs, and fall on Slough!<br />
It isn't fit for humans now,<br />
...
</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Betjeman_and_architecture">Betjeman and architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Betjeman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Betjeman and architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Architecture_of_London" title="Architecture of London">Architecture of London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Architecture_of_Leeds" title="Architecture of Leeds">Architecture of Leeds</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Buildings_and_architecture_of_Bath" title="Buildings and architecture of Bath">Buildings and architecture of Bath</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Betjeman,_London,_England,_GB,_IMG_4991_edit.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/John_Betjeman%2C_London%2C_England%2C_GB%2C_IMG_4991_edit.jpg/170px-John_Betjeman%2C_London%2C_England%2C_GB%2C_IMG_4991_edit.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="262" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/John_Betjeman%2C_London%2C_England%2C_GB%2C_IMG_4991_edit.jpg/255px-John_Betjeman%2C_London%2C_England%2C_GB%2C_IMG_4991_edit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/John_Betjeman%2C_London%2C_England%2C_GB%2C_IMG_4991_edit.jpg/340px-John_Betjeman%2C_London%2C_England%2C_GB%2C_IMG_4991_edit.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3614" data-file-height="5573" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Betjeman,_London,_England,_GB,_IMG_4991_edit.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Betjeman Statue at <a href="/wiki/St_Pancras_railway_station" title="St Pancras railway station">St Pancras</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Betjeman_Reads_William_Horton%27s_Petition_to_Save_Lewisham_Town_Hall,_1961.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/John_Betjeman_Reads_William_Horton%27s_Petition_to_Save_Lewisham_Town_Hall%2C_1961.jpg/170px-John_Betjeman_Reads_William_Horton%27s_Petition_to_Save_Lewisham_Town_Hall%2C_1961.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="195" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/John_Betjeman_Reads_William_Horton%27s_Petition_to_Save_Lewisham_Town_Hall%2C_1961.jpg/255px-John_Betjeman_Reads_William_Horton%27s_Petition_to_Save_Lewisham_Town_Hall%2C_1961.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/John_Betjeman_Reads_William_Horton%27s_Petition_to_Save_Lewisham_Town_Hall%2C_1961.jpg/340px-John_Betjeman_Reads_William_Horton%27s_Petition_to_Save_Lewisham_Town_Hall%2C_1961.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1742" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_Betjeman_Reads_William_Horton%27s_Petition_to_Save_Lewisham_Town_Hall,_1961.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>John Betjeman reads William Horton's Petition to Save <a href="/wiki/Broadway_Theatre,_Catford" title="Broadway Theatre, Catford">Lewisham Town Hall</a>, 1961</div></div></div>
<p>Betjeman had a fondness for <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian architecture</a> at a time when it was unfashionable,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> and was a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="Victorian Society">Victorian Society</a>. He wrote on this subject in <i>First and Last Loves</i> (1952) and more extensively in <i>London's Historic Railway Stations</i> in 1972, defending the beauty of 12 stations. He led the campaign to save <a href="/wiki/Holy_Trinity,_Sloane_Street" title="Holy Trinity, Sloane Street">Holy Trinity, Sloane Street</a> in London when it was threatened with demolition in the early 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-Pearce1989_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearce1989-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He fought a spirited but unsuccessful campaign to save the Propylaeum, known commonly as the <a href="/wiki/Euston_Arch" title="Euston Arch">Euston Arch</a>, London. He is considered instrumental in helping to save <a href="/wiki/St_Pancras_railway_station" title="St Pancras railway station">St Pancras railway station</a>, London, and was commemorated when it became an international terminus for <a href="/wiki/Eurostar" title="Eurostar">Eurostar</a> in November 2007. He called the plan to demolish St Pancras a "criminal folly".<sup id="cite_ref-Shukor2007_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shukor2007-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> About it he wrote, "What [the Londoner] sees in his mind's eye is that cluster of towers and pinnacles seen from Pentonville Hill and outlined against a foggy sunset, and the great arc of Barlow's train shed gaping to devour incoming engines, and the sudden burst of exuberant <a href="/wiki/Gothic_Revival_architecture" title="Gothic Revival architecture">Gothic</a> of the hotel seen from gloomy Judd Street."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBetjeman197211_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBetjeman197211-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On the reopening of St Pancras station in 2007, a <a href="/wiki/Statue_of_John_Betjeman" title="Statue of John Betjeman">statue of Betjeman</a> was commissioned from curators Futurecity. A proposal by artist <a href="/wiki/Martin_Jennings" title="Martin Jennings">Martin Jennings</a> was selected from a shortlist. The finished work was erected in the station at platform level, including a series of slate roundels depicting selections of Betjeman's writings.<sup id="cite_ref-Shukor2007_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shukor2007-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Betjeman was given the remaining two-year lease on <a href="/wiki/Victorian_Gothic" class="mw-redirect" title="Victorian Gothic">Victorian Gothic</a> architect <a href="/wiki/William_Burges" title="William Burges">William Burges</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Tower_House" title="The Tower House">Tower House</a> in Holland Park upon leaseholder Mrs E.R.B. Graham's death in 1962.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilson2011_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson2011-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Betjeman felt he could not afford the financial implications of taking over the house permanently, with his potential liability for £10,000 of renovations upon the expiration of the lease.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilson2011_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson2011-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> After damage from vandals, restoration began in 1966. Betjeman's lease included furniture from the house by Burges, and Betjeman gave three pieces, the <a href="/wiki/Zodiac_settle" title="Zodiac settle">Zodiac settle</a>, the Narcissus washstand, and the Philosophy cabinet, to <a href="/wiki/Evelyn_Waugh" title="Evelyn Waugh">Evelyn Waugh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ArtFund_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ArtFund-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Betjeman responded to architecture as the visible manifestation of society's spiritual life as well as its political and economic structure. He attacked speculators and bureaucrats for what he saw as their rapacity and lack of imagination. In the preface of his collection of architectural essays <i>First and Last Loves</i> he says, "We accept the collapse of the fabrics of our old churches, the thieving of lead and objects from them, the commandeering and butchery of our scenery by the services, the despoiling of landscaped parks and the abandonment to a fate worse than the workhouse of our country houses, because we are convinced we must save money."
</p><p>In a BBC film made in 1968 but not broadcast at that time, Betjeman described the sound of <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> to be of "Victorian buildings crashing to the ground". He went on to lambast <a href="/wiki/John_Poulson" title="John Poulson">John Poulson</a>'s British Railways House (now <a href="/wiki/City_House" title="City House">City House</a>), saying how it blocked all the light out to <a href="/wiki/Leeds_City_Square" class="mw-redirect" title="Leeds City Square">City Square</a> and was only a testament to money with no architectural merit. He also praised the architecture of <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Town_Hall" title="Leeds Town Hall">Leeds Town Hall</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> In 1969 Betjeman contributed the foreword to Derek Linstrum's <i>Historic Architecture of Leeds</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Linstrum1969_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Linstrum1969-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Betjeman was for over 20 years a trustee of the <a href="/wiki/Bath_Preservation_Trust" title="Bath Preservation Trust">Bath Preservation Trust</a> and was Vice-President from 1965 to 1971, at a time when <a href="/wiki/Bath,_Somerset" title="Bath, Somerset">Bath</a>—a city rich in <a href="/wiki/Georgian_architecture" title="Georgian architecture">Georgian architecture</a>—was coming under increasing pressure from modern developers, and a major road was proposed to cut across it.<sup id="cite_ref-Peterson2006_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Peterson2006-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>440</span></sup> He also created a short television documentary called <i>Architecture of Bath</i>, in which he voiced his concerns about the way the city's architectural heritage was being mistreated. From 1946 to 1948 he had served as Secretary to the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Preservation_Trust" title="Oxford Preservation Trust">Oxford Preservation Trust</a>.
</p><p>Betjeman was also instrumental in saving the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Cornwall_Hotel" title="Duke of Cornwall Hotel">Duke of Cornwall Hotel</a> in <a href="/wiki/Plymouth" title="Plymouth">Plymouth</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Betjeman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prizes">Prizes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Betjeman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Prizes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Society_for_the_Protection_of_Ancient_Buildings" title="Society for the Protection of Ancient Buildings">Society for the Protection of Ancient Buildings</a> annually presents a John Betjeman award to recognise the repair and conservation of places of worship in England and Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>&lt;/ref&gt;
</p><p>The John Betjeman Poetry Competition for Young People (2006–) is open to 10- to 13-year-olds living anywhere in the British Isles (including the Republic of Ireland), with a first prize of £1,000. In addition to prizes for individual finalists, state schools who enter pupils may win one of six one-day poetry workshops.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<li>In 2006, a blue plaque was installed on Betjeman's childhood home, 31 West Hill, <a href="/wiki/Highgate" title="Highgate">Highgate</a>, London N6.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2006 a blue plaque was erected at Garrard's Farm, <a href="/wiki/Uffington,_Oxfordshire" title="Uffington, Oxfordshire">Uffington, Oxfordshire</a> which had been his first married home.</li>
<li>A blue plaque has been erected at 43 Cloth Fair, opposite <a href="/wiki/St_Bartholomew-the-Great" title="St Bartholomew-the-Great">St Bartholomew-the-Great</a> church and near <a href="/wiki/St_Bartholomew%27s_Hospital" title="St Bartholomew&#39;s Hospital">St Bartholomew's Hospital</a>, where he lived and worked. He let the ground floor to Leonard Beddall-Smith, the conservation architect and Georgian specialist, who was the founder architect of the <a href="/wiki/Landmark_Trust" title="Landmark Trust">Landmark Trust</a>, who now own the building to celebrate Betjeman's time there.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Statue_of_John_Betjeman" title="Statue of John Betjeman">statue of John Betjeman</a> at <a href="/wiki/St_Pancras_railway_station" title="St Pancras railway station">St Pancras</a> station in London by sculptor <a href="/wiki/Martin_Jennings" title="Martin Jennings">Martin Jennings</a> was unveiled in 2007.</li>
<li>In 2012 Betjeman featured on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a> as author of the week on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Write_Stuff" title="The Write Stuff">The Write Stuff</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2014, a new street in <a href="/wiki/Cleobury_Mortimer" title="Cleobury Mortimer">Cleobury Mortimer</a>, Shropshire, was named 'Betjeman Way', in honour of the poet, and in recognition of the fact that the restoration of Cleobury Mortimer's church steeple won SPAB's John Betjeman award in 1994.</li>
<li>On 1 September 2014 Betjeman was the subject of the hour-long <a href="/wiki/BBC_Four" title="BBC Four">BBC Four</a> documentary <i>Return to Betjemanland</i>, presented by his biographer <a href="/wiki/A._N._Wilson" title="A. N. Wilson">A. N. Wilson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> At the start of the broadcast, there was a spoken tribute to Betjeman's daughter <a href="/wiki/Candida_Lycett_Green" title="Candida Lycett Green">Candida Lycett Green</a>, who had died just twelve days earlier on 19 August, aged 71.<sup id="cite_ref-Dowlen2014_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dowlen2014-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
