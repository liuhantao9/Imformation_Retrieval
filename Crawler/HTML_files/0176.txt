<p><br />
The <i><b>Civil Code of Quebec</b></i> (<b>CCQ</b>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Code civil du Québec</i>) is the <a href="/wiki/Civil_code" title="Civil code">civil code</a> in force in the province of <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, which came into effect on January 1, 1994. It replaced the <i><a href="/wiki/Civil_Code_of_Lower_Canada" title="Civil Code of Lower Canada">Civil Code of Lower Canada</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Code civil du Bas-Canada</i>) enacted by the <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_of_the_Province_of_Canada" title="Legislative Assembly of the Province of Canada">Legislative Assembly of the Province of Canada</a> in 1865, which had been in force since August 1, 1866.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scope">Scope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Code_of_Quebec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Scope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Code's scope is summarized in its preliminary provision:
</p>
<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tbody><tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“
</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">The <i>Civil Code of Québec</i>, in harmony with the <i><a href="/wiki/Quebec_Charter_of_Human_Rights_and_Freedoms" title="Quebec Charter of Human Rights and Freedoms">Charter of human rights and freedoms</a></i> (chapter C-12) and the general principles of law, governs persons, relations between persons, and property.
<p>The <i>Civil Code</i> comprises a body of rules which, in all matters within the letter, spirit or object of its provisions, lays down the <i><a href="/wiki/Jus_commune" title="Jus commune">jus commune</a></i>, expressly or by implication. In these matters, the <i>Code</i> is the foundation of all other laws, although other laws may complement the Code or make exceptions to it.
</p>
</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”
</td></tr>

</tbody></table>
<p>The Civil Code is in essence a body of rules and regulations that, in all matters treated by or in the spirit or vein of its provisions, sets forth the <i>jus commune</i>, or the law that applies to all of Quebec, either in express or implied terms. For the matters handled by the Code, it acts as the foundation of all other adjacent laws, although other laws may supplement the Code or make exceptions to it.<i></i>
</p><p>As the cornerstone of Quebec's legal system, the Civil Code is frequently amended in order to keep in step with the demands of modern society.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Layout_and_significant_changes">Layout and significant changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Code_of_Quebec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Layout and significant changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Civil Code of Quebec</i> comprises over 3,000 articles and is structured into major divisions and subdivisions called books, titles, chapters and subsections.  The Code is made up of ten books:
</p>
<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 22em; -webkit-column-width: 22em; column-width: 22em;">
<ol><li>Persons</li>
<li>The Family</li>
<li>Successions</li>
<li>Property</li>
<li>Obligations</li>
<li>Prior Claims and Hypothecs</li>
<li>Evidence</li>
<li>Prescription</li>
<li>Publication of Rights</li>
<li>Private International Law</li></ol>
</div>
<p>The <i>Code</i>  restatement of the civil law in Quebec as of the date of its adoption, including judicial interpretation of codal provisions, that included several significant changes from the former Code:
</p>
<dl><dd><ul><li>broad <a href="/wiki/Privacy" title="Privacy">privacy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Personality_rights" title="Personality rights">personality rights</a> protection,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>the adoption of rules on <a href="/wiki/Patrimony_of_affectation" title="Patrimony of affectation">patrimonies by appropriation</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> (analogous to, but conceptually different from, the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_trusts" class="mw-redirect" title="Law of trusts">law of trusts</a> in <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> jurisdictions),</li>
<li>the introduction of a requirement for parties to conduct themselves in <a href="/wiki/Good_faith" title="Good faith">good faith</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>the ability to create <a href="/wiki/Unincorporated_association" title="Unincorporated association">unincorporated associations</a> with the ability to sue and be sued<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>the ability to create <a href="/wiki/Hypothec" title="Hypothec">hypothecs</a> on <a href="/wiki/Movable_property" class="mw-redirect" title="Movable property">movable property</a><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>the introduction of a central register for personal and movable real rights<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>the codification of the rules relating to <a href="/wiki/Private_international_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Private international law">private international law</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Code_of_Quebec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adoption_of_the_Civil_Code_of_Lower_Canada">Adoption of the <i>Civil Code of Lower Canada</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Code_of_Quebec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Adoption of the Civil Code of Lower Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Civil_Code_of_Lower_Canada" title="Civil Code of Lower Canada">Civil Code of Lower Canada</a></div>
<p>The substantive law of the 1866 <i>Civil Code of Lower Canada</i> was derived primarily from the judicial interpretations of the law that had been in force to that date in Lower Canada. The work of the Commission on <a href="/wiki/Codification_(law)" title="Codification (law)">codification</a> was also inspired by some of the modernizations found in the 1804 <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_code" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleonic code">Napoleonic code</a>.  At the time of <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Confederation" title="Canadian Confederation">Canadian Confederation</a>, the Civil Code of Lower Canada replaced most of the laws inherited from the <a href="/wiki/Custom_of_Paris_in_New_France" title="Custom of Paris in New France">Custom of Paris</a> and incorporated some English law as it had been applied in Lower Canada such as the English law of <a href="/wiki/Investment_trust" title="Investment trust">trusts</a>.  The former Civil Code was also inspired by the <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Civil_Code" class="mw-redirect" title="Louisiana Civil Code">Louisiana Civil Code</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Field_Code" class="mw-redirect" title="Field Code">Field Code</a> movement in <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, and the Civil Code of the <a href="/wiki/Canton_of_Vaud" class="mw-redirect" title="Canton of Vaud">Canton of Vaud</a> (1819).
</p>
<h3><span id="Revision_process_.281955.E2.80.931991.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Revision_process_(1955–1991)">Revision process (1955–1991)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Code_of_Quebec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Revision process (1955–1991)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1955, the Government of Quebec embarked on a reform of the Civil Code.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The Civil Code Revision Office was established, headed by <a href="/wiki/Thibaudeau_Rinfret" title="Thibaudeau Rinfret">Thibaudeau Rinfret</a>, a former <a href="/wiki/Chief_Justice_of_Canada" title="Chief Justice of Canada">Chief Justice of Canada</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-people_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-people-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In 1960, the role of the Office was expanded to include the appointment of four codifiers to work on a definitive draft for the new Code.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1961, Rinfret stepped down from the Office, and was replaced by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Andr%C3%A9_Nadeau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="André Nadeau (page does not exist)">André Nadeau</a> who served until his appointment to the <a href="/wiki/Superior_Court_of_Quebec" class="mw-redirect" title="Superior Court of Quebec">Superior Court of Quebec</a> in 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-people_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-people-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Paul-Andr%C3%A9_Cr%C3%A9peau" title="Paul-André Crépeau">Paul-André Crépeau</a> was subsequently appointed to head the Office, where he served until 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-people_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-people-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The reform process that led to the replacement of the <i>Civil Code of Lower Canada</i> by the <i>Civil Code of Quebec</i> was one of the largest legislative recodification undertakings in any <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a> <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction" title="Jurisdiction">jurisdiction</a>. The Office produced reports, held consultations, and presented a Draft Civil Code with commentaries to the Quebec National Assembly on 15 August 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  After further consultations during the 1980s, portions of the Book on the Law of the Family were adopted.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The consultation process continued through to the early 1990s.
</p>
<h3><span id="Implementation_.281991.E2.80.931994.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementation_(1991–1994)">Implementation (1991–1994)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Code_of_Quebec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Implementation (1991–1994)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bill to enact the new Code was introduced into the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_Quebec" title="National Assembly of Quebec">National Assembly of Quebec</a> on 18 December 1990 by <a href="/wiki/Gil_R%C3%A9millard" title="Gil Rémillard">Gil Rémillard</a>, who was then Quebec's <a href="/wiki/List_of_Ministers_of_Justice_of_Quebec" title="List of Ministers of Justice of Quebec">Minister of Justice</a>. It received <a href="/wiki/Royal_assent" title="Royal assent">royal assent</a> on 8 December 1991. It did not come into force until 1 January 1994, as the necessary legislation to provide transitional rules determining what matters would be subject to the new Code was not passed until 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Harmonization_with_federal_law">Harmonization with federal law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Civil_Code_of_Quebec&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Harmonization with federal law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Canada" title="Government of Canada">Government of Canada</a> has been undertaking a review of all federal laws that deal with private law to ensure that they take into consideration the terminology, concepts and institutions of Quebec civil law.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In that regard, the following Acts have been passed:
</p>
<dl><dd><ul><li><i>Income Tax Amendments Act, 2000</i><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Federal Law—Civil Law Harmonization Act, No. 1</i><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Federal Law—Civil Law Harmonization Act, No. 2</i><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Federal Law—Civil Law Harmonization Act, No. 3</i><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></dd></dl>
<p>As part of the first Harmonization Act, the <i>Federal Law and Civil Law of the Province of Quebec Act</i> was passed, which came into effect on 1 June 2001,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> which:
</p>
<dl><dd><ul><li>repealed the provisions of the <i>CCLC</i> relating to areas under federal jurisdiction (insofar as they had not been already displaced by other federal Acts)</li>
<li>standardized the rules relating to <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a> that are to apply in Quebec as though they formed part of the <i>Civil Code</i></li></ul></dd></dl>
<p>It is estimated that, as of 2011, the federal harmonization project was 46% complete.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
