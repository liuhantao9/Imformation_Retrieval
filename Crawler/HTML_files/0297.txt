	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Lithuanian name</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p>A Lithuanian <a href="/wiki/Personal_name" title="Personal name">personal name</a>, as in most <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> cultures, consists of two main elements: the <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> (<i lang="lt" title="Lithuanian language text">vardas</i>) followed by the <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a> (<i lang="lt" title="Lithuanian language text">pavardė</i>). The usage of personal names in <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> is generally governed (in addition to personal taste and family custom) by three major factors: <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canon_law" title="Canon law">canon law</a>, and tradition. Lithuanian names always follow the rules of the <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_language" title="Lithuanian language">Lithuanian language</a>. Lithuanian male names have preserved the <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">Indo-European</a> masculine endings (<i lang="lt" title="Lithuanian language text">-as</i>; <i lang="lt" title="Lithuanian language text">-is</i>), although the rules are not as rigid as for <a href="/wiki/Latvian_name" title="Latvian name">Latvian names</a>, which preserve gendered endings even for foreign names.
</p>

<h2><span id="Vardas_.28given_name.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Vardas_(given_name)"><i>Vardas</i> (given name)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithuanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Vardas (given name)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A child in Lithuania is usually given one or two given names. Nowadays the second given name is rarely used in everyday situations, the use of a <a href="/wiki/Middle_name" title="Middle name">middle name</a> being considered pretentious. As well as modern names, parents can choose a name or names for their child from a long list of traditional names; these include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_language" title="Lithuanian language">Lithuanian</a> names of pre-Christian origin.</li></ul>
<p>These are the most ancient layer of Lithuanian personal names; a majority of them are dual-<a href="/wiki/Word_stem" title="Word stem">stemmed</a> personal names, of <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">Indo-European</a> origin. These ancient Lithuanian names are constructed from two interconnected <a href="/wiki/Word_stem" title="Word stem">stems</a>, the combination of which has been used to denote certain beneficial personal qualities, for example <i>Jo-gaila</i> means "a strong rider". Although virtually extinct following the <a href="/wiki/Christianization_of_Lithuania" title="Christianization of Lithuania">Christianization of Lithuania</a>, they continued to exist as <a href="/wiki/Surnames" class="mw-redirect" title="Surnames">surnames</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Go%C5%A1tautas" class="mw-redirect" title="Goštautas">Goštautas</a>, <a href="/wiki/K%C4%99sgaila" class="mw-redirect" title="Kęsgaila">Kęsgaila</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radvila" class="mw-redirect" title="Radvila">Radvila</a> or in their Slavicised versions, as well as in <a href="/wiki/Toponym" class="mw-redirect" title="Toponym">toponyms</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The existing surnames and written sources have allowed linguists such as <a href="/wiki/Kazimieras_B%C5%ABga" title="Kazimieras Būga">Kazimieras Būga</a> to reconstruct these names. Between the wars, these names returned to popular use after a long period of neglect. Children are often named in honor of the most revered historical Lithuanian rulers; these are some of the most popular names. They include <a href="/wiki/Vytautas" title="Vytautas">Vytautas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gediminas" title="Gediminas">Gediminas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Algirdas" title="Algirdas">Algirdas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_K%C4%99stutaitis" title="Sigismund Kęstutaitis">Žygimantas</a>. In line with the double-stemmed names, shorter variants containing only one stem were also used, such as <a href="/wiki/Vytenis" title="Vytenis">Vytenis</a> and <a href="/wiki/K%C4%99stutis" title="Kęstutis">Kęstutis</a>. Since there are few pre-Christian female names attested in written sources, they are often reconstructed from male variants, in addition to the historical <a href="/wiki/Birut%C4%97" title="Birutė">Birutė</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aldona_of_Lithuania" title="Aldona of Lithuania">Aldona</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rimgail%C4%97" title="Rimgailė">Rimgailė</a> etc.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Christian_name" title="Christian name">Christian names</a>, i.e. <a href="/wiki/List_of_Biblical_names" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Biblical names">Biblical names</a> or <a href="/wiki/Saint%27s_name" title="Saint&#39;s name">saint's names</a>.</li></ul>
<p>The use of Christian names in the Lithuanian language long predates the adoption of Christianity by Lithuanians. The linguistic data attest that first Biblical names started to be used in <a href="/wiki/Auk%C5%A1taitija" title="Aukštaitija">Aukštaitija</a> as early as the 11th century. The earliest stratum of such names originates from <a href="/wiki/Old_Church_Slavonic" title="Old Church Slavonic">Old Church Slavonic</a>; they were borrowed by <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodoxy" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Orthodoxy">Eastern Orthodoxy</a> in their <a href="/wiki/Byzantine" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine">Byzantine</a> versions. Examples of such names are <i>Antanas</i> (<a href="/wiki/Anthony_the_Great" title="Anthony the Great">St. Anthony</a>), <i>Povilas</i> or <i>Paulius</i> (<a href="/wiki/Paul_the_Apostle" title="Paul the Apostle">St. Paul</a>), <i>Andrius</i> (<a href="/wiki/St._Andrew" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Andrew">St. Andrew</a>) and <i>Jurgis</i> (<a href="/wiki/St._George" class="mw-redirect" title="St. George">St. George</a>). The later influx of Christian names came after the adoption of Christianity in 1387. They are mostly borrowed in their <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a> versions: <i>Jonas</i> (<a href="/wiki/John_the_Apostle" title="John the Apostle">St. John</a>), <i>Vladislovas</i>/<i>Vladas</i> (<a href="/wiki/St._Ladislaus" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Ladislaus">St. Ladislaus</a>), <i>Kazimieras</i>/<i>Kazys</i> (<a href="/wiki/St._Casimir" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Casimir">St. Casimir</a>), etc.
</p>
<ul><li>Lithuanian <a href="/wiki/Common_noun" class="mw-redirect" title="Common noun">common nouns</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hydronym" title="Hydronym">hydronyms</a> used as names.</li></ul>
<p>There are popular names constructed from the words for celestial bodies (<i>Saulė</i> for the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>, <i>Aušrinė</i> for <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">Venus</a>), events of nature (<i>Audra</i> for storm, <i>Aušra</i> for <a href="/wiki/Dawn" title="Dawn">dawn</a>, <i>Rasa</i> for <a href="/wiki/Dew" title="Dew">dew</a>, <i>Vėjas</i> for wind, <i>Aidas</i> for <a href="/wiki/Echo_(phenomenon)" class="mw-redirect" title="Echo (phenomenon)">echo</a>), plants (<i><a href="/wiki/Linas" title="Linas">Linas</a></i>/<i>Lina</i> for <a href="/wiki/Flax" title="Flax">flax</a>, <i>Eglė</i> for <a href="/wiki/Spruce" title="Spruce">spruce</a>), and river names (<i>Ūla</i>, <i>Vilija</i> for <a href="/wiki/River_Neris" class="mw-redirect" title="River Neris">River Neris</a>).
</p>
<ul><li>invented names from literature.</li></ul>
<p>Some names were created by the authors of literary works and spread in public use through them. Such names followed the rules of the Lithuanian language; therefore it is sometimes difficult to tell whether the name is fictitious and had never existed before. Notably, <i>Gražina</i>, <i>Živilė</i> by <a href="/wiki/Adam_Mickiewicz" title="Adam Mickiewicz">Adam Mickiewicz</a>, <i>Daiva</i> by <a href="/wiki/Vyd%C5%ABnas" title="Vydūnas">Vydūnas</a>, <i>Šarūnas</i> by <a href="/wiki/Vincas_Kr%C4%97v%C4%97" class="mw-redirect" title="Vincas Krėvė">Vincas Krėvė</a> and others.
</p>
<ul><li>names of Lithuanian pagan deities and mythological figures.</li></ul>
<p>There are some popular names of gods and goddesses from <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_mythology" title="Lithuanian mythology">Lithuanian mythology</a> that are used as personal names, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Laima" title="Laima">Laima</a></i>, goddess of luck, <i><a href="/wiki/%C5%BDemyna" title="Žemyna">Žemyna</a></i>, goddess of earth, <i><a href="/wiki/Gabija" title="Gabija">Gabija</a></i>, goddess of fire; <i>Žilvinas</i>, a serpent prince from the fairy tale <i><a href="/wiki/Egl%C4%97_the_Queen_of_Serpents" title="Eglė the Queen of Serpents">Eglė the Queen of Serpents</a></i>, <i>Jūratė</i>, goddess of the sea, and <i>Kastytis</i>, from the legend about <i><a href="/wiki/J%C5%ABrat%C4%97_and_Kastytis" title="Jūratė and Kastytis">Jūratė and Kastytis</a></i>.
</p><p>A distinctive practice dominated in the <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_Lithuania" title="Regions of Lithuania">ethnic region</a> of  <a href="/wiki/Lithuania_Minor" title="Lithuania Minor">Lithuania Minor</a>, then part of <a href="/wiki/East_Prussia" title="East Prussia">East Prussia</a>, where Lithuanized <a href="/wiki/German_name" title="German name">German personal names</a> were common, such as <i>Ansas</i> (<a href="/wiki/Hans_(name)" title="Hans (name)">Hans</a>), <i>Grėtė</i> (<a href="/wiki/Gretchen" title="Gretchen">Gretchen</a>), <i>Vilius</i> (<a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_(name)" title="Wilhelm (name)">Wilhelm</a>) among <a href="/wiki/Prussian_Lithuanians" title="Prussian Lithuanians">Prussian Lithuanians</a>. Some of them are still in use among Lithuanians.
</p><p>The choice of a given name is influenced by fashion. Many parents may name their child after a <a href="/wiki/Folk_hero" title="Folk hero">national hero</a> or heroine, some otherwise famous person, or a character from a book, film, or <a href="/wiki/TV_show" class="mw-redirect" title="TV show">TV show</a>. However, many names used in today's Lithuania have been in use since the ancient times.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sex_differentiation">Sex differentiation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithuanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sex differentiation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lithuanian male and female names are distinguished grammatically. Almost all Lithuanian female names end in the vowels <i>-a</i> or <i>-<a href="/wiki/%C4%96" title="Ė">ė</a></i>, while male names almost always end in <i>-s</i>, and rarely in a vowel <i>-a</i>. If a masculine name ending in <i>-a</i> has a feminine counterpart, it ends in <i>-ė</i>, e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Jogaila" class="mw-redirect" title="Jogaila">Jogaila</a></i> and <i>Jogailė</i>. Female double-stemmed Lithuanian names always end in <i>-ė</i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diminutives">Diminutives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithuanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diminutives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">Diminutives</a> are very popular in everyday usage, and are by no means reserved for children. The <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_language" title="Lithuanian language">Lithuanian language</a> allows for a great deal of creativity in this field. Most diminutives are formed by adding a <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffix</a>. For female names this may be <i>-elė</i>, <i>-utė</i>, <i>-ytė</i>, or <i>-užė</i>; certain suffixes are more common to specific names over the rest.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (September 2019)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Also, as in many other cultures, a person may informally use a <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nickname</a> (<i>pravardė</i>) in addition to or instead of a given name.
</p>
<h2><span id="Pavard.C4.97_.28surname.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Pavardė_(surname)"><i>Pavardė</i> (surname)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithuanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pavardė (surname)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lithuanian surnames, like those in most of Europe, are hereditary and generally <a href="/wiki/Patrilineality" title="Patrilineality">patrilineal</a>, i.e., passed from the father to his children.
</p><p>A married woman usually adopts her husband's name. However, other combinations are legally possible. The wife may keep her <a href="/wiki/Maiden_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Maiden name">maiden name</a> (<i>mergautinė pavardė</i>) or add her husband's surname to hers, thus creating a <a href="/wiki/Double-barrelled_name" title="Double-barrelled name">double-barrelled name</a>. It is also possible, though rare, for the husband to adopt his wife's surname or to add his wife's surname to his family name.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithuanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Family names first appeared in Lithuania around 1500,<sup id="cite_ref-Schmalstieg_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schmalstieg-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> but were reserved for the <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_nobility" title="Lithuanian nobility">Lithuanian nobility</a>. They usually derived from <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymics</a>.
</p><p>The most striking peculiarity of the historical Lithuanian heraldic system, which was adopted from the Polish one in the <a href="/wiki/Union_of_Horodlo" class="mw-redirect" title="Union of Horodlo">Union of Horodlo</a> in 1413, is that <b>a coat of arms does not belong to a single family</b>. A number of unrelated families (sometimes hundreds of them), usually with a number of different family names, may use a coat of arms, <b>and each coat of arms has its own name</b>.
</p><p>The use of family names gradually spread to other social groups: the <a href="/wiki/Townsfolk" class="mw-redirect" title="Townsfolk">townsfolk</a> by the end of the 17th century, then the <a href="/wiki/Peasantry" class="mw-redirect" title="Peasantry">peasantry</a>. People from the villages did not have last names until the end of the 18th century. In such cases their village of origin was usually noted in documents. The process ended only in the mid-19th century, and due partial <a href="/wiki/Polonization" title="Polonization">Polonization</a> of society at the time many names were influenced by Polish form of the name.<sup id="cite_ref-Schmalstieg_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schmalstieg-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithuanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Based on origin, several groups of Lithuanian family names may be recognized.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cognominal">Cognominal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithuanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cognominal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Cognominal_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="Cognominal surname">cognominal surname</a> derives from a person's nickname, usually based a physical or character trait.
</p><p>Examples: 
</p>
<ul><li>Naujokas, Naujokaitis – from <i>naujas</i> ("new one")</li>
<li>Kairys, Kairelis, – "<a href="/wiki/Leftie" class="mw-redirect" title="Leftie">leftie</a>", from <i>kairė</i> ("left side")</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Occupational">Occupational</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithuanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Occupational">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Examples of <a href="/wiki/Occupational_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="Occupational surname">occupational surnames</a>: 
</p>
<ul><li>Kalvis, Kalvelis, Kalvaitis – from <i>kalvis</i> ("<a href="/wiki/Blacksmith" title="Blacksmith">blacksmith</a>")</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponymic">Toponymic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithuanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Toponymic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Toponymic_surname" title="Toponymic surname">toponymic surname</a>  usually derives from the name of a village or town,  or the name of a topographic feature.
</p><p>Examples:
</p>
<ul><li>Užugiris – from across the forest (<i>už girios</i>);</li>
<li>Kalnietis – from the mountains (<i>kalnai</i>).</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Patronymic">Patronymic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithuanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Patronymic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Patronymic_surname" title="Patronymic surname">patronymic surname</a> derives from a given name of a person and usually ends in a suffix suggesting a family relation.
</p><p>Examples:
</p>
<ul><li>Jonaitis, Janavičius, Januitis – derived from <i>Jonas</i> (<a href="/wiki/John_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="John (name)">John</a>);</li>
<li>Adomaitis, Adamonis, Adamkus – derived from <i>Adomas</i> (<a href="/wiki/Adam_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Adam (name)">Adam</a>);</li>
<li>Lukauskis, Lukša, Lukošius, Lukoševičius – derived from <i>Lukas</i> (<a href="/wiki/Luke_(name)" title="Luke (name)">Luke</a>).</li></ul>
<p>For this group of names the use of suffixes that cognate to the Slavic equivalent, such as <i>-avičius</i> (cognate of "-owicz"), <i>-auskas</i> (cognate of "-owski") is common:  <a href="/wiki/Jankauskas" title="Jankauskas">Jankauskas</a> (cognate of Slavic <a href="/wiki/Jankowski" title="Jankowski">Jankowski</a>),  <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adamkevi%C4%8Dius&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Adamkevičius (page does not exist)">Adamkevičius</a> (cognate of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adamkowicz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Adamkowicz (page does not exist)">Adamkowicz</a>), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Luko%C5%A1evi%C4%8Dius&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lukoševičius (page does not exist)">Lukoševičius</a> (cognate of <a href="/wiki/Lukaszewicz" class="mw-redirect" title="Lukaszewicz">Lukaszewicz</a>).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Diminutives_2">Diminutives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithuanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Diminutives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A number of surnames are <a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">diminutives</a> of popular first names.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Butkus" title="Butkus">Butkus</a> from Butkintas</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Minkus" title="Minkus">Minkus</a> from Minkantas</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Norkus" title="Norkus">Norkus</a> from Norkantas</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Rimkus" title="Rimkus">Rimkus</a> from Rimkantas</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feminine_forms">Feminine forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithuanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Feminine forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formal_and_informal_use">Formal and informal use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithuanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Formal and informal use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lithuanians pay great attention to the correct way of referring to or addressing other people depending on the level of social distance, familiarity and politeness. The differences between formal and informal language include:
</p>
<ul><li>using surnames vs. given names;</li>
<li>using vs. not using honorific titles such as <i>Ponas</i> / <i>Ponia</i>;</li>
<li>using the third person singular forms vs. second person singular;</li>
<li>using second-person singular personal pronoun vs. second-person plural personal pronoun to address a single person.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formal_language">Formal language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithuanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Formal language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="Ponas.2FPonia"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ponas/Ponia">Ponas/Ponia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithuanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Ponas/Ponia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i>Ponas</i> and <i>Ponia</i> (<a href="/wiki/Vocative_case" title="Vocative case">vocative case</a> <i>Pone</i>, <i>Ponia</i>) are the basic honorific styles used in Lithuanian to refer to a man or woman, respectively. In the past, these styles were reserved to members of the <i><a href="/wiki/Szlachta" title="Szlachta">szlachta</a></i> and played more or less the same roles as "Lord" or "Sir" and "Lady" or "Madam" in English. Since the 19th century, they have come to be used in all strata of society and may be considered equivalent to the English "Mr." and "Ms." There is a separate style, <i>Panelė</i> ("Miss"), applied to an unmarried woman.
</p>
<h4><span id="Given_name.2Fsurname_order"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Given_name/surname_order">Given name/surname order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithuanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Given name/surname order">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The given name(s) normally comes before the surname. However, in a list of people sorted alphabetically by surname, the surname usually comes first. In many formal situations the given name is omitted altogether.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Informal_language">Informal language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lithuanian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Informal language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Informal forms of address are normally used only by relatives, close friends and colleagues. In such situations diminutives are often preferred to the standard forms of given names.
</p>
