<p><b>Cambridgeshire</b> (abbreviated <b>Cambs.</b>)<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Counties_of_England" title="Counties of England">county</a> in the <a href="/wiki/East_of_England" title="East of England">East of England</a>, bordering <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a> to the north, <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a> to the north-east, <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a> to the east, <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire" title="Hertfordshire">Hertfordshire</a> to the south, and <a href="/wiki/Bedfordshire" title="Bedfordshire">Bedfordshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northamptonshire" title="Northamptonshire">Northamptonshire</a> to the west. The city of <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a> is the <a href="/wiki/County_town" title="County town">county town</a>. Modern Cambridgeshire was formed in 1974 as an amalgamation of the counties of <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire_and_Isle_of_Ely" title="Cambridgeshire and Isle of Ely">Cambridgeshire and Isle of Ely</a> and <a href="/wiki/Huntingdon_and_Peterborough" title="Huntingdon and Peterborough">Huntingdon and Peterborough</a>, the former covering the <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">historic county</a> of Cambridgeshire (including the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Ely" title="Isle of Ely">Isle of Ely</a>) and the latter covering the historic county of <a href="/wiki/Huntingdonshire" title="Huntingdonshire">Huntingdonshire</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Soke_of_Peterborough" title="Soke of Peterborough">Soke of Peterborough</a>, historically part of <a href="/wiki/Northamptonshire" title="Northamptonshire">Northamptonshire</a>. It contains most of the region known as <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Fen" title="Silicon Fen">Silicon Fen</a>.
</p><p>Local government is divided between <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire_County_Council" title="Cambridgeshire County Council">Cambridgeshire County Council</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peterborough_City_Council" title="Peterborough City Council">Peterborough City Council</a>, which, since 1998, forms a separate <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authorities_of_England" title="Unitary authorities of England">unitary authority</a>. Under the county council, there are five district councils, <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_City_Council" title="Cambridge City Council">Cambridge City Council</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Cambridgeshire_District_Council" title="South Cambridgeshire District Council">South Cambridgeshire District Council</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Cambridgeshire_District_Council" title="East Cambridgeshire District Council">East Cambridgeshire District Council</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huntingdonshire_District_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Huntingdonshire District Council">Huntingdonshire District Council</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fenland_District_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Fenland District Council">Fenland District Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cambridgeshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Cambridgeshire" title="History of Cambridgeshire">History of Cambridgeshire</a></div>
<p>Cambridgeshire is noted as the site of <a href="/wiki/Flag_Fen" title="Flag Fen">Flag Fen</a> in <a href="/wiki/Fengate" class="mw-redirect" title="Fengate">Fengate</a>, one of the earliest-known <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> permanent settlements in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, compared in importance to <a href="/wiki/Balbridie" title="Balbridie">Balbridie</a> in Aberdeen, Scotland. <a href="/wiki/Must_Farm" class="mw-redirect" title="Must Farm">Must Farm</a> quarry, at <a href="/wiki/Whittlesey" title="Whittlesey">Whittlesey</a> has been described as 'Britain's <a href="/wiki/Pompeii" title="Pompeii">Pompeii</a> due to its relatively good condition, including the 'best-preserved <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> dwellings ever found'. A great quantity of <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">archaeological</a> finds from the <a href="/wiki/Stone_Age" title="Stone Age">Stone Age</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> were made in <a href="/wiki/East_Cambridgeshire" title="East Cambridgeshire">East Cambridgeshire</a>. Most items were found in <a href="/wiki/Isleham" title="Isleham">Isleham</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Cambridgeshire was recorded in the <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> as "Grantbridgeshire" (or rather <i>Grentebrigescire</i>) (related to the river Granta).
</p><p>Covering a large part of <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a>, Cambridgeshire today is the result of several local government unifications.  In 1888 when <a href="/wiki/County_council" title="County council">county councils</a> were introduced, separate councils were set up, following the traditional division of Cambridgeshire, for
</p>
<ul><li>the area in the south around Cambridge, and</li>
<li>the liberty of the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Ely" title="Isle of Ely">Isle of Ely</a>.</li></ul>
<p>In 1965, these two administrative counties were merged to form <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire_and_the_Isle_of_Ely" class="mw-redirect" title="Cambridgeshire and the Isle of Ely">Cambridgeshire and the Isle of Ely</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
Under the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a> this merged with the county to the west, <a href="/wiki/Huntingdon_and_Peterborough" title="Huntingdon and Peterborough">Huntingdon and Peterborough</a>, which had been formed in 1965, by the merger of <a href="/wiki/Huntingdonshire" title="Huntingdonshire">Huntingdonshire</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Soke_of_Peterborough" title="Soke of Peterborough">Soke of Peterborough</a> (the latter previously a part of <a href="/wiki/Northamptonshire" title="Northamptonshire">Northamptonshire</a> with its own county council). The resulting county was called simply Cambridgeshire.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since 1998, the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Peterborough" class="mw-redirect" title="City of Peterborough">City of Peterborough</a> has been a separately administered area, as a <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authorities_of_England" title="Unitary authorities of England">unitary authority</a>. It is associated with Cambridgeshire for ceremonial purposes such as <a href="/wiki/Lord-Lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord-Lieutenant">Lieutenancy</a>, and joint functions such as policing and the fire service.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2002, the conservation charity <a href="/wiki/Plantlife" title="Plantlife">Plantlife</a> unofficially designated Cambridgeshire's <a href="/wiki/County_flower" class="mw-redirect" title="County flower">county flower</a> as the <a href="/wiki/Pulsatilla_vulgaris" title="Pulsatilla vulgaris">Pasqueflower</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire_Regiment" title="Cambridgeshire Regiment">Cambridgeshire Regiment</a> (nicknamed the Fen Tigers), the county-based army unit, fought in the <a href="/wiki/Boer_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Boer War">Boer War</a> in South Africa, the First World War and Second World War.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Due to the county's flat terrain and proximity to the continent, during the Second World War the military built many airfields here for <a href="/wiki/RAF_Bomber_Command" title="RAF Bomber Command">RAF Bomber Command</a>, <a href="/wiki/RAF_Fighter_Command" title="RAF Fighter Command">RAF Fighter Command</a>, and the allied <a href="/wiki/USAAF" class="mw-redirect" title="USAAF">USAAF</a>.  In recognition of this collaboration, the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_American_Cemetery_and_Memorial" title="Cambridge American Cemetery and Memorial">Cambridge American Cemetery and Memorial</a> is located in <a href="/wiki/Madingley" title="Madingley">Madingley</a>. It is the only WWII burial ground in England for American servicemen who died during that event.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Most English counties have nicknames for their people, such as a "<a href="/wiki/Tyke_(dialect)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tyke (dialect)">Tyke</a>" from <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> and a "<a href="/wiki/Yellowbelly_(Lincolnshire)" title="Yellowbelly (Lincolnshire)">Yellowbelly</a>" from <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>. The traditional <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nicknames</a> for people from Cambridgeshire are "Cambridgeshire Camel"<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> or "Cambridgeshire Crane", referring to the wildfowl that were once abundant in the <a href="/wiki/Fens" class="mw-redirect" title="Fens">fens</a>. The term "Fen Tigers" is sometimes used to describe the people who live and work in the fenlands.<sup id="cite_ref-Pryor1991_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pryor1991-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
Original historical documents relating to Cambridgeshire are held by <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire_Archives_and_Local_Studies" title="Cambridgeshire Archives and Local Studies">Cambridgeshire Archives and Local Studies</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cambridgeshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl><dd><i>See also <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_Cambridgeshire" title="Geology of Cambridgeshire">Geology of Cambridgeshire</a></i></dd></dl>
<p>Large areas of the county are extremely low-lying and <a href="/wiki/Holme,_Cambridgeshire" title="Holme, Cambridgeshire">Holme Fen</a> is notable for being the UK's lowest physical point at 2.75&#160;m (9&#160;ft) below sea level. The highest point is in the village of <a href="/wiki/Great_Chishill" title="Great Chishill">Great Chishill</a> at 146&#160;m (480&#160;ft) above sea level. Other <a href="/wiki/Topographic_prominence" title="Topographic prominence">prominent</a> hills are <a href="/wiki/Little_Trees_Hill" title="Little Trees Hill">Little Trees Hill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wandlebury_Hill" title="Wandlebury Hill">Wandlebury Hill</a> (both at 74&#160;m (243&#160;ft)) in the <a href="/wiki/Gog_Magog_Hills" title="Gog Magog Hills">Gog Magog Hills</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rivey_Hill" title="Rivey Hill">Rivey Hill</a> above <a href="/wiki/Linton,_Cambridgeshire" title="Linton, Cambridgeshire">Linton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rowley%27s_Hill" title="Rowley&#39;s Hill">Rowley's Hill</a> and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Madingley_Hills&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Madingley Hills (page does not exist)">Madingley Hills</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Green_belt">Green belt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cambridgeshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Green belt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Green_Belt" title="Cambridge Green Belt">Cambridge Green Belt</a></div>
<p>Cambridgeshire contains all its <a href="/wiki/Green_belt_(United_Kingdom)" title="Green belt (United Kingdom)">green belt</a> around the city of Cambridge, extending to places such as <a href="/wiki/Waterbeach" title="Waterbeach">Waterbeach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lode,_Cambridgeshire" title="Lode, Cambridgeshire">Lode</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duxford" title="Duxford">Duxford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Abingdon" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Abingdon">Little &amp; Great Abingdon</a> and other communities a few miles away in nearby districts, to afford a protection from the conurbation. It was first drawn up in the 1950s.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cambridgeshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_and_secondary">Primary and secondary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cambridgeshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Primary and secondary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For a more comprehensive list, see <a href="/wiki/List_of_schools_in_Cambridgeshire" title="List of schools in Cambridgeshire">List of schools in Cambridgeshire</a>.</div>
<p>Cambridgeshire has a completely comprehensive education system with 12 independent schools and over 240 state schools, not including <a href="/wiki/Sixth_form_college" title="Sixth form college">sixth form colleges</a>.
</p><p>Some of the secondary schools act as <a href="/wiki/Village_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Village College">Village Colleges</a>, institutions unique to Cambridgeshire. For example, <a href="/wiki/Bottisham_Village_College" title="Bottisham Village College">Bottisham Village College</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tertiary">Tertiary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cambridgeshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tertiary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cambridgeshire is home to a number of institutes of <a href="/wiki/Higher_education" title="Higher education">higher education</a>:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a> – <a href="/wiki/List_of_oldest_universities_in_continuous_operation" title="List of oldest universities in continuous operation">second-oldest</a> university in the English-speaking world, and regarded as one of the most prestigious academic institutions in the world</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anglia_Ruskin_University" title="Anglia Ruskin University">Anglia Ruskin University</a> – has a <a href="/wiki/Campus" title="Campus">campus</a> located in Cambridge and a base at <a href="/wiki/Fulbourn" title="Fulbourn">Fulbourn</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Open_University" title="Open University">Open University</a> – has a regional centre located in Cambridge</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/University_Centre_Peterborough" title="University Centre Peterborough">University Centre Peterborough</a> – operated by Anglia Ruskin University and <a href="/wiki/Peterborough_Regional_College" title="Peterborough Regional College">Peterborough Regional College</a>, located in Peterborough</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/College_of_West_Anglia" title="College of West Anglia">College of West Anglia</a> has a campus at Milton, on the northern outskirts of Cambridge and a campus at <a href="/wiki/Wisbech" title="Wisbech">Wisbech</a>.</li></ul>
<p>In addition, <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Regional_College" title="Cambridge Regional College">Cambridge Regional College</a> and <a href="/wiki/Huntingdonshire_Regional_College" title="Huntingdonshire Regional College">Huntingdonshire Regional College</a> both offer a limited range of higher education courses in conjunction with partner universities.

<p>Cambridgeshire has a maritime temperate climate which is broadly similar to the rest of the United Kingdom, though it is drier than the UK average due to its low altitude and easterly location, the prevailing southwesterly winds having already deposited moisture on higher ground further west. Average winter temperatures are cooler than the English average, due to Cambridgeshire's inland location and relative nearness to continental Europe, which results in the moderating maritime influence being less strong. Snowfall is slightly more common than in western areas, due to the relative winter coolness and easterly winds bringing occasional snow from the North Sea. In summer temperatures are average or slightly above, due to less cloud cover. It reaches 25&#160;°C (77&#160;°F) on around 10 days each year, and is comparable to parts of Kent and East Anglia.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cambridgeshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cambridgeshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Various <a href="/wiki/Football" title="Football">forms of football</a> have been popular in Cambridgeshire since medieval times at least. In 1579 one match played at <a href="/wiki/Chesterton,_Cambridge" title="Chesterton, Cambridge">Chesterton</a> between townspeople and <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Cambridge University">Cambridge University</a> students ended in a violent brawl that led the Vice-Chancellor to issue a decree forbidding them to play "footeball” outside of college grounds.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> During the nineteenth century, several formulations of the laws of football, known as the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_rules" title="Cambridge rules">Cambridge rules</a>, were created by students at the University.  One of these codes, dating from 1863, had a significant influence on the creation of the original <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_the_Game_(association_football)" title="Laws of the Game (association football)">laws</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Football_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="Football Association">Football Association</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cambridgeshire is also the birthplace of <a href="/wiki/Bandy" title="Bandy">bandy</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> now an IOC accepted sport.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> According to documents from 1813, <a href="/wiki/Bury_Fen_Bandy_Club" class="mw-redirect" title="Bury Fen Bandy Club">Bury Fen Bandy Club</a> was undefeated for 100 years. A member of the club, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Goodman_Tebbutt" title="Charles Goodman Tebbutt">Charles Goodman Tebbutt</a>, wrote down the first official rules in 1882.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-23">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Tebbutt was instrumental in spreading the sport to many countries.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain_Bandy_Federation" title="Great Britain Bandy Federation">Great Britain Bandy Federation</a> is based in Cambridgeshire.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Fen_skating" title="Fen skating">Fen skating</a> is a traditional form of skating in the <a href="/wiki/The_Fens" title="The Fens">Fenland</a>. The <a href="/wiki/National_Ice_Skating_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="National Ice Skating Association">National Ice Skating Association</a> was set up in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a> in 1879, they took the top Fen skaters to the worldspeed skating championships where James Smart became world champion.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>On 6–7 June 2015, the inaugural Tour of Cambridgeshire cycle race took place on closed roads across the county. The event was an official <a href="/wiki/Union_Cycliste_Internationale" title="Union Cycliste Internationale">UCI</a> qualification event, and consisted of a Time Trial on the 6th, and a <a href="/wiki/Gran_Fondo" title="Gran Fondo">Gran Fondo</a> event on the 7th.  The Gran Fondo event was open to the public, and over 6000 riders took part in the 128&#160;km (80&#160;mi) race.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_art">Contemporary art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cambridgeshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Contemporary art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cambridge is home to the <a href="/wiki/Kettle%27s_Yard" title="Kettle&#39;s Yard">Kettle's Yard</a> gallery and the artist-run Aid and Abet project space. Nine miles west of Cambridge next to the village of <a href="/wiki/Bourn" title="Bourn">Bourn</a> is <a href="/wiki/Wysing_Arts_Centre" title="Wysing Arts Centre">Wysing Arts Centre</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
Cambridge Open Studios is the regions largest arts organisation with over 500 members. Every year, more than 370 artists open their doors to visitors during four weekends in July. <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="https://www.camopenstudios.co.uk/">https://www.camopenstudios.co.uk/</a>
</p>
