<p>A <b>local area network</b> (<b>LAN</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Computer_network" title="Computer network">computer network</a> that interconnects computers within a limited area such as a residence, school, laboratory, university campus or office building.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> By contrast, a <a href="/wiki/Wide_area_network" title="Wide area network">wide area network</a> (WAN) not only covers a larger geographic distance, but also generally involves <a href="/wiki/Leased_line" title="Leased line">leased telecommunication circuits</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> are the two most common technologies in use for local area networks. Historical technologies include <a href="/wiki/ARCNET" title="ARCNET">ARCNET</a>, <a href="/wiki/Token_ring" title="Token ring">Token ring</a>, and <a href="/wiki/AppleTalk" title="AppleTalk">AppleTalk</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_area_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The increasing demand and use of computers in universities and research labs in the late 1960s generated the need to provide high-speed interconnections between computer systems. A 1970 report from the <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Radiation_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="Lawrence Radiation Laboratory">Lawrence Radiation Laboratory</a> detailing the growth of their "Octopus" network gave a good indication of the situation.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A number of experimental and early commercial LAN technologies were developed in the 1970s. <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Ring_(computer_network)" title="Cambridge Ring (computer network)">Cambridge Ring</a> was developed at Cambridge University starting in 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ethernet" title="Ethernet">Ethernet</a> was developed at <a href="/wiki/PARC_(company)" title="PARC (company)">Xerox PARC</a> between 1973 and 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-metcalfe_video_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-metcalfe_video-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/ARCNET" title="ARCNET">ARCNET</a> was developed by <a href="/wiki/Datapoint" title="Datapoint">Datapoint</a> Corporation in 1976 and announced in 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It had the first commercial installation in December 1977 at <a href="/wiki/Chase_Manhattan_Bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Chase Manhattan Bank">Chase Manhattan Bank</a> in New York.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The development and proliferation of <a href="/wiki/Personal_computer" title="Personal computer">personal computers</a> using the <a href="/wiki/CP/M" title="CP/M">CP/M</a> operating system in the late 1970s, and later <a href="/wiki/DOS" title="DOS">DOS</a>-based systems starting in 1981, meant that many sites grew to dozens or even hundreds of computers. The initial driving force for networking was to share <a href="/wiki/Computer_data_storage" title="Computer data storage">storage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Printer_(computing)" title="Printer (computing)">printers</a>, both of which were expensive at the time. There was much enthusiasm for the concept, and for several years, from about 1983 onward, computer industry pundits would regularly declare the coming year to be, "The year of the LAN".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In practice, the concept was marred by proliferation of incompatible <a href="/wiki/Physical_layer" title="Physical layer">physical layer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Network_protocol" class="mw-redirect" title="Network protocol">network protocol</a> implementations, and a plethora of methods of sharing resources. Typically, each vendor would have its own type of network card, cabling, protocol, and <a href="/wiki/Network_operating_system" title="Network operating system">network operating system</a>. A solution appeared with the advent of <a href="/wiki/Novell_NetWare" class="mw-redirect" title="Novell NetWare">Novell NetWare</a> which provided even-handed support for dozens of competing card and cable types, and a much more sophisticated operating system than most of its competitors. Netware dominated the personal computer LAN business from early after its introduction in 1983 until the mid-1990s when Microsoft introduced <a href="/wiki/Windows_NT" title="Windows NT">Windows NT</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Of the competitors to NetWare, only <a href="/wiki/Banyan_Vines" class="mw-redirect" title="Banyan Vines">Banyan Vines</a> had comparable technical strengths, but Banyan never gained a secure base. <a href="/wiki/3Com" title="3Com">3Com</a> produced <a href="/wiki/3%2BShare" title="3+Share">3+Share</a>  and Microsoft produced <a href="/wiki/MS-Net" title="MS-Net">MS-Net</a>. These then formed the basis for collaboration between <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> and <a href="/wiki/3Com" title="3Com">3Com</a> to create a simple network operating system <a href="/wiki/LAN_Manager" title="LAN Manager">LAN Manager</a> and its cousin, IBM's <a href="/wiki/LAN_Server" class="mw-redirect" title="LAN Server">LAN Server</a>. None of these enjoyed any lasting success.
</p><p>In 1983, TCP/IP was first shown capable of supporting actual defense department applications on a Defense Communication Agency LAN test bed located at Reston, Virginia.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The TCP/IP-based LAN successfully supported <a href="/wiki/Telnet" title="Telnet">Telnet</a>, <a href="/wiki/FTP" class="mw-redirect" title="FTP">FTP</a>, and a Defense Department teleconferencing application.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This demonstrated the feasibility of employing TCP/IP LANs to interconnect <a href="/wiki/Worldwide_Military_Command_and_Control_System" title="Worldwide Military Command and Control System">Worldwide Military Command and Control System</a> (WWMCCS) computers at command centers throughout the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  However, WWMCCS was superseded by the <a href="/wiki/Global_Command_and_Control_System" title="Global Command and Control System">Global Command and Control System</a> (GCCS) before that could happen.
</p><p>During the same period, <a href="/wiki/Unix_workstation" class="mw-redirect" title="Unix workstation">Unix workstations</a> were using TCP/IP networking. Although this market segment is now much reduced, the technologies developed in this area continue to be influential on the Internet and in both <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">Linux</a> and Apple <a href="/wiki/Mac_OS_X" class="mw-redirect" title="Mac OS X">Mac OS X</a> networking&#8212;and the TCP/IP protocol has replaced <a href="/wiki/IPX" class="mw-redirect" title="IPX">IPX</a>, <a href="/wiki/AppleTalk" title="AppleTalk">AppleTalk</a>, <a href="/wiki/NetBIOS_Frames_protocol" title="NetBIOS Frames protocol">NBF</a>, and other protocols used by the early PC LANs.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cabling">Cabling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_area_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cabling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early Ethernet (<a href="/wiki/10BASE-5" class="mw-redirect" title="10BASE-5">10BASE-5</a> and <a href="/wiki/10BASE-2" class="mw-redirect" title="10BASE-2">10BASE-2</a>) used <a href="/wiki/Coaxial_cable" title="Coaxial cable">coaxial cable</a>. <a href="/wiki/Shielded_twisted_pair" class="mw-redirect" title="Shielded twisted pair">Shielded twisted pair</a> was used in IBM's <a href="/wiki/Token_Ring" class="mw-redirect" title="Token Ring">Token Ring</a> LAN implementation. In 1984, <a href="/wiki/StarLAN" title="StarLAN">StarLAN</a> showed the potential of simple <a href="/wiki/Unshielded_twisted_pair" class="mw-redirect" title="Unshielded twisted pair">unshielded twisted pair</a> by using <a href="/wiki/Category_3_cable" title="Category 3 cable">Cat3</a> cable&#8212;the same cable used for telephone systems. This led to the development of <a href="/wiki/10BASE-T" class="mw-redirect" title="10BASE-T">10BASE-T</a> (and <a href="/wiki/Ethernet_over_twisted_pair" title="Ethernet over twisted pair">its successors</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Structured_cabling" title="Structured cabling">structured cabling</a> which is still the basis of most commercial LANs today.
</p><p>While <a href="/wiki/Optical_fiber_cable" class="mw-redirect" title="Optical fiber cable">optical fiber cable</a> is common for links between <a href="/wiki/Network_switch" title="Network switch">network switches</a>, use of <a href="/wiki/Fiber_to_the_desktop" class="mw-redirect" title="Fiber to the desktop">fiber to the desktop</a> is rare.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wireless_media">Wireless media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_area_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wireless media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Wireless_LAN" title="Wireless LAN">wireless LAN</a>, users have unrestricted movement within the coverage area. Wireless networks have become popular in residences and small businesses, because of their ease of installation. Most wireless LANs use <a href="/wiki/Wi-Fi" title="Wi-Fi">Wi-Fi</a> as it is built into <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tablet_computer" title="Tablet computer">tablet computers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laptop" title="Laptop">laptops</a>. Guests are often offered <a href="/wiki/Internet_access" title="Internet access">Internet access</a> via a <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(Wi-Fi)" title="Hotspot (Wi-Fi)">hotspot</a> service.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_aspects">Technical aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Local_area_network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Technical aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Network_topology" title="Network topology">Network topology</a> describes the layout of interconnections between devices and network segments. At the <a href="/wiki/Data_link_layer" title="Data link layer">data link layer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_layer" title="Physical layer">physical layer</a>, a wide variety of LAN topologies have been used, including <a href="/wiki/Ring_network" title="Ring network">ring</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bus_network" title="Bus network">bus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mesh_network" class="mw-redirect" title="Mesh network">mesh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Star_network" title="Star network">star</a>.
</p><p>Simple LANs generally consist of cabling and one or more <a href="/wiki/Network_switch" title="Network switch">switches</a>.  A switch can be connected to a <a href="/wiki/Router_(computing)" title="Router (computing)">router</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cable_modem" title="Cable modem">cable modem</a>, or <a href="/wiki/ADSL_modem" class="mw-redirect" title="ADSL modem">ADSL modem</a> for <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> access. A LAN can include a wide variety of other network devices such as <a href="/wiki/Firewall_(computing)" title="Firewall (computing)">firewalls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Load_balancing_(computing)" title="Load balancing (computing)">load balancers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Network_intrusion_detection_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Network intrusion detection system">network intrusion detection</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Advanced LANs are characterized by their use of redundant links with switches using the <a href="/wiki/Spanning_tree_protocol" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanning tree protocol">spanning tree protocol</a> to prevent loops, their ability to manage differing traffic types via <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_service" title="Quality of service">quality of service</a> (QoS), and their ability to segregate traffic with <a href="/wiki/VLAN" class="mw-redirect" title="VLAN">VLANs</a>.
</p><p>At the higher network layers, protocols such as <a href="/wiki/NetBIOS_Frames_protocol" title="NetBIOS Frames protocol">NetBEUI</a>, <a href="/wiki/IPX/SPX" title="IPX/SPX">IPX/SPX</a>, <a href="/wiki/AppleTalk" title="AppleTalk">AppleTalk</a> and others were once common, but the <a href="/wiki/Internet_Protocol_Suite" class="mw-redirect" title="Internet Protocol Suite">Internet Protocol Suite</a> (TCP/IP) has prevailed as a standard of choice.
</p><p>LANs can maintain connections with other LANs via leased lines, leased services, or across the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> using <a href="/wiki/Virtual_private_network" title="Virtual private network">virtual private network</a> technologies. Depending on how the connections are established and secured, and the distance involved, such linked LANs may also be classified as a <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_area_network" title="Metropolitan area network">metropolitan area network</a> (MAN) or a <a href="/wiki/Wide_area_network" title="Wide area network">wide area network</a> (WAN).
</p>
