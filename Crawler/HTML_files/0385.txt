	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Signum manus</i></h1>

<p><i><b>Signum manus</b></i> (sometimes also known as <i>Chrismon</i>) refers to the medieval practice, current from the <a href="/wiki/Merovingian_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Merovingian period">Merovingian period</a> until the 14th century in the <a href="/wiki/Frankish_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Frankish Empire">Frankish Empire</a> and its successors, of signing a document or charter with a special type of <a href="/wiki/Monogram" title="Monogram">monogram</a> or <a href="/wiki/Royal_cypher" title="Royal cypher">royal cypher</a>.
</p><p>The term <i>Chrismon</i> was introduced in <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">New Latin</a> specifically as a term for the <a href="/wiki/Chi_Rho" title="Chi Rho">Chi Rho</a> monogram. As this symbol was used in Merovingian documents at the starting point of what would diversify into the tradition of "cross-signatures", German scholarship of the 18th century extended use of the term <i>Chrismon</i> to the entire field.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In medievalist <a href="/wiki/Paleography" class="mw-redirect" title="Paleography">paleography</a> and <i>Diplomatik</i> (<i>ars diplomaticae</i>, i.e. the study of <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/diploma#Latin" class="extiw" title="wikt:diploma">documents or charters</a>), the study of these signatures or sigils was known as <i>Chrismologia</i> or <i>Chrismenlehre</i>, while the study of <a href="/wiki/Christian_cross_variants" title="Christian cross variants">cross variants</a> was known as <i>Staurologia</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Chrismon</i> in this context may refer to the <a href="/wiki/Merovingian_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Merovingian period">Merovingian period</a> abbreviation <i>I. C. N.</i> for <i>in Christi nomine</i>, later (in the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Carolingian period">Carolingian period</a>) also <i>I. C.</i> for <i>in Christo</i>, and still later (in the high medieval period) just <i>C.</i> for <i>Christus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A cross symbol was often drawn as an invocation at the beginning of documents in the early medieval West. 
At the end of documents, commissioners or witnesses would sign with a <i>signum manus</i>, often also in the form of a simple cross. This practice is widespread in Merovingian documents of the 7th and 8th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> A related development is the widespread use of the cross symbol on the obverse side of early medieval coins, interpreted as the <i>signum manus</i> of the moneyer.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
The tradition of minting coins with the monogram of the ruling monarch on the obverse side originates in the 5th century, both in Byzantium and in Rome. This tradition was continued in the 6th century by Germanic kings, including the Merovingians. These early designs were <i>box</i> monograms. The first <i>cruciform</i> monogram was used by <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">Justinian I</a> in the 560s. <a href="/wiki/Tiberius_III" title="Tiberius III">Tiberius III</a> used a cruciform monogram with the letters <i>R, M</i> for Rome and <i>T, B</i> for Tiberius; <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_III" title="Pope Gregory III">Pope Gregory III</a> used the letters <i>G, R, E, O</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The earliest surviving Merovingian royal charters, dating to the 7th century, have the box monograms of <a href="/wiki/Chlothar_II" title="Chlothar II">Chlothar II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clovis_II" title="Clovis II">Clovis II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Later in the 7th century, the use of royal monograms was abandoned entirely by the Merovingian kings; instead, royal wax seals were first attached to the documents, and the kings would sign their name in full.
</p><p>The <i>signum manus</i> in the form of a modified cross symbol first appears in charters of both Frankish Gaul and Anglo-Saxon England in the late 7th and early 8th century. <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a>  first used his cruciform monogram, likely inspired by the earlier papal monograms, in 769, and he would continue to use it for the rest of his reign. The monogram spells <i>KAROLVS</i>, with the 
consonants <i>K, R, L, S</i> at the ends of the cross-arms, and the vowels <i>A, O, V</i> displayed in ligature at the center.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> 
<a href="/wiki/Louis_the_Pious" title="Louis the Pious">Louis the Pious</a> abandoned the cross monogram, using again a H-type or box monogram.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
