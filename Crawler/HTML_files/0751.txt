<p><b>Next </b> (<a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">LSE</a>:&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.londonstockexchange.com/exchange/searchengine/search.html?lang=en&amp;x=0&amp;y=0&amp;q=NXT">NXT</a>), styled as <b>next</b>, is a British <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">multinational</a> clothing, footwear and home products retailer headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Enderby,_Leicestershire" title="Enderby, Leicestershire">Enderby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leicestershire" title="Leicestershire">Leicestershire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FT_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FT-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It has around 700 stores, of which circa 500 are in the United Kingdom, and circa 200 across Europe, Asia and the Middle East.<sup id="cite_ref-glance_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-glance-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Next is the largest clothing retailer by sales in the United Kingdom, having overtaken <a href="/wiki/Marks_%26_Spencer" title="Marks &amp; Spencer">Marks &amp; Spencer</a> in early 2012<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It is listed on the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a> and is a constituent of the <a href="/wiki/FTSE_100_Index" title="FTSE 100 Index">FTSE 100 Index</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Next_plc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hepworth">Hepworth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Next_plc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hepworth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The company was founded by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Hepworth_(tailor)" title="Joseph Hepworth (tailor)">Joseph Hepworth</a> in <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> in 1864 as a tailor under the name of <b>Joseph Hepworth &amp; Son</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-history_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Initially Hepworth was in partnership with James Rhodes, but the partnership was dissolved in 1872.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On his own, Hepworth expanded the company rapidly, becoming a pioneer for the development of chain stores in Britain. By 1884 the company had 100 outlets.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For much of its history Hepworth was predominantly in the ready-to-wear suit market,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and in 1963 the company brought in the celebrated Saville Row designer <a href="/wiki/Hardy_Amies" title="Hardy Amies">Hardy Amies</a> to help revitalise its ready-to-wear suit collection.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Kendall.27s_and_Next"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Kendall's_and_Next">Kendall's and Next</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Next_plc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Kendall&#039;s and Next">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1981 the company bought womenswear retailer Kendall and Sons for £1.75 million from the retail conglomerate Combined English Stores. This gave Hepworth over 600 shops in British high streets.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The intention was to redevelop Kendall's stores as a womenswear chain of shops to complement Hepworth as a chain of menswear stores. <a href="/wiki/Terence_Conran" title="Terence Conran">Terence Conran</a>, the designer, was Chairman of Hepworth at this time and he recruited <a href="/wiki/George_Davies_(retailer)" title="George Davies (retailer)">George Davies</a> to work at Kendall's. However Davies's concept was to create a new chain, called Next, initially by converting Kendall's stores. The first Next shops opened on 12 February 1982, with the Kendall's conversion complete by the end of 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-history_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Next_-_Oxford_Street_1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Next_-_Oxford_Street_1.jpg/220px-Next_-_Oxford_Street_1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Next_-_Oxford_Street_1.jpg/330px-Next_-_Oxford_Street_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Next_-_Oxford_Street_1.jpg/440px-Next_-_Oxford_Street_1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Next_-_Oxford_Street_1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A branch of Next showing the old logos on <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Street" title="Oxford Street">Oxford Street</a> in London in 2005.</div></div></div>
<p>Made chief executive in 1984, Davies then converted 50 Hepworth stores to the Next format, extending the total concept look at the same time to cover menswear. This allowed the development of mini department stores across the entire footprint, selling women's and men's clothes. This was added to by the introduction of Next interiors to stores which were deemed in the "right demographical areas." In 1986, Davies moved the group's headquarters from Leeds to <a href="/wiki/Leicester" title="Leicester">Leicester</a>, to be closer to the main garment manufacturers, and the company name was changed to Next plc.<sup id="cite_ref-history_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1987, the group acquired Combined English Stores and the Grattan catalogue company. Extending first to introduce Next childrenswear, Davies then introduced the Next Directory.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By 1988, "after seven years of growth, Next had over-expanded suicidally" .. "some stores were not bringing in enough to pay the rent."<sup id="cite_ref-indy_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-indy-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Davies was sacked and the share price fell to 7p.<sup id="cite_ref-indy_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-indy-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Chairman Sir David Jones, accused him of being egotistical and taking Next to the verge of bankruptcy.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In October 1988 Next sold 433 jewellery stores in the United Kingdom, which principally traded under the Salisburys and Zales brands, to the <a href="/wiki/Signet_Jewelers" title="Signet Jewelers">Ratners Group</a> for US$232 million.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The company bought the youth brand Lipsy in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In Autumn 2009, Next launched an online catalogue for the United States offering clothing, shoes and accessories for women, men and children.<sup id="cite_ref-direct_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-direct-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Next's prices in Ireland attracted criticism in 2009 when the company was one of four retailers accused of failing to pass on exchange rate savings to shoppers in the Republic.<sup id="cite_ref-IT_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IT-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2010, a <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> investigation found Next was breaking the <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Protection_(Distance_Selling)_Regulations_2000" title="Consumer Protection (Distance Selling) Regulations 2000">Consumer Protection (Distance Selling) Regulations 2000</a> by billing customers for its delivery costs even if goods were returned within the seven working days.<sup id="cite_ref-law_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-law-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> A spokesman for Next admitted that they had been doing this for three years but promised to comply by August 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-law_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-law-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Trading_Standards" title="Trading Standards">Trading Standards</a> said that the DSRs had been in force for ten years, and there was no excuse for not adhering to them.<sup id="cite_ref-law_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-law-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014, Next launched localized cross-border sales to <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Living_wages">Living wages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Next_plc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Living wages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 2014 the <a href="/wiki/Living_Wage_Foundation" title="Living Wage Foundation">Living Wage Foundation</a> bought Next shares and attended the annual general meeting in an attempt to persuade the company to pay at least £6.70 and become one of the UK's 700 living wage employers. Next was targeted because it claimed to be a good employer and was thriving. <a href="/wiki/George_Bain_(academic)" title="George Bain (academic)">Professor Sir George Bain</a> who set the minimum wage in 1999 said employers could afford to pay much more but acknowledged enforcement could cause unemployment in the retail sector.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2014, the company was one of several retailers criticised by <a href="/wiki/Janice_Turner" title="Janice Turner">Janice Turner</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> for failing to pay a living wage.  UK taxpayers pay £28&#160;billion to low-paid workers and Turner says retail companies – which have the highest proportion of low paid workers – are exploiting austerity and effectively adding staff wages to the UK welfare bill. When asked why, despite record profits their lowest paid workers were so poorly paid, Next replied that they had thirty applicants for every job advertised.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations">Operations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Next_plc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Operations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Next has three main channels: Next Retail, a chain of 550+ retail branches in the United Kingdom; Next Directory, a home shopping catalogue and Website with more than 3&#160;million active customers, and Next International, with 180+ international stores.<sup id="cite_ref-FT_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FT-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Its other businesses include Next Sourcing, for own brand products; Lipsy, which designs and sells its own branded younger women's fashion products through wholesale, retail and website channels.<sup id="cite_ref-FT_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FT-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Next_Forever_Comfort_shoe_in_suede.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Next_Forever_Comfort_shoe_in_suede.gif/220px-Next_Forever_Comfort_shoe_in_suede.gif" decoding="async" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Next_Forever_Comfort_shoe_in_suede.gif/330px-Next_Forever_Comfort_shoe_in_suede.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Next_Forever_Comfort_shoe_in_suede.gif/440px-Next_Forever_Comfort_shoe_in_suede.gif 2x" data-file-width="625" data-file-height="499" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Next_Forever_Comfort_shoe_in_suede.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Next Forever Comfort shoe in suede</div></div></div>
<p>In September 2018, the company's Irish operations profits were separated from Next plc.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Next (Ireland) Ltd was formally established to focus on the distribution of products in its physical stores and its online store in the Republic of Ireland. At the same time Next Germany was established.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Logos_and_marketing">Logos and marketing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Next_plc&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Logos and marketing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Next_former_logo.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5e/Next_former_logo.png/220px-Next_former_logo.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="80" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/5e/Next_former_logo.png 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="109" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Next_former_logo.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Next logo used from 1991 to 2007</div></div></div>
<p>Until circa 1991 Next used a lower case <a href="/wiki/Courier_(typeface)" title="Courier (typeface)">Courier</a>-style typeface in black against a white background for its logo. This was replaced by the capitalised NEXT logo in a <a href="/wiki/Roman_type" title="Roman type">Roman</a>-serif style type face. There were some variations of this such as the logo with each letter of NEXT in an individual square and in some stores in 2005/6 had the Next logo in a varying blue &amp; black background with "X's" printed on them, as opposed to the black background. In addition, some variations in typeface occurred during the logo's use – including similar fonts that had serifs positioned above the "T" crossbar, similar to <a href="/wiki/Garamond" title="Garamond">Garamond</a> and others that had more in common with <a href="/wiki/Times_New_Roman" title="Times New Roman">Times New Roman</a>. In 2007 a new <b>next</b> logo was introduced, although the previous logo continued to be used until stock was exhausted.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Next clothing often carries reference to the origins of the company in 1982 with use of "82" or "1982" as a design feature on clothes in all ranges.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Prior to 2007 Next only advertised immediately prior to a sale, usually through brief television spots and newspaper advertising. In 2007 following a "disappointing" 7.2% fall in <a href="/wiki/Like_for_like" title="Like for like">like for like</a> sales, it announced it was investing "£17&#160;million over the next three years to revive its existing stores and product offering" + an additional £10m for marketing.<sup id="cite_ref-yas_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yas-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Yasmin_Le_Bon" title="Yasmin Le Bon">Yasmin Le Bon</a> who modelled in the first Next Directory in Spring 1988 featured in an on-line fashion show.<sup id="cite_ref-yas_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yas-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 2007, to celebrate its 25th anniversary, Next launched its first television campaign in twelve years named 'Ali's Party' with the song '<a href="/wiki/Suddenly_I_See" title="Suddenly I See">Suddenly I See</a>' and starring Brazilian supermodel <a href="/wiki/Alessandra_Ambrosio" title="Alessandra Ambrosio">Alessandra Ambrosio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-maduk_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maduk-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> All extra casts were Next employees, otherwise nicknamed 'nextras'. A second advert featuring Ambrosio, was screened in November 2007 and the songs were regularly played instore during the campaign.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An advert directed by <a href="/wiki/Ben_Watts" title="Ben Watts">Ben Watts</a> and filmed on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Seine" title="Seine">River Seine</a> was shown in September 2010 to reflect the season’s <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Parisian</a> chic styles. It was soundtracked by <a href="/wiki/The_Specials" title="The Specials">The Specials</a>’ "<a href="/wiki/A_Message_to_You,_Rudy" class="mw-redirect" title="A Message to You, Rudy">A Message to You, Rudy</a>" and starred Brazilian model <a href="/wiki/Emanuela_de_Paula" title="Emanuela de Paula">Emanuela de Paula</a> and Spanish actor <a href="/wiki/Jon_Kortajarena" title="Jon Kortajarena">Jon Kortajarena</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
