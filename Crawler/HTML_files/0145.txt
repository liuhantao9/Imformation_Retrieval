<p>Heritable <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">family names</a> were generally adopted rather late within <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">Nobility</a> were the first to take names that would be passed on from one generation to the next. Later, <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">artisans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Merchant" title="Merchant">merchants</a> in <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">cities</a> took heritable names. Family names (surnames) were still used together with <i>primary <a href="/wiki/Patronym" class="mw-redirect" title="Patronym">patronyms</a></i> (father's name plus an <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">affix</a> denoting relationship), which were used by all social classes. This meant that most families until modern times did not have surnames. Scandinavian <a href="/wiki/Patronym" class="mw-redirect" title="Patronym">patronyms</a> were generally derived from the father's given name with the addition of a <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffix</a> meaning 'son' or 'daughter' or by occupation like Møller - ( Miller ) naming tradition remained commonly used throughout the Scandinavian countries during the time of surname formation.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
Forms of the patronymic <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffixes</a> include: <i>-son</i>, <i>-sen</i>, <i>-fen</i>, <i>-søn</i>, <i>-ler</i>, <i>-zen</i>, <i>-zon/zoon</i>, and <i>-ssøn  </i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Denmark">Denmark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandinavian_family_name_etymology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Denmark">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most common Danish family name <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surnames</a> are <a href="/wiki/Patronymic_surname" title="Patronymic surname">patronymic</a> and end in <i>-sen</i>; for example <i>Rasmussen</i>, originally meaning "son of Rasmus" (Rasmus' son). Descendants of Danish or Norwegian immigrants to the United States frequently have similar names ending in the suffix "-sen" or have changed the spelling to "-son". 
Approximately one-third of the <a href="/wiki/Danish_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Danish people">Danish</a> population bear one of the ten most common surnames. More than two-thirds have a patronym ending in <i>-sen</i> in their full name. Many of these patronymics are, however, very rare, local or testimony of unusual descent, e.g. <b>Heilesen</b> from <a href="/wiki/Jutland" title="Jutland">Northern Jutland</a>, <b>Holdensen</b> and <b>Boldsen</b> from the former <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Schleswig" title="Duchy of Schleswig">Duchy of Schleswig</a> or Common etymological classes of surnames are occupational (e.g., <i>Møller</i> - miller, <i>Schmidt</i> - smith, and <i>Fisker</i> - fisher, for example names taken after a village or farmstead inhabited by ancestors.
</p><p>Other higher class people took heritable surnames during the following centuries, <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a> often <i>Latinized</i> names (e.g. <i>Pontoppidan</i> made from <i>Broby</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">artisans</a> often <i>Germanized</i> names. Naming acts applying to all citizens were issued 1771 (for the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Schleswig" title="Duchy of Schleswig">Duchy of Schleswig</a> only) and in 1828. The rural population only reluctantly gave up the traditional primary patronyms. Several naming acts replaced the first; in 1856, 1904, 1961, 1981, 2005. The result of the first act was that most people took a patronymic surname as their heritable family name, with the overwhelming dominance of a few surnames as a consequence. Later acts have attempted to motivate people to change to surnames that would allow safer identification of individuals.
</p><p>In the table, the top surnames in <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> are listed as of 1971<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The general tendency over the past century has been to give up the commonest names and adopt less frequently-used ones.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Norway">Norway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandinavian_family_name_etymology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Norway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The most common Norwegian surnames were originally patronymic, commonly ending with the suffixes "-ssen", "-sson", "-sdatter", "-sdotter" which is the genitive s plus the word <i>sen</i> or <i>son</i> for son or <i>datter</i> or <i>dotter</i> for daughter.  The genitive <i>s</i> was often dropped; compare Hanssen and Hansen.  In 1923, it was ordered by law that each family should have a single, hereditary last name. Surnames derived from placenames commonly originated as farm names. Most families took a patronymic name, but some adopted a farm name.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Today, the patronymic names are increasingly being left for the toponyms; 22.4% of the Norwegian population had a "-sen"-name in 2009, while the share is down to 18.4% for the newborns of 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The most common Norwegian surnames would include many names which originated as farm names:
Bakke/Bakken (hill or rise), Berg/Berge (mountain or hill), Dahl/Dal (valley), Haugen/Haugan (hill or mound), Lie (side of a valley), Moen (meadow), or Rud (clearing).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scandinavian_family_name_etymology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sweden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Swedish_name" title="Swedish name">Swedish name</a></div>
<p>The most common surnames in Sweden are originally patronymic. Family names ending with the suffix  "sson" are the most common names in Sweden. In 1901, the Names Adoption Act was passed, which abolished the patronymic practice. From 1901, everyone had to have a family name that was passed down to the next generation. 
</p><p>Many family names consist of items from nature, for example Lind/Lindberg (linden/lime + mountain), Berg/Bergkvist (mountain/mountain + twig), Alström/Ahlström (alder + stream), or Dahl/Dahlin (valley). Sometimes the first part of such a composite name refers to the family's place of origin e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Strindberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Strindberg">Strindberg</a> family originating from ShurieStrinne; the second part being just ornamental. Families also frequently have military-oriented names such as Skarpsvärd (sharp sword), <a href="/wiki/Sk%C3%B6ld" title="Sköld">Sköld</a> (shield) and Stolt (proud). Those names were originally assigned to soldiers under the military allotment system in effect from the 16th century. As in Denmark, the clergy Latinized their names up to about the 18th century, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus" title="Carl Linnaeus">Linnaeus</a>. Due to the greater diversity of these names each specific name is less common than most patronymic names.
