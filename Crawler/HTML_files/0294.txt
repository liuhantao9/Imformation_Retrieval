	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Korean name</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For names of the country, see <a href="/wiki/Names_of_Korea" title="Names of Korea">Names of Korea</a>.</div>
<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Naming customs of Korean culture</div><table class="infobox" style="width: 22em"><tbody><tr><td colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-size: 125%; background-color: #b0c4de;">Korean name</td></tr><tr><th scope="row" style="font-weight: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Hangul</a></th><td><div style="display:inline;font-size:1rem;"><span lang="ko-Hang" title="Korean language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%EC%9D%B4%EB%A6%84" class="extiw" title="wikt:이름">이름</a> / <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%EC%84%B1%EB%AA%85" class="extiw" title="wikt:성명">성명</a></span></div></td></tr><tr><th scope="row" style="font-weight: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">Hanja</a></th><td><div style="display:inline;font-size:1rem;"><span lang="ko-Hani" title="Korean language text">이름 / <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A7%93%E5%90%8D" class="extiw" title="wikt:姓名">姓名</a></span></div></td></tr><tr><th scope="row" style="font-weight: normal;"><a href="/wiki/Revised_Romanization_of_Korean" title="Revised Romanization of Korean">Revised Romanization</a></th><td><i lang="ko-Latn" title="Revised Romanization of Korean transliteration">ireum /<br />seongmyeong</i></td></tr><tr><th scope="row" style="font-weight: normal;"><a href="/wiki/McCune%E2%80%93Reischauer" title="McCune–Reischauer">McCune–Reischauer</a></th><td><i lang="ko-Latn" title="McCune–Reischauer transliteration">irŭm / sŏngmyŏng</i></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>A <b>Korean name</b> consists of a <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a> followed by a <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>, as used by the <a href="/wiki/Korean_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Korean people">Korean people</a> in both <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean language</a>, <i>ireum</i> or <i>seongmyeong</i> usually refers to the family name (<i>seong</i>) and given name (<i>ireum</i> in a narrow sense) together.
</p><p>Traditional Korean family names typically consist of only one syllable.  There is no <a href="/wiki/Middle_name" title="Middle name">middle name</a> in the English language sense.  Many Koreans have their given names made of a <a href="/wiki/Generation_name" title="Generation name">generational name</a> syllable and an individually distinct syllable, though this practice is declining in the younger generations.  The generational name syllable is shared by siblings in <a href="/wiki/North_Korea" title="North Korea">North Korea</a>, and by all members of the same generation of an extended family in <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>. Married men and women keep their full personal names, and children inherit the father's family name unless otherwise settled when registering the marriage.
</p><p>The family names are subdivided into <i><a href="/wiki/Bon-gwan" title="Bon-gwan">bon-gwan</a></i> (clans), i.e. extended families which originate in the lineage system used in previous historical periods. Each clan is identified by a specific place, and traces its origin to a common <a href="/wiki/Patrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrilineal">patrilineal</a> ancestor.
</p><p>Early names based on the <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean language</a> were recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms_of_Korea" title="Three Kingdoms of Korea">Three Kingdoms</a> period (57 BCE – 668 CE), but with the growing adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_writing_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese writing system">Chinese writing system</a>, these were gradually replaced by names based on <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese characters</a> (<a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">hanja</a>). During periods of <a href="/wiki/Mongol" class="mw-redirect" title="Mongol">Mongol</a> influence, the ruling class supplemented their Korean names with <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_name" title="Mongolian name">Mongolian names</a>.
</p><p>Because of the many changes in <a href="/wiki/Korean_romanization" class="mw-redirect" title="Korean romanization">Korean romanization practices</a> over the years, modern Koreans, when using <a href="/wiki/List_of_languages_by_writing_system#Latin_script" title="List of languages by writing system">languages written in Latin script</a>, romanize their names in various ways, most often approximating the pronunciation in <a href="/wiki/English_orthography" title="English orthography">English orthography</a>. Some keep the original order of names, while others reverse the names to match the usual <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western</a> pattern.
</p><p>According to the population and housing census of 2000 conducted by the South Korean government, there are a total of 286 <a href="/wiki/List_of_Korean_surnames" title="List of Korean surnames">surnames</a> and 4,179 <a href="/wiki/Bon-gwan" title="Bon-gwan">clans</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Fewer than 300 (approximately 280)<sup id="cite_ref-HanCinema_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HanCinema-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Korean family names were in use in 2000, and the three most common (<a href="/wiki/Kim_(Korean_surname)" title="Kim (Korean surname)">Kim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lee_(Korean_surname)" title="Lee (Korean surname)">Lee</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Park_(Korean_surname)" title="Park (Korean surname)">Park</a>) account for nearly half of the population. For various reasons, there is a growth in the number of Korean surnames.<sup id="cite_ref-HanCinema_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HanCinema-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-USLC_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USLC-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Each family name is divided into one or more clans (<i><a href="/wiki/Bon-gwan" title="Bon-gwan">bon-gwan</a></i>), identifying the clan's city of origin. For example, the most populous clan is <a href="/wiki/Kim_(Korean_surname)" title="Kim (Korean surname)">Gimhae Kim</a>; that is, the <a href="/wiki/Kim_(Korean_surname)" title="Kim (Korean surname)">Kim</a> clan from the city of <a href="/wiki/Gimhae" title="Gimhae">Gimhae</a>. Clans are further subdivided into various <i>pa</i>, or branches stemming from a more recent common ancestor, so that a full identification of a person's family name would be clan-surname-branch. For example, "Gyeongju Yissi" also romanized as "Gyeongju Leessi" (<a href="/wiki/Gyeongju" title="Gyeongju">Gyeongju</a> Lee clan, or Lee clan of Gyeongju) and "Yeonan-Yissi" (Lee clan of <a href="/wiki/Yeonan" class="mw-redirect" title="Yeonan">Yeonan</a>) are, technically speaking, completely different surnames, even though both are, in most places, simply referred to as "Yi" or "Lee". This also means people from the same clan are considered to be of same blood, such that marriage of a man and a woman of same surname and <i>bon-gwan</i> is considered a strong taboo, regardless of how distant the actual lineages may be, even to the present day.
</p><p>Traditionally, Korean women keep their family names after their marriage, but their children take the father's surname. In the premodern, patriarchal Korean society, people were extremely conscious of familial values and their own family identities. Korean women keep their surnames after marriage based on traditional reasoning that it is inherited from their parents and ancestors, and cannot be changed. According to traditions, each clan publishes a comprehensive genealogy (<i><a href="/wiki/Jokbo" class="mw-redirect" title="Jokbo">jokbo</a></i>) every 30 years.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Around a dozen two-syllable surnames are used, all of which rank after the 100 most common surnames. The five most common family names, which together make up over half of the Korean population, are used by over 20 million people in South Korea.<sup id="cite_ref-nso_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nso-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the 2015 census, it was revealed that foreign-origin family names were becoming more common in South Korea, due to naturalised citizens transcribing their surnames in <a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">hangul</a>. Between 2000 and 2015, more than 4,800 new surnames were registered. During the census, a total of 5,582 distinct surnames were collected, 73% of which do not have corresponding <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">hanja</a> characters. It was also revealed that despite the surge in the number of surnames, the ratio of top 10 surnames had not changed. 44.6% of South Koreans are still named Kim, Lee or Park, while the rest of the top 10 are made up of Choi, Jeong, Kang, Jo, Yoon, Jang and Lim.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Given_names">Given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Korean_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Korean_given_names" title="List of Korean given names">List of Korean given names</a></div>
<p>Traditionally, given names are partly determined by <a href="/wiki/Generation_name" title="Generation name">generation names</a>, a custom originating in China. One of the two characters in a given name is unique to the individual, while the other is shared by all people in a family generation. In both North and South Korea, generational names are usually no longer shared by cousins, but are still commonly shared by brothers and sisters.<sup id="cite_ref-northnames_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-northnames-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Given names are typically composed of <i><a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">hanja</a></i>, or Chinese characters. In North Korea, the <i>hanja</i> are no longer used to write the names, but the meanings are still understood; thus, for example, the syllable <i>cheol</i> (철, <span lang="ko-Hani" title="Korean language text">鐵</span>) is used in boys' names and means "iron".
</p>

<p>In South Korea, section 37 of the Family Registry Law requires that the<i> hanja</i> in personal names be taken from a restricted list.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Unapproved <i>hanja</i> must be represented by <i><a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">hangul</a></i> in the <a href="/wiki/Family_registry" class="mw-redirect" title="Family registry">family registry</a>. In March 1991, the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_South_Korea" title="Supreme Court of South Korea">Supreme Court of South Korea</a> published the <b>Table of Hanja for Personal Name Use</b>, which allowed a total of 2,854 <i>hanja</i> in new South Korean given names (as well as 61 alternative forms).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The list was expanded in 1994, 1997, 2001, 2005, 2007, 2008, 2010, 2013 and 2015. Thus, 8,142 <i>hanja</i> are now permitted in South Korean names (including the <a href="/wiki/Hanmun_gyoyukyong_gicho_hanja" class="mw-redirect" title="Hanmun gyoyukyong gicho hanja">set of basic <i>hanja</i></a>), in addition to a small number of alternative forms.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The use of an official list is similar to Japan's use of the <i><a href="/wiki/Jinmeiy%C5%8D_kanji" title="Jinmeiyō kanji">jinmeiyō kanji</a></i> (although the characters do not entirely coincide).
</p><p>While the traditional practice is still largely followed, since the late 1970s, some parents have given their children names that are native Korean words, usually of two syllables. Popular given names of this sort include <a href="/wiki/Haneul" class="mw-redirect" title="Haneul">Haneul</a> (<span lang="ko-Hang"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%ED%95%98%EB%8A%98" class="extiw" title="wikt:하늘">하늘</a></span>; "Heaven" or "Sky"), <a href="/wiki/Areum_(name)" title="Areum (name)">Areum</a> (<span lang="ko-Hang"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%EC%95%84%EB%A6%84" class="extiw" title="wikt:아름">아름</a></span>; "Beauty"), Iseul (<span lang="ko-Hang"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%EC%9D%B4%EC%8A%AC" class="extiw" title="wikt:이슬">이슬</a></span>; "Dew") and <a href="/wiki/Seulgi" class="mw-redirect" title="Seulgi">Seulgi</a> (<span lang="ko-Hang"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%EC%8A%AC%EA%B8%B0" class="extiw" title="wikt:슬기">슬기</a></span>; "Wisdom").<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Between 2008 and 2015, the proportion of such names among South Korean newborns rose from 3.5% to 7.7%. The most popular such names in 2015 were Han-gyeol (한결) for boys and Sarang (<span lang="ko-Hang"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%EC%82%AC%EB%9E%91" class="extiw" title="wikt:사랑">사랑</a></span>; "Love") for girls.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Despite this trend away from traditional practice, people's names are still recorded in both <i>hangul</i> and <i>hanja</i> (if available) on official documents, in family genealogies, and so on.
</p><p>Originally, there was no legal limitation on the length of names in South Korea. As a result, some people registered extremely long given names composed of native Korean words, such as the 16-syllable Haneulbyeollimgureumhaennimbodasarangseureouri (<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%ED%95%98%EB%8A%98" class="extiw" title="wikt:하늘">하늘</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%EB%B3%84%EB%8B%98" class="extiw" title="wikt:별님">별님</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%EA%B5%AC%EB%A6%84" class="extiw" title="wikt:구름">구름</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%ED%96%87%EB%8B%98" class="extiw" title="wikt:햇님">햇님</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%EB%B3%B4%EB%8B%A4" class="extiw" title="wikt:보다">보다</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%EC%82%AC%EB%9E%91" class="extiw" title="wikt:사랑">사랑</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%EC%8A%A4%EB%9F%AC%EC%9A%B0%EB%A6%AC" class="extiw" title="wikt:스러우리">스러우리</a>; roughly, "More beloved than the stars in the sky and the sun in the clouds"). However, beginning in 1993, new regulations required that the given name be five syllables or shorter.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Korean_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forms_of_address">Forms of address</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Korean_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Forms of address">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Korean_honorifics" title="Korean honorifics">Korean honorifics</a></div>
<p>The usage of names is governed by strict norms in traditional Korean society. It is generally considered rude to address people by their given names in <a href="/wiki/Korean_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Korean culture">Korean culture</a>. This is particularly the case when dealing with adults or one's elders.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It is acceptable to call someone by his or her given name if he or she is the same age as the speaker. However, it is considered rude to use someone's given name if that person's age is a year older than the speaker. This is often a source of <a href="/wiki/Pragmatics" title="Pragmatics">pragmatic</a> difficulty for learners of Korean as a foreign language, and for Korean learners of Western languages.
</p><p>A variety of replacements are used for the actual name of the person. It is acceptable among adults of similar status to address the other by their full name, with the suffix <i>ssi</i> (氏, 씨) added. However, it is inappropriate to address someone by the surname alone, even with such a suffix.<sup id="cite_ref-proper_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-proper-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Whenever the person has an official rank, it is typical to address him or her by the name of that rank (such as "Manager"), often with the honorific <i>nim</i> (님) added. In such cases, the full name of the person may be appended, although this can also imply the speaker is of higher status.<sup id="cite_ref-proper_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-proper-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Among children and close friends, it is common to use a person's birth name.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_nicknames">Traditional nicknames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Korean_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Traditional nicknames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Among the common people, who have suffered from high child mortality, children were often given <i>amyeong</i> (childhood name), to wish them long lives by avoiding notice from the messenger of death.<sup id="cite_ref-Daum_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daum-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> These sometimes-insulting <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nicknames</a> are used sparingly for children today.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After marriage, women usually lost their <i>amyeong</i>, and were called by a <i>taekho</i>, referring to their town of origin.<sup id="cite_ref-Daum_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daum-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition, <a href="/wiki/Teknonymy" title="Teknonymy">teknonymy</a>, or referring to parents by their children's names, is a common practice. It is most commonly used in referring to a mother by the name of her eldest child, as in "Cheolsu's mom" (철수 엄마). However, it can be extended to either parent and any child, depending upon the context.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Korean_names_and_gender">Korean names and gender</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Korean_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Korean names and gender">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Korean given names' correlation to gender is complex, and by comparison to European languages less consistent.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Certain Sino-Korean syllables carry masculine connotations, others feminine, and others unisex.  These connotations may vary depending on whether the character is used as the first or second character in the given name.  A <i><a href="/wiki/Generation_name" title="Generation name">dollimja</a></i> generational marker, once confined to male descendants but now sometimes used for women as well, may further complicate gender identification.  Native Korean given names show similar variation.
</p><p>A further complication in Korean text is that the singular pronoun used to identify individuals has no gender.<sup id="cite_ref-Hee-Geun_Yoon_pp.121-126_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hee-Geun_Yoon_pp.121-126-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>  This means that automated translation often misidentifies or fails to identify individuals' gender in Korean text and thus presents stilted or incorrect English output.  (Conversely, English source text is similarly missing information about social status and age critical to smooth Korean-language rendering.)<sup id="cite_ref-Hee-Geun_Yoon_pp.121-126_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hee-Geun_Yoon_pp.121-126-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Children traditionally take their father’s family name.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Under South Korean Civil Law effective 1 January 2008, though, children may be legally given the last name of either parent or even that of a step-parent.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Korean_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The use of names has evolved over time. The first recording of Korean names appeared as early as in the early <a href="/wiki/Three_Kingdoms_of_Korea" title="Three Kingdoms of Korea">Three Kingdoms period</a>. The adoption of Chinese characters contributed to Korean names.  A complex system, including <a href="/wiki/Courtesy_name" title="Courtesy name">courtesy names</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pen_name" title="Pen name">pen names</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Posthumous_name" title="Posthumous name">posthumous names</a> and childhood names, arose out of <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucian</a> tradition. The courtesy name system in particular arose from the <i><a href="/wiki/Classic_of_Rites" class="mw-redirect" title="Classic of Rites">Classic of Rites</a></i>, a core text of the Confucian canon.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the Three Kingdoms period, native given names were sometimes composed of three syllables like Misaheun (미사흔) and Sadaham (사다함), which were later transcribed into <i><a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">hanja</a></i> (未斯欣, 斯多含). The use of family names was limited to kings in the beginning, but gradually spread to aristocrats and eventually to most of the population.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some recorded family names are apparently native Korean words, such as toponyms. At that time, some characters of Korean names might have been read not by their Sino-Korean pronunciation, but by their native reading. For example, the native Korean name of <a href="/wiki/Yeon_Gaesomun" title="Yeon Gaesomun">Yeon Gaesomun</a> (연개소문; 淵蓋蘇文), the first Grand Prime Minister of <a href="/wiki/Goguryeo" title="Goguryeo">Goguryeo</a>, can linguistically be reconstructed as "Eol Kasum" (/*älkasum/).<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Early <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a> names are also believed to represent <a href="/wiki/Old_Korean" title="Old Korean">Old Korean</a> vocabulary; for example, <a href="/wiki/Bak_Hyeokgeose" class="mw-redirect" title="Bak Hyeokgeose">Bak Hyeokgeose</a>, the name of the founder of Silla, was pronounced something like "Bulgeonuri" (弗矩內), which can be translated as "bright world".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In older traditions, if the name of a baby is not chosen by the third trimester, the responsibility of choosing the name fell to the oldest son of the family. Often, this was the preferred method as the name chosen was seen as good luck.
</p><p>According to the chronicle <i><a href="/wiki/Samguk_Sagi" class="mw-redirect" title="Samguk Sagi">Samguk Sagi</a></i>, family names were bestowed by kings upon their supporters. For example, in 33 CE, King <a href="/wiki/Yuri_of_Silla" title="Yuri of Silla">Yuri</a> gave the six headmen of Saro (later <a href="/wiki/Silla" title="Silla">Silla</a>) the names Lee (이), Bae (배), Choi (최), Jeong (정), Son (손) and Seol (설). However, this account is not generally credited by modern historians, who hold that Confucian-style surnames as above were more likely to have come into general use in the fifth and subsequent centuries, as the Three Kingdoms increasingly adopted the Chinese model.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Only a handful of figures from the Three Kingdoms period are recorded as having borne a <a href="/wiki/Courtesy_name" title="Courtesy name">courtesy name</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Seol_Chong" title="Seol Chong">Seol Chong</a>. The custom only became widespread in the <a href="/wiki/Goryeo" title="Goryeo">Goryeo</a> period, as Confucianism took hold among the literati.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> In 1055, Goryeo established a new law limiting access to the civil service examination to those with family names.<sup id="cite_ref-Daum_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daum-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For men of the aristocratic <i><a href="/wiki/Yangban" title="Yangban">yangban</a></i> class, a complex system of alternate names emerged by the <a href="/wiki/Joseon" title="Joseon">Joseon</a> period. On the other hand, commoners typically only had a first name.<sup id="cite_ref-Daum_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daum-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Surnames were originally a privilege reserved for the <i>yangban</i> class, but members of the <a href="/wiki/Jungin" class="mw-redirect" title="Jungin">middle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sangmin" title="Sangmin">common</a> classes of Joseon society frequently paid to acquire a surname from a <i>yangban</i> and be included into a <a href="/wiki/Bon-gwan" title="Bon-gwan">clan</a>; this practice became rampant by the 18th century,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> leading to a significant growth in the <i>yangban</i> class but conversely diluting and weakening its social dominance.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> For instance, in the region of <a href="/wiki/Daegu" title="Daegu">Daegu</a>, the <i>yangban</i> who had comprised 9.2% of Daegu's demographics in 1690 rose to 18.7% in 1729, 37.5% in 1783, and 70.3% in 1858.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> It was not until the <a href="/wiki/Gabo_Reform" title="Gabo Reform">Gabo Reform</a> of 1894 that members of the <a href="/wiki/Cheonmin" title="Cheonmin">outcast</a> class were allowed to adopt a surname.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> According to a census called the <i>minjeokbu</i> (民籍簿) completed in 1910, more than half of the Korean population did not have a surname at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-Daum_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daum-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For a brief period after the <a href="/wiki/Mongol_invasion_of_Korea" class="mw-redirect" title="Mongol invasion of Korea">Mongol invasion of Korea</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Goryeo_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Goryeo dynasty">Goryeo dynasty</a>, Korean kings and aristocrats had both <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Mongolian names">Mongolian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sino-Korean_vocabulary" title="Sino-Korean vocabulary">Sino-Korean</a> names. The scions of the ruling class were sent to the <a href="/wiki/Yuan_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Yuan Dynasty">Yuan</a> court for schooling.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> For example, King <a href="/wiki/Gongmin_of_Goryeo" title="Gongmin of Goryeo">Gongmin</a> had both the Mongolian name Bayan Temür (伯顏帖木兒) and the Sino-Korean name Wang Gi (王祺) (later renamed Wang Jeon (王顓)).<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/S%C5%8Dshi-kaimei" title="Sōshi-kaimei">Sōshi-kaimei</a></div>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Korea_under_Japanese_rule" title="Korea under Japanese rule">period of Japanese colonial rule</a> of Korea (1910–1945), Koreans were forced to adopt <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>-language names.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1939, as part of <a href="/wiki/Governor-General_of_Korea" title="Governor-General of Korea">Governor-General</a> <a href="/wiki/Jiro_Minami" class="mw-redirect" title="Jiro Minami">Jiro Minami</a>'s policy of cultural assimilation (同化政策; <i>dōka seisaku</i>), Ordinance No. 20 (commonly called the "Name Order", or <i>Sōshi-kaimei</i> (創氏改名) in <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>) was issued, and became law in April 1940.<sup id="cite_ref-doka_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-doka-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Although the Japanese Governor-General officially prohibited compulsion, low-level officials effectively forced Koreans to adopt Japanese-style family and given names. By 1944, about 84% of the population had registered Japanese family names.<sup id="cite_ref-doka_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-doka-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Sōshi</i> (Japanese) means the creation of a Japanese family name (<i>shi</i>, Korean <i>ssi</i>), distinct from a Korean family name or <i>seong</i> (Japanese <i>sei</i>). Japanese family names represent the families they belong to and can be changed by marriage and other procedures, while Korean family names represent paternal linkages and are unchangeable. Japanese policy dictated that Koreans either could register a completely new Japanese family name unrelated to their Korean surname, or have their Korean family name, in Japanese form, automatically become their Japanese name if no surname was submitted before the deadline.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the liberation of Korea from Japanese rule, the Name Restoration Order (조선 성명 복구령; 朝鮮姓名復舊令) was issued on October 23, 1946, by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Military_Government_in_Korea" title="United States Army Military Government in Korea">United States military administration</a> south of the <a href="/wiki/38th_parallel_north" title="38th parallel north">38th parallel north</a>, enabling Koreans to restore their original Korean names if they wished.
</p><p>Japanese conventions of creating given names, such as using "子" (Japanese <i>ko</i> and Korean <i>ja</i>) in feminine names, is seldom seen in present-day Korea, both North and South. In the North, a campaign to eradicate such Japanese-based names was launched in the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-northnames_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-northnames-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In the South, and presumably in the North as well, these names are regarded as old and unsophisticated.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Romanization_and_pronunciation">Romanization and pronunciation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Korean_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Romanization and pronunciation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In English-speaking nations, the three most common family names are often written and pronounced as "Kim" (김), "Lee"(South) or "Rhee"(North) (이, 리), and "Park" (박).
</p><p>The initial sound in "Kim" shares features with both the English 'k' (in initial position, an aspirated voiceless velar stop) and "hard g" (an unaspirated voiced velar stop). When pronounced initially, Kim starts with an unaspirated voiceless <a href="/wiki/Velar_stop" title="Velar stop">velar stop</a> sound; it is voiceless like <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/k/</span>, but also unaspirated like <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɡ/</span>. As aspiration is a distinctive feature in Korean but voicing is not, "Gim" is more likely to be understood correctly. However, "Kim" is used as romanized name in both North and South Korea.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The family name "Lee" is romanized as 리 (<i>ri</i>) in North Korea and as 이 (<i>i</i>) in South Korea. In the former case, the initial sound is a <a href="/wiki/Liquid_consonant" title="Liquid consonant">liquid consonant</a>. There is no distinction between the alveolar liquids <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/l/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span>, which is why "Lee" and "Rhee" are both common spellings. In South Korea, the pronunciation of the name is simply the English vowel sound for a "long e", as in 'see'. This pronunciation is also often spelled as "Yi"; the Northern pronunciation is commonly romanized "Ri".<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Korean, the name that is usually romanized as "Park" actually has no 'r' sound. Its initial sound is an unaspirated <a href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_stop" title="Voiced bilabial stop">voiced bilabial stop</a>, like English 'b' at the beginning of words. The vowel is <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Korean" title="Help:IPA/Korean">[a]</a></span>, similar to the 'a' in <a href="/wiki/Father" title="Father">father</a> and the 'a' in <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a>, so the name is also often transcribed "Pak, "Bak" and "Bahk." <sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many Korean names were romanized incorrectly from their actual pronunciation. For instance, Kim, Lee and Park are pronounced more closer to Ghim, Yi and Bahk in Korea. In order to correct this problem, South Korea's Ministry of Culture, Sports has developed the <a href="/wiki/Revised_Romanization_of_Korean" title="Revised Romanization of Korean">Revised Romanization of Korean</a> to replace the older <a href="/wiki/McCune%E2%80%93Reischauer" title="McCune–Reischauer">McCune–Reischauer</a> system in the year 2000 and now the <a href="/wiki/Official" title="Official">official</a> spelling of these three names has been changed to <a href="/wiki/Kim_(Korean_surname)" title="Kim (Korean surname)">Gim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lee_(Korean_surname)" title="Lee (Korean surname)">I</a> and <a href="/wiki/Park_(Korean_surname)" title="Park (Korean surname)">Bak</a>.
</p><p>South Korea's Ministry of Culture, Sports and Tourism is encouraging those who "newly" register their passports to follow the <a href="/wiki/Revised_Romanization_of_Korean" title="Revised Romanization of Korean">Revised Romanization of Korean</a> if possible, but it is not a mandatory and people are allowed to register their English name freely given that it's their first registration.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Korean_names_in_English">Korean names in English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Korean_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Korean names in English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ban_Ki-Moon_Davos_2011.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Ban_Ki-Moon_Davos_2011.jpg/220px-Ban_Ki-Moon_Davos_2011.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Ban_Ki-Moon_Davos_2011.jpg/330px-Ban_Ki-Moon_Davos_2011.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Ban_Ki-Moon_Davos_2011.jpg/440px-Ban_Ki-Moon_Davos_2011.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5244" data-file-height="3690" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ban_Ki-Moon_Davos_2011.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ban_Ki-moon" title="Ban Ki-moon">Ban Ki-moon</a> in <a href="/wiki/Davos" title="Davos">Davos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> - the usual presentation of Korean names in English, as shown here, is to put the family name first (Ban is the family name)</div></div></div>
<p>In English publications, usually Korean names are written in the original order, with the family name first and the given name last. This is the case in Western newspapers. Koreans living and working in Western countries have their names in the Western order, with the given name first and the family name last. The usual presentation of Korean names in English is similar to those of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese names">Chinese names</a> and differs from those of <a href="/wiki/Japanese_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese names">Japanese names</a>, where they, in English publications, are usually written in a reversed order with the family name last.<sup id="cite_ref-PowerpC4-2_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PowerpC4-2-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
