<p><b>José Manuel Durão Barroso</b> (<small>Portuguese:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Portuguese" title="Help:IPA/Portuguese">[ʒuˈzɛ mɐˈnwɛl duˈɾɐ̃w bɐˈʁozu]</a></span>; born 23 March 1956) is a <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_people" title="Portuguese people">Portuguese</a> politician and university teacher, currently serving as non-executive Chairman of <a href="/wiki/Goldman_Sachs_International" class="mw-redirect" title="Goldman Sachs International">Goldman Sachs International</a>. He previously served as the 11th <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_European_Commission" title="President of the European Commission">President of the European Commission</a> and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Prime_Ministers_of_Portugal" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Prime Ministers of Portugal">115th</a> <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Portugal" title="Prime Minister of Portugal">Prime Minister of Portugal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_career">Academic career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jos%C3%A9_Manuel_Barroso&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Academic career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Durão Barroso (as he is known in Portugal<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>) graduated in Law from the Faculty of Law of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Lisbon" title="University of Lisbon">University of Lisbon</a>. He subsequently obtained a Diploma in <a href="/wiki/European_Studies" class="mw-redirect" title="European Studies">European Studies</a> from the <a href="/wiki/European_University_Institute" title="European University Institute">European University Institute</a>, and received a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">MA</a> degree <a href="/wiki/Latin_honors" title="Latin honors">with honours</a> in both Political Science and Social Sciences from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Geneva" title="University of Geneva">University of Geneva</a> in <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>. His academic career continued as an assistant professor in the Faculty of Law of the University of Lisbon. Barroso did <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">PhD research</a> at <a href="/wiki/Georgetown_University" title="Georgetown University">Georgetown University</a> and Georgetown's <a href="/wiki/Edmund_A._Walsh_School_of_Foreign_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="Edmund A. Walsh School of Foreign Service">Edmund A. Walsh School of Foreign Service</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> but his CV does not list any doctoral degree (except honorary).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He is a 1998 graduate of the <a href="/wiki/Georgetown_Leadership_Seminar" title="Georgetown Leadership Seminar">Georgetown Leadership Seminar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Back in <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>, Barroso became director of the Department for International Relations at <a href="/wiki/Lus%C3%ADada_University" title="Lusíada University">Lusíada University</a> (Universidade Lusíada).
</p><p>Barroso is now a policy fellow at the Liechtenstein Institute on Self-Determination at Princeton University<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and the Frederick H. Schultz Class of 1951 Visiting Professor of International Economic Policy at Princeton's Woodrow Wilson School of Public and International Affairs <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Woodrow Wilson School">Woodrow Wilson School</a>, where he teaches with <a href="/wiki/Wolfgang_F._Danspeckgruber" title="Wolfgang F. Danspeckgruber">Wolfgang F. Danspeckgruber</a> on the EU in International Affairs. Barroso also teaches at the <a href="/wiki/Graduate_Institute_of_International_and_Development_Studies" title="Graduate Institute of International and Development Studies">Graduate Institute of International and Development Studies</a> and at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Geneva" title="University of Geneva">University of Geneva</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
At <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20160327114331/http://www.catolicalaw.fd.lisboa.ucp.pt/en">Católica Global School of Law</a>, he teaches since 2015 the seminar on "The Dynamics of European Union Institutions", for both LL.M. programmes – <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20141129083040/http://www.catolicalaw.fd.lisboa.ucp.pt/en/programmes/ll-m-law-in-a-european-and-global-context">Law in a European and Global Context</a> and <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20141129083146/http://www.catolicalaw.fd.lisboa.ucp.pt/en/programmes/ll-m-international-business-law">International Business Law</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_political_career">Early political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jos%C3%A9_Manuel_Barroso&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Barroso's political activity began in his late teens, during the <a href="/wiki/Estado_Novo_(Portugal)" title="Estado Novo (Portugal)">Estado Novo</a> regime in Portugal, before the <a href="/wiki/Carnation_Revolution" title="Carnation Revolution">Carnation Revolution</a> of 25 April 1974. In his university days, he was one of the leaders of the underground <a href="/wiki/Maoism" title="Maoism">Maoist</a> MRPP (Re-Organized Movement of the Proletariat Party, later <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Workers%27_Communist_Party" title="Portuguese Workers&#39; Communist Party">Portuguese Workers' Communist Party</a> (PCTP/MRPP), Communist Party of the Portuguese Workers/Revolutionary Movement of the Portuguese Proletariat).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In an interview with the newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Expresso_(Portuguese_newspaper)" class="mw-redirect" title="Expresso (Portuguese newspaper)">Expresso</a></i>, he said that he had joined MRPP to fight the only other student body movement, also underground, which was controlled by the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Communist_Party" title="Portuguese Communist Party">Portuguese Communist Party</a>. Despite this justification there is a very famous political 1976 interview recorded by the Portuguese state-run television channel, <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A1dio_e_Televis%C3%A3o_de_Portugal" title="Rádio e Televisão de Portugal">RTP</a>, in which Barroso, as a politically minded student during the post-Carnation Revolution turmoil known as <a href="/wiki/Processo_Revolucion%C3%A1rio_Em_Curso" title="Processo Revolucionário Em Curso">PREC</a>, criticises the bourgeois <a href="/wiki/Education_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Education system">education system</a> which "throws students against workers and workers against students."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In December 1980, Barroso joined the right-of-centre PPD (Democratic Popular Party, later PPD/PSD-<a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_(Portugal)" title="Social Democratic Party (Portugal)">Social Democratic Party</a>), where he remains to the present day.
</p><p>In 1985, under the PSD government of <a href="/wiki/An%C3%ADbal_Cavaco_Silva" title="Aníbal Cavaco Silva">Aníbal Cavaco Silva</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_Prime_Ministers_of_Portugal" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Prime Ministers of Portugal">113th</a> <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Portugal" title="Prime Minister of Portugal">Prime Minister of Portugal</a>, Barroso was named Under-Secretary of State in the Ministry of Home Affairs. In 1987 he became a member of the same government as he was elevated to Secretary of State for Foreign Affairs and Cooperation (answering to the Minister of Foreign Affairs), a post he was to hold for the next five years. In this capacity he was the driving force behind the <a href="/wiki/Bicesse_Accords" title="Bicesse Accords">Bicesse Accords</a> of 1990, which led to a temporary armistice in the <a href="/wiki/Angolan_Civil_War" title="Angolan Civil War">Angolan Civil War</a> between the ruling <a href="/wiki/MPLA" title="MPLA">MPLA</a> and the opposition <a href="/wiki/UNITA" title="UNITA">UNITA</a>. He also supported independence for <a href="/wiki/East_Timor" title="East Timor">East Timor</a>, a former Portuguese colony, then a province of <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> by force. In 1992, Barroso was promoted to the post of <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Foreign_Affairs_(Portugal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Minister of Foreign Affairs (Portugal)">Minister of Foreign Affairs</a>, and served in this capacity until the defeat of the PSD in the 1995 general election.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prime_Minister_of_Portugal">Prime Minister of Portugal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jos%C3%A9_Manuel_Barroso&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prime Minister of Portugal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While in opposition, Barroso was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Assembly_of_the_Republic_(Portugal)" title="Assembly of the Republic (Portugal)">Assembly of the Republic</a> in 1995 as a representative for <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>. There, he became chairman of the Foreign Affairs Committee. In 1999 he was elected president of his political party, PSD, succeeding <a href="/wiki/Marcelo_Rebelo_de_Sousa" title="Marcelo Rebelo de Sousa">Marcelo Rebelo de Sousa</a> (a professor of law), and thus became Leader of the Opposition. <a href="/wiki/2002_Portuguese_legislative_election" title="2002 Portuguese legislative election">Parliamentary elections</a> in 2002 gave the PSD enough seats to form a coalition government with the right-wing Portuguese <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Party_(Portugal)" class="mw-redirect" title="People&#39;s Party (Portugal)">People's Party</a>, and Barroso subsequently became Prime Minister of Portugal on 6 April 2002.
</p><p>As Prime Minister, facing a growing budget deficit, he made a number of difficult decisions and adopted strict reforms. He vowed to reduce public expenditure, which made him unpopular among leftists and public servants.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. His purpose was to lower the public budget deficit to a 3% target (according to the demands of EU rules), and official data during the 2002–2004 period stated that the target was being attained.
</p>

<p>In March 2003, Barroso hosted U.S President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>, British Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a> and Spanish Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mar%C3%ADa_Aznar" title="José María Aznar">José María Aznar</a> in the Portuguese island of Terceira, in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>. The four leaders finalised the controversial US-led <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">2003 invasion of Iraq</a>. Under Barroso's leadership, Portugal became part of the "<a href="/wiki/Coalition_of_the_willing" title="Coalition of the willing">coalition of the willing</a>" for the invasion and <a href="/wiki/History_of_Iraq_(2003%E2%80%932011)" title="History of Iraq (2003–2011)">occupation of Iraq</a>, sending non-combat troops.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> On 30 January 2003, Barroso signed <i><a href="/wiki/The_letter_of_the_eight" title="The letter of the eight">The letter of the eight</a></i> supporting <a href="/wiki/Preparations_for_2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="Preparations for 2003 invasion of Iraq">US. policy on Iraq</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Barroso did not finish his term as he had been nominated as <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_European_Commission" title="President of the European Commission">President of the European Commission</a> on 5 July 2004. Barroso arranged with Portuguese President <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Sampaio" title="Jorge Sampaio">Jorge Sampaio</a> to nominate <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Santana_Lopes" title="Pedro Santana Lopes">Pedro Santana Lopes</a> as a substitute Prime Minister of Portugal. Santana Lopes led the PSD/PP coalition for a few months until early 2005, when new elections were called. When the Portuguese Socialist Party won the elections it produced an estimation that by the end of the year the budget deficit would reach 6.1%,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> which it used to criticise Barroso's and Santana Lopes's economic policies.
</p>

<p>In 2004, the proposed <a href="/wiki/Treaty_establishing_a_Constitution_for_Europe" title="Treaty establishing a Constitution for Europe">European Constitution</a> and now the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Lisbon" title="Treaty of Lisbon">Treaty of Lisbon</a> included a provision that the choice of President must take into account the result of <a href="/wiki/Elections_in_the_European_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Elections in the European Union">Parliamentary elections</a> and the candidate supported by the victorious <a href="/wiki/Europarty" class="mw-redirect" title="Europarty">Europarty</a> in particular. That provision was not in force in the nomination in 2004, but the centre-right <a href="/wiki/European_People%27s_Party" title="European People&#39;s Party">European People's Party</a> (EPP), who won the elections, pressured for a candidate from its own ranks. In the end, José Manuel Barroso, the EPP candidate, was chosen by the <a href="/wiki/European_Council" title="European Council">European Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On the same basis, the EPP again endorsed Barroso for a second term during the 2009 European election campaign and, after the EPP again won the elections, was able to secure his nomination by the <a href="/wiki/European_Council" title="European Council">European Council</a> on 17 June 2009. On 3 September 2009, Barroso unveiled his manifesto for his second term.<sup id="cite_ref-José_Manuel_Barroso_unveils_manifesto_for_second_term_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-José_Manuel_Barroso_unveils_manifesto_for_second_term-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> On 16 September 2009, Barroso was re-elected by the European Parliament for another five years.<sup id="cite_ref-José_Manuel_Barroso_wins_second_term_as_European_Commission_President_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-José_Manuel_Barroso_wins_second_term_as_European_Commission_President-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PN_MEPs_welcome_election_of_José_Manuel_Barroso_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PN_MEPs_welcome_election_of_José_Manuel_Barroso-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Since he completed his second term he became only the second Commission president to serve two terms, after <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Delors" title="Jacques Delors">Jacques Delors</a>. That Commission's term of office ran until 31 October 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During his first presidency, the following important issues were on the Commission's agenda:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> applying for EU membership</li>
<li>The reform of the institutions (<a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Lisbon" title="Treaty of Lisbon">Treaty of Lisbon</a>)</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Bolkestein_directive" class="mw-redirect" title="Bolkestein directive">Bolkestein directive</a>, aimed at creating a single market for services within the EU</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lisbon_Strategy" title="Lisbon Strategy">Lisbon Strategy</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Galileo_positioning_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Galileo positioning system">Galileo positioning system</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Doha_Development_Agenda" class="mw-redirect" title="Doha Development Agenda">Doha Development Agenda</a> negotiations</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/European_Institute_of_Innovation_and_Technology" title="European Institute of Innovation and Technology">European Institute of Innovation and Technology</a></li>
<li>An EU climate change package</li></ul>

Addressing the EU parliament in Strasbourg, Mr Barroso said such a move was necessary to combat the continent's economic crisis. He said he believed Greece would be able to stay in the eurozone if it stood by its commitments. Mr Barroso also set out plans for a single supervisory mechanism for all banks in the eurozone.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jos%C3%A9_Manuel_Barroso&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2005, <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Welt" title="Die Welt">Die Welt</a></i> reported that Barroso had spent a week on the yacht of the Greek shipping billionaire <a href="/wiki/Spiro_Latsis" class="mw-redirect" title="Spiro Latsis">Spiro Latsis</a>. It emerged soon afterwards that this had occurred only a month before the Commission approved 10&#160;million euros of Greek state aid for Latsis's shipping company – though the state aid decision had been taken by the previous European Commission before Barroso took up his post.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In response to this revelation, <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Farage" title="Nigel Farage">Nigel Farage</a> MEP of the <a href="/wiki/UK_Independence_Party" title="UK Independence Party">UK Independence Party</a> persuaded around 75 MEPs from across the political spectrum to back a motion of no confidence in Barroso, so as to compel him to appear before the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> to be questioned on the matter.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The motion was tabled on 12 May 2005, and Barroso appeared before Parliament as required at a debate on 26 May 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The motion itself was heavily defeated.
</p><p>In response to criticism for his choice of a less fuel efficient <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Touareg" title="Volkswagen Touareg">Volkswagen Touareg</a>, amid EU legislation of targets drastically to reduce car <span class="nowrap"><span class="chemf nowrap">CO<span style="display:inline-block; margin-bottom:-0.3em; vertical-align:-0.4em; line-height:1.2em; font-size:70%; text-align:left;"><br />2</span></span></span> emissions, Barroso dismissed this as "overzealous moralism".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2008, amid sharp food price rises and mounting <a href="/wiki/Food_vs_fuel" class="mw-redirect" title="Food vs fuel">food vs fuel</a> concerns, Barroso insisted that <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuel</a> use was "not significant" in pushing up food prices.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The following month, he announced a study that would look into the issue.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The backdoor approval of the GE potato, by President Barroso, has met a wave of strong opposition from EU member-states. The governments of Greece, Austria, Luxembourg, Italy, Hungary and France have all publicly announced that they will not allow the GE potato to be grown in their countries.
</p><p>Barroso has expressed criticism of national governments arguing "Decisions taken by the most democratic institutions in the world are very often wrong."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2013 Barroso said that Europe was not the cause of the problems for Ireland; Ireland caused a problem for Europe. Following the bailout exit, in December 2013, the Irish government's bid to get backdated funding for the banking sector was rejected as the head of the European Commission blamed the Irish banks, regulators and government for the difficulties in the country. Barroso said the problems in the Irish banks caused a "major destabilisation" in the euro, rather than structural problems with the currency itself, "I am saying this because it would be wrong to give the impression that Europe has created a problem for Ireland and now Europe has to help Ireland. In fact, it was the banking sector in Ireland—it was one of the biggest problems in the world in terms of banking stability what happened in Ireland."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Barroso was heavily criticised for taking a position as a chairman and senior adviser to the international arm of Goldman Sachs. He did so two months after the 18 month “cooling-off” period for EU officials after they leave their posts. Barroso's move was especially sensitive because Goldman Sachs is an American institution that played a questionable role in the financial crisis that nearly broke the euro. Furthermore, Barroso announced his move to the London-based subsidiary of Goldman Sachs shortly after the Brexit referendum. The European Commission agreed to an unprecedented ethics inquiry into the move. The independent panel concluded there were “not sufficient grounds to establish a violation of the duty of integrity and discretion” and accepted Barroso’s assurances that he would not be lobbying on behalf of the bank’s clients."<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

He will also be an adviser to the bank.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> This position has been regarded as quite controversial, and has led Barroso's successor <a href="/wiki/Jean-Claude_Juncker" title="Jean-Claude Juncker">Jean-Claude Juncker</a> to launch an ethics investigation.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jos%C3%A9_Manuel_Barroso&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>José Manuel Durão Barroso is the son of Luís António Saraiva Barroso and his wife Maria Elisabete de Freitas Durão. In 1980 he married Maria Margarida Pinto Ribeiro de Sousa Uva, with whom he has three sons. Sousa Uva died from <a href="/wiki/Uterine_cancer" title="Uterine cancer">uterine cancer</a> in 2016, at the age of 60.
</p><p>Apart from Portuguese, Barroso is fluent in French,<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> speaks Spanish and English and has taken a course to acquire a basic knowledge of German.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
