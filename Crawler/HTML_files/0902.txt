<p><b>Keighley</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: style="font-size:85%">(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:En-uk-Keighley.ogg" title="About this sound"><img 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a></span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/59/En-uk-Keighley.ogg" class="internal" the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Bradford" title="City of Bradford">City of Bradford</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, England, 11 miles (18&#160;km) north-west of <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a>, 11 miles (18&#160;km) south of <a href="/wiki/Ilkley" title="Ilkley">Ilkley</a>, 13 miles (21&#160;km), north of <a href="/wiki/Halifax,_West_Yorkshire" title="Halifax, West Yorkshire">Halifax</a>, 12 miles (19&#160;km) south-east of <a href="/wiki/Skipton" title="Skipton">Skipton</a>, and 20 miles (32&#160;km) north-west of <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Confluence" title="Confluence">confluence</a> of the rivers <a href="/wiki/River_Aire" title="River Aire">Aire</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Worth" title="River Worth">Worth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NatStatsKeighleyCP_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NatStatsKeighleyCP-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">Historically</a> in the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>, Keighley lies between <a href="/wiki/Airedale" title="Airedale">Airedale</a> and Keighley Moors. The town is the terminus of the <a href="/wiki/Keighley_and_Worth_Valley_Railway" title="Keighley and Worth Valley Railway">Keighley and Worth Valley Railway</a>, a heritage steam branch line which has been restored and runs through the <a href="/wiki/Worth_Valley" title="Worth Valley">Worth Valley</a> to <a href="/wiki/Oxenhope" title="Oxenhope">Oxenhope</a> via <a href="/wiki/Oakworth" title="Oakworth">Oakworth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Haworth" title="Haworth">Haworth</a>. At the 2011 census, Keighley had a population of 56,348.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponymy">Toponymy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keighley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Toponymy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name Keighley, which has gone through many changes of spelling throughout its history, means "Cyhha's farm or clearing",<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and was mentioned in the <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> as "In Cichhelai, Ulchel, and Thole, and Ravensuar, and William had six carucates to be taxed."
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keighley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Henry de Keighley, a <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a> <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knight</a>, was granted a charter to hold a market in Keighley on 17 October 1305 by <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">King Edward I</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-marketcharter_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marketcharter-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Tax_per_head" class="mw-redirect" title="Tax per head">poll tax</a> records of 1379 show that the population of Keighley, in the <a href="/wiki/Wapentake" class="mw-redirect" title="Wapentake">wapentake</a> of <a href="/wiki/Staincliffe_(wapentake)" class="mw-redirect" title="Staincliffe (wapentake)">Staincliffe</a> in the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>, was 109 people (47 couples and 15 single people).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1753 the Union stage coach departed on the <a href="/wiki/Keighley_and_Kendal_Turnpike" title="Keighley and Kendal Turnpike">Keighley and Kendal Turnpike</a> from what was the Devonshire Arms coaching inn on the corner of Church Street and High Street. Rebuilt about 1788, this public house has a classical style pedimented doorcase with engaged Tuscan columns in the high fashion of that age. The original route towards <a href="/wiki/Skipton" title="Skipton">Skipton</a> was Spring Gardens Lane – Hollins Lane – Hollins Bank Lane.<sup id="cite_ref-keighley_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-keighley-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Keighley was to become an <a href="/wiki/Intersection_(road)" title="Intersection (road)">intersection</a> with other <a href="/wiki/Toll_road" title="Toll road">turnpikes</a> including the Two-Laws to Keighley branch of the Toller Lane – Blue Bell turnpike (1755) from <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a> to <a href="/wiki/Colne" title="Colne">Colne</a>; the Bradford to Keighley turnpike (1814); and the Keighley—<a href="/wiki/Halifax,_West_Yorkshire" title="Halifax, West Yorkshire">Halifax</a> turnpike.
</p>

Hattersley">Geo. Hattersley</a>, Keighley on display at <a href="/wiki/Queen_Street_Mill" title="Queen Street Mill">Queen Street Mill</a> Textile Museum, Burnley</div></div></div>
<p>The town's industries have typically been in <a href="/wiki/Textiles" class="mw-redirect" title="Textiles">textiles</a>, particularly <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a> processing. In addition to the manufacture of textiles there were several large factories making textile machinery. These included <a href="/wiki/Dean,_Smith_%26_Grace" title="Dean, Smith &amp; Grace">Dean, Smith &amp; Grace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geo._Hattersley" title="Geo. Hattersley">George Hattersley &amp; Son</a> and Prince, Smith &amp; Stell. The first of these operated as a manufacturer of CNC machine tools, particularly precision <a href="/wiki/Lathe" title="Lathe">lathes</a>, until 2008.
</p><p>The 1842 <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> Directory description of Keighley reads "Its parish had no dependent townships though it is about six miles (9.7&#160;km) long and four miles (6.4&#160;km) broad, and comprises 10,160 acres (4,112&#160;ha) of land (including a peaty moor of about 2,000 acres or 800&#160;ha) and a population which amounted, in the year 1801, to 5,745."
</p><p>The town was incorporated as a <a href="/wiki/Municipal_borough" title="Municipal borough">municipal borough</a> on 28 July 1882 under the provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Municipal_Corporations_Act_1882" title="Municipal Corporations Act 1882">Municipal Corporations Act 1882</a> in the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>.    In 1938 the boundaries of the borough and civil parish of Keighley were expanded to include the former urban districts and civil parishes of <a href="/wiki/Haworth" title="Haworth">Haworth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oakworth" title="Oakworth">Oakworth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxenhope" title="Oxenhope">Oxenhope</a> along with the parish of <a href="/wiki/East_Morton" title="East Morton">Morton</a> from the abolished <a href="/w/index.php?title=Keighley_Rural_District&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Keighley Rural District (page does not exist)">Keighley Rural District</a> and a small part of the <a href="/wiki/Bingley" title="Bingley">Bingley</a> urban district.
</p><p>On 1 April 1974 Keighley borough became part of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Bradford" title="City of Bradford">City of Bradford Metropolitan District</a> in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a> in the newly formed county of <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>. The merger caused a lot of bitterness among Keighley people who resented being 'taken over' by Bradford and accused the city's council of neglecting the town.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Civil parish status was restored to Keighley in 2002,<sup id="cite_ref-makeshistory_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-makeshistory-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> providing it with its own <a href="/wiki/Town_Council#United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Town Council">town council</a>. The council's 30 members elect a mayor from amongst their number once a year. The parish boundaries are based on but not identical to the pre-1938 borough boundaries.
</p><p>The town has a local history society, <a href="/wiki/Keighley_and_District_Local_History_Society" title="Keighley and District Local History Society">Keighley and District Local History Society</a>, and a family history society, Keighley and District Family History Society.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Keighley lies at the <a href="/wiki/Confluence" title="Confluence">confluence</a> of the rivers <a href="/wiki/River_Worth" title="River Worth">Worth</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Aire" title="River Aire">Aire</a> in <a href="/wiki/Airedale" title="Airedale">Airedale</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/South_Pennines" title="South Pennines">South Pennines</a>. Its northern boundary is with <a href="/wiki/Bradley,_North_Yorkshire" title="Bradley, North Yorkshire">Bradley</a> and its southern limit is the edge of <a href="/wiki/Oxenhope" title="Oxenhope">Oxenhope</a>. To the west, the town advances up the hill to the suburb of Black Hill and in the east it terminates at the residential neighbourhoods of <a href="/wiki/Long_Lee" title="Long Lee">Long Lee</a> and Thwaites Brow. The outlying northeastern suburb of <a href="/wiki/Riddlesden" title="Riddlesden">Riddlesden</a> is sometimes incorrectly referred to as a separate village but is part of the town.
</p><p>Past Black Hill and via Braithwaite Edge Road lies Braithwaite village which leads to <a href="/wiki/Laycock,_West_Yorkshire" title="Laycock, West Yorkshire">Laycock</a>, which was mentioned in the <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i>. Laycock is a conservation area which overlooks the hamlet of Goose Eye.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/River_Aire" title="River Aire">River Aire</a> passes through north eastern Keighley, dividing the neighbourhood of Stockbridge and running roughly parallel to the <a href="/wiki/Leeds_and_Liverpool_Canal" title="Leeds and Liverpool Canal">Leeds and Liverpool Canal</a>. The Worth links up with the Aire in Stockbridge and runs south-westerly, dividing eastern Keighley from central and western districts of the town. The Worth is lined with abandoned, semi-derelict industrial sites and tracts of waste ground dating from the period when Keighley thrived as a major textile centre.
</p><p>Parts of Keighley are prone to flooding and the town was particularly badly hit in by floods 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Since then, millions have been spent on strengthening flood defences.
</p><p>Other outlying villages around the town are <a href="/wiki/Oakworth" title="Oakworth">Oakworth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cross_Roads,_West_Yorkshire" title="Cross Roads, West Yorkshire">Cross Roads</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haworth" title="Haworth">Haworth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stanbury" title="Stanbury">Stanbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxenhope" title="Oxenhope">Oxenhope</a>. The two main settlements to the north are <a href="/wiki/Silsden" title="Silsden">Silsden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steeton,_West_Yorkshire" title="Steeton, West Yorkshire">Steeton</a>. Although these villages are often referred to as separate places they are part of the wider Keighley area. These areas add a total of 22,669 to the Keighley area, taking the population of the wider Keighley area up to 74,098 (<a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2001" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Census 2001">2001 Census</a>).
</p><p>To the north east is <a href="/wiki/Rombalds_Moor" title="Rombalds Moor">Rombalds Moor</a> which contains many signs of <a href="/wiki/Stone_age" class="mw-redirect" title="Stone age">stone age</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bronze_age" class="mw-redirect" title="Bronze age">bronze age</a> occupation including <a href="/wiki/Cup_and_ring_mark" title="Cup and ring mark">cup and ring marks</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and as it drops back down into <a href="/wiki/Wharfedale" title="Wharfedale">Wharfedale</a> and the town of <a href="/wiki/Ilkley" title="Ilkley">Ilkley</a>, approximately five miles away, becomes the more famous <a href="/wiki/Ilkley_Moor" title="Ilkley Moor">Ilkley Moor</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amenities">Amenities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keighley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Amenities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Much of the town centre has been pedestrianised. Keighley has three large supermarkets, Morrisons, Sainsbury's and Asda. The Airedale shopping centre, is a large indoor <a href="/wiki/Shopping_mall" title="Shopping mall">shopping precinct</a> which houses most of the town's high street retail chains. There are several budget supermarkets situated in small retail parks around the town. Keighley benefits from an <a href="/wiki/Railway_electrification_system" title="Railway electrification system">electrified railway</a> service with connections to Leeds, Bradford, Shipley, Bingley, Skipton, Carlisle and Morecambe. The <a href="/wiki/Keighley_and_Worth_Valley_railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Keighley and Worth Valley railway">Keighley and Worth Valley railway</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Heritage_railway" title="Heritage railway">heritage steam railway</a>, which links the town with Haworth, Oakworth, Oxenhope and the Bronte Country. Keighley has one cinema, <a href="/wiki/Keighley_Picture_House" title="Keighley Picture House">The Picture House</a> on North Street which was restored from derelict condition in 1996 by Northern Morris Associated Cinemas.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keighley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:All_Saints_Church_-_Highfield_Lane_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1008149.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/All_Saints_Church_-_Highfield_Lane_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1008149.jpg/220px-All_Saints_Church_-_Highfield_Lane_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1008149.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/All_Saints_Church_-_Highfield_Lane_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1008149.jpg/330px-All_Saints_Church_-_Highfield_Lane_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1008149.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/All_Saints_Church_-_Highfield_Lane_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1008149.jpg/440px-All_Saints_Church_-_Highfield_Lane_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1008149.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="496" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:All_Saints_Church_-_Highfield_Lane_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1008149.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>All Saints Church</div></div></div>
<p>Keighley has a <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish church</a> Keighley Shared Church<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and is home to many <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> denominations. It has churches and places of worship for <a href="/wiki/Anglicans" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglicans">Anglicans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methodists" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodists">Methodists</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Reformed_Church" title="United Reformed Church">United Reformed</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mormons" title="Mormons">Mormons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quakers" title="Quakers">Quakers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salvation_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="Salvation Army">Salvation Army</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah&#39;s Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a>. Keighley has a significant <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> minority re-established following the repeal of the penal laws. The Catholic population was boosted in the mid-19th century with the arrival of Irish immigrants escaping the 1840s <a href="/wiki/Great_Famine_(Ireland)" title="Great Famine (Ireland)">potato famine</a> who came to work in the textile and weaving industries. Keighley has two Roman Catholic churches (St Anne's – 1840 and St Joseph's – 1934) and four <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> schools (St Anne's – 1857, St Joseph's – 1922, Our Lady of Victories – 1960 and Holy Family – 1964).
</p><p>The first <a href="/wiki/Spiritualists%27_National_Union" title="Spiritualists&#39; National Union">spiritualist</a> church in Britain was founded at Keighley in 1853 by David Richmond,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> who although not originally from the town, stayed for many years and helped to establish the movement throughout the country. Spiritualism was at its height during Victorian times and Keighley Spiritualist church remains open.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Emily_Street_Mosque_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1401593.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Emily_Street_Mosque_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1401593.jpg/220px-Emily_Street_Mosque_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1401593.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Emily_Street_Mosque_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1401593.jpg/330px-Emily_Street_Mosque_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1401593.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Emily_Street_Mosque_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1401593.jpg/440px-Emily_Street_Mosque_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1401593.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Emily_Street_Mosque_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1401593.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Emily Street Mosque</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslims</a> make up the second largest religious group in the town. According to the 2011 census there were more than 12,400 Muslims in Keighley in March of that year. Most had started coming to Britain in the 1960s from the <a href="/wiki/Mirpur_District" title="Mirpur District">Mirpur</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Azad_Kashmir" title="Azad Kashmir">Azad Kashmir</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Sylhet" title="Sylhet">Sylhet</a> region of <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>. As of 2013 there were eight mosques in Keighley, including the purpose-built Markazi Jamia Masjid ('Central Community Mosque') in Emily Street, the Ghosia Mosque, in Cark Road, the Shahjalal Jamia Mosque, on Temple Row, and the Bait al-Aman Ahmadiyya Mosque, at the junction of Worth Way and Longcroft.
</p><p>There is a <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhist</a> centre on Lawkholme Crescent, in the town centre. The Keighley Kadampa Buddhist Centre is used by lay and ordained Buddhist practitioners and also runs day and evening classes for newcomers to the faith.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Architecture">Architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keighley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like many other British towns and cities, Keighley was extensively remodelled in the 1960s and lost many historic buildings. However, the town managed to retain some of its heritage and has many <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a> buildings. The local <a href="/wiki/Gritstone" title="Gritstone">millstone grit</a> gives many of the buildings a distinctive look.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/East_Riddlesden_Hall" title="East Riddlesden Hall">East Riddlesden Hall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cliffe_Castle_Museum" title="Cliffe Castle Museum">Cliffe Castle Museum</a> and Whinburn Mansion are fine, <a href="/wiki/Country_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Country house">country houses</a>. There are large town houses along <a href="/wiki/Skipton" title="Skipton">Skipton</a> Road which contrast with the cramped rows of terraces in the streets behind them.
</p><p>The town's central library was the first <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_library" title="Carnegie library">Carnegie library</a> in England opened in 1904 with a grant of £10,000 from <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Carnegie" title="Andrew Carnegie">Andrew Carnegie</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The library has undergone refurbishment which was completed in 2007. Many of the town's former mill buildings are still intact.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flats_in_Keighley.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Flats_in_Keighley.jpg/220px-Flats_in_Keighley.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Flats_in_Keighley.jpg/330px-Flats_in_Keighley.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Flats_in_Keighley.jpg/440px-Flats_in_Keighley.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flats_in_Keighley.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>High-Rise Flats in Keighley</div></div></div>
<p>The town centre contains modern buildings such as Leeds City College and examples of Victorian commercial architecture, including the long terrace of Cavendish Street with its 220-yard (⅛ mile/a <a href="/wiki/Furlong" title="Furlong">furlong</a>) ornamental canopy. There is an award-winning bus station which opened in 2002 near the Airedale Shopping Centre.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> There are several <a href="/wiki/Tower_block" class="mw-redirect" title="Tower block">tower blocks</a> in Parkwood Rise, Holycroft and <a href="/wiki/Ingrow" title="Ingrow">Ingrow</a> and a central <a href="/wiki/Multi-storey_car_park" class="mw-redirect" title="Multi-storey car park">multi-storey car park</a>.
</p><p>Amongst the modern houses in Laycock, 2 miles (3.2&#160;km) outside Keighley town centre is a 17th-century three-storey <a href="/wiki/Manor_house" title="Manor house">manor house</a> (which is said to be the former wing of a much bigger property), converted barns and 18th-century cottages.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_attractions">Local attractions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keighley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Local attractions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On the outskirts of town is Cliffe Hall, also known as Cliffe Castle, now <a href="/wiki/Cliffe_Castle_Museum" title="Cliffe Castle Museum">Keighley Museum</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Keighley is the location of the <a href="/wiki/Keighley_and_Worth_Valley_Railway" title="Keighley and Worth Valley Railway">Keighley and Worth Valley Railway</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Heritage_railway" title="Heritage railway">heritage railway</a> that passes through <a href="/wiki/Haworth" title="Haworth">Haworth</a> (part of the <a href="/wiki/Bront%C3%AB_Country" title="Brontë Country">Brontë Country</a>, home of <a href="/wiki/Anne_Bront%C3%AB" title="Anne Brontë">Anne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Bront%C3%AB" title="Charlotte Brontë">Charlotte</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emily_Bront%C3%AB" title="Emily Brontë">Emily Brontë</a>) and terminates at <a href="/wiki/Oxenhope" title="Oxenhope">Oxenhope</a>. At Ingrow is the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Rail_Travel" title="Museum of Rail Travel">Museum of Rail Travel</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Top_Withens" title="Top Withens">Top Withens</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bront%C3%AB_Waterfall" title="Brontë Waterfall">Brontë Waterfall</a> are within walking distance of <a href="/wiki/Stanbury" title="Stanbury">Stanbury</a>, a mile and a half from Haworth. <a href="/wiki/East_Riddlesden_Hall" title="East Riddlesden Hall">East Riddlesden Hall</a> is in Riddlesden. Keighley Police Museum is in the Keighley Civic Centre opposite the Town Square. It is the old police station and has many pieces of police memorabilia, including a Victorian horse-drawn Black Mariah.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keighley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Local high schools are <a href="/wiki/University_Academy_Keighley" title="University Academy Keighley">University Academy Keighley</a> in <a href="/wiki/Utley,_West_Yorkshire" title="Utley, West Yorkshire">Utley</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Oakbank_School,_Keighley" title="Oakbank School, Keighley">Oakbank School</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Parkside_School,_Cullingworth" title="Parkside School, Cullingworth">Parkside School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cullingworth" title="Cullingworth">Cullingworth</a> <sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Family_Catholic_School,_Keighley" title="Holy Family Catholic School, Keighley">Holy Family Catholic School</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Keighley College, the local campus of <a href="/wiki/Leeds_City_College" title="Leeds City College">Leeds City College</a>, formerly known as Park Lane College, is situated near <a href="/wiki/Keighley_railway_station" title="Keighley railway station">Keighley railway station</a> on Bradford Road.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In 2010, the college opened this new £30 million campus, moving away from the former site on Cavendish Street which was in need of repair, and has since been demolished. The college includes an Industrial Centre of Excellence and a nationally acclaimed Star Centre facility,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> designed to encourage more young people to study <a href="/wiki/STEM_fields" class="mw-redirect" title="STEM fields">STEM</a> subjects (science, technology, engineering, and mathematics). This features a mock <a href="/wiki/Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr._Mission_Control_Center" title="Christopher C. Kraft Jr. Mission Control Center">mission control centre</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Planetarium" title="Planetarium">planetarium</a>, a simulated rocky <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a> surface and many other <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">space</a>-related items.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport_and_entertainment">Sport and entertainment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keighley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sport and entertainment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Keighley_Cougars" title="Keighley Cougars">Keighley Cougars</a> RLFC are a semi-professional rugby league team based at Royd Ings Avenue, they play in the <a href="/wiki/Co-operative_Championship" class="mw-redirect" title="Co-operative Championship">Co-operative Championship</a> continuing a recent history of promotion to and relegation from this league over the last 3 years. The ground's historical/traditional name is <b>Lawkholme Lane</b> but has more recently been named <a href="/wiki/Cougar_Park" title="Cougar Park">Cougar Park</a>. <a href="/wiki/Keighley_RUFC" title="Keighley RUFC">Keighley RUFC</a> are based at Rose Cottage, Utley and play in Yorkshire league one.
</p><p>Keighley is home to <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Taylor_Brewery" title="Timothy Taylor Brewery">Timothy Taylor Brewery</a>, the makers of <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Real_Ale" title="Campaign for Real Ale">CAMRA</a>, <a href="/wiki/Champion_Beer_of_Britain" title="Champion Beer of Britain">Champion Beer of Britain</a> award-winning ales Landlord and Boltmaker (previously known as 'Best Bitter'). They also brew Ram Tam, Golden Best, Dark Mild and a new French style blonde ale, Le Champion, which was first brewed for the Tour de France in 2014 and has been brewed for the Tour de Yorkshire in 2015 and 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> They also own many pubs in the area, including the Albert Hotel, Boltmakers Arms, Burlington Arms, Lord Rodney and Royal Oak in Keighley.
</p><p>Keighley has a popular local music scene. There have been various venues where local bands play. Most notable was the now defunct CJ's bar (also known as Chrome, VW's, Cheese and Trumpet) that played host to many popular touring bands. Examples of local bands are <a href="/wiki/The_Sailmakers" title="The Sailmakers">the Sailmakers</a>, the Undecided, <a href="/wiki/Foxes_Faux" title="Foxes Faux">Foxes Faux</a>, <a href="/wiki/Random_Hand" title="Random Hand">Random Hand</a>, the Get Guns, Eyesore Angels and Dead Message who recently parted ways after 9 years. The British rock bands <a href="/wiki/Skeletal_Family" title="Skeletal Family">Skeletal Family</a> and <a href="/wiki/Terrorvision" title="Terrorvision">Terrorvision</a> were also originally formed in Keighley.
</p><p>Keighley's <a href="/wiki/Keighley_Picture_House" title="Keighley Picture House">Picture House</a>, a cinema on North Street opened in 1913 making it one of the oldest in Britain. A brief closure in the mid-1990s prevented it from being listed as one of the oldest in continuous operation – a record that goes to the <a href="/wiki/Curzon_Community_Cinema,_Clevedon" title="Curzon Community Cinema, Clevedon">Curzon Cinema</a> which opened in <a href="/wiki/Clevedon" title="Clevedon">Clevedon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a> in 1911.
</p><p>The Airedale Shopping Centre houses the 12-foot (3.7&#160;m) tall statue of the giant Rombald holding a boulder above his head. It was moved to a different part of the centre when a glass ceiling was added to the whole complex. A public consultation favoured placing the statue on the Beechcliffe roundabout on the A650 road approaching Keighley from the east.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> According to local legend the giant Rombald threw a giant rock at his enemies (or in some versions of the tale his wife) killing them. The rock is the "calf" of "cow and calf" rock fame which can be seen today at the top of Rombald's Moor on <a href="/wiki/Ilkley_Moor" title="Ilkley Moor">Ilkley Moor</a>.
</p><p>On 6 July 2014, Stage 2 of the 2014 <a href="/wiki/Tour_de_France" title="Tour de France">Tour de France</a> from <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>, passed through the town. It was also the location of the stage's intermediate sprint after 42.6 miles (68.6&#160;km). The 20 points for the Points jersey was claimed by <a href="/wiki/Blel_Kadri" title="Blel Kadri">Blel Kadri</a> of AG2R La Mondiale.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_film">In film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keighley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: In film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Keighley was the setting for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Blow_Dry" title="Blow Dry">Blow Dry</a></i> starring <a href="/wiki/Josh_Hartnett" title="Josh Hartnett">Josh Hartnett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Rickman" title="Alan Rickman">Alan Rickman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bill_Nighy" title="Bill Nighy">Bill Nighy</a>. <i>Blow Dry</i> opens with the announcement that the small town of Keighley will host the year 2000 British Hair Championships. Keighley's mayor (<a href="/wiki/Warren_Clarke" title="Warren Clarke">Warren Clarke</a>) is thrilled about the news, but when he announces it to the town's press, they all yawn disapprovingly. The film although set in Keighley was shot in several locations.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 2004 documentary Edge of the City, about the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Bradford" title="City of Bradford">City of Bradford</a> Social Services, and the people and problems they deal with, was partly filmed in Keighley, and concerned sexual abuse of underage <a href="/wiki/White_British" title="White British">white</a> girls by some <a href="/wiki/British_Asian" title="British Asian">Asian</a> men.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most of the 2004 film <i><a href="/wiki/Yasmin_(2004_film)" title="Yasmin (2004 film)">Yasmin</a></i> was shot in Keighley. Written by <a href="/wiki/Simon_Beaufoy" title="Simon Beaufoy">Simon Beaufoy</a> and mostly filmed in Lawkholme, it tells the story of a British Muslim woman who has her life disrupted by the impact of the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a> on America. Beaufoy said the film was originally set in <a href="/wiki/Oldham" title="Oldham">Oldham</a>, but "worked its way across the Pennines".<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Keighley_and_Worth_Valley_Railway" title="Keighley and Worth Valley Railway">Keighley and Worth Valley Railway</a> (KWVR), running steam trains from Keighley to <a href="/wiki/Haworth" title="Haworth">Haworth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxenhope" title="Oxenhope">Oxenhope</a>, has been used in several films, including <a href="/wiki/The_Railway_Children_(1970_film)" title="The Railway Children (1970 film)"><i>The Railway Children</i></a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Yanks" title="Yanks">Yanks</a></i>, the film of the <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a> musical <i>The Wall</i> and an episode of the long-running situation comedy, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_of_the_Summer_Wine" class="mw-redirect" title="The Last of the Summer Wine">The Last of the Summer Wine</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/A_Touch_of_Frost_(TV_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="A Touch of Frost (TV series)">A Touch of Frost</a></i> starring <a href="/wiki/David_Jason" title="David Jason">David Jason</a> was also filmed at the railway line close to <a href="/wiki/Ingrow_(West)_railway_station" title="Ingrow (West) railway station">Ingrow West</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A great part of the 2004 <a href="/wiki/BBC_television_drama" title="BBC television drama">BBC television drama</a> <i><a href="/wiki/North_and_South_(2004_mini-series)" class="mw-redirect" title="North and South (2004 mini-series)">North and South</a></i> was shot on Keighley, with Dalton Mill being one of the serial's main locations.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1950s set British feature film <i><a href="/wiki/Between_Two_Women_(2000_film)" title="Between Two Women (2000 film)">Between Two Women</a></i> (2000) was filmed extensively in and around Keighley and its mills, in particular around the railway and close to the main town railway station.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The same director's next film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jealous_God_(film)" title="The Jealous God (film)">The Jealous God</a></i> (2005) also featured <a href="/wiki/Keighley_railway_station" title="Keighley railway station">Keighley railway station</a> and nearby streets.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The film <a href="/wiki/God%27s_Own_Country_(2017_film)" title="God&#39;s Own Country (2017 film)">God's Own Country</a> about the young life of a sheep farmer was filmed in <a href="/wiki/Laycock,_West_Yorkshire" title="Laycock, West Yorkshire">Laycock</a> and also at Keighley bus station<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Christopher_Ingham">Christopher Ingham</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keighley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Christopher Ingham">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Utley Cemetery contains the grave of Christopher Ingham, a veteran of the conflict against <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>. He was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Wellington" class="mw-redirect" title="Duke of Wellington">Duke of Wellington</a>'s elite 95th Rifle Regiment and fought in ten battles against the French in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> including the <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">Spanish Peninsula War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Waterloo" title="Battle of Waterloo">Battle of Waterloo</a>, for which he was awarded several medals, including the Peninsula Medal. He died in 1866. Some local historians believe Mr Ingham's heroism may have inspired the author <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Cornwell" title="Bernard Cornwell">Bernard Cornwell</a>'s saga about Major <a href="/wiki/Richard_Sharpe_(fictional_character)" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard Sharpe (fictional character)">Richard Sharpe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The TV series episode <i><a href="/wiki/Sharpe%27s_Justice" title="Sharpe&#39;s Justice">Sharpe's Justice</a></i>, which focuses on the roots of the title character, is set in and around Keighley.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hindenburg_parcel">Hindenburg parcel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keighley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Hindenburg parcel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On 22 May 1936, the <a href="/wiki/Zeppelin" title="Zeppelin">Zeppelin</a> <a href="/wiki/Hindenburg-class_airship" title="Hindenburg-class airship">Hindenburg</a> crossed Yorkshire in a diversion of her normal route between the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> As the airship passed over the town, a parcel was dropped and landed in the High Street, where two boys picked it up. The parcel contained a bunch of carnations, a small silver and jet crucifix, some postage stamps, a picture postcard and some Hindenburg notepaper.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The note was written by John P Schulte, who called himself the first flying priest. The note requested that the carnations and crucifix be placed on the grave of his brother, Lieutenant Franz Schulte, who died as a <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Prisoner of War">Prisoner of War</a> in the Keighley area, and who was, at that time, buried at Morton Cemetery 2 miles (3.2&#160;km) east of Keighley (though the letter states he is buried at Skipton, this was incorrect).<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Opinions_about_Keighley">Opinions about Keighley</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keighley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Opinions about Keighley">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town was mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/John_Cooper_Clarke" title="John Cooper Clarke">John Cooper Clarke</a> poem, <i>"<a href="/wiki/Burnley" title="Burnley">Burnley</a>"</i>: "I'll tell you now and I'll tell you briefly, I don't ever want to go to Keighley."
</p><p>In 2003, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Idler_(1993)" title="The Idler (1993)">The Idler</a></i> magazine set up an online poll to decide which were the 50 worst places to live in Britain. The results were published in the book <i><a href="/wiki/Crap_Towns" title="Crap Towns">Crap Towns: The 50 Worst Places to Live in the UK</a></i>. Keighley came in at number 40. Keighley's local newspaper, the <i><a href="/wiki/Keighley_News" title="Keighley News">Keighley News</a></i>, reported the reaction of Councillor Andrew Mallinson, chairman of Keighley Town Centre Management Group: "On the positive side, it's nice to know that out of all the towns in the country, Keighley has got a mention! But on a serious note, as a group, we take any complaints or concerns seriously and are always striving to improve the town centre's image."
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Keighley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Keighley_Town_Hall_-_geograph.org.uk_-_414714.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Keighley_Town_Hall_-_geograph.org.uk_-_414714.jpg/220px-Keighley_Town_Hall_-_geograph.org.uk_-_414714.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Keighley_Town_Hall_-_geograph.org.uk_-_414714.jpg/330px-Keighley_Town_Hall_-_geograph.org.uk_-_414714.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Keighley_Town_Hall_-_geograph.org.uk_-_414714.jpg/440px-Keighley_Town_Hall_-_geograph.org.uk_-_414714.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Keighley_Town_Hall_-_geograph.org.uk_-_414714.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Keighley Town Hall (built in 1902)</div></div></div>
<p>Keighley is represented in the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a> by <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">Member of Parliament</a> (MP) <a href="/wiki/John_Grogan_(British_politician)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Grogan (British politician)">John Grogan</a>, who won the seat at the <a href="/wiki/2017_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="2017 United Kingdom general election">2017 general election</a>. Grogan had a majority of just 249 over the previous incumbent, Conservative <a href="/wiki/Kris_Hopkins" title="Kris Hopkins">Kris Hopkins</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, Hopkins won the seat at the <a href="/wiki/2015_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="2015 United Kingdom general election">2015 general election</a> – securing a second term. Hopkins increased the Conservatives vote share in the area from 41.9%<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> in 2010 to 44.3%<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> in 2015. The Conservatives won the seat in 2010, taking over from <a href="/wiki/Ann_Cryer" title="Ann Cryer">Ann Cryer</a> who had been in office since 1997.
</p><p>Keighley was contested by the <a href="/wiki/British_National_Party" title="British National Party">British National Party</a> (BNP) in the <a href="/wiki/2005_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="2005 United Kingdom general election">May 2005 general election</a> when the party's leader <a href="/wiki/Nick_Griffin" title="Nick Griffin">Nick Griffin</a> stood for Parliament. He was defeated by Ann Cryer, one of a small number of <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> MPs with an increased majority. In March 2006, the town's mayoress, Rose Thompson, announced she had joined the BNP.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2006, the leader of <a href="/wiki/City_of_Bradford" title="City of Bradford">Bradford District Council</a>, Conservative Councillor Kris Hopkins, was quoted in the <i><a href="/wiki/Craven_Herald_%26_Pioneer" title="Craven Herald &amp; Pioneer">Craven Herald &amp; Pioneer</a></i> as suggesting it might be a good idea for Keighley to become an independent authority once again.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Keighley has had a <a href="/wiki/Town_Council#United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Town Council">town council</a> since 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-makeshistory_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-makeshistory-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In 2015, a police probe launched the previous year had developed into a fraud investigation following an external audit report – highlighting a number of financial weaknesses and poor governance in the council's 2012/12 accounts.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A turf war between local drug gangs resulted in the murder of four Asian men in a &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap">5<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span>-month period, from September 2001 to February 2002. Those killed were Yasser Hussain Nazir, Yasser Khan, Zaber Hussain and Qadir Ahmed. Qadir, was stabbed and beaten to death near Victoria Park after being ambushed and chased by rival gang members. The killings sparked a police investigation leading to the arrest of a number of men who were given jail sentences.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Keighley has the unique record of having the first recorded <a href="/wiki/Town_twinning" class="mw-redirect" title="Town twinning">town twinning</a> agreement in the world entering an agreement with <a href="/wiki/Poix-du-Nord" title="Poix-du-Nord">Poix-du-Nord</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> in 1920.<sup id="cite_ref-France_Magazine_-_Twin_Towns_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-France_Magazine_-_Twin_Towns-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lga_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lga-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>  This actually followed an even earlier <a href="/wiki/Sister_city" title="Sister city">sister city</a> arrangement with two communes on the outskirts of <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, France – <a href="/wiki/Suresnes" title="Suresnes">Suresnes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Puteaux" title="Puteaux">Puteaux</a> – starting in 1905.<sup id="cite_ref-France_Magazine_-_Twin_Towns_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-France_Magazine_-_Twin_Towns-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
