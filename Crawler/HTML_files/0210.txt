	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Patronymic</h1>

<p>A <b>patronymic</b>, or <b>patronym</b>, is a component of a <a href="/wiki/Personal_name" title="Personal name">personal name</a> based on the <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> of one's father, grandfather (i.e., an <b>avonymic</b>),<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> or an earlier male ancestor. A component of a name based on the name of one's mother or a female ancestor is a <a href="/wiki/Matronymic" title="Matronymic">matronymic</a>. Each is a means of conveying <a href="/wiki/Lineage_(anthropology)" title="Lineage (anthropology)">lineage</a>.
</p><p>Patronymics are still in use, including mandatory use, in many countries worldwide, although their use has largely been replaced by or transformed into <a href="/wiki/Patronymic_surname" title="Patronymic surname">patronymic surnames</a>. Examples of such transformations include common English surnames such as <a href="/wiki/Johnson" title="Johnson">Johnson</a> (son of John).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_of_terms">Origins of terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins of terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The usual noun and adjective in English is <i>patronymic</i>, but as a noun this exists in <a href="/wiki/Free_variation" title="Free variation">free variation</a> alongside <i>patronym</i>. <sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> The first part of the word <i>patronym</i> comes from <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> πατήρ <i>patēr</i> "father" (<small><a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">GEN</a></small> πατρός <i>patros</i> whence the <a href="/wiki/Combining_form" class="mw-redirect" title="Combining form">combining form</a> πατρο- <i>patro</i>-);<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> the second part comes from Greek ὄνυμα <i>onyma</i>, a variant form of ὄνομα <i>onoma</i> "name".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In the form <i>patronymic</i>, this stands with the addition of the suffix <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-%CE%B9%CE%BA%CF%8C%CF%82" class="extiw" title="wikt:-ικός">-ικός</a> (<i>-ikos</i>), which was originally used to form adjectives with the sense ‘pertaining to’ (thus 'pertaining to the father's name'). These forms are attested in <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_Greek" class="mw-redirect" title="Hellenistic Greek">Hellenistic Greek</a> as πατρώνυμος (<i>patrōnymos</i>) and πατρωνυμικός (<i>patrōnymikos</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The form <i>patronym</i>, first attested in English in 1834, was borrowed into English from French <i>patronyme</i>, which had previously borrowed the word directly from Greek. <i>Patronymic</i>, first attested in English in 1612, has a more complex history. Both Greek words had entered Latin, and, from Latin, French. The English form <i>patronymic</i> was borrowed through the mutual influence of French and Latin on English.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In many areas around the world, patronyms predate the use of <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family names</a>. Family names in many <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">Scandinavian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgian_language" title="Georgian language">Georgian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">Armenian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic</a> languages originate from patronyms, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Wilson_(surname)" class="mw-redirect" title="Wilson (surname)">Wilson</a> (son of <a href="/wiki/William_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="William (name)">William</a>), <a href="/wiki/FitzGerald_(surname)" title="FitzGerald (surname)">FitzGerald</a> (son of <a href="/wiki/Gerald" title="Gerald">Gerald</a>), <a href="/wiki/Powell_(surname)" title="Powell (surname)">Powell</a> (from "ap <a href="/wiki/Hywel" title="Hywel">Hywel</a>"), <a href="/wiki/Fern%C3%A1ndez" title="Fernández">Fernández</a> (son of <a href="/wiki/Fernando" title="Fernando">Fernando</a>), <a href="/wiki/Rodr%C3%ADguez_(surname)" title="Rodríguez (surname)">Rodríguez</a> (son of <a href="/wiki/Rodrigo" title="Rodrigo">Rodrigo</a>), <a Other cultures which formerly used patronyms have switched to the more widespread style of passing the father's last name to the children (and wife) as their own.
In <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_name" title="Icelandic name">family names</a> are unusual; Icelandic law favours the use of patronyms (and more recently, matronyms) over family names.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traditionally Muslim and non-Arabic speaking African people, such as <a href="/wiki/Hausa_people" title="Hausa people">Hausa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fulani" class="mw-redirect" title="Fulani">Fulani</a> people usually (with some exceptions) follow the Arab naming pattern.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The word or phrase meaning "son of" is, however, omitted. So Mohamed son of Ibrahim son of Ahmed is "Mohamed Ibrahim Ahmed", and Mohamed Ibrahim Ahmed's son Ali is "Ali Mohamed Ibrahim".
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethiopia_and_Eritrea">Ethiopia and Eritrea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ethiopia and Eritrea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Naming_conventions_in_Ethiopia_and_Eritrea" title="Naming conventions in Ethiopia and Eritrea">Naming conventions in Ethiopia and Eritrea</a></div>
<p>Ethiopians and Eritreans have no concept of family name and surname. If one is to refer to a person with a single name he/she will always use the person's given name. Ethiopian and Eritreans use a naming pattern very similar to the Arab naming pattern, but with one exception: no suffix or prefix. The full name is written as: First name (given name) followed by the father's name, and last by the grandfather's name. For example, Sara Yohannes Petros is Sara (given name) Yohannes (father's name) Petros (grandfather's name). The grandfather's name is usually only used in official documents. The father's name is not considered a middle name. Instead, it is considered a last name. The same is true for females; they do not take their husband's last name. They go independently by their given name, followed by their father's name, and then their grandfather's name, even after marriage.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Kenya">Kenya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Kenya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some Kenyan communities used patronyms. As of 2010 the practice has largely dropped off with the use of just the father's last name as a surname.
<a href="/wiki/Kalenjin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Kalenjin language">Kalenjin</a> use 'arap' meaning 'son of'; 
<a href="/wiki/Gikuyu_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Gikuyu language">Kikuyu</a> used 'wa' meaning 'of'. Because of polygamy, matronyms were also used and 'wa' used to identify which wife the child was born of; 
<a href="/wiki/Maasai_language" title="Maasai language">Maasai</a> use 'ole' meaning 'son of'; 
<a href="/wiki/Meru_language" title="Meru language">Meru</a> use 'mto' abbreviated M' thus son of Mkindia would be M'Mkindia, pronounced Mto Mkindia.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mozambique">Mozambique</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mozambique">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Patronymic naming is very common in parts of Mozambique.  Although the practice is not universal, patronymic naming has been documented in the Zambezia province.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nigeria">Nigeria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nigeria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Now not as prominent as before, many people of southern Nigeria took their father's given name (father's first name) as their surname. It could also be the father's prominent nickname, which takes the place of a first name. An example would be a man named Kolade Fabiyi, who had a son named Dele. The son's name would now be Dele Kolade, not Dele Fabiyi. This is used to distinguish between extended family who would have the same last name, like cousin. This custom has dropped to the modern English one due to increase in British style education.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Somalia">Somalia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Somalia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Somalis use their paternal grandfather's given name as their legal surname for documentation purpose. They also use the term "ina" or "iña" meaning "the son of" or "the daughter of," which is similar to other African- and the Arab-naming patterns. For example, the name "Ahmed Mohamed Ali Farah" means "Ahmed son of Mohamed son of Ali son of Farah." When stating one's lineage, one will say "Ahmed ina Mohamed" (Ahmed, the son of Mohamed). To identify themselves and the sub-clan they belong to, Somalis memorize their long lineage back a common ancestor. Women never adopt their husbands' patronym but keep theirs for life.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Among the <a href="/wiki/Zulu_people" title="Zulu people">Zulu</a> patronymics were used in the pre-colonial era. The prefix "ka" was attached to the father's name, for example <a href="/wiki/Shaka_kaSenzangakhona" class="mw-redirect" title="Shaka kaSenzangakhona">Shaka kaSenzangakhona</a> means Shaka son of Senzangakhona. The practice disappeared from everyday use with the introduction of the modern European style surname system but still remains part of traditional cultural practices, particularly in the case of chieftains and royalty where reciting lineages forms a part of many ceremonial occasions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Yi_people" title="Yi people">Yi people</a>'s sons' given names are based on the last one or two syllables of father's name. <a href="/wiki/Hani_people" title="Hani people">Hani people</a> also have patronymic customs.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Taiwan">Taiwan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Taiwan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Atayal_people" title="Atayal people">Atayal people</a>'s names are followed by the name of their father, both son and daughter are patronymic. 
<a href="/wiki/Amis_people" title="Amis people">Amis people</a>'s son names are also followed by the father's name, while a daughter's name is followed by her mother's name. Whereas the <a href="/wiki/Seediq_people" title="Seediq people">Seediqs</a> often get to choose which of their parents’ name to go after their own.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">December 2017</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Patronymy is common in parts of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. For example, if a father is named Khurram Suleman (a Muslim masculine name), he might name his son Taha Khurram, who in turn might name his son Ismail Taha. As a result, unlike <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surnames</a>, patronymics will not pass down through many generations.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">Tamil Nadu</a> and some parts of <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Karnataka" title="South Karnataka">South Karnataka</a>, patronymy is predominant. This is a significant departure from the rest of the country where caste names are mostly employed as surnames. This came into common use during the 1950s and 1960s when the Dravidian movement campaigned against the use one's caste as part of the name.
</p><p>However, rather than using the father's full name, only the first letter — popularly known as the initial — is prefixed to the given name. For example, if a person's given name is <i>Saravanan</i> and his father's <i>Krishnan</i>, then the full name is <i>K. Saravanan</i> and is seldom expanded, even in official records. Only where it is forced by stipulation — such as when applying for an Indian passport which does not usually allow initials — is the initial expanded and the name rendered as "Krishnan Saravanan". Some families follow the tradition of retaining the name of the hometown, the grandfather's name, or both as initials. The celebrated Indian English novelist <a href="/wiki/R._K._Narayan" title="R. K. Narayan">R. K. Narayan</a>'s name at birth was Rasipuram Krishnaswami Ayyar Narayanaswami, which was shortened at the behest of his writer friend, <a href="/wiki/Graham_Greene" title="Graham Greene">Graham Greene</a>. Rasipuram, the first name, is a <a href="/wiki/Toponym" class="mw-redirect" title="Toponym">toponym</a> and Krishnaswami Ayyar, the second name, is a patronym.
</p><p>In Tamil Nadu the use of initials and/or surname is up to the prerogative of the person with no strict rules. The late chief minister Karunanidhi preferred to be referred as M. Karunanidhi where the initial M stood for Muthuvel - his father's given name. M.Karunanidhi's son prefers to be referred as M.K.Stalin incorporating both his fathers and grandfather's names. However M.K.Stalin's son prefers to be referred as Udhayanidhi Stalin. Where Udhayanidhi is his given name and Stalin his father's given name as his surname rather than as an initial.
</p><p>Likewise, cricketer Ashwin son of Ravichandran prefers to be referred to as R.Ashwin or Ravichandran Ashwin. This is because commentators in sports often call players by their last names only and it would be embarrassing to call him by his father's name, therefore he puts his own given name in the last.
</p><p>Another upcoming trend is to expand the initials to reflect how it would sound in the native language. For example, Karuppiah prefers to be called Pala. Karuppiah instead of P. Karuppiah and his son Palaniappan prefers Karu. Palaniappan. Cinema director Ranjith prefers Pa. Ranjith instead of P. Ranjith as Pa sounds closer to the name in Tamil rather than P which sounds like Pe unlike the first syllable Pa.
</p><p>Celebrated scientist M. Annadurai would expand his name as Mayilsami Annadurai, however he has to be referred to as Annadurai as referring to him as Mayilsami would be referring to him with his father's given name which could be embarrassing for him.
</p><p>While the usage of caste names as surnames/last names is discouraged(but not banned) in Tamil Nadu, such usage by out of state people is greeted with indifference. So Lakshmi Menon, Shilpa Shetty, Pranab Mukherjee, Somnath Chaterjee etc. are referred by their preferred names which include their caste names. Likewise old Tamil names with caste in them are also fully used while referring to them such as Pasumpoan Muthuramalinga Thevar, U.Ve. Swaminatha Iyer etc.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">Maharashtra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Karnataka" title="Karnataka">Karnataka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gujarat" title="Gujarat">Gujarat</a>, a very common convention among the Hindu communities is to have the patronymic as the middle name. Examples:
</p>
<ul><li>Cricketer <a href="/wiki/Sunil_Gavaskar" title="Sunil Gavaskar">Sunil Gavaskar</a>'s full name is Sunil Manohar Gavaskar, where Manohar is his father's given name. Sunil Gavaskar's son <a href="/wiki/Rohan_Gavaskar" title="Rohan Gavaskar">Rohan Gavaskar</a> would be Rohan Sunil Gavaskar, and so on.</li>
<li>Cricketer <a href="/wiki/Sachin_Tendulkar" title="Sachin Tendulkar">Sachin Tendulkar</a>'s full name is Sachin Ramesh Tendulkar, where Ramesh is his father's given name.</li>
<li>First Deputy Prime Minister and first Home Minister <a href="/wiki/Sardar_Vallabhbhai_Patel" class="mw-redirect" title="Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel">Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel</a>'s full name is Vallabhbhai Jhaverbhai Patel, where Jhaverbhai is his father's given name.</li>
<li>India's 15th Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Narendra_Modi" title="Narendra Modi">Narendra Modi</a>, famously took oath of office as the Prime Minister of India as Narendra Damodardas Modi, where in Damodardas is his father's given name. He prefers to write his full name including his father's name as his middle name.</li></ul>
<p>This system works for both boys and girls, except that after marriage, a woman takes her husband's given name as her middle name – her new middle name is no longer a patronymic.
</p><p>Indians, particularly <a href="/wiki/Tamils" title="Tamils">Tamils</a> in <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, often continue the patronymic tradition; this entails having a single given name, followed by son / daughter of, followed by their father's name.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Malaysian_Indians" title="Malaysian Indians">Malaysian Indians</a> may also follow this custom with "son" or "daughter" of being replaced by "anak lelaki" or "anak perempuan", respectively.
</p><p>Indians of the <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> <a href="/wiki/Isma%27ili" class="mw-redirect" title="Isma&#39;ili">Isma'ili</a> <a href="/wiki/Fiqh" title="Fiqh">sect</a> also have patronymic middle names which use the father's first name and the grandfather's first name plus a family name. Someone called "Ramazan Rahim Ali Manji" might call his son "Karim Ramazan Rahim Manji" and his granddaughter might be called "Zahra Karim Ramazan Manji".
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Southeast_Asia">Southeast Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Southeast Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a>, ethnic Malays generally follow the Arabic patronymic naming system of <i>given name</i> + bin/binti + <i>father's name</i>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a>, the ruling family of the monarch uses <i>given name</i> + <i>ibni</i> + <i>father's name</i> instead of using bin/binti.
</p><p>In Indonesia, there are a number of ethnic groups with different naming systems. The <a href="/wiki/Batak_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Batak people">Batak</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_Sumatra" title="North Sumatra">North Sumatra</a> (Sumatra Utara) give every child the family's name. Sometimes the family's name is prefixed by Huta-, Batu-, etc., but most use Si-, such as Sitanggang, Sihombing, Sibutar-butar, Sinaga, or Sitohang. The family's name is given from the father's family. For example, if the father's name is Boggi Sinaga who married to Moetia Siregar then all children will be given family's name of Sinaga.
</p><p>In Sunda a similar cultural rule is used to that of the Batak. The family's name for <a href="/wiki/Sunda_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunda language">Sunda</a> is -Wijaya, but this isn't true for all <a href="/wiki/Sundanese_people" title="Sundanese people">Sundanese</a> families.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_East">Middle East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Middle East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Arabic">Arabic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Arabic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Arabic_name#Nasab" title="Arabic name">Arabic name §&#160;Nasab</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>, the word "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ibn" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:ibn">ibn</a>" (<span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">ابن</span> or <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">بن</span>: "bin", "ben" and sometimes "ibni" and "ibnu" to show the <a href="/wiki/Arabic_nouns_and_adjectives#Case" title="Arabic nouns and adjectives">grammatical case</a> of the noun) is the equivalent of the "-son" suffix discussed above. (The prefix ben- is used similarly in Hebrew.) In addition, "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bint" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:bint">bint</a>" (<span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">بنت</span>) means "daughter of". Thus, for example, "Ali ibn `Amr" means "Ali son of `Amr". In Classical Arabic, the word <i>ibn</i> is written as <i>bn</i> between two names, since the case ending of the first name then supplies a vowel. Consequently, ibn is often written as "b.", as bint is often written as "bt.," in name formulas rendered from Arabic into Roman characters. Thus Hisham ibn al-Kalbi is alternatively written as Hisham b. al-Kalbi. However, the pronunciation "bin" is dialectal and has nothing to do with either the spelling or pronunciation in Classical Arabic. The word "Abu" ("Aba" or "Abi" in different grammatical cases) means "father of", so "Abu `Ali" is another name for "`Amr".
</p><p>In medieval times, an <a href="/wiki/Illegitimacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Illegitimacy">illegitimate child</a> of unknown parentage would sometimes be termed "ibn Abihi", "son of his father" (notably <a href="/wiki/Ziyad_ibn_Abihi" title="Ziyad ibn Abihi">Ziyad ibn Abihi</a>.) In the <a href="/wiki/Qur%27an" class="mw-redirect" title="Qur&#39;an">Qur'an</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a> (<a href="/wiki/Islamic_view_of_Jesus" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic view of Jesus">Isa</a> in Arabic) is consistently termed "`Isa ibn Maryam" - a <a href="/wiki/Matronymic" title="Matronymic">matronymic</a> (in the Qur'an, Jesus has no father; see <a href="/wiki/Islamic_view_of_Jesus" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic view of Jesus">Islamic view of Jesus</a>). An Arabic patronymic can be extended as far back as family tree records will allow: thus, for example, <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun" title="Ibn Khaldun">Ibn Khaldun</a> gives his own full name as "`Abd ar-Rahman ibn Muhammad ibn Muhammad ibn Muhammad ibn al-Hasan ibn Muhammad ibn Jabir ibn Muhammad ibn Ibrahim ibn `Abd ar-Rahman ibn Khaldun".
</p><p>Patronymics are still standard in parts of the Arab world, notably <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> and Iraq. (In the case of Iraq, with the omitted ibn or bint.) However, most of the Arab world has switched to a family name system.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (May 2015)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  As in English, the new family names are sometimes based on what was formerly a patronymic. Another form widely used in the Arab world is the usage of both the patronymic and a family name, often using both the father's and paternal grandfathers given name in sequence after the own given name, and then the family name. In <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> for example, full names are formed by combining the given name of an individual with the given name of their father (sometimes the father is skipped and the paternal grandfather's given name is used instead, sometimes both father and paternal grandfather are used), along with the town, village, or clan name. For instance, Hayder Muhammed al-Tikriti is the son of Muhammed named Hayder, and he is from the town of <a href="/wiki/Tikrit" title="Tikrit">Tikrit</a>. In Saudi Arabia naming conventions are similar to Iraq's but family names are used much more often.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Aramaic">Aramaic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Aramaic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Aramaic language">Aramaic</a>, the prefix <i><b>bar-</b></i> means "son" and is used as a prefix meaning "son of". In the Bible, Peter is called Bar-Jonah in <a href="/wiki/Matthew_16:17" class="mw-redirect" title="Matthew 16:17">Matthew 16:17</a> and Nathanael is possibly called Bartholomew because he is the son of Tolmai (or son of Ptolemy, with "P" being reduced). The titles can also be figurative, for example in <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Acts" class="mw-redirect" title="Book of Acts">Acts</a> 4:36-37 a man named Joseph is called <a href="/wiki/Barnabas" title="Barnabas">Barnabas</a> meaning "son of consolation".
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Assyrian">Assyrian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Assyrian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_people" title="Assyrian people">Assyrians</a> for centuries have used the patronymic <i><b>bet</b></i> or <i><b>bit</b></i> literally meaning "house" in <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_Neo-Aramaic" title="Assyrian Neo-Aramaic">Assyrian Neo-Aramaic</a>; however, in the context of the name it means "from the house of [the father's name]."
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Jewish">Jewish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Jewish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Jewish_name" title="Jewish name">Jewish name</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Jew" class="mw-redirect" title="Jew">Jews</a> have historically used <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> patronymic names after the <a href="/wiki/Bar_Kokhba_revolt" title="Bar Kokhba revolt">Bar Kokhba revolt</a>, before which the most common language was <a href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">Aramaic</a>. In the Jewish patronymic system the first name is followed by either <i><b>ben-</b></i> or <i><b>bat-</b></i> ("son of" and "daughter of", respectively), and then the father's name, mother's name, or both. In <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Aramaic language">Aramaic</a>, the first name was followed by <i><b>bar-</b></i> or <i><b>bat-</b></i> ("son of" and "daughter of", respectively).
</p><p>Permanent family surnames exist today, first by <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">Sephardic Jews</a> in 10th or 11th century <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberia</a> and by <a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi_Jews" title="Ashkenazi Jews">Ashkenazi Jews</a> in the late 18th century, when Austria passed the first law requiring Jews adopt surnames. Similar laws were passed in <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a>, France, regions of Germany, and other countries.
</p><p>While Jews now have permanent surnames for everyday life, the patronymic form is still used in religious life. It is used in <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">synagogue</a> and in documents in <a href="/wiki/Halakha" title="Halakha">Jewish law</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Ketubah" title="Ketubah">ketubah</a> (marriage contract). Many Sephardic Jews used the Arabic <i>ibn</i> instead of <i>bat</i> or <i>ben</i> when it was the norm. The Spanish family <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Ezra_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Ibn Ezra (disambiguation)">Ibn Ezra</a> is one example.
</p><p>There is a strong cultural pressure for <a href="/wiki/Aliyah" title="Aliyah">immigrants</a> to <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">modern Israel</a> to <a href="/wiki/Hebraization_of_surnames" title="Hebraization of surnames">Hebraize</a> their names.<sup id="cite_ref-Kaganoff_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaganoff-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> 
This practice is especially common among Ashkenazic immigrants, because most of their names were taken during the period from the end of the 18th century to the middle of the 19th century. For example, <a href="/wiki/Golda_Meir" title="Golda Meir">Golda Meir</a> was born "Golda Mabovitch", took the name "Golda Meyerson" after her marriage to American Morris Meyerson, and, upon making <a href="/wiki/Aliyah" title="Aliyah">Aliyah</a> and at the urging of <a href="/wiki/Moshe_Sharett" title="Moshe Sharett">Moshe Sharett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hebraization_of_surnames" title="Hebraization of surnames">Hebraized</a> her last name to <a href="/wiki/Meir_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Meir (name)">Meir</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Neil_Caplan_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Neil_Caplan-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Persian">Persian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Persian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>, patronymics <span lang="fa" dir="rtl" title="Persian language text">پَتوَند</span> are formed by names ending with the suffix "Pur" <span lang="fa" dir="rtl" title="Persian language text">پور</span> for men and "Dokht" <span lang="fa" dir="rtl" title="Persian language text">دُخت</span> for women. For example: Shahpoor (son of king) and Sinapoor (son of Sina). Depending on country, some suffixes are more common than others. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, the suffix "pur" is common while in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, the suffix "Zadah" <span lang="fa" dir="rtl" title="Persian language text">زاده</span> or "Zad" <span lang="fa" dir="rtl" title="Persian language text">زاد</span> is common, although <a href="/wiki/Zadeh" title="Zadeh">Zadeh</a> is common in Iran.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Europe patronyms were formerly widespread but later became confined to Scandinavia and Iceland.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="English">English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In England, names ending with the suffix "son" were often originally patronymic. In addition, the archaic <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> (more specifically, <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">Norman</a>) prefix <i>fitz</i> (cognate with the modern French <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fils" class="extiw" title="wikt:fils">fils</a></i>, meaning "son") appears in England's aristocratic family lines dating from the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a>, and also among the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Irish">Anglo-Irish</a>. Thus there are names such as Fitzgerald and Fitzhugh. Of particular interest is the name "Fitzroy", meaning "son of [the] king", which was used by illegitimate royal children who were acknowledged as such by their fathers.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Irish.2C_Scottish.2C_and_Manx"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Irish,_Scottish,_and_Manx">Irish, Scottish, and Manx</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Irish, Scottish, and Manx">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Irish_name#Patronyms_and_other_additives" title="Irish name">Irish name §&#160;Patronyms and other additives</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Celtic_onomastics#Surname_prefixes" title="Celtic onomastics">Celtic onomastics §&#160;Surname prefixes</a></div>
<p><span id="Ireland"></span><span id="Scotland"></span><span id="Celtic"></span><span id="Gaelic"></span>
</p>

<p>The use of "Mac" in some form was prevalent in <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manx_language" title="Manx language">Manx</a>, in all of which it denotes "son". "Mc" is also a frequent anglicisation in both Scotland and Ireland. In Ireland, the forms "Mag" and "M'" are encountered. The prefix "Mac" is used to form a patronym, such as "Mac Coinnich" – or the anglicized 'Mackenzie' – son of Coinneach/Kenneth. The female equivalent of <i>Mac</i> is <i>Nic</i>, condensed from <i>nighean mhic</i> (in Scottish Gaelic) or <i>iníon mhic</i> (in Irish), both meaning daughter. For example, the Scottish Gaelic surname, <i>Nic Dhòmhnaill</i> meaning 'daughter of a son of Dòmhnall' (in English, Donald), as in <i>Mairi Nic Dhòmhnaill</i>, or Mary MacDonald.
</p><p>At the north end of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Ulster" title="Ulster">Ulster</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galloway" title="Galloway">Galloway</a> (indeed as far north as <a href="/wiki/Argyll" title="Argyll">Argyll</a>), "Mac" was frequently truncated in speech to /k/, leading to such anglicisations as "Qualtrough" (Son of Walter) &amp; "Quayle" (son of Paul, cf. <a href="/wiki/MacPhail_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="MacPhail (disambiguation)">MacPhail</a>) – usually beginning with "C", "K" or "Q". In Ireland, this truncation resulted in surnames such as "Guinness" (son of Aonghus, cf. MacAonghusa) beginning usually in "C" or "G" for patronymics prefixed with Mac, and in "H" (e.g. "Hurley" (descendant of Jarlath, cf. Ua h-Iarfhlatha/O'Hurley)) for surnames prefixed with "O". Colloquial Scottish Gaelic also has other patronymics of a slightly different form for individuals, still in use (for more information please see: <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_personal_naming_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Gaelic personal naming system">Scottish Gaelic personal naming system</a>).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Welsh_and_Cornish">Welsh and Cornish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Welsh and Cornish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><span id="Wales"></span><span id="Cornwall"></span>
</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Welsh_surnames" title="Welsh surnames">Welsh surnames</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornish_surnames" title="Cornish surnames">Cornish surnames</a></div>

<p>Before the 1536 <a href="/wiki/Laws_in_Wales_Acts_1535%E2%80%931542" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws in Wales Acts 1535–1542">Act of Union</a>, the Welsh did not generally employ surnames, but instead used <a href="/wiki/Epithet" title="Epithet">epithets</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Selyf_Sarffgadau" class="mw-redirect" title="Selyf Sarffgadau">Selyf Sarffgadau</a>, "Selyf the Battle-Serpent"), patronyms (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Rhodri_the_Great" title="Rhodri the Great">Rhodri ap Merfyn</a>, "Rhodri son of Merfyn"), and (much less often) matronyms (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Rhodri_the_Great" title="Rhodri the Great">Rhodri ap Nest</a>, "Rhodri son of Nest") to identify people. Welsh, as a <a href="/wiki/P-Celtic_and_Q-Celtic_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="P-Celtic and Q-Celtic languages">P-Celtic language</a>, originally used <i>map</i> or <i>mab</i> instead of the <a href="/wiki/P-Celtic_and_Q-Celtic_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="P-Celtic and Q-Celtic languages">Q-Celtic</a> <i>mac</i> employed in Ireland and Scotland. These were later simplified to the <a href="/wiki/Modern_Welsh" class="mw-redirect" title="Modern Welsh">modern Welsh</a> <i>ap</i> and <i>ab</i>. A common practice is to use <i>ab</i> before a father's name beginning with a vowel (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Llywelyn_ab_Iorwerth" class="mw-redirect" title="Llywelyn ab Iorwerth">Llywelyn ab Iorwerth</a>), but the two alternative forms are also employed arbitrarily in many sources.
</p><p>Daughters were indicated by <i>ferch</i> or <i>verch</i> (<a href="/wiki/Welsh_language#mutation" title="Welsh language">mutated</a> from <i>merch</i>, "girl, daughter"). <i>Angharad verch Owain</i> would be "Angharad, daughter of Owain".
</p><p>After the Acts of Union, this led to many Welsh surnames being variants of their father or ancestor's personal name: <i>ap</i> or <i>ab Ieuan</i> often became "Evans"; <i>ap Rhys</i>, "Price"; <i>ap</i> or <i>ab Owain</i>, "Bowen"; <i>ap Hywel</i>, "Powell" or "Howell". In addition to these Anglicised baptismal and official names, patronyms continued to be commonly employed in Welsh until the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, particularly in the north and west of Wales. Patronyms were sometimes employed within the English names as well by using the father's personal name as the sons' <a href="/wiki/Middle_name" title="Middle name">middle name</a>.
</p><p>Perhaps because <a href="/wiki/Cornish_surnames" title="Cornish surnames">Cornwall</a> was legally incorporated into England earlier than Wales was, patronyms (e.g.[m]ap Ros&gt;Rouse, [m]ap Richard&gt;Pritchard, Davies, Evans) are less common there than <a href="/wiki/Toponymic_surname" title="Toponymic surname">toponyms</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Tresillian" title="Tresillian">Tresillian</a>, Trevithick, <a href="/wiki/Nankivell" title="Nankivell">Nanskeval/Nankeville</a>) and occupational surnames (e.g. <a href="/wiki/An_Gof" title="An Gof">An Gof</a>, [An] Gove, <i>(<a href="/wiki/Blacksmith" title="Blacksmith">Blacksmith</a>)</i>; Helyer (Cornish dialect - possibly a <a href="/wiki/Slater" title="Slater">slater</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hunter" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunter">huntsman</a> (helgher)).<sup id="cite_ref-Kernow_names_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kernow_names-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dutch">Dutch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Dutch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>, patronymics were often used in place of <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family names</a> or as middle names. Patronymics were composed of the father's name plus an ending <i>-zoon</i> for sons, <i>-dochter</i> for daughters. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Abel_Tasman" title="Abel Tasman">Abel Janszoon Tasman</a> is "Abel son of Jan Tasman", and <a href="/wiki/Kenau_Simonsdochter_Hasselaer" title="Kenau Simonsdochter Hasselaer">Kenau Simonsdochter Hasselaer</a>: "Kenau, daughter of Simon Hasselaer". In written form, these endings were often abbreviated as <i>-sz.</i> and <i>-dr.</i> respectively e.g. Jeroen Corneli<b>sz</b>. "Jeroen son of Cornelis", or <a href="/wiki/Dirck_Jacobsz." title="Dirck Jacobsz.">Dirck Jacobsz</a>. The endings <i>-s</i>, <i>-se</i> and <i>-sen</i> were also commonly used for sons and often for daughters too. In the northern provinces, <i>-s</i>, as <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">genitive case</a>, was almost universally used for both sons and daughters. The suffix -<i>x</i> as in "Tacx" or "Hendrix" also denoted the son or daughter of..., and is now integrated as a complete name.
</p><p>Patronymics were common in the Dutch <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">United Provinces</a> until the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> invasion in 1795 and subsequent annexation in 1810. As the Netherlands were now a province of France, a registry of births, deaths and marriages was established in 1811, whereupon <a href="/wiki/Emperor" title="Emperor">emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoleon</a> forced the Dutch to register and adopt a distinct surname.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="French">French</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: French">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>In France, the terms <i>patronyme</i> and <i>nom patronymique</i> had long been used interchangeably to designate the family name, meaning that it is inherited from the father.
</p><p>The tradition of patronymic lineage is still used among some Canadian descendants of French colonists: in the oral tradition of many <a href="/wiki/Acadians" title="Acadians">Acadians</a>, for example, <i>Marc à Pierre à Gérard</i> (lit. "Marc of Pierre of Gérard"), means "Marc, son of Pierre, grandson of Gérard".
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Iberian_peninsula">Iberian peninsula</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Iberian peninsula">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_naming_customs" title="Spanish naming customs">Spanish naming customs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_naming_customs" class="mw-redirect" title="Portuguese naming customs">Portuguese naming customs</a></div>
<p>In the past, both in Spanish and Portuguese, the endings <i>-ez</i> and <i>-es</i> tended to be conflated since pronunciation was quite similar in the two languages. Today, Portuguese has been fully standardized to <i>-es</i>; Spanish is also standardized to <i>-ez</i>, but it is very common to see archaic endings in <i>-es</i>. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Pires_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Pires (disambiguation)">Pires</a>/<a href="/wiki/Peres" title="Peres">Peres</a> and <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A9rez" title="Pérez">Pérez</a> are the modern equivalents of English "<a href="/wiki/Peterson_(name)" title="Peterson (name)">Peterson</a>" in Portuguese and Spanish.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, there are some surnames which had a patronymic genesis, while still common they no longer indicate patronymic usage. For instance, <i>Álvares</i> was the son of <i>Álvaro</i> and <i>Gonçalves</i> was the son of <i>Gonçalo</i> (it was the case of <a href="/wiki/Nuno_%C3%81lvares_Pereira" title="Nuno Álvares Pereira">Nuno Álvares Pereira</a>, son of Álvaro and Gonçalves Pereira, son of Gonçalo Pereira). Other cases include <i>Rodrigues</i> (<i>son of Rodrigo</i>), <i>Nunes</i> (<i>son of Nuno</i>) and <i>Fernandes</i> (<i>son of Fernando</i>).
In the same way the surname Soares means son of Soeiro (in Latin Suarius). It comes from Latin Suaricius (son of Suarius); the Latin <a href="/wiki/Genitive" class="mw-redirect" title="Genitive">genitive</a> suffix <i>-icius/a</i> was used to indicate a patronymic. Later it became Suáriz, Suárez and eventually Soares. Another theory attributes the Iberian -ez style patronymics to Germanic (Visigothic) rather than Latin influence.<sup id="cite_ref-port-names1_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-port-names1-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-port-names2_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-port-names2-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> patronyms follow a similar pattern to the Portuguese (e.g., <a href="/wiki/L%C3%B3pez" title="López">López</a>: of Lope; <a href="/wiki/Hern%C3%A1ndez" title="Hernández">Hernández</a>: of Hernán; <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lvarez_(surname)" title="Álvarez (surname)">Álvarez</a>: of Álvaro). Common endings include -ez, -az, -is, and -oz. However, not all names with similar endings are necessarily patronymic. For example, Chávez is not the son of <i>Chavo</i>, but comes from <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">Galician</a> or <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> <i>Chaves</i>, meaning "keys", and its "s" stands for the plural form, as in key/keys in English.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A list of some Iberian patronymics:<sup id="cite_ref-port-names1_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-port-names1-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-port-names2_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-port-names2-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Scandinavian_languages">Scandinavian languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Scandinavian languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_name" title="Icelandic name">Icelandic name</a></div>
<p>In Norse custom patronyms and matronyms were formed by using the ending -son (later -søn and -sen in <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">Norwegian</a> and <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>) to the <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">genitive</a> form of the father's name to indicate "son of", and -dóttir (<a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a> and Faroese -dóttir, <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a> and Norwegian -dotter, Danish and Norwegian -datter) for "daughter of". The resulting patronymic was generally used as a last name; however a third name, a so-called byname based on location or personal characteristic, was often added to differentiate people and could eventually develop into a kind of <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a>. Some <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Modern">Early Modern</a> examples of the latter practice, where the patronymic was placed after the given name and was followed by the surname, are Norwegian <a href="/wiki/Peder_Clauss%C3%B8n_Friis" title="Peder Claussøn Friis">Peder Claussøn Friis</a>, the son of Nicolas Thorolfsen Friis (Claus in Claussøn being short for Nicolas) and Danish <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Kingo" title="Thomas Kingo">Thomas Hansen Kingo</a>, the son of Hans Thomsen Kingo.
</p><p>Eventually, most Nordic countries replaced or complemented this system with the prevailing "international" standard of inherited family names. In Norway, for example, the <a href="/wiki/Storting" title="Storting">parliament</a> passed a family name act in 1923, citing the rising population and the need to avoid the confusion of new last names in every generation. The law does allow a person to retain a patronymic as a <a href="/wiki/Middle_name" title="Middle name">middle name</a> in addition to the surname, as was common in Early Modern times; this is not a common practice, but does occur, a modern example being <a href="/wiki/Audhild_Gregoriusdotter_Rotevatn" title="Audhild Gregoriusdotter Rotevatn">Audhild Gregoriusdotter Rotevatn</a>. The Danish government outlawed the practice in 1856 and eased the regulations in 1904 to deal with the limited number of patronymics. In Sweden the practice of children keeping their father's and wives keeping their husband's patronymic as a surname occurred in the 18th century but was first prevalent in the late 19th century, still present yet uncommon in the early 20th century, and finally abolished in 1966. In 1982 the right to use patronyms (and matronyms) was partially restored, and from 1 July 2017 parents in Sweden are free to give their children patronyms/matronyms at birth instead of inherited family names.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Matronyms were used exceptionally if the child was born out of wedlock or if the mother was much more high-born or well known than the father, a historical example being <a href="/wiki/Sweyn_Estridsson" class="mw-redirect" title="Sweyn Estridsson">Sweyn Estridsson</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Icelandic_name" title="Icelandic name">In Iceland</a>, patronymics or matronymics are still used as last names and this is in fact compulsory by law, with a handful of exceptions. The father's name (usually in the genitive case) plus the word <i>son</i> for sons, <i>dóttir</i> for daughters.<sup id="cite_ref-namelaw_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-namelaw-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B3hanna_Sigur%C3%B0ard%C3%B3ttir" title="Jóhanna Sigurðardóttir">Jóhanna Sigurðardóttir</a> (i.e. "Jóhanna, daughter of Sigurð[ur]").
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Finnish">Finnish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Finnish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, the use of patronymics was a result of relatively recent Swedish influence and remained uncommon outside official documents. Only in the 19th century did the use of patronymics gain any sort of popularity among the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish-speaking</a> lower classes.<sup id="cite_ref-paikkala_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-paikkala-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Family names became obligatory in Finland by law in 1920.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Historically, patronymics were composed in Swedish fashion: the father's name and the suffix -n for genitive plus the word <i>poika</i> for sons, <i>tytär</i> for daughters. For example, Tuomas Abrahaminpoika (to be read in English as "Tuomas, Abraham's son") and Martta Heikintytär (to be read in English as "Martta, Heikki's daughter").<sup id="cite_ref-paikkala_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-paikkala-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bulgarian">Bulgarian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Bulgarian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a>, the patronymics are <i>-ov</i>/<i>-ev</i> and <i>-ova</i>/<i>-eva</i> for men and women, respectively. These are identical to the endings of <i>family</i> names in Bulgarian and some other Slavic family names (such as names in Russian and <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a>). In Bulgarian official documents, the patronymic is inserted before the surname - e.g. <i>Ivan Marinov Yordanov</i> would be the son of <i>Marin Yordanov</i>.
</p>

<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Armenian">Armenian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Armenian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Armenian_surnames" class="mw-redirect" title="Armenian surnames">Armenian surnames</a></div>
<p>The use of patronymics was introduced in Armenia by Russians during the times of the Russian Empire and the Soviet Union. Previously to that period, the use of patronymics was very limited. Patronymics are usually formed by the addition of "i" ("of", pronounced as <i>ee</i>) to the father's name, e.g. if father's name is "Armen", the corresponding patronymic would be "Armeni" (of Armen). Russified version of the same patronymic would be "Armenovich" for males and "Armenovna" for females. After Armenia regained its independence from the Soviet Union in 1991 a massive decline in the use of Russified patronymics occurred; nowadays few Armenians use patronymics outside of official contexts.
</p><p>Many Armenian surnames were once patronymics first used by distant ancestors or clan founders. These are characterized by the suffix "-ian" in Western Armenian, often transliterated as "-yan" in Eastern Armenian. These are appended to the given name, i.e. Kardash<b>ian</b>, Asdvadzadour<b>ian</b>, Hagop<b>ian</b>, Khachadour<b>ian</b>, Mardiros<b>ian</b>, Bedros<b>ian</b>, Sarkiss<b>ian</b>, etc. Note that the suffix "-ian" was also appended to trades, as in Adakhtsakordz<b>ian</b> (issued from the carpenter), Chalian (issued from the candlemaker, using the Turkish word "chal", meaning candle).
</p><p>Of particular note are the surnames of the children of married priests, or kahanas. Though not as common nowadays, it was customary for a long time for these children (particularly the sons) to change their last names to the name-in-religion of their father. For example, the son of Ter (Reverend) Bartev would change his last name to Ter Bartevian.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Azerbaijani">Azerbaijani</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Azerbaijani">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijani_language" title="Azerbaijani language">Azeri</a>, patronymics are formed through <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/o%C4%9Ful" class="extiw" title="wikt:oğul">-oğlu</a></i> (sometimes transliterated as <i>ogly</i>) for males and <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/q%C4%B1z" class="extiw" title="wikt:qız">qızı</a></i> (often transliterated as <i>gizi</i> or <i>kizi</i>) for females. Prior to the late 19th–early 20th century, patronymics were used as an essential part of a person's full name, i.e. <i>Sardar Ilyas oğlu</i> ("Sardar, son of Ilyas") and <i>Mina Nabi qızı</i> ("Mina, daughter of Nabi"), since surnames were mostly non-existent before <a href="/wiki/Sovietization" title="Sovietization">Sovietization</a> (with the exception of the upper and some middle-class families). After surnames were commonly adopted in <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a> in the 1920s, patronymics still remained parts of full names, i.e. <i>Sardar Ilyas oğlu Aliyev</i> ("Sardar Aliyev, son of Ilyas"). Nowadays in Azerbaijan, patronymics sometimes replace surnames in unofficial use. Normally in such case, they are spelled as one word (i.e. <i>Eldar Mammadoğlu</i>, <i>Sabina Yusifqızı</i>). Many Azeri surnames are also derived from Persian-style patronymics ending in <i>-zadeh</i> (<i>Kazimzadeh</i>, <i>Mehdizadeh</i>, etc.). They are found among both Caucasian and Iranian Azeris. However unlike the former, Azeris in Iran do not generally use patronymics in <i>oglu</i> / <i>qizi</i>. Azeri patronymics are not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a> surnames in <i>-oğlu</i> and <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> surnames in -ογλού (<i>-oglou</i>), which do not have specific female versions and do not reflect names of fathers.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Georgian">Georgian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Georgian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Georgian_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="Georgian surname">Georgian surname</a></div>
<p>In Georgian, patronymics, when used, come with the addition of <i>s</i> to the end of the father's name, followed by <i>dze</i> for a man and <i>asuli</i> for a woman. For example, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Joseph Stalin</a>'s actual name was Ioseb Besarionis Dze Jugashvili. <i>s</i> in Georgian is a possessive, and <i>dze</i> and <i>asuli</i> mean male and female descendant. After collapse of the USSR, patronymics in Georgia are disused as part of Russian tradition.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
Georgian last names derive mostly from patronymics, nicknames and places of origin. Two common elements in Georgian last names, <i>dze</i> and <i>shvili</i> mean son of, and child, respectively.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Greek_and_Greek_Cypriot">Greek and Greek Cypriot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Greek and Greek Cypriot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>Most <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> surnames are patronymics by origin, albeit in various forms depending on ancestral locality. Diminutive suffixes which denote "<i>son of</i>", or more generally "<i>descendant of</i>", are produced as follows: starting with the given name Δημήτριος, <i>Dēmétrios</i>, for example, the patronymic surnames Dēmētr<i>ópoulos</i> (<a href="/wiki/Peloponnese" title="Peloponnese">Peloponnese</a>), Dēmētr<i>ákos</i> (<a href="/wiki/Laconia" title="Laconia">Laconia</a>), Dēmētr<i>éas</i> (<a href="/wiki/Messenia" class="mw-redirect" title="Messenia">Messenian</a> <a href="/wiki/Mani_Peninsula" title="Mani Peninsula">Mani</a>), Dēmētr<i>átos</i> (<a href="/wiki/Cephalonia" title="Cephalonia">Cephalonia</a>), Dēmētr<i>ákēs</i> (<a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>), Dēmētr<i>iádēs</i>/Dēmētr<i>-ídēs</i> (<a href="/wiki/Pontus_(region)" title="Pontus (region)">Pontus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>), Dēmētr<i>éllēs</i> (<a href="/wiki/Lesbos" title="Lesbos">Lesbos</a>), Dēmétr<i>oglou</i> (<a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>) (identical to Turkish patronym <i>-oğlu</i>), or simply <i>Dēmētríou</i> (esp. common in <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, the first name in the <a href="/wiki/Genitive" class="mw-redirect" title="Genitive">Genitive</a>) are formed. The same principle can apply to surnames deriving from professions, for example from παπάς, <i>papás</i>, priest, one derives the surnames <i>Papadópoulos, Papadákos, Papadéas, Papadátos, Papadákēs, Papadéllēs, Papazoglou</i> etc., all of which signify a "priest's son". The same principle(s) may apply in combination, e.g. <i>Papanikoláou, Papanikolópoulos</i>, "the son of the priest Nikolaos". A daughter's family name is the same as the son's, but <i>always</i> declined in the <a href="/wiki/Genitive" class="mw-redirect" title="Genitive">Genitive</a>, e.g. <i>Dēmētropoúlou, Papanikoláou</i> etc.
</p><p>In addition to these surnames, <i>actual</i> patronymics are used in official documents as "middle names" preceding the surname. For example, the children of a <i>Ioánnis Papadópoulos</i> are, say, <i>María Ioánnou Papadopoúlou</i> and <i>Andréas Ioánnou Papadópoulos</i> (<i>Ioánnou</i> is the <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">genitive case</a> form of <i>Ioánnis</i>). Traditionally, a married woman would adopt her husband's family name. Now, however, women in Greece can keep their own surname if they so choose.
</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>, patronyms were traditionally formed with the ending <i>-fi</i> (sometimes spelled as <i>-fy</i> or <i>-ffy</i>). This system is no longer in common use, though traces of it can still be found in some frequent present-day surnames such as <i>Pálfi</i> (son of Paul), <i>Győrfi</i>, <i>Bánfi</i> or in the name of the famous poet <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A1ndor_Pet%C5%91fi" title="Sándor Petőfi">Sándor Petőfi</a> (who chose this Hungarian form instead of his Slavic birth name <i>Petrovics</i>). In the Old Hungarian period (10th−16th century, see <a href="/wiki/History_of_Hungarian" class="mw-redirect" title="History of Hungarian">History of Hungarian</a>), when surnames were not in common use, the full genitive was represented as in <i>Péter fia András</i> (<i>Peter's son Andrew</i>); these forms are in frequent use in charters and legal documents dated back to that time.
</p>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a>, the endings <i>-escu</i> and <i>-eanu</i> were used, as in <i>Petrescu</i>, 'son of Petre (Peter)'; many modern Romanian family names were formed from such patronymics. Less commonly, matronymics formed with the <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">genitive</a> form (using the prefix <i>a-</i>) were used, as in <i>Amariei</i>, '(son/daughter) of Maria'.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Russian">Russian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: Russian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/East_Slavic_languages" title="East Slavic languages">East Slavic languages</a>, the endings <i>-ovich</i>, <i>-evich</i> and <i>-ich</i> are used to form patronymics for men. It would be cognate to the Latin genitive -ici, used for marking family line, and also as equivalent to: 'little' -Vladic= 'the little Vlad'-. For women, the ending is <i>-yevna</i>, <i>-ovna</i> or <i>-ichna</i>. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>, a man named Ivan with a father named Nikolay would be known as Ivan Nikolayevich or "Ivan, son of Nikolay" (<i>Nikolayevich</i> being a patronymic). Likewise, a woman named Lyudmila with a father named Nikolay would be known as Lyudmila Nikolayevna or "Lyudmila, daughter of Nikolay" (<i>Nikolayevna</i> being a patronymic). For masculine names ending in a vowel, such as Ilya or Foma, when they are used as a base for patronymic, the corresponding endings are <i>-ich</i> (for men) and <i>-inichna</i> (for women). Examples in titles  of classical Russian literature include<i> <a href="/wiki/The_Belkin_Tales" title="The Belkin Tales">The Tales of the Late Ivan Petrovich Belkin</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Death_of_Ivan_Ilyich" title="The Death of Ivan Ilyich">The Death of Ivan Ilyich</a></i> and "<a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_How_Ivan_Ivanovich_Quarreled_with_Ivan_Nikiforovich" title="The Tale of How Ivan Ivanovich Quarreled with Ivan Nikiforovich">The Tale of How Ivan Ivanovich Quarreled with Ivan Nikiforovich</a>".
</p><p>In Russia, the patronymic is an official part of the name, used in all official documents, and when addressing somebody both formally and among friends.<sup id="cite_ref-rus1_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rus1-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rus2_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rus2-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Individuals are addressed by their given name followed by patronymic (e.g., "Mikhail Nikolayevich") in many situations including on formal occasions, by colleagues at work, by acquaintances, or when addressed by someone younger in age.<sup id="cite_ref-rus1_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rus1-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rus3_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rus3-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> It is becoming more common for younger individuals (under 50) to drop the patronymic at work.<sup id="cite_ref-rus3_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rus3-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In informal situations, if a person is called by a <a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">diminutive</a> (such as Misha for Mikhail or Nastya for Anastasia), the patronymic is not used.<sup id="cite_ref-rus2_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rus2-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Colloquial" class="mw-redirect" title="Colloquial">colloquial</a>, informal speech, it is also possible to contract the ending of a patronymic: thus Nikolayevich becomes Nikolaich, and Stepan Ivanovich becomes Stepan Ivanych or simply Ivanych as the <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> may be omitted altogether. In this case the contraction, if possible, is obligatory: Ivan Sergeyevich Sidorov may be called "Sergeich" or, more rarely, "Sergeyevich". In contrast to male names, if a woman is called by her patronymic name without a given name, the patronymic is usually not contracted: "Ivanovna" but "Mar' Ivanna"; "Sergeyevna"/"Sergevna" is one exception, where both forms are fine. Typically, a patronymic name alone is a familiar form of addressing an older female.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Serbian">Serbian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Serbian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">December 2013</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><a href="/wiki/Vuk_Karad%C5%BEi%C4%87" title="Vuk Karadžić">Vuk Karadžić</a> reported in the 19th century that Serbs sometimes used their old family names, and sometimes patronymics. Vuk Karadžić himself used patronymic Stefanović (son of Steven), and sometimes Karadzić, old family name. However, nowadays, the patronymic names in Serbia are mostly used on legal documents, and have the form of the father's name that says the child is 'of so and so'... example: Marija Dragoljuba Pavlovic, where Dragoljub is the father's name and 'Dragoljuba' literally means 'of Dragoljub'.
</p><p>In Serbia, Croatia and Bosnia, the patronymic names do not change form between <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">masculine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">feminine</a>.  Example: Marija Dragoljuba Pavlović (Dragoljub is the father's name; Dragoljuba is the form that says she is his daughter, or literally 'of Dragoljub').
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkish">Turkish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Turkish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Turkish_name" title="Turkish name">Turkish name</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffixes</a> used to indicate paternal ancestry are <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/o%C4%9Ful" class="extiw" title="wikt:oğul">-oğlu</a></i> and <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/zadeh" class="extiw" title="wikt:zadeh">-zade</a></i>, which indicate the ancestry as coming from a certain man. Like many other patronymics in other languages, with the formalization of naming conventions by laws in the <a href="/wiki/Late_modern_history" class="mw-redirect" title="Late modern history">late modern</a> <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_history" title="Contemporary history">contemporary age</a> many turned into surnames. After the '<a href="/wiki/Surname_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Surname Law">Surname revolution</a>' in 1934, many people chose professions or habitat as surnames with or without the suffix <i>-oğlu</i>, such as <i>Elbeyioğlu</i>, <i>Bakkaloğlu</i> or <i>Giritlioğlu</i> and with <i>-zade</i> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Begzada" title="Begzada">Beyzade</a></i>, <i>Mehmedzade</i>, <i>Yusufzade</i>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ukrainian">Ukrainian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Ukrainian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_name" title="Ukrainian name">Ukrainian name</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a>, the female patronymic always ends with <i>-івна</i> <i>(-ivna)</i> or <i>-ївна</i> <i>(-yivna)</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-LPUkraine_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LPUkraine-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The male patronymic always ends with -<i>ович</i> <i>(-ovych)</i> or <i>-йович</i> <i>(-yovych)</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-LPUkraine_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LPUkraine-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Exception: <i>Illia (Ілля)</i> -&gt; <i>Illich (Ілліч)</i> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/%C3%89lie_Metchnikoff" title="Élie Metchnikoff">Illia Illich Mechnikov</a>), <i>Sava (Сава)</i> -&gt; <i>Savych (Савич)</i>, <i>Iakiv (Яків)</i> -&gt; <i>Iakovych (Якович)</i><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>.
</p><p>Patronyms are part of full name and obligatory in formal messages. They're frequent in common speech, e.g. to call a person in respectful manner (in form of name followed by patronym) and to accent an informal message in formal environment, such as between colleagues who have good relationships at work (in form of patronym without name and family name).
</p>
