<p>The <b>surgeon general of the United States</b> is the operational head of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Public_Health_Service_Commissioned_Corps" title="United States Public Health Service Commissioned Corps">U.S. Public Health Service Commissioned Corps</a> (PHSCC) and thus the leading spokesperson on matters of <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">federal government of the United States</a>. The surgeon general's office and staff are known as the <b>Office of the Surgeon General</b> (<b>OSG</b>) which is housed within the Office of the Assistant Secretary for Health.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The U.S. surgeon general is nominated by the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">president of the United States</a> and confirmed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senate</a>. The surgeon general must be appointed from individuals who (1) are members of the Regular Corps of the U.S. Public Health Service, and (2) have specialized training or significant experience in public health programs.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The surgeon general serves a four-year term of office and, depending on whether the current <a href="/wiki/United_States_Assistant_Secretary_for_Health" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Assistant Secretary for Health">assistant secretary for health</a> is a Public Health Service commissioned officer, is either the senior or next most senior uniformed officer of the commissioned corps, holding the rank of a <a href="/wiki/Vice_admiral_(United_States)" title="Vice admiral (United States)">vice admiral</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-grades_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grades-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The current surgeon general is <a href="/wiki/Jerome_Adams" title="Jerome Adams">Jerome Adams</a>, having taken office on September 5, 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Responsibilities">Responsibilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surgeon_General_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Responsibilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PUBLIC_HEALTH_SERVICE_LOGO.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/PUBLIC_HEALTH_SERVICE_LOGO.PNG/120px-PUBLIC_HEALTH_SERVICE_LOGO.PNG" decoding="async" width="120" height="120" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/PUBLIC_HEALTH_SERVICE_LOGO.PNG/180px-PUBLIC_HEALTH_SERVICE_LOGO.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/PUBLIC_HEALTH_SERVICE_LOGO.PNG/240px-PUBLIC_HEALTH_SERVICE_LOGO.PNG 2x" data-file-width="579" data-file-height="579" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PUBLIC_HEALTH_SERVICE_LOGO.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Seal of the U.S. Public Health Service, 1798</div></div></div>
<p>The surgeon general reports to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Assistant_Secretary_for_Health" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Assistant Secretary for Health">assistant secretary for health</a> (ASH), who may be a four-star <a href="/wiki/Admiral_(United_States)" title="Admiral (United States)">admiral</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Public_Health_Service_Commissioned_Corps" title="United States Public Health Service Commissioned Corps">commissioned corps</a>, and who serves as the principal adviser to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Health_and_Human_Services" title="United States Secretary of Health and Human Services">secretary of health and human services</a> on public health and <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_science" class="mw-redirect" title="Healthcare science">scientific</a> issues. The <a href="/wiki/Surgeon_General" class="mw-redirect" title="Surgeon General">surgeon general</a> is the overall head of the Commissioned Corps, a 6,500-member cadre of uniformed <a href="/wiki/Health_care_provider" class="mw-redirect" title="Health care provider">health professionals</a> who are on call 24 hours a day, and can be dispatched by the secretary of HHS or the assistant secretary for Health in the event of a public health emergency.
</p><p>The surgeon general is also the ultimate award authority for several public health awards and decorations, the highest of which that can be directly awarded is the <a href="/wiki/Surgeon_General%27s_Medallion" title="Surgeon General&#39;s Medallion">Surgeon General's Medallion</a> (the highest award bestowed by board action is the <a href="/wiki/Public_Health_Service_Distinguished_Service_Medal" title="Public Health Service Distinguished Service Medal">Public Health Service Distinguished Service Medal</a>). The surgeon general also has many informal duties, such as educating the American public about health issues and advocating healthy lifestyle choices.
</p><p>The office also periodically issues health warnings. Perhaps the best known example of this is the <a href="/wiki/Tobacco_packaging_warning_messages" title="Tobacco packaging warning messages">surgeon general's warning label</a> that has been present on all packages of American tobacco cigarettes since 1966.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> A similar health warning has appeared on <a href="/wiki/Alcoholic_Beverage_Labeling_Act" title="Alcoholic Beverage Labeling Act">alcoholic beverages labels</a> since 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surgeon_General_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PHSCC_Collar_Device.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/PHSCC_Collar_Device.png/120px-PHSCC_Collar_Device.png" decoding="async" width="120" height="120" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/PHSCC_Collar_Device.png/180px-PHSCC_Collar_Device.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/PHSCC_Collar_Device.png/240px-PHSCC_Collar_Device.png 2x" data-file-width="492" data-file-height="494" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PHSCC_Collar_Device.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>US Public Health Service Collar Device</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CAP_DEVICE_UNMOUNTED_PHS.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/CAP_DEVICE_UNMOUNTED_PHS.PNG/120px-CAP_DEVICE_UNMOUNTED_PHS.PNG" decoding="async" width="120" height="117" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/CAP_DEVICE_UNMOUNTED_PHS.PNG/180px-CAP_DEVICE_UNMOUNTED_PHS.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/CAP_DEVICE_UNMOUNTED_PHS.PNG/240px-CAP_DEVICE_UNMOUNTED_PHS.PNG 2x" data-file-width="488" data-file-height="476" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CAP_DEVICE_UNMOUNTED_PHS.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>US Public Health Service Cap Device</div></div></div>
<p>In 1798, Congress established the Marine Hospital Fund, a network of hospitals that cared for sick and disabled seamen. The Marine Hospital Fund was reorganized along military lines in 1870 and became the <a href="/wiki/Marine_Hospital_Service" title="Marine Hospital Service">Marine Hospital Service</a>—predecessor to today’s <a href="/wiki/United_States_Public_Health_Service" title="United States Public Health Service">United States Public Health Service</a>. The service became a separate bureau of the Treasury Department with its own staff, administration, headquarters in Washington, D.C, and the position of supervising surgeon (later <b>surgeon general</b>).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After 141 years under the Treasury Department, the Service came under the Federal Security Agency in 1939, then the Department of Health, Education, and Welfare (HEW) in 1953, and finally the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services" title="United States Department of Health and Human Services">United States Department of Health and Human Services (HHS)</a>.
</p><p>Some surgeons general are notable for being outspoken and/or advocating controversial proposals on how to reform the <a href="/wiki/Health_care_in_the_United_States" title="Health care in the United States">U.S. health system</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The office is not a particularly powerful one, and has little direct statutory impact on policy-making, but Surgeons General are often vocal advocates of precedent-setting, far-sighted, unconventional, or even unpopular health policies.
</p>
<ul><li>On January 11, 1964, Rear Admiral <a href="/wiki/Luther_Terry" title="Luther Terry">Luther Terry</a>, M.D., published a landmark <a href="/wiki/Smoking_and_Health:_Report_of_the_Advisory_Committee_to_the_Surgeon_General_of_the_United_States" title="Smoking and Health: Report of the Advisory Committee to the Surgeon General of the United States">report</a> saying that smoking may be hazardous to health,<sup id="cite_ref-Fenster_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fenster-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> sparking nationwide anti-smoking efforts. Terry and his committee defined cigarette smoking of nicotine as <i>not an <a href="/wiki/Substance_dependence" title="Substance dependence">addiction</a>.</i> The committee itself consisted largely of physicians who themselves smoked. This report went uncorrected for 24 years.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In 1986, Vice Admiral Dr. <a href="/wiki/C._Everett_Koop" title="C. Everett Koop">C. Everett Koop</a>'s report on AIDS called for some form of AIDS education in the early grades of elementary school, and gave full support for using condoms for disease prevention.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> He also resisted pressure from the <a href="/wiki/Reagan_administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Reagan administration">Reagan administration</a> to report that <a href="/wiki/Abortion" title="Abortion">abortion</a> was psychologically harmful to women, stating he believed it was a moral issue rather than one concerning the public health.</li>
<li>In 1994, Vice Admiral Dr. <a href="/wiki/Joycelyn_Elders" title="Joycelyn Elders">Joycelyn Elders</a> spoke at a United Nations conference on AIDS. She was asked whether it would be appropriate to promote masturbation as a means of preventing young people from engaging in riskier forms of sexual activity. She replied, "I think that it is part of human sexuality, and perhaps it should be taught."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Elders also spoke in favor of studying drug legalization. In a reference to the national <a href="/wiki/Abortion_in_the_United_States" title="Abortion in the United States">abortion</a> issue, she said, "We really need to get over this love affair with the fetus and start worrying about children."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> She was fired by President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> in December 1994.</li></ul>
<p>The U.S. <a href="/wiki/Surgeon_General_of_the_United_States_Army" title="Surgeon General of the United States Army">Army</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surgeon_General_of_the_United_States_Navy" title="Surgeon General of the United States Navy">Navy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Surgeon_General_of_the_United_States_Air_Force" title="Surgeon General of the United States Air Force">Air Force</a> also have officers overseeing medical matters in their respective services who hold the title <i>Surgeon General.</i>
</p><p>The insignia of the surgeon general, and the USPHS, use the <a href="/wiki/Caduceus_as_a_symbol_of_medicine" title="Caduceus as a symbol of medicine">caduceus</a> as opposed to the <a href="/wiki/Rod_of_Asclepius" title="Rod of Asclepius">Rod of Asclepius</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_rank">Service rank</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surgeon_General_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Service rank">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:127px;"><a href="/wiki/File:US_PHS_O9_insignia.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/US_PHS_O9_insignia.svg/125px-US_PHS_O9_insignia.svg.png" decoding="async" width="125" height="214" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/US_PHS_O9_insignia.svg/188px-US_PHS_O9_insignia.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/US_PHS_O9_insignia.svg/250px-US_PHS_O9_insignia.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="245" data-file-height="420" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:US_PHS_O9_insignia.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The stars, shoulder boards, and sleeve stripes of the surgeon general</div></div></div>
<p>The surgeon general is a commissioned officer in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Public_Health_Service_Commissioned_Corps" title="United States Public Health Service Commissioned Corps">U.S. Public Health Service Commissioned Corps</a>, one of the seven <a href="/wiki/Uniformed_services_of_the_United_States" title="Uniformed services of the United States">uniformed services of the United States</a>, and by law holds the rank of <a href="/wiki/Vice_admiral_(United_States)" title="Vice admiral (United States)">vice admiral</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-grades_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grades-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Officers of the Public Health Service Commissioned Corps are classified as non-combatants, but can be subjected to the <a href="/wiki/Uniform_Code_of_Military_Justice" title="Uniform Code of Military Justice">Uniform Code of Military Justice</a> (UCMJ) and the <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Conventions" title="Geneva Conventions">Geneva Conventions</a> when designated by the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">commander-in-chief</a> as a military force or if they are detailed or assigned to work with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_armed_forces" class="mw-redirect" title="United States armed forces">armed forces</a>. Officer members of these services wear uniforms that are similar to those worn by the United States Navy, except that the commissioning devices, buttons, and insignia are unique. Officers in the U.S. Public Health Service wear unique devices that are similar to U.S. Navy staff corps officers (e.g., Navy Medical Service Corps, <a href="/wiki/Navy_Supply_Corps" title="Navy Supply Corps">Supply Corps</a>, etc.).
</p><p>The only surgeon general to actually hold the rank of a four-star <a href="/wiki/Admiral_(United_States)" title="Admiral (United States)">admiral</a> was <a href="/wiki/David_Satcher" title="David Satcher">David Satcher</a> (born 1941, served 1998–2002). This was because he served simultaneously in the positions of surgeon general (three-star) and <a href="/wiki/Assistant_Secretary_for_Health" title="Assistant Secretary for Health">assistant secretary for health</a> (which is a four-star office).<sup id="cite_ref-satcher_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-satcher-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Woodworth" title="John Maynard Woodworth">John Maynard Woodworth</a> (1837–1879, served 1871–1879), the first holder of the office as "Supervising Surgeon", is the only surgeon general to not hold a rank.
</p>
