<p>A <b>mother</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Female" title="Female">female</a> parent of a <a href="/wiki/Child" title="Child">child</a>. Mothers are <a href="/wiki/Women" class="mw-redirect" title="Women">women</a> who inhabit or perform the role of bearing some relation to their <a href="/wiki/Children" class="mw-redirect" title="Children">children</a>, who may or may not be their biological <a href="/wiki/Offspring" title="Offspring">offspring</a>. Thus, dependent on the context, women can be considered mothers by virtue of having given <a href="/wiki/Childbirth" title="Childbirth">birth</a>, by raising their child(ren), supplying their ovum for <a href="/wiki/Fertilisation" title="Fertilisation">fertilisation</a>, or some combination thereof. Such conditions provide a way of delineating the concept of motherhood, or the state of being a mother. Women who meet the third and first categories usually fall under the terms 'birth mother' or 'biological mother', regardless of whether the individual in question goes on to parent their child. Accordingly, a woman who meets only the second condition may be considered an <a href="/wiki/Adoption" title="Adoption">adoptive</a> mother, and those who meet only the first or only the third a <a href="/wiki/Surrogate_pregnancy" class="mw-redirect" title="Surrogate pregnancy">surrogacy</a> mother.
</p><p>An <b>adoptive mother</b> is a female who has become the child's parent through the legal process of <a href="/wiki/Adoption" title="Adoption">adoption</a>. A <b>biological mother</b> is the female genetic contributor to the creation of the infant, through <a href="/wiki/Sexual_intercourse" title="Sexual intercourse">sexual intercourse</a> or <a href="/wiki/Egg_donation" title="Egg donation">egg donation</a>. A biological mother may have legal obligations to a child not raised by her, such as an obligation of monetary support. A <b>putative mother</b> is a female whose biological relationship to a child is alleged but has not been established. A <b><a href="/wiki/Stepmother" title="Stepmother">stepmother</a></b> is a female who is the wife of a child's father and they may form a family unit, but who generally does not have the legal rights and responsibilities of a parent in relation to the child.
</p><p>The above concepts defining the role of mother are neither exhaustive nor universal, as any definition of 'mother' may vary based on how social, cultural, and religious roles are defined. The parallel conditions and terms for males: those who are (typically biologically) <a href="/wiki/Father" title="Father">fathers</a> do not, by definition, take up the role of fatherhood. Motherhood and fatherhood are not limited to those who are or have <a href="/wiki/Parenting" title="Parenting">parented</a>. Women who are <a href="/wiki/Pregnant" class="mw-redirect" title="Pregnant">pregnant</a> may be referred to as expectant mothers or mothers-to-be, though such applications tend to be less readily applied to (biological) fathers or adoptive parents.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The process of becoming a mother has been referred to as "matrescence".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjective</a> "maternal" refers to a mother and comparatively to "paternal" for a <a href="/wiki/Father" title="Father">father</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verb</a> "to mother" means to procreate or to sire a child from which also derives the noun "mothering". Related <a href="/wiki/Term_of_endearment" title="Term of endearment">terms of endearment</a> are <i>mom</i> (<i>mama</i>, <i>mommy</i>), <i>mum</i>, <i>mumsy</i>, mamacita (<i>ma</i>, <i>mam</i>) and <i>mammy</i>. A female role model that children can look up to is sometimes referred to as a <i>mother-figure</i>.
</p>

<p>Biological motherhood for <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">humans</a>, as in other <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">mammals</a>, occurs when a <a href="/wiki/Pregnant" class="mw-redirect" title="Pregnant">pregnant</a> female gestates a fertilized ovum (the "egg"). A female can become pregnant through <a href="/wiki/Sexual_intercourse" title="Sexual intercourse">sexual intercourse</a> after she has begun to <a href="/wiki/Ovulate" class="mw-redirect" title="Ovulate">ovulate</a>. In well-nourished girls, <a href="/wiki/Menarche" title="Menarche">menarche</a> (the first <a href="/wiki/Menstrual_cycle" title="Menstrual cycle">menstrual period</a>)  usually takes place around the age of 12 or 13.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Typically, a <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a> develops from the viable <a href="/wiki/Zygote" title="Zygote">zygote</a>, resulting in an <a href="/wiki/Embryo" title="Embryo">embryo</a>. <a href="/wiki/Gestation" title="Gestation">Gestation</a> occurs in the woman's <a href="/wiki/Uterus" title="Uterus">uterus</a> until the fetus (assuming it is carried to <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy_duration" class="mw-redirect" title="Pregnancy duration">term</a>) is sufficiently developed to be born. In humans, gestation is often around 9 months in duration, after which the woman experiences labor and gives birth. This is not always the case, however, as some babies are born <a href="/wiki/Preterm_birth" title="Preterm birth">prematurely</a>, late, or in the case of <a href="/wiki/Stillbirth" title="Stillbirth">stillbirth</a>, do not survive gestation. Usually, once the baby is born, the mother produces <a href="/wiki/Milk" title="Milk">milk</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Lactation" title="Lactation">lactation</a> process. The mother's <a href="/wiki/Breast_milk" title="Breast milk">breast milk</a> is the source of <a href="/wiki/Antibodies" class="mw-redirect" title="Antibodies">antibodies</a> for the infant's <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">immune system</a>, and commonly the sole source of nutrition for newborns before they are able to eat and digest other foods; older infants and toddlers may continue to be breastfed, in combination with other foods, which should be introduced from approximately six months of age.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Childlessness" title="Childlessness">Childlessness</a> is the state of not having children. Childlessness may have personal, social or political significance. Childlessness may be <a href="/wiki/Voluntary_childlessness" title="Voluntary childlessness">voluntary childlessness</a>, which occurs by choice, or may be involuntary due to health problems or social circumstances. Motherhood is usually voluntary, but may also be the result of <a href="/wiki/Forced_pregnancy" title="Forced pregnancy">forced pregnancy</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy_from_rape" title="Pregnancy from rape">pregnancy from rape</a>. Unwanted motherhood occurs especially in cultures which practice <a href="/wiki/Forced_marriage" title="Forced marriage">forced marriage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Child_marriage" title="Child marriage">child marriage</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-biological_mother">Non-biological mother</span></h2>
<p><i>Mother</i> can often apply to a woman other than the biological parent, especially if she fulfills the main social role in raising the child. This is commonly either an <a href="/wiki/Adoptive" class="mw-redirect" title="Adoptive">adoptive</a> mother or a <a href="/wiki/Stepmother" title="Stepmother">stepmother</a> (the biologically unrelated <a href="/wiki/Significant_other" title="Significant other">partner</a> of a child's <a href="/wiki/Father" title="Father">father</a>). The term "<a href="/wiki/Othermother" title="Othermother">othermother</a>" or "other mother" is also used in some contexts for women who provide care for a child not biologically their own in addition to the child's primary mother.
</p><p>Adoption, in various forms, has been practiced throughout history, even predating human civilization.<sup id="cite_ref-Conn2013_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conn2013-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Modern systems of adoption, arising in the 20th century, tend to be governed by comprehensive <a href="/wiki/Statutes" class="mw-redirect" title="Statutes">statutes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Regulations" class="mw-redirect" title="Regulations">regulations</a>. In recent decades, <a href="/wiki/International_adoption" title="International adoption">international adoptions</a> have become more and more common.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Adoption_in_the_United_States" title="Adoption in the United States">Adoption in the United States</a> is common and relatively easy from a legal point of view (compared to other Western countries).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In 2001, with over 127,000 adoptions, the US accounted for nearly half of the total number of adoptions worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surrogate_mother">Surrogate mother</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Surrogacy" title="Surrogacy">Surrogacy</a></div>
<p>A surrogate mother is a woman who bears a child that came from another woman's fertilized ovum on behalf of a couple unable to give birth to children. Thus the surrogate mother carries and gives birth to a child that she is not the biological mother of. Surrogate motherhood became possible with advances in <a href="/wiki/Reproductive_technologies" class="mw-redirect" title="Reproductive technologies">reproductive technologies</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/In_vitro" title="In vitro">in vitro</a> fertilization.
</p><p>Not all women who become pregnant via in vitro fertilization are surrogate mothers. Surrogacy involves both a genetic mother, who provides the ovum, and a gestational (or surrogate) mother, who carries the child to term.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motherhood_in_same-sex_relationships">Motherhood in same-sex relationships</span></h3>
<p>The possibility for lesbian and bisexual women in <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_relationship" title="Same-sex relationship">same-sex relationships</a> (or women without a partner) to become mothers has increased over the past few decades<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (October 2014)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> due to technological developments. Modern <a href="/wiki/Gay_parenting" class="mw-redirect" title="Gay parenting">lesbian parenting</a> (a term that somewhat <a href="/wiki/Bisexual_erasure" title="Bisexual erasure">erases</a> the bisexual case) originated with women who were in heterosexual relationships who later identified as lesbian or bisexual, as changing attitudes provided more acceptance for non-heterosexual relationships. Another way for such women to become mothers is through <a href="/wiki/LGBT_adoption" title="LGBT adoption">adopting</a> or <a href="/wiki/Foster_parent" class="mw-redirect" title="Foster parent">foster parenting</a>. There is also the option of self-<a href="/wiki/Insemination" title="Insemination">insemination</a> and clinically assisted donor insemination, forms of <a href="/wiki/Artificial_insemination" title="Artificial insemination">artificial insemination</a>. As fertility technology has advanced, more females not in a heterosexual relationship have become mothers through <a href="/wiki/In_vitro_fertilization" class="mw-redirect" title="In vitro fertilization">in vitro fertilization</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hooks2000_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hooks2000-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The social roles associated with motherhood are variable across time, culture, and social class.
<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Historically, the role of women was confined to some extent to being a mother and wife, with women being expected to dedicate most of their energy to these roles, and to spend most of their time taking care of the home. In many cultures, women received significant help in performing these tasks from older female relatives, such as mothers in law or their own mothers.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mother_and_Child_-_Neak_Pean_-_Angkor_-_Cambodia.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Mother_and_Child_-_Neak_Pean_-_Angkor_-_Cambodia.JPG/220px-Mother_and_Child_-_Neak_Pean_-_Angkor_-_Cambodia.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Mother_and_Child_-_Neak_Pean_-_Angkor_-_Cambodia.JPG/330px-Mother_and_Child_-_Neak_Pean_-_Angkor_-_Cambodia.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Mother_and_Child_-_Neak_Pean_-_Angkor_-_Cambodia.JPG/440px-Mother_and_Child_-_Neak_Pean_-_Angkor_-_Cambodia.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="3072" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mother_and_Child_-_Neak_Pean_-_Angkor_-_Cambodia.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mother and child in <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a></div></div></div>
<p>Regarding <a href="/wiki/Women_in_the_workforce" title="Women in the workforce">women in the workforce</a>, mothers are said to often follow a "<a href="/wiki/Mommy_track" title="Mommy track">mommy track</a>" rather than being entirely "<a href="/wiki/Career_women" class="mw-redirect" title="Career women">career women</a>". Mothers may be <a href="/wiki/Housewife" title="Housewife">stay at home mothers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Working_parent" title="Working parent">working mothers</a>. In recent decades there has been an increase in <a href="/wiki/Stay-at-home_dad" title="Stay-at-home dad">stay at home fathers</a> too. Social views on these arrangements vary significantly by culture: in Europe for instance, in German-speaking countries there is a strong tradition of mothers exiting the workforce and being homemakers.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Mothers have historically fulfilled the primary role in raising children, but since the late 20th century, the role of the father in child care has been given greater prominence and social acceptance in some Western countries.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The 20th century also saw more and more women entering paid work. <a href="/wiki/Mothers%27_rights" title="Mothers&#39; rights">Mothers' rights</a> within the workforce include <a href="/wiki/Maternity_leave" class="mw-redirect" title="Maternity leave">maternity leave</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parental_leave" title="Parental leave">parental leave</a>.
</p><p>The social role and experience of motherhood varies greatly depending upon location. Mothers are more likely than fathers to encourage assimilative and communion-enhancing patterns in their children.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Mothers are more likely than fathers to acknowledge their children's contributions in conversation.<sup id="cite_ref-speech_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-speech-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The way mothers speak to their children (<a href="/wiki/Baby_talk" title="Baby talk">"motherese"</a>) is better suited to support very young children in their efforts to understand speech (in context of the reference English) than fathers.<sup id="cite_ref-speech_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-speech-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the 1970s, <a href="/wiki/In_vitro_fertilization" class="mw-redirect" title="In vitro fertilization">in vitro fertilization</a> has made pregnancy possible at ages well beyond "natural" limits, generating ethical controversy and forcing significant changes in the social meaning of motherhood.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> This is, however, a position highly biased by Western world locality: outside the Western world, in-vitro fertilization has far less prominence, importance or currency compared to primary, basic healthcare, women's basic health, reducing infant mortality and the prevention of life-threatening diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Polio" title="Polio">polio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Typhus" title="Typhus">typhus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a>.
</p><p>Traditionally, and still in most parts of the world today, a mother was expected to be a <a href="/wiki/Married" class="mw-redirect" title="Married">married</a> woman, with birth outside of marriage carrying a strong <a href="/wiki/Social_stigma" title="Social stigma">social stigma</a>. Historically, this stigma not only applied to the mother, but also to her child. This continues to be the case in many parts of the developing world today, but in many Western countries the situation has changed radically, with single motherhood being much more socially acceptable now. For more details on these subjects, see <a href="/wiki/Legitimacy_(family_law)" title="Legitimacy (family law)">Legitimacy (family law)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Single_parent" title="Single parent">single parent</a>.
</p><p>The total fertility rate (TFR), that is, the number of children born per woman, differs greatly from country to country. The TFR in 2013 was estimated to be highest in <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">Niger</a> (7.03 children born per woman) and lowest in <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> (0.79 children/woman).<sup id="cite_ref-cia.gov_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cia.gov-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the TFR was estimated for 2013 at 2.06 births per woman.<sup id="cite_ref-cia.gov_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cia.gov-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In 2011, the average age at first birth was 25.6 and 40.7% of births were to unmarried women.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_and_safety_issues">Health and safety issues</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Maternal_mortality" class="mw-redirect" title="Maternal mortality">Maternal mortality</a></div>

<p>A <i>maternal death</i> is defined by <a href="/wiki/World_Health_Organization" title="World Health Organization">WHO</a> as "the death of a woman while pregnant or within 42 days of termination of pregnancy, irrespective of the duration and site of the pregnancy, from any cause related to or aggravated by the pregnancy or its management but not from accidental or incidental causes".<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>About 56% of maternal deaths occur in <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a> and another 29% in <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2006, the organization <a href="/wiki/Save_the_Children" title="Save the Children">Save the Children</a> has <a href="/wiki/Save_the_Children_State_of_the_World%27s_Mothers_report" title="Save the Children State of the World&#39;s Mothers report">ranked</a> the countries of the world, and found that <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> countries are the safest places to give birth, whereas countries in sub-Saharan Africa are the least safe to give birth.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> This study argues a mother in the bottom ten ranked countries is over 750 times more likely to <a href="/wiki/Maternal_mortality" class="mw-redirect" title="Maternal mortality">die in pregnancy or childbirth</a>, compared to a mother in the top ten ranked countries, and a mother in the bottom ten ranked countries is 28 times more likely to <a href="/wiki/Infant_mortality" title="Infant mortality">see her child die</a> before reaching their first birthday.
</p><p>The most recent data suggests that <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">Luxembourg</a> are the safest countries in terms of maternal death and <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">Central African Republic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malawi" title="Malawi">Malawi</a> are the most dangerous.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Childbirth" title="Childbirth">Childbirth</a> is an inherently dangerous and risky process, subject to many complications. The "natural" mortality rate of childbirth—where nothing is done to avert maternal death—has been estimated as being 1500 deaths per 100,000 births.<sup id="cite_ref-vanlerberghe_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vanlerberghe-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Modern medicine has greatly alleviated the risk of childbirth. In modern Western countries the current maternal mortality rate is around 10 deaths per 100,000 births.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious">Religious</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Parvati_Ganesha.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Parvati_Ganesha.jpg/220px-Parvati_Ganesha.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="309" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Parvati_Ganesha.jpg/330px-Parvati_Ganesha.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Parvati_Ganesha.jpg/440px-Parvati_Ganesha.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1054" data-file-height="1479" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Parvati_Ganesha.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Hindu" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> mother goddess <a href="/wiki/Parvati" title="Parvati">Parvati</a> feeding her son, the elephant-headed wisdom god <a href="/wiki/Ganesha" title="Ganesha">Ganesha</a></div></div></div>
<p>Nearly all world religions define tasks or roles for mothers through either <a href="/wiki/Religious_law" title="Religious law">religious law</a> or through the <a href="/wiki/Glorification" title="Glorification">glorification</a> of mothers who served in substantial religious events. There are many examples of religious law relating to mothers and women.
</p><p>Major world religions which have specific religious law or <a href="/wiki/Biblical_canon" title="Biblical canon">scriptural canon</a> regarding mothers include: <a href="/wiki/Women_in_Christianity" title="Women in Christianity">Christianity</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Women_in_Judaism" title="Women in Judaism">Judaism</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Gender_roles_in_Islam" title="Gender roles in Islam">Islam</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Some examples of honoring motherhood include the <a href="/wiki/Mary_(mother_of_Jesus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mary (mother of Jesus)">Madonna</a> or <i>Blessed Virgin Mother <a href="/wiki/Blessed_Virgin_Mary" class="mw-redirect" title="Blessed Virgin Mary">Mary</a></i> for <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholics</a>, and the multiple positive references to active womanhood as a mother in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Proverbs" title="Book of Proverbs">Book of Proverbs</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Hindu" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindu">Hindu</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Mother_Goddess#Hinduism" class="mw-redirect" title="Mother Goddess">Mother Goddess</a> and <a href="/wiki/Demeter" title="Demeter">Demeter</a> of ancient Greek pre-Christian belief are also mothers.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mother-offspring_violence">Mother-offspring violence</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Orestes_Pursued_by_the_Furies_by_William-Adolphe_Bouguereau_(1862)_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Orestes_Pursued_by_the_Furies_by_William-Adolphe_Bouguereau_%281862%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/220px-Orestes_Pursued_by_the_Furies_by_William-Adolphe_Bouguereau_%281862%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="183" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Orestes_Pursued_by_the_Furies_by_William-Adolphe_Bouguereau_%281862%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/330px-Orestes_Pursued_by_the_Furies_by_William-Adolphe_Bouguereau_%281862%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Orestes_Pursued_by_the_Furies_by_William-Adolphe_Bouguereau_%281862%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/440px-Orestes_Pursued_by_the_Furies_by_William-Adolphe_Bouguereau_%281862%29_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" data-file-width="7884" data-file-height="6547" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Orestes_Pursued_by_the_Furies_by_William-Adolphe_Bouguereau_(1862)_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Orestes_Pursued_by_the_Furies" title="Orestes Pursued by the Furies">Orestes Pursued by the Furies</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/William-Adolphe_Bouguereau" title="William-Adolphe Bouguereau">William-Adolphe Bouguereau</a>. <a href="/wiki/Clytemnestra" title="Clytemnestra">Clytemnestra</a> was murdered by <a href="/wiki/Orestes" title="Orestes">Orestes</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Furies" class="mw-redirect" title="Furies">Furies</a> torment him for his crime</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Matricide" title="Matricide">Matricide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Filicide" title="Filicide">Filicide</a></div>
<p>History records many conflicts between mothers and their children. Some even resulted in murder, such as the conflict between <a href="/wiki/Cleopatra_III_of_Egypt" title="Cleopatra III of Egypt">Cleopatra III of Egypt</a> and her son <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_X" class="mw-redirect" title="Ptolemy X">Ptolemy X</a>.
</p><p>In modern cultures, matricide (the killing of one's mother) and filicide (the killing of one's son or daughter) have been studied but remain poorly understood. <a href="/wiki/Psychosis" title="Psychosis">Psychosis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Schizophrenia" title="Schizophrenia">schizophrenia</a> are common causes of both,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-West_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-West-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> and young, indigent mothers with a history of <a href="/wiki/Domestic_abuse" class="mw-redirect" title="Domestic abuse">domestic abuse</a> are slightly more likely to commit filicide.<sup id="cite_ref-West_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-West-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Mothers are more likely to commit filicide than fathers when the child is 8 years old or younger.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Matricide is most frequently committed by adult sons.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States in 2012, there were 130 matricides (0.4 per million people) and 383 filicides (1.2 per million), or 1.4 incidents per day.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_art">In art</span></h2>

<p>Throughout history mothers with their children have often been the subject of artistic works, such as paintings, sculptures or writings.
</p><p>Fourth century grave reliefs on the island of <a href="/wiki/Rhodes" title="Rhodes">Rhodes</a> depicted mothers with children.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Paintings of mothers with their children have a long tradition in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. In the 18th century, these works embodied the Enlightenment's preoccupation with strong family bonds and the relation between mothers and children.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the end of the nineteenth century, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Cassatt" title="Mary Cassatt">Mary Cassatt</a> was a painter well known for her portraits of mothers.
</p><p>Many contemporary movies portray mothers.
</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mama_and_papa" title="Mama and papa">Mama and papa</a></div>
<p>The proverbial "first word" of an infant often sounds like "ma" or "mama". This strong association of that sound with "mother" has persisted in nearly every language on earth, countering the natural localization of language.
</p><p>Familiar or colloquial terms for <i>mother</i> in English are:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Ma</i>(মা), <i>Amma</i> (আম্মা), <i>Ammu</i> (আম্মু) used in <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a></li>
<li><i>Aama</i>, <i>Mata</i> used in <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a></li>
<li><i>Mom</i> and <i>mommy</i> are used in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and parts of the <a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_(county)" title="West Midlands (county)">West Midlands</a> including <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.</li>
<li><i>Inay</i>, <i>Nanay</i>, <i>Mama</i>, <i>Ma</i>, <i>Mom</i>, <i>Mommy</i> are used in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a></li>
<li><i>Mum</i> and <i>mummy</i> and <i>mama</i> are used in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">Charles, Prince of Wales</a> publicly addressed his mother Queen <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a> as "Mummy" on the occasion of her Diamond Jubilee.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Ma</i>, <i>mam</i>, and <i>mammy</i> are used in <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, the Northern areas of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>; it is also used in some areas of the United States.</li></ul>
<p>In many other languages, similar pronunciations apply:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Amma</i> (அம்மா) or <i>Thai</i> (தாய்)  in <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil</a></li>
<li><i>Maa</i>, <i>aai</i>, <i>amma</i>, and <i>mata</i> are used in languages of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> like <a href="/wiki/Assamese_language" title="Assamese language">Assamese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marathi_language" title="Marathi language">Marathi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telugu_language" title="Telugu language">Telugu</a> etc.</li>
<li><i>Mamá</i>, <i>mama</i>, <i>ma</i>, and <i>mami</i> in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a></li>
<li><i>Mama</i> in <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">Slovak</a></li>
<li><i>Māma</i> (妈妈/媽媽) in <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a></li>
<li><i>Máma</i> in <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a></li>
<li><i>Maman</i> in <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> and <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a></li>
<li><i>Ma</i>, <i>mama</i> in <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">Indonesian</a></li>
<li><i>Mamaí</i>, <i>mam</i> in <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a></li>
<li><i>Mamma</i> in <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">Icelandic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latvian_language" title="Latvian language">Latvian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a></li>
<li><i>Dayik</i> in <a href="/wiki/Kurdish_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Kurdish language">Kurdish</a></li>
<li><i>Māman</i> or <i>mādar</i> in <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a></li>
<li><i>Mamãe</i> or <i>mãe</i> in <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a></li>
<li><i>Mā̃</i> (ਮਾਂ) in <a href="/wiki/Punjabi_language" title="Punjabi language">Punjabi</a></li>
<li><i>Mama</i> in <a href="/wiki/Swahili_language" title="Swahili language">Swahili</a></li>
<li><i>Em</i> (אם) in <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a></li>
<li><i>A'ma</i> (ܐܡܐ) in <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Aramaic language">Aramaic</a></li>
<li><i>Má</i> or <i>mẹ</i> in <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a></li>
<li><i>Mam</i> in <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a></li>
<li><i>Eomma</i> (엄마, <small>pronounced&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Korean" title="Help:IPA/Korean">[ʌmma]</a></span>) in <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a></li>
<li>In many south Asian cultures and the Middle East, the mother is known as <i>amma</i>, <i>oma</i>, <i>ammi</i> or "ummi", or variations thereof. Many times, these terms denote affection or a maternal role in a child's life.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h3>

<p>The modern English word is from Middle English moder, from Old English mōdor, from Proto-Germanic *mōdēr (cf. East Frisian muur, Dutch moeder, German Mutter), from Proto-Indo-European *méh₂tēr (cf. Irish máthair, Tocharian A mācar, B mācer, Lithuanian mótė). Other cognates include Latin māter, Greek μήτηρ, Common Slavic *mati (thence Russian мать (mat’)), Persian مادر (madar), and Sanskrit मातृ (mātṛ).
</p>
