	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Turnpike trusts</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about road tolls in the United Kingdom between the 17th to 19th centuries. For other toll roads, see <a href="/wiki/Toll_road" title="Toll road">Toll road</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">

</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hyde_park_turnpike_toll_gate.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/51/Hyde_park_turnpike_toll_gate.jpg/300px-Hyde_park_turnpike_toll_gate.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="191" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/51/Hyde_park_turnpike_toll_gate.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="312" data-file-height="199" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hyde_park_turnpike_toll_gate.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_London" title="Hyde Park, London">Hyde Park Gate</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, erected by the <a href="/wiki/Kensington" title="Kensington">Kensington</a> Turnpike Trust.  This was the first toll point encountered along the <a href="/wiki/Bath_Road" class="mw-redirect" title="Bath Road">Bath Road</a>, upon leaving London.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Turnpike trusts</b> were bodies set up by individual <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Parliament_in_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Acts of Parliament in the United Kingdom">acts of Parliament</a>, with powers to collect road <a href="/wiki/Toll_road" title="Toll road">tolls</a> for maintaining the principal roads in <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Britain</a> from the 17th but especially during the 18th and 19th centuries. At the peak, in the 1830s, over 1,000 trusts<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  administered around 30,000 miles (48,000&#160;km) of <b>turnpike road</b> in England and Wales, taking tolls at almost 8,000 toll-gates and side-bars.<sup id="cite_ref-Searlep798_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Searlep798-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the early 19th century the concept of the turnpike trust was adopted and adapted to manage roads within the British Empire (Ireland, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, India, and South Africa) and in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-Searlep798_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Searlep798-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Turnpikes declined with the <a href="/wiki/Railway_mania" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway mania">coming of the railways</a> and then the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1888" title="Local Government Act 1888">Local Government Act 1888</a> gave responsibility for maintaining main roads to <a href="/wiki/County_council" title="County council">county councils</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county borough</a> councils.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Turnpike_trusts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "turnpike" originates from the similarity of the gate used to control access to the road, to the barriers once used to defend against attack by <a href="/wiki/Cavalry" title="Cavalry">cavalry</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Cheval_de_frise" title="Cheval de frise">Cheval de frise</a>).  The turnpike consisted of a row of <a href="/wiki/Pike_(weapon)" title="Pike (weapon)">pikes</a> or bars, each sharpened at one end, and attached to horizontal members which were secured at one end to an upright pole or axle, which could be rotated to open or close the gate.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precursors_to_turnpike_trusts">Precursors to turnpike trusts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Turnpike_trusts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Precursors to turnpike trusts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Highgate_wide.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/73/Highgate_wide.jpg/220px-Highgate_wide.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="131" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/73/Highgate_wide.jpg/330px-Highgate_wide.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/73/Highgate_wide.jpg/440px-Highgate_wide.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2460" data-file-height="1470" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Highgate_wide.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Great_North_Road_(Great_Britain)" title="Great North Road (Great Britain)">Great North Road</a> near Highgate on the approach to London before turnpiking.  The highway was deeply rutted and spread onto adjoining land.</div></div></div> 
<p><a href="/wiki/Pavage" title="Pavage">Pavage</a> grants, originally made for paving the marketplace or streets of towns, began also to be used for maintaining some roads between towns in the 14th century. These grants were made by <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">letters patent</a>, almost invariably for a limited term, presumably the time likely to be required to pay for the required works.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Tudor statutes had placed responsibility on each <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">parish</a> <a href="/wiki/Vestry" title="Vestry">vestry</a> to maintain all its roads.  This arrangement was adequate for roads that the parishioners used themselves but proved unsatisfactory for the principal highways that were used by long-distance travellers and waggoners.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> During the late 17th century, the piecemeal approach to road maintenance caused acute problems on the main routes into London. As trade increased, the growing numbers of heavy carts and carriages led to serious deterioration in the state of these roads and this could not be remedied by the use of parish statute labour. A parliamentary bill was tabled in 1621/22 to relieve the parishes responsible for part of the Great North Road by imposing a scale of tolls on various sorts of traffic. The toll revenue was to be used in repairing the road, however, the bill was defeated. During the following forty years, the idea of making travellers contribute to the repair of roads was raised on several occasions.<sup id="cite_ref-natarch1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-natarch1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many parishes continued to struggle to find funds to repair major roads and in <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire" title="Hertfordshire">Hertfordshire</a> <a href="/wiki/Way_warden" class="mw-redirect" title="Way warden">way wardens</a> on behalf of the vestries stood frequent trial at <a href="/wiki/Quarter_session" class="mw-redirect" title="Quarter session">quarter sessions</a> for their failure to keep the Old North Road in a good state of repair. In 1656 the parish of <a href="/wiki/Radwell,_Hertfordshire" title="Radwell, Hertfordshire">Radwell, Hertfordshire</a> petitioned their local sessions for help to maintain their section of the Great North Road. Probably as a result judges on the Hertfordshire, <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Huntingdonshire" title="Huntingdonshire">Huntingdonshire</a> circuit represented the matter to Parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-webb157_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webb157-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It then passed an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">act</a> that gave the local <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_peace" title="Justice of the peace">justices of the peace</a> powers to erect toll-gates on a section of the road, between <a href="/wiki/Wadesmill" title="Wadesmill">Wadesmill</a>, Hertfordshire; <a href="/wiki/Caxton,_Cambridgeshire" title="Caxton, Cambridgeshire">Caxton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Stilton" title="Stilton">Stilton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huntingdonshire" title="Huntingdonshire">Huntingdonshire</a> for 11 years, the revenues so raised to be used for the maintenance of the road in their jurisdictions.<sup id="cite_ref-webb157_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webb157-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-natarch1_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-natarch1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The toll-gate erected at Wadesmill was the prototype in England. Parliament then gave similar powers to the justices in other counties in England and Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-webb157_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webb157-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> An example is the first Turnpike Act for Surrey in 1696, during the reign of <a href="/wiki/William_III_of_England" title="William III of England">William III</a> for enhanced repairs between <a href="/wiki/Reigate" title="Reigate">Reigate</a> in Surrey and <a href="/wiki/Crawley" title="Crawley">Crawley</a> in Sussex.<sup id="cite_ref-gov4_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gov4-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The act made provision to erect <a href="/wiki/Toll_roads" class="mw-redirect" title="Toll roads">turnpikes</a>, and appoint toll collectors; also to appoint surveyors, who were authorized by order of the justices to borrow money at five per cent interest, on security of the tolls.<sup id="cite_ref-gov4_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gov4-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_first_turnpike_trusts">The first turnpike trusts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Turnpike_trusts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The first turnpike trusts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fyfield_act_1738.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/39/Fyfield_act_1738.jpg/170px-Fyfield_act_1738.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="287" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/39/Fyfield_act_1738.jpg/255px-Fyfield_act_1738.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/3/39/Fyfield_act_1738.jpg/340px-Fyfield_act_1738.jpg 2x" data-file-width="721" data-file-height="1217" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fyfield_act_1738.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The front page of the Act to create the <a href="/wiki/Fyfield,_Oxfordshire" title="Fyfield, Oxfordshire">Fyfield</a> Turnpike Trust on the Great Road to Gloucester in 1738</div></div></div> 
<p>The first scheme that had trustees who were not justices was established through a Turnpike Act in 1707, for a section of the London-<a href="/wiki/Chester,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Chester, England">Chester</a> road between Fornhill (near <a href="/wiki/Hockliffe" title="Hockliffe">Hockliffe</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Stony_Stratford" title="Stony Stratford">Stony Stratford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-holla_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-holla-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The basic principle was that the trustees would manage resources from the several parishes through which the highway passed, augment this with tolls from users from outside the parishes and apply the whole to the maintenance of the main highway. This became the pattern for the turnpiking of a growing number of highways, sought by those who wished to improve flow of commerce through their part of a county. <sup id="cite_ref-webb157_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webb157-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The proposal to turnpike a particular section of road was normally a local initiative and a separate Act of Parliament was required to create each trust. The Act gave the trustees responsibility for maintaining a specified part of the existing highway. It provided them with powers to achieve this; the right to collect tolls from those using the road was particularly important. Local gentlemen, clergy and merchants were nominated as trustees and they appointed a clerk, a treasurer and a surveyor to actually administer and maintain the highway. These officers were paid by the trust. Trustees were not paid, though they derived indirect benefits from the better transport, which improved access to markets and led to increases in rental income and trade.<sup id="cite_ref-webb159_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webb159-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first action of a new trust was to erect turnpike gates at which a fixed toll was charged. The Act gave a  maximum toll allowable for each class of vehicle or animal – for instance one shilling and six pence for a coach pulled by four horses, a penny for an unladen horse and ten pence for a drove of 20 cows. The trustees could call on a portion of the statute duty from the parishes, either as labour or by a cash payment. The trust applied the income to pay for labour and materials to maintain the road. They were also able to mortgage future tolls to raise loans for new structures and for more substantial improvements to the existing highway. <sup id="cite_ref-webb159_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webb159-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tollgates_London_1801.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Tollgates_London_1801.jpg/250px-Tollgates_London_1801.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="184" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Tollgates_London_1801.jpg/375px-Tollgates_London_1801.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Tollgates_London_1801.jpg/500px-Tollgates_London_1801.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1400" data-file-height="1029" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tollgates_London_1801.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of the Turnpike Tollgates in London 1801.</div></div></div>
<p>The trusts applied some funds to erecting <a href="/wiki/Toll_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Toll house">tollhouses</a> that accommodated the pikeman or toll-collector beside the turnpike gate. Although trusts initially organised the collection of tolls directly, it became common for them to auction a lease to collect tolls. Specialist toll-farmers would make a fixed payment to the trust for the lease and then organise the day-to-day collection of the money, leaving themselves with a profit on their operations over a year.<sup id="cite_ref-webb159_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webb159-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The powers of a trust were limited, normally to 21 years, after which it was assumed that the responsibility for the now-improved road would be handed back to the parishes. However, trusts routinely sought new powers before this time limit, usually citing the need to pay off the debts incurred in repairing damage caused by a rising volume of traffic, or in building new sections of road.<sup id="cite_ref-webb159_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-webb159-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_growth_of_the_turnpike_system">The growth of the turnpike system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Turnpike_trusts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The growth of the turnpike system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Woodstock_Rollrt_1751tolls.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/77/Woodstock_Rollrt_1751tolls.jpg/170px-Woodstock_Rollrt_1751tolls.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="168" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/77/Woodstock_Rollrt_1751tolls.jpg/255px-Woodstock_Rollrt_1751tolls.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/7/77/Woodstock_Rollrt_1751tolls.jpg/340px-Woodstock_Rollrt_1751tolls.jpg 2x" data-file-width="834" data-file-height="826" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Woodstock_Rollrt_1751tolls.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The schedule of maximum tolls allowed on the <a href="/wiki/Woodstock,_Oxfordshire" title="Woodstock, Oxfordshire">Woodstock</a> to Rollright Turnpike Trust on the Great Road to Worcester in 1751</div></div></div> 
<p>During the first three decades of the 18th century, sections of the main radial roads into London were put under the control of individual turnpike trusts. The pace at which new turnpikes were created picked up in the 1750s as trusts were formed to maintain the cross-routes between the Great Roads radiating from London. Roads leading into some provincial towns, particularly in Western England, were put under single trusts and key roads in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> were turnpiked. In South Wales, the roads of complete counties were put under single turnpike trusts in the 1760s. A further surge of trust formation occurred in the 1770s, with the turnpiking of subsidiary connecting roads, routes over new bridges, new routes in the growing industrial areas and roads in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. About 150 trusts were established by 1750; by 1772 a further 400 were established and, in 1800, there were over 700 trusts.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In 1825 about 1,000 trusts controlled 18,000 miles (29,000&#160;km) of road in England and Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Frome-turnpike-sign.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Sign marking boundary between Parish and Turnpike Trust responsibility, Frome, Somerset" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Frome-turnpike-sign.jpg/220px-Frome-turnpike-sign.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="331" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Frome-turnpike-sign.jpg/330px-Frome-turnpike-sign.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Frome-turnpike-sign.jpg/440px-Frome-turnpike-sign.jpg 2x" data-file-width="532" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Frome-turnpike-sign.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Roadside sign marking boundary between Parish and Turnpike Trust responsibility, Christchurch Road East, Frome, Somerset</div></div></div><p>The Acts for these new trusts and the renewal Acts for the earlier trusts incorporated a growing list of powers and responsibilities. From the 1750s, Acts required trusts to erect <a href="/wiki/Milestone" title="Milestone">milestones</a> indicating the distance between the main towns on the road. Users of the road were obliged to follow what were to become rules of the road, such as driving on the left and not damaging the road surface. Trusts could take additional tolls during the summer to pay for watering the road in order to lay the dust thrown up by fast-moving vehicles. Parliament also passed a few general Turnpike Acts dealing with the administration of the trusts and restrictions on the width of wheels – narrow wheels were said to cause a disproportionate amount of damage to the road.
</p><p>The rate at which new trusts were created slowed in the early 19th century but the existing trusts were making major investments in highway improvement. The government had been directly involved in the building of military roads in Scotland following a rebellion in 1745, but the first national initiative was a scheme to aid communications with <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. Between 1815&#160;and&#160;1826 <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Telford" title="Thomas Telford">Thomas Telford</a> undertook a major reorganization of the existing trusts along the London to <a href="/wiki/A5_road_(Great_Britain)" title="A5 road (Great Britain)">Holyhead Road</a>, and the construction of large sections of new road to avoid hindrances, particularly in North Wales. 
</p><p>By 1838 the turnpike trusts in England were collecting £1.5 million p.a. from leasing the collection of tolls but had a cumulative debt of £7 million, mainly as mortgages.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Even at its greatest extent, the turnpike system only administered a fifth of the roads in Britain; the majority being maintained by the parishes.  A trust would typically be responsible for about 20 miles (32&#160;km) of highway, although exceptions such as the <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a> Turnpike Trust controlled 147 miles (237&#160;km) of roads radiating from the city. On the <a href="/wiki/Bath_Road" class="mw-redirect" title="Bath Road">Bath Road</a> for instance, a traveller from London to the head of the <a href="/wiki/Thames_Valley" title="Thames Valley">Thames Valley</a> in Wiltshire would pass through the jurisdiction of seven trusts, paying a toll at the gates of each. Although a few trusts built new bridges (e.g. at <a href="/wiki/Shillingford" title="Shillingford">Shillingford</a> over the Thames), most bridges remained a county responsibility. A few bridges were built with private funds and tolls taken at these (e.g., the present <a href="/wiki/Swinford_Toll_Bridge" title="Swinford Toll Bridge">Swinford Toll Bridge</a> over the Thames).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation_of_turnpike_trusts">Operation of turnpike trusts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Turnpike_trusts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Operation of turnpike trusts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Quality">Quality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Turnpike_trusts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Quality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Round_house_at_stanton_drew.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Round_house_at_stanton_drew.JPG/170px-Round_house_at_stanton_drew.JPG" decoding="async" width="170" height="198" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Round_house_at_stanton_drew.JPG/255px-Round_house_at_stanton_drew.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Round_house_at_stanton_drew.JPG/340px-Round_house_at_stanton_drew.JPG 2x" data-file-width="523" data-file-height="610" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Round_house_at_stanton_drew.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/The_Round_House,_Stanton_Drew" title="The Round House, Stanton Drew">The Round House</a> (Old Toll House) at <a href="/wiki/Stanton_Drew" title="Stanton Drew">Stanton Drew</a></div></div></div>
<p>The quality of early turnpike roads was varied.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Although turnpiking did result in some improvement to each highway, the technologies used to deal with geological features, drainage, and the effects of weather, were all in their infancy. Road construction improved slowly, initially through the efforts of individual surveyors such as <a href="/wiki/John_Metcalf_(civil_engineer)" title="John Metcalf (civil engineer)">John Metcalf</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> in the 1760s.  19th-century engineers made great advances, notably <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Telford" title="Thomas Telford">Thomas Telford</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Loudon_McAdam" title="John Loudon McAdam">John Loudon McAdam</a>. <sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The engineering work of Telford on the <a href="/wiki/A5_road_(Great_Britain)" title="A5 road (Great Britain)">Holyhead Road (now the A5)</a> in the 1820s reduced the journey time of the London <a href="/wiki/Stagecoach" title="Stagecoach">mail coach</a> from 45 hours to just 27 hours, and the best mail coach speeds rose from 5-6&#160;mph (8–10&#160;km/h) to 9-10&#160;mph (14–16&#160;km/h). McAdam and his sons were employed as general surveyors (consultant engineers) to many of the main turnpike trusts in southern England. They recommended the building of new sections of road to avoid obstructions, eased steep slopes and directed the relaying of existing road-beds with carefully graded stones to create a dry, fast-running surface (known as <a href="/wiki/Macadam" title="Macadam">Macadamising</a>). Coach design improved to take advantage of these better roads and in 1843 the London-to-<a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a> mail coach could complete the 170-mile (270-km) journey in 17 hours.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_impact">Social impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Turnpike_trusts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Social impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rebecca_Riots" title="Rebecca Riots">Rebecca Riots</a></div>
<p>The introduction of toll gates had been resented by local communities which had freely used the routes for centuries. Early Acts had given magistrates powers to punish anyone damaging turnpike property, such as defacing milestones, breaking turnpike gates or avoiding tolls. Opposition was particularly intense in mountainous regions where good routes were scarce. In <a href="/wiki/Mid_Wales" title="Mid Wales">Mid Wales</a> in 1839, new tolls on old roads sparked protests known as the <a href="/wiki/Rebecca_Riots" title="Rebecca Riots">Rebecca Riots</a>. There were sporadic outbursts of vandalism and violent confrontation by gangs of 50 to 100 or more local men, and gatekeepers were told that if they resisted they would be killed. In 1844, the ringleaders were caught and <a href="/wiki/Penal_transportation" title="Penal transportation">transported</a> to Australia as convicts.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  However, the result was that toll gates were dismantled and the trusts abolished in the six counties of <a href="/wiki/South_Wales" title="South Wales">South Wales</a>, their powers being transferred to a roads board for each county.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_end_of_the_system">The end of the system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Turnpike_trusts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The end of the system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By the early Victorian period toll gates were perceived as an impediment to free trade. The multitude of small trusts were frequently charged with being inefficient in use of resources and potentially suffered from petty corruption.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">railway era</a> spelt disaster for most turnpike trusts.  Although some trusts in districts not served by railways managed to increase revenue, most did not.  In 1829, the year before the <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_and_Manchester_Railway" title="Liverpool and Manchester Railway">Liverpool and Manchester Railway</a> opened, the Warrington and Lower Irlam Trust had receipts of £1,680 but, by 1834, this had fallen to £332.  The Bolton and Blackburn Trust had an income of £3,998 in 1846, but in 1847 following the completion of a <a href="/wiki/Ribble_Valley_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Ribble Valley Line">railway</a> between the two towns, this had fallen to £3,077 and, in 1849, £1,185.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The debts of many trusts became significant; forced mergers of solvent and debt-laden trusts became frequent, and by the 1870s it was feasible for Parliament to close the trusts progressively without leaving an unacceptable financial burden on local communities. From 1871, all applications for renewal were sent to a Turnpike Trust Commission.  This arranged for existing Acts to continue, but with the objective of discharging the debt, and returning the roads to local administration, which was by then by  highway boards.  The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1888" title="Local Government Act 1888">Local Government Act 1888</a> gave responsibility for maintaining main roads to <a href="/wiki/County_council" title="County council">county councils</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county borough</a> councils. When a trust was ended, there were often great celebrations as the gates were thrown open. The assets of the trust, such as tollhouses, gates and sections of surplus land beside the road were auctioned off to reduce the debt, and mortgagees were paid at whatever rate in the pound the funds would allow. 
</p><p>The legacy of the turnpike trust is the network of roads that still form the framework of the main road system in Britain. In addition, many roadside features such as milestones and tollhouses have survived,<sup id="cite_ref-tpt_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tpt-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> despite no longer having any function in the modern road management system.
</p>
