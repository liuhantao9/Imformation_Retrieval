<p>A <b>naming taboo</b> is a cultural <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">taboo</a> against speaking or writing the given names of exalted persons in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and within the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_cultural_sphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese cultural sphere">Chinese cultural sphere</a>. It was enforced by several laws throughout Imperial China, but its cultural and possibly religious origins predate the <a href="/wiki/Qin_dynasty" title="Qin dynasty">Qin dynasty</a>. Not respecting the appropriate naming taboos was considered a sign of lacking education and respect, and brought shame both to the offender and the offended person.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kinds">Kinds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_taboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Kinds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>The <i>naming taboo of the state</i> (国讳; 國諱) discouraged the use of the emperor's given name and those of his ancestors.  For example, during the <a href="/wiki/Qin_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Qin Dynasty">Qin Dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qin_Shi_Huang" title="Qin Shi Huang">Qin Shi Huang</a>'s given name Zheng (政) was avoided, and the first month of the year "Zhèng Yuè" (政月: the administrative month) was rewritten into "Zhēng Yuè" (正月: the upright month, note the change in the tone of pronunciation) and furthermore renamed as "Duān Yuè" (端月: the proper/upright month). The strength of this taboo was reinforced by law; transgressors could expect serious punishment for writing an emperor's name without modifications. In 1777, <a href="/wiki/Wang_Xihou" title="Wang Xihou">Wang Xihou</a> in his dictionary criticized the <a href="/wiki/Kangxi_dictionary" class="mw-redirect" title="Kangxi dictionary">Kangxi dictionary</a> and wrote the <a href="/wiki/Qianlong_Emperor" title="Qianlong Emperor">Qianlong Emperor</a>'s name without leaving out any stroke as required. This disrespect resulted in his and his <a href="/wiki/Nine_exterminations" class="mw-redirect" title="Nine exterminations">family's executions</a> and confiscation of their property.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> This type of naming taboo is no longer observed in modern China.</li>
<li>The <i>naming taboo of the clan</i> (家讳; 家諱) discouraged the use of the names of one's own ancestors. Generally, ancestor names going back to seven generations were avoided. In diplomatic documents and letters between clans, each clan's naming taboos were observed.</li>
<li>The <i>naming taboo of the holinesses</i> (圣人讳; 聖人諱) discouraged the use of the names of respected people.  For example, writing <a href="/wiki/Confucius" title="Confucius">Confucius</a>' name was taboo during the <a href="/wiki/Jin_Dynasty_(1115-1234)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jin Dynasty (1115-1234)">Jin Dynasty</a>.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Methods_to_avoid_offence">Methods to avoid offence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_taboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Methods to avoid offence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Omitting_a_stroke.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Omitting_a_stroke.png/200px-Omitting_a_stroke.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Omitting_a_stroke.png/300px-Omitting_a_stroke.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Omitting_a_stroke.png/400px-Omitting_a_stroke.png 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Omitting_a_stroke.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Avoidance of naming taboo: Example of omitting a stroke. The last stroke of each character of Kangxi Emperor's given name "玄" (xuán) and "燁" (yè) is omitted.</div></div></div>
<p>There were three ways to avoid using a taboo character:
</p>
<ul><li>Changing the character to another one, usually a <a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">synonym</a> or which sounded similar to the character being avoided. For example, the Xuanwu Gate (玄武門: the <a href="/wiki/Black_Tortoise" title="Black Tortoise">Black Warrior</a> Gate) of the <a href="/wiki/Forbidden_City" title="Forbidden City">Forbidden City</a> was renamed as "Shenwu" (神武門: <a href="/wiki/Gate_of_Divine_Might" class="mw-redirect" title="Gate of Divine Might">Gate of Divine Might</a>) in order to avoid using a character from the <a href="/wiki/Kangxi_Emperor" title="Kangxi Emperor">Kangxi Emperor</a>'s name, Xuanye (玄燁).</li>
<li>Leaving the character as a blank.</li>
<li>Omitting a stroke in the character, usually the final stroke.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_history">In history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_taboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Throughout Chinese history, there were emperors whose names contained common characters who would try to alleviate the burden of the populace in practicing name avoidance. For example, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Xuan_of_Han" title="Emperor Xuan of Han">Emperor Xuan of Han</a>, whose given name Bingyi (病已) contained two very common characters, changed his name to Xun (詢), a far less common character, with the stated purpose of making it easier for his people to avoid using his name.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Taizong_of_Tang" title="Emperor Taizong of Tang">Emperor Taizong of Tang</a>, whose given name Shimin (世民) also contained two very common characters, ordered that name avoidance only required the avoidance of the characters <i>Shi</i> and <i>Min</i> in direct succession and that it did not require the avoidance of those characters in isolation.
</p><p>However, Emperor Taizong's son <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Gaozong_of_Tang" title="Emperor Gaozong of Tang">Emperor Gaozong of Tang</a> effectively made this edict ineffective after his death, by requiring the complete avoidance of the characters <i>Shi</i> and <i>Min</i>, necessitating the <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Tang_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Chancellor of Tang Dynasty">chancellor</a> <a href="/wiki/Li_Shiji" title="Li Shiji">Li Shiji</a> to change his name to Li Ji.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In later dynasties, princes were frequently given names that contained uncommon characters to make it easier for the public to avoid them, should they become emperor later in life.
</p><p>The custom of naming taboo had a built-in contradiction: without knowing what the emperors' names were one could hardly be expected to avoid them, so somehow the emperors' names had to be informally transmitted to the populace to allow them to learn them in order to avoid them.  In one famous incident in 435, during the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Wei_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Wei Dynasty">Northern Wei Dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goguryeo" title="Goguryeo">Goguryeo</a> ambassadors made a formal request that the imperial government issue them a document containing the emperors' names so that they could avoid offending the emperor while submitting their king's petition.  <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Taiwu_of_Northern_Wei" title="Emperor Taiwu of Northern Wei">Emperor Taiwu of Northern Wei</a> agreed and issued them such a document.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  However, the mechanism of how the regular populace would be able to learn the emperors' names remained generally unclear throughout Chinese history.
</p><p>This taboo is important to keep in mind when studying ancient historical texts from the cultural sphere, as historical characters and/or locations may be renamed if they happen to share a name with the emperor in power (or previous emperors of the same dynasty) when the text was written. Thus, the study of naming taboos can also help date an ancient text.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_countries">In other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_taboo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In other countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Japan was heavily influenced by the naming taboo. In modern Japan, it concerns only the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Japan" title="Emperor of Japan">Emperor of Japan</a>, whom people only refer as Tennō Heika (天皇陛下, his Majesty the Emperor) or Kinjō Heika (今上陛下, his current Majesty). Ancient Japanese people had so much respect for this custom that historians forgot the actual names of many historical figures or even sometimes the correct pronunciation, as many <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">Han characters</a> have multiple pronunciations. For example, the son of <a href="/wiki/Oda_Nobunaga" title="Oda Nobunaga">Oda Nobunaga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oda_Nobukatsu" title="Oda Nobukatsu">Oda Nobukatsu</a>, is often called Oda Nobuo; but it mainly concerns more ancient historical figures like <a href="/wiki/Murasaki_Shikibu" title="Murasaki Shikibu">Murasaki Shikibu</a>, whose actual name may forever remain a mystery.
</p><p>In Vietnam, the family name <a href="/wiki/Huang_(surname)#Hoàng/Huỳnh" title="Huang (surname)">Hoàng</a> (黄) was changed to <a href="/wiki/Huang_(surname)#Hoàng/Huỳnh" title="Huang (surname)">Huỳnh</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Vietnam" title="Southern Vietnam">South</a> due to the naming taboo of Lord <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_Ho%C3%A0ng" title="Nguyễn Hoàng">Nguyễn Hoàng</a>'s name.  Similarly, the family name "<a href="/wiki/V%C5%A9" title="Vũ">Vũ</a>" (武) is known as "Võ" in the South.<sup id="cite_ref-ThierryInscriptions_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ThierryInscriptions-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
