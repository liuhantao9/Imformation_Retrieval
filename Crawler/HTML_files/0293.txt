	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Kalmyk names</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>Besides their own Kalmyk (<a href="/wiki/Oirats" title="Oirats">Oirat</a>) names, Kalmyks also use <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Standard_Tibetan" title="Standard Tibetan">Tibetan</a> names, which came into their culture through <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Buddhism" title="Tibetan Buddhism">Tibetan Buddhism</a>. Contemporary Kalmyks can also have Russian and other European names, due to the Kalmyks' orientation towards the Russian language, after they joined <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-D_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Original Kalmyk (<a href="/wiki/Oirats" title="Oirats">Oirat</a>) <i>individual names from a <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantic</a> point of view</i>:<sup id="cite_ref-D_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>The names of folk Kalmyk heroes - Sanal, Mergen, Tzayatr, Khontr, Mingian, Savr;</li>
<li>Derived from place names - Elistina, Ytsl, Altzkhuta, Kolor;</li>
<li>Words that describe colour or number - Tzatan (white), Notan (green), Ulan (red), Zurtan (six), Tavn (five);</li>
<li>Words that describe positive personal traits: Söökhlö (beautiful - female), Duya (gracious - female), Bata (tough);</li>
<li>Words that describe worldly goods - Bayn (wealth), Dzhirgan (happiness, life), Bayr (joy), Altn (gold).</li></ol>
<p><a href="/wiki/Standard_Tibetan" title="Standard Tibetan">Tibetan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> <i>individual names from a <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantic</a> point of view</i>:<sup id="cite_ref-D_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Astronomical objects - Angarak (Mars), Bembö (Saturn), Adyan (Sun);</li>
<li>Buddhist terms - Sants (enlightened), Buinta (goodness);</li>
<li>Positive personal traits and properties - Dzhav (protection), Tzeren (long life).</li></ol>
<p>Throughout history, <i>Kalmyks were known for using <a href="/wiki/Amulet" title="Amulet">amulet</a>-names</i>:<sup id="cite_ref-D_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Words denoting animal names - Chon (wolf), Elya (eagle), Nokha (dog), Ayuka (bear cub);</li>
<li>Different plant names - Tzetzgö (flower);</li>
<li>Among the names given to protect someone from "bad spirits", non-Kalmyk names with distinctive transcription were also used - Vazki (from Vasiliy), Mikula (from Nicolay), Yagur (from Yegor). Some contemporary Kalmyk surnames are derived from amulet-names: Nokhaev, Chonov, Chonaev.</li></ol>
<p>Double names were also quite well spread among the Kalmyks (in the Yandyko-Mochazhny, Khosheytsky and Erketenevsky uluses) - Sanzh-Garya (in the Khosheutsky Kalmyk ulus Sants-Ara), Erdny-Khal, Tzeren-Dorts. Today, double names are reserved for patronymics. Another Kalmyk custom was that of the khadm (name taboo), required by a new wife. When entering her husband's family, she would create a new name, by distorting his parents' names, instead of Badm -Yadm, instead of Dorts - Yerts.<sup id="cite_ref-D_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the introduction of the Russian administration (in the 16th century), Kalmyks received family names, derived from that of the father, like - Erdnyev (from Erdny), Badmaev (from Badma), Kichikov (from Kichik). If a Kalmyk was called Nema, his surname would be Dordzhyev, from his father's name Dorts but Nema's children's surnames would be Nemaev. Gradually, the family name became hereditary and a patronymic was added, derived from the father's name.<sup id="cite_ref-D_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-D-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
