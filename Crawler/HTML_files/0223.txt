	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Regnal name</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>A <b>regnal name</b>, or <b>reign name</b>, is the name used by <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarchs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">popes</a> during their <a href="/wiki/Reign" title="Reign">reigns</a> and, subsequently, historically. Since ancient times, some monarchs have chosen to use a different name from their original name when they accede to the monarchy. 
</p><p>The regnal name is usually followed by a <a href="/wiki/Regnal_number" title="Regnal number">regnal number</a>, written as a <a href="/wiki/Roman_numeral" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman numeral">Roman numeral</a>, to differentiate that monarch from others who have used the same name while ruling the same realm. In some cases, the monarch has more than one regnal name, but the regnal number is based on only one of those names, for example <a href="/wiki/Charles_X_Gustav_of_Sweden" title="Charles X Gustav of Sweden">Charles X Gustav of Sweden</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Tupou_V" title="George Tupou V">George Tupou V</a> of <a href="/wiki/Tonga" title="Tonga">Tonga</a>. If a monarch reigns in more than one realm, he or she may carry different ordinals in each one, as some realms may have had different numbers of rulers of the same regnal name. For example, the same person was both <a href="/wiki/James_VI_and_I" title="James VI and I">King James I of England and King James VI of Scotland</a>.
</p><p>The ordinal is not normally used for the first ruler of the name, but is used in historical references once the name is used again. Thus, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Queen Elizabeth I</a> of England was called simply "Elizabeth of England" until the accession of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a> almost four centuries later in 1952; subsequent historical references to the earlier queen retroactively refer to her as Elizabeth I. However, <a href="/wiki/Paul_I_of_Russia" title="Paul I of Russia">Tsar Paul I of Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Umberto_I_of_Italy" title="Umberto I of Italy">King Umberto I of Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_I_of_Spain" title="Juan Carlos I of Spain">King Juan Carlos I of Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haile_Selassie_I_of_Ethiopia" class="mw-redirect" title="Haile Selassie I of Ethiopia">Emperor Haile Selassie I of Ethiopia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_I" title="Pope John Paul I">Pope John Paul I</a> all used the ordinal I (first) during their reigns, while <a href="/wiki/Pope_Francis" title="Pope Francis">Pope Francis</a> does not. In spoken English, such names are pronounced as "Elizabeth the First", "George the Sixth", etc.
</p><p>In some countries in Asia, monarchs took or take <a href="/wiki/Chinese_era_name" title="Chinese era name">era names</a>. While era names as such are not used in many monarchies, sometimes eras are named after a monarch (usually long-lived), or a succession of monarchs of the same name. This is customary; there is no formal or general rule. For example, the whole period during which a succession of four Georges (<a href="/wiki/George_I_of_Great_Britain" title="George I of Great Britain">George I</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_II_of_Great_Britain" title="George II of Great Britain">II</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_III_of_Great_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="George III of Great Britain">III</a>, and <a href="/wiki/George_IV_of_Great_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="George IV of Great Britain">IV</a>) of the Hanoverian dynasty reigned in Great Britain became known as the <a href="/wiki/Georgian_era" title="Georgian era">Georgian era</a>. Conversely, although there were many Edwards, the <a href="/wiki/Edwardian_era" title="Edwardian era">Edwardian era</a> always refers to the reign of <a href="/wiki/Edward_VII" title="Edward VII">Edward VII</a> at the beginning of the 20th century.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Monarchies">Monarchies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regnal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Monarchies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_rulers">Ancient rulers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regnal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient rulers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Throne_name_(Ancient_Egypt)" class="mw-redirect" title="Throne name (Ancient Egypt)">Throne name (Ancient Egypt)</a></div>
<p>Ancient rulers in many parts of the world took regnal names or throne names which were different from their personal name.  This is known to be true, for instance, of several kings of <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">Assyria</a>, and appears to be the case for several Kings of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Judah" title="Kingdom of Judah">Judah</a>.  In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">Ancient Egypt</a>, Pharaohs took a number of names – the <i><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_royal_titulary#(Throne_name_(praenomen)" title="Ancient Egyptian royal titulary">praenomen</a></i> being the most commonly used, on occasion in conjunction with their personal name.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regnal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regnal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ethiopia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Ethiopian_Empire" title="Ethiopian Empire">Ethiopian Empire</a>, especially during the <a href="/wiki/Solomonic_dynasty" title="Solomonic dynasty">Solomonic dynasty</a>, many <a href="/wiki/List_of_Emperors_of_Ethiopia" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Emperors of Ethiopia">Emperors</a> would take a throne name, though this was not a general practice; a great number of rulers would remain known during their reign by their birth names. <a href="/wiki/Yekuno_Amlak_of_Ethiopia" class="mw-redirect" title="Yekuno Amlak of Ethiopia">Yekuno Amlak</a>, the founder of the Solomonic dynasty, took his father's name, Tasfa Iyasus, as his throne name. <a href="/wiki/Yagbe%27u_Seyon_of_Ethiopia" class="mw-redirect" title="Yagbe&#39;u Seyon of Ethiopia">Yagbe'u Seyon</a>, his son and heir, took the throne name <a href="/wiki/Solomon" title="Solomon">Salomon</a> after the biblical figure. <a href="/wiki/Amda_Seyon_I_of_Ethiopia" class="mw-redirect" title="Amda Seyon I of Ethiopia">Amda Seyon</a> took the throne name Gebre Mesqel, "slave of the <a href="/wiki/Christian_cross" title="Christian cross">cross</a>"; <a href="/wiki/Tewodros_I_of_Ethiopia" class="mw-redirect" title="Tewodros I of Ethiopia">Tewodros I</a> was Walda Ambasa, "son of the lion"; <a href="/wiki/Sarwe_Iyasus" title="Sarwe Iyasus">Sarwe Iyasus</a> was Mehreka Nañ "distributor of your [the <a href="/wiki/Names_of_God" title="Names of God">Lord</a>'s] mercy"; etc. Tafari Makonnen, the last sovereign Emperor of Ethiopia, took as his throne name <a href="/wiki/Haile_Selassie_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Haile Selassie I">Haile Selassie</a>, meaning "Power of the <a href="/wiki/Trinity" title="Trinity">Trinity</a>".
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nigeria">Nigeria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regnal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nigeria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the various extant <a href="/wiki/List_of_Nigerian_traditional_states" title="List of Nigerian traditional states">traditional states</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, the regnal names of the titled monarchs, who are known locally as the <a href="/wiki/Nigerian_traditional_rulers" title="Nigerian traditional rulers">traditional rulers</a>, serve two very important functions within the monarchical system. Firstly, seeing as how most states are organised in such a way as to mean that <i>all</i> of the legitimate descendants of the first man or woman to arrive at the site of any given community are considered its dynastic <a href="/wiki/Heir" class="mw-redirect" title="Heir">heirs</a>, their thrones are usually rotated amongst almost endless pools of contending cousins who all share the names of the founders of their houses as primary surnames. In order to tell them all apart from one another, secondary surnames are also used for the <a href="/wiki/Sept" title="Sept">septs</a> of each of the royal families that are eligible for the aforementioned rotations, names that often come from the names of state of the first members of their immediate lineages to rule in their lands. Whenever any of their direct heirs ascend the thrones, they often use their septs' names as reign names as well, using the appropriate ordinals to differentiate themselves from the founders of the said septs. An example of this is found in the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Lagos" title="Lagos">Lagos</a>, where the Adeniji-Adele family is distinguished from their numerous Adele cousins by the word <i>Adeniji</i>, which was actually the first name of the reigning founder of their branch of the dynasty, the <a href="/wiki/Oba_(ruler)" title="Oba (ruler)">Oba</a> <a href="/wiki/Adeniji_Adele" title="Adeniji Adele">Adeniji Adele II</a>. This distinction notwithstanding, both groups of <a href="/wiki/Dynast" class="mw-redirect" title="Dynast">dynasts</a> (as well as a number of other ones that don't have the name Adele as an official surname, such as that of the Oloye <a href="/wiki/Adekunle_Ojora" title="Adekunle Ojora">Adekunle Ojora</a>, a prominent nobleman of royal descent) are part of what is known as the Adele Ajosun Ruling House of Lagos.
</p><p>Beyond that which is described above, regnal names also serve in Nigeria and indeed in much of Africa as chronological markers in much the same way that those of Europe do (e.g. the <i>Victorian</i> era). Whenever one hears of a person describing what happened at the time when so and so ruled over any particular place or people, what he or she is actually saying is that an event happened within a finite period of time, one that is equal to the duration of the reign of the monarch in question. Now seeing as how it is possible (and in fact common, particularly among the southern tribes) for one individual to have several different names and aliases in a single life, a certain degree of uniformity in usage is required if the history of an entire state is to be tied to his or her name. It is for this reason that when new monarchs are enthroned, the uniqueness of their names is usually considered to be a matter of considerable importance (even when it is caused by nothing more than the adding of ordinals to them or the allowing of more than a generation to pass before their subsequent usage). An example of this can be found in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Benin" title="Kingdom of Benin">kingdom of Benin</a>, where the throne name of <a href="/wiki/Erediauwa_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Erediauwa I">Erediauwa I</a> became the surname of all of his immediate family in the Eweka royal house of the state, thus nominally tying them and their descendants to the era of his reign. This is especially obvious when their branch's name is compared to the last names of the said king's brothers and their heirs, named the Akenzuas after his father <a href="/wiki/Akenzua_II" title="Akenzua II">Akenzua II</a>, and his uncles and their heirs, named the Ewekas after his grandfather <a href="/wiki/Eweka_II" title="Eweka II">Eweka II</a>.
</p><p>In the case of the comparatively small number of Nigerian monarchs, such as Obi <a href="/wiki/Nnaemeka_Alfred_Ugochukwu_Achebe" class="mw-redirect" title="Nnaemeka Alfred Ugochukwu Achebe">Nnaemeka Achebe</a> of <a href="/wiki/Onitsha" title="Onitsha">Onitsha</a>, who do not make use of regnal names as a result of a variety of reasons, pre-coronation names are maintained during their reigns.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regnal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In parts of <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>, it is more a rule than an exception that monarchs take additional names when ascending, and quite often discard the name they were known by as princes. Often the assumed name is different from the childhood name, and a new <a href="/wiki/Temple_name" title="Temple name">temple name</a> could be assumed. A <a href="/wiki/Posthumous_name" title="Posthumous name">posthumous name</a> is sometimes accorded to a deceased monarch. Rebel leaders may also take regnal names. The regnal names of some monarchs were long, for example <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BD_Th%C3%A1i_T%E1%BB%95" title="Lý Thái Tổ">Lý Thái Tổ</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jiajing_Emperor" title="Jiajing Emperor">Emperor Shizong of Ming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Gojong_of_the_Korean_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Emperor Gojong of the Korean Empire">Emperor Gojong of the Korean Empire</a>. While many rulers in east Asia (including in China, Vietnam, Korea and Japan) took regnal names based on <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese characters</a>, some monarchs of <a href="/wiki/Xu_(state)" title="Xu (state)">Xu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xiongnu" title="Xiongnu">Xiongnu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tuyuhun_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Tuyuhun Kingdom">Tuyuhun Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rouran_Khaganate" title="Rouran Khaganate">Rouran Khaganate</a>, <a href="/wiki/G%C3%B6kt%C3%BCrks" title="Göktürks">Göktürks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uyghur_Khaganate" title="Uyghur Khaganate">Uyghur Khaganate</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Mongol_Khans" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Mongol Khans">Mongol</a> took Chinese transliterated non-Chinese regnal names. For details on the multiple names assumed by individual East Asian monarchs, see:

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regnal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hungary">Hungary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regnal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hungary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the Medieval Age, when the <a href="/wiki/House_of_%C3%81rp%C3%A1d" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Árpád">House of Árpád</a> disappeared in 1301, two of the monarchs that claimed the throne and were crowned chose a different name. <a href="/wiki/Otto_III,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="Otto III, Duke of Bavaria">Otto III, Duke of Bavaria</a> became Bela V of Hungary, taking the name of his maternal grandfather, <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_IV_of_Hungary" title="Béla IV of Hungary">Béla IV of Hungary</a>. In the other hand, <a href="/wiki/Wenceslaus_III_of_Bohemia" title="Wenceslaus III of Bohemia">Wenceslaus III of Bohemia</a> signed his royal documents in Hungary as Ladislas, this being a very traditional name in the Kingdom.
</p><p>Later during the first half of the 14th century, <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_Hungary" title="Charles I of Hungary">Charles I of Hungary</a> signed as "<i>Carolus rex</i>", but in fact his birth name was the Italian <i>Caroberto</i>. This is why he is often referred to by Hungarian historians as "Charles Robert of Hungary".
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Netherlands">Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regnal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>All ruling male members of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Orange-Nassau" title="House of Orange-Nassau">House of Orange-Nassau</a> bore the name <a href="/wiki/Willem" title="Willem">Willem</a> (William). The current king of the Netherlands was christened <a href="/wiki/Willem-Alexander_of_the_Netherlands" title="Willem-Alexander of the Netherlands">Willem-Alexander</a>. During an interview in 1997 he said he intended to rule under the name of Willem IV, but he had a change of mind. In a televised interview just before his inauguration, he announced he would continue to use the name Willem-Alexander, saying "I spent 46 years of my life under the name Willem-Alexander, and specifically under the nickname of Alexander. I think it would be weird to discard that because I become king of the country." Furthermore, he said he did not consider himself "a mere number", adding that regnal numbers reminded him of Dutch cattle naming conventions.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Poland">Poland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regnal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Poland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>When the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Piast" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Piast">House of Piast</a> disappeared and the <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuanian</a> <a href="/wiki/House_of_Jagiellon" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Jagiellon">House of Jagiellon</a> was elected in the figure of the High Duke <a href="/wiki/Jogaila" class="mw-redirect" title="Jogaila">Jogaila</a>, this monarch took the name of Władysław II, in honour of the previous Polish king (<a href="/wiki/W%C5%82adys%C5%82aw_I_the_Elbow-high" title="Władysław I the Elbow-high">Władysław I the Elbow-high</a>) with this traditional name. Similarly, when the Elector of Saxony, Frederick Augustus I, was elected king in 1697, he took the name of Augustus II. His son Frederick Augustus II crowned in 1734, also took the name of Augustus, becoming Augustus III.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Portugal">Portugal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regnal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Portugal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/List_of_Portuguese_monarchs" title="List of Portuguese monarchs">monarchs of Portugal</a> have traditionally used their first baptismal name as their regnal name upon their accession. The only notable exception was <a href="/wiki/Sancho_I_of_Portugal" title="Sancho I of Portugal">Sancho I</a>, who was born Martin of Burgundy (<i>Martinho de Borgonha</i>, in Portuguese). As he was a younger son, Martin was expected to join the clergy, and was named after <a href="/wiki/Martin_of_Tours" title="Martin of Tours">Saint Martin of Tours</a>, on whose feast day he had been born. When the <a href="/wiki/Heir_apparent" title="Heir apparent">heir apparent</a>, Henry, died, the prince's name was changed to Sancho, one with a more established royal tradition in the other Iberian monarchies (Navarre, Castile and Aragon).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regnal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Though most <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Monarchy of the United Kingdom">monarchs of the United Kingdom</a> have used their first baptismal name as their regnal name, on three occasions monarchs have chosen a different name.
</p><p>First, <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> had been christened Alexandrina Victoria, but took the throne under the name Victoria.
</p><p>When Victoria's son, Prince Albert Edward, became king in 1901, he took the regnal name <a href="/wiki/Edward_VII" title="Edward VII">Edward VII</a>, against the wish of his late mother.<sup id="cite_ref-Regnal_Names_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Regnal_Names-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The new king declared that he chose the name Edward alone as an honoured name borne by six of his predecessors, and that he did not wish to diminish the status of <a href="/wiki/Prince_Albert_of_Saxe-Coburg_and_Gotha" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince Albert of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha">his father</a>, with whom alone among royalty the name Albert should be associated.
</p><p>In 1936, after the <a href="/wiki/Edward_VIII_abdication_crisis" title="Edward VIII abdication crisis">abdication crisis</a>, Prince Albert, Duke of York, assumed the throne as <a href="/wiki/George_VI" title="George VI">King George VI</a> rather than "King Albert". His full name was Albert Frederick Arthur George; like Edward VII and Victoria he used another of his names.
</p><p>There has been speculation that the current <a href="/wiki/Heir_apparent" title="Heir apparent">heir apparent</a> to the British throne, <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">Charles, Prince of Wales</a>, whose full name is Charles Philip Arthur George, may elect not to be known as "Charles III" out of concern about comparisons with <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_England" title="Charles II of England">Charles II of England</a> (who was known for his Catholic sympathies), <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I of England</a> (who was executed after the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Jacobitism" title="Jacobitism">Jacobite</a> memory of the "Young Pretender" <a href="/wiki/Charles_Edward_Stuart" title="Charles Edward Stuart">Charles Edward Stuart</a> (who claimed the title "Charles III").<sup id="cite_ref-CallMeGeorge_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CallMeGeorge-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He may instead choose to be known as George VII in honour of his grandfather.<sup id="cite_ref-CallMeGeorge_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CallMeGeorge-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> However, the Prince has not as yet announced any decision.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regnal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/Robert_III_of_Scotland" title="Robert III of Scotland">John, Earl of Carrick</a> ascended the Scottish throne in 1390, it was deemed imprudent for him to take the regnal name of "John II", as recent kings named John had turned out badly: <a href="/wiki/John_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="John of England">in England</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/John_Balliol" title="John Balliol">in Scotland</a>. Furthermore, royal propaganda of the time held that John Balliol had not been a legitimate king of Scotland, making the new king's <a href="/wiki/Regnal_number" title="Regnal number">regnal number</a> also a tricky issue. To avoid these problems, John took the regnal name of <i>Robert III</i>, honouring <a href="/wiki/Robert_II_of_Scotland" title="Robert II of Scotland">his father</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_I_of_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert I of Scotland">great-grandfather</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Upon the accession, in 1952, of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a> the title <i>Elizabeth II</i> caused controversy in Scotland as there had never been a Scottish <i>Elizabeth I</i>. The Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> informed the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">British House of Commons</a> that the practice since the Union was to use the higher numeral. New <a href="/wiki/Royal_Mail" title="Royal Mail">Royal Mail</a> post boxes in Scotland, bearing the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Cypher" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Cypher">cypher</a> <i>E<small>II</small>R</i>, were vandalised, after which, to avoid further problems, post boxes and Royal Mail vehicles in Scotland bore only the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Scotland" title="Crown of Scotland">Crown of Scotland</a>. A legal case, <i><a href="/wiki/MacCormick_v._Lord_Advocate" class="mw-redirect" title="MacCormick v. Lord Advocate">MacCormick v. Lord Advocate</a></i> (1953 SC 396), contested the right of the Queen to title herself <i>Elizabeth II</i> in Scotland, arguing that to do so would be a breach of the <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Union_1707" title="Acts of Union 1707">Act of Union</a>. The case, however, was lost on the grounds that the pursuers had no title to sue the Crown, and also that the numbering of monarchs was part of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_prerogative" title="Royal prerogative">royal prerogative</a>, and thus not governed by the Act of Union.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_Empire">Roman Empire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regnal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Roman Empire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Emperors" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Emperors">Roman Emperors</a> usually had the titles of "Imperator Caesar Augustus" in their names. (which made their regnal names) Caesar came from the cognomen of <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Gaius Julius Caesar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Imperator" title="Imperator">Imperator</a> meant Commander and <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a> meant venerable or majestic. The name usually went in two ways, Imperator (<a href="/wiki/Praenomen" title="Praenomen">Praenomen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nomen_(Roman_name)" title="Nomen (Roman name)">Nomen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cognomen" title="Cognomen">Cognomen</a>) Caesar Augustus or Imperator Caesar (Praenomen, Nomen and Cognomen) Augustus. Also, Imperator became a Praenomen of Roman Emperors, Augustus and Caesar became a cognomen of theirs.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_offices">Religious offices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regnal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Religious offices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catholic_Church">Catholic Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regnal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Catholic Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Papal_name" title="Papal name">Papal name</a></div>
<p>Immediately after a new pope is elected, and accepts the election, he is asked by the <a href="/wiki/Dean_of_the_College_of_Cardinals" title="Dean of the College of Cardinals">Dean of the College of Cardinals</a>, "By what name shall you be called?" The new pope chooses the name by which he will be known. The senior Cardinal Deacon, or <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Protodeacon" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardinal Protodeacon">Cardinal Protodeacon</a>, then appears on the balcony of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter%27s_Basilica" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Peter&#39;s Basilica">Saint Peter's Basilica</a> to proclaim the new Pope, informing the world of the man elected Pope, and under which name he would be known during his reign.
</p>
<blockquote lang="la" style="font-style:italic; width:42%; margin:1em 4%; float:left;"><div class="poem">
<p>Annuntio vobis gaudium magnum:<br />
<a href="/wiki/Habemus_Papam" class="mw-redirect" title="Habemus Papam">Habemus Papam</a>,<br />
Eminentissimum ac Reverendissimum Dominum,<br />
Dominum [forename],<br />
Sanctæ Romanæ Ecclesiæ Cardinalem [surname],<br />
qui sibi nomen imposuit [papal name].
</p>
</div></blockquote>
<blockquote style="width:42%; margin:1em 4%; float:left;"><div class="poem">
<p>I announce to you a great joy:<br />
We have a Pope,<br />
The Most Eminent and Most Reverend Lord,<br />
Lord [forename],<br />
Cardinal of the Holy Roman Church [surname],<br />
who conferred upon himself the name [papal name].
</p>
</div></blockquote>
<p>During the first centuries of the church, priests elected <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Rome" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Rome">bishop of Rome</a> continued to use their baptismal names after their elections. The custom of choosing a new name began in AD 533 with the election of <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_II" title="Pope John II">Mercurius</a>. Mercurius had been named after the Roman god <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(mythology)" title="Mercury (mythology)">Mercury</a>, and decided that it would not be appropriate for a pope to be named after a Roman god. Mercurius subsequently decreed that he would be known as John II. Since the end of the tenth century the pope has customarily chosen a new name for himself during his Pontificate; however, until the 16th century some pontiffs used their <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">baptismal names</a>.
</p><p>The last pope to use his baptismal name was <a href="/wiki/Pope_Marcellus_II" title="Pope Marcellus II">Pope Marcellus II</a> in 1555, a choice that was even then quite exceptional. The names chosen by popes are not based on any system other than general honorifics. They have been based on immediate predecessors, mentors, political similarity, or even after family members—as was the case with <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XXIII" title="Pope John XXIII">Pope John XXIII</a>. The practice of using the baptismal name as papal name has not been ruled out and future popes could elect to continue using their original names after being elected pope.
</p><p>Often the new pontiff's choice of name upon being elected to the papacy is seen as a signal to the world of whom the new pope will emulate or what policies he will seek to enact. Such is the case with <a href="/wiki/Benedict_XVI" class="mw-redirect" title="Benedict XVI">Benedict XVI</a> who, in fact, explained the <a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI#Choice_of_name" title="Pope Benedict XVI">reasons for his choice of name</a> during his first General Audience in St. Peter's Square, on 27 April 2005. On that occasion, he said that he wanted to remember "<a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XV" title="Pope Benedict XV">Pope Benedict XV</a>, that courageous prophet of peace, who guided the Church through turbulent times of war", and also "<a href="/wiki/Saint_Benedict_of_Nursia" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Benedict of Nursia">Saint Benedict of Nursia</a>, co-patron of Europe, whose life evokes the Christian roots of Europe".
</p><p>There has never been a <a href="/wiki/Pope_Peter_II" title="Pope Peter II">Pope Peter II</a>. Even though there is no specific prohibition against choosing the name Peter, bishops elected to the Papacy have refrained from doing so even if their own given name was Peter. This is because of a tradition that only <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Saint Peter</a> should have that honor. In the 10th century <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XIV" title="Pope John XIV">John XIV</a> used the regnal name <i>John</i> because his given name was Peter. While some <a href="/wiki/Antipope" title="Antipope">antipopes</a> did take the name Peter II, their claims are not recognized by the mainstream Roman Catholic Church, and each of these men only either has or had a minuscule following that recognized their claims.
</p><p>Probably because of the controversial <a href="/wiki/Antipope_John_XXIII" title="Antipope John XXIII">Antipope John XXIII</a>, new popes avoided taking the regnal name John for over 600 years until the election of <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XXIII" title="Pope John XXIII">Angelo Cardinal Roncalli</a> in 1958. Immediately after his election, there was some confusion as to whether he would be known as John XXIII or John XXIV. Cardinal Roncalli thus moved to immediately resolve by declaring that he would be known as John XXIII.
</p><p>In 1978, <a href="/wiki/Albino_Luciani" class="mw-redirect" title="Albino Luciani">Albino Luciani</a> became the first pope to use two names for his regnal name when he took the name <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_I" title="Pope John Paul I">John Paul I</a>, including the "I". He took the "John Paul" name to honor both John XXIII and <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_VI" title="Pope Paul VI">Paul VI</a>. With the unexpected death of John Paul I a little over a month later, <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Karol Wojtyła</a> took the name John Paul II to honor his immediate predecessor.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Antipope" title="Antipope">Antipopes</a> also have regnal names, and also use the ordinal to show their position in the line of previous pontiffs with their names. For example, <a href="/wiki/David_Bawden" title="David Bawden">David Bawden</a> took the name Michael I when declared pope in 1990.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_caliphates">Islamic caliphates</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Regnal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Islamic caliphates">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_caliphs" class="mw-redirect" title="List of caliphs">List of caliphs</a></div>
<p>The use of regnal names (in <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>, <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">laḳab</i></i> (sing.) <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">alḳāb</i></i> (pl.)<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>) was uncommon in the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Islamic_history" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval Islamic history">Medieval Islamic era</a> until the <a href="/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate" title="Abbasid Caliphate">Abbasid Caliphate</a>, when the first Abbasid caliph, <a href="/wiki/As-Saffah" title="As-Saffah">Abu al-Abbas Abdullah ibn Muhammad</a>, who overthrew the <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate" title="Umayyad Caliphate">Umayyad Caliphate</a>, used the laḳab as-Saffah ("the Generous"). This name carried a messianic association, a theme that would be continued by as-Saffah's successors.<sup id="cite_ref-Crone2005_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crone2005-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The use of regnal names among the caliphs lasted throughout the reign of the Abbasid Caliphate, until the institution was deposed after the defeat of the <a href="/wiki/Mamluk_Sultanate_(Cairo)" title="Mamluk Sultanate (Cairo)">Mamluk Sultanate</a> and the capture of Caliph <a href="/wiki/Al-Mutawakkil_III" title="Al-Mutawakkil III">Al-Mutawakkil III</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="Ottoman Army">Ottoman Army</a> in 1517.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Fatimid_Caliphate" title="Fatimid Caliphate">Fatimid caliphs</a> adopted the Abbasid use of <i>alḳāb</i> to assert their claims of authority.<sup id="cite_ref-Safran2000_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Safran2000-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
