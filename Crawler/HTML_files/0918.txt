<p><b>Yeadon</b> is a town within the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan borough</a> of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Leeds" title="City of Leeds">City of Leeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.
</p><p>The majority of the town sits in the <a href="/wiki/Otley_and_Yeadon_(ward)" title="Otley and Yeadon (ward)">Otley and Yeadon</a> <a href="/wiki/Electoral_ward" class="mw-redirect" title="Electoral ward">ward</a> of <a href="/wiki/Leeds_City_Council" title="Leeds City Council">Leeds City Council</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Leeds_North_West_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Leeds North West (UK Parliament constituency)">Leeds North West</a> <a href="/wiki/UK_Parliament_constituencies" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Parliament constituencies">parliamentary constituency</a>. A southern part of the town (the area north of Swincar Avenue on Kirk Lane and south of the <a href="/wiki/A65_road" title="A65 road">A65</a>) is situated in the <a href="/wiki/Guiseley_and_Rawdon_(ward)" title="Guiseley and Rawdon (ward)">Guiseley and Rawdon</a> ward and the <a href="/wiki/Pudsey_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Pudsey (UK Parliament constituency)">Pudsey</a> constituency. 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Leeds_Bradford_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Leeds Bradford International Airport">Leeds Bradford International Airport</a> is located immediately east of the town and the population according to the 2011 Census was 22,233.<sup id="cite_ref-NOMIS_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NOMIS-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeadon,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_times">Early times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeadon,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The name comes from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i lang="ang" title="Old English language text">gaeh</i> and <i lang="ang" title="Old English language text">dūn</i> meaning <i>steep hill</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-KEPN_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KEPN-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and the hilly part of the High Street has been known as "the Steep" for centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-History_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  It was one of three hill settlements: Rawdon, Yeadon and Baildon, and it has been suggested that Rawdon was the main one, with Yeadon being used for burial (there are burial urns nearby) and other religious purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-History_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_Period">Medieval Period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeadon,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Medieval Period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was part of the <a href="/wiki/Elmet" title="Elmet">Kingdom of Elmet</a> until overrun by the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxons</a> who transferred religion to <a href="/wiki/Guiseley" title="Guiseley">Guiseley</a> by building a church and divided Yeadon into two areas with <a href="/wiki/Saxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Saxon">Saxon</a> lords (<a href="/wiki/Thegn" title="Thegn">thegns</a>): it remained divided until 1630,as is shown by the names Yeadon and Nether Yeadon. Following the <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">Danish conquest</a> of Yorkshire it became part of the <a href="/wiki/Wapentake_of_Skyrack" class="mw-redirect" title="Wapentake of Skyrack">Wapentake of Skyrack</a> but still with Saxon lords.<sup id="cite_ref-History_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman conquest">Norman conquest</a> the two manors were taken from their Saxon lords and given to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Percy" title="House of Percy">House of Percy</a> (present Yeadon village) and the Meschines family (Nether Yeadon).  According to the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_book" class="mw-redirect" title="Domesday book">Domesday book</a> it comprised four <a href="/wiki/Carucate" title="Carucate">carucates</a> or about 328 acres.  Much of the area was later acquired by <a href="/wiki/Bolton_Priory" title="Bolton Priory">Bolton Priory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kirkstall_Abbey" title="Kirkstall Abbey">Kirkstall Abbey</a> who exerted considerable power until the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Monasteries" title="Dissolution of the Monasteries">Dissolution of the Monasteries</a>.  In 1354 the villagers won a court case against Kirkstall Abbey over 300 acres of moorland between Yeadon <a href="/wiki/Tarn_(lake)" title="Tarn (lake)">Tarn (lake)</a> and Horsforth, which now accommodates <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Bradford_Airport" title="Leeds Bradford Airport">Leeds Bradford Airport</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-History_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In this period there were only thirty or so households scattered around three points: first where the town centre is now; secondly, the main cluster round where the Woolpack is now, and a smaller group of cottages further to the West along Yeadon Gill as Nether Yeadon, probably a marketplace. Sheep were farmed and the wool made into cloth locally, while Yeadon Gill provided water power for a <a href="/wiki/Corn_mill" class="mw-redirect" title="Corn mill">corn mill</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-History_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_history">Later history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeadon,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Green_Lane_chimneys_Yeadon_Jan_2018.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Green_Lane_chimneys_Yeadon_Jan_2018.jpg/220px-Green_Lane_chimneys_Yeadon_Jan_2018.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Green_Lane_chimneys_Yeadon_Jan_2018.jpg/330px-Green_Lane_chimneys_Yeadon_Jan_2018.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Green_Lane_chimneys_Yeadon_Jan_2018.jpg/440px-Green_Lane_chimneys_Yeadon_Jan_2018.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="3072" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Green_Lane_chimneys_Yeadon_Jan_2018.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Former factory chimneys on Green Lane</div></div></div>
<p>Despite the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> the area prospered and the population more than doubled to over 400 during the 17th century, with 93 hearths (i.e. fireplace with chimney) recorded. Medieval parcels of land were traded into more efficient farms and a new mill built. The town was no more divided and had a significant social organization with <a href="/wiki/Poor_relief" title="Poor relief">Poor relief</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Constable#United_Kingdom_2" title="Constable">Constable</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-History_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 18th century saw the establishment of a school (now Layton Cottage) and more stone houses in place of the <a href="/wiki/Wattle_and_daub" title="Wattle and daub">wattle and daub</a> cottages, a windmill and the first steam engine operated mill. The people worshipped at the parish church in Guiseley, some distance away, and started demanding their own church in 1714: however they did not get one till 1844.<sup id="cite_ref-History_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 19th century, the population rose rapidly from 1,695 in 1801 to 4,109 in 1851 and 7,396 in 1891 as it became a manufacturing rather than farming town.<sup id="cite_ref-Conserve_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conserve-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  It was particularly noted for women's apparel.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE&#39;&#39;EB&#39;&#39;1888_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE&#39;&#39;EB&#39;&#39;1888-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> To assist trade, the New Road (now <a href="/wiki/A65_road" title="A65 road">A65</a>) was built in 1827 through Yeadon, linking Kirkstall with Guiseley and the railway followed in the 1840s. In 1845 it was administratively separated from Guiseley.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE&#39;&#39;EB&#39;&#39;1888_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE&#39;&#39;EB&#39;&#39;1888-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> By 1853 it had gas piped by the "Yeadon and Guiseley Gas Light Co".<sup id="cite_ref-History_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  The watercourse became badly polluted by effluent from <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shipley,_West_Yorkshire" title="Shipley, West Yorkshire">Shipley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bingley" title="Bingley">Bingley</a>, leading to a successful court case against Bradrod Corporation in 1868. The Yeadon Waterworks sank a new well in 1861 and began the Cold Harbour Reservoir in 1877.<sup id="cite_ref-History_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>   The town's <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Health" class="mw-redirect" title="Board of Health">Board of Health</a> was established in 1863,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTE&#39;&#39;EB&#39;&#39;1888_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTE&#39;&#39;EB&#39;&#39;1888-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and set up the cemetery and buildings in 1876.  Finally, 1880 the town had its own "Yeadon Town Hall and Mechanics' Institute".<sup id="cite_ref-History_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century_onwards">20th century onwards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeadon,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 20th century onwards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Increasing social unrest and labour disputes in the early 20th century led to a major <a href="/wiki/Lockout_(industry)" title="Lockout (industry)">lockout</a> and subsequent hunger marches in 1913. As a result of the dispute, the working week in the textile mills was shortened, and workers received a pay rise, although this fell short of the demands of the trade unions who in consequence streamlined their organisation.<sup id="cite_ref-Macartney_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macartney-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In 1937 it became part of the <a href="/wiki/Aireborough" title="Aireborough">Aireborough</a> district, being the most populous township of over ten thousand.<sup id="cite_ref-History_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeadon,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main town is on a hill with south facing slope into the <a href="/wiki/River_Aire" title="River Aire">Aire valley</a>.  The geology is sandstone and <a href="/wiki/Millstone_grit" class="mw-redirect" title="Millstone grit">millstone grit</a>, which has been extensively quarried and used for buildings, with impervious shale and clay to the north, which allowed the formation of a natural hilltop lake, Yeadon Tarn which was enlarged for industrial use.  Leeds Bradford Airport occupies the high plateau of Yeadon Moor to the east.<sup id="cite_ref-Conserve_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conserve-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Chronostratigraphy" title="Chronostratigraphy">chronostratigraphy</a>, the term Yeadonian—for a British <a href="/wiki/Stage_(stratigraphy)" title="Stage (stratigraphy)">sub-stage</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> period—is derived from the study of a geological site at the brick and tile works in Yeadon.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The main part of the town is bounded by the A65 road to the southwest and the <a href="/wiki/A658_road" title="A658 road">A658 road</a> to the southeast. South of the junction of these two roads are the areas of Nether Yeadon and Little London. To the west of the A65 is the area known as New Scarborough, containing many 19th century houses. A public park, Nunroyd Park, to the northwest provides a green break between the housing of Yeadon and Guiseley.  This was formerly the house and lands of a mill owner, and before that the parkland belonging to <a href="/wiki/Nunnery" class="mw-redirect" title="Nunnery">nunnery</a>, <i>royd</i> meaning a woodland clearing.<sup id="cite_ref-Conserve_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conserve-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Evidence of the medieval settlement is seen in fields to the north in Yeadon Banks which follow the strips of this time, and in the irregular and organic location of buildings around Town Street and Ivegate, described in 1878, as "a bewildering labyrinth of
yards and courts and intricate lanes".  By contrast the 19th century development produced a regular grid of terraced houses, many of which remain, while the pattern of streets remains for those that have gone.  There are also a number of former mill and dyeworks sites.<sup id="cite_ref-Conserve_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conserve-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_transport">Public transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeadon,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Public transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Yeadon had a <a href="/wiki/Otley_and_Ilkley_Joint_Railway#Yeadon_branch" title="Otley and Ilkley Joint Railway">rail connection</a> from <a href="/wiki/Guiseley_railway_station" title="Guiseley railway station">Guiseley</a> which opened in 1894. The line was closed in 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It is now a nature path and most of its length can still be walked, from the Old Engine Fields, off Henshaw Lane down Guiseley retail park where it joined the Guiseley Branch Line.  The line continued over Henshaw Lane where an old bridge can still be seen. Immediately after the bridge was the railway station, near the Station Inn in the (now) council storage yard. The line terminated opposite Trinity Church (previously Benton Congregational Church) in Rawdon. Yeadon Station was mostly used for goods, and the line served several large mills directly along its route (Leafield Mill, Kirk Lane Mill, Westfield Mill, Green Lane Mill, a soap works and a dye works). The only passenger trains were special services. The first train to leave Yeadon Station in 1905 held 500 people. The following year, trains took passengers from Yeadon to <a href="/wiki/Blackpool" title="Blackpool">Blackpool</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Yeadon (Green Lane) was connected by tram to Leeds from 1909 until the 1950s. Northbound, Yeadon is served by bus route <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Tiger" title="Yorkshire Tiger">747</a> to <a href="/wiki/Harrogate" title="Harrogate">Harrogate</a>, <a href="/wiki/First_Leeds" title="First Leeds">33, 34 and 97</a>  to <a href="/wiki/Otley" title="Otley">Otley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guiseley" title="Guiseley">Guiseley</a> respectively, and the <a href="/wiki/Keighley_Bus_Company" title="Keighley Bus Company">966</a> to <a href="/wiki/Guiseley" title="Guiseley">Guiseley</a>. Southbound, the town is served by the 747 to <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a> via <a href="/wiki/Greengates" title="Greengates">Greengates</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Tiger" title="Yorkshire Tiger">737</a> to Bradford via <a href="/wiki/Shipley,_West_Yorkshire" title="Shipley, West Yorkshire">Shipley</a>, 33, 34 and 97 to <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeadon,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Yeadon was part of the old, large Guiseley Parish but a church, St John the Evangelist Church, was built in 1844 as a <a href="/wiki/Commissioners%27_church" title="Commissioners&#39; church">Commissioners' church</a> with a grant of £300.  It was designed by Walker Rawsthorne.  It originally had a tower, but this was demolished as unsafe in 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-StJohn_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StJohn-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The New Life Community Church was formerly St Andrew's Parish Church (C of E), built by Thomas Healey of Bradford in 1891 and is Grade II listed.<sup id="cite_ref-NLCC_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NLCC-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> There is also a Roman Catholic church (St Peter and St Paul,)<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> a Methodist church developed from a <a href="/wiki/Primitive_Methodist" class="mw-redirect" title="Primitive Methodist">Primitive Methodist</a> Chapel,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and a meeting hall for <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Brethren" title="Plymouth Brethren">Plymouth Brethren</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_features">Notable features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeadon,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Notable features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Town_Hall">Town Hall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeadon,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Town Hall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Yeadon_Town_Hall_29_June_2018_01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Yeadon_Town_Hall_29_June_2018_01.jpg/220px-Yeadon_Town_Hall_29_June_2018_01.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="237" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Yeadon_Town_Hall_29_June_2018_01.jpg/330px-Yeadon_Town_Hall_29_June_2018_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Yeadon_Town_Hall_29_June_2018_01.jpg/440px-Yeadon_Town_Hall_29_June_2018_01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2039" data-file-height="2199" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Yeadon_Town_Hall_29_June_2018_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Yeadon Town Hall</div></div></div>
<p>Facing North on Town Hall Square by the High Street, and originally built as the Yeadon Town Hall and <a href="/wiki/Mechanics%27_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Mechanics&#39; Institute">Mechanics' Institute</a> in 1880<sup id="cite_ref-History_3-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/William_Hill_(architect)" title="William Hill (architect)">William Hill</a> of Leeds, it is a <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">Grade II listed building</a> of <a href="/wiki/Course_(architecture)" title="Course (architecture)">coursed</a> dressed <a href="/wiki/Sandstone" title="Sandstone">sandstone</a>, with slate roofs in <a href="/wiki/French_Gothic_architecture" title="French Gothic architecture">French Gothic style</a>, featuring a central tower with a clock.<sup id="cite_ref-BLBTH_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BLBTH-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  The architect was selected after a competition with more than 200 entries, with a budget of £5000.<sup id="cite_ref-History_3-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The building was used as a registrar's office in the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Television" class="mw-redirect" title="Yorkshire Television">Yorkshire Television</a> programme <i><a href="/wiki/The_Beiderbecke_Tapes" title="The Beiderbecke Tapes">The Beiderbecke Tapes</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Low_Hall">Low Hall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeadon,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Low Hall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Low_Hall,_Yeadon_29_Jan_2018_01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Low_Hall%2C_Yeadon_29_Jan_2018_01.jpg/220px-Low_Hall%2C_Yeadon_29_Jan_2018_01.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Low_Hall%2C_Yeadon_29_Jan_2018_01.jpg/330px-Low_Hall%2C_Yeadon_29_Jan_2018_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Low_Hall%2C_Yeadon_29_Jan_2018_01.jpg/440px-Low_Hall%2C_Yeadon_29_Jan_2018_01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Low_Hall,_Yeadon_29_Jan_2018_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Low Hall</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Arthur_Mee" title="Arthur Mee">Arthur Mee</a> wrote "Though Yorkshire has many greater houses, it has few more charming than Low Hall on the fringe of Yeadon."<sup id="cite_ref-Mee_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mee-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  It is a Grade II listed building on Gill Lane, a former 17th century farmhouse (with a datestone "W S 1658"), remodelled in the 19th century, of coursed sandstone and slate roof.<sup id="cite_ref-BLBLH_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BLBLH-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  It was built in 1624, and has a porch added by William Sale in 1658, believed to be taken from <a href="/wiki/Esholt_Priory" title="Esholt Priory">Esholt Priory</a> following the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Monasteries" title="Dissolution of the Monasteries">Dissolution of the Monasteries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AHS_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AHS-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mee_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mee-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It contains a <a href="/wiki/Tudor_period" title="Tudor period">Tudor</a> <a href="/wiki/Priest_hole" title="Priest hole">Priest hole</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mee_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mee-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yeadon_Tarn">Yeadon Tarn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeadon,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Yeadon Tarn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Yeadon_tarn.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/80/Yeadon_tarn.jpg/220px-Yeadon_tarn.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/80/Yeadon_tarn.jpg/330px-Yeadon_tarn.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/80/Yeadon_tarn.jpg/440px-Yeadon_tarn.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="681" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Yeadon_tarn.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Yeadon Tarn</div></div></div>
<p>Yeadon <a href="/wiki/Tarn_(lake)" title="Tarn (lake)">Tarn</a> (also known as Yeadon Dam) is located between High Street and the airport runway. During the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> it was drained to prevent enemy aircraft using its reflection as a landmark to identify the nearby Avro factory.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> 
The tarn is used for sailing and fishing. <a href="/wiki/Mallard" title="Mallard">Mallard</a> ducks, <a href="/wiki/Swan" title="Swan">swans</a> and a sizable population of <a href="/wiki/Canada_goose" title="Canada goose">Canada geese</a> can be found at the tarn. There is a park with a BMX bike track adjacent to it.<sup id="cite_ref-Visit_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Visit-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeadon,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Yeadon, along with neighbouring towns <a href="/wiki/Guiseley" title="Guiseley">Guiseley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rawdon,_West_Yorkshire" title="Rawdon, West Yorkshire">Rawdon</a>, formed <a href="/wiki/Aireborough" title="Aireborough">Aireborough</a> <a href="/wiki/Urban_district_(Great_Britain_and_Ireland)" title="Urban district (Great Britain and Ireland)">Urban District</a>, which was created in 1937 and abolished in 1974. Yeadon still hosts local Rugby Union side Aireborough RUFC at Nunroyd Park.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>Yeadon (except for the area north of Swincar Avenue on Kirk Lane, and the area south of the <a href="/wiki/A65_road" title="A65 road">A65</a>) is now in the Leeds City Council Ward of <a href="/wiki/Otley_and_Yeadon_(ward)" title="Otley and Yeadon (ward)">Otley and Yeadon</a> ward. It is represented by three councillors. As of 2017 these are Ryk Downes, Colin Campbell and Sandy Lay of the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a>. <a href="/wiki/Leeds_City_Council" title="Leeds City Council">Leeds City Council</a> now control the public services in Yeadon. Yeadon falls into two constituencies; the majority is in <a href="/wiki/Leeds_North_West_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Leeds North West (UK Parliament constituency)">Leeds North West</a>, which is held by the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a>, represented by <a href="/wiki/Alex_Sobel" title="Alex Sobel">Alex Sobel</a>, and some falls into <a href="/wiki/Pudsey_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Pudsey (UK Parliament constituency)">Pudsey</a>, which is held by the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative Party</a>, represented by <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Andrew" title="Stuart Andrew">Stuart Andrew</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amenities">Amenities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeadon,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Amenities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Yeadon has a developed town centre and most of the businesses are situated around the High Street. There is a <a href="/wiki/Morrisons" title="Morrisons">Morrisons</a> supermarket as well as several other chains of shops, such as KC's Express (est. 1994). There are also building societies, estate agents and public houses. There are also both <a href="/wiki/Travelodge" title="Travelodge">Travelodge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Premier_Travel_Inn" class="mw-redirect" title="Premier Travel Inn">Premier Travel Inn</a> hotels situated near the airport.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeadon,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">Cricket</a> has been played in Yeadon at least since around 1850 when scores of people were reported to play on Yeadon Moor after work ended on a Saturday teatime. Play continued as long as the light would allow. The origins of cricket in Yeadon are not definitely known, but at that time Yeadon boasted two teams, Topenders and Lowenders. The two teams decided to amalgamate at a meeting held in the Old Victoria Hotel, at the junction of Sandy Way and the Green, in 1859. The newly amalgamated club adopted the name Yeadon United Cricket Club, ‘united’ being dropped some time later.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The legendary <a href="/wiki/W._G._Grace" title="W. G. Grace">W. G. Grace</a> played at the Swan ground in 1877 with a United South XI. Grace was bowled for nought by an elated bowler who was then admonished by his captain for removing the man the crowd had paid to see.
</p><p>Yeadon FC won promotion to the FA's Wharfedale Triangle Premier League in the 2018-19 season.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yeadon,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jet2.com_headquarters,_Leeds_Bradford_International_Airport_(24th_July_2010)_003.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Jet2.com_headquarters%2C_Leeds_Bradford_International_Airport_%2824th_July_2010%29_003.jpg/220px-Jet2.com_headquarters%2C_Leeds_Bradford_International_Airport_%2824th_July_2010%29_003.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="59" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Jet2.com_headquarters%2C_Leeds_Bradford_International_Airport_%2824th_July_2010%29_003.jpg/330px-Jet2.com_headquarters%2C_Leeds_Bradford_International_Airport_%2824th_July_2010%29_003.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Jet2.com_headquarters%2C_Leeds_Bradford_International_Airport_%2824th_July_2010%29_003.jpg/440px-Jet2.com_headquarters%2C_Leeds_Bradford_International_Airport_%2824th_July_2010%29_003.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="985" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jet2.com_headquarters,_Leeds_Bradford_International_Airport_(24th_July_2010)_003.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Jet2.com" title="Jet2.com">Jet2.com</a>'s headquarters at <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Bradford_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Leeds Bradford International Airport">Leeds Bradford International Airport</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Avro" title="Avro">Avro</a> had a factory next to <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Bradford_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Leeds Bradford International Airport">Yeadon Aerodrome</a> from 1938 to 1946 which produced many of the company's wartime planes, including the <a href="/wiki/Avro_Lancaster" title="Avro Lancaster">Lancaster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avro_Lincoln" title="Avro Lincoln">Lincoln</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avro_York" title="Avro York">York</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avro_Anson" title="Avro Anson">Anson</a>. Approximately 700 <a href="/wiki/Avro_Lancaster" title="Avro Lancaster">Lancasters</a>  were produced at Yeadon. The town retains links with <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Bradford_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Leeds Bradford International Airport">Leeds Bradford International Airport</a>, with a considerable percentage of the local population employed there. Aviation heritage in Yeadon is kept alive by the activities of 2168 (Yeadon) Squadron Air Training Corps.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The airline, <a href="/wiki/Jet2.com" title="Jet2.com">Jet2.com</a>, has its head office at Leeds Bradford Airport,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> as does <a href="/wiki/Dart_Group" title="Dart Group">Dart Group</a>, Jet2.com's parent company.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
