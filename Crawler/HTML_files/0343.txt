<p><b>Diplomatic rank</b> is a system of professional and <a href="/wiki/Social_rank" class="mw-redirect" title="Social rank">social rank</a> used in the world of <a href="/wiki/Diplomacy" title="Diplomacy">diplomacy</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_relation" class="mw-redirect" title="International relation">international relations</a>. A diplomat's rank determines many ceremonial details, such as the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_precedence" title="Order of precedence">order of precedence</a> at official processions, <a href="/wiki/Seating_plan" title="Seating plan">table seatings</a> at state dinners, the person to whom <a href="/wiki/Letter_of_credence" title="Letter of credence">diplomatic credentials</a> should be presented, and the title by which the diplomat should be addressed.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_diplomacy">International diplomacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: International diplomacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ranks">Ranks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ranks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The current system of diplomatic ranks was established by the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Convention_on_Diplomatic_Relations" title="Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations">Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations</a> (1961).<sup id="cite_ref-vienna1961_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vienna1961-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> There are three ranks, two of which remain in use:
</p>
<ol><li><b><a href="/wiki/Ambassador" title="Ambassador">Ambassador</a></b>. An Ambassador is a <a href="/wiki/Head_of_mission" title="Head of mission">head of mission</a> who is accredited to the receiving country's <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a>. They head a <a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_mission" title="Diplomatic mission">diplomatic mission</a> known as an <a href="/wiki/Embassy" class="mw-redirect" title="Embassy">embassy</a>, which is usually headquartered in a <a href="/wiki/Chancery_(diplomacy)" title="Chancery (diplomacy)">chancery</a> in the receiving state's <a href="/wiki/Capital_city" title="Capital city">capital</a>.
<ol><li>A <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_nuncio" class="mw-redirect" title="Apostolic nuncio">papal nuncio</a> is considered to have Ambassadorial rank, and he presides over a <a href="/wiki/Nunciature" class="mw-redirect" title="Nunciature">nunciature</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth countries</a> send a <a href="/wiki/High_Commissioner_(Commonwealth)" class="mw-redirect" title="High Commissioner (Commonwealth)">High Commissioner</a> who presides over a <a href="/wiki/High_commissioner" title="High commissioner">High Commission</a> and has the same diplomatic rank as an Ambassador.</li></ol></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Envoy_(title)" title="Envoy (title)">Minister</a></b>. A Minister was a <a href="/wiki/Head_of_mission" title="Head of mission">head of mission</a> who was accredited to the receiving country's head of state. A Minister headed a <a href="/wiki/Legation" title="Legation">legation</a> rather than an embassy. After World War II, the embassy became the standard form of diplomatic mission, and the rank of Minister is now obsolete. Many countries use the title minister-counsellor to refer to the deputy head of a mission,<sup id="cite_ref-HistoryAtState_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoryAtState-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> but he does not hold the rank of Minister.
<ol><li>An envoy or an internuncio is also considered to have the rank of Minister.</li></ol></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Charg%C3%A9_d%27affaires" title="Chargé d&#39;affaires">Chargé d'affaires</a></b>:
<ol><li>A chargé d'affaires <i>en pied</i> is a permanent <a href="/wiki/Head_of_mission" title="Head of mission">head of mission</a> who is accredited by his country's <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Minister" class="mw-redirect" title="Foreign Minister">Foreign Minister</a> to the receiving nation's Foreign Minister, in cases where the two governments have not reached an agreement to exchange ambassadors.</li>
<li>A chargé d'affaires <i>ad interim</i> is a diplomat who temporarily heads a diplomatic mission in the absence of an ambassador.</li></ol></li></ol>
<p>The body of diplomats accredited to a country form the <a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_corps" title="Diplomatic corps">diplomatic corps</a>. Ambassadors have precedence over chargés, and precedence within each rank is determined by the date on which <a href="/wiki/Letter_of_credence" title="Letter of credence">diplomatic credentials</a> were presented.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The longest-serving ambassador is the <a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_corps#Dean_of_the_Diplomatic_Corps" title="Diplomatic corps">Dean of the Diplomatic Corps</a>, who speaks for the entire diplomatic corps on matters of diplomatic privilege and protocol. In many Catholic countries, the <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_nuncio" class="mw-redirect" title="Apostolic nuncio">papal nuncio</a> is always considered the Dean of the Diplomatic Corps.
</p>
<h3><span id="Historical_ranks.2C_1815.E2.80.931961"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_ranks,_1815–1961">Historical ranks, 1815–1961</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Historical ranks, 1815–1961">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ranks established by the Vienna Convention (1961) modify a more elaborate system of ranks that was established by the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Vienna" title="Congress of Vienna">Congress of Vienna</a> (1815):<sup id="cite_ref-vienna1815_en_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vienna1815_en-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li><b><a href="/wiki/Ambassador" title="Ambassador">Ambassadors</a></b>, Legates, and Nuncios were personal representatives of their sovereign.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Envoy_(title)" title="Envoy (title)">Envoys</a></b> and Ministers represented their government, and were accredited to the receiving sovereign.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Resident_(title)" title="Resident (title)">Ministers resident</a></b> formed an intermediate class, between ministers and chargés. This rank was created by the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Aix-la-Chapelle_(1818)" title="Congress of Aix-la-Chapelle (1818)">Congress of Aix-la-Chapelle (1818)</a><sup id="cite_ref-vienna1818_en_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vienna1818_en-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Charg%C3%A9_d%27affaires" title="Chargé d&#39;affaires">Chargés d'affaires</a></b> were accredited by their Foreign Minister to the receiving Foreign Minister.</li></ol>
<p>The rank of <i>Envoy</i> was short for "Envoy Extraordinary and Minister Plenipotentiary", and was more commonly known as <i>Minister</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-HistoryAtState_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoryAtState-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> For example, the <i>Envoy Extraordinary and Minister Plenipotentiary of the United States to the French Empire</i> was known as the "<a href="/wiki/United_States_Minister_to_France" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Minister to France">United States Minister to France</a>" and addressed as "Monsieur le Ministre".<sup id="cite_ref-washburne_memoirs_1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-washburne_memoirs_1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-washburne_memoirs_2_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-washburne_memoirs_2-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An Ambassador was regarded as the personal representative of his sovereign as well as his government.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Only major monarchies would exchange Ambassadors with each other, while smaller monarchies and republics only sent Ministers. Because of diplomatic reciprocity, <a href="/wiki/Great_Power" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Power">Great Powers</a> would only send a Minister to a smaller monarchy or a republic.<sup id="cite_ref-allen2012_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allen2012-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> For example, in the waning years of the <a href="/wiki/Second_French_Empire" title="Second French Empire">Second French Empire</a>, the United Kingdom sent an Ambassador to Paris, while <a href="/wiki/Union_between_Sweden_and_Norway" title="Union between Sweden and Norway">Sweden-Norway</a> and the United States sent Ministers.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The rule that only monarchies could send Ambassadors was more honored in the breach than the observance. This had been true even before the Congress of Vienna, as England continued to appoint ambassadors after becoming <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_England" title="Commonwealth of England">a republic</a> in 1649.<sup id="cite_ref-satow1932_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-satow1932-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Countries that overthrew their monarchs proved to be unwilling to accept the lower rank accorded to a republic. After the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Prussian_War" title="Franco-Prussian War">Franco-Prussian War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/French_Third_Republic" title="French Third Republic">French Third Republic</a> continued to send and receive ambassadors.<sup id="cite_ref-washburne_memoirs_2_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-washburne_memoirs_2-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The rule became increasingly untenable as the United States grew into a <a href="/wiki/Great_Power" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Power">Great Power</a>. The United States followed the French precedent in 1893 and began to exchange ambassadors with other Great Powers.<sup id="cite_ref-HistoryAtState_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoryAtState-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Historically, the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_precedence" title="Order of precedence">order of precedence</a> had been a matter of great dispute. European powers agreed that the <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_nuncio" class="mw-redirect" title="Apostolic nuncio">papal nuncio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Imperial</a> Ambassador would have precedence, but could not agree on the relative precedence of the kingdoms and smaller countries. In 1768, the French and Russian ambassadors to Great Britain even fought a <a href="/wiki/Duel" title="Duel">duel</a> over who had the right to sit next to the Imperial Ambassador at a court ball. After several diplomatic incidents between their ambassadors, France and Spain agreed in 1761 to let the date of arrival determine their precedence. In 1760, Portugal attempted to apply seniority to all ambassadors, but the rule was rejected by the other European courts.<sup id="cite_ref-satow1932_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-satow1932-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Congress of Vienna finally put an end to these disputes over precedence. After an initial attempt to divide countries into three ranks faltered on the question of which country should be in each rank, the Congress instead decided to divide <i>diplomats</i> into three ranks. A fourth rank was added by the <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_Aix-la-Chapelle_(1818)" title="Congress of Aix-la-Chapelle (1818)">Congress of Aix-la-Chapelle (1818)</a>. Each diplomatic rank had precedence over the lower ranks, and precedence within each rank was determined by the date that their <a href="/wiki/Letter_of_credence" title="Letter of credence">credentials</a> were presented. The papal nuncio could be given a different precedence than the other ambassadors. The <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> had ceased to exist in 1806, so the Austrian ambassador would accumulate seniority along with the other ambassadors.<sup id="cite_ref-satow1932_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-satow1932-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bilateral_diplomacy">Bilateral diplomacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bilateral diplomacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Diplomatic_hierarchy.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Diplomatic_hierarchy.png/250px-Diplomatic_hierarchy.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="505" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9f/Diplomatic_hierarchy.png 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="606" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Diplomatic_hierarchy.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The distinction between managers and officers is not necessarily as apparent. Senior officers (such as first and second secretaries) often manage junior diplomats and locally hired staff.</div></div></div>
<p>In modern diplomatic practice, there are a number of diplomatic ranks below Ambassador. Since most missions are now headed by an ambassador, these ranks now rarely indicate a mission's (or its host nation's) relative importance, but rather reflect the diplomat's individual seniority within their own nation's diplomatic career path and in the diplomatic corps in the host nation:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Ambassador</b> (High Commissioner in <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> missions to other Commonwealth countries); <a href="/wiki/Ambassador_at_large" class="mw-redirect" title="Ambassador at large">ambassador at large</a></li>
<li><b>Minister</b></li>
<li><b>Minister-Counsellor</b></li>
<li><b>Counsellor</b></li>
<li><b>First Secretary</b></li>
<li><b>Second Secretary</b></li>
<li><b>Third Secretary</b></li>
<li><b>Attaché</b></li>
<li><b>Assistant Attaché</b></li></ul>
<p>The term <i><a href="/wiki/Attach%C3%A9" title="Attaché">attaché</a></i> is used for any diplomatic agent who does not fit in the standard diplomatic ranks, often because they are not (or were not traditionally) members of the sending country's diplomatic service or <a href="/wiki/Foreign_ministry" class="mw-redirect" title="Foreign ministry">foreign ministry</a>, and were therefore only "attached" to the diplomatic mission. The most frequent use is for <a href="/wiki/Military_attach%C3%A9" title="Military attaché">military attachés</a>, but the diplomatic title may be used for any specific individual or position as required, generally related to a specific or technical field. Since administrative and technical staff benefit from only limited <a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_immunity" title="Diplomatic immunity">diplomatic immunity</a>, some countries may routinely appoint support staff as attachés. Attaché does not, therefore, denote any rank or position (except in Soviet and post-Soviet diplomatic services, where attaché is the lowest diplomatic rank of a career diplomat). Note that many traditional functionary roles, such as <a href="/wiki/Press_attach%C3%A9" class="mw-redirect" title="Press attaché">press attaché</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cultural_attach%C3%A9" title="Cultural attaché">cultural attaché</a>, are not formal titles in diplomatic practice, although they may be used as a matter of custom.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multilateral_diplomacy">Multilateral diplomacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Multilateral diplomacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Furthermore, outside this traditional pattern of bilateral diplomacy, as a rule on a permanent residency basis (though sometimes doubling elsewhere), certain ranks and positions were created specifically for multilateral diplomacy:
</p>
<ul><li>An <a href="/wiki/Ambassador-at-large" title="Ambassador-at-large">ambassador-at-large</a> is equivalent to an ambassador and assigned specific tasks or region in which he is assigned various assignments aimed at multi track diplomacy.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Permanent_representative" title="Permanent representative">permanent representative</a> is the equivalent of an ambassador, normally of that rank, but accredited to an international body (mainly by member—and possibly observer states), not to a head of state.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Resident_(title)" title="Resident (title)">resident representative</a> (or sometimes simply <i>representative</i>) is also a member of the diplomatic corps, but is below the rank of ambassador. A representative is accredited by an international organization (generally a <a href="/wiki/Category:United_Nations_specialized_agencies" title="Category:United Nations specialized agencies">United Nations agency</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Bretton_Woods_system" title="Bretton Woods system">Bretton Woods</a> institution) to a country's government. The resident representative typically heads the country office of that international organization within that country.</li>
<li>A <b>special ambassador or honorary ambassador</b> is a government's specialist diplomat in a particular field, not posted in residence, but often traveling around the globe.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Office_of_the_United_States_Trade_Representative" title="Office of the United States Trade Representative">U.S.&#160;Trade Representative</a> (USTR) is an ambassador of Cabinet rank, in charge of U.S. delegations in multilateral trade negotiations (since 1962). The USTR's Special Agricultural Negotiator also typically holds an ambassadorial appointment.</li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_worldwide">Usage worldwide</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Usage worldwide">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most countries worldwide have some form of internal rank, roughly parallel to the diplomatic ranks, which are used in their <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Service_Institute" title="Foreign Service Institute">foreign service</a> or <a href="/wiki/Civil_service" title="Civil service">civil service</a> in general. The correspondence is not exact, however, for various reasons, including the fact that according to diplomatic usage, all Ambassadors are of equal rank, but Ambassadors of more senior rank are typically sent to more important postings. Some countries may make specific links or comparisons to <a href="/wiki/Military_rank" title="Military rank">military ranks</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australian_Department_of_Foreign_Affairs_and_Trade">Australian Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Australian Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Officers from the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Foreign_Affairs_and_Trade_(Australia)" title="Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade (Australia)">Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade</a> (DFAT) are graded into four broad bands (BB1 to BB4), with the Senior Executive Service (SES Band&#160;1 to SES Band&#160;3) following above.
</p><p>Ambassadors, High Commissioners and Consuls-General usually come from the Senior Executive Service, although in smaller posts the head of mission may be a BB4&#160;officer. Generally speaking (and there are variations in ranking and nomenclature between posts and positions), Counsellors are represented by BB4&#160;officers; Consuls and First and Second Secretaries are BB3&#160;officers and Third Secretaries and Vice Consuls are BB2&#160;officers. DFAT only posts a limited number of low-level BB1&#160;staff abroad. In large <a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_missions_of_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Diplomatic missions of Australia">Australian missions</a> an SES&#160;officer who is not the head of mission could be posted with the rank of Minister.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazilian_Diplomatic_Service">Brazilian Diplomatic Service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Brazilian Diplomatic Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are six ranks in the Brazilian Ministry of External Affairs (<i><i lang="pt-BR" title="Portuguese language text">Itamaraty</i></i>):
</p>
<ul><li><i><i lang="pt-BR" title="Portuguese language text">Terceiro-Secretário</i></i> ("Third secretary")</li>
<li><i><i lang="pt-BR" title="Portuguese language text">Segundo-Secretário</i></i> ("Second secretary")</li>
<li><i><i lang="pt-BR" title="Portuguese language text">Primeiro-Secretário</i></i> ("First secretary")</li>
<li><i><i lang="pt-BR" title="Portuguese language text">Conselheiro</i></i> ("Counsellor")</li>
<li><i><i lang="pt-BR" title="Portuguese language text">Ministro de Segunda Classe</i></i> ("Minister, second class")</li>
<li><i><i lang="pt-BR" title="Portuguese language text">Ministro de Primeira Classe</i></i> ("Minister, first class")</li></ul>
<p><i><i lang="pt-BR" title="Portuguese language text">Embaixador</i></i> is the honorary dignity conceded permanently when a Minister of First Class assumes a Post overseas. It can also be a temporary assignment, when carried on by a lower-rank diplomat or Brazilian politician of high level.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="British_Diplomatic_Service">British Diplomatic Service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: British Diplomatic Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Diplomatic_Service" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Diplomatic Service">Her Majesty's Diplomatic Service</a> differentiates between officers in the "Senior Management Structure" (SMS; equivalent to the Senior Civil Service grades of the <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Civil_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Civil Service">Home Civil Service</a>) and those in the "delegated grades".  SMS officers are classified into three pay-bands, and will serve in the <a href="/wiki/Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Office" title="Foreign and Commonwealth Office">Foreign and Commonwealth Office</a> in London as (in descending order of seniority) <a href="/wiki/Permanent_Under-Secretary_of_State_for_Foreign_Affairs" title="Permanent Under-Secretary of State for Foreign Affairs">Permanent Under-Secretary</a>, Directors-General, Directors, or Heads of Department; overseas they will be Ambassadors (High Commissioners in Commonwealth countries), or Consuls-General, Deputy Heads of Mission or Counsellors for larger posts. (Deputy Heads of Mission at the historically most significant Embassies, for example those <a href="/wiki/Embassy_of_the_United_Kingdom,_Washington,_D.C." title="Embassy of the United Kingdom, Washington, D.C.">in Washington</a> and <a href="/wiki/Embassy_of_the_United_Kingdom,_Paris" title="Embassy of the United Kingdom, Paris">in Paris</a>, are known as Ministers.)
</p><p>In the "delegated grades", officers are graded by number from 1 to 7; the grades are grouped into bands lettered A‑D (grades 1 and 2 are in Band A; 3 in B; 4 and 5 in C; and 6 and 7 in D). Overseas, A2&#160;grade officers hold the title of Attache, B3‑grade officers are Third Secretaries; C4s are Second Secretaries; and C5s and D6s are First Secretaries. D7&#160;officers are usually Deputy Heads of Mission in medium-sized posts or Heads of Mission in small posts.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="French_Diplomatic_Service"><a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> Diplomatic Service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: French Diplomatic Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are five ranks in the French Diplomatic Service:<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
(in ascending order)
</p>
<ul><li><i><i lang="fr" title="French language text">Secrétaire de chancellerie</i></i></li>
<li><i><i lang="fr" title="French language text">Secrétaire des affaires étrangères</i></i></li>
<li><i><i lang="fr" title="French language text">Conseiller des affaires étrangères</i></i></li>
<li><i><i lang="fr" title="French language text">Ministre plénipotentiaire</i></i>, the most common rank for <a href="/wiki/Head_of_mission" title="Head of mission">heads of mission</a>, but it also applies to some ministers-counsellors in important embassies</li>
<li><i><i lang="fr" title="French language text">Ambassadeur de France</i></i>, an honorary dignity</li></ul>
<p>There are two additional ranks for ICT specialists (also in ascending order):
</p>
<ul><li><i><i lang="fr" title="French language text">Secrétaire des systèmes d'information et de communication</i></i></li>
<li><i><i lang="fr" title="French language text">Attaché des systèmes d'information et de communication</i></i></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Italian_diplomatic_career">Italian diplomatic career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Italian diplomatic career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, ranks and functions are not exactly connected: each rank can cover several functions.  Moreover, several exceptions apply.
</p>
<ul><li><i><i lang="it" title="Italian language text">Segretario di legazione in prova</i></i> ("Secretary of Legation in probation period"): 9-month training period at the beginning of the career (no other functions)</li>
<li><i><i lang="it" title="Italian language text">Segretario di legazione</i></i> ("Secretary of Legation"): second secretary at an embassy, head of vice-consulate, vice-consul</li>
<li><i><i lang="it" title="Italian language text">Consigliere di legazione</i></i> ("Counsellor of Legation"): counsellor at an embassy, consul (head of a first-class general consulate)</li>
<li><i><i lang="it" title="Italian language text">Consigliere d'ambasciata</i></i> ("Counsellor of Embassy"): first counsellor at an embassy, consul</li>
<li><i><i lang="it" title="Italian language text">Ministro plenipotenziario</i></i> ("Minister Plenipotentiary"): ambassador (as for functions), minister-counsellor at an embassy, head of a Directorate at the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_(Italy)" title="Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Italy)">Ministry of Foreign Affairs</a></li>
<li><i><i lang="it" title="Italian language text">Ambasciatore</i></i> ("Ambassador"): ambassador (thus both as for the rank and for the functions), General Secretary or head of a Directorate at the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Foreign_Affairs_(Italy)" title="Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Italy)">Ministry of Foreign Affairs</a></li></ul>
<p>There are about 30 people who hold the rank of Ambassador.  Therefore, most of the about 150 Italian embassies or permanent representations are held by a Minister Plenipotentiary: traditionally, ambassadors are appointed to the most important representations, such as <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Delhi" title="New Delhi">New Delhi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peking" class="mw-redirect" title="Peking">Peking</a> embassies and representations to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">UN</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_Diplomatic_Corps"><a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> Diplomatic Corps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Spanish Diplomatic Corps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the merger of the Consular and Diplomatic Corps, the current eight grades of Spanish career diplomats are (in ascending order):
</p>
<ul><li><i><i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">Funcionario en prácticas</i></i> ("Trainee Diplomat"): title held during the one-year training program at the Diplomatic School.</li>
<li><i><i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">Secretario de Embajada de tercera clase</i></i> ("Embassy Secretary, Third Class") or Secretary.</li>
<li><i><i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">Secretario de Embajada de segunda clase</i></i> ("Embassy Secretary, Second Class")</li>
<li><i><i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">Secretario de Embajada de primera clase</i></i>. ("Embassy Secretary, First Class")</li>
<li><i><i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">Consejero</i></i> or <i><i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">Concillor</i></i>, lowest grade to be appointed Consul-General.</li>
<li><i><i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">Ministro Plenipotenciario de tercera clase</i></i> ("Minister Plenipotentiary, Third Class") commonly known as Minister, lowest grade to be appointed Ambassador.</li>
<li><i><i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">Ministro Plenipotenciario de segunda clase</i></i>. ("Minister Plenipotentiary, Second Class")</li>
<li><i><i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">Ministro Plenipotenciario de primera clase</i></i>. ("Minister Plenipotentiary, First Class")</li>
<li><i><i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">Embajador de España</i></i> ("Ambassador of Spain"): not all Spanish Ambassadors hold this grade, which is limited by law to 3% of the total Corps.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_Foreign_Service"><a href="/wiki/United_States_Foreign_Service" title="United States Foreign Service">United States Foreign Service</a></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: United States Foreign Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Members of the Foreign Service consist of five groups, including <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Service_Officer" title="Foreign Service Officer">Foreign Service Officers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Service_Specialists" class="mw-redirect" title="Foreign Service Specialists">Foreign Service Specialists</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  Like officers in the U.S. military, Foreign Service Officers are members of the Foreign Service who are commissioned by the President.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  As with Warrant Officers in the U.S. military, Foreign Service Specialists are technical leaders and experts, commissioned not by the President but by the Secretary of State.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>  Ranks descend from the highest, FS‑1, <a href="/wiki/General_Schedule#Military_rank_equivalency" class="mw-redirect" title="General Schedule">equivalent</a> to a full <a href="/wiki/Colonel_(United_States)" title="Colonel (United States)">Colonel</a> in the military, to FS‑9, the lowest rank in the U.S.&#160;Foreign Service personnel system.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  (Most entry-level Foreign Service members begin at the FS‑5 or FS‑6 level.) Personal rank is distinct from and should not be confused with the diplomatic or consular rank assigned at the time of appointment to a particular diplomatic or consular mission.
</p><p>In a large mission, several Senior Diplomats may serve under the Ambassador as Minister-Counselors, Counselors, and First Secretaries; in a small mission, a diplomat may serve as the lone Counselor of Embassy.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consular_counterpart">Consular counterpart</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Diplomatic_rank&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Consular counterpart">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Formally the <a href="/wiki/Consul_(representative)" title="Consul (representative)">consular career</a> (ranking in descending order: consul-general, consul, vice-consul, consular agent; equivalents with <a href="/wiki/Consular_immunity" title="Consular immunity">consular immunity</a> limited to official acts only include honorary consul-general, honorary consul, and honorary vice-consul) forms a separate hierarchy. Many countries do not internally have a separate consular path or stream, and the meaning of "consular" responsibilities and functions will differ from country to country. Other titles, including "vice consul-general", have existed in the past. Consular titles may be used concurrently with diplomatic titles if the individual is assigned to an embassy. Diplomatic immunity is more limited for consular officials without other diplomatic accreditation, and broadly limited to immunity with respect to their official duties.
</p><p>At a separate consular post, the official will have only a consular title. Officials at consular posts may therefore have consular titles, but not be involved in traditional consular activities, and actually be responsible for trade, cultural, or other matters.
</p><p>Consular officers, being nominally more distant from the politically sensitive aspects of diplomacy, can more easily render a wide range of services to private citizens, enterprises, et cetera. They may be more numerous since diplomatic missions are posted only in a nation's capital, while consular officials are stationed in various other cities as well. However, it is not uncommon for individuals to be transferred from one hierarchy to the other, and for consular officials to serve in a capital carrying out strictly consular duties within the "consular section" of a diplomatic post, e.g.,&#160;within an embassy. Some countries routinely provide their embassy officials with <a href="/wiki/Consular_commission" title="Consular commission">consular commissions</a>, including those without formal consular responsibilities, since a consular commission allows the individual to legalize documents, sign certain documents, and undertake certain other necessary functions.
</p><p>Depending on the practice of the individual country, "consular services" may be limited to services provided for citizens or residents of the sending country, or extended to include, for example, visa services for nationals of the host country.
</p><p>Sending nations may also designate incumbents of certain positions as holding consulary authority by virtue of their office, while lacking individual accreditation, immunity and inviolability.  For example, 10&#160;U.S.C. §§&#160;936 and 1044a identify various U.S.&#160;military officers (and authorize the service secretaries to identify others) who hold general authority as a notary and consul of the United States for, respectively, purposes of military administration and those entitled to military legal assistance. A nation may also declare that its senior merchant sea captain in a given foreign <a href="/wiki/Port" title="Port">port</a>—or its merchant sea captains generally—has consulary authority for merchant seamen.
</p>
