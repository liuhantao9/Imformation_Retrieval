<p><b>Patrilineality</b>, also known as the <b>male line</b>, the <b>spear side</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> or <b>agnatic kinship</b>, is a common <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a> system in which an individual's family membership derives from and is recorded through his or her father's lineage. It generally involves the inheritance of property, rights, names or titles by persons related through male kin.
</p><p>A patriline ("father line") is a person's father, and additional ancestors, as traced only through males.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_Bible">In the Bible</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrilineality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In the Bible">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the Bible, family and tribal membership appears to be transmitted through the father. For example, a person is considered to be a priest or Levite, if his father is a priest or Levite, and the members of all the twelve tribes are called Israelites because their father is Israel (<a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">Jacob</a>). Because of this they are called the "chosen people" by virtue of being "sons of Israel"; that is, the biological male descendants of Israel, who is referred to as their "father" in the sense that he is their lineal male ancestor.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Agnatic_succession">Agnatic succession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrilineality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Agnatic succession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Patrilineal or agnatic succession gives priority to or restricts inheritance of a throne or fief to heirs, male or female, descended from the original title holder through males only. Traditionally, agnatic succession is applied in determining the names and membership of European <a href="/wiki/Dynasty" title="Dynasty">dynasties</a>. The prevalent forms of <a href="/wiki/Dynasty" title="Dynasty">dynastic</a> succession in Europe, Asia and parts of Africa (but see the <a href="/wiki/Rain_Queen" title="Rain Queen">Rain Queen</a>) were <a href="/wiki/Male-preference_primogeniture" class="mw-redirect" title="Male-preference primogeniture">male-preference primogeniture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agnatic_primogeniture" class="mw-redirect" title="Agnatic primogeniture">agnatic primogeniture</a> or <a href="/wiki/Agnatic_seniority" title="Agnatic seniority">agnatic seniority</a> until after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.
</p><p>By the 21st century most ongoing European monarchies had replaced their traditional agnatic succession with <a href="/wiki/Absolute_primogeniture" class="mw-redirect" title="Absolute primogeniture">absolute primogeniture</a>, meaning that the first child born to a monarch inherits the throne, regardless of the child's sex. However, there is a change of dynasty.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Salic_law">Salic law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrilineality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Salic law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Variations of <a href="/wiki/Salic_law" title="Salic law">Salic law</a>, generally understood in modern times to mean exclusion of women as hereditary monarchs, restricted succession to thrones and inheritance of <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fiefs</a> or land to men in parts of medieval and later Europe. Once common,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> strict Salic inheritance has been officially revoked in all extant European monarchies except the <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Liechtenstein" class="mw-redirect" title="Principality of Liechtenstein">Principality of Liechtenstein</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic_genealogy">Genetic genealogy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Patrilineality&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Genetic genealogy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Genealogical_DNA_test" title="Genealogical DNA test">Genealogical DNA test</a> and <a href="/wiki/Y-chromosomal_Adam" title="Y-chromosomal Adam">Y-chromosomal Adam</a></div>
<p>The fact that human <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">Y-chromosome</a> DNA (Y-DNA) is paternally inherited enables patrilines and agnatic kinships of men to be traced through genetic analysis.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Y-chromosomal_Adam" title="Y-chromosomal Adam">Y-chromosomal Adam</a> (Y-MRCA) is the patrilineal <a href="/wiki/Most_recent_common_ancestor" title="Most recent common ancestor">most recent common ancestor</a> from whom all Y-DNA in living men is descended. An identification of a very rare and previously unknown Y-chromosome variant in 2012 led researchers to estimate that Y-chromosomal Adam lived 338,000 years ago (237,000 to 581,000 years ago with <a href="/wiki/95%25_confidence_interval" class="mw-redirect" title="95% confidence interval">95% confidence</a>), judging from <a href="/wiki/Molecular_clock" title="Molecular clock">molecular clock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genetic_marker" title="Genetic marker">genetic marker</a> studies.<sup id="cite_ref-Mendez13_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mendez13-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Before this discovery, estimates of the date when Y-chromosomal Adam lived were much more recent, estimated to be tens of thousands of years.
</p>
