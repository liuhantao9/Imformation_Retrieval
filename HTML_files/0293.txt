<p>The <b>Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</b> (<b>JCPC</b>) is the highest <a href="/wiki/Appellate_court" title="Appellate court">court of appeal</a> for certain British territories and <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> countries. Established on 13 August 1833 to hear appeals formerly heard by the <a href="/wiki/King-in-Council" title="King-in-Council">King-in-Council</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> the Privy Council formerly acted as the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_court" title="Supreme court">court of last resort</a> for the entire <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> (other than for the United Kingdom itself), and continues to act as the highest <a href="/wiki/Appellate_court" title="Appellate court">court of appeal</a> for several independent <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> nations, the <a href="/wiki/Crown_Dependencies" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown Dependencies">Crown Dependencies</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories" title="British Overseas Territories">British Overseas Territories</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Howell_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Howell-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Formally a statutory committee of <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Her Majesty's Most Honourable Privy Council</a>, the Judicial Committee consists of <a href="/wiki/List_of_Lords_Justices_of_Appeal" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Lords Justices of Appeal">senior judges</a> who are Privy Councillors: they are predominantly <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Justice of the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Justices</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Senior_judge" class="mw-redirect" title="Senior judge">senior judges</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a>. It is often referred to as the <b>Privy Council</b>. In <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_realm" title="Commonwealth realm">Commonwealth realms</a>, appeals are nominally made to "Her Majesty in Council" (i.e. the <a href="/wiki/British_monarch" class="mw-redirect" title="British monarch">British monarch</a> as formally advised by her <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Privy Counsellors</a>), who then refers the case to the Judicial Committee for "advice", while in <a href="/wiki/Republics_in_the_Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Republics in the Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth republics</a> retaining the JCPC as their final court of appeal, appeals are made directly to the Judicial Committee itself. The panel of judges (typically five in number) hearing a particular case is known as "the Board". The "report" of the Board is always accepted by the Queen in Council as judgment. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origins of the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council can be traced back to the <i><a href="/wiki/Curia_regis" title="Curia regis">curia regis</a></i>, or royal council. In theory, the King was the fount of justice, and petitions for redress of wrongs arising from his courts were addressed to him. That power was gradually taken over by <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">Parliament</a> (which evolved out of the <i>curia regis</i>) within England, but the <a href="/wiki/King-in-Council" title="King-in-Council">King-in-Council</a> (which also evolved out of the <i>curia regis</i>) retained jurisdiction to hear petitions from the King's non-English possessions, such as the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> and, later on, from England's colonies.<sup id="cite_ref-Howell_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Howell-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The task of hearing appeals was given to a series of short-lived committees of the Privy Council. In 1679, appellate jurisdiction was given to the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Trade" title="Board of Trade">Board of Trade</a>, before being transferred to a standing Appeals Committee in 1696. By the nineteenth century, the growth of the British Empire, which had greatly expanded the appellate jurisdiction of the Privy Council (despite the loss of appeals from the American colonies), had put great strains on the existing arrangements. In particular, the Appeals Committee had to hear cases in a variety of legal systems, such as <a href="/wiki/Hindu_law" title="Hindu law">Hindu law</a>, with which its members were unfamiliar.<sup id="cite_ref-Howell_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Howell-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1833, at the instigation of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Brougham,_1st_Baron_Brougham_and_Vaux" title="Henry Brougham, 1st Baron Brougham and Vaux">Lord Brougham</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor" title="Lord Chancellor">Lord Chancellor</a>, Parliament passed the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_Act_1833&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Judicial Committee Act 1833 (page does not exist)">Judicial Committee Act 1833</a>. The Act established a statutory committee of the Privy Council, known as <b>The Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</b>, to hear appeals to the <a href="/wiki/King-in-Council" title="King-in-Council">King-in-Council</a>. In addition to colonial appeals, later legislation gave the Judicial Committee appellate jurisdiction over a range of miscellaneous matters, such as patents, ecclesiastical matters, and <a href="/wiki/Prize_(law)" title="Prize (law)">prize</a> suits.<sup id="cite_ref-Howell_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Howell-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> At its height, the Judicial Committee was said to be the court of final appeal for over a quarter of the world.
</p><p>In the twentieth century, the jurisdiction of the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council shrank considerably, as British <a href="/wiki/Dominion" title="Dominion">Dominions</a> established their own courts of final appeal and as British colonies became independent, although many retained appeals to the Privy Council post-independence. Canada abolished Privy Council appeals in 1949, India and South Africa in 1950, and New Zealand in 2003. Currently, twelve Commonwealth countries outside of the United Kingdom retain Privy Council appeals, in addition to various British and New Zealand territories. The Judicial Committee also retains jurisdiction over a small number of domestic matters in the United Kingdom, reduced by the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a> in 2009.
</p>

<p>The United Kingdom does not have a single highest national court; the Judicial Committee is the highest court of appeal in some cases, while in most others the highest court of appeal is the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a>. (In Scotland the highest court in criminal cases is the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Justiciary" title="High Court of Justiciary">High Court of Justiciary</a>; the Supreme Court is the highest court in civil cases and matters arising from <a href="/wiki/Devolution_issues" class="mw-redirect" title="Devolution issues">Scottish devolution</a>, the latter previously having been dealt with by the Judicial Committee.)
</p><p>The Judicial Committee of the Privy Council has jurisdiction in the following domestic matters:
</p>
<ul><li>Appeals against schemes of the <a href="/wiki/Church_Commissioners" title="Church Commissioners">Church Commissioners</a> (who control the estate of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>).</li>
<li>Appeals from the <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_court" title="Ecclesiastical court">ecclesiastical courts</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Arches_Court" title="Arches Court">Arches Court</a> of Canterbury and the <a href="/wiki/Chancery_Court_of_York" title="Chancery Court of York">Chancery Court of York</a>) in non-doctrinal faculty cases.</li>
<li>Appeals from the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Chivalry" title="High Court of Chivalry">High Court of Chivalry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Appeals from the <a href="/wiki/Admiralty_court" title="Admiralty court">Court of Admiralty</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cinque_Ports" title="Cinque Ports">Cinque Ports</a>.</li>
<li>Appeals from <a href="/wiki/Prize_court" title="Prize court">prize courts</a>.</li>
<li>Appeals from the Disciplinary Committee of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Veterinary_Surgeons" title="Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons">Royal College of Veterinary Surgeons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Disputes under the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_Disqualification_Act_1975" title="House of Commons Disqualification Act 1975">House of Commons Disqualification Act 1975</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Additionally, the government may (through the Queen) refer any issue to the committee for "consideration and report" under section 4 of the Judicial Committee Act 1833.
</p><p>The Judicial Committee of the Privy Council is the Court of Final Appeal for the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>. It hears appeals from the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Arches" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Arches">Arches Court</a> of Canterbury and the <a href="/wiki/Chancery_Court_of_York" title="Chancery Court of York">Chancery Court of York</a>, except on matters of doctrine, ritual or ceremony, which go to the <a href="/wiki/Court_for_Ecclesiastical_Causes_Reserved" class="mw-redirect" title="Court for Ecclesiastical Causes Reserved">Court for Ecclesiastical Causes Reserved</a>. By the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Church_Discipline_Act_1840&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Church Discipline Act 1840 (page does not exist)">Church Discipline Act 1840</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Appellate_Jurisdiction_Act_1876" title="Appellate Jurisdiction Act 1876">Appellate Jurisdiction Act 1876</a> all archbishops and bishops of the Church of England became eligible to be members of the Judicial Committee.
</p><p>Prior to the coming into force of the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Reform_Act_2005" title="Constitutional Reform Act 2005">Constitutional Reform Act 2005</a>, the Privy Council was the court of last resort for <a href="/wiki/Devolution_issues" class="mw-redirect" title="Devolution issues">devolution issues</a>. On 1 October 2009 this jurisdiction was transferred to the new <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Authority_of_Privy_Council_decisions_in_domestic_British_courts">Authority of Privy Council decisions in domestic British courts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Authority of Privy Council decisions in domestic British courts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Judgments of the Judicial Committee are not generally <a href="/wiki/Persuasive_authority#Binding_precedent" class="mw-redirect" title="Persuasive authority">binding</a> on courts within the United Kingdom, having only <a href="/wiki/Persuasive_authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Persuasive authority">persuasive authority</a>, but are binding on all courts within any other Commonwealth country from which an appeal is heard. Where a binding precedent of the UK Supreme Court, or of the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_House_of_Lords" class="mw-redirect" title="Judicial Committee of the House of Lords">House of Lords</a>, or of the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Appeal of England and Wales">Court of Appeal</a> conflicts with that of a decision of the Judicial Committee on English law, English courts are required to follow the domestic decision over that of the Judicial Committee except when the Judicial Committee has in its decision expressly directed the domestic court to follow its new decision.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> However, given the overlap between the membership of the Judicial Committee and of the Supreme Court, the decisions of the former are extremely persuasive and usually followed.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overseas_jurisdiction">Overseas jurisdiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Overseas jurisdiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Judicial Committee holds jurisdiction in appeals from the following 31 jurisdictions (including twelve independent nations):
</p><p>Appeal is to "Her Majesty in Council" from eight independent nations, and nineteen other jurisdictions:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_realm" title="Commonwealth realm">Commonwealth realms</a> of <a href="/wiki/Antigua_and_Barbuda" title="Antigua and Barbuda">Antigua and Barbuda</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">The Bahamas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Kitts_and_Nevis" title="Saint Kitts and Nevis">Saint Kitts and Nevis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lucia" title="Saint Lucia">Saint Lucia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Vincent_and_the_Grenadines" title="Saint Vincent and the Grenadines">Saint Vincent and the Grenadines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tuvalu" title="Tuvalu">Tuvalu</a>.</li>
<li>The New Zealand <a href="/wiki/Associated_state" title="Associated state">associated states</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cook_Islands" title="Cook Islands">Cook Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Niue" title="Niue">Niue</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Crown_Dependencies" class="mw-redirect" title="Crown Dependencies">Crown Dependencies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Jersey" title="Law of Jersey">Jersey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">Guernsey</a> (including Guernsey's own dependencies of <a href="/wiki/Alderney" title="Alderney">Alderney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sark" title="Sark">Sark</a>), and appeals from the <a href="/wiki/Staff_of_Government_Division" title="Staff of Government Division">Staff of Government Division</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/British_overseas_territories" class="mw-redirect" title="British overseas territories">British overseas territories</a> of <a href="/wiki/Anguilla" title="Anguilla">Anguilla</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Virgin_Islands" title="British Virgin Islands">British Virgin Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Islands" title="Cayman Islands">Cayman Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">Gibraltar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Montserrat" title="Montserrat">Montserrat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saint_Helena,_Ascension_and_Tristan_da_Cunha" title="Saint Helena, Ascension and Tristan da Cunha">St Helena, Ascension and Tristan da Cunha</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos_Islands" title="Turks and Caicos Islands">Turks and Caicos Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pitcairn_Islands" title="Pitcairn Islands">Pitcairn Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Antarctic_Territory" title="British Antarctic Territory">British Antarctic Territory</a>, the <a href="/wiki/British_Indian_Ocean_Territory" title="British Indian Ocean Territory">British Indian Ocean Territory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Georgia_and_the_South_Sandwich_Islands" title="South Georgia and the South Sandwich Islands">South Georgia and the South Sandwich Islands</a>.</li>
<li>The United Kingdom's <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_Base_Areas" class="mw-redirect" title="Sovereign Base Areas">Sovereign Base Areas</a> of Akrotiri and Dhekelia, in <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Appeal is directly to the Judicial Committee from three independent <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_republic" class="mw-redirect" title="Commonwealth republic">Commonwealth republics</a>:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, and also, if the case involves constitutional rights, <a href="/wiki/Kiribati" title="Kiribati">Kiribati</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Appeal to the head of state:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a>, an independent monarchy of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth of Nations</a> that is not a Commonwealth realm, has an agreement with the United Kingdom that the Judicial Committee hears cases in which an appeal to the <a href="/wiki/Sultan_of_Brunei" class="mw-redirect" title="Sultan of Brunei">Sultan</a> has been made, and reports back to him.<sup id="cite_ref-brunei_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brunei-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Members">Members</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Members">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following are members of the Judicial Committee:
</p>
<ul><li>Justices of the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a> (before the establishment of that court in 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_Appeal_in_Ordinary" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord of Appeal in Ordinary">Lords of Appeal in Ordinary</a>)</li>
<li>Other Lords of Appeal (senior judges) from within the United Kingdom</li>
<li>Privy Counsellors who are (or have been) judges of the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Appeal of England and Wales">Court of Appeal of England and Wales</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Inner_House" title="Inner House">Inner House</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Session" title="Court of Session">Court of Session</a> in Scotland, or of the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_in_Northern_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Appeal in Northern Ireland">Court of Appeal in Northern Ireland</a></li>
<li>Judges of certain superior courts in Commonwealth nations, who are appointed Privy Counsellors for the purpose of sitting in the JCPC</li></ul>
<p>The bulk of the Committee's work is done by the Supreme Court Justices, who are paid to work full-time in both the Supreme Court and the Privy Council. Overseas judges may not sit when certain UK domestic matters are being heard, but will often sit when appeals from their own countries are being heard.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Procedure">Procedure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Procedure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most appeals to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council are formally appeals to "Her Majesty in Council". Appeals from Brunei are formally to the <a href="/wiki/Sultan_of_Brunei" class="mw-redirect" title="Sultan of Brunei">Sultan and Yang di-Pertuan</a>, while appeals from republics within the Commonwealth are directly to the Judicial Committee. Appeals are generally by leave of the local Court of Appeal, although the Judicial Committee retains discretionary power to grant leave to appeal as well.
</p><p>After hearing an appeal, the panel of judges which heard the case (known as "the Board") issues its decision in writing. For appeals to Her Majesty in Council, the decision is framed in the form of advice to Her Majesty, which is inevitably followed and given effect by being embodied in an <a href="/wiki/Order_in_Council" title="Order in Council">Order in Council</a>. Formerly, the Judicial Committee could only give a unanimous report, but since the Judicial Committee (Dissenting Opinions) Order 1966, <a href="/wiki/Dissenting_opinion" title="Dissenting opinion">dissenting opinions</a> have been allowed.
</p><p>The Judicial Committee is not bound by its own previous decisions, but may depart from them in exceptional circumstances if following its previous decisions would be unjust or contrary to public policy.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Judicial Committee of the Privy Council is based in London. From its establishment to 2009, it mainly met in the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_Chamber" class="mw-redirect" title="Privy Council Chamber">Privy Council Chamber</a> in <a href="/wiki/Downing_Street" title="Downing Street">Downing Street</a>, although increase in the Judicial Committee's business in the twentieth century required it to sit simultaneously in several panels, which met elsewhere. The Chamber, designed by <a href="/wiki/John_Soane" title="John Soane">John Soane</a>, was often criticised for its interior design, and was extensively remodelled in 1845 by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Barry" title="Charles Barry">Sir Charles Barry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Howell_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Howell-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> On 1 October 2009, the Judicial Committee moved to the former <a href="/wiki/Middlesex_Guildhall" title="Middlesex Guildhall">Middlesex Guildhall</a> building, which had been refurbished in 2007 to provide a home for both the JCPC and the newly created <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a>. In this renovated building, Court 3 is used for Privy Council sittings.
</p><p>In recent years, the Judicial Committee has occasionally sat outside of London. Between 2005 and 2010 it sat twice in Mauritius and three times in the Bahamas.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline_in_Commonwealth_appeals">Decline in Commonwealth appeals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Decline in Commonwealth appeals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Initially, all <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_realm" title="Commonwealth realm">realms</a> and their territories maintained a right of appeal to the Privy Council. Many of those Commonwealth countries that became republics, or which had indigenous monarchies, preserved the Judicial Committee's jurisdiction by agreement with the United Kingdom. However, retention of a right of appeal to a court located overseas, made up mostly of British judges who may be out of tune with local values, has often come to be seen as incompatible with notions of an independent nation's sovereign status, and so a number of Commonwealth members have ended the right of appeal from their jurisdiction. The <a href="/wiki/Balfour_Declaration_of_1926" title="Balfour Declaration of 1926">Balfour Declaration of 1926</a>, while not considered to be <i><a href="/wiki/Lex_scripta" title="Lex scripta">lex scripta</a></i>, severely limited the conditions under which the Judicial Committee might hear cases:<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>From these discussions it was clear that it was no part of the policy of His Majesty's Government in Great Britain that questions affecting judicial appeals should be determined otherwise than in accordance with the wishes of the part of the Empire primarily affected&#160;...</p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1901, the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Australia" title="Constitution of Australia">Constitution of Australia</a> limited appeals from the new federal <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Australia" title="High Court of Australia">High Court of Australia</a> to the Privy Council, by prohibiting appeals on constitutional matters unless leave is granted by the High Court on <i>inter se</i> questions. Appeals on non-constitutional matters were not prohibited, but the federal <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Australia" title="Parliament of Australia">Parliament of Australia</a> had the power to legislate to limit them. The right of appeal from federal courts (including territory supreme courts) was abolished through the <i>Privy Council (Limitation of Appeals) Act 1968</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Appeals from state courts, a continuation of the right to appeal decisions of colonial courts before 1901, continued, until they were also abolished by the <a href="/wiki/Australia_Act_1986" title="Australia Act 1986">Australia Act 1986</a>, which was enacted by both the UK and Australian parliaments, on the request of all the state governments. The <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Australia" title="Constitution of Australia">Australian Constitution</a> retains the provision allowing the <a href="/wiki/High_Court_of_Australia" title="High Court of Australia">High Court of Australia</a> to permit appeals to the Privy Council on <i><a href="/wiki/Inter_se" title="Inter se">inter se</a></i> questions. However, the High Court has stated that it will not give such permission, that the jurisdiction to do so "has long since been spent", and that it is obsolete.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council_cases_originating_in_Canada" title="List of Judicial Committee of the Privy Council cases originating in Canada">List of Judicial Committee of the Privy Council cases originating in Canada</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Newfoundland_cases_of_the_Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council_(pre-1949)" title="List of Newfoundland cases of the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council (pre-1949)">List of Newfoundland cases of the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council (pre-1949)</a></div>
<p>Canada created its own <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Canada" title="Supreme Court of Canada">Supreme Court</a> in 1875 and abolished appeals to the Privy Council in criminal cases in 1933.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Despite this, some decisions by the Supreme Court of Canada went on to appeal in the JCPC, including notably the <a href="/wiki/Persons_Case" class="mw-redirect" title="Persons Case">Persons Case</a> (<i>Edwards v Canada (AG)</i>), which established that women were "persons" under the <a href="/wiki/British_North_America_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="British North America Act">British North America Act</a> (Canada's <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Canada" title="Constitution of Canada">early Constitution</a>) eligible to sit in the <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Canada" title="Senate of Canada">Senate of Canada</a>. It also established what came to be known as the "<a href="/wiki/Living_tree_doctrine" title="Living tree doctrine">living tree doctrine</a>" in Canadian Constitutional law, which says that a constitution is organic and must be read in a broad and liberal manner so as to adapt it to changing times.
</p><p>In 1949, all appeals to the Privy Council were abolished, but prior to this, there were several factors that served to limit the effectiveness of measures to reduce appeals:
</p>
<ul><li>Appeals of rulings from the various provincial courts of appeal could still be made directly to the Privy Council, without first going to the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Canada" title="Supreme Court of Canada">Supreme Court of Canada</a>.</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Cushing_v._Dupuy" class="mw-redirect" title="Cushing v. Dupuy">Cushing v. Dupuy</a></i> (1885),<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> the Privy Council held that the ability to grant special leave to appeal to the Privy Council was unaffected, as the prerogative of the Crown cannot be taken away except by express words.</li>
<li>In <i><a href="/wiki/Nadan_v_The_King" class="mw-redirect" title="Nadan v The King">Nadan v The King</a></i> (1926),<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> the Privy Council ruled that the provision of the <a href="/wiki/Criminal_Code_(Canada)" title="Criminal Code (Canada)">Criminal Code</a> barring appeals to the Privy Council was <i><a href="/wiki/Ultra_vires" title="Ultra vires">ultra vires</a></i> of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Canada" title="Parliament of Canada">Parliament of Canada</a> as it was contrary to s. 2 of the <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Laws_Validity_Act_1865" title="Colonial Laws Validity Act 1865">Colonial Laws Validity Act 1865</a>.</li></ul>
<p><i>Nadan</i>, together with the <a href="/wiki/King%E2%80%93Byng_Affair" class="mw-redirect" title="King–Byng Affair">King–Byng Affair</a>, was a major irritant for Canada and provoked the discussion at the <a href="/wiki/1926_Imperial_Conference" title="1926 Imperial Conference">1926 Imperial Conference</a> which led to the <a href="/wiki/Balfour_Declaration_1926" class="mw-redirect" title="Balfour Declaration 1926">Balfour Declaration</a>, which declared the United Kingdom and the <a href="/wiki/Dominion" title="Dominion">Dominions</a> to be
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>... autonomous Communities within the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>, equal in status, in no way subordinate one to another in any aspect of their domestic or external affairs, though united by a common allegiance to <a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">the Crown</a>, and freely associated as members of the British <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth of Nations</a>.
</p></blockquote>
<p>With that Declaration and its statutory confirmation in the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Westminster_1931" title="Statute of Westminster 1931">Statute of Westminster 1931</a> (Imp, 22–23 Geo 5, c.4)<sup id="cite_ref-westminster_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-westminster-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> the impediment to abolishing appeals to the Privy Council, whether or not it had been legitimate, was comprehensively removed. Criminal appeals to the Privy Council were ended in 1933. Moves to extend the abolition to civil matters were shelved during the growing international crisis of the 1930s but re-tabled after World War II, and civil appeals ended in 1949, with an amendment of the Supreme Court Act.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Cases begun before 1949 were still allowed to appeal after 1949, and the final case to make it to the Council was not until 1959 with the case of <i><a href="/wiki/Ponoka-Calmar_Oils_v_Wakefield" title="Ponoka-Calmar Oils v Wakefield">Ponoka-Calmar Oils v Wakefield</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The JCPC played a controversial role in the evolution of Canadian <a href="/wiki/Federalism" title="Federalism">federalism</a> in that, whereas most Fathers of Confederation in negotiating the union of the British North American colonies against the backdrop of the American Civil War wished to ensure a strong central government vis-à-vis relatively weak provinces, appeals to the JCPC in constitutional matters progressively shifted the balance in favour of the provinces.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> While a few commentators have suggested that Canadian <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a> retain the right to appeal to the Privy Council because their treaties predate their relationship to Canada, the JCPC has not entertained any such appeal since 1867 and the dominant view is that no such appeal right exists.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caribbean_Community">Caribbean Community</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Caribbean Community">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The nations of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Community" title="Caribbean Community">Caribbean Community</a> voted in 2001 to abolish the right of appeal to the Privy Council in favour of a <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Court_of_Justice" title="Caribbean Court of Justice">Caribbean Court of Justice</a> (CCJ). Some debate between member countries and also the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> had repeatedly delayed the court's date of inauguration. As of 2005, <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> replaced the process of appeals to Her Majesty in Council with the CCJ, which had then come into operation. The Republic of <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> also enacted local legislation allowing the CCJ to have jurisdiction over their sovereign final court of appeals system. Belize acceded to the Appellate Jurisdiction of the CCJ on 1 June 2010. As it stands, a few other CARICOM states appear to be ready for the abolition of appeals to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council in the immediate future. The government of <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> in particular had come close and attempted to abolish appeals to the Judicial Committee without the support of the opposition in Parliament; however, it was ruled by the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council that the procedure used in Jamaica to bypass the opposition was incorrect and unconstitutional.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Another attempt will also be forthcoming.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Caribbean governments have been coming under increased pressure from their electorates<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> to devise ways to override previous rulings by the JCPC such as <i><a href="/wiki/Pratt_v_A-G" class="mw-redirect" title="Pratt v A-G">Pratt v A-G</a></i> (Jamaica, 1993),<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/R_v_Hughes" title="R v Hughes">R v Hughes</a></i> (Saint Lucia, 2002), <i><a href="/wiki/Fox_v_R" title="Fox v R">Fox v R</a></i> (Saint Kitts and Nevis, 2002), <i><a href="/wiki/Reyes_v_R" title="Reyes v R">Reyes v R</a></i> (2002, Belize), <i><a href="/wiki/Boyce_v_R" title="Boyce v R">Boyce v R</a></i> (Barbados, 2004), and <i><a href="/wiki/Matthew_v_S" title="Matthew v S">Matthew v S</a></i> (Trinidad and Tobago, 2004), all of which are Privy Council judgments concerning the death penalty in the Caribbean region.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The then President of the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lord_Phillips_of_Worth_Matravers" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Phillips of Worth Matravers">Lord Phillips of Worth Matravers</a>, has voiced displeasure with <a href="/wiki/Anglophone_Caribbean" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglophone Caribbean">Caribbean</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth countries</a> continuing to rely on the British JCPC. During an interview Lord Phillips was quoted by the <i><a href="/wiki/Financial_Times" title="Financial Times">Financial Times</a></i> as saying that <span style="padding-right:.15em;">"</span>'in an ideal world' Commonwealth countries—including those in the Caribbean—would stop using the Privy Council and set up their own final courts of appeal instead".<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 18 December 2006, the Judicial Committee made history when for the first time in more than 170 years it ventured outside London, holding a five-day sitting in <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">the Bahamas</a>. Lords Bingham, Brown, Carswell, and Scott, and Baroness Hale travelled to the Bahamas for the special sitting at the invitation of <a href="/wiki/Joan_Sawyer" title="Joan Sawyer">Dame Joan Sawyer</a>, then the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Court_of_Appeal_of_the_Bahamas" title="President of the Court of Appeal of the Bahamas">President of the Court of Appeal of the Bahamas</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> the Committee returned to the Bahamas in December 2007 for a second sitting. On the latter occasion, Lords Hope, Rodger, Walker, and Mance, and Sir Christopher Rose, heard several cases. At the end of the sitting, Lord Hope indicated that there may be future sittings of the Committee in the Bahamas,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> and the Committee has indeed sat in the Bahamas again, in 2009.
</p>
<h3><span id="Sri_Lanka_.28Ceylon.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Sri_Lanka_(Ceylon)">Sri Lanka (Ceylon)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Sri Lanka (Ceylon)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a>, formerly <a href="/wiki/Dominion_of_Ceylon" title="Dominion of Ceylon">Ceylon</a>, abolished appeals to the Privy Council in 1972, on becoming a republic. Previously, the Privy Council had ruled in <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ibralebbe_v_The_Queen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ibralebbe v The Queen (page does not exist)">Ibralebbe v The Queen</a></i> that it remained the highest court of appeal in Ceylon notwithstanding the <a href="/wiki/Independence_of_Sri_Lanka" class="mw-redirect" title="Independence of Sri Lanka">country's independence</a> as a <a href="/wiki/British_Dominions" class="mw-redirect" title="British Dominions">Dominion</a> in 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Gambia">The Gambia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: The Gambia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_Gambia" title="The Gambia">The Gambia</a> retained the right of appeal to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council under the <a href="/wiki/Gambia_Independence_Act_1964" title="Gambia Independence Act 1964">Gambia Independence Act 1964</a>, even after The Gambia became a <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_republic" class="mw-redirect" title="Commonwealth republic">Commonwealth republic</a> in April 1970 under Sir <a href="/wiki/Dawda_Jawara" title="Dawda Jawara">Dawda Jawara</a>. Appeals were still taken to the J.C.P.C. from 1994 to 1998, when <a href="/wiki/Yahya_Jammeh" title="Yahya Jammeh">Yahya Jammeh</a>, the then dictator and <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Gambia" title="President of the Gambia">President of the Gambia</a> decided to restructure the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gambian_judiciary&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gambian judiciary (page does not exist)">Gambian judiciary</a> under the 1997 <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Gambia" title="Constitution of the Gambia">Constitution of the Gambia</a> to replace the J.C.P.C. with the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_Gambia" title="Supreme Court of the Gambia">Supreme Court of the Gambia</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grenada">Grenada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Grenada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenadian</a> appeals to the Privy Council were temporarily abolished from 1979 until 1991, as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Grenadian_Revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Grenadian Revolution">Grenadian Revolution</a>, which brought Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Bishop" title="Maurice Bishop">Maurice Bishop</a> to power. <a href="/w/index.php?title=People%27s_Law_84&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="People&#39;s Law 84 (page does not exist)">People's Law 84</a> was enacted to this effect. In 1985, <i><a href="/wiki/Mitchell_v_DPP" title="Mitchell v DPP">Mitchell v DPP</a></i> affirmed Grenada's right to unilaterally abolish appeals to the Privy Council. In 1991, Grenada restored the JCPC's jurisdiction.
</p><p>In 2016, there was a proposal in the <a href="/wiki/Grenadian_constitutional_referendum,_2016" class="mw-redirect" title="Grenadian constitutional referendum, 2016">Grenadian constitutional referendum, 2016</a> to terminate appeals from Grenada to the JCPC and to replace the JCPC with the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Court_of_Justice" title="Caribbean Court of Justice">Caribbean Court of Justice</a>. This was rejected by a 56.73% majority, which means the JCPC remains Grenada's highest court.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Guyana">Guyana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Guyana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> retained the right of appeal to the Privy Council until the government of Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Forbes_Burnham" title="Forbes Burnham">Forbes Burnham</a> passed the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council (Termination of Appeals) Act 1970.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hong_Kong">Hong Kong</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Hong Kong">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hong Kong's court system changed following the <a href="/wiki/Transfer_of_sovereignty_over_Hong_Kong" class="mw-redirect" title="Transfer of sovereignty over Hong Kong">transfer of sovereignty</a> from the United Kingdom to <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People&#39;s Republic of China">China</a> on 1 July 1997, with the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Final_Appeal_of_Hong_Kong" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Final Appeal of Hong Kong">Court of Final Appeal</a> serving as the highest judicial authority of the <a href="/wiki/Special_Administrative_Region" class="mw-redirect" title="Special Administrative Region">Special Administrative Region</a> (SAR), and (pursuant to Article 158 of the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Basic_Law" title="Hong Kong Basic Law">Basic Law</a>) the power of final interpretation vested not in the Court of Final Appeal of Hong Kong but in the <a href="/wiki/Standing_Committee_of_the_National_People%27s_Congress_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Standing Committee of the National People&#39;s Congress of China">Standing Committee of the National People's Congress of China</a>.
</p><p>Decisions of the Privy Council on Hong Kong appeals before the 1&#160;July 1997 remain binding on the courts of Hong Kong.  This accords with the principle of continuity of the legal system enshrined in Article 8 of the Basic Law.  Decisions of the Privy Council on non-Hong Kong appeals are of persuasive authority only.  Such decisions were not binding on the courts in Hong Kong under the doctrine of precedent before 1 July 1997 and are not binding today.  Decisions of the House of Lords before 1 July 1997 stand in a similar position. It is of the greatest importance that the courts of Hong Kong should derive assistance from overseas jurisprudence, particularly from the final appellate courts of other common law jurisdictions. This is recognised by Article 84 of the Basic Law.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pursuant to Article 158 of the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_Basic_Law" title="Hong Kong Basic Law">Basic Law</a> (the constitutional instrument of the SAR), the power of final interpretation of the Basic Law is vested not in the Court of Final Appeal of Hong Kong but in the <a href="/wiki/Standing_Committee_of_the_National_People%27s_Congress_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Standing Committee of the National People&#39;s Congress of China">Standing Committee of the National People's Congress of China</a>, which, unlike the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council, is a political body rather than an independent and impartial tribunal of last resort.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> retained the right of appeal from the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Court_of_India" title="Federal Court of India">Federal Court of India</a> to the Privy Council after the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Dominion_of_India" title="Dominion of India">Dominion of India</a>. Following the replacement of the Federal Court with the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_India" title="Supreme Court of India">Supreme Court of India</a> in January 1950, the Abolition of Privy Council Jurisdiction Act 1949 came into effect, ending the right of appeal to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Irish_Free_State">Irish Free State</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Irish Free State">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council_cases_on_appeal_from_the_Irish_Free_State" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Judicial Committee of the Privy Council cases on appeal from the Irish Free State">List of Judicial Committee of the Privy Council cases on appeal from the Irish Free State</a></div>
<p>The right of appeal to the Privy Council was provided for in the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Irish_Free_State" title="Constitution of the Irish Free State">Constitution of the Irish Free State</a> until its abolition in 1933 by an Act of the <a href="/wiki/Oireachtas" title="Oireachtas">Oireachtas</a> amending said constitution.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moore_v_Attorney-General_of_the_Irish_Free_State&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Moore v Attorney-General of the Irish Free State (page does not exist)">Moore v Attorney-General of the Irish Free State</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> the right of the Oireachtas to abolish appeals to the Privy Council was challenged as a violation of the 1921 <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Treaty" title="Anglo-Irish Treaty">Anglo-Irish Treaty</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mooreDecision_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mooreDecision-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The then <a href="/wiki/Attorney_General_for_England_and_Wales" title="Attorney General for England and Wales">Attorney General for England and Wales</a> (<a href="/wiki/Sir_Thomas_Inskip" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Thomas Inskip">Sir Thomas Inskip</a>) is reported to have warned the then <a href="/wiki/Attorney-General_of_the_Irish_Free_State" class="mw-redirect" title="Attorney-General of the Irish Free State">Attorney-General of the Irish Free State</a> (<a href="/wiki/Conor_Maguire" title="Conor Maguire">Conor Maguire</a>) that the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a> had no right to abolish appeals to the Privy Council.<sup id="cite_ref-mooreDecision_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mooreDecision-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The Judicial Committee of the Privy Council itself ruled that the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a> Government had that right under the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Westminster_1931" title="Statute of Westminster 1931">Statute of Westminster 1931</a> (Imp.).<sup id="cite_ref-mooreDecision_52-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mooreDecision-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jamaica">Jamaica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Jamaica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 2015, the Jamaican <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Jamaica" title="Parliament of Jamaica">House of Representatives</a> approved with the necessary two-thirds majority bills to end legal appeals to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council and make the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Court_of_Justice" title="Caribbean Court of Justice">Caribbean Court of Justice</a> Jamaica's final court of appeal. The reform will be debated by the Jamaican Senate; however, the government needed the support of at least one opposition Senator for the measures to be approved by the required two-thirds majority.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_general_election,_2016" class="mw-redirect" title="Jamaican general election, 2016">2016 general election</a> was called before the reforms could be brought to the Senate for a final vote. The Jamaican Labour Party, which opposed the changes, won the election and has promised to hold a referendum on the issue.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The date of the event predicted near this tag has passed. (June 2019)">needs update</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Malaysia">Malaysia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Malaysia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> abolished appeals to the Privy Council in criminal and constitutional matters in 1978, and in civil matters in 1985.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Proposals to abolish appeals to the Privy Council in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> date back to the early 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-public_policy_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-public_policy-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> It was not until October 2003 that New Zealand law was changed to abolish appeals to the Privy Council in respect of all cases heard by the Court of Appeal of New Zealand after the end of 2003, in favour of a <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_New_Zealand" title="Supreme Court of New Zealand">Supreme Court of New Zealand</a>. In 2008, Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/John_Key" title="John Key">John Key</a> ruled out any abolition of the Supreme Court and return to the Privy Council.<sup id="cite_ref-key_pc_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-key_pc-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Judgment was delivered on 3 March 2015 in the last appeal from New Zealand to be heard by the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pakistan">Pakistan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Pakistan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Dominion_of_Pakistan" title="Dominion of Pakistan">Dominion of Pakistan</a> retained the right of appeal to the Privy Council from the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Court_of_Pakistan" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Court of Pakistan">Federal Court of Pakistan</a> until the Privy Council (Termination of Jurisdiction) Act 1950 was passed. The Federal Court of <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> remained the highest court until 1956, when the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Pakistan" title="Supreme Court of Pakistan">Supreme Court of Pakistan</a> was established.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhodesia">Rhodesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Rhodesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhodesian_Constitution_of_1965&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Rhodesian Constitution of 1965 (page does not exist)">Rhodesian Constitution of 1965</a> coming into effect as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Rhodesia%27s_Unilateral_Declaration_of_Independence" title="Rhodesia&#39;s Unilateral Declaration of Independence">Unilateral Declaration of Independence</a>, appeals continued to be accepted by the Privy Council as late as 1969 due to the fact that under international law, Rhodesia remained a British colony until gaining its independence as <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a> in April 1980.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Singapore">Singapore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Singapore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Singapore abolished Privy Council appeals in all cases save those involving the death penalty, or in civil cases where the parties had agreed to such a right of appeal, in 1989. The remaining rights of appeal were abolished in 1994.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>South Africa abolished the right of appeal to the Privy Council from the Appellate Division of the then <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_South_Africa" title="Supreme Court of South Africa">Supreme Court of South Africa</a> in 1950 under the terms of the Privy Council Appeals Act, 1950.
</p>
