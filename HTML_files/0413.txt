<p><b>Hakka</b> is one of the major groups of <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Chinese" title="Varieties of Chinese">varieties of Chinese</a>, spoken natively by the <a href="/wiki/Hakka_people" title="Hakka people">Hakka people</a> throughout southern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a> and throughout the <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">diaspora</a> areas of <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Chinese" title="Overseas Chinese">overseas Chinese</a> communities around the world.
</p><p>Due to its primary usage in scattered isolated regions where communication is limited to the local area, Hakka has developed numerous <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">varieties</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">dialects</a>, spoken in different provinces, such as <a href="/wiki/Guangdong" title="Guangdong">Guangdong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guangxi" title="Guangxi">Guangxi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hainan" title="Hainan">Hainan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fujian" title="Fujian">Fujian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sichuan" title="Sichuan">Sichuan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hunan" title="Hunan">Hunan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jiangxi" title="Jiangxi">Jiangxi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guizhou" title="Guizhou">Guizhou</a>, as well as in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>. Hakka is not <a href="/wiki/Mutually_intelligible" class="mw-redirect" title="Mutually intelligible">mutually intelligible</a> with <a href="/wiki/Yue_Chinese" title="Yue Chinese">Yue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wu_Chinese" title="Wu Chinese">Wu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_Min" title="Southern Min">Southern Min</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin</a> or other branches of Chinese, and itself contains a few mutually unintelligible varieties. It is most closely related to <a href="/wiki/Gan_Chinese" title="Gan Chinese">Gan</a> and is sometimes classified as a variety of Gan, with a few northern Hakka varieties even being partially mutually intelligible with southern Gan. There is also a possibility that the similarities are just a result of shared <a href="/wiki/Areal_feature" title="Areal feature">areal features</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Taiwan, where Hakka is the native language of a significant minority of the island's residents, is a center for the study and preservation of the language. Pronunciation differences exist between the <a href="/wiki/Taiwanese_Hakka" title="Taiwanese Hakka">Taiwanese Hakka</a> dialects and Mainland China's Hakka dialects; even in Taiwan, two major local varieties of Hakka exist.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Meixian_dialect" title="Meixian dialect">Meixian dialect</a> (Moiyen) of northeast <a href="/wiki/Guangdong" title="Guangdong">Guangdong</a> in China has been taken as the "standard" dialect by the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People&#39;s Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>. The Guangdong Provincial Education Department created an official <a href="/wiki/Romanization" title="Romanization">romanization</a> of Moiyen in 1960, one of four languages receiving this status in Guangdong.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hakka_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name of the Hakka people who are the predominant original native speakers of the variety literally means "guest families" or "guest people": <i>Hak</i> 客 (Mandarin: <i>kè</i>) means "guest", and <i>ka</i> 家 (Mandarin: <i>jiā</i>) means "family". Among themselves, Hakka people variously called their language <i>Hak-ka-fa</i> (<i>-va</i>) 客家話, <i>Hak-fa</i> (<i>-va</i>) 客話, <i>Tu-gong-dung-fa</i> (<i>-va</i>) 土廣東話, literally "Native Guangdong language", and <i>Ngai-fa</i> (<i>-va</i>)𠊎話, "My/our language". In Tonggu county (铜鼓县), Jiangxi province, people call their language <i>Huai-yuan-fa</i> 怀远話.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hakka_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hakka_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is commonly believed that <a href="/wiki/Hakka_people" title="Hakka people">Hakka people</a> have their origins in several episodes of migration from northern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> into southern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> during periods of war and civil unrest<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> dating back as far as the end of <a href="/wiki/Western_Jin" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Jin">Western Jin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The forebears of the Hakka came from present-day <a href="/wiki/Central_Plains_(China)" class="mw-redirect" title="Central Plains (China)">Central Plains</a> provinces of <a href="/wiki/Henan" title="Henan">Henan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shaanxi" title="Shaanxi">Shaanxi</a>, and brought with them features of Chinese varieties spoken in those areas during that time. (Since then, the speech in those regions has evolved into dialects of modern <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">Mandarin</a>). The presence of many archaic features occur in modern Hakka, including final consonants <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">-p -t -k</span>, as are found in other modern southern Chinese varieties, but which have been lost in Mandarin.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Laurent_Sagart" title="Laurent Sagart">Laurent Sagart</a> (2002)<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> considers Hakka and southern <a href="/wiki/Gan_Chinese" title="Gan Chinese">Gan Chinese</a> to be sister dialects that descended from a single common ancestral language (Proto-Southern Gan) spoken in central Jiangxi during the <a href="/wiki/Song_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Song Dynasty">Song Dynasty</a>. In Hakka and southern Gan, Sagart (2002) identifies a non-Chinese <a href="/wiki/Substratum" class="mw-redirect" title="Substratum">substratum</a> that is possibly <a href="/wiki/Hmong-Mien_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Hmong-Mien languages">Hmong-Mien</a>, an archaic layer, and a more recent Late <a href="/wiki/Middle_Chinese" title="Middle Chinese">Middle Chinese</a> layer. Lexical connections between Hakka, Kra-Dai, and Hmong-Mien have also been suggested by Deng (1999).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to the migration of its speakers, Hakka may have been influenced by other language areas through which the Hakka-speaking forebears migrated. For instance, common vocabulary is found in Hakka, <a href="/wiki/Min_Chinese" title="Min Chinese">Min</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/She_language" title="She language">She</a> (Hmong–Mien) languages.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Today, most <a href="/wiki/She_people" title="She people">She people</a> in Fujian and Zhejiang speak <a href="/wiki/Shehua" title="Shehua">Shehua</a>, which is closely related to Hakka.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_development">Linguistic development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hakka_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Linguistic development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A regular pattern of sound change can generally be detected in Hakka, as in most Chinese varieties, of the derivation of <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phonemes</a> from earlier forms of Chinese. Some examples:
</p>
<ul><li>Characters such as 武 (war, martial arts) or 屋 (room, house), are pronounced roughly <i>mwio</i> and <i>uk</i> (<i>mjuX</i> and <i>ʔuwk</i> in <a href="/wiki/Baxter%27s_transcription_for_Middle_Chinese" title="Baxter&#39;s transcription for Middle Chinese">Baxter's transcription</a>) in Early <a href="/wiki/Middle_Chinese" title="Middle Chinese">Middle Chinese</a>, have an initial <i>v</i> phoneme in Hakka, being <i>vu</i> and <i>vuk</i> in Hakka respectively. Like in Mandarin, labiodentalisation process also changed <i>mj-</i> to a w-like sound in Hakka before grave vowels, while Cantonese retained the original distinction (compare Mandarin 武 <i>wǔ</i>, 屋 <i>wū</i>, Cantonese 武 <i>mou</i><sup>5</sup>, 屋 <i>uk</i><sup>1</sup>).</li>
<li>Middle Chinese initial phonemes /ɲ/ (<i>ny</i> in Baxter’s transcription) of the characters 人 and 日, among others, merged with <i>ng-</i> /ŋ/ initials in Hakka (人 <i>ngin</i>, 日 <i>ngit</i>). For comparison, in Mandarin, /ɲ/ became <i>r-</i> (人 <i>rén</i>, 日 <i>rì</i>), while in Cantonese, it merged with initial /j/ (人 <i>yan</i><sup>4</sup>, 日 <i>yat</i><sup>6</sup>).</li>
<li>The initial consonant phoneme exhibited by the character 話 (word, speech; Mandarin <i>huà</i>) is pronounced <i>f</i> or <i>v</i> in Hakka (<i>v</i> does not properly exist as a distinct unit in many Chinese varieties).</li>
<li>The initial consonant of 學 <i><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">hɔk</span></i> usually corresponds with an <i>h</i> [h] approximant in Hakka and a voiceless alveo-palatal fricative (<i>x</i> [ɕ]) in Mandarin.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hakka_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Phonology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Meixian_dialect#Phonology" title="Meixian dialect">Meixian dialect §&#160;Phonology</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">Dialects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hakka_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dialects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hakka has as many regional dialects as there are counties with Hakka speakers as the majority. Some of these Hakka dialects are not mutually intelligible with each other. Surrounding <a href="/wiki/Mei_County,_Guangdong" class="mw-redirect" title="Mei County, Guangdong">Meixian</a> are the counties of <a href="/wiki/Pingyuan_County,_Guangdong" title="Pingyuan County, Guangdong">Pingyuan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dabu_County" title="Dabu County">Dabu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jiaoling" class="mw-redirect" title="Jiaoling">Jiaoling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xingning,_Guangdong" title="Xingning, Guangdong">Xingning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wuhua_County" title="Wuhua County">Wuhua</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fengshun" class="mw-redirect" title="Fengshun">Fengshun</a>. Each is said to have its own special phonological points of interest. For instance, Xingning lacks the codas <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[-m]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[-p]</span>. These have merged into <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[-n]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[-t]</span>, respectively. Further away from Meixian, the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> dialect lacks the <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[-u-]</span> medial, so, whereas Meixian pronounces the character 光 as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[kwɔŋ˦]</span>, Hong Kong Hakka dialect pronounces it as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[kɔŋ˧]</span>, which is similar to the Hakka spoken in neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Shenzhen" title="Shenzhen">Shenzhen</a>.
</p><p>As much as endings and vowels are important, the tones also vary across the dialects of Hakka. The majority of Hakka dialects have six tones. However, there are dialects which have lost all of their  <a href="/wiki/Checked_tone" title="Checked tone">checked tone</a> (Ru Sheng), and the characters originally of this tone class are distributed across the non-Ru tones. Such a dialect is <a href="/wiki/Changting_County" title="Changting County">Changting</a> which is situated in the Western Fujian province. Moreover, there is evidence of the retention of an earlier Hakka tone system in the dialects of <a href="/wiki/Haifeng" class="mw-redirect" title="Haifeng">Haifeng</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lufeng,_Guangdong" title="Lufeng, Guangdong">Lufeng</a> situated on coastal south eastern <a href="/wiki/Guangdong" title="Guangdong">Guangdong</a> province. They contain a yin-yang splitting in the Qu tone, giving rise to seven tones in all (with yin-yang registers in Ping and Ru tones and a Shang tone).
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, there are two main dialects: Sixian and Hailu (alternatively known as Haifeng; Hailu refers to <a href="/wiki/Haifeng_County" title="Haifeng County">Haifeng County</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lufeng,_Guangdong" title="Lufeng, Guangdong">Lufeng County</a>). Most Hakka dialect speakers found on Taiwan originated from these two regions. Sixian speakers come from Jiaying Prefecture (<a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hant">嘉應</span>), mainly from the four counties of Chengxiang (now <a href="/wiki/Meixian_District" title="Meixian District">Meixian District</a>), Zhengping (now <a href="/wiki/Jiaoling" class="mw-redirect" title="Jiaoling">Jiaoling</a>), <a href="/wiki/Xingning,_Guangdong" title="Xingning, Guangdong">Xingning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pingyuan_County,_Guangdong" title="Pingyuan County, Guangdong">Pingyuan</a>. Most dialects of <a href="/wiki/Taiwanese_Hakka" title="Taiwanese Hakka">Taiwanese Hakka</a>, except Sixian and Dabu, preserved <a href="/wiki/Postalveolar_consonant" title="Postalveolar consonant">postalveolar consonants</a> (<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[tʃ]</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[tʃʰ]</span>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ʃ]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ʒ]</span>), which are uncommon in other southern Chinese varieties.
</p>

<p><i>Ethnologue</i> reports the dialects as <i>Yue-Tai</i> (Meixian, Wuhua, Raoping, Taiwan Kejia: Meizhou above), <i>Yuezhong </i>(Central Guangdong), <i>Huizhou, Yuebei</i> (Northern Guangdong), <i>Tingzhou</i> (Min-Ke), <i>Ning-Long</i> (Longnan), <i>Yugui, Tonggu.</i>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">Vocabulary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hakka_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Vocabulary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Like other southern Chinese varieties, Hakka retains single syllable words from earlier stages of Chinese; thus a large number of syllables are distinguished by tone and final consonant. This reduces the need for compounding or making words of more than one syllable. However, it is also similar to other Chinese varieties in having words which are made from more than one syllable.

<p>Hakka prefers the verb <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[kɔŋ˧˩]</span> <span lang="zh-Hant" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%AC%9B" class="extiw" title="wikt:講">講</a></span> when referring to <i>saying</i> rather than the Mandarin <i>shuō</i> <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%AA%AA" class="extiw" title="wikt:說">說</a> (Hakka <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[sɔt˩]</span>).

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_systems">Writing systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hakka_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Writing systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Various dialects of Hakka have been written in a number of Latin orthographies, largely for religious purposes, since at least the mid-19th century.
</p><p>Previously, the single largest work in Hakka was the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> and <a href="/wiki/Psalms" title="Psalms">Psalms</a> (1993, 1138 pp., see <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.worldscriptures.org/pages/chinesehakka.html">The Bible in Chinese: Hakka</a>), but since 2012 that has been surpassed by the publication of the complete Hakka Bible known as the <a href="/wiki/Hakka_Bible:_Today%27s_Taiwan_Hakka_Version" title="Hakka Bible: Today&#39;s Taiwan Hakka Version">Today's Taiwan Hakka Version</a> and includes the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> along with audio recordings. These works render Hakka in both romanization (<a href="/wiki/Pha%CC%8Dk-fa-s%E1%B9%B3" title="Pha̍k-fa-sṳ">pha̍k-fa-sṳ</a>) and Han characters (including ones unique to Hakka) and are based on the dialects of Taiwanese Hakka speakers. The work of Biblical translation is being performed by missionaries of the <a href="/wiki/Presbyterian_Church_in_Canada" title="Presbyterian Church in Canada">Presbyterian Church in Canada</a>.
</p><p>The popular <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Prince" title="The Little Prince">The Little Prince</a></i> has also been translated into Hakka (2000), specifically the <a href="/wiki/Miaoli_County" title="Miaoli County">Miaoli</a> dialect of Taiwan (itself a variant of the <a href="/wiki/Sixian_dialect" title="Sixian dialect">Sixian dialect</a>). This also was dual-script, albeit using the <a href="/wiki/Tongyong_Pinyin" title="Tongyong Pinyin">Tongyong Pinyin</a> scheme.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hakka_Chinese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The world's only primarily Hakka-language television channel is <a href="/wiki/Hakka_TV" title="Hakka TV">Hakka TV</a> in Taiwan, a state-run broadcasting service started in 2003.
</p>
