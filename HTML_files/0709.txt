<p><b>William Lee</b> "<b>Billy</b>" <b>Currie</b> (born 1 April 1950, <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>, West Yorkshire, England<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) is an English violist, violinist, pianist, keyboardist, and songwriter.  He is best known for his work with <a href="/wiki/New_wave_music" title="New wave music">new wave</a> band <a href="/wiki/Ultravox" title="Ultravox">Ultravox</a>, who achieved their greatest commercial success in the 1980s.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Billy_Currie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Currie had an interest in music from an early age. As a child he sang a lot and at the age of ten he got a <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">guitar</a>. In 1961 he picked up the <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violin</a> at school and also sang in a <a href="/wiki/Choir" title="Choir">choir</a>. A year later he was in the school orchestra, but had to change instrument to <a href="/wiki/Viola" title="Viola">viola</a> to get a place at the <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_School_of_Music" class="mw-redirect" title="Huddersfield School of Music">Huddersfield School of Music</a>, which he joined in 1965. The education was a preparation to become a orchestral player, learning viola with <a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a> as his second instrument and also studying <a href="/wiki/Composer#Training" title="Composer">composition</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harmony" title="Harmony">harmony</a>. During this time he joined a <a href="/wiki/String_quartet" title="String quartet">string quartet</a>, played viola in an orchestra and performed with a viola, clarinet and piano trio. At this time Currie was excited to discover composers like <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9la_Bart%C3%B3k" title="Béla Bartók">Béla Bartók</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Schoenberg" title="Arnold Schoenberg">Arnold Schoenberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Honegger" title="Arthur Honegger">Arthur Honegger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Varese" class="mw-redirect" title="Edgar Varese">Edgar Varese</a>. By 1967 he became increasingly interested in <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock music</a>, excited by the fact that bands created something unique and original together. In 1969 he had qualified for a place at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Music" title="Royal Academy of Music">Royal Academy of Music</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> for further education, but decided not to go. Currie was interested in breaking into other areas of music and joined a rock band instead, playing viola. In the early 1970's he was a member of the experimental art group The Ritual Theatre, which included four classically trained musicians and four dancers. With this group Currie performed in <a href="/wiki/Holland" title="Holland">Holland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Festival" class="mw-redirect" title="Edinburgh Festival">Edinburgh Festival</a> and various other places.
</p><p>In 1974, Currie left Ritual Theatre to join the glam rock band <a href="/wiki/Tiger_Lily_(UK_band)" title="Tiger Lily (UK band)">Tiger Lily</a> on viola and keyboard. In 1976, after several name changes, the band ultimately became <a href="/wiki/Ultravox" title="Ultravox">Ultravox</a> and recorded three albums, <i><a href="/wiki/Ultravox!_(album)" title="Ultravox! (album)">Ultravox!</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ha!-Ha!-Ha!" title="Ha!-Ha!-Ha!">Ha!-Ha!-Ha!</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Systems_of_Romance" title="Systems of Romance">Systems of Romance</a></i>, before singer <a href="/wiki/John_Foxx" title="John Foxx">John Foxx</a> left to pursue a solo career in March 1979.
</p><p>During Ultravox's subsequent hiatus, Currie dedicated himself to different projects. He collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Gary_Numan" title="Gary Numan">Gary Numan</a> who had admired the <i>Systems Of Romance</i> album and asked Currie to record some songs that were included on his first solo album, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pleasure_Principle_(Gary_Numan_album)" title="The Pleasure Principle (Gary Numan album)">The Pleasure Principle</a></i>. Currie toured with Numan's band during 1979. During soundchecks for the tour Currie, with Gary Numan band musicians <a href="/wiki/Dramatis" title="Dramatis">Chris Payne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cedric_Sharpley" class="mw-redirect" title="Cedric Sharpley">Cedric Sharpley</a> began to develop a song of their own called "Toot City".  Currie had also joined the studio-based band <a href="/wiki/Visage_(band)" title="Visage (band)">Visage</a>, fronted by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Strange" title="Steve Strange">Steve Strange</a>, that also included <a href="/wiki/Midge_Ure" title="Midge Ure">Midge Ure</a>. Currie and Ure developed a solid working relationship. Visage drummer <a href="/wiki/Rusty_Egan" title="Rusty Egan">Rusty Egan</a> encouraged Currie to ask Ure to join the defunct Ultravox as lead singer/guitarist. Whilst composing material for a new Ultravox album and for the debut Visage album, Ure collaborated with Currie on the "Toot City" track which eventually became "<a href="/wiki/Fade_to_Grey_(Visage_song)" title="Fade to Grey (Visage song)">Fade to Grey</a>". The single became a huge hit for Visage in early 1981. However, this was surpassed by Ultravox's hit "<a href="/wiki/Vienna_(Ultravox_song)" title="Vienna (Ultravox song)">Vienna</a>", released around the same time. Both Ultravox and Visage became highly successful recording acts in the early 1980s. Ure decided to leave Visage to focus on Ultravox full-time in 1982. Currie remained with Visage for a while longer, but he too had left by 1984.
</p><p>From 1980–86, Ultravox released seven top 10 albums, including a live album and a "greatest hits" compilation and featured in <a href="/wiki/Live_Aid" title="Live Aid">Live Aid</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wembley_Stadium_(1923)" title="Wembley Stadium (1923)">Wembley Stadium</a> on 13 July 1985. During the last days of the band, there were tensions between Currie and Ure. With an underwhelming response to their 1986 album <i><a href="/wiki/U-Vox" title="U-Vox">U-Vox</a></i>, the band effectively disintegrated.
</p><p>In 1988, Currie put out an <a href="/wiki/Instrumental" title="Instrumental">instrumental</a> solo album <i>Transportation</i>, released on the IRS NoSpeak <a href="/wiki/Record_label" title="Record label">record label</a>. <a href="/wiki/Steve_Howe_(guitarist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Steve Howe (guitarist)">Steve Howe</a> played guitar on some of the tracks. In 1989 he formed the band <a href="/w/index.php?title=Humania_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Humania (band) (page does not exist)">Humania</a>. An album by them called <i>Sinews of Soul</i> was released in 2005. 
</p><p>In 1992, Currie reformed Ultravox with a new line-up (himself being the only original member involved). With <a href="/wiki/Tony_Fennell" class="mw-redirect" title="Tony Fennell">Tony Fennell</a> on vocals, they recorded the album "Revelation". At the outset of the subsequent tour, Fennell left to be replaced by Sam Blue. The tour took in the former USSR and <a href="/wiki/Malta" title="Malta">Malta</a> amongst other locations. Many of the synthesisers for which Currie was famous, apart from the often-volatile OSCar and Minimoog, were abandoned for this tour, replaced with Korg Wavestations and T-series keyboards. A further Ultravox album featuring this line-up, "Ingenuity", was released in 1994. Both albums were commercially unsuccessful.
</p><p>Since 2001 Billy Currie has released several solo albums on his own label. These include <i>Unearthed</i>, completed in 1998, and <i>Keys and the Fiddle</i>, which includes Curries soundtrack to the short film <i>The Fragile Skin</i> (1999), new material and a previously unreleased 1983 collaboration with Steve Howe and <a href="/wiki/Hazel_O%27Connor" title="Hazel O&#39;Connor">Hazel O'Connor</a>.
</p><p>In 2009 and 2010, the "classic" Ultravox line-up from the 1980s (Currie, Ure, Chris Cross, and Warren Cann) reformed for two successful tours – their first together in over twenty years. In 2016 Currie announced his retirement from Ultravox. In May 2016 he released his 10th solo album <i>Doppel</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Instruments">Instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Billy_Currie&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Currie is noted for his use of analogue synthesisers, such as the <a href="/wiki/Generalmusic" title="Generalmusic">Elka</a> Rhapsody, and in particular his trademark <a href="/wiki/Solo_(music)" title="Solo (music)">soloing</a> sound, which typically consisted of soaring fluid lines on an <a href="/wiki/ARP_Odyssey" title="ARP Odyssey">ARP Odyssey</a>, making use of oscillator sync, later re-created with the Oxford Synthesiser Company "OSCar" synth. Currie's original instrument whilst at music college was the <a href="/wiki/Viola" title="Viola">viola</a>, and he frequently added solo violin or viola to the arrangements of songs he played on, a relatively unusual choice in pop music.
At the height of Ultravox's career, Billy Currie's keyboard rig included a Yamaha CP70 electric piano and SS30 string machine, CS80 synthesiser, a PPG Wave 2.2 digital synth, a Sequential Circuits Prophet T8 synth as well as his trademark ARP Odyssey. Other synths used by Ultravox included the Moog MiniMoog, Yamaha GS1 (the pad sound on "Lament") and Emulator II sampler.
</p>
