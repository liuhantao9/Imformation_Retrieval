<p>The <b>Caribbean Community</b> (<b>CARICOM</b> or <b>CC</b>) is an organisation of fifteen <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> nations and dependencies having primary objectives to promote economic integration and cooperation among its members, to ensure that the benefits of integration are equitably shared, and to coordinate foreign policy.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The organisation was established in 1973. Its major activities involve coordinating economic policies and development planning; devising and instituting <a href="/wiki/Projects_of_the_Caribbean_Community" title="Projects of the Caribbean Community">special projects</a> for the less-developed countries within its jurisdiction; operating as a regional single market for many of its members (Caricom Single Market); and handling regional trade disputes. The secretariat headquarters is in <a href="/wiki/Georgetown,_Guyana" title="Georgetown, Guyana">Georgetown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>. CARICOM is an official <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Observer" class="mw-redirect" title="United Nations Observer">United Nations Observer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>CARICOM was established by the English-speaking parts of the Caribbean, and currently includes all the independent anglophone island countries plus <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Montserrat" title="Montserrat">Montserrat</a>, as well as all other British Caribbean territories and <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> as associate members. English was its sole working language into the 1990s. The organization has become multilingual with the addition of <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch-speaking</a> <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> in 1995 and <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Creole" title="Haitian Creole">Haitian</a>- and <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>-speaking <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> in 2002. Furthermore, it has been suggested that <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> should also become a working language.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In July 2012, CARICOM announced that they were considering making French and Dutch official languages.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In 2001, the heads of government signed a revised <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Chaguaramas" title="Treaty of Chaguaramas">Treaty of Chaguaramas</a> that cleared the way to transform the idea of a <a href="/wiki/Common_market" class="mw-redirect" title="Common market">common market</a> CARICOM into a <a href="/wiki/CARICOM_Single_Market_and_Economy" title="CARICOM Single Market and Economy">Caribbean (CARICOM) Single Market and Economy</a>. Part of the revised treaty establishes and implements the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Court_of_Justice" title="Caribbean Court of Justice">Caribbean Court of Justice</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Membership">Membership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Community&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Membership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Member_states_of_the_Caribbean_Community" title="Member states of the Caribbean Community">Member states of the Caribbean Community</a></div>
<p>Currently CARICOM has 15 full members, 5 associate members and 8 observers. All of the associate members are <a href="/wiki/British_overseas_territories" class="mw-redirect" title="British overseas territories">British overseas territories</a>, and it is currently not established what the role of the associate members will be. The observers are states which engage in at least one of CARICOM's technical committees. Although the group has close ties with <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, that nation was excluded due to lack of full democratic internal political arrangement. In 2017 Cuba and the Caribbean Community (CARICOM) bloc signed the "CARICOM-Cuba Trade and Economic Cooperation Agreement"<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> to facilitate closer ties.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisational_structure">Organisational structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Community&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Organisational structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Structures comprised by the overall Caribbean Community (CARICOM).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Under Article 4 CARICOM breaks its 15 member states into two groups: Less Developed Countries (LDCs) and More Developed Countries (MDCs).<sup id="cite_ref-revised_chaguaramas_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revised_chaguaramas-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The countries of CARICOM which are designated as Less Developed Countries (LDCs) are:<sup id="cite_ref-revised_chaguaramas_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revised_chaguaramas-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Antigua &amp; Barbuda</li>
<li>Belize</li>
<li>Commonwealth of Dominica</li>
<li>Grenada</li>
<li>Republic of Haiti</li>
<li>Montserrat</li>
<li>Federation of St. Kitts &amp; Nevis</li>
<li>St. Lucia</li>
<li>St. Vincent &amp; the Grenadines</li></ul>
<p>The countries of CARICOM which are designated as More Developed Countries (MDCs) are:<sup id="cite_ref-revised_chaguaramas_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revised_chaguaramas-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Commonwealth of the Bahamas</li>
<li>Barbados</li>
<li>Co-operative Republic of Guyana</li>
<li>Jamaica</li>
<li>Republic of Suriname</li>
<li>Republic of Trinidad and Tobago</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chairmanship">Chairmanship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Community&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chairmanship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The post of Chairman (Head of CARICOM) is held in rotation by the regional Heads of State (for the republics) and Heads of Government (for the realms) of CARICOM's 15 member states. These include: Antigua and Barbuda, Belize, Dominica, Grenada, Haiti, Montserrat, St. Kitts and Nevis, St. Lucia, St. Vincent and the Grenadines, Bahamas, Barbados, Guyana, Jamaica, Suriname, Trinidad and Tobago.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heads_of_government">Heads of government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Community&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Heads of government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CARICOM contains a quasi-Cabinet of the individual <a href="/wiki/CARICOM_Heads_of_Government" title="CARICOM Heads of Government">Heads of Government</a>. These heads are given specific specialised portfolios of responsibility for overall regional development and integration.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secretariat">Secretariat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Community&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Secretariat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Secretariat_of_the_Caribbean_Community" title="Secretariat of the Caribbean Community">Secretariat of the Caribbean Community</a>, The term of office of the Secretary-General is five years, which may be renewed. (Chief Administrative Organ)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Secretary-General_of_the_Caribbean_Community" title="Secretary-General of the Caribbean Community">Secretary-General of the Caribbean Community</a>, the CARICOM Secretary General (Chief Executive) handles foreign and community relations.</li>
<li>Deputy Secretary-General of the Caribbean Community, handles human and social Development.</li>
<li>General Counsel of the Caribbean Community, handles trade and economic integration.</li></ul>
<p>The goal statement of the CARICOM Secretariat is:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>To provide dynamic leadership and service, in partnership with Community institutions and Groups, toward the attainment of a viable, internationally competitive and sustainable Community, with improved quality of life for all.
</p></blockquote>

<p><b>The Caribbean Court of Justice</b> (CCJ) is based in <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Spain" title="Port of Spain">Port of Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a> and was chiefly developed to act as a settlement unit for disputes on the functioning of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Single_Market_and_Economy" class="mw-redirect" title="Caribbean Single Market and Economy">Caribbean (CARICOM) Single Market and Economy</a> (CSME) (known as "original jurisdiction"). In addition, some of the region's Commonwealth Caribbean member states of CARICOM have opted to supplement original jurisdiction with "appellate jurisdiction" which by practice replaces the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Privy Council</a> (in London, United Kingdom) with the CCJ.
</p><p>As of 2018, the majority of member states continue to utilize the Privy Council as their final appellate court and three member states do not use the CCJ for either its original jurisdiction or its appellate jurisdiction because they have either not signed the Revised Treaty of Chaguaramas (the Bahamas and Haiti) or are a current United Kingdom Overseas Territory (Montserrat). A handful of various public <a href="/wiki/Propositions" class="mw-redirect" title="Propositions">propositions</a> have been held in several countries of CARICOM polling on public support for transitioning of appellate jurisdiction to the CCJ, and to date the majority of these measures held have failed.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_and_song">Standard and song</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Community&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Standard and song">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The flag of the Caribbean Community was chosen and approved in November 1983 at the Conference of Heads of Government Meeting in Port of Spain, Trinidad. The original design by the firm of WINART Studies in Georgetown, Guyana was substantially modified at the July 1983 Meeting of the Conference of Heads of Government.<sup id="cite_ref-CARICOM_Standard_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CARICOM_Standard-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The flag was first flown on 4 July 1984 in Nassau, Bahamas at the fifth Meeting of the Conference of Heads of Government.<sup id="cite_ref-CRW_Flags_CARICOM_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CRW_Flags_CARICOM-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The flag features a blue background, but the upper part is a light blue representing sky and the lower, a darker blue representing the Caribbean Sea. The yellow circle in the centre represents the sun on which is printed in black the logo of the Caribbean Community, two interlocking Cs. The two Cs are in the form of broken links in a chain, symbolising both unity and a break with the colonial past. The narrow ring of green around the sun represents the vegetation of the region.<sup id="cite_ref-CARICOM_Standard_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CARICOM_Standard-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For CARICOM's 40th anniversary, a competition to compose an official song or anthem for CARICOM was launched in April 2013<sup id="cite_ref-History_created_with_new_CARICOM_song_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History_created_with_new_CARICOM_song-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> to promote choosing a song that promoted unity and inspired CARICOM identity and pride. A regional panel of judges comprising independent experts in music was nominated by member states and the CARICOM Secretariat. Three rounds of competition condensed 63 entries to a final three, from which judges chose <i>Celebrating CARICOM</i> by Michele Henderson of Dominica<sup id="cite_ref-History_created_with_new_CARICOM_song_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History_created_with_new_CARICOM_song-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> in March 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-Song_Factsheet_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Song_Factsheet-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Henderson won a US$10,000 prize.<sup id="cite_ref-Song_Competition_Terms_of_Reference_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Song_Competition_Terms_of_Reference-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Her song was produced by her husband, Roland Delsol Jr., and arranged by Earlson Matthew. It also featured Michael Ferrol on drums and choral input from the St. Alphonsus Choir. It was re-produced for CARICOM by Carl Beaver Henderson of Trinidad and Tobago.<sup id="cite_ref-Song_Factsheet_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Song_Factsheet-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A second-place entry titled <i>My CARICOM</i> came from Jamaican Adiel Thomas<sup id="cite_ref-History_created_with_new_CARICOM_song_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History_created_with_new_CARICOM_song-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> who won US$5,000,<sup id="cite_ref-Song_Competition_Terms_of_Reference_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Song_Competition_Terms_of_Reference-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and a third-place song titled <i>One CARICOM</i> by Carmella Lawrence of St. Kitts and Nevis,<sup id="cite_ref-History_created_with_new_CARICOM_song_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History_created_with_new_CARICOM_song-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> won US$2,500.<sup id="cite_ref-Song_Competition_Terms_of_Reference_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Song_Competition_Terms_of_Reference-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The other songs from the top-ten finalists (in no particular order) were:
</p>
<ul><li><i>One Region one Caribbean</i> from Anguilla,</li>
<li><i>One Caribbean Family</i> from Jamaica,</li>
<li><i>CARICOM’s Light</i> from St. Vincent &amp; the Grenadines,</li>
<li><i>We Are CARICOM</i> from Dominica,</li>
<li><i>Together As one</i> from Dominica,</li>
<li><i>Blessed CARICOM</i> from Jamaica,</li>
<li><i>Together We Rise</i> from Jamaica.<sup id="cite_ref-Song_Factsheet_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Song_Factsheet-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The first official performance of <i>Celebrating CARICOM</i> by Henderson took place on Tuesday 1 July 2014 at the opening ceremony for the Thirty-Fifth Regional Meeting of the Conference of Heads of Government in Antigua and Barbuda.<sup id="cite_ref-History_created_with_new_CARICOM_song_15-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History_created_with_new_CARICOM_song-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Community&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CARICOM, originally the Caribbean Community and Common Market, was established by the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Chaguaramas" title="Treaty of Chaguaramas">Treaty of Chaguaramas</a><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> which took effect on 1 August 1973. The first four signatories were <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>.
</p><p>CARICOM superseded the 1965–1972 <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Free_Trade_Association" title="Caribbean Free Trade Association">Caribbean Free Trade Association</a> (CARIFTA) organised to provide a continued economic linkage between the English-speaking countries of the Caribbean after the dissolution of the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies_Federation" title="West Indies Federation">West Indies Federation</a>, which lasted from 3 January 1958 to 31 May 1962.
</p><p>A revised Treaty of Chaguaramas established the Caribbean Community including the CARICOM Single Market and Economy (CSME) and was signed by the <a href="/wiki/CARICOM_Heads_of_Government" title="CARICOM Heads of Government">CARICOM Heads of Government</a> of the Caribbean Community on 5 July 2001 at their Twenty-Second Meeting of the Conference in Nassau, The <a href="/wiki/Bahamas" class="mw-redirect" title="Bahamas">Bahamas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-revised_chaguaramas_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revised_chaguaramas-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The revised treaty cleared the way to transform the idea of a <a href="/wiki/Common_market" class="mw-redirect" title="Common market">common market</a> CARICOM into the <a href="/wiki/CARICOM_Single_Market_and_Economy" title="CARICOM Single Market and Economy">Caribbean (CARICOM) Single Market and Economy</a>.
</p><p>Haiti's membership in CARICOM remained effectively suspended from 29 February 2004 through early June 2006 following the <a href="/wiki/2004_Haitian_coup_d%27%C3%A9tat" title="2004 Haitian coup d&#39;état">2004 Haitian coup d'état</a> and the removal of <a href="/wiki/Jean-Bertrand_Aristide" title="Jean-Bertrand Aristide">Jean-Bertrand Aristide</a> from the presidency.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> CARICOM announced that no democratically elected government in CARICOM should have its leader deposed. The fourteen other heads of government sought to have Aristide fly from Africa to Jamaica and share his account of events with them, which infuriated the interim Haitian prime minister, <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_Latortue" title="Gérard Latortue">Gérard Latortue</a>, who announced he would take steps to take Haiti out of CARICOM. CARICOM thus voted on suspending the participation of Haitian officials from the councils of CARICOM.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Following the presidential election of <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Pr%C3%A9val" title="René Préval">René Préval</a>, Haitian officials were readmitted and Préval himself gave the opening address at the CARICOM Council of Ministers meeting in July.
</p><p>Since 2013 the CARICOM-bloc and with the Dominican Republic have been tied to the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> via an <a href="/wiki/Economic_Partnership_Agreements" title="Economic Partnership Agreements">Economic Partnership Agreements</a> signed in 2008 known as <a href="/wiki/CARIFORUM" title="CARIFORUM">CARIFORUM</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cpdcngo.org_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cpdcngo.org-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  The treaty grants all members of the European Union and CARIFORUM equal rights in terms of trade and investment. Under Article 234 of the agreement, the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Justice" title="European Court of Justice">European Court of Justice</a> handles dispute resolution between CARIFORUM and European Union states.<sup id="cite_ref-jamaica-gleaner.com_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jamaica-gleaner.com-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Thousands of Caricom nationals live within other member states of the Community.
</p><p>An estimated 30,000 Jamaicans legally reside in other CARICOM member states,<sup id="cite_ref-30,000_Jamaicans_residing_in_CARICOM_member_states_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30,000_Jamaicans_residing_in_CARICOM_member_states-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> mainly in the Bahamas (5,600),<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Antigua &amp; Barbuda (estimated 12,000),<sup id="cite_ref-Golding_calls_on_Jamaicans_in_Antigua_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Golding_calls_on_Jamaicans_in_Antigua-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Barbados and Trinidad &amp; Tobago).<sup id="cite_ref-30,000_Jamaicans_residing_in_CARICOM_member_states_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30,000_Jamaicans_residing_in_CARICOM_member_states-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Also, an estimated 150 Jamaicans live and work in Montserrat.<sup id="cite_ref-Golding_calls_on_Jamaicans_in_Antigua_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Golding_calls_on_Jamaicans_in_Antigua-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> A November 21, 2013 estimated put 16,958 Jamaicans residing illegally in Trinidad &amp; Tobago, as according to the records of the Office of the Chief Immigration Officer, their entry certificates would have since expired.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> By October 2014, the estimated Jamaicans residing illegally in Trinidad and Tobago was 19,000 along with an estimated 7,169 Barbadians and 25,884 Guyanese residing illegally.<sup id="cite_ref-7,000_illegal_Bajans_in_T&amp;T_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7,000_illegal_Bajans_in_T&amp;T-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> An estimated 8,000 Trinidadians and Tobagonians live in Jamaica.<sup id="cite_ref-Bissessar_celebrates_new_high_commission_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bissessar_celebrates_new_high_commission-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

Considering them, the total area reaches the 2 300 297 km².</div></div></div>
<p>Barbados hosts a large diaspora population of Guyanese, of whom (in 2005) 5,032 lived there permanently as citizens, permanent residents, immigrants (with immigrant status) and Caricom skilled nationals; 3,200 were residing in Barbados temporarily under work permits, as students, or with "reside and work" status. A further 2,000-3,000 Guyanese were estimated to be living illegally in Barbados at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-Guyanese,_British_and_Americans_in_Barbados_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guyanese,_British_and_Americans_in_Barbados-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Migration between Barbados and Guyana has deep roots, going back over 150 years, with the most intense period of Barbadian migration to then-British Guiana occurring between 1863 and 1886, although as late as the 1920s and 1930s Barbadians were still leaving Barbados for British Guiana.<sup id="cite_ref-When_Barbadians_emigrated_to_Guyana_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-When_Barbadians_emigrated_to_Guyana-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Migration between Guyana and Suriname also goes back a number of years. An estimated 50,000 Guyanese had migrated to Suriname by 1986<sup id="cite_ref-Ethnologue_Suriname_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ethnologue_Suriname-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Guyanese_Creole_Survey_Report_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guyanese_Creole_Survey_Report-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> In 1987 an estimated 30-40,000 Guyanese were in Suriname.<sup id="cite_ref-Guyanese_Vital_In_Suriname_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guyanese_Vital_In_Suriname-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Many Guyanese left Suriname in the 1970s and 1980s, either voluntarily by expulsion. Over 5,000 were expelled in January 1985 alone.<sup id="cite_ref-Nervous_neighbours_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nervous_neighbours-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> in the instability Suriname experienced following independence, both coups and civil war.<sup id="cite_ref-Guyanese_Creole_Survey_Report_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guyanese_Creole_Survey_Report-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> In 2013 an estimated 11,530 Guyanese had emigrated to Suriname and 4,662 Surinamese to Guyana.<sup id="cite_ref-Guyana_Migration_Profiles_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guyana_Migration_Profiles-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Association_of_Caribbean_States">Association of Caribbean States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Community&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Association of Caribbean States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CARICOM was instrumental in the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Caribbean_States" title="Association of Caribbean States">Association of Caribbean States</a> (ACS) on 24 July 1994. The original idea for the Association came from a recommendation of the West Indian Commission, established in 1989 by the CARICOM heads of state and government. The Commission advocated both deepening the integration process (through the CARICOM Single Market and Economy) and widening it through a separate regional organisation encompassing all states in the Caribbean.<sup id="cite_ref-Evolution_of_the_ACS_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Evolution_of_the_ACS-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>CARICOM accepted the commission's recommendations and opened dialogue with other Caribbean states, the Central American states and the Latin American nations of Colombia, Venezuela and Mexico which border the Caribbean, for consultation on the proposals of the West Indian Commission.<sup id="cite_ref-Evolution_of_the_ACS_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Evolution_of_the_ACS-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At an October 1993 summit the heads of state and government of CARICOM and the presidents of the then-<a href="/wiki/G-3_(Latin_America)" class="mw-redirect" title="G-3 (Latin America)">Group of Three</a> (Colombia, Mexico and Venezuela) formally decided to create an association grouping all states of the Caribbean basin. A work schedule for its formation was adopted. The aim was to create the association in less than a year, an objective which was achieved with the formal creation of the ACS.<sup id="cite_ref-Evolution_of_the_ACS_39-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Evolution_of_the_ACS-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Community_of_Latin_American_and_Caribbean_States">Community of Latin American and Caribbean States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Community&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Community of Latin American and Caribbean States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CARICOM was also involved in the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Community_of_Latin_American_and_Caribbean_States" title="Community of Latin American and Caribbean States">Community of Latin American and Caribbean States</a> (CELAC) on 3 December 2010. The idea for CELAC originated at the <a href="/wiki/Rio_Group" title="Rio Group">Rio Group</a>–Caribbean Community Unity Summit on 23 February 2010 in Mexico.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mexico&#39;s_Latin_American_Summit_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mexico&#39;s_Latin_American_Summit-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rio_Group_approves_expansion_at_Unity_Summit_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rio_Group_approves_expansion_at_Unity_Summit-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Union:_Economic_Partnership_Agreements">European Union: Economic Partnership Agreements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Community&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: European Union: Economic Partnership Agreements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since 2013, the CARICOM-bloc and the Dominican Republic have been tied to the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> via an <a href="/wiki/Economic_Partnership_Agreements" title="Economic Partnership Agreements">Economic Partnership Agreements</a> known as <a href="/wiki/CARIFORUM" title="CARIFORUM">CARIFORUM</a> signed in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-cpdcngo.org_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cpdcngo.org-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  The treaty grants all members of the European Union and CARIFORUM equal rights in terms of trade and investment.  Within the agreement under Article 234, the <a href="/wiki/European_Court_of_Justice" title="European Court of Justice">European Court of Justice</a> also carries dispute resolution mechanisms between CARIFORUM and the European Union states.<sup id="cite_ref-jamaica-gleaner.com_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jamaica-gleaner.com-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="OHADAC_Project">OHADAC Project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Community&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: OHADAC Project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In May 2016, Caricom's court of original jurisdiction, the CCJ, signed a memorandum of understanding (MOU) with the <a href="/wiki/African,_Caribbean_and_Pacific_Group_of_States" title="African, Caribbean and Pacific Group of States">ACP</a> Legal Association based in Guadeloupe recognising and supporting the goals of implementing a harmonised business law framework in the Caribbean through ACP Legal Association's <a href="/w/index.php?title=OHADAC_Project&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="OHADAC Project (page does not exist)">OHADAC Project</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CCJ_signs_MOU_on_OHADAC_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CCJ_signs_MOU_on_OHADAC-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>OHADAC is the acronym for the French "Organisation pour l'Harmonisation du Droit des Affaires en les Caraïbes", which translates into English as "Organisation for the Harmonisation of Business Law in the Caribbean". The OHADAC Project takes inspiration from a <a href="/wiki/OHADA" title="OHADA">similar organisation in Africa</a> and aims to enhance economic integration across the entire Caribbean and facilitate increased trade and international investment through unified laws and alternative dispute resolution methods.<sup id="cite_ref-CCJ_signs_MOU_on_OHADAC_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CCJ_signs_MOU_on_OHADAC-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
