	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Single (music)</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Type of music release usually containing one or two tracks</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">

</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:45rpm.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/45rpm.jpg/220px-45rpm.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="218" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f0/45rpm.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="297" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:45rpm.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>45 <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_per_minute" title="Revolutions per minute">rpm</a> single record with large central hole as used in the US for <a href="/wiki/Jukebox" title="Jukebox">jukeboxes</a></div></div></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Music_industry" title="Music industry">music industry</a>, a <b>single</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Art_release#Music" title="Art release">release</a>, typically a song <a href="/wiki/Sound_recording" class="mw-redirect" title="Sound recording">recording</a> of fewer tracks than an <a href="/wiki/LP_record" title="LP record">LP record</a> or an <a href="/wiki/Album" title="Album">album</a>. This can be released for <a href="/wiki/Record_sales" title="Record sales">sale</a> to the public in a variety of different formats. In most cases, a single is a song that is released separately from an album, although it usually also appears on an album. Typically, these are the songs from albums that are released separately for <a href="/wiki/Promotional_single" class="mw-redirect" title="Promotional single">promotional uses</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Music_download" title="Music download">digital download</a> or commercial radio airplay and are expected to be the most popular. In other cases a recording released as a single may not appear on an album.
</p><p>Despite being referred to as a single, in the era of <a href="/wiki/Music_download" title="Music download">digital distribution</a>, singles can include up to as many as three tracks. The biggest digital music distributor, <a href="/wiki/ITunes_Store" title="ITunes Store">iTunes Store</a>, accepts as many as three tracks less than ten minutes each as a single.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Any more than three tracks on a musical release or thirty minutes in total running time is either an <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">extended play</a> (EP) or, if over six tracks long, an album.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Historically, when mainstream music was purchased via vinyl records, singles would be released double-sided. That is to say, they were released with an <a href="/wiki/A-side_and_B-side" title="A-side and B-side">A-side and B-side</a>, on which two singles would be released, one on each side.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Moreover, traditionally, only the most popular songs from a previously released album would be released as a single. In more contemporary forms of music consumption, artists release most, if not all, of the tracks on an album as singles.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Single_(music)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origins of the single are in the late 19th century, when music was distributed on <a href="/wiki/Phonograph_cylinder" title="Phonograph cylinder">phonograph cylinders</a> that held two to four minutes worth of audio. These were then superseded by <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" class="mw-redirect" title="Gramophone record">gramophone records</a>, which initially also had a short-duration of playing time per record side. In the first 2-3 decades of the 20th century, almost all commercial music releases were in effect singles (the exceptions were usually for <a href="/wiki/Classical_music" title="Classical music">classical music</a> pieces, where multiple physical storage media items were bundled together and sold as an <a href="/wiki/Album" title="Album">album</a>). Gramophone discs were manufactured with a range of playback speeds (from 16 <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_per_minute" title="Revolutions per minute">rpm</a> to 78 rpm) and in several sizes (including 12-inch/30&#160;cm). By about 1910, however, the 10-inch (25&#160;cm), 78 rpm <a href="/wiki/Shellac" title="Shellac">shellac</a> disc had become the most commonly used format.
</p><p>The inherent technical limitations of the gramophone disc defined the standard format for commercial recordings in the early 20th century. The relatively crude disc-cutting techniques of the time and the thickness of the needles used on <a href="/wiki/Record_players" class="mw-redirect" title="Record players">record players</a> limited the number of grooves per inch that could be inscribed on the disc surface, and a high rotation speed was necessary to achieve acceptable recording and playback fidelity. 78 rpm was chosen as the standard because of the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_motor" title="Synchronous motor">electrically powered, synchronous</a> turntable motor in 1925, which ran at 3600 rpm with a 46:1 gear ratio, resulting in a rotation speed of 78.26 rpm.
</p><p>With these factors applied to the 10-inch format, songwriters and performers increasingly tailored their output to fit the new medium. The 3-minute single remained the standard into the 1960s, when the availability of microgroove recording and improved mastering techniques enabled recording artists to increase the duration of their recorded songs. The breakthrough came with <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Like_a_Rolling_Stone" title="Like a Rolling Stone">Like a Rolling Stone</a>". Although <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">CBS</a> tried to make the record more "radio friendly" by cutting the performance into halves, and separating them between the two sides of the <a href="/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride" title="Polyvinyl chloride">vinyl</a> <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" class="mw-redirect" title="Gramophone record">disc</a>, both Dylan and his fans demanded that the full six-minute <a href="/wiki/Take" title="Take">take</a> be placed on one side, and that radio stations play the song in its entirety.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_era">Digital era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Single_(music)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Digital era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As digital downloading and audio streaming have become more prevalent, it has become possible for every track on an album to also be available separately. Nevertheless, the concept of a single for an album has been retained as an identification of a more heavily promoted or more popular song (or group of songs) within an album collection. The demand for music downloads skyrocketed after the launch of <a href="/wiki/Apple_Computer" class="mw-redirect" title="Apple Computer">Apple's</a> <a href="/wiki/ITunes_Store" title="ITunes Store">iTunes Store</a> (then called <a href="/wiki/ITunes_version_history" class="mw-redirect" title="ITunes version history">iTunes Music Store</a>) in January 2001 and the creation of portable music and <a href="/wiki/Digital_audio_player" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital audio player">digital audio players</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/IPod" title="IPod">iPod</a>.
</p><p>In September 1997, with the release of <a href="/wiki/Duran_Duran" title="Duran Duran">Duran Duran</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Electric_Barbarella" title="Electric Barbarella">Electric Barbarella</a>" for paid downloads, <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a> became the first <a href="/wiki/Major_label" class="mw-redirect" title="Major label">major label</a> to sell a digital single from a well-known artist. Previously, <a href="/wiki/Geffen_Records" title="Geffen Records">Geffen Records</a> also released <a href="/wiki/Aerosmith" title="Aerosmith">Aerosmith</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Get_a_Grip" title="Get a Grip">Head First</a>" digitally for free.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In 2004, <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA) introduced digital single certification due to significant sales of digital formats, with <a href="/wiki/Gwen_Stefani" title="Gwen Stefani">Gwen Stefani</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Hollaback_Girl" title="Hollaback Girl">Hollaback Girl</a>" becoming RIAA's first platinum digital single.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 2013, RIAA incorporated on-demand streams into the digital single certification.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Single sales in the United Kingdom reached an all-time low in January 2005, as the popularity of the compact disc was overtaken by the then-unofficial medium of the music download. Recognizing this, On 17 April 2005, <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">Official UK Singles Chart</a> added the download format to the existing format of physical CD singles. <a href="/wiki/Gnarls_Barkley" title="Gnarls Barkley">Gnarls Barkley</a> was the first act to reach No.1 on this chart through downloads alone in April 2006, for their debut single <a href="/wiki/Crazy_(Gnarls_Barkley_song)" title="Crazy (Gnarls Barkley song)">"Crazy"</a>, which was released physically the following week. On 1 January 2007 digital downloads (including unbundled album tracks<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>) became eligible from the point of release, without the need for an accompanying physical.<sup id="cite_ref-OCCPack_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OCCPack-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Sales gradually improved in the following years, reaching a record high in 2008 that still proceeded to be overtaken in 2009, 2010 and 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_physical_singles">Types of physical singles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Single_(music)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Types of physical singles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:140405_Wega-Dual-300-01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/140405_Wega-Dual-300-01.jpg/220px-140405_Wega-Dual-300-01.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/140405_Wega-Dual-300-01.jpg/330px-140405_Wega-Dual-300-01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/140405_Wega-Dual-300-01.jpg/440px-140405_Wega-Dual-300-01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1800" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:140405_Wega-Dual-300-01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>45 rpm EP on a turntable, ready to be played</div></div></div>
<p>Singles have been issued in various formats, including 7-inch (18&#160;cm), 10-inch (25&#160;cm), and <a href="/wiki/12-inch_single" class="mw-redirect" title="12-inch single">12-inch</a> (30&#160;cm) <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" class="mw-redirect" title="Gramophone record">vinyl</a> discs (usually playing at 45 rpm); 10-inch (25-cm) shellac discs (playing at 78 rpm); <a href="/wiki/Cassette_single" title="Cassette single">cassette</a>, 8 and 12&#160;cm (3- and 5-inch) <a href="/wiki/CD_single" title="CD single">CD singles</a> and 7-inch (18&#160;cm) plastic <a href="/wiki/Flexi_disc" title="Flexi disc">flexi discs</a>. Other, less common, formats include singles on <a href="/wiki/Digital_Compact_Cassette" title="Digital Compact Cassette">Digital Compact Cassette</a>, <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Laserdisc" class="mw-redirect" title="Laserdisc">LD</a>, as well as many non-standard sizes of vinyl disc (5-inch/12&#160;cm, 8-inch/20&#160;cm, etc.).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="7-inch_format"><span id="7-inch"></span>7-inch format</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Single_(music)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 7-inch format">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The most common form of the vinyl single is the <b>45</b> or <b>7-inch</b>. The names are derived from its play speed, 45&#160;rpm, and the standard diameter, 7&#160;inches (18&#160;cm).
</p><p>The 7-inch 45&#160;rpm record was released 31 March 1949 by <a href="/wiki/RCA_Victor" class="mw-redirect" title="RCA Victor">RCA Victor</a> as a smaller, more durable and higher-<a href="/wiki/Fidelity" title="Fidelity">fidelity</a> replacement for the <a href="/wiki/78_rpm" class="mw-redirect" title="78 rpm">78 rpm</a> shellac discs.<sup id="cite_ref-ffrsing_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ffrsing-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The first 45&#160;rpm records were <a href="/wiki/Monaural" title="Monaural">monaural</a>, with recordings on both sides of the disc. As <a href="/wiki/Stereophonic_sound" title="Stereophonic sound">stereo</a> recordings became popular in the 1960s, almost all 45&#160;rpm records were produced in stereo by the early 1970s. <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a>, which had released the &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap">33<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>3</sub></span>&#160;rpm 12-inch <a href="/wiki/LP_record" title="LP record">vinyl LP</a> in June 1948, also released &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap">33<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>3</sub></span>&#160;rpm 7-inch vinyl singles in March 1949, but they were soon eclipsed by the RCA Victor 45. The first regular production 45&#160;rpm record pressed was "PeeWee the Piccolo" RCA Victor 47-0146 pressed 7 December 1948 at the Sherman Avenue plant in <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis" title="Indianapolis">Indianapolis</a>, R.O. Price, plant manager.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
The claim made that 48-0001 by <a href="/wiki/Eddy_Arnold" title="Eddy Arnold">Eddy Arnold</a> was the first 45 is evidently incorrect (even though as of this writing 48-0000 has not turned up) since all 45s were released simultaneously with the 45 player on the 29 March date. There was plenty of information 'leaked' to the public about the new 45 rpm system through front-page articles in <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> magazine on 4 December 1948 and again on 8 January 1949. RCA was trying to blunt the lead Columbia had established in releasing their &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap">33<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>3</sub></span>&#160; LP system back in June 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="12-inch_format"><span id="12-inch"></span>12-inch format</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Single_(music)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 12-inch format">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:12_Inch_Single_BBQ_Band.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/12_Inch_Single_BBQ_Band.jpg/220px-12_Inch_Single_BBQ_Band.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/12_Inch_Single_BBQ_Band.jpg/330px-12_Inch_Single_BBQ_Band.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/12_Inch_Single_BBQ_Band.jpg/440px-12_Inch_Single_BBQ_Band.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2520" data-file-height="2404" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:12_Inch_Single_BBQ_Band.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A twelve-inch gramophone record</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/12-inch_single" class="mw-redirect" title="12-inch single">12-inch single</a></div>
<p>Although 7 inches remained the standard size for vinyl singles, <a href="/wiki/12-inch_single" class="mw-redirect" title="12-inch single">12-inch singles</a> were introduced for use by <a href="/wiki/DJs" class="mw-redirect" title="DJs">DJs</a> in <a href="/wiki/Discotheque" class="mw-redirect" title="Discotheque">discos</a> in the 1970s. The longer playing time of these singles allowed the inclusion of extended dance mixes of tracks. In addition, the larger surface area of the 12-inch discs allowed for wider grooves (larger amplitude) and greater separation between grooves, the latter of which results in less cross-talk. Consequently, they are less susceptible to wear and scratches. The 12-inch single is still considered a standard format for <a href="/wiki/Dance_music" title="Dance music">dance music</a>, though its popularity has declined in recent years.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Single_(music)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The sales of singles are recorded in <a href="/wiki/Record_charts" class="mw-redirect" title="Record charts">record charts</a> in most countries in a <a href="/wiki/Top_40" title="Top 40">Top 40</a> format. These charts are often published in magazines and numerous <a href="/wiki/Television_program" class="mw-redirect" title="Television program">television shows</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radio_programming" title="Radio programming">radio</a> programs count down the list. In order to be eligible for inclusion in the charts the single must meet the requirements set by the charting company, usually governing the number of songs and the total playing time of the single.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gold_record_for_Put_a_Little_Love_in_Your_Heart.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Gold_record_for_Put_a_Little_Love_in_Your_Heart.JPG/220px-Gold_record_for_Put_a_Little_Love_in_Your_Heart.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Gold_record_for_Put_a_Little_Love_in_Your_Heart.JPG/330px-Gold_record_for_Put_a_Little_Love_in_Your_Heart.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Gold_record_for_Put_a_Little_Love_in_Your_Heart.JPG/440px-Gold_record_for_Put_a_Little_Love_in_Your_Heart.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="6000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gold_record_for_Put_a_Little_Love_in_Your_Heart.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The single of "Put a Little Love in Your Heart" was a hit record for <a href="/wiki/Jackie_DeShannon" title="Jackie DeShannon">Jackie DeShannon</a> in 1968. It was certified Gold in the United States when it sold more than 1,000,000 copies.</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Popular_music" title="Popular music">popular music</a>, the commercial and artistic importance of the single (as compared to the <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a> or <a href="/wiki/Album" title="Album">album</a>) has varied over time, technological development, and according to the audience of particular artists and genres. Singles have generally been more important to artists who sell to the youngest purchasers of music (younger <a href="/wiki/Teenager" class="mw-redirect" title="Teenager">teenagers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pre-teen" class="mw-redirect" title="Pre-teen">pre-teens</a>), who tend to have more limited financial resources. <sup id="cite_ref-ffrsing_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ffrsing-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Starting in the mid-sixties, albums became a greater focus and more important as artists created albums of uniformly high quality and coherent themes, a trend which reached its apex in the development of the <a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept album</a>. Over the 1990s and early 2000s, the single generally received less and less attention in the United States as albums, which on <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">compact disc</a> had virtually identical production and distribution costs but could be sold at a higher price, became most retailers' primary method of selling music. Singles continued to be produced in the UK and Australia, surviving the transition from compact disc to digital download.
</p><p>The discontinuation of the single has been cited as a major marketing mistake by the record companies considering it eliminated an inexpensive recording format for young fans to use to become accustomed to purchasing music. In its place was the predominance of the album which alienated customers by the expense of purchasing an expensive format for only one or two songs of interest. This in turn encouraged interest in <a href="/wiki/File_sharing" title="File sharing">file sharing</a> software on the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">internet</a> like <a href="/wiki/Napster" title="Napster">Napster</a> for single recordings initially which began to seriously undercut the music recording market.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dance_music" title="Dance music">Dance music</a>, however, has followed a different commercial pattern, and the single, especially the 12-inch vinyl single, remains a major method by which dance music is distributed.
</p><p>A curious development has been the popularity of mobile phone <a href="/wiki/Ringtone" title="Ringtone">ringtones</a> based on pop singles (on some modern phones, the actual single can be used as a ringtone). In September 2007, <a href="/wiki/Sony_BMG" title="Sony BMG">Sony BMG</a> announced they would introduce a new type of CD single, called "ringles", for the 2007 holiday season. The format included three songs by an artist, plus a ringtone accessible from the user's computer. Sony announced plans to release 50 ringles in October and November, while <a href="/wiki/Universal_Music_Group" title="Universal Music Group">Universal Music Group</a> expected to release somewhere between 10 and 20 titles.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In a reversal of this trend, a single has been released based on a ringtone itself. The <a href="/wiki/Crazy_Frog" title="Crazy Frog">Crazy Frog</a> ringtone, which was a cult hit in Europe in 2004, was released as a <a href="/wiki/Mashup_(music)" title="Mashup (music)">mashup</a> with "<a href="/wiki/Axel_F" title="Axel F">Axel F</a>" in June 2005 amid a massive publicity campaign and subsequently hit #1 on the UK charts.
</p><p>The term <i>single</i> is sometimes regarded as a misnomer, since one record usually contains two songs: the <a href="/wiki/A-side_and_B-side" title="A-side and B-side">A-side and B-side</a>. In 1982, CBS marketed one-sided singles at a lower price than two-sided singles.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_South_Korea">In South Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Single_(music)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In South Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Music_of_South_Korea" title="Music of South Korea">South Korean music</a>, the terminology for "albums" and "singles" is unique and includes an additional term, the "<b>single album</b>" (<a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>&#58;&#160;<span lang="ko-Hang" title="Korean language text">싱글 음반</span>&#59; <a href="/wiki/Revised_Romanization_of_Korean" title="Revised Romanization of Korean">RR</a>&#58;&#160;<i><i lang="ko-Latn" title="Korean-language romanization">singgeul eumban</i></i>), a category of releases that is not found outside of Korea. In English, the word "album" in ordinary usage refers to an LP-length music release with multiple tracks. By contrast, the Korean word for "album" (<a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>&#58;&#160;<span lang="ko-Hang" title="Korean language text">음반</span>&#59; <a href="/wiki/Revised_Romanization_of_Korean" title="Revised Romanization of Korean">RR</a>&#58;&#160;<i><i lang="ko-Latn" title="Korean-language romanization">eumban</i></i>) denotes a musical recording of any length released on physical media; it is closer in meaning to the English words "record" or "release". Although the terms "single albums" and "singles" are similar and sometimes may even overlap in meaning, depending on context, they are considered two distinct release types in South Korea. A "single album" refers to a physical release (like CD, LP, or some other media) collecting one or more singles, while a "single" is only a song itself, typically as a downloaded file or streamable song. The <a href="/wiki/Gaon_Album_Chart" title="Gaon Album Chart">Gaon Album Chart</a> tracks sales of all "offline" albums released as physical media, meaning that single albums compete alongside full-length studio albums (and all other albums). The <a href="/wiki/Gaon_Digital_Chart" title="Gaon Digital Chart">Gaon Digital Chart</a>, which tracks downloads and streams, is regarded as the official "singles" chart.
</p><p>As a distinct release type, the single album developed during the CD era in the 1990s. Single albums, typically including about two or three songs, were marketed as a more affordable alternative to a full-length CD album.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The term "single album" is sometimes used to refer to a release that would simply be called a "single" in western contexts, such as a 7-inch 45<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>rpm record released before the advent of downloadable music.
</p><p>To give an example of the differences between full-length albums, single albums, and singles: the K-pop boy band <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang_(South_Korean_band)" title="Big Bang (South Korean band)">Big Bang</a> has a full-length studio album, titled <i><a href="/wiki/Made_(Big_Bang_album)" title="Made (Big Bang album)">MADE</a></i>, which was originally released as a series of four single albums: <i><a href="/wiki/M_(Big_Bang_single)" class="mw-redirect" title="M (Big Bang single)">M</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/A_(Big_Bang_single)" class="mw-redirect" title="A (Big Bang single)">A</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/D_(Big_Bang_single)" class="mw-redirect" title="D (Big Bang single)">D</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/E_(Big_Bang_single)" class="mw-redirect" title="E (Big Bang single)">E</a></i>. Two singles were included on each of these single albums; the first in the series, <i>M</i>, contains the singles "<a href="/wiki/Loser_(Big_Bang_song)" title="Loser (Big Bang song)">Loser</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Bae_Bae" title="Bae Bae">Bae Bae</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A single album is distinct from a single even if it only includes one song. The single "<a href="/wiki/Gotta_Go_(Chungha_song)" title="Gotta Go (Chungha song)">Gotta Go</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Chungha_(singer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chungha (singer)">Chungha</a> was released on a single album titled <i>XII</i>, which was a one-track CD. Even though "Gotta Go" was the only song on <i>XII</i>, the two releases carry different titles and charted separately: <i>XII</i> reached No.<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>4 on the Gaon Album Chart, while "Gotta Go" reached No.<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>2 on the Gaon Digital Chart.
</p>
