	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Wayana language</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Wayana</b> (also referred to as Ojana, Ajana, Aiana, Ouyana, Uajana, Upurui, Oepoeroei, Roucouyen, Oreocoyana, Orkokoyana, Urucuiana, Urukuyana, and Alucuyana in the literature) is a language of the <a href="/wiki/Cariban_languages" title="Cariban languages">Cariban family</a>, spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Wayana_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Wayana people">Wayana</a> people, who live mostly in the borderlands of <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Brazil, they live along the <a href="/wiki/Paru_River" title="Paru River">Paru</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jari_River" title="Jari River">Jari</a> rivers, in Suriname<a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">,</a> along the <a href="/wiki/Tapanahony_River" title="Tapanahony River">Tapanahoni</a> and <a href="/wiki/Paloemeu_River" title="Paloemeu River">Paloemeu</a> rivers, and in French Guiana, along the upper <a href="/wiki/Maroni_(river)" title="Maroni (river)">Maroni Rive</a>r and its tributaries.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The exact number of Wayana is unclear. The issue is complicated because counts are done on a per-country basis. <a href="/wiki/SIL_International" title="SIL International">SIL</a> <a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">Ethnologue</a> lists 850 users of the language and 1,950 ethnic Wayana in all countries, using counts from 2006 and 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Instituto Socioambental, a Brazilian NGO, lists 1,629 ethnic Wayana, using counts from 2002 and 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The count of ethnic Wayana individuals is further complicated due to the close ties that the Wayana share with other ethnic groups in the region, especially the <a href="/wiki/Aparai_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Aparai people">Aparai</a> in Brazil, to the extent that they are sometimes considered one group, the Wayana-Aparai.<sup id="cite_ref-pib.socioambiental.org_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pib.socioambiental.org-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wayana_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Wayana are considered to be an amalgamation of several smaller ethnic groups, having subsumed the Upurui, Kukuyana, Opagwana, and Kumarawana, among others. They have extremely close ties with the Aparai, with whom they have lived and intermarried for at least 100 years in Brazil, though both the Aparai and Wayana consider themselves separate ethnic groups, differentiating themselves linguistically and through cultural practices.<sup id="cite_ref-pib.socioambiental.org_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pib.socioambiental.org-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> There are few Aparai in Wayana territory outside of Brazil.
</p><p>This unique situation is a result of European contact and interethnic conflict in the past four centuries. Disease forced dwindling populations of smaller ethnic groups to amalgamate with the Wayana in the 18th century for increased survival.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  Also in the 18th century, around the time of <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Brazil" title="Colonial Brazil">European arrival in the Brazilian interior</a>, the Wayana, whose original territory is considered to be Northern Brazil, started to migrate to Suriname and French Guiana due to conflicts resulting indirectly from colonization.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> As a result, there are three distinct Wayana territories today. The remaining Wayana in Brazil forged ties with the Aparai, who had migrated to their territory, and continue to maintain kinship and trade ties with Wayana in Suriname and French Guiana.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Wayana (and Aparai) were involved heavily with trade, especially with the <i>meikolo</i> (Black Maroons), as well as Brazilian and Surinamese traders until the end of the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In the 20th century, Wayana started working in rubber extraction, and started to settle in villages, ending their traditionally nomadic way of life.<sup id="cite_ref-:7_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  By mid-century, missionaries (including a SIL project from 1962 to 1992) and government institutions started to gain presence in Brazilian Wayana-Aparai territory, implementing government health and education services. <a href="/wiki/Funda%C3%A7%C3%A3o_Nacional_do_%C3%8Dndio" title="Fundação Nacional do Índio">FUNAI</a> has maintained a post, and has granted assistance in the region since 1973.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_family">Language family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wayana_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Language family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Cariban languages—to which Wayana belongs—are distributed throughout Northern South America, in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Brazil" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Brazil">Northern Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>, with speakers also in <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_Brazil&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Central Brazil (page does not exist)">Central Brazil</a>. There are an estimated 25 remaining Cariban languages, with references to over 100 in the historical literature. Many of the Cariban languages referenced in the literature, and others unknown to Europeans, have gone extinct due to European contact. The number of speakers of all Cariban languages is estimated to be 60,000 to 100,000, though more than half speak the Carib language proper, <a href="/wiki/Macushi_language" title="Macushi language">Makushi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pemon_language" title="Pemon language">Pemong</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Kap%C3%B3ng_language" title="Kapóng language">Kapong</a> (the last 3 are closely related). Most Cariban languages have 100 to 3,000 speakers.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Documentation of the both the extinct and remaining languages is scant in many cases, consisting of wordlists with flawed phonetic transcription and little grammatical description. Classification of Cariban languages into branches is therefore difficult, though many recent descriptive research projects are rectifying the lack of accurate data of the living Cariban languages.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Wayana is considered by Kaufman (1994, as cited by Gildea, 2003)<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> to be part of his proposed central branch, but a more recent classification (Gildea, 2005)<sup id="cite_ref-:3_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> considers it still in search of a branch.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_on_Wayana">Research on Wayana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wayana_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Research on Wayana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wayana, compared to its many of its Cariban peers, is very well described. The first work on Wayana was wordlists by Crevaux (1882, as cited by Tavares, 2005),<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Coudreau (1892),<sup id="cite_ref-:5_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and De Goeje (1909, as cited by Tavares, 2005).<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> De Goeje also published an incomplete grammar detailing some morphological aspects of Wayana in 1946 (as cited by Tavares, 2005).<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Walter Jackson's 1972 "A Wayana Grammar" builds on this grammar and clarifies some of the flaws in De Goeje's description of morphology and phonology, but still leaves many morphological and syntactic distinctions untouched.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kenneth Howell's 1982 MA thesis describes a specific aspect of Wayana syntax – constituent order.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The most comprehensive work on Wayana is the 2005 PhD dissertation of Petronila Tavares, "A Grammar of Wayana",<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> which is a descriptive grammar of Wayana's phonology, morphology, and syntax. Wayana is morphologically rich, and thus, most emphasis is given to morphology. The grammar provides little theoretical elaboration, "hop[ing] that future researchers will fill this gap" (Tavares, 2005, pp.&#160;6).<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Eliane Camargo, who started fieldwork among the Wayana at the same time as Tavares, has also significantly contributed to the study of Wayana language, with many descriptions of specific features of Wayana, such as nominal possession, postpositions, word order,<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and nominative aspect markers.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> She wrote a Wayana-Portuguese dictionary in 1997,<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> as well as a Wayana-French dictionary in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Lastly, she has written several works of linguistic anthropology, concerning bi-/multi-lingualism amongst the Wayana.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:5_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Much anthropological work has been conducted regarding the Wayana. Early ethnographic studies include de Goeje<sup id="cite_ref-:5_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and Huralt,<sup id="cite_ref-:5_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> among others. The Wayana's relationship with the Aparai has also elicited many ethnographic studies on subjects such as their bilingualism (Camargo 1997),<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> their kinship (Koehn 1975)<sup id="cite_ref-:5_12-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and their exchange patterns (Barbosa 2007).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> More recently, the Wayana-Aparai were also part of ProDocult, which is an effort from the Brazilian government to document indigenous culture, by creating digital archives and training indigenous peoples in documentary filmmaking and anthropological research.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The Wayana-Aparai project focused on food systems and traditional knowledge of flora and fauna.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> As well, Karen Hough (2008)<sup id="cite_ref-:7_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:7-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> published an MPhil thesis in ethnolinguistics on spatial perception in Wayana, taking up Tavares' urge for more theoretical elaboration of Wayana grammar.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_Status">Language Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wayana_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Language Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The vitality of the language is also variant in different areas. In Brazil it is considered <a href="/wiki/Endangered_language" title="Endangered language">moribund</a> by Ethnologue, with grandparents being the only active users; however, Tavares (2005)<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> mentions some villages on the Paru river where the language is in active use by children and adults, though the general trend is toward Aparai bilingualism and dominance.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> While Tavares does not consider the language healthy, it is possibly not moribund everywhere. According to Ethnologue, in French Guiana it is shifting, with active use by adults and little transmission to children. In Suriname, it is developing, with all generations using it and some use of standardized literature.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wayana_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview_of_Morphology">Overview of Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wayana_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Overview of Morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wayana's <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">morphology</a>, like that of other Cariban languages, is very rich, and makes extensive use of <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">affixes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> There are five major speech classes in Wayana: <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">nouns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verbs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Postpositions" class="mw-redirect" title="Postpositions">postpositions</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">adverbs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_particle" title="Grammatical particle">particles</a>. Each class can be distinguished by the presence (or absence) of specific affixes. Particles take no affixes, and are not <a href="/wiki/Bound_morphemes" class="mw-redirect" title="Bound morphemes">morphologically bound</a>. They "expres[s] many different meanings such as <a href="/wiki/Evidentiality" title="Evidentiality">evidentiality</a>, <a href="/wiki/Negation_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Negation (linguistics)">negation</a> … [and] emotivity …".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>. Barring particles, all members of each speech class are clearly recognizable by the affixes that they can take. In her "Grammar of Wayana", Tavares addresses the major aspects of each speech class in-depth. In regards to nouns, she discusses <a href="/wiki/Possession_(linguistics)" title="Possession (linguistics)">possessive</a>, personal, and number affixes, Wayana pronouns, and special cases such as sound symbolic words. For verbs, she outlines the two "sets" of verbs found in Wayana ("Set I" –on which tense, aspect, and mood are marked through affixes, and <i>t</i>-V-(<i>he</i>) verbs – so-called due to their characteristic ambifix). She also addresses <a href="/wiki/Valency_(linguistics)" title="Valency (linguistics)">valence-changing</a> morphemes, and <a href="/wiki/Gerundive" title="Gerundive">gerundive verb forms</a>. For postpositions, Tavares examines the personal prefixes, and spatial suffixes, as well as semantic classes of postpositions (including spatial postpositions). <a href="/wiki/Morphological_derivation" title="Morphological derivation">Derivational morphology</a> is addressed for all classes, as class change (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Nominalization" title="Nominalization">nominalization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Verbalisation" title="Verbalisation">verbalization</a>) through affixes occurs often. Derivation is especially important for adverbs, as most adverbs are derived from other speech classes. Tavares presents the few underived adverbs that exist, as well the processes of adverbialization.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

</p><p>It is important to note that nouns in Wayana fall under three classes, in regards to possessibility: unpossessable, optionally possessable, and inherently possessed. Unpossessable nouns do not take possessive prefixes or suffixes. Optionally possessed nouns are semantically diverse, and can occur in either possessed or unpossessed form (most examples given use optionally possessed nouns). Inherently possessed nouns "denote entities that stand in a stable, intrinsic relationship with another entity" (Tavares, 2005, pp.&#160;136), and must always be possessed. Kinship terms (such as in 5a.) and some body parts fall under this category. Inherently possessed nouns often, but not always, take –ø as the possessive suffix.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Valence_Changing_Morphemes">Valence Changing Morphemes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wayana_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Valence Changing Morphemes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Valency_(linguistics)" title="Valency (linguistics)">Valency</a> refers to the number of participants that a verb takes. In Wayana, the subject of an <a href="/wiki/Intransitive_verb" title="Intransitive verb">intransitive</a> sentence (<i>S</i>) is distinguished from subject of a <a href="/wiki/Transitive_verb" title="Transitive verb">transitive</a> sentence (<i>A</i>) and the object of a transitive sentence (<i>O</i>). The valency on a <a href="/wiki/Verb_stem" class="mw-redirect" title="Verb stem">verb stem</a> can be decreased or increased by using certain affixes. Valence can be decreased by only one affix – the detransitivizing prefix – but can be increased by four different suffixes. Causational morphemes (such as the Wayana <i>–po</i>) are not considered as valence-increasing, since the participants they introduce are optional.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The detransitivizing prefix is realized as <i>e-, ëh-, ët</i>-, depending on the succeeding phonological environment. The prefix can be taken by a transitive verb stem to detransitivize it, so that only one argument can be taken; the verb is then "marked as an <i>SA</i> intransitive verb" (Tavares, 2005, pp.&#160;253), meaning that it only takes one argument, which is the subject and agent. For example, we see, how, in 6a. and b., a verb stem that usually requires another participant, can be satisfied by just one acting as an agent, in the presence of the detransitivizing prefix.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Syntax">Syntax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wayana_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Syntax">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Case_and_Agreement">Case and Agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wayana_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Case and Agreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wayana's case system presents "an … unprecedented type of <a href="/wiki/Split_ergativity" title="Split ergativity">split ergativ[ity]</a>" (Tavares, 2005, pp.&#160;412),<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> where there are two verb types – Set I and <i>t</i>-V-<i>(h)e.</i> The former presents an unclassified mixed system (sometimes analyzed as <a href="/wiki/Active%E2%80%93stative_language" title="Active–stative language">active-stative</a>, or inverse) while the latter presents <a href="/wiki/Ergative_case" title="Ergative case">ergative</a> case. In both verb sets, the subject of an intransitive sentence (<i>S</i>) is distinguished from subject of a transitive sentence (<i>A</i>) and the object of a transitive sentence (<i>O</i>), as mentioned previously. There is no grammatical property that seems to trigger the use of one set over the other. Both systems can "occur independent of tense, with all persons … in main clauses, and with all verb stems" (Tavares, 2005, pp.&#160;234),<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and the choice of verb set is perhaps dependent on discourse factors.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Set_I_Verbs">Set I Verbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wayana_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Set I Verbs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Set I verbs in Wayana take a variety of personal prefixes, marking first, second, dual (first and second), and third person. The prefix used differs based on the valency of the verb in question, and person(s) being marked.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For transitive verbs, one prefix marks both subject (<i>A</i>), and object (<i>O</i>). The different prefixes, based on which person <i>A</i> and <i>O</i> are marking, are below.

</p><p>When one of the arguments of the transitive verb is a speech act participant (SAP) - either first person, second person or dual person (both first and second person) - and the other argument is a third person, the personal prefix differs based on whether the speech act participant is acting on the third person (is <i>A</i>), or is being acted on by the third person (is <i>O</i>). Often, first situation is termed direct, and the latter inverse. We can see this demonstrated by the differing prefixes for 1A3O (direct) and 3A1O (inverse), as well as 2A3O (direct) and 3A2O (inverse).<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This distinction between direct and inverse prefixes is crucial when examining intransitive verbs. While each prefix marks the subject (<i>S</i>), as the chart below shows, there are two distinct sets of personal prefixes for each SAP person. One set matches the direct prefixes in transitive verbs, and the other set matches inverse prefixes. Thus, the <i>S</i> is split, and referred to either as <i>SA</i> or <i>SO</i>, depending on which transitive prefix set the intransitive prefix resembles. <i>SA</i> corresponds to the direct prefixes, and <i>SO</i>, the inverse. For example, 1SA prefix <i>"w-"</i> is the same as the 1A3O direct transitive prefix.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

</p><p>There have been a few analyses proposed to explain Wayana's person marking, including active-stative. If one focuses on the first and second person prefixes, that might be reasonable, as S <i>is</i> marked either as <i>A</i> or O, a characteristic of active-stative languages. Yet this analysis holds for first and second person only – the other persons (i.e. dual person) "do not present such a binary opposite" (Tavares, 2005, pp.&#160;208); as well, the "semantic basis is lacking" (Tavares, 2005, pp.&#160;208). Other analyses include: inverse (Gildea, 1998, as cited by Tavares) and  portmanteau prefixes (Hoff, 1995, as cited by Tavares). Instead, it is likely that the set I verbs in Wayana are of a mixed system that is not fully encompassed by one single analysis.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="t-V-.28h.29e_Verbs"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="t-V-(h)e_Verbs"><i>t</i>-V-<i>(h)e</i> Verbs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wayana_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: t-V-(h)e Verbs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Verbs in this set, characterized by the ambifix <i>t-</i> -<i>(h)</i>e around the verb root, are less complicated than set I verbs in regards to case. They are not inflected for person, tense, evidentiality, or number, and, as mentioned, present ergative case marking. The intransitive subject, <i>S</i>, and transitive object, <i>O</i> are both unmarked (having absolutive case), while the transitive object is marked for ergative case with the ergative morpheme, <i>ja</i>. Two examples follow:
</p>
<table class="wikitable">
<tbody><tr>
<td colspan="2">10. Malonme  ïwokan        epuu     tëpkëlei         pasina ja
<p>malonme   ï-woka-nu epulu-ø te-pïkëlë-he pahina ja
</p><p>then 1-fishhook-Pss pole-Pss  T-Break.O-He fish.sp Erg
</p><p>"Then, a pasina (fish) broke the pole of  my fishhook"
</p>
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>11. Tïkai ïu
<p>tï-ka-he    ïwu
</p><p>T-say-He 1Pro<a href="#_ftn3">[3]</a>
</p><p>"I said"
</p><p><small>(1Pro = First person pronoun)</small>
</p>
</td>
<td>12. Kopë telen tumëkhe
<p>kopë telenu t-umëkï-he
</p><p>rain    huge   T-come-He
</p><p>"A huge rain came"
</p>
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><small>Data from Tavares (2005), pp.&#160;231</small>
</p><p>In the transitive sentence, 10., the <i>A</i>, which is the pasina fish, is marked as such by the <i>ja</i> ergative morpheme. The <i>O</i>, the speaker's fishhook pole, is unmarked. In the intransitive sentences, 11. and 12., the <i>S</i> (the speaker, and "a huge rain", respectively) are completely unmarked, just as the <i>O</i> is in 10..
</p><p>Wayana thus shows split ergativity, with one set of verbs presenting ergative case, and the other, a mixed system. Wayana is a special case in split ergativity, since it seems that the two sets are not grammatically  or semantically triggered, and occur freely in discourse. For example, both 13. and 14. use the verb root ("<i>ënepï</i>"/"bring"), yet 13. is marked as a <i>t-</i> -<i>(h)</i>e verb, and 14. is marked as a set I.
</p>
<table class="wikitable">
<tbody><tr>
<td>13. Ulu   tënephe      Kilili ja
<p>ulu   t-ënepï-he   kilili ja
</p><p>manioc T-bring.O-he Kilili Erg
</p><p>"Kilili brought manioc"
</p>
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td>14. Menep
<p>m-enepï-ø
</p><p>2A3O-bring.O-RecPst
</p><p>"You brought it"
</p>
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><small>Data from Tavares (2005), pp.&#160;201, 202</small>
</p><p>Even another Cariban language, <a href="/wiki/Tiriy%C3%B3_language" title="Tiriyó language">Tiriyó</a>, with split ergativity (and similar person-marking), "restrict[s]" <i>t-V-(h)e</i> verbs (the Tiriyo cognate) to the remote past" (Tavares, 2005, pp.&#160;234), making Wayana's case system quite unique, even amongst Cariban languages.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Semantics">Semantics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wayana_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Semantics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tense">Tense</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wayana_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Tense">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wayana explicitly marks tense only on set I verbs, where tense morphemes are bound with aspect, modality, and number. Broadly, there are two categories of tenses – non-past, and past, which includes recent past, distant past, and habitual past; all tenses are marked with affixes. Aspect is not marked on Wayana verbs, but is "impl[ied] to different degrees" (Tavares, 2005, pp.&#160;211) in each tense, or else given by context or other morphemes. Below is a chart of the suffixes for each tense.
</p>

<p><small>Modified from Tavares (2005), pp.&#160;210</small>
</p><p>The non-past suffix, <i>-ja</i> can refer to future events, facts about the world, and non-past sentences can encode aspect, such as imperfective habitual or continuous aspect. In the examples below, we see how habitual aspect is marked through another morpheme (15.), how near or distant future is marked through context (saying "in a minute" versus "next year") (16a., 16b.). We also see in 17. that, since perfective aspect is not explicitly marked, the non-past can be understood as either imperfect and continuous ("I am making bread"), or perfect ("I will make bread"), based on the context of speech.
</p>

</p>

</p><p>"A man killed a big jaguar yesterday"
</p>
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><small>Data from Tavares (2005), pp.&#160;215</small>
</p><p>The distant past (referring to an event that occurred more than 24 hours before speech) is marked by two different affixes, <i>-ne</i> and <i>kun</i>-. Their distribution is based on the person of the verb's arguments. <i>–ne</i> occurs when one or both of the verb's arguments is an SAP, or when there is a free (pro)noun object before the verb in a 3A3O configuration. <i>Kun</i>- "occurs elsewhere" and marks third person as well as distant past. 20. gives an example of the distant past, using the –<i>ne</i> suffix (as there is a preverbal free noun <i>O</i>), and 21 gives an example of <i>kun-</i>.
</p>

</p><p>"Then, he saw it."

</p><p>All past affixes only "imply perfective meaning" (Tavares 2005, pp.&#160;212) on their own. However, two habitual suffixes can mark habitual aspect in the distant past. They are <i>-(j)(ë)mëhneja</i> and <i>-(h)e</i>. The former takes the same personal prefixes as non-past forms do, while the latter takes no personal prefixes, though they seem to be identical, semantically – marking a repeated event in the past.
</p><p>22. and 23. provide examples of <i>-(j)(ë)mëhneja</i> and -<i>(h)e</i>, respectively.
</p>

</p><p>While set I verbs mark tense, <i>t-V-(h)</i>e verbs do not have morphology to do so. They do, however, refer to events in the past, present, and future, with different aspects. Tavares does not make it clear in her grammar how those events are referred to, but likely it is through context, and other morphemes, such as the habitual morpheme <i>hepï</i> (from the data presented on Tavares 2005, pp.&#160;232).<sup id="cite_ref-:1_8-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
