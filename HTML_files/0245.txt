<p><b>Maroons</b> were <a href="/wiki/Africans" class="mw-redirect" title="Africans">Africans</a> and their <a href="/wiki/African_diaspora_in_the_Americas" title="African diaspora in the Americas">descendants in the Americas</a> who formed settlements away from <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">chattel slavery</a>. Some had escaped from <a href="/wiki/Plantation#Slavery" title="Plantation">plantations</a>, but others had always been free, like those born among them in freedom. They often mixed with <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">indigenous peoples</a>, thus creating distinctive <a href="/wiki/Creole_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Creole culture">creole cultures</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The American Spanish  word <i>cimarrón</i> is often given as the source of the English word <i>maroon</i> used to describe the runaway slave communities of Florida and of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Dismal_Swamp_maroons" title="Great Dismal Swamp maroons">Great Dismal Swamp</a> on the border of Virginia and North Carolina, on colonial islands of the Caribbean, and other parts of the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>. Lyle Campbell says the Spanish word <i>cimarrón</i> means "wild, unruly" or "runaway slave".<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell2000_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell2000-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The linguist Leo Spitzer, writing in the journal <i>Language</i>, says, "If there is a connection between Eng. <i>maroon</i>, Fr. <i>marron</i>, and Sp. <i>cimarrón</i>, Spain (or Spanish America) probably gave the word directly to England (or English America)."<sup id="cite_ref-Spitzer1938_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spitzer1938-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Cuban philologist José Juan Arrom has traced the origins of the word <i>maroon</i> further than the Spanish <i>cimarrón</i>, used first in Hispaniola to refer to feral cattle, then to enslaved Indians who escaped to the hills, and by the early 1530s to enslaved Africans who did the same. He proposes that the American Spanish word derives ultimately from the Arawakan root word <i>simarabo</i>, construed as "fugitive", in the <a href="/wiki/Arawakan_languages" title="Arawakan languages">Arawakan language</a> spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno" title="Taíno">Taíno</a> people native to the island.<sup id="cite_ref-Arrom1983_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arrom1983-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ArromArévalo1986_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ArromArévalo1986-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Price1996_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Price1996-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Arrom2000_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arrom2000-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Maroons_In_Ambush_On_The_Dromilly_Estate_In_The_Parish_Of_Trelawney,_Jamaica_in_1795.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/The_Maroons_In_Ambush_On_The_Dromilly_Estate_In_The_Parish_Of_Trelawney%2C_Jamaica_in_1795.jpg/220px-The_Maroons_In_Ambush_On_The_Dromilly_Estate_In_The_Parish_Of_Trelawney%2C_Jamaica_in_1795.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="168" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/The_Maroons_In_Ambush_On_The_Dromilly_Estate_In_The_Parish_Of_Trelawney%2C_Jamaica_in_1795.jpg/330px-The_Maroons_In_Ambush_On_The_Dromilly_Estate_In_The_Parish_Of_Trelawney%2C_Jamaica_in_1795.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/The_Maroons_In_Ambush_On_The_Dromilly_Estate_In_The_Parish_Of_Trelawney%2C_Jamaica_in_1795.jpg/440px-The_Maroons_In_Ambush_On_The_Dromilly_Estate_In_The_Parish_Of_Trelawney%2C_Jamaica_in_1795.jpg 2x" data-file-width="931" data-file-height="712" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Maroons_In_Ambush_On_The_Dromilly_Estate_In_The_Parish_Of_Trelawney,_Jamaica_in_1795.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1810 <a href="/wiki/Aquatint" title="Aquatint">aquatint</a> of a maroon raid on the Dromilly estate, Jamaica.</div></div></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>, as early as 1512, enslaved Africans escaped from Spanish captors and either joined indigenous peoples or eked out a living on their own.<sup id="cite_ref-Drake1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Drake1-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Sir <a href="/wiki/Francis_Drake" title="Francis Drake">Francis Drake</a> enlisted several <i>cimarrones</i> during his raids on the Spanish.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> As early as 1655, escaped Africans had formed their own communities in inland <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, and by the 18th century, <a href="/wiki/Nanny_Town" title="Nanny Town">Nanny Town</a> and other villages began to fight for independent recognition.<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When runaway Blacks and Amerindians banded together and subsisted independently they were called Maroons. On the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Caribbean islands">Caribbean islands</a>, they formed bands and on some islands, armed camps. Maroon communities faced great odds against their surviving attacks by hostile colonists,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> obtaining food for subsistence living,<sup id="cite_ref-Ohadike2002_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ohadike2002-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> as well as reproducing and increasing their numbers. As the planters took over more land for crops, the Maroons began to lose ground on the small islands. Only on some of the larger islands were organized Maroon communities able to thrive by growing crops and hunting. Here they grew in number as more Blacks escaped from <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantations</a> and joined their bands. Seeking to separate themselves from Whites, the Maroons gained in power and amid increasing hostilities, they raided and pillaged plantations and harassed planters until the planters began to fear a massive revolt of the enslaved Blacks.<sup id="cite_ref-brief_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brief-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The early Maroon communities were usually displaced. By 1700, Maroons had disappeared from the smaller islands. Survival was always difficult as the Maroons had to fight off attackers as well as attempt to grow food.<sup id="cite_ref-brief_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brief-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> One of the most influential Maroons was <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Mackandal" title="François Mackandal">François Mackandal</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Houngan" title="Houngan">houngan</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Vodou" title="Haitian Vodou">voodoo</a> priest, who led a six-year rebellion against the white plantation owners in <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> that preceded the <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Revolution" title="Haitian Revolution">Haitian Revolution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, there were maroon communities in the mountains, where African refugees who escaped the brutality of slavery and joined refugee <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Taíno people">Taínos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Aimes_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aimes-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Before roads were built into the mountains of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, heavy <a href="/wiki/Shrubland" title="Shrubland">brush</a> kept many escaped maroons hidden in the southwestern hills where many also intermarried with the natives. Escaped Blacks sought refuge away from the coastal plantations of <a href="/wiki/Ponce,_Puerto_Rico" title="Ponce, Puerto Rico">Ponce</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
Remnants of these communities remain to this day (2006) for example in <a href="/wiki/Vi%C3%B1ales" title="Viñales">Viñales</a>, Cuba,<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Adjuntas,_Puerto_Rico" title="Adjuntas, Puerto Rico">Adjuntas</a>, Puerto Rico.
</p><p>Maroon communities emerged in many places in the Caribbean (<a href="/wiki/Saint_Vincent_(island)" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Vincent (island)">St.&#160;Vincent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a>, for example), but none were seen as such a great threat to the British as the <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_Maroons" title="Jamaican Maroons">Jamaican Maroons</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> A British governor signed a treaty in 1739 and 1740 promising them 2,500 acres (1,012&#160;ha) in two locations, to bring an end to the warfare between the communities. In exchange they were to agree to capture other escaped Blacks. They were paid a bounty of two dollars for each African returned.<sup id="cite_ref-Colonies_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Colonies-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Beginning in the late 17th century, Jamaican Maroons fought British colonists to a draw and eventually signed treaties in the mid-18th century that effectively freed them a century before the <a href="/wiki/Slavery_Abolition_Act_1833" title="Slavery Abolition Act 1833">Slavery Abolition Act 1833</a>, which came into effect in 1838. To this day, the Jamaican Maroons are to a significant extent autonomous and separate from Jamaican society. The physical isolation used to their advantage by their ancestors has today led to their communities remaining among the most inaccessible on the island. In their largest town, <a href="/wiki/Accompong" title="Accompong">Accompong</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Elizabeth_Parish,_Jamaica" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Elizabeth Parish, Jamaica">parish of St.&#160;Elizabeth</a>, the Leeward Maroons still possess a vibrant community of about 600. Tours of the village are offered to foreigners and a large festival is put on every January 6 to commemorate the signing of the peace treaty with the British after the <a href="/wiki/First_Maroon_War" title="First Maroon War">First Maroon War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Campbell_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Campbell-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the plantation colony of <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>, which England ceded to the Netherlands in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Breda_(1667)" title="Treaty of Breda (1667)">Treaty of Breda</a>, escaped Blacks revolted and started to build their own villages from the end of the 17th century. As most of the plantations existed in the eastern part of the country, near the <a href="/wiki/Commewijne_River" title="Commewijne River">Commewijne River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marowijne_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Marowijne River">Marowijne River</a>, the <i>Marronage</i> (i.e.,&#160;running away) took place along the river borders and sometimes across the borders of <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>. By 1740 the Maroons had formed clans and felt strong enough to challenge the Dutch colonists, forcing them to sign peace treaties. On October 10, 1760, the Ndyuka signed such a treaty forged by Adyáko Benti Basiton of <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, a former enslaved African from Jamaica who had learned to read and write and knew about the Jamaican treaty. The treaty is still important, as it defines the territorial rights of the Maroons in the <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>-rich inlands of Suriname.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Maroon_flag_outside_St_John%E2%80%99s_Church,_Freetown.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Maroon_flag_outside_St_John%E2%80%99s_Church%2C_Freetown.jpg/220px-Maroon_flag_outside_St_John%E2%80%99s_Church%2C_Freetown.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Maroon_flag_outside_St_John%E2%80%99s_Church%2C_Freetown.jpg/330px-Maroon_flag_outside_St_John%E2%80%99s_Church%2C_Freetown.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Maroon_flag_outside_St_John%E2%80%99s_Church%2C_Freetown.jpg/440px-Maroon_flag_outside_St_John%E2%80%99s_Church%2C_Freetown.jpg 2x" data-file-width="693" data-file-height="693" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Maroon_flag_outside_St_John%E2%80%99s_Church,_Freetown.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Maroon flag in <a href="/wiki/Freetown" title="Freetown">Freetown</a>, Sierra Leone</div></div></div>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Afro-American_religion" title="Afro-American religion">Afro-American religion</a></div>
<p>Slaves escaped frequently within the first generation of their arrival from Africa and often preserved their <a href="/wiki/African_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="African languages">African languages</a> and much of their culture and <a href="/wiki/Traditional_African_religions" title="Traditional African religions">religion</a>. African traditions included such things as the use of medicinal herbs together with special drums and dances when the herbs are administered to a sick person. Other African healing traditions and rites have survived through the centuries.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Jungle" title="Jungle">jungles</a> around the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> offered food, shelter, and isolation for the escaped slaves. Maroons sustained themselves by growing vegetables and hunting. Their survival depended upon military abilities and culture of these communities, using guerrilla tactics and heavily fortified dwellings involving traps and diversions. Some defined leaving the community as desertion and therefore punishable by death.<sup id="cite_ref-Price_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Price-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> They also originally raided plantations. During these attacks, the maroons would burn crops, steal livestock and tools, kill slavemasters, and invite other slaves to join their communities. Individual groups of Maroons often allied themselves with the local <a href="/wiki/Amerindian" class="mw-redirect" title="Amerindian">indigenous tribes</a> and occasionally assimilated into these populations. Maroons played an important role in the histories of <a href="/wiki/History_of_Brazil" title="History of Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_Suriname" title="History of Suriname">Suriname</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_Puerto_Rico" title="History of Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_Haiti" title="History of Haiti">Haiti</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Dominican_Republic" title="History of the Dominican Republic">Dominican Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_Cuba" title="History of Cuba">Cuba</a>, and <a href="/wiki/History_of_Jamaica" title="History of Jamaica">Jamaica</a>.
</p><p>There is much variety among Maroon <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">cultural</a> groups because of differences in history, geography, African nationality, and the culture of indigenous people throughout the <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">Western Hemisphere</a>.
</p><p>Maroon settlements often possessed a clannish, outsider identity. They sometimes developed <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">Creole languages</a> by mixing European tongues with their original African languages. One such Maroon Creole language, in Suriname, is <a href="/wiki/Saramaccan_language" title="Saramaccan language">Saramaccan</a>. At other times, the Maroons would adopt variations of local European language (<a href="/wiki/Creolization" title="Creolization">Creolization</a>) as a common tongue, for members of the community frequently spoke a variety of mother tongues.<sup id="cite_ref-Price_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Price-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Maroons created their own independent communities, which in some cases have survived for centuries, and until recently remained separate from mainstream <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a>. In the 19th and 20th centuries, Maroon communities began to disappear as forests were razed, although some countries, such as <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> and Suriname, still have large Maroon populations living in the forests. Recently, many of them moved to cities and towns as the process of <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a> accelerates.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_Maroons">Types of Maroons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Types of Maroons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A typical maroon community in the early stage usually consists of three types of people.<sup id="cite_ref-Price_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Price-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most of them were slaves who ran away right after they got off the ships. They refused to accept enslavement and often tried to find ways to go back to Africa.
</p><p>The second group were slaves who had been working on plantations for a while. Those slaves were usually somewhat adjusted to the slave system but had been abused by the plantation owners - often with excessive brutality. Others ran away when they were being sold suddenly to a new owner.
</p><p>The last group of maroons were usually skilled slaves with particularly strong ideals against the slave system.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_colonial_governments">Relationship with colonial governments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Relationship with colonial governments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Maroonage was a constant threat to New World plantation societies.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  Punishments for recaptured maroons were severe, like removing the Achilles tendon, amputating a leg, castration, and being roasted to death.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Maroon communities had to be inaccessible and were located in inhospitable environments in order to be sustainable.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> For example, maroon communities were established in remote swamps in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_United_States" title="Southern United States">southern United States</a>; in deep canyons with sinkholes but little water or fertile soil in Jamaica; and in deep jungles of <a href="/wiki/The_Guianas" title="The Guianas">the Guianas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Maroon communities turned the severity of their environments to their advantage to hide and defend their communities.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  Disguised pathways, false trails, booby traps, underwater paths, quagmires and quicksand, and natural features were all used to conceal maroon villages.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Maroon men utilized exemplary guerrilla warfare skills to fight their European enemies. <a href="/wiki/Nanny_of_the_Maroons" title="Nanny of the Maroons">Nanny</a>, the famous Jamaican Maroon, developed guerrilla warfare tactics that are still used today by many militaries around the world.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  European troops used strict and established strategies while maroon men attacked and retracted quickly, used ambush tactics, and fought when and where they wanted to.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Even though colonial governments were in a perpetual state of hatred toward the maroon communities, individuals in the colonial system traded goods and services with maroons.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> They also traded with white settlers and Native American communities.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  Maroon communities played interest groups off of one another.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  At the same time, maroon communities were also used as pawns when colonial powers clashed.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Absolute secrecy and loyalty of members was crucial to the survival of maroon communities.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> To ensure this loyalty, maroon communities used severe methods to protect against desertion and spies.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  New members were brought to communities by way of detours so they couldn't find their way back and served probationary periods, often as slaves.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  Crimes such as desertion and adultery were punishable by death.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_24-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographical_distribution">Geographical distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geographical distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Maroon communities were formed among the <a href="/wiki/Afro-Asians_in_South_Asia" title="Afro-Asians in South Asia">Afro-Asians in South Asia</a> who resisted slavery.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> These communities of maroons still inhabit the South Asian countries.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Caribbean_islands">Caribbean islands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Caribbean islands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cuba">Cuba</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cuba">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, escaped slaves had joined refugee <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADnos" class="mw-redirect" title="Taínos">Taínos</a> in the mountains to form maroon communities.<sup id="cite_ref-Aimes_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aimes-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Remnants of these communities remain to this day (2006), for example in <a href="/wiki/Vi%C3%B1ales" title="Viñales">Viñales</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Dominica.2C_Saint_Lucia_and_St._Vincent"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Dominica,_Saint_Lucia_and_St._Vincent">Dominica, Saint Lucia and St. Vincent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Dominica, Saint Lucia and St. Vincent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Similar Maroon communities developed on islands across the Caribbean, such as the <a href="/wiki/Garifuna_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Garifuna people">Garifuna</a>. Many of the Garifuna were deported to the mainland, where some eventually settling along the Mosquito Coast or in Belize.
</p><p>Saint Lucia had a well trained Neg Mawon (Maroon) army (Armee Francaise dans les bois) comprising about 2500 men who surrendered to the British at Morne Fortune in 1796. They were deported to England that same year as prisoners of war and were held at Portchester Castle in Hampshire, South West England.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dominican_Republic">Dominican Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Dominican Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i>See <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Dominican_Republic" title="History of the Dominican Republic">History of the Dominican Republic</a>.</i>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Haiti">Haiti</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Haiti">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The French encountered many forms of slave resistance during the 17th and 18th centuries. The African slaves who fled to remote mountainous areas were called <i>marron</i> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>) or <i>mawon</i> (<a href="/wiki/Haitian_Creole" title="Haitian Creole">Haitian Creole</a>), meaning "escaped slave". The maroons formed close-knit communities that practised <a href="/wiki/Small-scale_agriculture" title="Small-scale agriculture">small-scale agriculture</a> and hunting. They were known to return to plantations to free family members and friends. On a few occasions they also joined the <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Taíno people">Taíno</a> settlements, who had escaped the Spanish in the 17th century. Certain maroon factions became so formidable that they made treaties with local colonial authorities, sometimes negotiating their own independence in exchange for helping to hunt down other escaped slaves. <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="https://login.avoserv2.library.fordham.edu/login?url=https://search.credoreference.com/content/entry/sharpeeman/maroons/0?institutionId=3205">[1]</a>.
</p><p>Other slave resistance efforts against the French plantation system were more direct. The maroon leader <a href="/wiki/Mackandal" class="mw-redirect" title="Mackandal">Mackandal</a> led a movement to poison the drinking water of the plantation owners in the 1750s. <a href="/wiki/Boukman" class="mw-redirect" title="Boukman">Boukman</a> declared war on the French plantation owners in 1791, sparking off the <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Revolution" title="Haitian Revolution">Haitian Revolution</a>. A statue called the <i><a href="/wiki/Le_N%C3%A8gre_Marron" class="mw-redirect" title="Le Nègre Marron">Le Nègre Marron</a></i> or the <i>Nèg Mawon</i> is an iconic bronze bust that was erected in the heart of <a href="/wiki/Port-au-Prince" title="Port-au-Prince">Port-au-Prince</a> to commemorate the role of maroons in Haitian independence.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Jamaica">Jamaica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Jamaica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_Maroons" title="Jamaican Maroons">Jamaican Maroons</a></div>
<p>Escaped slaves during the Spanish occupation of the island of Jamaica fled to the interior and joined the <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno" title="Taíno">Taíno</a> living there, forming refugee communities. Additional slaves gained freedom during the confusion surrounding the 1655 English <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Jamaica" title="Invasion of Jamaica">Invasion of Jamaica</a>. Refugee slaves continued to join them through the decades until the abolition of slavery in 1838. During the late 17th and 18th centuries, the British tried to capture them because they occasionally raided <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantations</a>, and made expansion into the interior more difficult. An increase in armed confrontations over decades led to the <a href="/wiki/First_Maroon_War" title="First Maroon War">First Maroon War</a> in the 1730s, but the British were unable to defeat the Maroons. They finally settled with the groups by treaty in 1739 and 1740, allowing them to have autonomy in their communities in exchange for agreeing to be called to military service with the colonists if needed.
</p><p>Due to tensions and repeated conflicts with Maroons from Trelawny Town, the <a href="/wiki/Second_Maroon_War" title="Second Maroon War">Second Maroon War</a> erupted in 1795. After the governor tricked the Trelawny Maroons into surrendering, the colonial government deported approximately 600 captive maroons to <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>. Due to their difficulties and those of <a href="/wiki/Black_Loyalists" class="mw-redirect" title="Black Loyalists">Black Loyalists</a> settled at Nova Scotia and England after the American Revolution, Great Britain established a colony in <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone" title="Sierra Leone">Sierra Leone</a> in West Africa. It offered ethnic Africans a chance to set up their own community there, beginning in 1792. Around 1800 a number of <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_Maroons_in_Sierra_Leone" title="Jamaican Maroons in Sierra Leone">Jamaican Maroons</a> were transported to <a href="/wiki/Freetown" title="Freetown">Freetown</a>, the first settlement of <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone" title="Sierra Leone">Sierra Leone</a>.
</p><p>The only Leeward Maroon settlement that retained formal autonomy on Jamaica after the Second Maroon War was <a href="/wiki/Accompong" title="Accompong">Accompong</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Elizabeth_Parish" title="Saint Elizabeth Parish">Saint Elizabeth Parish</a>, whose people had abided by their 1739 treaty with the British. A Windward Maroon community is also located at Charles Town, on <a href="/wiki/Buff_Bay_River" title="Buff Bay River">Buff Bay River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Portland_Parish" title="Portland Parish">Portland Parish</a>. Another is at <a href="/wiki/Moore_Town,_Jamaica" title="Moore Town, Jamaica">Moore Town</a> (formerly Nanny Town), also in the parish of Portland. In 2005 the music of the Moore Town Maroons was declared by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> as a 'Masterpiece of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity.'<sup id="cite_ref-moore_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moore-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> A fourth community is at Scott's Hall, also in the parish of Portland.<sup id="cite_ref-conf_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conf-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Accompong's autonomy was ratified by the government of Jamaica when the island gained independence in 1962.
</p><p>The government has tried to encourage survival of the other Maroon settlements. Since 2008, the Jamaican government and Maroon communities have held the Annual International Maroon Conference at rotating communities around the island. Maroons from other Caribbean, Central and South America nations are invited.<sup id="cite_ref-conf_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-conf-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In 2016 Accompong's colonel and a delegation traveled to the <a href="/wiki/Ashanti_Empire" title="Ashanti Empire">Kingdom of Ashanti</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a> to renew ties with the <a href="/wiki/Akan_people" title="Akan people">Akan</a> and Asante people of their ancestors.<sup id="cite_ref-ghana_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ghana-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Puerto_Rico">Puerto Rico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Puerto Rico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Taíno people">Taíno</a> families from neighboring Utuado moved into the southwestern mountain ranges, along with escaped African slaves who intermarried with them. DNA analysis of contemporary persons from this area shows maternal ancestry from the <a href="/wiki/Mandinka_people" title="Mandinka people">Mandinka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wolof_people" title="Wolof people">Wolof</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fulani" class="mw-redirect" title="Fulani">Fulani</a> peoples through the <a href="/wiki/MtDNA" class="mw-redirect" title="MtDNA">mtDNA</a> African <a href="/wiki/Haplotype" title="Haplotype">haplotype</a> associated with them, <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_L1_(mtDNA)" title="Haplogroup L1 (mtDNA)">L1b</a>, which is present here. This was carried by African slaves who escaped from plantations around Ponce and formed communities with the <a href="/wiki/Arawak" title="Arawak">Arawak</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno" title="Taíno">Taíno</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kalinago" class="mw-redirect" title="Kalinago">Kalinago</a>) in the mountains.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Arawak" title="Arawak">Arawak</a> lineages (<a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Taíno people">Taíno</a> people represented within <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_A_(mtDNA)" title="Haplogroup A (mtDNA)">haplogroups A</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kalinago" class="mw-redirect" title="Kalinago">Kalinago</a> people represented within <a href="/wiki/Haplogroup_C_(mtDNA)" title="Haplogroup C (mtDNA)">haplogroups C</a>) can also be found in this area.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_America">Central America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Central America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="Belize.2C_Guatemala.2C_Honduras.2C_and_Nicaragua"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Belize,_Guatemala,_Honduras,_and_Nicaragua">Belize, Guatemala, Honduras, and Nicaragua</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Belize, Guatemala, Honduras, and Nicaragua">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Several different maroon societies developed around the Gulf of Honduras. Some were found in the interior of modern-day <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a>, along the trade routes by which <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">silver</a> mined in the Pacific side of the <a href="/wiki/Isthmus" title="Isthmus">isthmus</a> was carried by slaves down to coastal towns such as Trujillo or <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Puerto Cortés">Puerto Caballos</a> to be shipped to Europe. When slaves escaped, they went to the mountains for safety. In 1648 the English bishop of Guatemala, Thomas Gage, reported active bands of maroons numbering in the hundreds along these routes.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Miskito_Sambu" title="Miskito Sambu">Miskito Sambu</a> were a maroon group who formed from slaves who revolted on a Portuguese ship around 1640, wrecking the vessel on the coast of Honduras-Nicaragua and escaping into the interior. They intermarried with the indigenous people over the next half-century. They eventually rose to leadership of the <a href="/wiki/Mosquito_Coast" title="Mosquito Coast">Mosquito Coast</a>, and led extensive slave raids against Spanish-held territories in the first half of the 18th century.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Garifuna_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Garifuna people">Garifuna</a> are descendants of maroon communities that developed on the island of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Vincent_and_the_Grenadines" title="Saint Vincent and the Grenadines">Saint Vincent</a>. They were deported to the coast of Honduras in 1797. From their original landing place in <a href="/wiki/Roatan_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Roatan Island">Roatan Island</a>, the maroons moved to Trujillo. Gradually groups migrated south into the Mosquito Kingdom and north into <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">Belize</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Panama">Panama</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Panama">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cimarron_people_(Panama)" title="Cimarron people (Panama)">Cimarron people (Panama)</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bayano" title="Bayano">Bayano</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Mandinka_people" title="Mandinka people">Mandinka</a> man who had been enslaved and taken to Panama in 1552, led a rebellion that year against the <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_New_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Viceroyalty of New Spain">Spanish</a> in Panama. He and his followers escaped to found villages in the lowlands. Later these people, known as <a href="/wiki/Cimarron_people_(Panama)" title="Cimarron people (Panama)">Cimarron</a>, assisted Sir <a href="/wiki/Francis_Drake" title="Francis Drake">Francis Drake</a> in fighting against the Spanish.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico">Mexico</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Mexico">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>See <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_Yanga" title="Gaspar Yanga">Gaspar Yanga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afro-Latin_American#Mexico" class="mw-redirect" title="Afro-Latin American">Afro-Latin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afro-Mexican#Palenques" class="mw-redirect" title="Afro-Mexican">Afro-Mexican</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Nova_Scotia">Nova Scotia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Nova Scotia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone_Creole_people#Maroons_and_other_transatlantic_immigrants" title="Sierra Leone Creole people">Sierra Leone Creole people §&#160;Maroons and other transatlantic immigrants</a></div>
<p>From 1796 to 1800, <a href="/wiki/Black_Nova_Scotians" title="Black Nova Scotians">550 maroons</a>, who had been deported from Jamaica after the Second Maroon War, lived in <a href="/wiki/Nova_Scotia" title="Nova Scotia">Nova Scotia</a>. In 1800, they were <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_Maroons_in_Sierra_Leone" title="Jamaican Maroons in Sierra Leone">sent to Sierra Leone</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Black_Indians" class="mw-redirect" title="Black Indians">Black Indians</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Florida">Florida</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Florida">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Black_Seminoles" title="Black Seminoles">Black Seminoles</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Black_Seminoles" title="Black Seminoles">Black Seminoles</a>, who allied with <a href="/wiki/Seminole_Indians" class="mw-redirect" title="Seminole Indians">Seminole Indians</a> in Florida, were one of the largest and most successful Maroon communities in the United States. Some intermarried and were culturally Seminole; others maintained a more African culture. Descendants of those who were removed with the Seminole to <a href="/wiki/Indian_Territory" title="Indian Territory">Indian Territory</a> in the 1830s are recognized as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Seminole_Freedmen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Seminole Freedmen (page does not exist)">Seminole Freedmen</a>. Many were formerly part of the <a href="/wiki/Seminole_Nation_of_Oklahoma" title="Seminole Nation of Oklahoma">Seminole Nation of Oklahoma</a>, but have been excluded since the late 20th century by new membership rules that require proving Native American descent from historic documents.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Louisiana">Louisiana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Louisiana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Until the mid-1760s, maroon colonies lined the shores of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Borgne" title="Lake Borgne">Lake Borgne</a>, just downriver of <a href="/wiki/New_Orleans" title="New Orleans">New Orleans</a>, Louisiana. These fugitive slaves controlled many of the <a href="/wiki/Canal" title="Canal">canals</a> and back-country passages from <a href="/wiki/Lake_Pontchartrain" title="Lake Pontchartrain">Lake Pontchartrain</a> to <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">the Gulf</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Rigolets" title="Rigolets">Rigolets</a>. These colonies were finally eradicated by militia from Spanish-controlled New Orleans led by <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Bouligny" title="Francisco Bouligny">Francisco Bouligny</a>. <a href="/wiki/Free_people_of_color" title="Free people of color">Free people of color</a> aided in the capture of these fugitives.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Carolina_and_Virginia">North Carolina and Virginia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: North Carolina and Virginia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Great_Dismal_Swamp_maroons" title="Great Dismal Swamp maroons">Great Dismal Swamp maroons</a></div>
<p>A large settlement of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Dismal_Swamp_maroons" title="Great Dismal Swamp maroons">Great Dismal Swamp maroons</a> lived in the marshlands of today's North Carolina and Virginia.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Quilombo" title="Quilombo">Quilombo</a></div>
<p>One of the best-known <i><a href="/wiki/Quilombo" title="Quilombo">quilombos</a></i> (maroon settlements) in <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> was <a href="/wiki/Palmares_(quilombo)" title="Palmares (quilombo)">Palmares</a> (the Palm Nation) which was founded in the early 17th century. At its height, it had a population of over 30,000 free people and was ruled by king <a href="/wiki/Zumbi" title="Zumbi">Zumbi</a>. Palmares maintained its independent existence for almost a hundred years until it was conquered by the Portuguese in 1694.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Colombia">Colombia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Colombia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Escaped slaves established independent communities along the remote Pacific coast, outside of the reach of the colonial administration. In Colombia the Caribbean coast still sees maroon communities like <a href="/wiki/San_Basilio_de_Palenque" title="San Basilio de Palenque">San Basilio de Palenque</a>, where the creole <a href="/wiki/Palenquero" title="Palenquero">Palenquero</a> language is spoken.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecuador">Ecuador</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Ecuador">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In addition to escaped slaves, survivors from <a href="/wiki/Shipwreck" title="Shipwreck">shipwrecks</a> formed independent communities along rivers of the northern coast and mingled with indigenous communities in areas beyond the reach of the colonial administration. Separate communities can be distinguished form the cantones Cojimies y Tababuela, <a href="/wiki/Esmeraldas_Canton" title="Esmeraldas Canton">Esmeraldas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limones,_Chiriqu%C3%AD" title="Limones, Chiriquí">Limones</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="French_Guiana_and_Suriname">French Guiana and Suriname</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroon_(people)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: French Guiana and Suriname">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_10024950_Portret_van_drie_Marron_mannen_en_een.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_10024950_Portret_van_drie_Marron_mannen_en_een.jpg/170px-Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_10024950_Portret_van_drie_Marron_mannen_en_een.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="237" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_10024950_Portret_van_drie_Marron_mannen_en_een.jpg/255px-Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_10024950_Portret_van_drie_Marron_mannen_en_een.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_10024950_Portret_van_drie_Marron_mannen_en_een.jpg/340px-Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_10024950_Portret_van_drie_Marron_mannen_en_een.jpg 2x" data-file-width="503" data-file-height="700" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_10024950_Portret_van_drie_Marron_mannen_en_een.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Maroon men in Suriname, picture taken between 1910–1935</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Suriname#Slavery_and_emancipation" title="History of Suriname">History of Suriname §&#160;Slavery and emancipation</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> (where Maroons account for about 15% of the population),<sup id="cite_ref-Kuipers2005_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kuipers2005-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> escaped slaves, or <a href="/wiki/Bushinengue" title="Bushinengue">Bushinengues</a>, fled to the interior and joined with indigenous peoples and created several independent tribes, among them the <a href="/wiki/Saramaka" title="Saramaka">Saramaka</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Paramakans" class="mw-redirect" title="Paramakans">Paramaka</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ndyuka_people" title="Ndyuka people">Ndyuka</a> (Aukan), the <a href="/wiki/Kwintis" class="mw-redirect" title="Kwintis">Kwinti</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Aluku" title="Aluku">Aluku (Boni)</a>, and the Matawai. By the 1980s the Bushinengues in Suriname had begun to fight for their land rights.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Between 1986 and 1991, the <a href="/wiki/Surinamese_Interior_War" title="Surinamese Interior War">Surinamese Interior War</a> was waged by the <a href="/wiki/Jungle_Commando" title="Jungle Commando">Jungle Commando</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_warfare" title="Guerrilla warfare">guerrilla</a> group fighting for the rights of the Maroon minority, against the military dictatorship of <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9si_Bouterse" title="Dési Bouterse">Dési Bouterse</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In 2005, following a ruling by the <a href="/wiki/Inter-American_Court_of_Human_Rights" title="Inter-American Court of Human Rights">Inter-American Court of Human Rights</a>, the Suriname government agreed to compensate survivors of the 1986 <a href="/wiki/Moiwana" title="Moiwana">Moiwana</a> village massacre, in which soldiers had slaughtered 39 unarmed Ndyuka people, mainly women and children.<sup id="cite_ref-Kuipers2005_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kuipers2005-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
