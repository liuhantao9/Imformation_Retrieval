	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Brownfield land</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">urban planning</a>, <b>brownfield land</b> is any previously developed land that is not currently in use, whether contaminated or not. The term is also used to describe <a href="/wiki/Real_property" title="Real property">land</a> previously used for <a href="/wiki/Industry" title="Industry">industrial</a> or commercial purposes with known or suspected <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> including <a href="/wiki/Soil_contamination" title="Soil contamination">soil contamination</a> due to <a href="/wiki/Hazardous_waste" title="Hazardous waste">hazardous waste</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Environment and Climate Change Canada defines brownfields as "abandoned, idle or underutilized commercial or industrial properties [typically located in urban areas] where past actions have caused environmental contamination, but which still have potential for redevelopment or other economic opportunities."<sup id="cite_ref-fcsp_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fcsp-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/US_EPA" class="mw-redirect" title="US EPA">US EPA</a> has defined brownfield not simply as a potential improvement site which has been previously improved, but also one that has potential impediments to improvement, such as "the presence or potential presence of a hazardous substance, pollutant, or contaminant".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  This comports well with an available general definition of the term, which scopes to "industrial or commercial property".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term <i>brownfields</i> first came into use on June 28, 1992, at a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">U.S. congressional</a> field hearing hosted by the Northeast Midwest Congressional Coalition. Also in 1992, the first detailed policy analysis of the issue was convened by the <a href="/wiki/Cuyahoga_County,_Ohio" title="Cuyahoga County, Ohio">Cuyahoga County</a> Planning Commission. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> selected Cuyahoga County as its first brownfield pilot project in September 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
The term applies more generally to previously used land or to sections of industrial or commercial facilities that are to be upgraded.<sup id="cite_ref-tandfonline5_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tandfonline5-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2002, <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">President George W. Bush</a> signed the Small Business Liability Relief and Brownfields Revitalization Act (the “Brownfields Law”) which provides grants and tools to local governments for the assessment, cleanup, and revitalization of brownfields. The motivation for this act was the success of the EPA’s brownfields program, which it started in the 1990s in response to several court cases that caused lenders to redline contaminated property for fear of liability under the Superfund. As of September 2015, the EPA estimates that the Brownfields program has resulted in 56,442 acres of land readied for reuse.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Mothballed brownfields</i> are properties that the owners are not willing to transfer or put to productive reuse.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (February 2017)">failed verification</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Brownfield status is a legal designation which places restrictions, conditions or incentives on redevelopment and use on the site.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United Kingdom, the term 'brownfield' has a colloquial meaning roughly equivalent to the American usage described above, i.e. vacant or derelict land or property, usually industrial in nature. In terms of <a href="/wiki/Town_and_country_planning_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Town and country planning in the United Kingdom">British Town and Country Planning</a>, however, the meaning of 'brownfield' is more complex, and is often conflated with the technical term 'previously developed land' (PDL). PDL was originally defined in planning policy for housing development in England and Wales, and was carefully distinguished in such policy from 'brownfield', which was undefined but considered to be different.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>26</span></sup> The definition from the 2012 National Planning Policy Guidance, which only applies to England, uses the terms 'brownfield' and 'previously developed land' interchangeably:<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><br />
"Land which is or was occupied by a permanent structure, including the curtilage of the developed land (although it should not be assumed that the whole of the curtilage should be developed) and any associated fixed surface infrastructure. This excludes: 
</p>
<ul><li>land that is or has been occupied by agricultural or forestry buildings;</li>
<li>land that has been developed for <a href="/wiki/Minerals_extraction" class="mw-redirect" title="Minerals extraction">minerals extraction</a> (<a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mining</a>) or waste disposal by landfill purposes where provision for restoration has been made through development control procedures;</li>
<li>land in built-up areas such as private residential gardens, parks, recreation grounds and allotments; and</li>
<li>land that was previously-developed but where the remains of the permanent structure or fixed surface structure have blended into the landscape in the process of time."</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Locations_and_contaminants">Locations and contaminants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Locations and contaminants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Generally, brownfield sites exist in a city's or town's industrial section, on locations with abandoned <a href="/wiki/Factory" title="Factory">factories</a> or commercial buildings, or other previously polluting operations like steel mills, refineries or landfills.<sup id="cite_ref-contamin_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-contamin-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
Small brownfields also may be found in older residential neighborhoods, as for example <a href="/wiki/Dry_cleaning" title="Dry cleaning">dry cleaning</a> establishments or <a href="/wiki/Gas_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Gas station">gas stations</a> produced high levels of subsurface contaminants.
</p><p>Typical contaminants found on contaminated brownfield land include <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> spillages, <a href="/wiki/Solvent" title="Solvent">solvents</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pesticide" title="Pesticide">pesticides</a>, heavy metals such as <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> (e.g., paints), <a href="/wiki/Tributyl_tin" class="mw-redirect" title="Tributyl tin">tributyl tins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">asbestos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-contamin_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-contamin-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  Old maps may assist in identifying areas to be tested.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brownfield_status_by_country">Brownfield status by country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Brownfield status by country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The primary issue facing all nations involved in attracting and sustaining new uses to brownfield sites is that industries are globally oriented and respond to global market forces.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This directly affects brownfield reuse, such as limiting the effective economic life of the use on the revitalized sites.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_2">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> estimates suggest there are over 500,000 brownfield sites contaminated at levels below the <a href="/wiki/Superfund" title="Superfund">Superfund</a> caliber (the most contaminated) in the country.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> While historic land use patterns created contaminated sites, the Superfund law has been criticized as creating the <i>brownfield phenomenon</i> where investment moves to greenfields for new development due to severe, no-fault liability schemes and other disincentives.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The Clinton-Gore administration and US <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">EPA</a> launched a series of brownfield policies and programs in 1993 to tackle this problem.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada_2">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> has an estimated 200,000 "contaminated sites" across the nation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> As of 2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> Canada had about 23,078 federally contaminated sites, from abandoned mines, to airports, lighthouse stations, and military bases, which are classified into N 1,2,or 3, depending on a score of contamination, with 5,300 active contaminated sites, 2,300 suspected sites and 15,000 listed as closed because remediated or no action was necessary.<sup id="cite_ref-inventory_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inventory-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The provincial governments have primary responsibility for brownfields.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The provinces´ legal mechanisms for managing risk are limited, as there are no tools such as "No Further Action" letters to give property owners finality and certainty in the cleanup and reuse process.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Yet, Canada has cleaned up sites and attracted investment to contaminated lands such as the <a href="/wiki/Moncton" title="Moncton">Moncton</a> rail yards.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> A strip of the Texaco lands in Mississauga is slated to be part of the <a href="/wiki/Waterfront_Trail" title="Waterfront Trail">Waterfront Trail</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> However, Imperial Oil has no plans to sell the 75-acre (30&#160;ha) property which has been vacant since the 1980s.
</p><p>According to their 2014 report on federally contaminated sites, the Parliamentary Budget Officer estimated that the "total liability for remediating Canada’s contaminated sites reported in the public accounts [was] $4.9 billion."<sup id="cite_ref-Canada_2014_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canada_2014-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1</span></sup> The report listed significant sites called the Big Five with a liability of $1.8 billion: <a href="/wiki/Faro,_Yukon" title="Faro, Yukon">Faro mine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colomac_Mine" title="Colomac Mine">Colomac Mine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giant_Mine" title="Giant Mine">Giant Mine</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Warning_System" title="North Warning System">Cape Dyer-DEW line</a> and <a href="/wiki/CFB_Goose_Bay" title="CFB Goose Bay">Goose Bay Air Base</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Port_Hope,_Ontario" title="Port Hope, Ontario">Port Hope, Ontario</a> site has a liability of $1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-Canada_2014_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canada_2014-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Port Hope has the largest volume of historic low-level <a href="/wiki/Radioactive" class="mw-redirect" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> wastes in Canada, resulting from "radium and uranium processing in Port Hope between 1933 and 1988 by the former Crown corporation <a href="/wiki/Eldorado_Mining_and_Refining_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="Eldorado Mining and Refining Limited">Eldorado Nuclear Limited</a> and its private sector predecessors.<sup id="cite_ref-Port_Hope_2015_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Port_Hope_2015-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> By 2010 it was projected that it would cost well over a billion dollars for the soil remediation project, it was the largest such cleanup in Canadian history. The effort is projected to be complete in 2022.<sup id="cite_ref-Port_Hope_cleanup_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Port_Hope_cleanup-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In July 2015 the $86,847,474 contract "to relocate the historic low-level radioactive waste and marginally contaminated soils from an existing waste management facility on the shoreline of Lake Ontario to the new, state-of-the-art facility about a kilometre north of the current site." was undertaken.<sup id="cite_ref-Port_Hope_2015_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Port_Hope_2015-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> There is also "$1.8 billion for general inventory sites" and "$200 million for other sites."<sup id="cite_ref-Canada_2014_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canada_2014-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>1</span></sup> The same report claimed the inventory currently lists 24,990 contaminated sites."
</p><p>The federal government exercises some control over environmental protection, the "provincial and territorial governments issue the bulk of legislation regarding
contaminated sites."<sup id="cite_ref-Canada_2014_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Canada_2014-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>4–5</span></sup> Under the Shared-Responsibility Contaminated Sites Policy Framework (2005), the government may provide funding for the remediation of nonfederal sites, if the contamination is related to federal government activities or national security. See Natural Resources Canada (2012)
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Denmark">Denmark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Denmark">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> lacks the large land base which creates the magnitude of brownfield issues facing countries such as Germany and the U.S., brownfield sites in areas critical to the local economies of Denmark’s cities require sophisticated solutions and careful interaction with affected communities. Examples include the cleanup and redevelopment of former and current ship building facilities along <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a>’s historic waterfront. Laws in Denmark require a higher degree of coordination of planning and reuse than is found in many other countries.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In France, brownfields are called "friches industrielles" and the Ministère de l'Écologie, du Développement Durable et de l'Énergie (MEDDE) maintains a database of polluted sites named BASOL, with "more than 4000 sites".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> of about 300 000 to 400 000 potentially polluted sites total (around 100 000 ha),<sup id="cite_ref-ademe_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ademe-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> in a historical inventory named BASIAS, maintained by the Agence de l'Environnement et de la Maitrise de l'Energie (ADEME).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> loses greenfields at a rate of about 1.2 square kilometres per day for settlement and transportation infrastructure. Each of the approximately 14,700 local municipalities is empowered to allocate lands for industrial and commercial use. Local control over reuse decisions of German brownfield sites ("Industriebrache") is a critical factor. Industrial sites tend to be remote due to zoning laws, and incur costly overhead for providing infrastructure such as utilities, disposal services and transportation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
In 1989, a brownfield of the Ruhrgebiet became <a href="/wiki/Internationale_Bauausstellung_Emscher_Park" title="Internationale Bauausstellung Emscher Park">Emscher Park</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom_2">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a> centuries of industrial use of lands which once formed the birthplace of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> have left entire regions in a brownfield status. New legislation in the UK may provide a needed incentive for brownfield cleanup and redevelopment. Cleanup laws are centered on the premise that the remediation should be "fit for the purpose".In 2018, the Campaign to Protect Rural England (CPRE) reported that the 17,656 sites (covering over 28,000 hectares of land) identified by local planning authorities at Brownfield Land Registers would provide enough land for a minimum of 1 million homes, which could rise to over 1.1 million once all registers are published. There is also brownfield capacity in areas in which the green belt is in danger, particularly in the Northwest, where local authorities have identified enough brownfield land to provide for 12 years of housing demand<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Redevelopment">Redevelopment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Redevelopment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Valuation_and_financing">Valuation and financing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Valuation and financing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Acquisition, adaptive re-use, and disposal of a brownfield site requires advanced and specialized <a href="/wiki/Real_estate_appraisal" title="Real estate appraisal">appraisal</a> analysis techniques.  For example, the <a href="/wiki/Highest_and_best_use" title="Highest and best use">highest and best use</a> of the brownfield site may be affected by the contamination, both before and after remediation.  Additionally, the value should take into account residual stigma and potential for third-party liability. Normal appraisal techniques frequently fail, and appraisers must rely on more advanced techniques, such as <a href="/wiki/Contingent_valuation" title="Contingent valuation">contingent valuation</a>, case studies, or statistical analyses.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (February 2017)">failed verification</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  A 2011 <a href="/wiki/University_of_Delaware" title="University of Delaware">University of Delaware</a> study has suggested a 17.5:1 return on dollars invested on brownfield redevelopment.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
A 2014 study of EPA brownfield cleanup grants from 2002 through 2008 found an average benefit value of almost $4 million per brownfield site (with a median of $2,117,982).<sup id="cite_ref-atl14_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atl14-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
To expedite the cleanup of brownfield sites in the US, some environmental firms have teamed up with insurance companies to underwrite the cleanup and provide a guaranteed cleanup cost to limit land developers' exposure to environmental remediation costs and pollution lawsuits. The environmental firm first performs an extensive investigation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Remediation_strategies">Remediation strategies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Remediation strategies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Innovative <a href="/wiki/Environmental_remediation" title="Environmental remediation">remediation</a> techniques used at distressed brownfields in recent years include <a href="/w/index.php?title=In_situ_thermal_remediation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="In situ thermal remediation (page does not exist)">in situ thermal remediation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bioremediation" title="Bioremediation">bioremediation</a> and <a href="/wiki/In_situ_oxidation" class="mw-redirect" title="In situ oxidation">in situ oxidation</a>. Often, these strategies are used in conjunction with each other or with other remedial strategies such as <a href="/wiki/Soil_vapor_extraction" title="Soil vapor extraction">soil vapor extraction</a>. In this process, vapor from the soil phase is extracted from soils and treated, which has the effect of removing contaminants from the soils and <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> beneath a site. Binders can be added to contaminated soil to prevent chemical leaching.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Some brownfields with heavy metal contamination have even been cleaned up through an innovative approach called <a href="/wiki/Phytoremediation" title="Phytoremediation">phytoremediation</a>, which uses deep-rooted plants to soak up metals in soils into the plant structure as the plant grows. After they reach maturity, the plants &#8211; which now contain the heavy metal contaminants in their tissues &#8211; are removed and disposed of as hazardous waste.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Research is under way to see if some brownfields can be used to grow crops, specifically for the production of <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">biofuels</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Michigan_State_University" title="Michigan State University">Michigan State University</a>, in collaboration with <a href="/wiki/DaimlerChrysler" class="mw-redirect" title="DaimlerChrysler">DaimlerChrysler</a> and NextEnergy, has small plots of <a href="/wiki/Soybean" title="Soybean">soybean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">corn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canola" class="mw-redirect" title="Canola">canola</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Switchgrass" class="mw-redirect" title="Switchgrass">switchgrass</a> growing in a former industrial dump site in <a href="/wiki/Oakland_County,_Michigan" title="Oakland County, Michigan">Oakland County, Michigan</a>. The intent is to see if the plants can serve two purposes simultaneously: assist with phytoremediation, and contribute to the economical production of <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Ethanol_fuel" title="Ethanol fuel">ethanol fuel</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The regeneration of brownfields in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and in other <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> countries has gained prominence due to <a href="/wiki/Greenfield_land" title="Greenfield land">greenfield land</a> restrictions as well as their potential to promote the <a href="/wiki/Urban_renaissance" title="Urban renaissance">urban renaissance</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tandfonline5_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tandfonline5-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Development of brownfield sites also presents an opportunity to reduce the environmental impact on communities, and considerable assessments need to take place in order to evaluate the size of this opportunity.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Many contaminated brownfield sites sit unused for decades because the cost of cleaning them to safe standards is more than the land would be worth after redevelopment. However, redevelopment has become more common in the first decade of the 21st century, as developable land has become less available in highly populated areas, and brownfields contribute to environmental stigma which can delay redevelopment.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>  Also, the methods of studying contaminated land have become more sophisticated and costly.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Some states and localities have spent considerable money assessing the <a href="/wiki/Contamination" title="Contamination">contamination</a> on local brownfield sites, to quantify the cleanup costs in an effort to move the redevelopment process forward. Therefore, federal and state programs have been developed to help developers interested in cleaning up brownfield sites and restoring them to practical uses.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In the process of cleaning contaminated brownfield sites, previously unknown <a href="/wiki/Underground_storage_tanks" class="mw-redirect" title="Underground storage tanks">underground storage tanks</a>, buried drums or buried railroad <a href="/wiki/Tank_car" title="Tank car">tank cars</a> containing wastes are sometimes encountered. Unexpected circumstances increase the cost for study and clean-up. As a result, the cleanup work may be delayed or stopped entirely. To avoid unexpected contamination and increased costs, many developers insist that a site be thoroughly investigated (via a <a href="/wiki/Phase_I_Environmental_Site_Assessment" class="mw-redirect" title="Phase I Environmental Site Assessment">Phase II Site Investigation</a> or Remedial Investigation) prior to commencing remedial cleanup activities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_and_residential">Commercial and residential</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Commercial and residential">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of 2006<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Station" title="Atlantic Station">Atlantic Station</a> project in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta" title="Atlanta">Atlanta</a>, was the largest brownfield redevelopment in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Dayton" class="mw-redirect" title="Dayton">Dayton</a>, like many other cities in the region, is developing <a href="/wiki/Tech_Town_(Dayton)" title="Tech Town (Dayton)">Tech Town</a> in order to attract technology-based firms to Dayton and revitalize the downtown area.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>In <a href="/wiki/Homestead,_Pennsylvania" title="Homestead, Pennsylvania">Homestead, Pennsylvania</a>, the site once occupied by <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Steel" class="mw-redirect" title="Carnegie Steel">Carnegie Steel</a> has been converted into a successful commercial center, <a href="/wiki/The_Waterfront" title="The Waterfront">The Waterfront</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania">Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania</a>, has successfully converted<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (February 2017)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> numerous former <a href="/wiki/Steel_mill" title="Steel mill">steel mill</a> sites into high-end residential, shopping, and offices. Examples of brownfield redevelopment in Pittsburgh include:
</p>
<ul><li>In Pittsburgh's <a href="/wiki/Squirrel_Hill" class="mw-redirect" title="Squirrel Hill">Squirrel Hill</a> neighborhood, a former <a href="/wiki/Slag" title="Slag">slag</a> dump for steel mills was turned into a $243 million residential development called <a href="/wiki/Summerset_at_Frick_Park" title="Summerset at Frick Park">Summerset at Frick Park</a>.</li>
<li>In Pittsburgh's <a href="/wiki/South_Side_(Pittsburgh)" title="South Side (Pittsburgh)">South Side</a> neighborhood, a former <a href="/wiki/LTV_Steel" class="mw-redirect" title="LTV Steel">LTV Steel</a> mill site was transformed into <a href="/wiki/Southside_Works" class="mw-redirect" title="Southside Works">Southside Works</a>, a mixed-use development that includes high-end entertainment, retail, offices, and housing.</li>
<li>In the <a href="/wiki/Hazelwood_(Pittsburgh)" title="Hazelwood (Pittsburgh)">Hazelwood (Pittsburgh)</a> neighborhood, a former <a href="/wiki/Jones_and_Laughlin" class="mw-redirect" title="Jones and Laughlin">Jones and Laughlin</a> steel mill site was transformed into a $104 million office park called <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh_Technology_Center" title="Pittsburgh Technology Center">Pittsburgh Technology Center</a>.</li>
<li>In Herr's Island, a 17-hectare (42-acre) island on the western bank of the <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_River" title="Allegheny River">Allegheny River</a>, a former rail stop for livestock and meatpacking was transformed into <a href="/wiki/Washington%27s_Landing" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington&#39;s Landing">Washington's Landing</a>, a waterfront center for commerce, manufacturing, recreation and upscale housing</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">Regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Brownfield_regulation_and_development" title="Brownfield regulation and development">Brownfield regulation and development</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_3">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United States, <a href="/wiki/Brownfield_regulation_and_development" title="Brownfield regulation and development">Brownfield regulation and development</a> is largely governed by state environmental agencies in cooperation with the <a href="/wiki/US_Environmental_Protection_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="US Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA). In 1995, the EPA launched the Brownfields Program, which was expanded in 2002 with the <a href="/wiki/Small_Business_Liability_Relief_and_Brownfields_Revitalization_Act" title="Small Business Liability Relief and Brownfields Revitalization Act">Brownfields Law</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-atl14_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atl14-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> 
The EPA, together with local and national government, can provide technical help and some funding for assessment and cleanup.<sup id="cite_ref-atl14_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atl14-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> From 2002 through 2013, the EPA awarded nearly 1,000 grants for clean up, for a total of almost $190 million. It can also provide <a href="/wiki/Tax_incentive" title="Tax incentive">tax incentives</a> for cleanup that is not paid for outright; specifically, cleanup costs are fully <a href="/wiki/Tax-deductible" class="mw-redirect" title="Tax-deductible">tax-deductible</a> in the year they are incurred.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Many of the most important provisions on liability relief are contained in state codes that can differ significantly from state to state.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom_3">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brownfield_land&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United Kingdom, regulation of contaminated land comes from Part IIA of the <a href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Act_1990" title="Environmental Protection Act 1990">Environmental Protection Act 1990</a>; responsibility falls on local authorities to create a "contaminated land register". For sites with dubious past and present uses the Local Planning Authority may ask for a desktop study, which is sometimes implemented as a condition in planning applications.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
