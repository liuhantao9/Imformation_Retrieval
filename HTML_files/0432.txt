<p>The <b>Feast of the Ascension of Jesus Christ</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> also called <b>Ascension Day</b>, <b>Ascension Thursday</b>, or sometimes <b>Holy Thursday</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-Forster_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forster-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Soane_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Soane-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> commemorates the Christian belief of the bodily <a href="/wiki/Ascension_of_Jesus" title="Ascension of Jesus">Ascension of Jesus</a> into heaven. It is one of the <a href="/wiki/Ecumenical" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecumenical">ecumenical</a> (i.e., universally celebrated) feasts of Christian churches, ranking with the feasts of the <a href="/wiki/Passion_(Christianity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Passion (Christianity)">Passion</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pentecost" title="Pentecost">Pentecost</a>. Ascension Day is traditionally celebrated on a Thursday, the fortieth day of <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a> (following the accounts given in <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.biblica.com/bible/?osis=nlt:Mark.16:19–16:19">Mark 16:19</a>, <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.biblica.com/bible/?osis=nlt:Luke.24:51–24:51">Luke 24:51</a> and 
<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.biblica.com/bible/?osis=nlt:Acts.1:2–1:2">Acts 1:2</a>), although some Christian denominations have moved the observance to the following Sunday.  In the Catholic Church in the United States, the day of observance varies by ecclesiastical province.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feast_of_the_Ascension&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The observance of this feast is of great antiquity. <a href="/wiki/Eusebius" title="Eusebius">Eusebius</a> seems to hint at the celebration of it in the 4th century.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> At the beginning of the 5th century, <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">St. Augustine</a> says that it is of <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Fathers" title="Apostolic Fathers">Apostolic</a> origin, and he speaks of it in a way that shows it was the universal observance of the Church long before his time. Frequent mention of it is made in the writings of <a href="/wiki/John_Chrysostom" title="John Chrysostom">St. John Chrysostom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gregory_of_Nyssa" title="Gregory of Nyssa">St. Gregory of Nyssa</a>, and in the <i><a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Constitutions" title="Apostolic Constitutions">Constitution of the Apostles</a></i>. The <i><a href="/wiki/Egeria_(pilgrim)" title="Egeria (pilgrim)">Pilgrimage of Aetheria</a></i> speaks of the vigil of this feast and of the feast itself, as they were kept in the church built over the grotto in <a href="/wiki/Bethlehem" title="Bethlehem">Bethlehem</a> in which Christ is traditionally regarded as having been born.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It may be that prior to the 5th century the event narrated in the Gospels was commemorated in conjunction with the feast of Easter or Pentecost. Some believe that the much-disputed forty-third decree of the <a href="/wiki/Synod_of_Elvira" title="Synod of Elvira">Synod of Elvira</a> (c. 300) condemning the practice of observing a feast on the fortieth day after Easter and neglecting to keep Pentecost on the fiftieth day, implies that the proper usage of the time was to commemorate the Ascension along with Pentecost. Representations of the <a href="/wiki/Sacred_mysteries#Christian_mysteries" title="Sacred mysteries">mystery</a> are found in <a href="/wiki/Diptych" title="Diptych">diptychs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fresco" title="Fresco">frescoes</a> dating as early as the 5th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Western">Western</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feast_of_the_Ascension&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Western">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Latin terms used for the feast, <i>ascensio</i> and, occasionally, <i>ascensa</i>, signify that Christ was raised up by his own powers, and it is from these terms that the holy day gets its name. In the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Communion</a>, "Holy Thursday" is listed as another name for Ascension Day.<sup id="cite_ref-Forster_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forster-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Soane_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Soane-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_Blake" title="William Blake">William Blake</a>'s poem "<a href="/wiki/Holy_Thursday_(Songs_of_Innocence)" title="Holy Thursday (Songs of Innocence)">Holy Thursday</a>" refers to Ascension Day; Thomas Pruen used the term to refer to Ascension Day in his <i>Illustration of the Liturgy of the Church of England</i>, published in 1820;<sup id="cite_ref-Pruen1820_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pruen1820-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Keene2000_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keene2000-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> however use of the term "Holy Thursday" to mean Ascension Day is rare,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and the term is more generally applied by most Christian denominations to <a href="/wiki/Maundy_Thursday" title="Maundy Thursday">Maundy Thursday</a> in <a href="/wiki/Holy_Week" title="Holy Week">Holy Week</a>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western Christianity</a>, the earliest possible date is April 30 (as in 1818 and 2285), the latest possible date is June 3 (as in 1943 and 2038). In <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a>, the Ascension of the Lord is a <a href="/wiki/Holy_Day_of_Obligation" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Day of Obligation">Holy Day of Obligation</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Communion</a>, Ascension Day is a <a href="/wiki/Principal_Feast" title="Principal Feast">Principal Feast</a>.
</p><p>The three days before Ascension Thursday are sometimes referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Rogation_days" title="Rogation days">Rogation days</a>, and the previous Sunday &#8212; the Sixth Sunday of Easter (or the Fifth Sunday <i>after</i> Easter) &#8212; as <a href="/wiki/Rogation_Sunday" class="mw-redirect" title="Rogation Sunday">Rogation Sunday</a>.
</p><p>Ascension has a <a href="/wiki/Vigil" title="Vigil">vigil</a> and, since the 15th century, an <a href="/wiki/Octave_(liturgical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Octave (liturgical)">octave</a>, which is set apart for a <a href="/wiki/Novena" title="Novena">novena</a> of preparation for Pentecost.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In traditional Methodist usage, <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Worship_for_Church_and_Home_(1965)" title="Book of Worship for Church and Home (1965)">The Book of Worship for Church and Home</a></i> (1965) provides the following <a href="/wiki/Collect" title="Collect">Collect</a> for Ascension Day, commonly called Holy Thursday:<sup id="cite_ref-BOW1964_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BOW1964-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Almighty God, whose blessed Son our Saviour Jesus Christ ascended far above all heavens, that he might fill all things: Mercifully give us faith to perceive that according to his promise he abideth with his Church on earth, even unto the end of the world; through the same thy Son Jesus Christ our Lord. <b>Amen.</b><sup id="cite_ref-BOW1964_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BOW1964-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> in a number of countries that do not observe the feast as a public holiday has obtained permission from the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Vatican</a> to move observance of the Feast of the Ascension from the traditional Thursday to the following Sunday, the Sunday before Pentecost. Similarly, <a href="/wiki/The_United_Methodist_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="The United Methodist Church">The United Methodist Church</a> allows the traditional celebration on Holy Thursday to be moved to Sunday.<sup id="cite_ref-Hickman2011_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hickman2011-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> This is in keeping with a trend to move <a href="/wiki/Holy_Day_of_Obligation" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Day of Obligation">Holy Days of Obligation</a> from weekdays to Sunday, to encourage more Christians to observe feasts considered important.<sup id="cite_ref-stcloud_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stcloud-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sun_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sun-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The decision to move a feast is made by the bishops of an <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_province" title="Ecclesiastical province">ecclesiastical province</a>, i.e. an <a href="/wiki/Archbishop" title="Archbishop">archbishop</a> and the neighbouring bishops. The switch to Sunday was made in 1992 by the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism_in_Australia" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism in Australia">church in Australia</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> before 1996 in parts of Europe;<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> in 1997 in <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism_in_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism in Ireland">Ireland</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> before 1998 <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism_in_Canada" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism in Canada">in Canada</a> and parts of the western United States;<sup id="cite_ref-stcloud_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stcloud-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> in many other parts <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism_in_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism in the United States">in the United States</a> from 1999;<sup id="cite_ref-stcloud_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stcloud-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism_in_England_and_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholicism in England and Wales">England and Wales</a> from 2007-2017, but in 2018 reinstated to <a href="/wiki/Thursday" title="Thursday">Thursday</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The U.S. ecclesiastical provinces which retain Thursday observance in 2009 are <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Boston" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Hartford" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Hartford">Hartford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_New_York" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of New York">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Newark" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Newark">Newark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Omaha" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Omaha">Omaha</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Philadelphia" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> When celebrated on Sunday, the earliest possible date is May 3, and the latest is June 6.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_and_Oriental_Orthodox">Eastern and Oriental Orthodox</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feast_of_the_Ascension&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eastern and Oriental Orthodox">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Church">Eastern Church</a> this feast is known in Greek as <i>Analepsis</i>, the "taking up", and also as the <i>Episozomene</i>, the "salvation from on high", denoting that by ascending into his glory Christ completed the work of our <a href="/wiki/Redemption_(theology)" title="Redemption (theology)">redemption</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Ascension is one of the Twelve <a href="/wiki/Great_Feasts" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Feasts">Great Feasts</a> of the Orthodox <a href="/wiki/Liturgical_year" title="Liturgical year">liturgical year</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ascension2007-04.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Ascension2007-04.jpg/200px-Ascension2007-04.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Ascension2007-04.jpg/300px-Ascension2007-04.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b8/Ascension2007-04.jpg 2x" data-file-width="384" data-file-height="512" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ascension2007-04.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Liturgy during the Feast of the Ascension in a Mumbai <a href="/wiki/Syriac_Orthodox_Church_in_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Syriac Orthodox Church in India">Syrian Orthodox Church</a></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Celebration">Celebration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feast_of_the_Ascension&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Celebration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The feast is always observed with an <a href="/wiki/All-night_vigil" title="All-night vigil">All-night vigil</a>. The day before is the <a href="/wiki/Afterfeast" title="Afterfeast">Apodosis</a> (leave-taking) of Easter (i.e., the last day of the Feast of Easter). Before the Vigil, the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paschal_hours&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Paschal hours (page does not exist)">Paschal hours</a> are said for the last time and the <a href="/wiki/Paschal_greeting" title="Paschal greeting">Paschal greeting</a> is exchanged.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The <i>Paroemia</i> (<a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> readings) at <a href="/wiki/Vespers" title="Vespers">Vespers</a> on the eve of the Feast are <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Isaiah#2:2" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Isaiah">Isaiah 2:2–3</a>; <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Isaiah#62:10" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Isaiah">Isaiah 62:10–63:3</a>, <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Isaiah#63:7" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Isaiah">63:7–9</a>; and <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Zechariah#14:1" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Zechariah">Zechariah 14:1–4</a>, <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Zechariah#14:8" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Zechariah">14:8–11</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> A <a href="/wiki/Lity_(Orthodox_Vespers)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lity (Orthodox Vespers)">Lity</a> is celebrated. The <a href="/wiki/Troparion" title="Troparion">troparion</a> of the day is sung, which says:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><div class="poem">
<p>O Christ God, You have ascended in Glory,<br />
Granting joy to Your disciples by the promise of the Holy Spirit.<br />
Through the blessing they were assured<br />
That You are the Son of God,<br />
The Redeemer of the world!
</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Polyeleos" title="Polyeleos">Polyeleos</a> at <a href="/wiki/Matins" title="Matins">Matins</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Epitaphios_(liturgical)" title="Epitaphios (liturgical)">Epitaphios</a>, which was placed on the altar on <a href="/wiki/Holy_Saturday" title="Holy Saturday">Holy Saturday</a> (either at Matins or the <a href="/wiki/Midnight_Office" class="mw-redirect" title="Midnight Office">Midnight Office</a>, depending on local custom) is taken from the altar and carried in procession around the church. It is then put in the place reserved for it. The Gospel is <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Mark#16:9" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Mark">Mark 16:9–20</a>. The kontakion is sung, which announces:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><div class="poem">
<p>When You did fulfill the dispensation for our sake,<br />
And unite earth to Heaven:<br />
You did ascend in glory, O Christ our God,<br />
Not being parted from those who love You,<br />
But remaining with them and crying:<br />
I am with you and no one will be against you.
</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Megalynarion" title="Megalynarion">megalynarion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Irmos" title="Irmos">irmos</a> from Ode IX of the Canon (also sung at liturgy) is:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><div class="poem">
<p>Magnify, O my soul, Christ the Giver of Life,<br />
Who has ascended from earth to heaven!<br />
We magnify you, the Mother of God,<br />
Who beyond reason and understanding<br />
gave birth in time to the Timeless One.
</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<p>At the <a href="/wiki/Divine_Liturgy" title="Divine Liturgy">Divine Liturgy</a>, special antiphons are sung in place of Psalms 102 and 145 and the Beatitudes. The <a href="/wiki/Epistle" title="Epistle">Epistle</a> is <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Acts#1:1" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Acts">Acts 1:1–12</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Gospel" title="Gospel">Gospel</a> is <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Luke#24:36" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Luke">Luke 24:36–53</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Observance">Observance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feast_of_the_Ascension&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Observance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ascension Thursday also commemorates the Holy <a href="/wiki/Georgian_Orthodox_Church" title="Georgian Orthodox Church">Georgian</a> <a href="/wiki/Martyr" title="Martyr">Martyrs</a> of Persia (17th–18th centuries).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Ascension has an <a href="/wiki/Afterfeast" title="Afterfeast">Afterfeast</a> of eight days. The Sunday after Ascension is the Sunday of the <a href="/wiki/Church_fathers" class="mw-redirect" title="Church fathers">Holy Fathers</a> of the <a href="/wiki/First_Ecumenical_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="First Ecumenical Council">First Ecumenical Council</a> at <a href="/wiki/Nicaea" title="Nicaea">Nicaea</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This council formulated the <a href="/wiki/Nicene_Creed" title="Nicene Creed">Nicene Creed</a> up to the words, "He (Jesus) ascended into heaven, and sits at the right hand of the Father; and shall come again, with glory, to judge the living and the dead; Whose kingdom shall have no end."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Inline_citation#When_you_must_use_inline_citations" title="Wikipedia:Inline citation"><span title="The text near this tag needs a citation. (May 2019)">This quote needs a citation</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The Afterfeast ends on the following Friday, the Friday before Pentecost. The next day is appropriately a <a href="/wiki/All_Souls_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="All Souls Day">Saturday of the Dead</a> (general commemoration of all faithful departed).
</p><p>The Eastern Orthodox Church uses a different method of <a href="/wiki/Computus" title="Computus">calculating</a> the <a href="/wiki/Date_of_Easter" class="mw-redirect" title="Date of Easter">date of Easter</a>, so the Eastern Orthodox commemoration of Ascension will usually be after the western observance (either one week, or four weeks, or five weeks later; but occasionally on the same day). The earliest possible date for the feast is May 13 (of the western calendar), and the latest possible date is June 16. Some of the Oriental Orthodox Churches, however, observe Ascension on the same date as the Western Churches.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Feast_of_the_Ascension&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Church_cantata#Ascension" title="Church cantata">feast has been associated</a> with specific hymns and other church music. The oldest hymn in German related to the feast is the <a href="/wiki/Leise" title="Leise">Leise</a> "<a href="/wiki/Christ_fuhr_gen_Himmel" title="Christ fuhr gen Himmel">Christ fuhr gen Himmel</a>", first published in 1480. <a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Johann Sebastian Bach</a> composed several <a href="/wiki/Bach_cantata" title="Bach cantata">cantatas</a> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Ascension_Oratorio" class="mw-redirect" title="Ascension Oratorio">Ascension Oratorio</a></i> to be performed in church services on the feast day. He first performed <i><a href="/wiki/Wer_da_gl%C3%A4ubet_und_getauft_wird,_BWV_37" title="Wer da gläubet und getauft wird, BWV 37">Wer da gläubet und getauft wird, BWV 37</a></i>, on 18 May 1724, <i><a href="/wiki/Auf_Christi_Himmelfahrt_allein,_BWV_128" title="Auf Christi Himmelfahrt allein, BWV 128">Auf Christi Himmelfahrt allein, BWV 128</a></i>, on 10 May 1725, <i><a href="/wiki/Gott_f%C3%A4hret_auf_mit_Jauchzen,_BWV_43" title="Gott fähret auf mit Jauchzen, BWV 43">Gott fähret auf mit Jauchzen, BWV 43</a></i>, on 30 May 1726 and the oratorio, <i><a href="/wiki/Lobet_Gott_in_seinen_Reichen,_BWV_11" title="Lobet Gott in seinen Reichen, BWV 11">Lobet Gott in seinen Reichen, BWV 11</a></i>, on 19 May 1735.
</p>
