	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Haydn Wood</b> (25 March 1882 &#8211; 11 March 1959) was a 20th-century <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Composer" title="Composer">composer</a> and concert <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">violinist</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haydn_Wood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Haydn Wood was born in the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a> town of <a href="/wiki/Slaithwaite" title="Slaithwaite">Slaithwaite</a> on 25 March 1882. When he was three years old his family moved to the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>, an island which was often a source of inspiration for the composer. His two older brothers were also musicians: Harry (1868-1939) was a violinist, composer and conductor known as "Manxland's King of Music",<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> while Daniel S Wood (1872-1927) was principal flautist with the <a href="/wiki/London_Symphony_Orchestra" title="London Symphony Orchestra">London Symphony Orchestra</a> from 1910, taught flute at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Music" title="Royal Academy of Music">Royal Academy of Music</a> and composed practice pieces that are still in use today.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1897, at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Music" title="Royal College of Music">Royal College of Music</a>, Haydn Wood studied violin with <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Fern%C3%A1ndez_Arb%C3%B3s" title="Enrique Fernández Arbós">Enrique Fernández Arbós</a> and composition with <a href="/wiki/Charles_Villiers_Stanford" title="Charles Villiers Stanford">Charles Villiers Stanford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In 1901, he was soloist at a special concert commemorating the opening of the Royal College of Music's Concert Hall. The concert was attended by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Joachim" title="Joseph Joachim">Joseph Joachim</a> (who had heard him play before) and <a href="/wiki/Pablo_de_Sarasate" title="Pablo de Sarasate">Pablo de Sarasate</a>. They were so impressed that they sent him to <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> for study with the renowned teacher <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Thomson" title="César Thomson">César Thomson</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Farnon_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farnon-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  He then embarked on a world tour, accompanying the Canadian soprano Dame <a href="/wiki/Emma_Albani" title="Emma Albani">Emma Albani</a> and they continued their association for a further eight years.<sup id="cite_ref-Farnon_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farnon-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1913 he toured extensively with the soprano Dorothy Court whom he married in 1909. During this period he wrote popular songs and contributed to musical comedy productions: for example, he contributed seven numbers to the musical play <i>Tina</i>, which opened at the Royal <a href="/wiki/Adelphi_Theatre" title="Adelphi Theatre">Adelphi Theatre</a> in London on 2 November 1915, and wrote all the music for <i>Cash on Delivery</i> at the Palace Theatre in 1917. In 1916, Haydn Wood composed his most popular song, "<a href="/wiki/Roses_of_Picardy" title="Roses of Picardy">Roses of Picardy</a>" for Dorothy,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> reportedly selling 50,000 sheet music copies per month and earning a six figure royalty sum <sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The song <i>Love's Garden of Roses</i> was written in 1915, but didn't become well known until popularised by <a href="/wiki/John_McCormack_(tenor)" title="John McCormack (tenor)">John McCormack</a>'s recording, one of the biggest hits of 1918.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Another big success came in 1923 with <i>A Brown Bird Singing</i>. By 1926 he was able to support himself as a full time composer for the first time. From 1939, he served as a director of the <a href="/wiki/Performing_Right_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="Performing Right Society">Performing Right Society</a>. On the occasion of his 70th birthday he was given a full concert dedicated to his music by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>. He died in a London nursing home on 11 March 1959, two weeks before his 77th birthday.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Haydn_Wood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Alongside his 200 or so individual songs and seven song cycles, Haydn Wood was a prolific composer of orchestral music, including 15 suites, nine rhapsodies, eight overtures, three concertante pieces and nearly 50 other works scored for a variety of forces.
</p><p>His orchestral pieces were primarily of the "<a href="/wiki/Light_music" title="Light music">light music</a>" style; a well known piece of his is the three-movement <i>Fantasy-Concerto</i>. Another is his <i>London Landmarks Suite</i>, particularly "Horse Guards, Whitehall", which was used for many years as the signature tune for the BBC Radio Series <a href="/wiki/Down_Your_Way" title="Down Your Way">Down Your Way</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In 2018 the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Concert_Orchestra" title="BBC Concert Orchestra">BBC Concert Orchestra</a> issued a new recording of the <i>Snapshots of London Suite</i> (1948) and premiere recordings of five other suites: <i>Egypta</i> (1929), <i>Three Famous Cinema Stars</i> (1929), <i>Cities of Romance</i> (1937), <i>Manx Countryside Sketches</i> (1943), and <i>Royal Castles</i> (1952).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The short orchestral work <i>Soliloquy</i> of 1946 is a miniature of more serious intent.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first of Haydn Wood's more extended concert pieces to gain attention was the <i>Phantasie Quartet</i> which won second prize in the first <a href="/wiki/Walter_Willson_Cobbett" title="Walter Willson Cobbett">W W Cobbett</a> chamber music competition of 1905 <sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The Piano Concerto was performed at a Patron’s Fund Concert at <a href="/wiki/Queen%E2%80%99s_Hall" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen’s Hall">Queen’s Hall</a> on 14 July 1909, with soloist Ellen Edwards and Stanford conducting.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It received its Proms premiere in 1915, also at Queen's Hall.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> His Violin Concerto of 1928 was first performed in March 1933, with soloist <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Brosa" title="Antonio Brosa">Antonio Brosa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Wood also wrote a Symphony (1908 - score now lost),<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and the <i>Philharmonic Variations</i> for cello and orchestra (1939).<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Isle of Man and folk tunes from the island inspired Wood's music, resulting in the compositions <i>Manx Rhapsody, Rhapsody Mylecharaine, Manx Countryside Sketches, Manx Overture,</i> and <i>Mannin Veen</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Tone_poem" class="mw-redirect" title="Tone poem">tone poem</a> <i lang="gv" title="Manx language text">Mannin Veen</i> (pub. 1933, <a href="/wiki/Manx_language" title="Manx language">Manx</a> for "Dear <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>") was based on four Manx folk tunes, and is one of two works written originally for <a href="/wiki/Wind_band" class="mw-redirect" title="Wind band">wind band</a> by Wood. The work is also occasionally performed in its orchestral version. Choral pieces include the short cantatas for chorus and orchestra <i>Lochinvar</i> (1912) and <i>Ode to Genius</i> (1940).
</p>
