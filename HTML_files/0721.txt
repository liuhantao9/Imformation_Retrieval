<p><b>Laurence Stephen Lowry</b> <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_British_Artists" title="Royal Society of British Artists">RBA</a> <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Arts" title="Royal Academy of Arts">RA</a></span> (/'laʊri/; <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">LAO</span>-ree</i></a>; 1 November 1887&#160;– 23 February 1976) was an English artist. Many of his drawings and paintings depict <a href="/wiki/Pendlebury" title="Pendlebury">Pendlebury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>, where he lived and worked for more than 40 years, and also <a href="/wiki/County_Borough_of_Salford" title="County Borough of Salford">Salford</a> and its surrounding areas.
</p><p>Lowry is famous for painting scenes of life in the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_district" title="Industrial district">industrial districts</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_West_England" title="North West England">North West England</a> in the mid-20th century. He developed a distinctive style of painting and is best known for his urban landscapes peopled with human figures often referred to as "matchstick men". He painted mysterious unpopulated landscapes, brooding portraits and the unpublished "marionette" works, which were only found after his death.
</p><p>Due to his use of stylized figures and the lack of weather effects in many of his landscapes he is sometimes characterized as a <a href="/wiki/Na%C3%AFve_art" title="Naïve art">naïve</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> "Sunday painter", although this is not the view of the galleries that have organised retrospectives of his works.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A large collection of Lowry's work is on permanent public display in <a href="/wiki/The_Lowry" title="The Lowry">The Lowry</a>, a purpose-built art gallery on <a href="/wiki/Salford_Quays" title="Salford Quays">Salford Quays</a>, named in his honour. Lowry rejected five <a href="/wiki/Orders,_decorations,_and_medals_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Orders, decorations, and medals of the United Kingdom">honours</a> during his life, including a <a href="/wiki/Knight_Bachelor" title="Knight Bachelor">knighthood</a> in 1968, and consequently holds the record for the most rejected British honours.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> On 26 June 2013 a major retrospective opened at the <a href="/wiki/Tate_Britain" title="Tate Britain">Tate Britain</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, his first at the Tate, and in 2014 his first solo exhibition outside the UK was held in <a href="/wiki/Nanjing" title="Nanjing">Nanjing</a>, China.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._S._Lowry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:117_Station_Road,_Pendlebury.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/117_Station_Road%2C_Pendlebury.jpg/220px-117_Station_Road%2C_Pendlebury.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="300" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/117_Station_Road%2C_Pendlebury.jpg/330px-117_Station_Road%2C_Pendlebury.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/117_Station_Road%2C_Pendlebury.jpg/440px-117_Station_Road%2C_Pendlebury.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1731" data-file-height="2364" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:117_Station_Road,_Pendlebury.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lowry's former home, 117 Station Road, <a href="/wiki/Pendlebury" title="Pendlebury">Pendlebury</a></div></div></div>
<p>Lowry was born on 1 November 1887 at 8 Barrett Street <a href="/wiki/Stretford" title="Stretford">Stretford</a>, which was then in <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It was a difficult birth, and his mother Elizabeth, who hoped for a girl, was uncomfortable even looking at him at first. Later she expressed envy of her sister Mary, who had "three splendid daughters" instead of one "clumsy boy". Lowry's father Robert, who was of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Irish" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Irish">northern Irish</a> descent,<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_1976_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_1976-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> worked as a clerk for the Jacob Earnshaw and Son Property Company and was a withdrawn and introverted man. Lowry once described him as "a cold fish" and "(the sort of man who) realised he had a life to live and did his best to get through it."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After Lowry's birth, his mother's health was too poor for her to continue teaching. She is reported to have been talented and respected, with aspirations of becoming a <a href="/wiki/Concert_pianist" class="mw-redirect" title="Concert pianist">concert pianist</a>. She was an irritable, nervous woman brought up to expect high standards by her stern father. Like him, she was controlling and intolerant of failure. She used illness as a means of securing the attention and obedience of her mild and affectionate husband and she dominated her son in the same way. Lowry maintained that he had an unhappy childhood, growing up in a repressive family atmosphere. Although his mother demonstrated no appreciation of her son's gifts as an artist, a number of books Lowry received as Christmas presents from his parents are inscribed to <i>"Our dearest Laurie"</i>. At school he made few friends and showed no academic aptitude. His father was affectionate towards him but was, by all accounts, a quiet man who was at his most comfortable fading into the background as an unobtrusive presence.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Much of Lowry's early years were spent in the leafy Manchester suburb of <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Park,_Manchester" title="Victoria Park, Manchester">Victoria Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rusholme" title="Rusholme">Rusholme</a>, but in 1909, when he was 22, due to financial pressures, the family moved to 117 Station Road in the industrial town of <a href="/wiki/Pendlebury" title="Pendlebury">Pendlebury</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Here the landscape comprised textile mills and factory chimneys rather than trees. Lowry later recalled: "At first I detested it, and then, after years I got pretty interested in it, then obsessed by it&#160;... One day I missed a train from Pendlebury – [a place] I had ignored for seven years&#160;– and as I left the station I saw the Acme Spinning Company's mill&#160;... The huge black framework of rows of yellow-lit windows standing up against the sad, damp charged afternoon sky. The mill was turning out&#160;... I watched this scene&#160;— which I'd looked at many times without seeing&#160;— with rapture&#160;..."<sup id="cite_ref-The_Lowry_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Lowry-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._S._Lowry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Peel_Building_University_of_Salford.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Peel_Building_University_of_Salford.jpg/220px-Peel_Building_University_of_Salford.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="232" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Peel_Building_University_of_Salford.jpg/330px-Peel_Building_University_of_Salford.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Peel_Building_University_of_Salford.jpg/440px-Peel_Building_University_of_Salford.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2292" data-file-height="2420" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Peel_Building_University_of_Salford.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Peel Building, where Lowry studied at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Salford" title="University of Salford">Royal Technical College, Salford</a>. It overlooks <a href="/wiki/Peel_Park,_Salford" title="Peel Park, Salford">Peel Park</a>, the subject of a number of his paintings. His pencil drawing "A View from the window of the Royal Technical College, Salford" (1924) was drawn from the balconied window on the upper floor.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>After leaving school, Lowry began a career working for the Pall Mall Company, later collecting rents. He would spend some time in his lunch hour at <a href="/wiki/Buile_Hill_Park" title="Buile Hill Park">Buile Hill Park</a><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and in the evenings took private art lessons in antique and freehand drawing. In 1905, he secured a place at the <a href="/wiki/Manchester_School_of_Art" title="Manchester School of Art">Manchester School of Art</a>, where he studied under the French <a href="/wiki/Impressionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Impressionist">Impressionist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Adolphe_Valette" title="Pierre Adolphe Valette">Pierre Adolphe Valette</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Lowry was full of praise for Valette as a teacher, remarking "I cannot over-estimate the effect on me of the coming into this drab city of Adolphe Valette, full of French impressionists, aware of everything that was going on in Paris".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In 1915 he moved on to the Royal Technical Institute, Salford (later to become the Royal Technical College, Salford and now the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Salford" title="University of Salford">University of Salford</a>) where his studies continued until 1925. There he developed an interest in industrial landscapes and began to establish his own style.<sup id="cite_ref-McLean_1978_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLean_1978-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lowry's oil paintings were originally impressionistic and dark in tone but D.&#160;B. Taylor of the <i><a href="/wiki/Manchester_Guardian" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchester Guardian">Manchester Guardian</a></i> took an interest in his work and encouraged him to move away from the sombre palette he was using. Taking this advice on board, Lowry began to use a white background to lighten the pictures.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_1976_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian_1976-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He developed a distinctive style of painting and is best known for his urban landscapes peopled with human figures, often referred to as "matchstick men". He also painted mysterious unpopulated landscapes, brooding portraits and the unpublished "marionette" works, which were only found after his death.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_of_his_parents">Death of his parents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._S._Lowry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Death of his parents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His father died in 1932, leaving debts. His mother, subject to neurosis and depression, became bedridden and dependent on her son for care. Lowry painted after his mother had fallen asleep, between 10pm and 2am, or, depending how tired he was, he might stay up for another hour adding features. Many paintings produced during this period were damning self-portraits (often referred to as the <i>"Horrible Heads"</i> series), which demonstrate the influence of <a href="/wiki/Expressionism" title="Expressionism">expressionism</a> and may have been inspired by an exhibition of <a href="/wiki/Vincent_van_Gogh" title="Vincent van Gogh">Vincent van Gogh</a>'s work at <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Art_Gallery" title="Manchester Art Gallery">Manchester Art Gallery</a> in 1931. He expressed regret that he received little recognition as an artist until the year his mother died (1939) and that she was not able to enjoy his success. From the mid-1930s until at least 1939, Lowry took annual holidays at <a href="/wiki/Berwick-upon-Tweed" title="Berwick-upon-Tweed">Berwick-upon-Tweed</a>. After the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">war</a> Lowry served as a volunteer fire watcher and became an official <a href="/wiki/War_artist" title="War artist">war artist</a> in 1943. In 1953, he was appointed Official Artist at the <a href="/wiki/Coronation_of_Queen_Elizabeth_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Coronation of Queen Elizabeth II">Coronation of Queen Elizabeth II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-M.E.N._20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.E.N.-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> 
After his mother's death in October 1939, he became depressed and over time, neglected the upkeep of his house to such a degree that the landlord repossessed it in 1948. He was not short of money and bought "The Elms" in <a href="/wiki/Mottram_in_Longdendale" title="Mottram in Longdendale">Mottram in Longdendale</a> then in <a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a>. The area was much more rural but Lowry professed to dislike both the house and the area:<sup id="cite_ref-Lowry&#39;s_City_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lowry&#39;s_City-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>They're nice folk, I've nothing against them, it's the place never could take to it. I can't explain it. I've often wondered...It does nothing for me. I know there's plenty to paint here but I haven't the slightest desire to work locally. I've done one painting of the local agricultural show. Was commissioned to paint the parish church but had to give it up, I couldn't do it.<sup id="cite_ref-Lowry&#39;s_City_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lowry&#39;s_City-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Although he considered the house ugly and uncomfortable, it was spacious enough to both set up his studio in the dining room, and to accommodate the collection of china and clocks that he had inherited from his mother; he stayed there until his death almost 30 years later.<sup id="cite_ref-Mottram_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mottram-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._S._Lowry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In later years, Lowry spent holidays at the Seaburn Hotel in <a href="/wiki/Sunderland" title="Sunderland">Sunderland</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=County_Tyne_and_Wear&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="County Tyne and Wear (page does not exist)">County Tyne and Wear</a>, painting scenes of the beach and nearby ports and coal mines.<sup id="cite_ref-McLean_1978_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLean_1978-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> When he had no sketchbook, Lowry drew scenes in pencil or <a href="/wiki/Charcoal" title="Charcoal">charcoal</a> on the back of envelopes, serviettes and cloakroom tickets and presented them to young people sitting with their families. Such serendipitous pieces are now worth thousands of pounds; a serviette sketch can be seen at the Sunderland Marriott Hotel (formerly the Seaburn Hotel).<sup id="cite_ref-Halley_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Halley-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>He was a secretive and mischievous man who enjoyed stories irrespective of their truth.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> His friends observed that his anecdotes were more notable for humour than accuracy and in many cases he set out deliberately to deceive. His stories about the fictional Ann were inconsistent and he invented other people as frameworks on which to hang his tales. The collection of clocks in his living room were all set at different times: to some people he said that this was because he did not want to know the real time; to others he claimed that it was to save him from being deafened by their simultaneous chimes.<sup id="cite_ref-Halley_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Halley-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The owner of an art gallery in Manchester who visited him at his home, The Elms, noted that while his armchair was sagging and the carpet frayed, Lowry was surrounded by items such as his beloved <a href="/wiki/Dante_Gabriel_Rossetti" title="Dante Gabriel Rossetti">Rossetti</a> drawing, <i>Proserpine</i>, as well as a <a href="/wiki/Lucian_Freud" title="Lucian Freud">Lucian Freud</a> drawing located between two <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Tompion" title="Thomas Tompion">Tompion</a> clocks.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lowry had many long-lasting friendships, including the Salford artist <a href="/wiki/Harold_Riley_(artist)" title="Harold Riley (artist)">Harold Riley</a>, and made new friends throughout his adult life. He bought works from young artists he admired, such as <a href="/wiki/James_Lawrence_Isherwood" title="James Lawrence Isherwood">James Lawrence Isherwood</a>, whose <i>Woman with Black Cat</i> hung on his studio wall.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> He maintained ongoing friendships with some of these artists. He befriended the 23-year-old <a href="/wiki/Cumberland" title="Cumberland">Cumberland</a> artist <a href="/wiki/Sheila_Fell" title="Sheila Fell">Sheila Fell</a> in November 1955, describing her as "the finest landscape artist of the mid-20th century".<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> He supported her career by buying several pictures that he gave to museums. Fell later described him as "A great humanist. To be a humanist, one has first to love human beings, and to be a great humanist, one has to be slightly detached from them." As he never married this affected his influence, but he did have several lady friends. At the age of 88 he said that he had "never had a woman".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although seen as a mostly solitary and private person, Lowry enjoyed attending football matches and was an ardent <a href="/wiki/Manchester_City_F.C._supporters" title="Manchester City F.C. supporters">supporter</a> of <a href="/wiki/Manchester_City_F.C." title="Manchester City F.C.">Manchester City</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._S._Lowry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lowry retired from the Pall Mall Property Company in 1952 on his 65th birthday.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> In 1957 an unrelated 13-year-old schoolgirl called Carol Ann Lowry wrote to him at her mother's urging to ask his advice on becoming an artist. He visited her home in <a href="/wiki/Heywood,_Greater_Manchester" title="Heywood, Greater Manchester">Heywood</a> and befriended the family. His friendship with Carol Ann Lowry lasted for the rest of his life.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> BBC Radio 4 broadcast in 2001 a dramatisation by Glyn Hughes of Lowry's relationship with Carol Ann.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1960s Lowry shared exhibitions in Salford with Warrington-born artist Reginald Waywell D.F.A.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lowry joked about retiring from the art world, citing his lack of interest in the changing landscape. Instead, he began to focus on groups of figures and odd imaginary characters. Unknown to his friends and the public, Lowry produced a series of erotic works that were not seen until after his death. The paintings depict the mysterious <i>"<a href="/wiki/Portrait_of_Ann" title="Portrait of Ann">Ann</a>"</i> figure, who appears in portraits and sketches produced throughout his lifetime, enduring sexually charged and humiliating tortures. When these works were exhibited at the Art Council's Centenary exhibition at the <a href="/wiki/Barbican_Centre" title="Barbican Centre">Barbican</a> in 1988, art critic Richard Dorment wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i> that these works "reveal a sexual anxiety which is never so much as hinted at in the work of the previous 60 years." The group of erotic works, which are sometimes referred to as "the mannequin sketches" or "marionette works", are kept at the Lowry Centre and are available for visitors to see on request. Some are also brought up into the public display area on a rotation system. Manchester author <a href="/wiki/Howard_Jacobson" title="Howard Jacobson">Howard Jacobson</a> has argued that the images are just part of Lowry's melancholy and tortured view of the world and that they would change the public perception of the complexity of his work if they were more widely seen.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_legacy">Death and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._S._Lowry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:LowryCentre.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/LowryCentre.jpg/220px-LowryCentre.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/LowryCentre.jpg/330px-LowryCentre.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/LowryCentre.jpg/440px-LowryCentre.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="698" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:LowryCentre.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Entrance to the Lowry Centre on Salford Quays</div></div></div>
<p>Lowry died of <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> at the Woods Hospital in <a href="/wiki/Glossop" title="Glossop">Glossop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a>, on 23 February 1976, aged 88. He was buried in the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Cemetery,_Manchester" title="Southern Cemetery, Manchester">Southern Cemetery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, next to his parents. He left an estate valued at £298,459, and a considerable number of artworks by himself and others to Carol Ann Lowry, who, in 2001, obtained trademark protection of the artist's signature.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lowry left a cultural legacy, his works often sold for millions of pounds and inspired other artists. <a href="/wiki/The_Lowry" title="The Lowry">The Lowry</a> art gallery in <a href="/wiki/Salford_Quays" title="Salford Quays">Salford Quays</a> was opened in 2000 at a cost of £106&#160;million; named after him, the 2,000-square-metre (22,000&#160;sq&#160;ft) gallery houses 55 of his paintings and 278 drawings&#160;– the world's largest collection of his work&#160;– with up to 100 on display.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In January 2005, a statue of him was unveiled in Mottram in Longdendale<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> 100 yards away from his home from 1948 until his death in 1976. The statue has been a target for vandals since it was unveiled.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> In 2006 the <a href="/wiki/Lowry_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Lowry Centre">Lowry Centre</a> in Salford hosted a contemporary dance performance inspired by the works of Lowry.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>To mark the centenary of his birth in 1987, Royston Futter, director of the L.&#160;S. Lowry Centenary Festival, on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Salford" title="City of Salford">City of Salford</a> and the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> commissioned the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ballet_Theatre" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Ballet Theatre">Northern Ballet Theatre</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gillian_Lynne" title="Gillian Lynne">Gillian Lynne</a> to create a dance drama in his honour. <i>A Simple Man</i> was choreographed and directed by Lynne, with music by <a href="/wiki/Carl_Davis" title="Carl Davis">Carl Davis</a> and starred <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Gable" title="Christopher Gable">Christopher Gable</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moira_Shearer" title="Moira Shearer">Moira Shearer</a> (in her last dance role). It was broadcast on BBC, for which it won a <a href="/wiki/BAFTA" class="mw-redirect" title="BAFTA">BAFTA</a> award as the best arts programme in 1988, and also performed live on stage in November 1987.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Further performances were held in London at <a href="/wiki/Sadler%27s_Wells" class="mw-redirect" title="Sadler&#39;s Wells">Sadler's Wells</a> in 1988,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> and again in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2011 a bronze statue of Lowry was installed in the basement of his favourite pub, Sam's Chop House.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 26 June 2013 a retrospective opened at the <a href="/wiki/Tate_Britain" title="Tate Britain">Tate Britain</a> in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, his first there, scheduled to run until 20 October.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> In 2014 his first solo exhibition outside the UK was held in <a href="/wiki/Nanjing" title="Nanjing">Nanjing</a>, China.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honours">Awards and honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._S._Lowry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Awards and honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:L.S._Lowry_01.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/L.S._Lowry_01.JPG/220px-L.S._Lowry_01.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/L.S._Lowry_01.JPG/330px-L.S._Lowry_01.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/L.S._Lowry_01.JPG/440px-L.S._Lowry_01.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="1125" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:L.S._Lowry_01.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>L. S. Lowry memorial at Mottram in Longdendale</div></div></div>
<p>Lowry was awarded an honorary <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">Master of Arts</a> degree by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Manchester" title="University of Manchester">University of Manchester</a> in 1945, and <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Letters" title="Doctor of Letters">Doctor of Letters</a> in 1961. In April 1955 Lowry was elected as an Associate Member of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Arts" title="Royal Academy of Arts">Royal Academy of Arts</a> and in April 1962 became a full <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academician" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Academician">Royal Academician</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-List_of_Royal_Academicians_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-List_of_Royal_Academicians-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> At the end of December of the same year his membership status evolved to that of Senior Academician having reached the age of 75.<sup id="cite_ref-List_of_Royal_Academicians_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-List_of_Royal_Academicians-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> He was given the freedom of the city of Salford in 1965.<sup id="cite_ref-M.E.N._20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.E.N.-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1975 he was awarded two honorary Doctor of Letters degrees by the Universities of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Salford" title="University of Salford">Salford</a> and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Liverpool" title="University of Liverpool">Liverpool</a>. In 1964, the art world celebrated his 77th birthday with an exhibition of his work and that of 25 contemporary artists who had submitted tributes at Monk's Hall Museum, <a href="/wiki/Eccles,_Greater_Manchester" title="Eccles, Greater Manchester">Eccles</a>. <a href="/wiki/The_Hall%C3%A9" title="The Hallé">The Hallé</a> orchestra performed a concert in his honour and Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Harold_Wilson" title="Harold Wilson">Harold Wilson</a> used Lowry's painting <i>The Pond</i> as his official Christmas card. Lowry's painting <i>Coming Out of School</i> was depicted on a postage stamp of highest denomination in a series issued by the Post Office depicting great British artists in 1968. <sup id="cite_ref-M.E.N._20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M.E.N.-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> 
Lowry twice declined appointment to the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">Order of the British Empire</a>: as an Officer (OBE) in 1955, and as a Commander (CBE) in 1961.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The reason for this is unclear. (May 2014)">why?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-BBC_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> He turned down a <a href="/wiki/Orders,_decorations,_and_medals_of_the_United_Kingdom#Knighthood" title="Orders, decorations, and medals of the United Kingdom">knighthood</a> in 1968, and appointments to the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Companions_of_Honour" title="Order of the Companions of Honour">Order of the Companions of Honour</a> (CH) in 1972 and 1976.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> He holds the record for the most honours declined.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_53-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quotations">Quotations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._S._Lowry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Quotations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Going_to_Work_-_L_S_Lowry.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Going_to_Work_-_L_S_Lowry.png/220px-Going_to_Work_-_L_S_Lowry.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Going_to_Work_-_L_S_Lowry.png/330px-Going_to_Work_-_L_S_Lowry.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Going_to_Work_-_L_S_Lowry.png/440px-Going_to_Work_-_L_S_Lowry.png 2x" data-file-width="963" data-file-height="720" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Going_to_Work_-_L_S_Lowry.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Going to Work</i> (1943), commissioned by the <a href="/wiki/War_Artists%27_Advisory_Committee" title="War Artists&#39; Advisory Committee">War Artists' Advisory Committee</a></div></div></div>
<ul><li>On the industrial landscape
<ul><li>"We went to Pendlebury in 1909 from a residential side of Manchester, and we didn't like it. My father wanted to go to get near a friend for business reasons. We lived next door, and for a long time my mother never got to like it, and at first I disliked it, and then after about a year or so I got used to it, and then I got absorbed in it, then I got infatuated with it. Then I began to wonder if anyone had ever done it. Seriously, not one or two, but seriously; and it seemed to me by that time that it was a very fine industrial subject matter. And I couldn't see anybody at that time who had done it – and nobody had done it, it seemed."<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>"Most of my land and townscape is composite. Made up; part real and part imaginary&#160;... bits and pieces of my home locality. I don't even know I'm putting them in. They just crop up on their own, like things do in dreams."<sup id="cite_ref-FT_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FT-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li>On his style
<ul><li>"I wanted to paint myself into what absorbed me&#160;... Natural figures would have broken the spell of it, so I made my figures half unreal. Some critics have said that I turned my figures into puppets, as if my aim were to hint at the hard economic necessities that drove them. To say the truth, I was not thinking very much about the people. I did not care for them in the way a social reformer does. They are part of a private beauty that haunted me. I loved them and the houses in the same way: as part of a vision.</li>
<li>"I am a simple man, and I use simple materials: <a href="/wiki/Bone_char" title="Bone char">ivory black</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vermilion" title="Vermilion">vermilion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prussian_blue" title="Prussian blue">prussian blue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yellow_ochre" class="mw-redirect" title="Yellow ochre">yellow ochre</a>, <a href="/wiki/White_lead" title="White lead">flake white</a> and no medium. That's all I've ever used in my paintings. I like oils&#160;... I like a medium you can work into over a period of time."<sup id="cite_ref-Artlyst_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Artlyst-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li>On painting his "Seascapes"
<ul><li>"It's the battle of life – the turbulence of the sea&#160;... I have been fond of the sea all my life, how wonderful it is, yet how terrible it is. But I often think&#160;... what if it suddenly changed its mind and didn't turn the tide? And came straight on? If it didn't stay and came on and on and on and on&#160;... That would be the end of it all."<sup id="cite_ref-Yan_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yan-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li>On art
<ul><li>"You don't need brains to be a painter, just feelings."<sup id="cite_ref-The_Lowry_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Lowry-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>"I am not an artist. I am a man who paints."<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>"If people call me a Sunday painter, I'm a Sunday painter who paints every day the week."<sup id="cite_ref-Hamilton_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hamilton-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Works">Works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._S._Lowry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dwelling,_Ordsall_Lane,_Salford_(by_LS_Lowry).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/90/Dwelling%2C_Ordsall_Lane%2C_Salford_%28by_LS_Lowry%29.jpg/220px-Dwelling%2C_Ordsall_Lane%2C_Salford_%28by_LS_Lowry%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="174" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/90/Dwelling%2C_Ordsall_Lane%2C_Salford_%28by_LS_Lowry%29.jpg/330px-Dwelling%2C_Ordsall_Lane%2C_Salford_%28by_LS_Lowry%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/9/90/Dwelling%2C_Ordsall_Lane%2C_Salford_%28by_LS_Lowry%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="355" data-file-height="281" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dwelling,_Ordsall_Lane,_Salford_(by_LS_Lowry).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>"Oldfield Road Dwellings, Salford"</i>, 1927, oil on wood, 43.2&#160;cm ×&#160;53.3&#160;cm (17.0&#160;in ×&#160;21.0&#160;in), <a href="/wiki/Tate_Gallery" class="mw-redirect" title="Tate Gallery">Tate Gallery</a></div></div></div>
<p>Lowry's work is held in many public and private collections. The largest collection is held by <a href="/wiki/Salford_City_Council" title="Salford City Council">Salford City Council</a> and displayed at The Lowry. Its collection has about 400 works.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> X-ray analyses have revealed hidden figures under his drawings – the "Ann" figures. <i>Going to the Match</i> is owned by the Professional Footballers' Association (PFA) and is displayed at The Lowry along with a preparatory pencil sketch.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Tate_Gallery" class="mw-redirect" title="Tate Gallery">Tate Gallery</a> in London owns 23 works. The City of Southampton owns <i>The Floating Bridge</i>, <i>The Canal Bridge</i> and <i>An Industrial Town</i>. His work is featured at <a href="/wiki/MOMA" class="mw-redirect" title="MOMA">MOMA</a>, in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Christchurch_Art_Gallery_Te_Puna_o_Waiwhetu" class="mw-redirect" title="Christchurch Art Gallery Te Puna o Waiwhetu">Christchurch Art Gallery Te Puna o Waiwhetu</a> in <a href="/wiki/Christchurch" title="Christchurch">Christchurch</a>, New Zealand has <i>Factory at Widnes</i> (1956) in its collection. The painting was one of the gallery's most important acquisitions of the 1950s and remains the highlight of its collection of modern British art.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the early days of his career Lowry was a member of the Manchester Group of Lancashire artists, exhibiting with them at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Margo_Ingham&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Margo Ingham (page does not exist)">Margo Ingham</a>'s Mid-Day Studios in Manchester.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> He made a small painting of the Mid-Day Studios which is in the collection of the Manchester City Art Gallery.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During his life Lowry made about 1,000 paintings and over 8,000 drawings.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stolen_Lowry_works">Stolen Lowry works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._S._Lowry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Stolen Lowry works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Five Lowry art works were stolen from the Grove Fine Art Gallery in <a href="/wiki/Cheadle_Hulme" title="Cheadle Hulme">Cheadle Hulme</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stockport" title="Stockport">Stockport</a> on 2 May 2007. The most valuable were <i>The Viaduct</i>, estimated value of £700,000 and <i>The Tanker Entering the Tyne</i>, which is valued at over £500,000. <i>The Surgery</i>, <i>The Bridge at Ringley</i> and <i>The Street Market</i> were also stolen.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> The paintings were later found in a house in <a href="/wiki/Halewood" title="Halewood">Halewood</a> near <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recently_attributed_works">Recently attributed works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._S._Lowry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Recently attributed works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In July 2015 three works - <i>Lady with Dogs</i>, <i>Darby and Joan</i> and <i>Crowd Scene</i> - featured in the <a href="/wiki/BBC_One" title="BBC One">BBC One</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/Fake_or_Fortune%3F" title="Fake or Fortune?">Fake or Fortune?</a></i>. The programme enlisted the help of various experts to determine whether the paintings were genuine or forgeries. The works in question had been bought in the 1960s by a <a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a> businessman, Gerald Ames, but their <a href="/wiki/Provenance" title="Provenance">provenance</a> was poor, and it was noted that Lowry was "probably the most faked British artist, his deceptively simple style of painting making him a soft target for forgers". All three works were judged to be genuine by a panel of experts, and the total value of the paintings was estimated to be in excess of £200,000.
The programme also revealed that whilst Lowry often claimed to use only five specific colors (lead white, ivory black, vermillion, Prussian blue, and yellow ochre), he also used titanium white and zinc white. This use of other paints became helpful when determining that the above works were genuine.<sup id="cite_ref-Fake_or_Fortune_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fake_or_Fortune-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Art_market">Art market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._S._Lowry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Art market">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In March 2014 fifteen of Lowry's works, from the A.J. Thompson Collection, were auctioned at <a href="/wiki/Sotheby%27s" title="Sotheby&#39;s">Sotheby's</a> in London; the total sale estimate of £15 million was achieved, even though two paintings failed to reach their reserve price and were withdrawn.<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> Thompson, owner of the <i>Salford Express</i>, collected only Lowry paintings, starting in 1982. The auction included the paintings <i>Peel Park, Salford</i> and <i>Piccadilly Circus, London</i>, Lowry's most expensive painting at auction to date, which fetched £5.6 million in 2011 but only £5.1 million in 2014. Lowry painted very few London scenes, and only two depict <a href="/wiki/Piccadilly_Circus" title="Piccadilly Circus">Piccadilly Circus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=L._S._Lowry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><div class="poem">
<p>So you hide all Lowry's paintings<br />

</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<ul><li>In the BBC's <i>Fake or Fortune?</i> experts determined whether three works were genuine Lowry paintings or forgeries. Experts analysed the paint used in one of the paintings, but the white paint did not match the flake white Lowry claimed to have always used. Lowry claimed to have only used five colours, <a href="/wiki/White_lead" title="White lead">flake white (lead white)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivory_black" class="mw-redirect" title="Ivory black">ivory black</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vermilion" title="Vermilion">vermillion red</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prussian_blue" title="Prussian blue">Prussian blue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yellow_ochre" class="mw-redirect" title="Yellow ochre">yellow ochre</a>, produced by <a href="/wiki/Winsor_%26_Newton" title="Winsor &amp; Newton">Winsor &amp; Newton</a>. Photographic evidence from the 1950s, however, showed that he had experimented with both <a href="/wiki/Titanium_white" class="mw-redirect" title="Titanium white">titanium white</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zinc_white" class="mw-redirect" title="Zinc white">zinc white</a>: <i>Darby and Joan</i> contained traces of zinc white. The same painting was also plainly visible in a contemporary BBC documentary film.<sup id="cite_ref-Fake_or_Fortune_88-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fake_or_Fortune-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li>The 2019 film <i><a href="/wiki/Mrs_Lowry_%26_Son" title="Mrs Lowry &amp; Son">Mrs Lowry &amp; Son</a></i>, directed by <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Noble" title="Adrian Noble">Adrian Noble</a> and starring <a href="/wiki/Vanessa_Redgrave" title="Vanessa Redgrave">Vanessa Redgrave</a> and <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Spall" title="Timothy Spall">Timothy Spall</a>, depicts the fraught relationship between Lowry and his elderly bed-ridden mother between 1934 and 1939.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
