<p>The <b>Office of Naval Intelligence</b> (<b>ONI</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Military_intelligence" title="Military intelligence">military intelligence</a> agency of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>. Established in 1882 primarily to advance the Navy's modernization efforts,<sup id="cite_ref-Proud_History_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Proud_History-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> ONI is the oldest member of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Intelligence_Community" title="United States Intelligence Community">United States Intelligence Community</a> and serves as the nation's premier source of maritime intelligence. Since the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>, its mission has broadened to include real-time reporting on the developments and activities of foreign <a href="/wiki/Navy" title="Navy">navies</a>; protecting maritime resources and interests; monitoring and countering transnational maritime threats; providing technical, operational, and tactical support to the U.S. Navy and its partners; and surveying the global maritime environment. ONI employs over 3,000 military and civilian personnel worldwide and is headquartered at the <a href="/wiki/National_Maritime_Intelligence_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="National Maritime Intelligence Center">National Maritime Intelligence Center</a> in <a href="/wiki/Suitland,_Maryland" title="Suitland, Maryland">Suitland, Maryland</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Office_of_Naval_Intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite playing an active and decisive role in the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, in the following years the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Navy">U.S. Navy</a> fell into precipitous decline. A lack of both federal funding and public interest reduced the Navy's size, prestige, and technological superiority; whereas steel ships were increasingly the norm, the U.S. Navy was entirely wood-based. Towards the end of the 19th century, American naval power had become vastly obsolete compared to Europe, and even lagged behind the navies of less developed nations such as Ottoman Turkey and Chile.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In an era of <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">rapid industrialization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">globalized commerce</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Colonial_expansion" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonial expansion">colonial expansion</a>, the prevailing <a href="/wiki/Military_theory" title="Military theory">military theory</a> of the time held that <a href="/wiki/The_Influence_of_Sea_Power_upon_History" title="The Influence of Sea Power upon History">navies were critical</a> to the commercial and strategic interests of a nation, as well as a source of national prestige and power projection. In light of these developments, American naval officers and military strategists advocated for a larger and more technologically advanced navy that could protect the U.S.' vast maritime borders, safeguard its commercial interests, and project power abroad. Among the leading reformers was Navy Lieutenant <a href="/wiki/Theodorus_Bailey_Myers_Mason" class="mw-redirect" title="Theodorus Bailey Myers Mason">Theodorus Bailey Myers Mason</a>, who called for the creation of a naval intelligence office dedicated to gathering information on foreign navies and the latest in naval science to help rebuild the U.S. Navy.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/William_H._Hunt" title="William H. Hunt">William H. Hunt</a>, who served briefly as <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_the_Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of the Navy">Secretary of the Navy</a> under President James Garfield, formed a Naval Advisory Board tasked with rebuilding the Navy and bringing it up to par to global standards. Largely in response to Mason's recommendations, on March 23, 1882, Hunt issued General Order No. 292, which read:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>An "Office of Intelligence" is hereby established in the Bureau of Navigation for the purpose of collecting and recording such naval information as may be useful to the Department in time of war, as well as in peace.
</p><p>To facilitate this work, the Department Library will be combined with the "Office of Intelligence," and placed under the direction of the Chief of the Bureau of Navigation.
</p><p>Commanding and all other officers are directed to avail themselves of all opportunities which may arise to collect and to forward to the "Office of Intelligence" professional matters likely to serve the object in view.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The new Office of Naval Intelligence would be headquartered in the State, War and Navy Building (now the <a href="/wiki/Old_Executive_Office_Building" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Executive Office Building">Old Executive Office Building</a>), with Mason appointed as its first "Chief Intelligence Office".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup> As originally conceived, ONI assisted in the Navy's advancement by dispatching <a href="/wiki/Military_attach%C3%A9" title="Military attaché">naval attachés</a> around the world to acquire data and resources related to the latest in naval warfare. These findings would be analyzed, interpreted, and disseminated to Navy leaders and government officials, helping to inform policies and programs related to naval development.<sup id="cite_ref-Proud_History_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Proud_History-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion">Expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Office_of_Naval_Intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mason was succeeded as Chief Intelligence Officer by Rear Admiral <a href="/wiki/Raymond_P._Rodgers" title="Raymond P. Rodgers">Raymond P. Rodgers</a> in April 1885. In addition to intensifying ONI's research and surveillance of naval technology abroad, Rodger's four-year tenure saw ONI partner with the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_State" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Department of State">U.S. Department of State</a> in gathering information on strategic maritime interests such as <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samoa" title="Samoa">Samoa</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hawaii" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Hawaii">Kingdom of Hawaii</a>. ONI also began to develop capabilities in <a href="/wiki/Cryptography" title="Cryptography">cryptography</a>, which would foreshadow its evolution into a full-fledged military intelligence office.
</p><p>In 1890, one year after Rodger's departure from ONI, the office was transferred from the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Navigation_(U.S._Navy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bureau of Navigation (U.S. Navy)">Bureau of Navigation</a> to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_the_Navy" title="United States Secretary of the Navy">Secretary of the Navy</a>, solidifying its key role in the Navy's growth and development. ONI's emergence as a proper naval <a href="/wiki/Military_intelligence" title="Military intelligence">intelligence</a> arm began in earnest with the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">Spanish–American War</a> of 1898. Naval operations were critical in the conflict, and ONI was responsible for protecting Navy Personnel, providing tactical support, and implementing counter-intelligence measures. Nevertheless, weaknesses in its intelligence gathering were revealed.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>ONI grew in prominence under President <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Roosevelt" title="Theodore Roosevelt">Theodore Roosevelt</a>, a former Assistant Secretary of the Navy and naval enthusiast. His <a href="/wiki/Big_Stick_Policy" class="mw-redirect" title="Big Stick Policy">expansionist foreign policy</a> — and the central role the U.S. Navy played therein — made maritime intelligence more crucial. The sailing of the "<a href="/wiki/Great_White_Fleet" title="Great White Fleet">Great White Fleet</a>" around the world between 1906 and 1907, which included sixteen newly constructed steel <a href="/wiki/Battleships" class="mw-redirect" title="Battleships">battleships</a>, showcased new-found American seapower and validated ONI's efforts. By 1911, the U.S. was constructing super-dreadnoughts at a pace that would eventually become competitive with Britain.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>American entry into the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a> in 1917 marked a turning point in the office's history. President <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson" title="Woodrow Wilson">Woodrow Wilson</a> was an exponent of the importance of a strong navy to U.S. defense.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Under his administration, Congress authorized ONI's first major increase in personnel and funding, and expanded its role to include domestic security operations — namely the protection of U.S. ports, harbors, and maritime facilities from enemy infiltration and sabotage. ONI's mandate often entailed partnering with the departments of State, War, Justice, Commerce, and Labor. Due to the increasingly sensitive nature of its work, ONI also began to censor radio and mail communications, which further marked its development as a major intelligence office.
</p><p>During the 1920s and 1930s, many of ONI's activities were dedicated to Japan, which was an increasingly advanced and <a href="/wiki/Japanese_invasion_of_Manchuria" title="Japanese invasion of Manchuria">belligerent</a> naval power. The office investigated Japanese fortifications in the Pacific, acquired information on Japanese military aircraft and weaponry, and partnered with the U.S. Army's <a href="/wiki/Military_Intelligence_Division" class="mw-redirect" title="Military Intelligence Division">Military Intelligence Division</a> and the Federal Bureau of Investigation to monitor potentially subversive elements within the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese American">Japanese American</a> community;<sup id="cite_ref-Niiya-ONI_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Niiya-ONI-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> ONI's director, Rear Admiral Walter Stratton Anderson, met weekly with his counterparts in the FBI and MID to gather and share information on suspected internal threats. In 1929, <a href="/wiki/Chief_of_Naval_Operations" title="Chief of Naval Operations">Chief of Naval Operations</a> <a href="/wiki/William_D._Leahy" title="William D. Leahy">William D. Leahy</a> made permanent ONI's functions as an intelligence office, while in 1939, President Franklin D. Roosevelt granted the office considerable authority on matters of domestic security.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Office_of_Naval_Intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following Japan's <a href="/wiki/Attack_on_Pearl_Harbor" title="Attack on Pearl Harbor">attack on Pearl Harbor</a> in 1941, concerns about subversive activity by Japanese Americans grew more pressing. ONI commissioned Kenneth Ringle, assistant district intelligence officer for the Eleventh Naval District in Los Angeles, to conduct a thorough investigation of the resident Japanese population. He found little evidence of Japanese American saboteurs, and in his final report to President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Roosevelt</a>, advised against <a href="/wiki/Mass_incarceration" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass incarceration">mass incarceration</a>, a view that was shared by most ONI officials, but that was largely ignored by the Army and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_War" title="United States Department of War">War Department</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> would see another expansion of ONI's duties and a subsequent increase in its budget and staff. The office established two intelligence schools that trained hundreds of Intel officers for the Navy. Its Special Activities Branch offered critical intelligence on German <a href="/wiki/U-boat" title="U-boat">U-boat</a> technology, operations, and tactics, which proved decisive in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Atlantic" title="Battle of the Atlantic">Battle of the Atlantic</a>. ONI supplied U.S. forces with ship and aircraft recognition manuals, provided photographic specialists for identifying enemy vessels, assisted in naval mission planning, and was responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Translation" title="Translation">translation</a>, evaluation, and dissemination of intercepted Japanese communications.<sup id="cite_ref-Niiya-ONI_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Niiya-ONI-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cold_War">Cold War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Office_of_Naval_Intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cold War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While other parts of the Navy were downsized after the war, U.S. Fleet Admiral <a href="/wiki/Chester_Nimitz" class="mw-redirect" title="Chester Nimitz">Chester Nimitz</a> ensured ONI's continued strength, which was to prove important during the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>. Secretary of the Navy <a href="/wiki/James_Forrestal" title="James Forrestal">James Forrestal</a> broadened ONI's mandate to include investigations of major criminal and security matters.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In 1946, the Operational Intelligence Section was formed to provide fleet commanders with real-time analysis of the maritime activities and positions of foreign naval forces, namely the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Navy" title="Soviet Navy">Soviet Navy</a>. The Navy Field Operational Intelligence Office (NFOIO) was established in 1957 to provide more advanced <a href="/wiki/Signals_intelligence" title="Signals intelligence">signals intelligence</a> and timely information on the intent of enemy forces.
</p><p>ONI also made a concerted effort to enhance its technical and scientific resources, diversifying its personnel to reflect a wider range of expertise. The Navy Scientific and Technical Intelligence Center (NAVSTIC) was established in 1968 and shortly thereafter was folded into the Navy Reconnaissance and Technical Support Center (NRTSC). In response to the threat posed by nuclear-armed Soviet submarines, ONI developed the Sound Surveillance System (SOSUS) and the Ocean Surveillance Information system (OSIS), allowing the U.S. Navy to monitor and deter these threats.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consolidation_and_transformation">Consolidation and transformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Office_of_Naval_Intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Consolidation and transformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beginning in 1988, and following the <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Dissolution of the Soviet Union">end of the Cold War</a>, ONI's headquarters was moved to its current location in the National Maritime Intelligence Center (NMIC) in Suitland, Maryland. It was joined by <a href="/wiki/Coast_Guard_Intelligence_(CGI)" class="mw-redirect" title="Coast Guard Intelligence (CGI)">Coast Guard Intelligence (CGI)</a>, which is responsible for domestic maritime operations, and the <a href="/wiki/Marine_Corps_Intelligence_Activity" class="mw-redirect" title="Marine Corps Intelligence Activity">Marine Corps Intelligence Activity</a>, which supports expeditionary missions in <a href="/wiki/Littoral_zone" title="Littoral zone">littoral areas</a>. The housing of all three of the nation's principal maritime intelligence agencies was intended to better facilitate data sharing and coordination.
</p><p>Since the start of the <a href="/wiki/Global_War_on_Terror" class="mw-redirect" title="Global War on Terror">Global War on Terror</a> in 2001—and the subsequently large role played by the U.S. Navy in related conflicts in Afghanistan, Iraq, and the Horn of Africa—ONI has experienced further expansion of its duties and functions.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The year 2009 was characterized by a major reorganization of the office. Chief of Naval Operations Admiral Gary Roughead authorized ONI's conversion into a <a href="/wiki/Command_(military_formation)" title="Command (military formation)">command</a> with four subordinate commands, each with a specialized function: scientific and technical intelligence, operational intelligence, information services and technology, and expeditionary and special warfare support. All four commands were collocated in NMIC, which was afterward designated by the <a href="/wiki/Director_of_National_Intelligence" title="Director of National Intelligence">Director of National Intelligence</a> as the nation's central source for integrated strategic maritime intelligence.
</p><p>The same year, the Information Dominance Corps (IDC) was established by the Navy to train enlisted sailors and officers in a wide range of supporting intelligence capabilities. The IDC was redesignated in 2016 as the <a href="/wiki/Information_warfare" title="Information warfare">information warfare</a> Community (IWC), with a greater emphasis on inter-disciplinary expertise in sustaining the U.S. Navy's operational and technological superiority. ONI provides the IWC with critical maritime intelligence and real-time global maritime surveillance.
</p><p>The 21st century has also seen an extension of ONI's support beyond the Navy and U.S. government and towards relevant academic and commercial partners. In addition to operations related to the War on Terror, contemporary challenges that rely upon maritime intelligence include <a href="/wiki/Anti-piracy_measures_in_Somalia" title="Anti-piracy measures in Somalia">anti-piracy efforts</a>, surveillance of potential maritime conflict zones (such as the <a href="/wiki/Territorial_disputes_in_the_South_China_Sea" title="Territorial disputes in the South China Sea">territorial disputes in the South China Sea</a>), and monitoring the activities and developments in emerging rival navies (such as those of China, Russia, and Iran).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization_and_personnel">Organization and personnel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Office_of_Naval_Intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Organization and personnel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to its official website, ONI's organizational structure is specifically designed to "strengthen the Navy's conventional and irregular war fighting capacities, and to expand our foresight into new technologies, future platforms, weapons, sensors, C4ISR and cyber capabilities".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>ONI is based in the National Maritime Intelligence Center (NMIC), located on the grounds of the Suitland Federal Center in Suitland, Maryland. It is collocated with its four specialized subcommands, known as "Centers of Excellence" – the Nimitz Operational Intelligence Center, Farragut Technical Analysis Center, Kennedy Irregular Warfare Center, and the Hopper Information Services Center. Since 2009, the facility has been designed to facilitate 24-hour-a-day coordination, collaboration, and analysis of maritime intelligence among ONI's subcommands, as well as its counterparts in the Marine Corps and Coast Guard. This integration is intended to offer both comprehensive and rapid intelligence to a broad range of stakeholders.
</p><p>ONI is led by a commander, formally known as the Commander, Office of Naval Intelligence (COMONI), who also serves as Director of the NMIC. The COMONI's functions including fulfilling the national maritime intelligence duties required by the Navy, Department of Defense (DoD), and wider intelligence community.
</p><p>There is also a Deputy Commander, who serves as the Commander’s primary assistant and adviser; a Chief Staff Officer, who directs the activities of staff directors and officers, and serves as the point of contact for other commands; and the Command Master Chief, who leads the enlisted personnel and advises the COMONI, Deputy Commander, and Chief of Staff on command policy.
</p><p>ONI employs over 3,000 military and civilian personnel worldwide, including contractors. Its staff includes intelligence analysts, scientists, engineers, and other qualified specialists. In addition to its permanent staff, ONI is supported by more than 800 Navy Reservists, who assist the office during weekend drills and active duty.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nimitz_Operational_Intelligence_Center">Nimitz Operational Intelligence Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Office_of_Naval_Intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nimitz Operational Intelligence Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Named after World War II <a href="/wiki/Fleet_admiral_(United_States)" title="Fleet admiral (United States)">Fleet Admiral</a> <a href="/wiki/Chester_W._Nimitz" title="Chester W. Nimitz">Chester W. Nimitz</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Nimitz_Operational_Intelligence_Center" title="Nimitz Operational Intelligence Center">Nimitz Operational Intelligence Center</a> has responsibility for Maritime Domain Awareness (MDA) and Global Maritime Intelligence Integration (GMII), which allows it to maintain the U.S. Navy's warfighting superiority by delivering precise and timely information on the capability and position of enemy naval forces.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Farragut_Technical_Analysis_Center">Farragut Technical Analysis Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Office_of_Naval_Intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Farragut Technical Analysis Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Named for Admiral <a href="/wiki/David_Farragut" title="David Farragut">David Farragut</a>, the Farragut Technical Analysis Center is the U.S. Navy's Center of Excellence for strategic scientific and technical intelligence (S&amp;TI) analysis of foreign technologies, sensors, weapons, platforms, combat systems, C4ISR, and cyber capabilities. 
</p><p>In addition to its all-source capabilities, the Farragut Center conducts ONI's foreign materiel exploitation, signal intelligence analysis, modeling and simulation, and is home to the national maritime acoustic intelligence laboratory. The Farragut is one of four Centers of Excellence that are distinct Commands under the Office of Naval Intelligence (ONI).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kennedy_Irregular_Warfare_Center">Kennedy Irregular Warfare Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Office_of_Naval_Intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Kennedy Irregular Warfare Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Commanded by a captain, the Kennedy Center provides support to Navy Special Warfare and Navy Expeditionary Combat Command forces by providing intelligence on potential threats posed by <a href="/wiki/Asymmetrical_warfare" class="mw-redirect" title="Asymmetrical warfare">asymmetrical warfare</a>. Analysts are often called upon to perform other tasks and duties within this specialized area.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hopper_Information_Services_Center">Hopper Information Services Center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Office_of_Naval_Intelligence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Hopper Information Services Center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Named for Rear Admiral <a href="/wiki/Grace_Hopper" title="Grace Hopper">Grace Hopper</a>, the Hopper Center provides information services that support global maritime and intelligence operations. Its staff consists of more than 850 information technology specialists based in 42 locations in 11 countries. The center also assists in the integration, testing, fielding, and maintenance of advanced technologies utilized by ONI and its centers.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
