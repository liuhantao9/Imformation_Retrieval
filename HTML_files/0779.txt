<p>The <b>National Library of Israel</b> (<b>NLI</b>; <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span lang="he" dir="rtl">הספרייה הלאומית</span>&#8206;, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Hebrew" title="Romanization of Hebrew">romanized</a>:&#160;</small><i lang="he-Latn" title="Hebrew-language romanization">HaSifria HaLeumit</i>; <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl">المكتبة الوطنية في إسرائيل</span>&#8206;), formerly <b>Jewish National and University Library</b> (<b>JNUL</b>; <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>: <span lang="he" dir="rtl">בית הספרים הלאומי והאוניברסיטאי</span>&#8206;, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Hebrew" title="Romanization of Hebrew">romanized</a>:&#160;</small><i lang="he-Latn" title="Hebrew-language romanization">Beit Ha-Sfarim Ha-Le'umi ve-Ha-Universita'i</i>), is the library dedicated to collecting the cultural treasures of <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Jewish</a> <a href="/wiki/Cultural_heritage" title="Cultural heritage">heritage</a>. The library holds more than 5 million books, and is located on the <a href="/wiki/Givat_Ram" title="Givat Ram">Givat Ram</a> campus of the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_University_of_Jerusalem" title="Hebrew University of Jerusalem">Hebrew University of Jerusalem</a>.
</p><p>The National Library owns the world's largest collections of <a href="/wiki/Hebraica" class="mw-redirect" title="Hebraica">Hebraica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Judaica" class="mw-redirect" title="Judaica">Judaica</a>, and is the repository of many rare and unique manuscripts, books and artifacts.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Library_of_Israel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:B%27nai_B%27rith_library.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/B%27nai_B%27rith_library.jpg/220px-B%27nai_B%27rith_library.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/B%27nai_B%27rith_library.jpg/330px-B%27nai_B%27rith_library.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/B%27nai_B%27rith_library.jpg/440px-B%27nai_B%27rith_library.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="1035" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:B%27nai_B%27rith_library.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>B'nai Brith library, Jerusalem</div></div></div>
<p>The establishment of a Jewish National Library in Jerusalem was the brainchild of Joseph Chazanovitz (1844-1919). His idea was creating a "home for all works in all languages and literatures which have Jewish authors, even though they create in foreign cultures." Chazanovitz collected some 15,000 volumes which later became the core of the library.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/B%27nai_Brith" class="mw-redirect" title="B&#39;nai Brith">B'nai Brith</a> library, founded in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> in 1892, was the first public library in <a href="/wiki/Palestine_(region)" title="Palestine (region)">Palestine</a> to serve the Jewish community. The library was located on <a href="/wiki/B%27nai_Brith" class="mw-redirect" title="B&#39;nai Brith">B'nai Brith</a> street, between the <a href="/wiki/Meah_Shearim" class="mw-redirect" title="Meah Shearim">Meah Shearim</a> neighborhood and the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Compound" title="Russian Compound">Russian Compound</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Haaretz_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Ten years later, the Bet Midrash Abrabanel library, as it was then known, moved to Ethiopia Street.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In 1920, when plans were drawn up for the Hebrew University, the B'nai Brith collection became the basis for a university library. The books were moved to <a href="/wiki/Mount_Scopus" title="Mount Scopus">Mount Scopus</a> when the university opened five years later.<sup id="cite_ref-Haaretz_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1948, when access to the university campus on Mount Scopus was blocked, most of the books were moved to the university's temporary quarters in the Terra Sancta building in <a href="/wiki/Rehavia" title="Rehavia">Rehavia</a>. By that time, the university collection included over one million books. For lack of space, some of the books were placed in storerooms around the city. In 1960, they were moved to the new JNUL building in Givat Ram.<sup id="cite_ref-Haaretz_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the late 1970s, when the new university complex on Mount Scopus was inaugurated and the faculties of Law, Humanities and Social Science returned there, departmental libraries opened on that campus and the number of visitors to the Givat Ram library dropped. In the 1990s, the building suffered from maintenance problems such as rainwater leaks and insect infestation.<sup id="cite_ref-Haaretz_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2007 the library was officially recognized as The National Library of the State of Israel after the passage of the National Library Law.<sup id="cite_ref-Haaretz_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Haaretz-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The law, which came into effect on 23 July 2008, changed the library's name to "National Library of Israel" and turned it temporarily to a <a href="/wiki/Subsidiary_company" class="mw-redirect" title="Subsidiary company">subsidiary company</a> of the University, later to become a fully independent <a href="/wiki/Community_interest_company" title="Community interest company">community interest company</a>, jointly owned by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Israel" class="mw-redirect" title="Government of Israel">Government of Israel</a> (50%), the Hebrew University (25%) and other organizations.
</p><p>In 2011, the library launched a website granting public access to books, periodicals, maps, photos and music from its collections.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014, the project for a new home of the Library in Jerusalem was unveiled.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The 34,000 square meters building, designed by the <a href="/wiki/Basel" title="Basel">Basel</a>-based architecture firm <a href="/wiki/Herzog_%26_de_Meuron" title="Herzog &amp; de Meuron">Herzog &amp; de Meuron</a>, is scheduled for full completion in 2021.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Goals_and_objectives">Goals and objectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Library_of_Israel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Goals and objectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ardon_Windows_JNUL.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Ardon_Windows_JNUL.jpg/220px-Ardon_Windows_JNUL.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Ardon_Windows_JNUL.jpg/330px-Ardon_Windows_JNUL.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Ardon_Windows_JNUL.jpg/440px-Ardon_Windows_JNUL.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1152" data-file-height="864" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ardon_Windows_JNUL.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Mordecai_Ardon" title="Mordecai Ardon">Ardon</a> windows in the library lobby</div></div></div>
<p>The library's mission is to secure copies of all material published in Israel, in any language; all publications on the subject of Israel, the <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Israel" title="Land of Israel">Land of Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish people">Jewish people</a>, published in any language, in any country in the world; and all material published in <a href="/wiki/Hebrew" class="mw-redirect" title="Hebrew">Hebrew</a> or any of the languages spoken in the <a href="/wiki/Jewish_Diaspora" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish Diaspora">Jewish Diaspora</a> (such as <a href="/wiki/Yiddish" title="Yiddish">Yiddish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">Ladino</a>).
</p><p>By law, two copies of all printed matter published in Israel must be deposited in the National Library. In 2001, the law was amended to include audio and video recordings, and other non-print media.<sup id="cite_ref-history_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Many manuscripts, including some of the library's unique volumes such the 13th century <a href="/w/index.php?title=Worms_Mahzor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Worms Mahzor (page does not exist)">Worms Mahzor</a>, have been scanned and are now available on the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Special_collections">Special collections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Library_of_Israel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Special collections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Reading_room_of_the_National_Library_of_Israel_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Reading_room_of_the_National_Library_of_Israel_2.jpg/220px-Reading_room_of_the_National_Library_of_Israel_2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="129" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Reading_room_of_the_National_Library_of_Israel_2.jpg/330px-Reading_room_of_the_National_Library_of_Israel_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Reading_room_of_the_National_Library_of_Israel_2.jpg/440px-Reading_room_of_the_National_Library_of_Israel_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="528" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Reading_room_of_the_National_Library_of_Israel_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Reading room</div></div></div>
<p>Among the library's special collections are the personal papers of hundreds of outstanding Jewish figures, the National Sound Archives, the Laor Map Collection and numerous other collections of Hebraica and Judaica. The library also possesses some of <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a>'s manuscripts dealing with theological subjects.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The collection, donated by the family of the collector <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Yahuda" title="Abraham Yahuda">Abraham Yahuda</a>, includes a large number of works by Newton about mysticism, analyses of holy books, predictions about the end of days and the appearance of the ancient Temple in Jerusalem. It also contains maps that Newton sketched about mythical events to assist him in his end of days calculations.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The library houses the personal archives of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Buber" title="Martin Buber">Martin Buber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gershom_Scholem" title="Gershom Scholem">Gershom Scholem</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the occupation of <a href="/wiki/West_Jerusalem" title="West Jerusalem">West Jerusalem</a> by <a href="/wiki/Haganah" title="Haganah">Haganah</a> forces in May 1948, the libraries of a number Palestinians who fled the country as well as of other well-to-do Palestinians were transferred to the National Library.<sup id="cite_ref-Amit_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amit-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> These collections included those of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cattan" title="Henry Cattan">Henry Cattan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Khalil_Beidas" title="Khalil Beidas">Khalil Beidas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Khalil_al-Sakakini" title="Khalil al-Sakakini">Khalil al-Sakakini</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nashashibi" class="mw-redirect" title="Nashashibi">Aref Hikmet Nashashibi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> About 30,000 books were removed from homes in West Jerusalem, with another 40,000 taken from other cities in Mandatory Palestine. It is unclear whether the books were being kept and protected or if they were looted from the abandoned houses of their owners.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> About 6,000 of these books are in the library today indexed with the label AP – "Abandoned Property".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The books are cataloged, can be viewed from the Library's general catalog and are regularly consulted by the public, including Arab scholars from all over the world.
</p><p>The National Library of Israel completes its collection of the <a href="/wiki/Max_Brod" title="Max Brod">Max Brod</a> archive in  August 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
