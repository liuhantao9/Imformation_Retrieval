<p><b>Castle Hot Springs</b> is a recently restored historic resort in <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> that is listed on the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>. It is located within the <a href="/wiki/Hieroglyphic_Mountains" title="Hieroglyphic Mountains">Hieroglyphic Mountains</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Castle_Hot_Springs_(Arizona)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Apache_Wars" title="Apache Wars">Apache Wars</a> discouraged development of the area until the 1880s when the springs and the adjacent land were purchased by Frank Murphy for the construction of a <a href="/wiki/Destination_spa" title="Destination spa">health resort</a>.  The resort was completed in 1896 by the brother of the Arizona Governor <a href="/wiki/Oakes_Murphy" title="Oakes Murphy">Oakes Murphy</a>, and the newly renamed "Castle Hot Springs" was heavily advertised to potential clients.  During the resort's heyday in the 1920s it was visited by celebrities such as <a href="/wiki/Zane_Grey" title="Zane Grey">Zane Grey</a>, as well as famous families such as the <a href="/wiki/Rockefeller_family" title="Rockefeller family">Rockefellers</a>.
</p><p>The resort was used by the United States military as a rehabilitation center from 1943 to 1944 to treat injured veterans of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.  Future president <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> spent three months at the resort during this period to recover from his wounds suffered during the sinking of his ship, <a href="/wiki/Motor_Torpedo_Boat_PT-109" class="mw-redirect" title="Motor Torpedo Boat PT-109">PT-109</a>.
</p><p>The resort continued to be commercially operated until the main building was heavily damaged in a fire in 1976.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The resort still comprises its swimming pool, administrative building and guest house along with the springs, which produce 180,000 gallons of hot water a day.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The resort became a stop on the <a href="/wiki/Railroad_classes#Class_III" title="Railroad classes">short-line</a> <a href="/wiki/Arizona_and_California_Railroad" title="Arizona and California Railroad">Arizona and California Railroad</a> in the 1990s.
</p><p>The property was sold in March 2014 for $1.95 Million.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>   In March 2018, the resort announced a restoration was underway with plans for reopening in October of that year.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The new owners have extensively remodeled and added to the property, adding 12 spring bungalows - with hot spring fed tubs, and 17 sky-view cabins. The resort reopened to visitors in February of 2019. <sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
