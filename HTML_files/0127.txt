<p><b>Surinamese Dutch</b> (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i>Surinaams-Nederlands</i>, <small>Dutch pronunciation:&#32;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Dutch" title="Help:IPA/Dutch">[ˌsyːriˈnaːms ˈneːdərˌlɑnts]</a></span>) is the form of <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> spoken in <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> and is the official language in Suriname, a former <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> colony. Dutch is spoken as a native language by about 60% of the population, most of them being bilingual with <a href="/wiki/Sranan_Tongo" title="Sranan Tongo">Sranan Tongo</a> (national <i><a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Hindustani#Sarnami_Hindustani" title="Caribbean Hindustani">Hindustani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Javanese_language#Surinamese-Javanese" title="Javanese language">Javanese</a>, and other languages. Nevertheless, Dutch is the sole official language of the country. Surinamese Dutch is easily intelligible with other forms of Dutch. Furthermore, as opposed to other languages that have different forms in the Americas (e.g., <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a> vs. <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>) the regulation and thus standardised spelling of the Dutch language is done through a joint Dutch-Belgian-Surinamese organization, the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Language_Union" title="Dutch Language Union">Dutch Language Union</a>, and thus has no regional differences regarding spelling. Suriname has been an associate member of this <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Language_Union" title="Dutch Language Union">Nederlandse Taalunie</a> since 2005. Therefore, many typical Surinamese words were added to the official <a href="/wiki/Wordlist_of_the_Dutch_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Wordlist of the Dutch language">Wordlist of Standard Dutch</a>, known as "the Green Booklet" (<i>Groene Boekje</i>).
</p><p>Surinamese Dutch is generally easily distinguishable from other standardized forms of Dutch due to many <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanwords</a> and phrases adopted from <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Suriname" class="mw-redirect" title="Languages of Suriname">other languages spoken in Suriname</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surinamese_Dutch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Phonology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Surinamese Dutch, the voiced fricatives <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/v, z, ɣ/</span> have completely merged with the voiceless <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/f, s, x/</span> into voiceless <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiceless labiodental fricative">f</a>, <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless alveolar sibilant">s</a>, <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_fricative" title="Voiceless velar fricative">x</a>]</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDe_Schutter2013448,_451_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDe_Schutter2013448,_451-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_Surinamese_Dutch">History of Surinamese Dutch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surinamese_Dutch&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of Surinamese Dutch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dutch was introduced in what is now Suriname when <a href="/wiki/Paramaribo" title="Paramaribo">Paramaribo</a> and its environs became a Dutch colony. The remainder of Suriname, however, remained in <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British</a> hands. Only after the Dutch had lost <a href="/wiki/New_Netherland" title="New Netherland">New Netherland</a> (including present-day <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>) to the British did they in exchange receive the rest of Suriname. After this, Dutch became the language of communication between <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Suriname" title="Indigenous peoples in Suriname">Native Surinamese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africans" class="mw-redirect" title="Africans">Africans</a> and the Dutch colonial administration. In 1876, the language in addition became official in the <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Suriname" title="Education in Suriname">Surinamese education system</a> whilst new immigrants from <a href="/wiki/British_India" class="mw-redirect" title="British India">British India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a> also picked up the language. These immigrants also added features to spoken Dutch that are not present in the original <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> variants of Dutch.
</p>
