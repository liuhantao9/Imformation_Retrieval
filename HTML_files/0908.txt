<p><b>Jean Ann Kennedy Smith</b> (born February 20, 1928) is an American diplomat, activist, humanitarian,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and author who served as <a href="/wiki/United_States_Ambassador_to_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Ambassador to Ireland">United States Ambassador to Ireland</a> from 1993 to 1998. She is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_family" title="Kennedy family">Kennedy family</a>, the eighth of nine children born to <a href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph P. Kennedy">Joseph P. Kennedy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rose_Kennedy" title="Rose Kennedy">Rose Fitzgerald</a>, and is their last surviving child. Her siblings included President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>, Senator <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a>, Senator <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Ted Kennedy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Special_Olympics" title="Special Olympics">Special Olympics</a> founder <a href="/wiki/Eunice_Kennedy_Shriver" title="Eunice Kennedy Shriver">Eunice Kennedy Shriver</a>.
</p><p>As Ambassador to Ireland, Smith was instrumental in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_peace_process" title="Northern Ireland peace process">Northern Ireland peace process</a> as President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a>'s representative in Dublin. She was heavily criticized after urging the U.S. government to grant a visa to <a href="/wiki/Sinn_F%C3%A9in" title="Sinn Féin">Sinn Féin</a> President <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Adams" title="Gerry Adams">Gerry Adams</a>, although her family  claimed that this step influenced the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army" title="Irish Republican Army">Irish Republican Army</a> in its declaration of a ceasefire in 1994. <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a> <a href="/wiki/Mary_McAleese" title="Mary McAleese">Mary McAleese</a> conferred honorary Irish citizenship on Smith in 1998 in recognition of her service to the country.
</p><p>Smith is the founder of <a href="/wiki/Very_Special_Arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Very Special Arts">Very Special Arts</a> (VSA), an internationally recognized non-profit dedicated to creating a society where people with disabilities can engage with the arts. In 2011, she was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Medal_of_Freedom" title="Presidential Medal of Freedom">Presidential Medal of Freedom</a>, the highest civilian honor in the United States, by President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> for her work with VSA and with people with disabilities.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean_Kennedy_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jean Ann Kennedy was born in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, on her elder sister <a href="/wiki/Kathleen_Cavendish,_Marchioness_of_Hartington" title="Kathleen Cavendish, Marchioness of Hartington">Kathleen</a>'s eighth birthday.<sup id="cite_ref-jfklibrary_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfklibrary-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy was the eighth of nine children born to Joseph P. Kennedy and Rose Kennedy.<sup id="cite_ref-upi.com_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-upi.com-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Her siblings included U.S. President John F. Kennedy, U.S. Attorney General and U.S. Senator Robert F. Kennedy, U.S. Senator Ted Kennedy, and Special Olympics founder Eunice Kennedy Shriver.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> She has been described as the shyest and most guarded of the Kennedy children. She attended <a href="/wiki/Manhattanville_College" title="Manhattanville College">Manhattanville College</a> (at the time a <a href="/wiki/Society_of_the_Sacred_Heart" title="Society of the Sacred Heart">Society of the Sacred Heart</a> school, and still located in <a href="/wiki/Purchase,_New_York" title="Purchase, New York">Purchase, New York</a>), where she befriended future sisters-in-law <a href="/wiki/Ethel_Kennedy" title="Ethel Kennedy">Ethel Skakel</a> (who married Jean's older brother <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert</a> in 1950) and <a href="/wiki/Joan_Bennett_Kennedy" title="Joan Bennett Kennedy">Joan Bennett</a> (who married Jean's younger brother <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Ted</a> in 1958).<sup id="cite_ref-lastkennedy_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lastkennedy-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy graduated from Manhattanville in 1949.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean_Kennedy_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_involvement">Political involvement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean_Kennedy_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Political involvement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kennedy (known as Jean Kennedy Smith following her 1956 marriage to <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Edward_Smith" title="Stephen Edward Smith">Stephen Edward Smith</a>) was intricately involved with the political career of her older brother John. She worked on his 1946 Congressional campaign, his 1952 Senate campaign, and ultimately his presidential campaign in 1960. She and her siblings helped Kennedy knock on doors in primary states like Texas and Wisconsin and on the campaign trail played the role of sister more than volunteer, citing her parents' family lesson of "working together for something."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Smith and her husband were present at <a href="/wiki/Ambassador_Hotel_(Los_Angeles)" title="Ambassador Hotel (Los Angeles)">The Ambassador Hotel</a> in Los Angeles on June 5, 1968, during the  <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Assassination of Robert F. Kennedy">Assassination of Robert F. Kennedy</a> after he had won the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic</a> <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)_presidential_primaries,_1968" class="mw-redirect" title="Democratic Party (United States) presidential primaries, 1968">1968 California U.S. presidential primary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Very_Special_Arts">Very Special Arts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean_Kennedy_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Very Special Arts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1974, Smith founded <a href="/wiki/VSA_(Kennedy_Center)" title="VSA (Kennedy Center)">Very Special Arts</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-irishcentral.com_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irishcentral.com-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> now known as the Department of VSA and Accessibility at the John F. Kennedy Center for the Performing Arts. VSA provides arts and education programming for youth and adults with disabilities. As of 2011, VSA's programs reportedly served "some 276,000 students in 43 states and 52 countries".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Smith traveled extensively throughout the world on behalf of VSA to advocate for greater inclusion in the arts for people with disabilities. Her book, <i><a href="/wiki/Chronicles_of_Courage:_Very_Special_Artists" title="Chronicles of Courage: Very Special Artists">Chronicles of Courage: Very Special Artists</a>,</i> co-written with <a href="/wiki/George_Plimpton" title="George Plimpton">George Plimpton</a>, was published by <a href="/wiki/Random_House" title="Random House">Random House</a> in April 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-jfklibrary_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfklibrary-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Ambassador_to_Ireland">U.S. Ambassador to Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean_Kennedy_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: U.S. Ambassador to Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1993, Smith was appointed by U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> as the U.S. Ambassador to Ireland,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> continuing a legacy of diplomacy begun by her father, who was the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Ambassador_to_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Ambassador to the United Kingdom">United States Ambassador to the United Kingdom</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Administration_(government)" title="Administration (government)">administration</a> of U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> As ambassador, Smith played a pivotal role in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_peace_process" title="Northern Ireland peace process">Northern Ireland peace process</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> As a demonstration of her <a href="/wiki/Ecumenism" title="Ecumenism">ecumenical</a> views, on at least one occasion she received <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">communion</a> in a cathedral of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Ireland" title="Church of Ireland">Church of Ireland</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical_province#Anglican_Communion" title="Ecclesiastical province">autonomous province</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">Anglican Communion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a> <a href="/wiki/Mary_McAleese" title="Mary McAleese">Mary McAleese</a> conferred <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationality_law#By_grant_of_honorary_citizenship" title="Irish nationality law">honorary Irish citizenship</a> on Smith in 1998 in recognition of her service to the country.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> During a ceremony, McAleese praised Smith's "fixedness of purpose". Irish Taoiseach (Prime Minister) <a href="/wiki/Bertie_Ahern" title="Bertie Ahern">Bertie Ahern</a> told Smith, "You have helped bring about a better life for everyone throughout Ireland."<sup id="cite_ref-irishcentral.com_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irishcentral.com-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 4, 1998, about three months after the historic <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday_Agreement" title="Good Friday Agreement">Good Friday Agreement</a> of April 10, 1998, Smith retired as ambassador to Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-IT1998-07-04a_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IT1998-07-04a-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Sinn_F.C3.A9in_controversy"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Sinn_Féin_controversy">Sinn Féin controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean_Kennedy_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sinn Féin controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1994, Smith came to the forefront of American foreign policy when she championed the granting of a U.S. visa to <a href="/wiki/Sinn_Fein" class="mw-redirect" title="Sinn Fein">Sinn Fein</a> leader Gerry Adams. Smith has been lauded for her work in the region, but was criticized for supporting the visa. Her family have claimed this was a key step in the success of the peace process in the years that followed.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In her brother <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Ted</a>'s memoir, he described that "Jean was convinced that Adams no longer believed that continuing the armed struggle was the way to achieve the IRA's objective of a united Ireland," and that "It took only a couple of hours' conversation with Jean after we landed to discover what was the most important thing on her mind – the opportunity for a breakthrough in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> stalemate."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 1996, Smith was reprimanded by U.S. Secretary of State <a href="/wiki/Warren_Christopher" title="Warren Christopher">Warren Christopher</a> for retaliating against two <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Service_Officer" title="Foreign Service Officer">Foreign Service Officers</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Embassy_of_the_United_States_in_Dublin" class="mw-redirect" title="Embassy of the United States in Dublin">Embassy of the United States in Dublin</a>, Ireland, who had objected to her recommendation to the U.S. government to grant Adams the visa and had sent in a "<a href="/wiki/Dissent_Channel" title="Dissent Channel">Dissent Channel</a>" message.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Foreign_Service_Journal" title="Foreign Service Journal">Foreign Service Journal</a></i> called the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">U.S. State Department</a>'s report on the matter "scathingly critical".<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Her management of the embassy came under criticism by the <i><a href="/wiki/Boston_Herald" title="Boston Herald">Boston Herald</a></i> in December 1996 when she reportedly pressured embassy staff to spend taxpayer money to refurbish her residence in Dublin.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Smith was also allegedly to have violated U.S. <a href="/wiki/Conflict_of_interest" title="Conflict of interest">conflict-of-interest</a> laws. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">United States Department of Justice</a> issued a press release on September 22, 2000, announcing that she had paid US$5,000 in a civil settlement to resolve the allegations.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_later_work">Awards and later work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean_Kennedy_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Awards and later work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:145px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jean_Kennedy_Smith_and_Vicki_Kennedy.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/63/Jean_Kennedy_Smith_and_Vicki_Kennedy.jpg" decoding="async" width="143" height="185" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="143" data-file-height="185" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jean_Kennedy_Smith_and_Vicki_Kennedy.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kennedy Smith (left) with <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Reggie_Kennedy" title="Victoria Reggie Kennedy">Victoria Reggie Kennedy</a> in 2008</div></div></div>
<p>Smith has won several awards for her work in Ireland and in the disability community. She was awarded <a href="/wiki/Honorary_citizenship" title="Honorary citizenship">honorary citizenship</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of Ireland">Government of Ireland</a> in 1998<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and in 2007, Smith received the Gold Medal Award from the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89ire_Society_of_Boston" class="mw-redirect" title="Éire Society of Boston">Éire Society of Boston</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> for her peace efforts in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> and for her humanitarian work with disabled children. In 2009, Smith and <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Ted Kennedy</a> were honored with the Tipperary Peace Prize for their support of the peace process in Northern Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2011, President Barack Obama awarded Smith the <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Medal_of_Freedom" title="Presidential Medal of Freedom">Presidential Medal of Freedom</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> the nation's highest civilian honor, for her work with people with disabilities.
</p><p>On March 15, 2011, Smith was inducted into <i><a href="/wiki/Irish_America_(magazine)" title="Irish America (magazine)">Irish America</a></i> magazine's <a href="/wiki/Irish_America_Hall_of_Fame" title="Irish America Hall of Fame">Irish America Hall of Fame</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-KEN_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KEN-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Smith was listed as Ambassador Jean Kennedy Smith in the credits of the 2012 movie <i><a href="/wiki/Lincoln_(film)" title="Lincoln (film)">Lincoln</a></i> for portraying a "woman shouter".<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Memoir">Memoir</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean_Kennedy_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Memoir">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In October 2016, Smith published <i>The Nine of Us: Growing Up Kennedy</i>, a memoir of the Kennedy clan.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jean_Kennedy_Smith&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On May 19, 1956, Jean Kennedy married businessman <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Edward_Smith" title="Stephen Edward Smith">Stephen Edward Smith</a> in a small chapel of the Roman Catholic <a href="/wiki/St._Patrick%27s_Cathedral,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Patrick&#39;s Cathedral, New York">St. Patrick's Cathedral, New York</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-irishcentral.com_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irishcentral.com-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The Smiths maintained a lower profile than some other members of the extended Kennedy family. The Smiths had two sons, attorney Stephen Edward Smith Jr. and physician <a href="/wiki/William_Kennedy_Smith" title="William Kennedy Smith">William Kennedy Smith</a>. They adopted two daughters, Amanda Smith and Kym Smith.<sup id="cite_ref-upi.com_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-upi.com-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Stephen Edward Smith died of cancer on August 19, 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The following year, their younger son, <a href="/wiki/William_Kennedy_Smith" title="William Kennedy Smith">William</a>, was accused of rape in Florida, but was acquitted after a highly publicized trial.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT-WKS_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-WKS-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Smith's elder sister <a href="/wiki/Eunice_Kennedy_Shriver" title="Eunice Kennedy Shriver">Eunice Kennedy Shriver</a> died on August 11, 2009. Smith did not attend Eunice's funeral on August 14, 2009, choosing to stay with their brother Ted, who was ill; he died on August 25, 2009, leaving her as the last surviving child of Joseph and Rose Kennedy.<sup id="cite_ref-lastkennedy_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lastkennedy-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Smith attended Ted Kennedy's funeral on August 29, 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
