	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Jeans</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Blue_jeans_fading.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Blue_jeans_fading.png/220px-Blue_jeans_fading.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="168" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Blue_jeans_fading.png/330px-Blue_jeans_fading.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Blue_jeans_fading.png/440px-Blue_jeans_fading.png 2x" data-file-width="22750" data-file-height="17410" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Blue_jeans_fading.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Microscopic image of faded jeans fabric</div></div></div>
<p><b>Jeans</b> are a type of <a href="/wiki/Pants" class="mw-redirect" title="Pants">pants</a> or trousers, typically made from <a href="/wiki/Denim" title="Denim">denim</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dungaree_(fabric)" title="Dungaree (fabric)">dungaree</a> cloth. Often the term "jeans" refers to a particular style of trousers, called "blue jeans", which were invented by <a href="/wiki/Jacob_W._Davis" title="Jacob W. Davis">Jacob W. Davis</a> in partnership with <a href="/wiki/Levi_Strauss_%26_Co." title="Levi Strauss &amp; Co.">Levi Strauss &amp; Co.</a> in 1871<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and patented by <a href="/wiki/Jacob_W._Davis" title="Jacob W. Davis">Jacob W. Davis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Levi_Strauss" title="Levi Strauss">Levi Strauss</a> on May 20, 1873. Prior to the Levi Strauss patented trousers, the term "blue jeans" had been long in use for various garments (including trousers, overalls, and coats), constructed from blue-colored denim.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> "Jean" also references a (historic) type of sturdy cloth commonly made with a cotton warp and wool weft (also known as "Virginia cloth"). Jean cloth can be entirely cotton as well, similar to denim. Originally designed for <a href="/wiki/Miner" title="Miner">miners</a>, modern jeans was popularized as casual wear by Marlon Brando and James Dean in their 1950s films, particularly <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wild_One" title="The Wild One">The Wild One</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Rebel_Without_a_Cause" title="Rebel Without a Cause">Rebel Without a Cause</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> leading to the fabric becoming a symbol of rebellion among <a href="/wiki/Teenager" class="mw-redirect" title="Teenager">teenagers</a>, especially members of the <a href="/wiki/Greaser_(subculture)" title="Greaser (subculture)">greaser</a> subculture. From the 1960s onwards, jeans became common among various youth subcultures and subsequently the wider young members the general population. Nowadays, they are one of the most popular types of especially trousers in <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western culture</a>. Historic brands include <a href="/wiki/Levi_Strauss_%26_Co." title="Levi Strauss &amp; Co.">Levi's</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lee_(jeans)" title="Lee (jeans)">Lee</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wrangler_(jeans)" title="Wrangler (jeans)">Wrangler</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fabric">Fabric</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fabric">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Manifattura_genovese,_abito_da_festa_in_tela_di_genova_(jeans),_1850-1900_ca..JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Manifattura_genovese%2C_abito_da_festa_in_tela_di_genova_%28jeans%29%2C_1850-1900_ca..JPG/180px-Manifattura_genovese%2C_abito_da_festa_in_tela_di_genova_%28jeans%29%2C_1850-1900_ca..JPG" decoding="async" width="180" height="336" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Manifattura_genovese%2C_abito_da_festa_in_tela_di_genova_%28jeans%29%2C_1850-1900_ca..JPG/270px-Manifattura_genovese%2C_abito_da_festa_in_tela_di_genova_%28jeans%29%2C_1850-1900_ca..JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Manifattura_genovese%2C_abito_da_festa_in_tela_di_genova_%28jeans%29%2C_1850-1900_ca..JPG/360px-Manifattura_genovese%2C_abito_da_festa_in_tela_di_genova_%28jeans%29%2C_1850-1900_ca..JPG 2x" data-file-width="1836" data-file-height="3426" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Manifattura_genovese,_abito_da_festa_in_tela_di_genova_(jeans),_1850-1900_ca..JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A traditional women's Genoese dress in "blue jeans" (1890s)</div></div></div>
<p>Research on the trade of jean fabric shows that it emerged in the cities of <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/N%C3%AEmes" title="Nîmes">Nîmes</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. Gênes, the French word for <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">Genoa</a>, may be the origin of the word "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/jeans" class="extiw" title="wikt:jeans">jeans</a>". In Nîmes, weavers tried to reproduce jean fabric but instead developed a similar <a href="/wiki/Twill" title="Twill">twill</a> fabric that became known as denim, from <i>de <a href="/wiki/N%C3%AEmes" title="Nîmes">Nîmes</a></i>, meaning "from Nîmes". Genoa's jean fabric was a <a href="/wiki/Fustian" title="Fustian">fustian</a> textile of "medium quality and of reasonable cost", very similar to <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a> <a href="/wiki/Corduroy" title="Corduroy">corduroy</a> for which Genoa was famous, and was "used for work clothes in general". The <a href="/wiki/Genoese_navy" class="mw-redirect" title="Genoese navy">Genoese navy</a> equipped its sailors with jeans, as they needed a fabric which could be worn wet or dry.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Nîmes's "denim" was coarser, considered higher quality, and was used "for over garments such as smocks or overalls".<sup id="cite_ref-chetan_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chetan-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Nearly all <a href="/wiki/Indigo" title="Indigo">indigo</a>, needed for dyeing, came from indigo bush plantations in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> until the late 19th century. It was replaced by indigo synthesis methods developed in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Closeup_of_copper_rivet_on_jeans.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Closeup_of_copper_rivet_on_jeans.jpg/220px-Closeup_of_copper_rivet_on_jeans.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Closeup_of_copper_rivet_on_jeans.jpg/330px-Closeup_of_copper_rivet_on_jeans.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Closeup_of_copper_rivet_on_jeans.jpg/440px-Closeup_of_copper_rivet_on_jeans.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="532" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Closeup_of_copper_rivet_on_jeans.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Copper <a href="/wiki/Rivet" title="Rivet">rivets</a> for reinforcing pockets are a characteristic feature of blue jeans.</div></div></div>
<p>By the 17th century, jean was a crucial textile for working-class people in Northern Italy. This is seen in a series of genre paintings from around the 17th century attributed to an artist now nicknamed The Master of the Blue Jeans.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The ten paintings depict impoverished scenes with lower-class figures wearing a fabric that looks like denim. The fabric would have been Genoese jean, which was cheaper. Genre painting came to prominence in late 16th century, and the non-nobility subject matter in all ten paintings places them among others that portray similar scenes.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Dungaree was mentioned for the first time in the 17th century, when it was referred to as cheap, coarse thick cotton cloth, often colored blue but sometimes white, worn by impoverished people in what was then a region of <a href="/wiki/Bombay" class="mw-redirect" title="Bombay">Bombay</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> a dockside village called Dongri. This cloth was "dungri" in <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a>. Dungri was exported to <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and used for manufacturing of cheap, robust working clothes. In English, the word "dungri" became pronounced as "dungaree".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The term jeans appears first in 1795, when a Swiss banker by the name <a href="/wiki/Jean-Gabriel_Eynard" title="Jean-Gabriel Eynard">Jean-Gabriel Eynard</a> and his brother Jacques went to Genoa and both were soon heading a flourishing commercial concern. In 1800 <a href="/wiki/Massena" class="mw-redirect" title="Massena">Massena</a>'s troops entered the town and Jean-Gabriel was entrusted with their supply. In particular he furnished them with uniforms cut from blue cloth called "bleu de Genes" whence later derives the famous garment known worldwide as "blue jeans".<sup id="cite_ref-jeansculturalicon_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jeansculturalicon-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Levi_Strauss" title="Levi Strauss">Levi Strauss</a>, as a young man in 1851, went from Germany to New York to join his older brothers who ran a goods store. In 1853, he moved to <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> to open his own dry goods business. <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Davis_(inventor)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacob Davis (inventor)">Jacob Davis</a> was a tailor who often bought bolts of cloth from the <a href="/wiki/Levi_Strauss_%26_Co" class="mw-redirect" title="Levi Strauss &amp; Co">Levi Strauss &amp; Co</a>. <a href="/wiki/Wholesale" class="mw-redirect" title="Wholesale">wholesale</a> house. In 1872, Davis wrote to Strauss asking to partner with him to patent and sell clothing reinforced with <a href="/wiki/Rivet" title="Rivet">rivets</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Downey_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Downey-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">copper</a> rivets were to reinforce the points of stress, such as pocket corners and at the bottom of the button fly. Levi accepted Davis's offer,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and the two men received US patent No. 139,121 for an "Improvement in Fastening Pocket-Openings" on May 20, 1873.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Photo_%C3%A9tiquette_Levi%27s_501.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Photo_%C3%A9tiquette_Levi%27s_501.jpg/220px-Photo_%C3%A9tiquette_Levi%27s_501.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Photo_%C3%A9tiquette_Levi%27s_501.jpg/330px-Photo_%C3%A9tiquette_Levi%27s_501.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Photo_%C3%A9tiquette_Levi%27s_501.jpg/440px-Photo_%C3%A9tiquette_Levi%27s_501.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="375" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Photo_%C3%A9tiquette_Levi%27s_501.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The classic label for Levi 501 jeans.</div></div></div>
<p>Davis and Strauss experimented with different fabrics. An early attempt was brown <a href="/wiki/Cotton_duck" title="Cotton duck">cotton duck</a>, a bottom-weight fabric.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> Finding denim a more suitable material for work-pants, they began using it to manufacture their riveted pants. The denim used was produced by an American manufacturer. Popular legend incorrectly states that it was imported from <a href="/wiki/Nimes,_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Nimes, France">Nimes, France</a>. A popular myth is that Strauss initially sold brown canvas pants to miners, later dyed them blue, turned to using denim, and only after Davis wrote to him, added rivets.<sup id="cite_ref-Downey_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Downey-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Initially, Strauss' jeans were simply sturdy trousers worn by <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">factory workers</a>, miners, farmers, and cattlemen throughout the North American West.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> During this period, men's jeans had the <a href="/wiki/Fly_(clothing)" title="Fly (clothing)">fly</a> down the front, whereas women's jeans had the fly down the left side.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> When <a href="/wiki/Levi_Strauss_%26_Co." title="Levi Strauss &amp; Co.">Levi Strauss &amp; Co.</a> patented the modern, mass-produced prototype in the year 1873, there were two pockets in the front and one on the back with copper rivets. Later, the jeans were redesigned to today's industry standard of five pockets including a little watch pocket and copper rivets.<sup id="cite_ref-jeansculturalicon_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jeansculturalicon-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century_evolution">20th century evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 20th century evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fewer jeans were made during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, but 'waist overalls' were introduced to the world by US soldiers, who sometimes wore them  off duty.<sup id="cite_ref-The_History_of_Jeans_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_History_of_Jeans-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> By the 1960s, both men's and women's jeans had the <a href="/wiki/Zipper" title="Zipper">zipper</a> down the front. Historic photographs indicate that in the decades before they became a staple of fashion, jeans generally fit quite loosely, much like a pair of <a href="/wiki/Overall" title="Overall">bib overalls</a> without the bib. Indeed, until 1960, Levi Strauss called its flagship product "waist overalls" rather than "jeans".
</p><p>After <a href="/wiki/James_Dean" title="James Dean">James Dean</a> popularized them in the movie <i><a href="/wiki/Rebel_Without_a_Cause" title="Rebel Without a Cause">Rebel Without a Cause</a></i>, wearing jeans became a symbol of youth rebellion during the 1950s.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> During the 1960s the wearing of jeans became more acceptable, and by the 1970s it had become general fashion in the United States for casual wear.<sup id="cite_ref-60sjeans_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60sjeans-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> However, the acceptance of jean to becoming casual wear is still relatively low in Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Examples of intentional denim distressing strictly to make them more fashionable can be seen as early as 1935 in Vogue's June issue.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  Michael Belluomo, editor of <i>Sportswear International Magazine</i>, Oct/Nov 1987, P. 45, wrote that in 1965, Limbo, a boutique in the New York East Village, was "the first retailer to wash a new pair of jeans to get a used, worn effect, and the idea became a hit." He continued, "[Limbo] hired East Village artists to embellish the jeans with patches, decals, and other touches, and sold them for $200." In the early 1980s the denim industry introduced the <a href="/wiki/Stonewashed_jeans" class="mw-redirect" title="Stonewashed jeans">stone-washing</a> technique developed by GWG also known as "Great Western Garment Co." Donald Freeland of <a href="/wiki/Edmonton,_Alberta" class="mw-redirect" title="Edmonton, Alberta">Edmonton, Alberta</a> pioneered the method,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> which helped to bring denim to a larger and more versatile market. Acceptance of jeans continued through the 1980s and 1990s. Originally an esoteric fashion choice, in the 2010s jeans may be seen being worn by men and women of all ages.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Manufacturing_processes">Manufacturing processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Manufacturing processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dyeing">Dyeing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dyeing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Azo_dye" title="Azo dye">Azo dye</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Indigo_skeletal.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Indigo_skeletal.svg/220px-Indigo_skeletal.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="123" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Indigo_skeletal.svg/330px-Indigo_skeletal.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Indigo_skeletal.svg/440px-Indigo_skeletal.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="287" data-file-height="160" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Indigo_skeletal.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chemical structure of <a href="/wiki/Indigo_dye" title="Indigo dye">indigo dye</a>, the blue of blue jeans.</div></div></div>
<p>Traditionally, jeans were <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">dyed</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Blue" title="Blue">blue</a> color using natural <a href="/wiki/Indigo_dye" title="Indigo dye">indigo dye</a>. Most denim is now dyed using synthetic indigo. Approximately 20&#160;thousand tons of indigo are produced annually for this purpose, though only a few grams of the dye are required for each pair.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> For other colors of denim other dyes must be used. Currently, jeans are produced in any color that can be achieved with cotton.
</p><p>For more information on dyeing, refer to <a href="/wiki/Denim" title="Denim">denim</a> and the discussion there of using pigment dyes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-shrinking">Pre-shrinking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pre-shrinking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Group_at_Piazza_del_Popolo,_Rome.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Group_at_Piazza_del_Popolo%2C_Rome.jpg/220px-Group_at_Piazza_del_Popolo%2C_Rome.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Group_at_Piazza_del_Popolo%2C_Rome.jpg/330px-Group_at_Piazza_del_Popolo%2C_Rome.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Group_at_Piazza_del_Popolo%2C_Rome.jpg/440px-Group_at_Piazza_del_Popolo%2C_Rome.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3137" data-file-height="2084" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Group_at_Piazza_del_Popolo,_Rome.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Young people wearing a variety of jean styles, including <a href="/wiki/Carpenter_jeans" title="Carpenter jeans">carpenter jeans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bootcut_jeans" class="mw-redirect" title="Bootcut jeans">bootcut jeans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Drainpipe_jeans" class="mw-redirect" title="Drainpipe jeans">drainpipe jeans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Low-rise_jeans" class="mw-redirect" title="Low-rise jeans">lowrise jeans</a>. (Rome, 2008)</div></div></div>
<p>In 1962 Levi Strauss introduced pre-shrunk jeans, which did not shrink further after purchase, allowing the consumer to purchase a correctly fitting size.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> These jeans were known as the 505 regular fit jeans. The 505 are almost identical to the 501s with the exception of the button-fly. The Levi's Corporation also introduced a slim boot-cut fit known as 517 and 527. The difference between the two is the 517s sit at the waist line and the 527s sit below the waist line. Later, Levi's would develop other styles and fits such as the loose, slim, comfort, relaxed, skinny, and a regular fit with a tapered leg.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Used_and_distressed_looks">Used and distressed looks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Used and distressed looks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Reagan_with_horse.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Reagan_with_horse.jpg/200px-Reagan_with_horse.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="309" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Reagan_with_horse.jpg/300px-Reagan_with_horse.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Reagan_with_horse.jpg/400px-Reagan_with_horse.jpg 2x" data-file-width="495" data-file-height="764" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Reagan_with_horse.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> wearing stonewash denim associated with <a href="/wiki/Western_clothing" class="mw-redirect" title="Western clothing">Western clothing</a>, 1970s</div></div></div>
<p>The used or "<a href="/wiki/Stone_washing" title="Stone washing">acid wash</a>" look is created by means of abrading the jeans and/or treating them with chemicals, such as acryl resin, phenol, a hypochlorite, potassium permanganate, caustic soda, acids etc.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ripping or <a href="/wiki/Distressing" title="Distressing">distressing</a> of jeans, though also arising naturally as a result of wear and tear, is sometimes deliberately performed by suppliers - with distressed clothing sometimes selling for more than a nondistressed pair. For example, <a href="/wiki/Emilio_Pucci" title="Emilio Pucci">Pucci</a> sold "embellished mid-rise boyfriend jeans" for £600 (US$860).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sandblasting_or_abrading_with_sandpaper">Sandblasting or abrading with sandpaper</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sandblasting or abrading with sandpaper">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Consumers wanting jeans that appear worn can buy jeans that have been specially treated. To give the fabrics the worn look, <a href="/wiki/Abrasive_blasting" title="Abrasive blasting">sandblasting done with chemicals or by adding pumice stone to the washing process</a> or abrading with sandpaper is often done.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_and_humanitarian_impact">Environmental and humanitarian impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Environmental and humanitarian impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A typical pair of blue jeans uses 919 gallons (3479 liters) of water during its life cycle. This includes the water to irrigate the cotton crop, manufacture the jeans, and the numerous washes by the consumer.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The production of jeans with a "used look" can be more environmentally damaging than regular jeans,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> depending on how the waste compounds are processed. Sandblasting and treating with sandpaper has the risk of causing <a href="/wiki/Silicosis" title="Silicosis">silicosis</a> to the workers, and in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, more than 5,000 textile workers have been stricken with this disease, and 46 people are known to have died. Some companies have announced they are banning the use of sandblasting.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Care_and_wear">Care and wear</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Care and wear">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite most jeans being "pre-shrunk", they are still sensitive to slight further shrinkage and loss of color from being washed. The Levi Strauss company recommends avoiding washing jeans as much as possible. Carl Chiara, Levi Strauss director of brand and special projects, has a credo: The less you wash your jeans, the better your jeans become.<sup id="cite_ref-Wash_My_Jeans?_Hardly_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wash_My_Jeans?_Hardly-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> These and other suggestions to avoid washing jeans where possible have encountered criticism. Cory Warren, editor of <i>LS&amp;Co. Unzipped</i>, clarifies in a response to such a criticism:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Our advice is to wash less often, but clearly, you have to judge for yourself what's appropriate. Hot day, dirty job? Wash your jeans. Please! Cold day, office job? Maybe you can wear them twice or more before they go back to the washing machine. Personally, if I wear a pair of jeans to work on Friday—cool climate, office job—I tend to wear them on Saturday. And if Saturday is spent indoors and I'm not spilling food all over myself, I might even wear them on Sunday.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite>Corey Warren<sup id="cite_ref-Wash_My_Jeans?_Hardly_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wash_My_Jeans?_Hardly-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>For those who prefer to refrain from washing their jeans there have been suggestions to freeze them in order to kill the germs that cause odor. However, this advice has been disputed as ineffective and replaced with the suggestion of baking them for ten minutes at 250 degrees Fahrenheit.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> (120&#160;°C).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Jeans_in_the_law">Jeans in the law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Jeans in the law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Jeans are covered under laws regarding <a href="/wiki/Trousers" title="Trousers">trousers</a>. As well, there have been some notable legal cases involving jeans specifically:
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, in 1992, a 45-year-old driving instructor was accused of rape. When he picked up an 18-year-old girl for her first driving lesson, he allegedly raped her for an hour, then told her that if she was to tell anyone he would kill her. Later that night she told her parents and her parents agreed to help her press charges. While the alleged rapist was convicted and sentenced, the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Cassation_(Italy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Cassation (Italy)">Italian Supreme Court</a> overturned the conviction in 1998 because the victim wore tight jeans. It was argued that she must have necessarily had to help her attacker remove her jeans, thus making the act <a href="/wiki/Consensual" class="mw-redirect" title="Consensual">consensual</a> ("because the victim wore very, very tight jeans, she had to help him remove them ... and by removing the jeans ... it was no longer rape but consensual sex"). The Italian Supreme Court stated in its decision "it is a fact of common experience that it is nearly impossible to slip off tight jeans even partly without the active collaboration of the person who is wearing them."<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> This ruling sparked widespread feminist protest. The day after the decision, women in the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Parliament" title="Italian Parliament">Italian Parliament</a> protested by wearing jeans and holding placards that read "Jeans: An Alibi for Rape". As a sign of support, the California Senate and Assembly followed suit. Soon Patricia Giggans, Executive Director of the Los Angeles Commission on Assaults Against Women, (now Peace Over Violence) made <a href="/wiki/Denim_Day" title="Denim Day">Denim Day</a> an annual event. As of 2011<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup> at least 20 U.S. states officially recognize Denim Day in April. Wearing jeans on this day has become an international symbol of protest against such attitudes about sexual assault. As of 2008 the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Cassation_(Italy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Court of Cassation (Italy)">Italian Supreme Court</a> has overturned their findings, and there is no longer a "denim" defense to the charge of rape.
</p><p>In 2014, an Indian family court in <a href="/wiki/Mumbai" title="Mumbai">Mumbai</a> ruled that a husband objecting to his wife wearing a <a href="/wiki/Kurta" title="Kurta">kurta</a> and jeans and forcing her to wear a <a href="/wiki/Sari" title="Sari">sari</a> amounts to cruelty inflicted by the husband and can be a ground to seek divorce.<sup id="cite_ref-gulfnews1_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gulfnews1-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The wife was thus granted a divorce on the ground of cruelty as defined under section 27(1)(d) of Special Marriage Act, 1954.<sup id="cite_ref-gulfnews1_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gulfnews1-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trends">Trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Worldwide_market_for_jeans">Worldwide market for jeans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Worldwide market for jeans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>North America accounts for 39% of global purchases for jeans, followed by <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a> at 20%, Japan and Korea at 10% and the rest of the world at 31%.<sup id="cite_ref-worldwide_denim_market_report_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-worldwide_denim_market_report-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>United States consumers spent more than US$14 billion on jeans in 2004 and US$15 billion in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-Sullivan_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sullivan-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> US consumers bought US$13.8 billion of men's and women's jeans in the year that ended 30 April 2011, according to market-research firm NPD Group.<sup id="cite_ref-WSJ_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WSJ-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jeans_in_the_USSR">Jeans in the USSR</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Jeans in the USSR">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="box-Expand_section plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg" class="image"><img alt="[icon]" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" decoding="async" width="20" height="14" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="44" data-file-height="31" /></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">July 2015</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, jeans were the symbol of the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western</a> way of life. The "jeans fever" in the USSR started in 1957 during the <a href="/wiki/World_Festival_of_Youth_and_Students" title="World Festival of Youth and Students">World Festival of Youth and Students</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rua_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rua-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>  According to a 1961 Russian textile dictionary, jeans were initially referred to as a "worker's uniform" (рабочий костюм, <i>rabochii kostyum</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The jeans brand Rokotov and Fainberg is named after the defendants in the <a href="/wiki/Rokotov%E2%80%93Faibishenko_case" title="Rokotov–Faibishenko case">Rokotov–Faibishenko case</a>, who were executed for, among other things, trafficking in jeans.<sup id="cite_ref-rua_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rua-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although not outright banned, jeans were hard to come by in USSR. It was seen as a symbol of rebellion by the Soviet youth who wanted to emulate the style of film and rock stars of the West. The <a href="/wiki/Soviet_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Soviet government">Soviet government</a> resisted supplying the market with jeans as that would mean responding to the <a href="/wiki/Market_(economics)" title="Market (economics)">market</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Capitalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Capitalist">capitalist</a> principle.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> People went to great lengths, sometimes resorting to violence and other illegal activities to obtain real Western made jeans. This led to the creation of <a href="/wiki/Black_market" title="Black market">black markets</a> and <a href="/wiki/Counterfeit_consumer_goods" title="Counterfeit consumer goods">bootlegging</a> of jeans, which since has become an important cultural element in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="History of the Soviet Union">history of the Soviet Union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Market-share_shift_to_activewear">Market-share shift to activewear</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Market-share shift to activewear">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2014, teens were buying more <a href="/wiki/Fashion" title="Fashion">fashion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Athleisure" title="Athleisure">athleisure</a> clothing  from brands such as <a href="/wiki/Nike,_Inc." title="Nike, Inc.">Nike</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lululemon" class="mw-redirect" title="Lululemon">Lululemon</a> over denim classics from brands like <a href="/wiki/Abercrombie_%26_Fitch" title="Abercrombie &amp; Fitch">Abercrombie &amp; Fitch</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Activewear" class="mw-redirect" title="Activewear">Activewear</a> in 2014 comprised 28% of teens' apparel purchases, up from 6% in 2008. In 2014, Nike, Lululemon, <a href="/wiki/Under_Armour" title="Under Armour">Under Armour</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Adidas" title="Adidas">Adidas</a> were the most popular brands for athletic apparel among teen consumers. Fashion retailers have begun to adjust their offerings accordingly.  Bloomberg reports that Levi's, which is the world's most iconic denim company, stuck to its core product (denim) instead of adapting to consumer trends. As a result, Levi's sales decreased from over USD 7 billion to USD 4.8 billion in 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distressed_jeans">Distressed jeans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Distressed jeans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Recording_artist_Leigh_Jones_entertained_troops_at_Fort_Belvoir_090501_(4920457551).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Recording_artist_Leigh_Jones_entertained_troops_at_Fort_Belvoir_090501_%284920457551%29.jpg/170px-Recording_artist_Leigh_Jones_entertained_troops_at_Fort_Belvoir_090501_%284920457551%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="258" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Recording_artist_Leigh_Jones_entertained_troops_at_Fort_Belvoir_090501_%284920457551%29.jpg/255px-Recording_artist_Leigh_Jones_entertained_troops_at_Fort_Belvoir_090501_%284920457551%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8f/Recording_artist_Leigh_Jones_entertained_troops_at_Fort_Belvoir_090501_%284920457551%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="285" data-file-height="432" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Recording_artist_Leigh_Jones_entertained_troops_at_Fort_Belvoir_090501_(4920457551).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ripped jeans</div></div></div>
<p>Distressed denim emerged from the cultural <a href="/wiki/Punk_subculture" title="Punk subculture">punk</a> movement in the 1970s. Early punks tore apart consumer goods as an expression of their anger towards society.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Johnny Rotten of the <a href="/wiki/Sex_Pistols" title="Sex Pistols">Sex Pistols</a> manifested the British punk ideology, which was fighting against the status quo. Denim became a key target of this politically fueled deconstruction, with both men and women donning torn pants and jackets, accessorized with safety pins and slogans. The trend became popular again in the 1990s with the emergence of <a href="/wiki/Grunge" title="Grunge">grunge</a> fashion. If punk was "anti-fashion", grunge was "non-fashion". The grunge youth wore loose-fitting ripped jeans, flannel shirts or woolen <a href="/wiki/Pendleton_Woolen_Mills" title="Pendleton Woolen Mills">Pendletons</a> layered over T-shirts. Their anti-conformist approach to fashion led to the popularization of the casual chic look, a trend which continued into the 2000s.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Low-rise_jeans">Low-rise jeans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jeans&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Low-rise jeans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Media reported in 2017 that the trend of <a href="/wiki/Low-rise_pants" title="Low-rise pants">low-rise jeans</a>, famous in 1990s and 2000s as <a href="/wiki/Sagging_(fashion)" title="Sagging (fashion)">sagging</a>, was coming back in fashion due to celebrities like <a href="/wiki/Justin_Bieber" title="Justin Bieber">Justin Bieber</a> endorsing it.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
Low-rise jeans are usually worn 2-3 inches or more below the navel.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
