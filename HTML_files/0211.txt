<p><b>Independence</b> is a condition of a <a href="/wiki/Person" title="Person">person</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nation" title="Nation">nation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">country</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">state</a> in which its residents and population, or some portion thereof, exercise <a href="/wiki/Self-government" class="mw-redirect" title="Self-government">self-government</a>, and usually <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a>, over the territory. The opposite of independence is the status of a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dependent_territory.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dependent territory. (page does not exist)">dependent territory.</a>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_of_independence">Definition of independence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition of independence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Whether the attainment of independence is different from <a href="/wiki/Revolution" title="Revolution">revolution</a> has long been contested, and has often been debated over the question of <a href="/wiki/Violence" title="Violence">violence</a> as <a href="/wiki/Legitimacy_(family_law)" title="Legitimacy (family law)">legitimate</a> means to achieving sovereignty.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In general, revolutions aim only to redistribute power with or without an element of emancipation, such as in <a href="/wiki/Democratization" title="Democratization">democratization</a> <i>within</i> a state, which as such may remain unaltered. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Revolution" title="Mexican Revolution">Mexican Revolution</a> (1917) chiefly refers to a multi-factional conflict that eventually led to a <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Mexico" title="Constitution of Mexico">new constitution</a>; it has rarely been used to refer to the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_War_of_Independence" title="Mexican War of Independence">armed struggle</a> (1821) against Spain. However, some <a href="/wiki/War_of_independence" title="War of independence">wars of independence</a> have been described as revolutions, such as the ones in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">United States</a> (1783) and <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_National_Revolution" title="Indonesian National Revolution">Indonesia</a> (1949), while some revolutions that were specifically about a change in the political structure have resulted in breakaway states. <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>, for example, gained their independence during the revolutions occurring in <a href="/wiki/Xinhai_Revolution" title="Xinhai Revolution">China</a> (1911) and <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">Russia</a> (1917) respectively. Causes for a country or province wishing to seek independence are many, but most can be summed up as a feeling of inequality compared to the dominant power. The means can extend from peaceful demonstrations like in the case of <a href="/wiki/Indian_independence_movement" title="Indian independence movement">India</a> (1947), to a <a href="/wiki/Wars_of_national_liberation" title="Wars of national liberation">violent war</a> like in the case of <a href="/wiki/Algerian_War" title="Algerian War">Algeria</a> (1962).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distinction_between_independence_and_autonomy">Distinction between independence and autonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distinction between independence and autonomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Autonomous_entity" class="mw-redirect" title="Autonomous entity">Autonomy</a></i> refers to a kind of independence which has been granted by an overseeing <a href="/wiki/Authority" title="Authority">authority</a> that itself still retains ultimate authority over that territory (see <a href="/wiki/Devolution" title="Devolution">Devolution</a>). A <a href="/wiki/Protectorate" title="Protectorate">protectorate</a> refers to an autonomous region that depends upon a larger government for its protection as an autonomous region.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Declarations_of_independence">Declarations of independence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Declarations of independence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sometimes, a state wishing to achieve independence from a dominating power will issue a <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_independence" title="Declaration of independence">declaration of independence</a>; the earliest surviving example is <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_Arbroath" title="Declaration of Arbroath">Declaration of Arbroath</a> in 1320, with the most recent example being <a href="/wiki/Azawad" title="Azawad">Azawad</a>'s declaration of independence in 2012. Declaring independence and attaining it however, are quite different. A well-known successful example is the <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_Independence_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Declaration of Independence (United States)">U.S. Declaration of Independence</a> issued in 1776. The dates of established independence (or, less commonly, the commencement of revolution), are typically celebrated as a national <a href="/wiki/Public_holiday" title="Public holiday">holiday</a> known as an <a href="/wiki/Independence_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Independence Day">independence day</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_overview">Historical overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Independence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Historical overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Historically, there have been three major periods of declaring independence:
</p>
<ul><li>from the 1770s, beginning with the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a> through the 1830s, when the last royalist bastions fell at the close of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_American_wars_of_independence" title="Spanish American wars of independence">Spanish American wars of independence</a>;</li>
<li>the immediate aftermath of the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a> following the breakup of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Austro-Hungarian Empire">Austro-Hungarian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian</a>, <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">German</a> empires;</li>
<li>and 1945 to circa 1979, when seventy newly independent states emerged from the European <a href="/wiki/Colonial_empire" title="Colonial empire">colonial empires</a> and collapsed <a href="/wiki/III_Reich" class="mw-redirect" title="III Reich">nazi German Reich</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
