	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Sobriquet</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>sobriquet</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="/oʊ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;code&#39;">oʊ</span><span title="&#39;b&#39; in &#39;buy&#39;">b</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/eɪ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;face&#39;">eɪ</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">SOH</span>-bri-kay</i></a>) or <b>soubriquet</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nickname</a>, sometimes assumed, but often given by another and being descriptive in nature. Distinct from a <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a>, it typically is a familiar name used in place of a real name without the need of explanation, often becoming more familiar than the original name.
</p><p>The term <i>sobriquet</i> may apply to the nickname for a specific person, group of people, or place. Examples are Emiye Menelik, a name of Emperor <a href="/wiki/Menelik_II" title="Menelik II">Menelik II</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, who was popularly and affectionately recognized for his kindness ("emiye" means "mother" in <a href="/wiki/Amharic" title="Amharic">Amharic</a>); <a href="/wiki/Genghis_Khan" title="Genghis Khan">Genghis Khan</a>, who now is rarely recognized by his original name Temüjin; and Mohandas Gandhi, who is better known as <a href="/wiki/Mahatma_Gandhi" title="Mahatma Gandhi">Mahatma Gandhi</a> ("mahatma" means "great soul" in <a href="/wiki/Hindi_(language)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindi (language)">Hindi</a>). Well-known places often have sobriquets, such as <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, often referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Big_Apple" title="Big Apple">Big Apple</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sobriquet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The modern <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> spelling is <i lang="fr" title="French language text">sobriquet</i>. Two early variants of the term are found: <i lang="fr" title="French language text">soubriquet</i> and <i lang="fr" title="French language text">sotbriquet</i>. The first early spelling variant, "soubriquet", remains in use and is considered the likely origin. 
</p><p>The second early spelling variant suggests derivation from the initial form <i lang="fr" title="French language text">sot</i>, foolish, and the second part, <i lang="fr" title="French language text">briquet</i>, is a French adaptation of Italian <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">brichetto</i>, diminutive of <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">bricco</i>, knave, possibly connected with <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">briccone</i>, rogue, which is supposed to be a derivative of the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i lang="de" title="German language text">brechen</i>, to break; but the <a href="/wiki/Philologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Philologist">philologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Walter_William_Skeat" title="Walter William Skeat">Walter William Skeat</a> considers this spelling to be an example of <a href="/wiki/False_etymology" title="False etymology">false etymology</a>. The real origin is to be sought in the form <i lang="fr" title="French language text">soubriquet</i>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Littr%C3%A9" title="Émile Littré">Émile Littré</a> gives an early-fourteenth-century <i>soubsbriquet</i> as meaning a chuck under the chin, and this would be derived from <i>soubs</i>, mod. <i>sous</i> (<a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">sub</i>), under, and <i>briquet</i> or <i>bruchel</i>, the brisket, or lower part of the throat.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use">Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sobriquet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sobriquets often are found in music, sports, and politics. Candidates and political figures often are branded with sobriquets, either while living or posthumously. For example, <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">president of the United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a> came to be known as "Honest Abe".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/A_Dictionary_of_Modern_English_Usage" title="A Dictionary of Modern English Usage">A Dictionary of Modern English Usage</a></i> (1926) <a href="/wiki/Henry_Watson_Fowler" title="Henry Watson Fowler">Henry Watson Fowler</a> warned: "Now the sobriquet habit is not a thing to be acquired, but a thing to be avoided; &amp; the selection that follows is compiled for the purpose not of assisting but of discouraging it." He included the sobriquet among what he termed the "battered ornaments" of the language, but opinion on their use varies. Sobriquets remain a common feature of speech today.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sobriquet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>The King (of rock and roll) <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a>, famous, very well-known vocalist and musician.</li>
<li>Atatürk – <a href="/wiki/Mustafa_Kemal_Atat%C3%BCrk" title="Mustafa Kemal Atatürk">Mustafa Kemal</a>, first president of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Turkey" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of Turkey">Republic of Turkey</a>. <a href="/wiki/Mustafa_Kemal_Atat%C3%BCrk%27s_personal_life#Name" title="Mustafa Kemal Atatürk&#39;s personal life">Name bestowed on him</a> 24 November 1934 by the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_Grand_National_Assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="Turkish Grand National Assembly">Turkish Grand National Assembly</a>.</li>
<li>Banker to the Poor<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_Profile_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_Profile-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> – <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Yunus" title="Muhammad Yunus">Muhammad Yunus</a>, 2006 Nobel Peace Prize winner and managing director of <a href="/wiki/Grameen_Bank" title="Grameen Bank">Grameen Bank</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Apple" class="mw-redirect" title="The Big Apple">The Big Apple</a> – <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Blighty" title="Blighty">Blighty</a> – Great Britain (used by British servicemen abroad and expatriates)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Columbia_(name)" title="Columbia (name)">Columbia</a> – The United States or The <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>, poetic name</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dixie" title="Dixie">Dixie</a>, Dixieland – (from the <a href="/wiki/Mason%E2%80%93Dixon_line" title="Mason–Dixon line">Mason–Dixon line</a>); the eleven Southern states that seceded and fought against the U.S. in the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>; still used affectionately by Southerners</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Estate" title="Fourth Estate">Fourth Estate</a> – <a href="/wiki/Journalists" class="mw-redirect" title="Journalists">the press</a></li></ul>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Uncle_Sam" title="Uncle Sam">Uncle Sam</a> – the U.S. in general or specifically, its government (from the initials "U.S.")</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_war_to_end_all_wars" class="mw-redirect" title="The war to end all wars">The war to end all wars</a> – <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Origin_of_Chicago%27s_%22Windy_City%22_nickname" class="mw-redirect" title="Origin of Chicago&#39;s &quot;Windy City&quot; nickname">The Windy City</a> – Chicago, Illinois (also <a href="/wiki/Windy_City_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Windy City (disambiguation)">various other cities</a> including Wellington, New Zealand and Port Elizabeth, South Africa)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yankee" title="Yankee">Yankee</a> (or "Yank" for short) – an American of European ancestry not sympathetic to the Southern cause, originally only from the states that fought against the Confederacy in the Civil War, but now from any non-Southern state; used outside the U.S. to mean any American; sometimes derogatory in either usage</li></ul>
