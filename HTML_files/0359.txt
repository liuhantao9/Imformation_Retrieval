	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Bauxite</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>Bauxite</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">sedimentary rock</a> with a relatively high <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> content. It is the world's main source of aluminium. Bauxite consists mostly of the aluminium <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a> <a href="/wiki/Gibbsite" title="Gibbsite">gibbsite</a> (Al(OH)<sub>3</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Boehmite" title="Boehmite">boehmite</a> (γ-AlO(OH)) and <a href="/wiki/Diaspore" title="Diaspore">diaspore</a> (α-AlO(OH)), <a href="/wiki/Mixture" title="Mixture">mixed</a> with the two <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">iron oxides</a> <a href="/wiki/Goethite" title="Goethite">goethite</a> (FeO(OH)) and <a href="/wiki/Haematite" class="mw-redirect" title="Haematite">haematite</a> (Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>), the aluminium <a href="/wiki/Clay_minerals" title="Clay minerals">clay mineral</a> <a href="/wiki/Kaolinite" title="Kaolinite">kaolinite</a> (Al<sub>2</sub>Si<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>(OH)) and small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Anatase" title="Anatase">anatase</a> (TiO<sub>2</sub>) and <a href="/wiki/Ilmenite" title="Ilmenite">ilmenite</a> (FeTiO<sub>3</sub> or FeO.TiO<sub>2</sub>).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1821 the <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a> <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Berthier" title="Pierre Berthier">Pierre Berthier</a> discovered bauxite near the village of <a href="/wiki/Les_Baux-de-Provence" title="Les Baux-de-Provence">Les Baux</a> in <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">Provence</a>, southern <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary,_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="Berthier (1821) only claims in his own research article about his find of bauxite at Les Beaux in France:">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bauxite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bauxite_with_unweathered_rock_core._C_021.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Bauxite_with_unweathered_rock_core._C_021.jpg/220px-Bauxite_with_unweathered_rock_core._C_021.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Bauxite_with_unweathered_rock_core._C_021.jpg/330px-Bauxite_with_unweathered_rock_core._C_021.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Bauxite_with_unweathered_rock_core._C_021.jpg/440px-Bauxite_with_unweathered_rock_core._C_021.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1492" data-file-height="968" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bauxite_with_unweathered_rock_core._C_021.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bauxite with core of unweathered rock</div></div></div>
<p>Numerous classification schemes have been proposed for bauxite but, as of 1982<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bauxite&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, there was no consensus.<sup id="cite_ref-BatdossyKarst1982_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BatdossyKarst1982-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Vadász (1951) distinguished <a href="/wiki/Laterite" title="Laterite">lateritic</a> bauxites (silicate bauxites) from <a href="/wiki/Karst" title="Karst">karst</a> bauxite <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ores</a> (carbonate bauxites):<sup id="cite_ref-BatdossyKarst1982_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BatdossyKarst1982-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The carbonate bauxites occur predominantly in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> above <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_rock" title="Carbonate rock">carbonate rocks</a> (<a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dolomite_(mineral)" title="Dolomite (mineral)">dolomite</a>), where they were formed by lateritic <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">weathering</a> and residual accumulation of intercalated <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> layers – dispersed clays which were concentrated as the enclosing limestones gradually dissolved during <a href="/wiki/Chemical_weathering" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical weathering">chemical weathering</a>.</li>
<li>The lateritic bauxites are found mostly in the countries of the <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropics</a>.  They were formed by <a href="/wiki/Lateritization" class="mw-redirect" title="Lateritization">lateritization</a> of various <a href="/wiki/Silicate#Silicate_rock" title="Silicate">silicate rocks</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gneiss" title="Gneiss">gneiss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">basalt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syenite" title="Syenite">syenite</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">shale</a>.  In comparison with the iron-rich laterites, the formation of bauxites depends even more on intense weathering conditions in a location with very good drainage. This enables the dissolution of the <a href="/wiki/Kaolinite" title="Kaolinite">kaolinite</a> and the precipitation of the <a href="/wiki/Gibbsite" title="Gibbsite">gibbsite</a>.  Zones with highest aluminium content are frequently located below a <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxide" title="Iron oxide">ferruginous</a> surface layer.  The <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_hydroxide" title="Aluminium hydroxide">aluminium hydroxide</a> in the lateritic bauxite deposits is almost exclusively gibbsite.</li></ul>
<p>In the case of Jamaica, recent analysis of the soils showed elevated levels of <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a>, suggesting that the bauxite originates from recent <a href="/wiki/Miocene" title="Miocene">Miocene</a> <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_ash" title="Volcanic ash">ash</a> deposits from episodes of significant volcanism in Central America.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_trends">Production trends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bauxite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Production trends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_bauxite_production" title="List of countries by bauxite production">List of countries by bauxite production</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2005bauxite.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/2005bauxite.png/400px-2005bauxite.png" decoding="async" width="400" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/2005bauxite.png/600px-2005bauxite.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/2005bauxite.png/800px-2005bauxite.png 2x" data-file-width="1425" data-file-height="625" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2005bauxite.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bauxite output in 2005</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Weipa-bauxite-mine.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Weipa-bauxite-mine.jpg/220px-Weipa-bauxite-mine.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d3/Weipa-bauxite-mine.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="314" data-file-height="209" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Weipa-bauxite-mine.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>One of the world's largest bauxite mines in <a href="/wiki/Weipa,_Queensland" title="Weipa, Queensland">Weipa</a>, Australia</div></div></div>
<p>Australia is the largest producer of bauxite, followed by China.<sup id="cite_ref-usgs_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usgs-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In 2017, China was the top producer of aluminium with almost half of the world's production, followed by Russia, Canada, and India.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Although aluminium demand is rapidly increasing, known reserves of its bauxite ore are sufficient to meet the worldwide demands for aluminium for many centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  Increased <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_recycling" title="Aluminium recycling">aluminium recycling</a>, which has the advantage of lowering the cost in <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electric power</a> in producing aluminium, will considerably extend the world's bauxite reserves.
</p>

<p>Bauxite is usually <a href="/wiki/Surface_mining" title="Surface mining">strip mined</a> because it is almost always found near the surface of the terrain, with little or no <a href="/wiki/Overburden" title="Overburden">overburden</a>. As of 2010<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bauxite&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, approximately 70% to 80% of the world's dry bauxite production is processed first into <a href="/wiki/Alumina" class="mw-redirect" title="Alumina">alumina</a> and then into aluminium by <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Bauxite rocks are typically classified according to their intended commercial application: metallurgical, abrasive, cement, chemical, and refractory.
</p><p>Usually, bauxite ore is heated in a pressure vessel along with a <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> solution at a temperature of 150 to 200&#160;°C (300 to 390&#160;°F). At these temperatures, the <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> is dissolved as <a href="/wiki/Sodium_aluminate" title="Sodium aluminate">sodium aluminate</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Bayer_process" title="Bayer process">Bayer process</a>). The aluminium compounds in the bauxite may be present as <a href="/wiki/Gibbsite" title="Gibbsite">gibbsite</a>(Al(OH)<sub>3</sub>), <a href="/wiki/Boehmite" title="Boehmite">boehmite</a>(AlOOH) or <a href="/wiki/Diaspore" title="Diaspore">diaspore</a>(AlOOH); the different forms of the aluminium component will dictate the extraction conditions. The undissolved waste, <a href="/wiki/Bauxite_tailings" title="Bauxite tailings">bauxite tailings</a>, after the aluminium compounds are extracted contains <a href="/wiki/Iron_oxides" class="mw-redirect" title="Iron oxides">iron oxides</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silica" class="mw-redirect" title="Silica">silica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calcia" class="mw-redirect" title="Calcia">calcia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Titanium_dioxide" title="Titanium dioxide">titania</a> and some un-reacted <a href="/wiki/Alumina" class="mw-redirect" title="Alumina">alumina</a>. After separation of the residue by filtering, pure gibbsite is precipitated when the liquid is cooled, and then seeded with fine-grained aluminium hydroxide. The gibbsite is usually converted into <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">aluminium oxide</a>, Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, by heating in rotary kilns or fluid flash calciners to a temperature in excess of 1,000&#160;°C (1,830&#160;°F). This aluminium oxide is dissolved at a temperature of about 960&#160;°C (1,760&#160;°F) in molten <a href="/wiki/Cryolite" title="Cryolite">cryolite</a>. Next, this molten substance can yield metallic aluminium by passing an <a href="/wiki/Electric_current" title="Electric current">electric current</a> through it in the process of electrolysis, which is called the <a href="/wiki/Hall%E2%80%93H%C3%A9roult_process" title="Hall–Héroult process">Hall–Héroult process</a>, named after its American and French discoverers.
</p><p>Prior to the invention of this process, and prior to the <a href="/wiki/Deville_process" title="Deville process">Deville process</a>, aluminium ore was refined by heating ore along with elemental <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">sodium</a> or <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">potassium</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">vacuum</a>. The method was complicated and consumed materials that were themselves expensive at that time. This made early elemental aluminium more expensive than <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Source_of_gallium">Source of gallium</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bauxite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Source of gallium">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bauxite is the main source of the rare metal <a href="/wiki/Gallium" title="Gallium">gallium</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the processing of bauxite to <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">alumina</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Bayer_process" title="Bayer process">Bayer process</a>, gallium accumulates in the <a href="/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide" title="Sodium hydroxide">sodium hydroxide</a> liquor. From this it can be extracted by a variety of methods. The most recent is the use of <a href="/wiki/Ion-exchange_resin" title="Ion-exchange resin">ion-exchange resin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Achievable extraction efficiencies critically depend on the original concentration in the feed bauxite. At a typical feed concentration of 50 ppm, about 15 percent of the contained gallium is extractable.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The remainder reports to the <a href="/wiki/Red_mud" class="mw-redirect" title="Red mud">red mud</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_hydroxide" title="Aluminium hydroxide">aluminium hydroxide</a> streams.<sup id="cite_ref-Moskalyk_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moskalyk-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
