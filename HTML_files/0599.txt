<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bauxite">Bauxite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economy_of_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Bauxite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:En_explore_explore_tree_map_hs_export_sur_all_show_2012.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/En_explore_explore_tree_map_hs_export_sur_all_show_2012.png/220px-En_explore_explore_tree_map_hs_export_sur_all_show_2012.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/En_explore_explore_tree_map_hs_export_sur_all_show_2012.png/330px-En_explore_explore_tree_map_hs_export_sur_all_show_2012.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/En_explore_explore_tree_map_hs_export_sur_all_show_2012.png/440px-En_explore_explore_tree_map_hs_export_sur_all_show_2012.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="471" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:En_explore_explore_tree_map_hs_export_sur_all_show_2012.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Export Trading Treemap of 2012</div></div></div>
<p>The backbone of the <b>economy of <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a></b> is the export of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium_oxide" title="Aluminium oxide">aluminium oxide</a> (alumina) and small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> produced from <a href="/wiki/Bauxite" title="Bauxite">bauxite</a> mined in the country. In 1999, the <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> <a href="/wiki/Smelter" class="mw-redirect" title="Smelter">smelter</a> at <a href="/wiki/Paranam" title="Paranam">Paranam</a> was closed<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and mining at <a href="/w/index.php?title=Onverdacht&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Onverdacht (page does not exist)">Onverdacht</a> ceased; however, alumina exports accounted for 72% of Suriname's estimated export earnings of <a href="/wiki/US_dollar" class="mw-redirect" title="US dollar">US$</a>496.6 million in 2001. Suriname's bauxite deposits have been among the world's richest.
</p><p>Inexpensive power costs are Suriname's big advantage in the energy-intensive alumina and aluminium business. In the 1960s, the Aluminum Company of America (<a href="/wiki/Alcoa" title="Alcoa">Alcoa</a>) built the US$150-million Afobaka Dam<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> for the production of <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectric" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroelectric">hydroelectric</a> energy. This created the <a href="/wiki/Brokopondo_Reservoir" title="Brokopondo Reservoir">Brokopondo Reservoir</a> a 1,560&#160;km² lake, one of the largest artificial lakes in the world.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="West_Suriname_Plan">West Suriname Plan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economy_of_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: West Suriname Plan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/West_Suriname_Plan" title="West Suriname Plan">West Suriname Plan</a></div>
<p>In 1976–1977, a 100&#160;km long single track <a href="/wiki/Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway">railway</a> was constructed by <a href="/wiki/Washington_Group_International#Morrison_Knudsen_Co." title="Washington Group International">Morrison-Knudsen Co.</a> in West Suriname from the bauxite containing <a href="/wiki/Bakhuis_Mountains" title="Bakhuis Mountains">Bakhuis Mountains</a> to the town of <a href="/wiki/Apoera" title="Apoera">Apoera</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Courantyne_River" title="Courantyne River">Corantijn river</a>, to transport bauxite by river to processing plants elsewhere. The construction of this railway was financially funded by the Dutch government's independence/severance payments after November 25, 1975. After completion of this railway and associated facilities, for political and economical reasons it was never actually used and was left to be overgrown by the <a href="/wiki/Jungle" title="Jungle">jungle</a>. Also plans to construct a dam in the <a href="/wiki/Kabalebo_River" title="Kabalebo River">Kabalebo River</a> were developed but never fully executed.
</p><p>In 1984, <a href="/w/index.php?title=SURALCO&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="SURALCO (page does not exist)">SURALCO</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;&#91;<a href="/w/index.php?title=Suralco:nl&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Suralco:nl (page does not exist)">Suralco</a>&#93;</span>, a subsidiary of <a href="/wiki/Alcoa" title="Alcoa">Alcoa</a>, formed a <a href="/wiki/Joint_venture" title="Joint venture">joint venture</a> with the (at that time) <a href="/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell" title="Royal Dutch Shell">Royal Dutch Shell</a>-owned <a href="/wiki/Billiton" class="mw-redirect" title="Billiton">Billiton</a> Company, which did not process the bauxite it mined in Suriname. Under this agreement, both companies share risks and profits.
</p><p>The major mining sites at <a href="/wiki/Moengo" title="Moengo">Moengo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lelydorp" title="Lelydorp">Lelydorp</a> are maturing, and it is now estimated that their reserves will be depleted by 2006. Other proven reserves exist in the east, west, and north of the country sufficient to last until 2045. However, distance and <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a> make their immediate development costly. In October 2002, Alcoa and <a href="/wiki/BHP_Billiton" class="mw-redirect" title="BHP Billiton">BHP Billiton</a> signed a letter of intent as the basis for new joint ventures between the two companies, in which Alcoa will take part for 55% in all bauxite mining activities in West Suriname. The government and the companies are looking into cost-effective ways to develop the new mines. The preeminence of bauxite and ALCOA's continued presence in Suriname is a key element in the U.S.-Suriname economic relationship.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gold_mining">Gold mining</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economy_of_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Gold mining">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is one large scale gold mine operating in Suriname. This is the <a href="/wiki/Rosebel_Gold_Mine" class="mw-redirect" title="Rosebel Gold Mine">Rosebel Gold Mine</a>. Development of a second large scale mine called the Merian Gold Project was approved by the government of Suriname on June 7, 2013. This mining project would be a partnership of <a href="/wiki/Newmont_Mining_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Newmont Mining Corporation">Newmont Mining Corporation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alcoa_World_Alumina_and_Chemicals" title="Alcoa World Alumina and Chemicals">Alcoa World Alumina and Chemicals</a>. Merian is about 60 kilometres (40&#160;mi) south of the town of <a href="/wiki/Moengo,_Suriname" class="mw-redirect" title="Moengo, Suriname">Moengo</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Maroni_(river)" title="Maroni (river)">Marowijne River</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The government estimates there are another 20,000 small scale operators. Only 115 of these were registered by the government in 2009. The government calls these people porknokkers.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Because of unemployment in Suriname, some local people turn to small, illegal gold mining as their source of incomes. Gold mining has caused environmental damages in the country.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commission_for_the_Ordering_of_the_Gold_Mining_Sector">Commission for the Ordering of the Gold Mining Sector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economy_of_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Commission for the Ordering of the Gold Mining Sector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl><dt>Establishment of Ordening Goudsector (OGS)</dt></dl>
<p>Commission for the Ordering of the Gold Mining Sector (OGS) was established by the government in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> OGS is leading the reform effort to develop sustainable and environmentally responsible gold mining practices and transform informal small-scale gold mining into a viable sub-sector of the mining and national economy of Suriname.<sup id="cite_ref-gov.sr_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gov.sr-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Ban on mercury use in small-scale mining</dt></dl>
<p>Suriname does not produce chemical mercury and only allows mercury imports with a license. Since the 1990s these licenses were not issued anymore. Moreover, all licenses are used for mercury imports for medical use or research. Therefore, trade and import in mercury is illegal.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Mercury is used in the small-scale gold mining because smuggling made mercury available. However people who are caught with mercury in their possession will be judged and/or fined.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dwtonline.com_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dwtonline.com-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Foreign investment</dt></dl>
<p>On April 13, 2013, the government reached an agreement with multinational IAMGOLD to increase investment in Suriname.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Kaloti Mint House Suriname</dt></dl>
<p>On March 1, 2013, Kaloti Mint House Suriname laid its funding stone and is expected to start its refinery production by the first quarter of 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Kaloti Mint House will be instrumental in producing “clean gold” in Suriname.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Kaloti Mint House have been awarded the ISO 9001:2000 certification for gold and bullion manufacturing and ISO 14001 Environmental Certification. The company is presently applying for ISO 14025 for the Assaying of Gold and Silver. Kaloti will focus on melting and producing gold bars to international standards (999.9 purity) for local and international markets.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Minamata Treaty</dt></dl>
<p>In October 2013, the UN wants to adopt the Minamata Treaty to ban the user of mercury altogether in Suriname.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>The School of Mining</dt></dl>
<p>The Government initiated a training unit within the Ordening Goudsector called the School of Mining.<sup id="cite_ref-gov.sr_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gov.sr-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This training unit consists of 14 teachers. The teacher's training started February 2013 and is aimed at preparing them for the fieldwork. The duties of the teachers will be to provide hands-on training on the goldfields to small-scale gold miners. The teachers begin with prospecting and showing the small-scale miners more efficient ways to mine in their areas. Along the way they promote mercury free production methods. The Management of Ordening Goudsector hypothesizes that showing small-scale gold miners the benefits of new production methods will be the incentive itself to start the training programs.
</p><p><sup id="cite_ref-dwtonline.com_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dwtonline.com-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Entrepreneurial Credit Fund</dt></dl>
<p>The Ministry of Finance initiated a credit fund in March 2013 for small and medium scale entrepreneurs. Small-scale miners can become formally verified entrepreneurs as the piece of land will be viewed as a formal “title” by financial institutes. This means the miners will be eligible for credits and thus can acquire credit to upscale their production. A first amount of 35 million SRD (10.69 million USD, as of Monday, Apr 08, 2013, 04:15 PM GMT) is available for credit through the Central Bank.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Mining zones</dt></dl>
<p>The Mining Law says that one can only mine with a license from the Government. Ordening Goudsector regulates the concessionaires, how many machine owners there are and what the movements of small-scale miners are.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> New mining areas are still being issued. However it is important to note that data show that the interesting area to mine, especially for the small-scale miners who look for alluvial gold, is the <a href="/wiki/Greenstone_belt" title="Greenstone belt">Greenstone belt</a>. The greenstone runs from Guyana, through Suriname, into French Guyana. This greenstone belt, however, only counts for 15% of the Surinamese surface. At the moment almost all areas in this belt are already given out in concessions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_aid">Foreign aid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economy_of_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Foreign aid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the return to a more or less democratically elected government in 1991, Dutch aid resumed. The Dutch relationship continues to be an important factor in the economy, with the Dutch insisting that Suriname undertake economic reforms and produce specific plans acceptable to the Dutch for projects on which aid funds could be spent. In 2000, however, the Dutch revised the structure of their aid package and signaled to the Surinamese authorities their decision to disburse aid by sectoral priorities as opposed to individual projects. Although the present government is not in favor of this approach, it has identified sectors and is now working on sectoral analyses to present to the Dutch.
</p><p>After a short respite in 1991–1996, when measures taken in 1993 led to economic stabilization, a relatively stable exchange rate, low <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation</a>, sustainable fiscal policies, and growth, Suriname's economic situation deteriorated from 1996 to the present. This was due in large part to loose fiscal policies of the Wijdenbosch government, which, in the face of lower Dutch development aid, financed its deficit through credit extended by the central bank. As a consequence, the parallel market for foreign exchange soared so that by the end of 1998, the premium of the parallel market rate over the official rate was 85%. Since over 90% of import transactions took place at the parallel rate, inflation took off, with 12-month inflation growing from 0.5% at the end of 1996, to 23% at the end of 1998, and 113% at the end of 1999. The government also instituted a regime of stringent economic controls over prices, the exchange rate, imports, and exports, in an effort to contain the adverse efforts of its economic policies. The cumulative impact of soaring inflation, an unstable exchange rate, and falling real incomes led to a political crisis.
</p><p>Dutch aid stopped to a large extent after <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9si_Bouterse" title="Dési Bouterse">Dési Bouterse</a> was elected president. Aid from <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> has increased.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economy_of_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Suriname elected a new government in May 2000, but until it was replaced, the <a href="/wiki/Wijdenbosch" class="mw-redirect" title="Wijdenbosch">Wijdenbosch</a> government continued its loose fiscal and monetary policies. By the time it left office, the exchange rate in the <a href="/wiki/Parallel_market" class="mw-redirect" title="Parallel market">parallel market</a> had depreciated further, over 10% of <a href="/wiki/GDP" class="mw-redirect" title="GDP">GDP</a> had been borrowed to finance the fiscal deficit, and there was a significant <a href="/wiki/Monetary_overhang" title="Monetary overhang">monetary overhang</a> in the country. The new government dealt with these problems by devaluing the official exchange rate by 88%, eliminating all other exchange rates except the parallel market rate set by the banks and cambios, raising tariffs on water and electricity, and eliminating the <a href="/wiki/Subsidy" title="Subsidy">subsidy</a> on <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>. The new administration also rationalized the extensive list of price controls to 12 basic food items. More important, the government ceased all financing from the <a href="/wiki/Central_bank" title="Central bank">central bank</a>. It is attempting to broaden its economic base, establish better contacts with other nations and international financial institutions, and reduce its dependence on Dutch assistance. However, to date the government has yet to implement an investment law or to begin <a href="/wiki/Privatization" title="Privatization">privatization</a> of any of the 110 parastatal, nor has it given much indication that it has developed a comprehensive plan to develop the economy.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Oil">Oil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economy_of_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Oil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">Oil</a> is a promising sector. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Staatsolie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Staatsolie (page does not exist)">Staatsolie</a>, the state-owned oil company, produced 16,200 barrels (2,580&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) a day in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Staatsolie currently refines 7,350 barrels (1,169&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) a day at Tout Lui Faut in
the District of Wanica and is building more capacity to go to 15,000 barrels (2,400&#160;m<sup>3</sup>) a day.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hardwood">Hardwood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economy_of_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hardwood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some big companies are getting the <a href="/wiki/Hardwood" title="Hardwood">hardwood</a> out of the jungle. However, proposals for exploitation of the country's tropical forests and undeveloped regions of the interior traditionally inhabited by indigenous and <a href="/wiki/Maroon_(people)" title="Maroon (people)">Maroon</a> communities have raised the concerns of environmentalists and <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a> activists both in Suriname and abroad. These opposing parties are not yet strong in Suriname.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Banana">Banana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economy_of_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Banana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>State-owned banana producer <a href="/w/index.php?title=Surland&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Surland (page does not exist)">Surland</a> closed its doors on April 5, 2002, after its inability to meet payroll expenses for the second month in a row; it is still unclear if Surland will survive this crisis.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was remodeled as Stichting Behoud Bananen Sector Suriname (SBBS). The rumour was that because of political differences Surland was manipulated in shutting down. The SBBS has made banana profitable for the first time in 20 years.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_exports">Other exports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economy_of_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other exports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A member of <a href="/wiki/CARICOM" class="mw-redirect" title="CARICOM">CARICOM</a>, Suriname also exports in small numbers <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shrimp" title="Shrimp">shrimp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timber" class="mw-redirect" title="Timber">timber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Banana" title="Banana">bananas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruits</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vegetable" title="Vegetable">vegetables</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Currency">Currency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Economy_of_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Currency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Moreover, in January 2002, the government renegotiated <a href="/wiki/Civil_servant" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil servant">civil servant</a> wages (a significant part of the work force and a significant portion of government expenditure), agreeing to raises as high as 100%. Pending implementation of these wage increases and concerned that the government may be unable to meet these increased expenses, the local currency weakened from Sf 2200 in January 2002 to nearly Sf 2500 in April 2002. On March 26, 2003, the <a href="/wiki/Central_Bank_of_Suriname" title="Central Bank of Suriname">Central Bank of Suriname</a> (CBvS) adjusted the <a href="/wiki/Exchange_rate" title="Exchange rate">exchange rate</a> of the U.S. dollar. This action resulted in further devaluation of the <a href="/wiki/Surinamese_guilder" title="Surinamese guilder">Surinamese guilder</a>. The official exchange rate of the US$ was SF 2,650 for selling and SF 2,600 for purchasing. With the official exchange rate, the CBvS came closer to the exchange rate on the parallel market which sold the U.S. dollar for SF 3,250.
</p><p>From 2011: <a href="/wiki/Surinamese_dollar" title="Surinamese dollar">Surinamese dollar</a> (SRD) = 100 cent; USD 1.00 is ca. SRD 3.20 (May 2011)
</p><p>From 2004 to 2011: <a href="/wiki/Surinamese_dollar" title="Surinamese dollar">Surinamese dollar</a> (SRD) = 100 cent; USD 1.00 is ca. SRD 2.74 (July 2007)
</p><p>Before 2004: <a href="/wiki/Surinamese_gulden" class="mw-redirect" title="Surinamese gulden">Surinamese gulden</a> (SRG) = 100 cent, SRD 1 = SRG 1000; coins had extremely low official value and a much higher collector's value; their official value has now been multiplied by 1000: the value in SRD cent is equal to the former value in SRG cent. The same applies for "currency notes" (SRG 1 and 2.50).
</p><p>Surinamese guilders per US dollar - 2,346.75 (2002), 2,178.5 (2001), 1,322.47 (2000), 859.44 (1999), 401 (1998) <br />
Note: during 1998, the exchange rate splintered into four distinct rates; in January 1999 the government floated the guilder, but subsequently fixed it when the black-market rate plunged; the government then allowed trading within a band of SRG 500 around the official rate
</p>
