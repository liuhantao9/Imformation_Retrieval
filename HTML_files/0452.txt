<p>A <b>hillfort</b> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Earthworks_(engineering)" title="Earthworks (engineering)">earthworks</a> used as a <a href="/wiki/Fortification" title="Fortification">fortified</a> refuge or defended settlement, located to exploit a rise in elevation for defensive advantage. They are typically <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> and of the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Ages</a>. Some were used in the post-<a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> period. The fortification usually follows the contours of a hill, consisting of one or more lines of <a href="/wiki/Earthworks_(Archaeology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Earthworks (Archaeology)">earthworks</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Stockade" title="Stockade">stockades</a> or <a href="/wiki/Defensive_walls" class="mw-redirect" title="Defensive walls">defensive walls</a>, and external ditches. Hillforts developed in the Late Bronze and Early Iron Age, roughly the start of the <a href="/wiki/1st_millennium_BC" title="1st millennium BC">first millennium&#160;BC</a>, and were used in many <a href="/wiki/Celt" class="mw-redirect" title="Celt">Celtic</a> areas of <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">central</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">western Europe</a> until the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> conquest.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hillfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The terms "hill fort", "hill-fort" and "hillfort" are all used in the archaeological literature. They all refer to an elevated site with one or more <a href="/wiki/Rampart_(fortification)" title="Rampart (fortification)">ramparts</a> made of earth, stone and/or wood, with an external ditch. Many small early hillforts were abandoned, with the larger ones being redeveloped at a later date. Some hillforts contain houses.
</p><p>Similar but smaller and less defendable earthworks are found on the sides of hills. These are known as <a href="/wiki/Hill-slope_enclosure" title="Hill-slope enclosure">hill-slope enclosures</a> and may have been <a href="/wiki/Pen_(enclosure)" title="Pen (enclosure)">animal pens</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chronology">Chronology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hillfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chronology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>They are most common during later periods:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Urnfield_culture" title="Urnfield culture">Urnfield culture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Bronze_Age" title="Atlantic Bronze Age">Atlantic Bronze Age</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> (c. 1300 BC – 750 BC) <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hallstatt_culture" title="Hallstatt culture">Hallstatt culture</a> (c. 1200 BC – 500 BC) late Bronze Age to early Iron Age</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/La_T%C3%A8ne_culture" title="La Tène culture">La Tène culture</a> (c. 600 BC – 50 AD) late <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a></li></ul>
<p>Prehistoric Europe saw a growing population. It has been estimated that in about 5000&#160;BC during the Neolithic between 2 million and 5 million lived in Europe; in the Late Iron Age it had an estimated population of around 15 to 30 million. Outside Greece and Italy, which were more densely populated, the vast majority of settlements in the Iron Age were small, with perhaps no more than 50&#160;inhabitants. Hillforts were the exception, and were the home of up to 1,000&#160;people. With the emergence of <i><a href="/wiki/Oppida" class="mw-redirect" title="Oppida">oppida</a></i> in the Late Iron Age, settlements could reach as large as 10,000&#160;inhabitants.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> As the population increased so did the complexity of prehistoric societies. Around 1100&#160;BC hillforts emerged and in the following centuries spread through Europe. They served a range of purposes and were variously tribal centres, defended places, foci of ritual activity, and places of production.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the Hallstatt&#160;C period, hillforts became the dominant settlement type in the west of Hungary.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a> described the large late Iron Age hillforts he encountered during his campaigns in <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a> as <a href="/wiki/Oppidum" title="Oppidum">oppida</a>. By this time the larger ones had become more like cities than fortresses and many were assimilated as Roman towns.
</p><p>Hillforts were frequently occupied by conquering armies, but on other occasions the forts were destroyed, the local people forcibly evicted, and the forts left derelict. For example, <a href="/wiki/Solsbury_Hill" title="Solsbury Hill">Solsbury Hill</a> was sacked and deserted during the <a href="/wiki/Belgic" class="mw-redirect" title="Belgic">Belgic</a> invasions of southern Britain in the 1st century BC. Abandoned forts were sometimes reoccupied and refortified under renewed threat of foreign invasion, such as the Dukes' Wars in <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a>, and the successive invasions of Britain by <a href="/wiki/Roman_conquest_of_Britain" title="Roman conquest of Britain">Romans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saxons#Saxons_in_Britain" title="Saxons">Saxons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vikings#British_Isles" title="Vikings">Vikings</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historiography">Historiography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hillfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Historiography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Excavations at hillforts in the first half of the 20th&#160;century focussed on the defenses, based on the assumption that hillforts were primarily developed for military purposes. The exception to this trend began in the 1930s with a series of excavations undertaken by <a href="/wiki/Mortimer_Wheeler" title="Mortimer Wheeler">Mortimer Wheeler</a> at <a href="/wiki/Maiden_Castle,_Dorset" title="Maiden Castle, Dorset">Maiden Castle, Dorset</a>. From 1960 onwards, archaeologists shifted their attention to the interior of hillforts, re-examining their function.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Currently, <a href="/wiki/Post-processual_archaeology" title="Post-processual archaeology">post-processual archaeologists</a> regard hillforts as symbols of wealth and power.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Michael Avery has stated the traditional view of hillforts by saying, "The ultimate defensive weapon of European prehistory was the hillfort of the first millennium B.C.".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hillfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Beyond the simple definition of <i>hillfort</i>, there is a wide variation in types and periods from the Bronze Age to the Middle Ages.
Here are some considerations of general appearance and topology, which can be assessed without archaeological excavation:
</p>
<ul><li>Location
<ul><li><b>Hilltop Contour</b>: the classic hillfort; an inland location with a hilltop defensive position surrounded by artificial ramparts or steep natural slopes. Examples: <a href="/wiki/Brent_Knoll_Camp" title="Brent Knoll Camp">Brent Knoll</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mount_Ipf" class="mw-redirect" title="Mount Ipf">Mount Ipf</a>.</li>
<li><b>Inland Promontory</b>: an inland defensive position on a ridge or spur with steep slopes on 2 or 3 sides, and artificial ramparts on the level approaches. Example: <a href="/wiki/Lambert%27s_Castle" title="Lambert&#39;s Castle">Lambert's Castle</a>.</li>
<li><b>Interfluvial</b>: a promontory above the confluence of two rivers, or in the bend of a <a href="/wiki/Meander" title="Meander">meander</a>. Examples: <a href="/wiki/Alcimoennis" title="Alcimoennis">Kelheim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miholjanec" title="Miholjanec">Miholjanec</a>.</li>
<li><b>Lowland</b>: an inland location without special defensive advantages (except perhaps marshes), but surrounded by artificial ramparts; typical of later settled <i>oppida</i>. Examples: <a href="/wiki/Maiden_Castle,_Dorset" title="Maiden Castle, Dorset">Maiden Castle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Old_Oswestry" title="Old Oswestry">Old Oswestry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stonea_Camp" title="Stonea Camp">Stonea Camp</a>.</li>
<li><b>Sea Cliff</b>: a semi-circular crescent of ramparts backing on to a straight sea cliff; common on rocky Atlantic coasts, such as Ireland and Wales. Examples: <a href="/wiki/Daw%27s_Castle" title="Daw&#39;s Castle">Daw's Castle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dinas_Dinlle" title="Dinas Dinlle">Dinas Dinlle</a>, <a href="/wiki/D%C3%BAn_Aengus" class="mw-redirect" title="Dún Aengus">Dún Aengus</a>.</li>
<li><b>Sea Promontory</b>: a linear earthwork across a narrow neck of land leading to a peninsula with steep cliffs to the sea on three sides; common on indented Atlantic coasts, such as Ireland, Cornwall, Brittany and west Wales. Examples: <a href="/wiki/Huelgoat" title="Huelgoat">Huelgoat</a>; <a href="/wiki/The_Rumps" title="The Rumps">The Rumps</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Promontory_forts_of_Cornwall" title="Promontory forts of Cornwall">promontory forts of Cornwall</a>.</li>
<li><b>Sloping Enclosure</b> or <a href="/wiki/Hill-slope_enclosure" title="Hill-slope enclosure">Hill-slope enclosure</a>: smaller earthwork on gently sloping hillsides; not significant defensive position. Examples: <a href="/wiki/Goosehill_Camp" title="Goosehill Camp">Goosehill Camp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Plainsfield_Camp" title="Plainsfield Camp">Plainsfield Camp</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trendle_Ring" title="Trendle Ring">Trendle Ring</a>.</li></ul></li>
<li>Area
<ul><li><b>&gt; 20 <a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">ha</a></b>: very large enclosures, too diffuse to defend, probably used for domesticated animals. Example: <a href="/wiki/Bindon_Hill" title="Bindon Hill">Bindon Hill</a>.</li>
<li><b>1–20 ha</b>: defended areas large enough to support permanent tribal settlement. Example: <a href="/wiki/Scratchbury_Camp" title="Scratchbury Camp">Scratchbury Camp</a></li>
<li><b>&lt; 1 ha</b>: small enclosures, more likely to be individual farmsteads or animal pens. Example: <a href="/wiki/Trendle_Ring" title="Trendle Ring">Trendle Ring</a>.</li></ul></li>
<li>Ramparts, walls and ditches
<ul><li><b>Univallate</b>: a single circuit of ramparts for enclosure and defence. Example: <a href="/wiki/Solsbury_Hill" title="Solsbury Hill">Solsbury Hill</a>.</li>
<li><b>Bivallate </b>: a double circuit of defensive earthworks. Example: <a href="/wiki/Battlesbury_Camp" title="Battlesbury Camp">Battlesbury Camp</a>.</li>
<li><b>Multivallate</b>: more than one layer of defensive earthworks, outer works might not be complete circuits, but defend the weakest approaches; typically the inner circuit is original, with outer circuits added later. Example: <a href="/wiki/Cadbury_Castle,_Somerset" title="Cadbury Castle, Somerset">Cadbury Castle</a>.</li></ul></li>
<li>Entrances
<ul><li><b>Simple opening</b>: might indicate an enclosure, rather than a defended position; sometimes the main ramparts may turn inward or outward, and be widened and heightened to control the entrance. Example: <a href="/wiki/Dowsborough" title="Dowsborough">Dowsborough</a>.</li>
<li><b>Linear holloway</b>: straight parallel pair of ramparts dominating the entrance; projecting either inward, outward, or occasionally overlapped along the main rampart. Example: <a href="/wiki/Norton_Camp" title="Norton Camp">Norton Camp</a>.</li>
<li><b>Complex</b>: multiple overlapping outer works; staggered or interleaved multivallate ramparts; zig-zag entrance way, sling platforms and well planned lines of fire. Example: <a href="/wiki/Maiden_Castle,_Dorset" title="Maiden Castle, Dorset">Maiden Castle</a>.</li></ul></li></ul>
<p>Some forts were also settlements, while others were only occupied seasonally, or in times of strife. Archaeological excavation reveals more about the dates of occupation and modes of use. Typical features for excavation include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Rampart_(fortification)" title="Rampart (fortification)">Ramparts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ditch_(fortification)" title="Ditch (fortification)">ditches</a>
<ul><li>Original depths and profiles of ditches.</li>
<li>Rampart construction: <i><a href="/wiki/Murus_gallicus" class="mw-redirect" title="Murus gallicus">murus gallicus</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pfostenschlitzmauer" title="Pfostenschlitzmauer">pfostenschlitzmauer</a></i>.</li>
<li>Guardhouses and defended entrances.</li></ul></li>
<li>Settlement and occupation
<ul><li>Raised platforms, <a href="/wiki/Roundhouse_(dwelling)" title="Roundhouse (dwelling)">roundhouses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Longhouse" title="Longhouse">longhouses</a>.</li>
<li>Post holes for rectangular <a href="/wiki/Granary" title="Granary">granary</a> huts.</li>
<li>Pits for food storage, <a href="/wiki/Souterrain" title="Souterrain">souterrains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fogou" title="Fogou">fogous</a>.</li>
<li>Pottery</li>
<li>Coins, jewellery and hoards.</li></ul></li>
<li>Temples and peacetime burials
<ul><li>Platforms and temple foundations.</li>
<li>Graves and offerings</li></ul></li>
<li>Warfare
<ul><li>Weapons: sling-shot, shields, armour, swords, axes, spears, arrows.</li>
<li>Sieges and conquest: <a href="/wiki/Ballista" title="Ballista">ballista</a> bolts, ash layers, vitrified stones, burnt post holes.</li>
<li>Wartime burials: typically outside the ramparts:
<ul><li>Contemporary individual burials by local inhabitants.</li>
<li>Massed grave pits dug by a conquering army.</li></ul></li></ul></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="By_country">By country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hillfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: By country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Britain">Great Britain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hillfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Great Britain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hillforts_in_Britain" title="Hillforts in Britain">Hillforts in Britain</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:British_camp_central_mound_2005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/British_camp_central_mound_2005.jpg/220px-British_camp_central_mound_2005.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/British_camp_central_mound_2005.jpg/330px-British_camp_central_mound_2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/British_camp_central_mound_2005.jpg/440px-British_camp_central_mound_2005.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2272" data-file-height="1704" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:British_camp_central_mound_2005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The ramparts of the multivallate <a href="/wiki/British_Camp" title="British Camp">British Camp</a> in Herefordshire</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aerial_view_of_Dinas_Dinlle_Iron_Age_Hill_Fort.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Aerial_view_of_Dinas_Dinlle_Iron_Age_Hill_Fort.jpg/220px-Aerial_view_of_Dinas_Dinlle_Iron_Age_Hill_Fort.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Aerial_view_of_Dinas_Dinlle_Iron_Age_Hill_Fort.jpg/330px-Aerial_view_of_Dinas_Dinlle_Iron_Age_Hill_Fort.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Aerial_view_of_Dinas_Dinlle_Iron_Age_Hill_Fort.jpg/440px-Aerial_view_of_Dinas_Dinlle_Iron_Age_Hill_Fort.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="683" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aerial_view_of_Dinas_Dinlle_Iron_Age_Hill_Fort.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dinas Dinlle, Wales</div></div></div>
<p>The reason for the emergence of hillforts in Britain, and their purpose, has been a subject of debate. It has been argued that they could have been military sites constructed in response to invasion from continental Europe, sites built by invaders, or a military reaction to social tensions caused by an increasing population and consequent pressure on agriculture. The dominant view since the 1960s has been that the increasing use of iron led to social changes in Britain. Deposits of iron ore were located in different places to the tin and copper ore necessary to make bronze, and as a result trading patterns shifted and the old elites lost their economic and social status. Power passed into the hands of a new group of people.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Archaeologist <a href="/wiki/Barry_Cunliffe" title="Barry Cunliffe">Barry Cunliffe</a> believes that population increase still played a role and has stated "[the forts] provided defensive possibilities for the community at those times when the stress [of an increasing population] burst out into open warfare. But I wouldn't see them as having been built because there was a state of war. They would be functional as defensive strongholds when there were tensions and undoubtedly some of them were attacked and destroyed, but this was not the only, or even the most significant, factor in their construction".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hillforts in Britain are known from the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>, but the great period of hillfort construction was during the Iron Age, between 700 BC and the <a href="/wiki/Roman_conquest_of_Britain" title="Roman conquest of Britain">Roman conquest of Britain</a> in 43 AD. The Romans occupied some forts, such as the military garrison at <a href="/wiki/Hod_Hill" title="Hod Hill">Hod Hill</a>, and the temple at <a href="/wiki/Brean_Down" title="Brean Down">Brean Down</a>, but others were destroyed and abandoned. Partially articulated remains of between 28 and 40 men, women and children at <a href="/wiki/Cadbury_Castle,_Somerset" title="Cadbury Castle, Somerset">Cadbury Castle</a> were thought by the excavator<sup id="cite_ref-WTC_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WTC-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> to implicate the Cadbury population in a revolt in the 70s AD (roughly contemporary with that of Boudicca in the East of England), although this has been questioned by subsequent researchers.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> However, the presence of barracks on the hilltop in the decades following the conquest suggest an ongoing struggle to suppress local dissent.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Maiden_Castle,_Dorset" title="Maiden Castle, Dorset">Maiden Castle</a> in Dorset is the largest hillfort in England. Where Roman influence was less strong, such as uninvaded Ireland and unsubdued northern Scotland, hillforts were still built and used for several more centuries.
</p><p>There are over 2,000 Iron Age hillforts known in Britain of which nearly 600 are in Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Danebury" title="Danebury">Danebury</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>, is the most thoroughly investigated Iron Age hillfort in Britain, as well as the most extensively published.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cadbury_Castle,_Somerset" title="Cadbury Castle, Somerset">Cadbury Castle, Somerset</a> is the largest amongst forts reoccupied following the end of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman rule</a>, to defend against <a href="/wiki/Pirate" class="mw-redirect" title="Pirate">pirate</a> raids, and the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> invasions. The cemetery outside <a href="/wiki/Poundbury_Hill" title="Poundbury Hill">Poundbury Hill</a> contains east-facing Christian burials of the 4th century. The <a href="/wiki/Wansdyke_(earthwork)" title="Wansdyke (earthwork)">Wansdyke</a> was a new linear earthwork connected to the existing hillfort at <a href="/wiki/Maes_Knoll" title="Maes Knoll">Maes Knoll</a>, which defined the Celtic-Saxon border in south-west England during the period 577–652 AD.
</p><p>Some hillforts were re-occupied by the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxons</a> during the period of <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> raids. <a href="/wiki/King_Alfred" class="mw-redirect" title="King Alfred">King Alfred</a> established a network of coastal hillforts and lookout posts in <a href="/wiki/Wessex" title="Wessex">Wessex</a>, linked by a <i><a href="/wiki/Herepath" title="Herepath">Herepath</a></i>, or military road, which enabled his armies to cover Viking movements at sea. For example, see <a href="/wiki/Daw%27s_Castle" title="Daw&#39;s Castle">Daw's Castle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cynwit" title="Battle of Cynwit">Battle of Cynwit</a>.
</p><p>It has been suggested on reasonable evidence that many so-called hillforts were just used to pen in cattle, horses, or other domesticated animals. The large sprawling examples at <a href="/wiki/Bindon_Hill" title="Bindon Hill">Bindon Hill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bathampton_Down" title="Bathampton Down">Bathampton Down</a> are more than 50 acres (20&#160;<a href="/wiki/Hectare" title="Hectare">ha</a>). Even those that were defensive settlements in the Iron Age were sometimes used for coralling animals in later periods. For example, see <a href="/wiki/Coney%27s_Castle" title="Coney&#39;s Castle">Coney's Castle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dolebury_Warren" title="Dolebury Warren">Dolebury Warren</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pilsdon_Pen" title="Pilsdon Pen">Pilsdon Pen</a>. However, it is difficult to prove that people definitely did not dwell there, as lack of evidence is not proof of absence.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_Europe">Central Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hillfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Central Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kostolec-hradisko.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Kostolec-hradisko.jpg/220px-Kostolec-hradisko.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Kostolec-hradisko.jpg/330px-Kostolec-hradisko.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Kostolec-hradisko.jpg/440px-Kostolec-hradisko.jpg 2x" data-file-width="576" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kostolec-hradisko.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hillfort <a href="/wiki/Kostolec" title="Kostolec">Kostolec</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pie%C5%A1%C5%A5any" title="Piešťany">Piešťany</a> (<a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>).</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hallstatt_culture" title="Hallstatt culture">Hallstatt culture</a> and <a href="/wiki/La_T%C3%A8ne_culture" title="La Tène culture">La Tène culture</a> originated in what is now southern Germany, Switzerland, Austria, <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a> and the Czech Republic. However, hillforts were built also in Poland and further east, until the Middle Ages.
</p><p>The predominant form of rampart construction is <i><a href="/wiki/Pfostenschlitzmauer" title="Pfostenschlitzmauer">pfostenschlitzmauer</a></i>, or <i>Kelheim-style</i>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Migration_Period">Migration Period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hillfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Migration Period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the period of <a href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Antiquity">Late Antiquity</a> or <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">Migration Period</a> a large number of hilltop settlements were established both on the Roman imperial territory and on Germanic soil. However, the term embraces a wide range of very different settlements in high locations. At least a few of the Germanic settlements were protected by fortifications. Unlike the Romans, however, the Germanii did not use mortar at that time for their construction. Among the best known hill settlements in Germany are the <a href="/wiki/Runder_Berg" title="Runder Berg">Runder Berg</a> near <a href="/wiki/Bad_Urach" title="Bad Urach">Bad Urach</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gelbe_B%C3%BCrg" class="mw-redirect" title="Gelbe Bürg">Gelbe Bürg</a> near Dittenheim. Even in areas that were remote from the Roman Empire, such as southern Sweden, numerous hillfort sites of this period have been found.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portugal_and_Spain">Portugal and Spain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hillfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Portugal and Spain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Castro_de_Coa%C3%B1a.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Castro_de_Coa%C3%B1a.JPG/220px-Castro_de_Coa%C3%B1a.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Castro_de_Coa%C3%B1a.JPG/330px-Castro_de_Coa%C3%B1a.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Castro_de_Coa%C3%B1a.JPG/440px-Castro_de_Coa%C3%B1a.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1712" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Castro_de_Coa%C3%B1a.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hillfort at Coaña, <a href="/wiki/Asturias" title="Asturias">Asturias</a>, Spain</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Castro_culture" title="Castro culture">Castro culture</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Castros_in_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Castros in Spain">Castros in Spain</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asturias" title="Asturias">Asturias</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cantabria" title="Cantabria">Cantabria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">Basque Country</a>, <a href="/wiki/Province_of_%C3%81vila" title="Province of Ávila">province of Ávila</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Portugal" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Portugal">Northern Portugal</a> a <i>castro</i> is a fortified pre-<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> Iron Age village, usually located on a hill or some naturally easy defendable place.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The larger hillforts are also called <i>citanias</i>, <i>cividades</i> or <i>cidás</i> (English: <i>cities</i>).
They were located on hilltops, which allowed tactical control over the surrounding countryside and provided natural defences. They usually had access to a spring or small creek to provide water; some even had large reservoirs to use during <a href="/wiki/Siege" title="Siege">sieges</a>. Typically, a castro had one to five stone and earth walls, which complemented the natural defences of the hill. The buildings inside, most of them circular in shape, some rectangular, were about 3.5–15&#160;m (11–49&#160;ft) long; they were made out of stone with thatch roofs resting on a wood column in the centre of the building. In the major <a href="/wiki/Oppida" class="mw-redirect" title="Oppida">oppida</a> there were regular streets, suggesting some form of central organization. Castros vary in area from less than a hectare to some 50 hectare ones, and most were abandoned after the Roman conquest of the territory.
</p><p>Many castros were already established during the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Bronze_Age" title="Atlantic Bronze Age">Atlantic Bronze Age</a> period, pre-dating <a href="/wiki/Hallstatt_culture" title="Hallstatt culture">Hallstatt culture</a>.
</p><p>Many of the <a href="/wiki/Megalith" title="Megalith">megaliths</a> from the Bronze Age such as <a href="/wiki/Menhir" title="Menhir">menhirs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dolmen" title="Dolmen">dolmens</a>, which are frequently located near the castros, also pre-date the Celts in Portugal, <a href="/wiki/Asturias" title="Asturias">Asturias</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">Galicia</a> as well as in Atlantic France, Britain and Ireland. These megaliths were probably reused in syncretic rituals by the Celtic <a href="/wiki/Druid" title="Druid">Druids</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Celtiberians" title="Celtiberians">Celtiberian</a> people occupied an inland region in central northern Spain, straddling the upper valleys of the <a href="/wiki/Ebro" title="Ebro">Ebro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Douro" title="Douro">Douro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tajo" class="mw-redirect" title="Tajo">Tajo</a>. They built hillforts, fortified hilltop towns and <a href="/wiki/Oppida" class="mw-redirect" title="Oppida">oppida</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Numantia" title="Numantia">Numantia</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Estonia">Estonia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hillfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Estonia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:VarbolaRuins.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/VarbolaRuins.jpg/220px-VarbolaRuins.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/VarbolaRuins.jpg/330px-VarbolaRuins.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/VarbolaRuins.jpg/440px-VarbolaRuins.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:VarbolaRuins.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Varbola hillfort ruins in Estonia</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">Estonian</a> word for <i>hillfort</i> is <i>linnamägi</i> (plural <i>linnamäed</i>), meaning <i>hillfort</i> or <i>hillburgh</i>. There are several hundred hillforts or presumed ancient hillfort sites all over Estonia. Some of them, like <a href="/wiki/Toompea" title="Toompea">Toompea</a> in Tallinn or <a href="/wiki/Tartu" title="Tartu">Toomemägi</a> in Tartu, are governance centres used since ancient times up until today. Some others, like <a href="/wiki/Varbola_Stronghold" title="Varbola Stronghold">Varbola</a> are historical sites nowadays.
</p><p>Most likely the Estonian hillforts were in pre-Christian times administrative, economic and military centres of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Estonia" title="Ancient Estonia">Estonian tribes</a>. Although some of them were probably used only during times of crisis and stood empty in peacetime (for example Soontagana in Koonga parish, <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A4rnu_county" class="mw-redirect" title="Pärnu county">Pärnu county</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finland">Finland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hillfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Finland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a> word for <i>hillfort</i> is <i>linnavuori</i> (plural <i>linnavuoret</i>), meaning <i>fort hill</i> or <i>castle hill</i>, or alternatively <i>muinaislinna</i> meaning <i>ancient fort</i>, as opposed to bare <i>linna</i> which refers to medieval or later fortifications.
</p><p>One special feature about the Finnish hillforts that while most of them are located these days within some distance from the sea, but earlier many of the forts were located by the sea, due to <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">post-glacial rebound</a>.
</p><p>Finland has around 100 hillforts verified by excavations, and about 200 more suspected sites. The largest hillfort in Finland is the <a href="/wiki/Rapola_Castle" title="Rapola Castle">Rapola Castle</a>, another notable one is the <a href="/wiki/Old_Castle_of_Lieto" title="Old Castle of Lieto">Old Castle of Lieto</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hillfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Griahan_of_aileach.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Griahan_of_aileach.jpg/220px-Griahan_of_aileach.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Griahan_of_aileach.jpg/330px-Griahan_of_aileach.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Griahan_of_aileach.jpg/440px-Griahan_of_aileach.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Griahan_of_aileach.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Exterior view of the Ringfort <i><a href="/wiki/Grianan_of_Aileach" title="Grianan of Aileach">Grianan of Aileach</a></i> situated in County Donegal</div></div></div>
<p>Bronze Age and Iron Age hillforts are widely found in Ireland. They are large circular structures between 1 and 40 acres (most commonly 5–10 acres) in size, enclosed by a stone wall or earthen rampart or both. These would have been important tribal centres where the chief or king of the area would live with his extended family and support themselves by farming and renting cattle to their underlings.
</p><p>There are around 40 known hillforts in Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> About 12 are multivallate as distinguished by multiple ramparts, or a large <a href="/wiki/Counterscarp" title="Counterscarp">counterscarp</a> (outer bank). The imposing example at Mooghaun is defended by multiple stone walls.
</p><p>One must be careful to not confuse a hill-fort with a '<a href="/wiki/Ringfort" title="Ringfort">ringfort</a>' - a medieval settlement - a common archaeological feature across the whole island of Ireland, of which over 40,000 examples are known;<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> one source claims there may be 10,000 undiscovered ringforts.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some hillforts have cairns inside their boundaries and there are many speculations about this phenomenon, the theories range from being a strange cult religion to just coincidence the same kind of area as they both like (hill tops with commanding views of the local vicinity), the excavation at Freestone Hill in County Kilkenny has shown that there was indeed a ditch cut out around the cairn, evidence that they had respect for the feature no matter what they decided to believe about it.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latvia">Latvia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hillfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Latvia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_hillforts_in_Latvia" title="List of hillforts in Latvia">List of hillforts in Latvia</a></div>
<p>The Latvian word for <i>hillfort</i> is <i>pilskalns</i> (plural: pilskalni), from <i>pils</i> (castle) and <i>kalns</i> (hill).
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2010_09_04_7Tervete37.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/2010_09_04_7Tervete37.JPG/220px-2010_09_04_7Tervete37.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/2010_09_04_7Tervete37.JPG/330px-2010_09_04_7Tervete37.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/2010_09_04_7Tervete37.JPG/440px-2010_09_04_7Tervete37.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2814" data-file-height="1866" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2010_09_04_7Tervete37.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tērvete hillfort. Main <a href="/wiki/Semigallians" title="Semigallians">Semigallian</a> centre in the late Iron Age</div></div></div>
<p>Hillforts in <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a> offered not only military and administrative functions but they were also cultural and economic centres of some regions. Latvian hillforts generally were a part of a complex consisting of the main fortress, the settlement around it, one or more burial fields and nearby ritual sites. The first hillforts in Latvia, such as Daugmale hillfort, appeared during the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Some were continuously inhabited until the late <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bielenstein_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bielenstein-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Moora_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moora-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the Roman Iron Age, some of the Latvian hillforts (like Ķivutkalns) were abandoned or became sparsely populated. A new period in hillfort development started during the 5th-8th centuries AD, when many new hillforts appeared, in most cases, along the main trades routes - rivers. During the 10th-11th centuries, some of the hillforts became military fortresses with strong fortifications (like hillforts in <a href="/wiki/T%C4%93rvete" title="Tērvete">Tērvete</a>, <a href="/wiki/Talsi" title="Talsi">Talsi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Me%C5%BEotne" title="Mežotne">Mežotne</a>). Some of them are considered important political centres of the local peoples, who in this period were subjects of serious social political changes. That period was known for unrest and military activities, as well as power struggles between local aristocracy. Most of the Latvian hillforts were destroyed or abandoned during the <a href="/wiki/Livonian_Crusade" title="Livonian Crusade">Livonian Crusade</a> in the 13th century, but some were still used in the 14th century. In total, there are about 470 hillforts in Latvia.<sup id="cite_ref-Bielenstein_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bielenstein-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Moora_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moora-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lithuania">Lithuania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hillfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Lithuania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Piliakalniai.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Piliakalniai.jpg/220px-Piliakalniai.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="49" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Piliakalniai.jpg/330px-Piliakalniai.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Piliakalniai.jpg/440px-Piliakalniai.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3076" data-file-height="686" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Piliakalniai.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Piliakalnis complex in <a href="/wiki/Kernav%C4%97" title="Kernavė">Kernavė</a>, a <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Daubariu_piliakanis_d1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Daubariu_piliakanis_d1.jpg/220px-Daubariu_piliakanis_d1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="139" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Daubariu_piliakanis_d1.jpg/330px-Daubariu_piliakanis_d1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Daubariu_piliakanis_d1.jpg/440px-Daubariu_piliakanis_d1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1304" data-file-height="821" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Daubariu_piliakanis_d1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Daubariai piliakalnis in <a href="/wiki/Ma%C5%BEeikiai_district_municipality" class="mw-redirect" title="Mažeikiai district municipality">Mažeikiai district municipality</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_language" title="Lithuanian language">Lithuanian</a> word for <i>hillfort</i> is <i>piliakalnis</i> (plural <i>piliakalniai</i>), from <i>pilis</i> (=castle) and <i>kalnas</i> (=mountain, hill).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuania</a> has hillforts dating from the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> in the 1st millennium BC. The earliest examples in present-day Lithuania are found in the east of the country. Most of these forts were built or expanded between the fifth and fifteenth centuries, when they were used in the Dukes' Wars, and against the invasion of <a href="/wiki/Teutonic_Order" title="Teutonic Order">Teutonic Knights</a> from the west. Most forts were located on the banks of a river, or a confluence where two rivers met. These fortifications were typically wooden, although some had additional stone or brick walls. The hill was usually sculpted for defensive purposes, with the top flattened and the natural slopes made steeper for defence.
</p><p>During the early years of <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Lithuania" title="Grand Duchy of Lithuania">Grand Duchy of Lithuania</a> <i>piliakalniai</i> played a major role in conflicts with the <a href="/wiki/Livonian_Brothers_of_the_Sword" title="Livonian Brothers of the Sword">Livonian Order</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Teutonic_Knights" class="mw-redirect" title="Teutonic Knights">Teutonic Knights</a>. During this period the number of <i>piliakalniai</i> in use decreased, but those that remained had stronger fortifications. Two main defence lines developed: one along the <a href="/wiki/Neman_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Neman River">Neman River</a> (against the Teutonic Order) and another along the border with <a href="/wiki/Livonia" title="Livonia">Livonia</a>. Two other lines started to form, but did not fully develop. One was to protect <a href="/wiki/Vilnius" title="Vilnius">Vilnius</a>, the capital, and the other line in <a href="/wiki/Samogitia" title="Samogitia">Samogitia</a>, was a major target for both orders. This territory separated the two Orders and prevented joint action between them and Pagan Lithuania.
</p><p>According to the <i>Lietuvos piliakalnių atlasas</i> (English: <i>Atlas of Piliakalniai in Lithuania</i>), there were 826 <i>piliakalniai</i> in Lithuania. Some researchers present a total number of 840 known <i>piliakalnis</i> in 2007; the number is likely to increase as even more of them are discovered every year. Most <i>piliakalniai</i> are located near rivers and are endangered by erosion: many have partly collapsed as the flooded river has washed out the base of the hill. Now around 80 percent of <i>piliakalniai</i> are covered by forests and are hardly accessible to visitors.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scandinavia_and_Russia">Scandinavia and Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hillfort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Scandinavia and Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rikalan_linna.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Rikalan_linna.jpg/220px-Rikalan_linna.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Rikalan_linna.jpg/330px-Rikalan_linna.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Rikalan_linna.jpg/440px-Rikalan_linna.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rikalan_linna.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hillfort in <a href="/wiki/Halikko" title="Halikko">Halikko</a>, Finland</div></div></div>
<p>In Scandinavia and northern Russia, <i>hillforts</i> are fortifications from the Iron Age which may have had several functions. They are usually located on the crests of hills and mountains making use of precipices and marshes which worked as natural defences. The crests' more accessible parts were defended with walls of stone and outer walls in the slopes beneath are common. Round and closed, so-called <i><a href="/wiki/Ring_fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Ring fort">ring forts</a></i> are common even on flat ground. The walls often have remaining parts of stone, which were probably the support of pales. They often have well delineated gateways, the gates of which were probably of wood. Hillforts with strong walls are often located beside old trade routes and have an offensive character, whereas others are reclusive and were weakly fortified, probably only for hiding during raids.
</p><p>Many forts, located centrally in densely populated areas, were permanently settled strongholds and can show traces of settlements both inside and outside. Older place names containing the element <i>sten</i>/<i>stein</i> were usually hillforts.
</p><p>In Sweden, there are 1,100 known hillforts with a strong concentration on the northern west coast and in eastern <a href="/wiki/Svealand" title="Svealand">Svealand</a>. In <a href="/wiki/S%C3%B6dermanland" title="Södermanland">Södermanland</a> there are 300, in <a href="/wiki/Uppland" title="Uppland">Uppland</a> 150, <a href="/wiki/%C3%96sterg%C3%B6tland" title="Östergötland">Östergötland</a> 130, and 90 to 100 in each of <a href="/wiki/Bohusl%C3%A4n" title="Bohuslän">Bohuslän</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gotland" title="Gotland">Gotland</a>. Norway has about 400 hillforts, Denmark has 26.
</p>
