<p><b>Woolen</b> (<a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>) or <b>woollen</b> (<a href="/wiki/English_in_the_Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="English in the Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth English</a>) is a type of <a href="/wiki/Yarn" title="Yarn">yarn</a> made from <a href="/wiki/Carding" title="Carding">carded</a> <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">wool</a>.  Woolen yarn is soft, light, stretchy, and full of air. It is thus a good insulator, and makes a good <a href="/wiki/Knitting" title="Knitting">knitting</a> yarn. Woolen yarn is in contrast to <a href="/wiki/Worsted" title="Worsted">worsted</a> yarn, in which the fibers are <a href="/wiki/Combing" title="Combing">combed</a> to lie <a href="/wiki/Parallel_(geometry)" title="Parallel (geometry)">parallel</a> rather than carded, producing a hard, strong yarn.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_manufacture">Commercial manufacture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Commercial manufacture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The woolen and worsted process both require that the wool (and other similar animal fibers, <a href="/wiki/Cashmere_wool" title="Cashmere wool">cashmere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camel" title="Camel">camel</a>, etc.) be cleaned before mechanical processing. Woolen and worsted nomenclatures apply only to the textile processing of animal fibers, but it has become common to include fiber blends under these terms.
</p><p>The resultant fabrics will be classified as being either woolen or worsted, but this designation is assigned during fiber processing and yarn formation, not in the cloth or finished garment.
</p><p>A woven woolen fabric is one which is subjected to fabric <a href="/wiki/Finishing_(textiles)" title="Finishing (textiles)">finishing</a> techniques designed to add a directional pile&#160;— in that the end consumer can 'stroke' the garment in a single direction (shoulder to cuff etc.), such as a casual jacket. This feels like the fibers are directionally arranged.
</p><p>Woolen yarn formation is also very common for <a href="/wiki/Knitwear" class="mw-redirect" title="Knitwear">knitwear</a>, where the resultant garment has some bulk and the requirement for visual aesthetics (of fiber alignment) is minimal.
</p><p>The worsted processing route is more complex and requires the removal of short fibers and the use of a focused mechanical process to make the individual fibers parallel to each other. The yarn formation process is significantly more comprehensive and results in a very sleek yarn which will offer a clean looking woven fabric, such as for <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/suiting" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:suiting">suitings</a>. The worsted process is significantly more expensive and is seldom used for knitwear.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Handspinning_woolen_yarn">Handspinning woolen yarn</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Handspinning woolen yarn">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Long_draw_(spinning)" class="mw-redirect" title="Long draw (spinning)">long draw (spinning)</a></div>
<p>Woolen yarn is handspun using the <a href="/wiki/Long_draw_(spinning)" class="mw-redirect" title="Long draw (spinning)">long draw</a> technique, and the yarn is spun from a <a href="/wiki/Rolag" title="Rolag">rolag</a>. Most handspinners make a blend of a woolen and worsted yarn, using techniques from both categories, and thus ending up with a mix. The first step to spin a true woolen yarn, however, is to <a href="/wiki/Carding" title="Carding">card</a> the fiber into a <a href="/wiki/Rolag" title="Rolag">rolag</a> using <a href="/wiki/Carding" title="Carding">handcarders</a>.
</p><p>The rolag is spun without much stretching of the fibers from the cylindrical configuration. This is done by allowing twist into a short section of the rolag, and then pulling back, without letting the rolag change position in your hands, until the yarn is the desired thickness. The twist will concentrate in the thinnest part of the roving, thus when the yarn is pulled, the thicker sections with less twist will tend to thin out. Once the yarn is the desired thickness, enough twist is added to make the yarn strong. Then the yarn is wound onto the bobbin, and the process starts again.
</p>
