	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Naming law</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>naming law</b> restricts the names that parents can legally give to their children, usually to protect the child from being given an offensive or embarrassing name. Many countries around the world have such laws, with most governing the meaning of the name, while some only govern the scripts in which it is written.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Azerbaijan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Certain names have been proscribed by Azerbaijan.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bbc.com_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bbc.com_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Naming_laws_in_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Naming laws in the People&#39;s Republic of China">Naming laws in the People's Republic of China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Naming_taboo" title="Naming taboo">Naming taboo</a></div>
<p>In Imperial China, a <a href="/wiki/Naming_taboo" title="Naming taboo">naming taboo</a> prevented people from using the same names as the reigning Emperor.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Denmark">Denmark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Denmark">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Under the Law on Personal Names,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> first names are picked from a list of approved names (18,000 female names and 15,000 male names as of Jan 1st 2016).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> One can also apply to <a href="https://da.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ankestyrelsen" class="extiw" title="da:Ankestyrelsen">Ankestyrelsen</a> for approval of new names, e.g. common first names from other countries. Names must indicate gender, cannot have surname character, and must follow Danish orthography (e.g. Cammmilla with three <i>m'</i>s is not allowed).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Finland">Finland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Finland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At present, the Names Act of 1985 requires that all Finnish citizens and residents have at least one and at the most three first names. Persons who do not have a first name are obligated to adopt one when they are entered into the Finnish national population database. Parents of new-born children must name their child and inform the population registry within two months of the child's birth. The name may be chosen freely, but it must not be
</p>
<ul><li>a name used primarily by persons of the other sex</li>
<li>a name foreign to the naming tradition in Finland</li>
<li>a surname, except a patronymic as last given name</li>
<li>a name already used by a sibling, if this is to be the only given name.</li></ul>
<p>Waivers may be granted if valid family, religious or ethnic reasons give grounds to use a name contrary to these principles. Persons may change their first names once without a specific reason. For subsequent changes, valid reasons must be presented.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Further information: <a href="/wiki/Finnish_name" title="Finnish name">Finnish name</a></i>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since 1993 the choice has been free in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> unless it is decided that the name is contrary to the interests of the child. Before that time the choice of first names was dictated by French laws that decreed which names were acceptable.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_Bonaparte" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon Bonaparte">Napoleon Bonaparte</a> created the law.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Names have to be approved by the local registration office, called <a href="/wiki/Standesamt" title="Standesamt">Standesamt</a>, which generally consults a list of first names and foreign embassies for foreign names. The name has to indicate gender, it cannot be a last name or a product, and it cannot negatively affect the child. If the name submitted is denied, it can be appealed; otherwise a new name has to be submitted. A fee is charged for each submission.<sup id="cite_ref-cnn.com_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn.com-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Umlauts (<i>ä, ö, ü</i>) and/or the letter <i><b>ß</b></i> in family names are recognized as an important reason for a name change. (Even just the change of the spelling, e.g. from <i>Müller</i> to <i>Mueller</i> or from <i>Weiß</i> to <i>Weiss</i>, is regarded as name change. In German ID cards and passports, however, such names are spelled in two different ways: the correct way in the non-machine-readable zone of the document [Müller] and transcribed [Mueller] in the machine-readable zone of the document, so persons unfamiliar with German orthography may get the impression that the document is a forgery. German credit cards may use the correct or the transcribed spelling only. It is recommended to use the exactly same spelling in the machine-readable zone of the passport for airline tickets, visas, etc. and to refer to this zone if being asked questions.) Internationally and by many electronic systems, <i>ä / ö / ü</i> are transcribed as <i>ae / oe / ue</i>, and <i>ß</i> is transcribed as <i>ss</i>.
</p><p>During the Nazi period, Germany had a list of approved names to choose from that was passed on January 5, 1938 as the "Second Regulation under the law re The changing of Family and Given names." The law had one list of names for ethnic Germans and another for Jews.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hungary">Hungary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hungary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A child's name must be chosen from a list of pre-approved names.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> If the intended name is not on the list, the parents need to apply for approval. Applications are considered by the <a href="/wiki/Research_Institute_for_Linguistics_of_the_Hungarian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Research Institute for Linguistics of the Hungarian Academy of Sciences">Research Institute for Linguistics of the Hungarian Academy of Sciences</a> following a set of principles.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
Children born to a foreign citizen may have their name chosen according to foreign law.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Iceland">Iceland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Iceland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Parents are limited to choosing children's names from the Personal Names Register, which is a list of about 1800 names for each gender.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
The <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_Naming_Committee" title="Icelandic Naming Committee">Icelandic Naming Committee</a> maintains the list and hears requests for exceptions.
</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_name" title="Icelandic name">Icelandic name</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Italy">Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Names considered ridiculous or shameful are banned by law.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Japanese_name#Regulations" title="Japanese name">Japanese name §&#160;Regulations</a></div>
<p>Similarly to China, Japan has a certain set of characters that can be used in a child's name.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Kyrgyzstan">Kyrgyzstan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Kyrgyzstan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some Kyrgyz have been russifying their names.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A law to ban russified names was proposed.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Malaysia">Malaysia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Malaysia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On and after 2006, the National Registration Department of Malaysia (JPN) may decline to register objectionable or undesirable names, including names based on titles, numbers, colors, vegetables, fruits, vulgarities, and equipment. Parents who wish to register such names despite JPN objection must make a statutory declaration to that effect.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Star_Malaysia_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Star_Malaysia-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Laws_of_Malaysia:_Birth_and_Death_Registration_Act_1957_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Laws_of_Malaysia:_Birth_and_Death_Registration_Act_1957-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Under the Births, Deaths and Marriages Registration Act of 1995, names are prohibited which "might cause offence to a reasonable person; or [...] is unreasonably long; or without adequate justification, [...] is, includes, or resembles, an official title or rank."<sup id="cite_ref-cnn.com_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn.com-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This is determined by the Department of Internal Affairs, which is responsible for registering names at birth.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The most commonly rejected name is "Justice", which is a formal title for judges in New Zealand.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Norway">Norway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Norway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In April 2009, a six-year-old Norwegian boy named Christer pressed his parents to send a letter to King <a href="/wiki/Harald_V_of_Norway" title="Harald V of Norway">Harald V</a> to approve his name being changed to "<a href="/wiki/Sonic_X" title="Sonic X">Sonic X</a>". They allowed Christer to write it himself but did not send it until he badgered them further, and the king responded that he could not approve the change because Christer was not eighteen years old.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Portugal">Portugal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Portugal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Portugal has a set list of names approved and not approved published periodically by the Institute of Registration at the Ministry of Justice.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Saudi_Arabia">Saudi Arabia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Saudi Arabia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A list of 50 were proscribed by Saudi Arabia.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Sweden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Naming_law_in_Sweden" title="Naming law in Sweden">Naming law in Sweden</a></div>
<p>Swedish first names "shall not be approved if they can cause offense or can be supposed to cause discomfort for the one using it, or names which for some obvious reason are not suitable as a first name."
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Tajikistan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_Tajikistan" title="Islam in Tajikistan">Islam in Tajikistan</a></div>
<p>The authorities of <a href="/wiki/Tajikistan" title="Tajikistan">Tajikistan</a> have announced the preparation of a list of 3,000 pre-approved names, all referred to Tajik culture, thus banning Arabic/Islamic names and suffixes, deemed divisive.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Among increasingly religious Tajiks, Islamic-Arabic names have become more popular over Tajik names.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Tajik government has used the word "prostitute" to label hijab wearing women and enforced shaving of beards, in addition to considering the outlawing of Arabic-Islamic names for children and making people use Tajik names.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Tajikistan President Rakhmon (Rahmon) has said that the Persian epic Shahnameh should be used as a source for names, with his proposed law hinting that Muslim names would be forbidden after his anti hijab and anti beard laws.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The UK has no law restricting permitted names, but names that contain <a href="/wiki/Obscenities" class="mw-redirect" title="Obscenities">obscenities</a>, <a href="/wiki/Numeral_system" title="Numeral system">numerals</a>, misleading titles, or are impossible to pronounce are likely to be rejected by the Registering Officer.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Naming_in_the_United_States" title="Naming in the United States">Naming in the United States</a></div>
<p>Restrictions vary by state, and most are imposed for the sake of practicality. For example, several states limit the number of characters in a name, due to limitations in the software used for official record keeping. For similar reasons, some states ban the use of <a href="/wiki/Numeral_system" title="Numeral system">numerals</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pictograms" class="mw-redirect" title="Pictograms">pictograms</a>. A few states ban the use of <a href="/wiki/Obscenity" title="Obscenity">obscenities</a>.  Conversely, a few states, such as <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>, have no naming laws whatsoever.  Courts have interpreted the <a href="/wiki/Due_Process_Clause" title="Due Process Clause">Due Process Clause</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Fourteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Free_Speech_Clause" class="mw-redirect" title="Free Speech Clause">Free Speech Clause</a> of the <a href="/wiki/First_Amendment" class="mw-redirect" title="First Amendment">First Amendment</a> as generally supporting the traditional parental right to choose their children's names.
</p><p>One naming law that some find restrictive is California's ban on <a href="/wiki/Diacritical" class="mw-redirect" title="Diacritical">diacritical</a> marks, such as in the name José. The Office of Vital Records in the <a href="/wiki/California_Department_of_Public_Health" title="California Department of Public Health">California Department of Public Health</a> requires that names contain only the 26 <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">alphabetical</a> characters of the <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English language</a>. There is no law restricting the informal use of diacritical marks and many parents do this.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Zaire">Zaire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naming_law&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Zaire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Authenticit%C3%A9_(Zaire)" title="Authenticité (Zaire)">Authenticité (Zaire)</a></div>
<p>Zairians were urged to drop their Western or Christian names in favor of Zairian names as part of <a href="/wiki/Authenticit%C3%A9_(Zaire)" title="Authenticité (Zaire)">Authenticité</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Meditz,_Sandra_W._and_Tim_Merrill_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meditz,_Sandra_W._and_Tim_Merrill-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Zairian dictator Joseph-Désiré Mobutu changed his name to Mobutu Sese Seko Kuku Ngbendu Wa Za Banga (more commonly abbreviated to Mobutu Sese Seko).
</p>
