<p><b>South Vietnam</b>, officially the <b>Republic of Vietnam</b> (<b>RVN</b>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">Vietnamese</a>: <i lang="vi">Việt Nam Cộng Hòa</i>; <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">République du Viêt Nam</i>, <abbr title="literal translation">lit.</abbr><span style="white-space:nowrap;">&#8201;Vietnam[ese] Republic</span>), was a country that existed from 1955 to 1975, the period when the southern portion of <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Western_Bloc" title="Western Bloc">Western Bloc</a> during part of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>. It received <a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_recognition" title="Diplomatic recognition">international recognition</a> in 1949 as the "<a href="/wiki/State_of_Vietnam" title="State of Vietnam">State of Vietnam</a>"  (a self-governing entity in the <a href="/wiki/French_colonial_empire" title="French colonial empire">French Empire</a>), which was a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a> (1949–1955). This became the "Republic of Vietnam" in 1955. Its capital was <a href="/wiki/Saigon" class="mw-redirect" title="Saigon">Saigon</a> (now Ho Chi Minh City). South Vietnam was bordered by <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnam" title="North Vietnam">North Vietnam</a> to the north, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Laos" title="Kingdom of Laos">Laos</a> to the northwest, <a href="/wiki/Khmer_Republic" title="Khmer Republic">Cambodia</a> to the southwest, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> across the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Thailand" title="Gulf of Thailand">Gulf of Thailand</a> to the southwest, and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> across the <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">South China Sea</a> to the east and southeast.
</p><p>The Republic of Vietnam was proclaimed on 26 October 1955, with <a href="/wiki/Ng%C3%B4_%C4%90%C3%ACnh_Di%E1%BB%87m" class="mw-redirect" title="Ngô Đình Diệm">Ngô Đình Diệm</a> as its first president, after having briefly served as premier under Emperor <a href="/wiki/B%E1%BA%A3o_%C4%90%E1%BA%A1i" title="Bảo Đại">Bảo Đại</a> who was exiled.<sup id="cite_ref-Bühler2001p71_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bühler2001p71-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Its sovereignty was recognized by the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and 87 other nations. It had membership in several special committees of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, but its application for full membership was rejected in 1957 because of a Soviet veto (neither South nor North Vietnam were members of the UN during the Vietnam War, but the united Vietnam became a member state in 1977).<sup id="cite_ref-DOA1975_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DOA1975-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Doyle2010_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Doyle2010-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> South Vietnam's origins can be traced to the French colony of <a href="/wiki/French_Cochinchina" title="French Cochinchina">Cochinchina</a>, which consisted of the southern third of Vietnam which was Cochinchina [Nam Kỳ], a subdivision of <a href="/wiki/French_Indochina" title="French Indochina">French Indochina</a>, and the southern half of Central Vietnam or <a href="/wiki/Annam_(French_protectorate)" title="Annam (French protectorate)">Annam</a> [Trung Kỳ] which was a French protectorate. After the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, the anti-Japanese <a href="/wiki/Vi%E1%BB%87t_Minh" title="Việt Minh">Việt Minh</a> guerrilla forces, led by <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh" title="Ho Chi Minh">Ho Chi Minh</a>, proclaimed the establishment of a <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Vietnam" class="mw-redirect" title="Democratic Republic of Vietnam">Democratic Republic of Vietnam</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hanoi" title="Hanoi">Hanoi</a> in September 1945, issuing a Declaration of Independence modeled on the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence" title="United States Declaration of Independence">US one from 1776</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1949, anti-communist <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_people" title="Vietnamese people">Vietnamese</a> politicians formed a rival government in Saigon led by former <a href="/wiki/List_of_Vietnamese_monarchs" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Vietnamese monarchs">emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/B%E1%BA%A3o_%C4%90%E1%BA%A1i" title="Bảo Đại">Bảo Đại</a>. Bảo Đại was <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(politics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Deposition (politics)">deposed</a> by Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Ng%C3%B4_%C4%90%C3%ACnh_Di%E1%BB%87m" class="mw-redirect" title="Ngô Đình Diệm">Ngô Đình Diệm</a> in 1955, who proclaimed himself president after a <a href="/wiki/1955_State_of_Vietnam_referendum" title="1955 State of Vietnam referendum">referendum</a>. Diệm was <a href="/wiki/Arrest_and_assassination_of_Ngo_Dinh_Diem" title="Arrest and assassination of Ngo Dinh Diem">killed in a military coup</a> led by general <a href="/wiki/D%C6%B0%C6%A1ng_V%C4%83n_Minh" title="Dương Văn Minh">Dương Văn Minh</a> in 1963, and a series of short-lived military governments followed. General <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_V%C4%83n_Thi%E1%BB%87u" title="Nguyễn Văn Thiệu">Nguyễn Văn Thiệu</a> then led the country after a US-encouraged civilian presidential election from 1967 until 1975. The beginnings of the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> occurred in 1955 with an uprising by the newly organized <a href="/wiki/National_Liberation_Front_for_South_Vietnam" class="mw-redirect" title="National Liberation Front for South Vietnam">National Liberation Front for South Vietnam</a> (Việt Cộng), armed and supported by the <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnam" title="North Vietnam">North Vietnam</a>, with backing mainly from <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. Larger escalation of the insurgency occurred in 1965 with <a href="/wiki/Role_of_the_United_States_in_the_Vietnam_War" title="Role of the United States in the Vietnam War">American intervention</a> and the introduction of regular forces of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">Marines</a>, followed by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">Army</a> units to supplement the cadre of military advisors guiding the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Vietnam_Military_Forces" title="Republic of Vietnam Military Forces">Southern armed forces</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Operation_Rolling_Thunder" title="Operation Rolling Thunder">regular bombing campaign</a> over North Vietnam was conducted by offshore <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">US Navy</a> airplanes, warships, and aircraft carriers joined by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">Air Force</a> squadrons through 1966 and 1967. Fighting peaked up to that point during the <a href="/wiki/Tet_Offensive" title="Tet Offensive">Tet Offensive</a> of February 1968, when there were over a million South Vietnamese soldiers and 500,000 <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">US soldiers</a> in South Vietnam. Later on the war the initial guerrilla war turned into a more conventional fight as the balance of power became equalized. An even larger, armored invasion from the North commenced during the <a href="/wiki/Easter_Offensive" title="Easter Offensive">Easter Offensive</a> following US ground-forces withdrawal, and had nearly overran some major northern cities until beaten back.
</p><p>Despite a truce agreement under the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Peace_Accords" title="Paris Peace Accords">Paris Peace Accords</a>, concluded in January 1973, after a torturous five years of on and off negotiations, fighting continued almost immediately afterwards. The regular <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Army_of_Vietnam" title="People&#39;s Army of Vietnam">North Vietnamese army</a> and Việt Cộng auxiliaries launched a major <a href="/wiki/1975_Spring_Offensive" title="1975 Spring Offensive">second combined-arms conventional invasion in 1975</a>. Communist forces <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Saigon" title="Fall of Saigon">overran Saigon</a> on 30 April 1975, marking the end of the Republic of Vietnam. On July 2, 1976, the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Revolutionary_Government_of_the_Republic_of_South_Vietnam" title="Provisional Revolutionary Government of the Republic of South Vietnam">Provisional Revolutionary Government of the Republic of South Vietnam</a> (which had taken over administration of the country) and the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Vietnam" class="mw-redirect" title="Democratic Republic of Vietnam">Democratic Republic of Vietnam</a> merged to form the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Republic_of_Vietnam" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialist Republic of Vietnam">Socialist Republic of Vietnam</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Names_of_Vietnam" title="Names of Vietnam">Names of Vietnam</a></div>
<p>The official name of the South Vietnamese state was <i>Việt Nam Cộng hòa</i> (Republic of Vietnam) and the French name was referred to as <i>République du Viêt Nam</i>. The North was known as the "Democratic Republic of Vietnam".
</p><p><i>Việt Nam</i> (<small>Vietnamese pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Vietnamese" title="Help:IPA/Vietnamese">[vjə̀tnam]</a></span>) was the name adopted by Emperor <a href="/wiki/Gia_Long" title="Gia Long">Gia Long</a> in 1804.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated38_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated38-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It is a variation of "<a href="/wiki/Nam_Vi%E1%BB%87t" class="mw-redirect" title="Nam Việt">Nam Việt</a>" (<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%8D%97" class="extiw" title="wikt:南">南</a> <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%B6%8A" class="extiw" title="wikt:越">越</a>, <i>Southern <a href="/wiki/Yue_peoples" class="mw-redirect" title="Yue peoples">Việt</a></i>), a name used in ancient times.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated38_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated38-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 1839, Emperor <a href="/wiki/Minh_M%E1%BA%A1ng" title="Minh Mạng">Minh Mạng</a> renamed the country <i>Đại Nam</i> ("Great South").<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In 1945, the nation's official name was changed back to "Vietnam". The name is also sometimes rendered as "Viet Nam" in English.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The term "South Vietnam" became common usage in 1954, when the <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Conference_(1954)" class="mw-redirect" title="Geneva Conference (1954)">Geneva Conference</a> provisionally partitioned Vietnam into communist and non-communist parts.
</p><p>Other names of this state were commonly used during its existence such as <i>Free Vietnam</i> and the <i>Government of Viet Nam</i> (GVN).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding_of_Vietnam">Founding of Vietnam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Founding of Vietnam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:030630-N-0000X-001.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/030630-N-0000X-001.jpg/250px-030630-N-0000X-001.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="312" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/030630-N-0000X-001.jpg/375px-030630-N-0000X-001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/030630-N-0000X-001.jpg/500px-030630-N-0000X-001.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2271" data-file-height="2835" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:030630-N-0000X-001.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>About 1 million Vietnamese refugees left the newly created communist North Vietnam during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Passage_to_Freedom" title="Operation Passage to Freedom">Operation "Passage to Freedom"</a> (October 1954).</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Conference_(1954)" class="mw-redirect" title="Geneva Conference (1954)">Geneva Conference (1954)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Passage_to_Freedom" title="Operation Passage to Freedom">Operation Passage to Freedom</a>, and <a href="/wiki/1955_State_of_Vietnam_referendum" title="1955 State of Vietnam referendum">1955 State of Vietnam referendum</a></div>
<p>Before World War II, the southern third of Vietnam was the concession (nhượng địa) of <a href="/wiki/French_Cochinchina" title="French Cochinchina">Cochinchina</a>, which was administered as part of <a href="/wiki/French_Indochina" title="French Indochina">French Indochina</a>. A French governor-general (toàn quyền) in Hanoi administered all the five parts of Indochina (Tonkin, Annam, Cochinchina, Laos, and Cambodia) while Cochinchina (Nam Kỳ) was under a French governor (thống đốc), but the difference from the other parts was that most indigenous intellensia and wealthy were naturalized French (Tourane of Đà Nẵng in the central third of Vietnam also enjoyed this privilege because this city was a concession too.) The northern third of Vietnam (then the <a href="/wiki/Colony" title="Colony">colony</a> (thuộc địa) of <a href="/wiki/Tonkin" title="Tonkin">Tonkin</a> (Bắc Kỳ) was under a French resident general (thống sứ).  Between Tonkin in the north and Cochinchina in the south was the protectorate (xứ bảo hộ) of <a href="/wiki/Annam_(French_protectorate)" title="Annam (French protectorate)">Annam</a> (Trung Kỳ), under a French resident superior (khâm sứ).  A Vietnamese emperor, <a href="/wiki/B%E1%BA%A3o_%C4%90%E1%BA%A1i" title="Bảo Đại">Bảo Đại</a>, residing in <a href="/wiki/Hu%E1%BA%BF" title="Huế">Huế</a>, was the nominal ruler of Annam and Tonkin, which had parallel French and Vietnamese systems of administration, but his influence was less in Tonkin than in Annam.  Cochinchina had been annexed by France in 1862 and even elected a deputy to the <a href="/wiki/French_National_Assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="French National Assembly">French National Assembly</a>. It was more <a href="/wiki/%C3%89volu%C3%A9" title="Évolué">"evolved"</a>, and French interests were stronger than in other parts of <a href="/wiki/Indochina" class="mw-redirect" title="Indochina">Indochina</a>, notably in the form of French-owned <a href="/wiki/Rubber_plantation" class="mw-redirect" title="Rubber plantation">rubber plantations</a>. <a href="/wiki/French_Indochina_in_World_War_II" title="French Indochina in World War II">During World War II</a>, Indochina was administered by <a href="/wiki/Vichy_France" title="Vichy France">Vichy France</a> and occupied by Japan. Japanese troops overthrew the French administration on 9 March 1945, Emperor Bảo Đại proclaimed Vietnam independent. When the Japanese surrendered in 1945, Emperor Bảo Đại abdicated, and Việt Minh leader <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh" title="Ho Chi Minh">Hồ Chí Minh</a> proclaimed the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Vietnam" class="mw-redirect" title="Democratic Republic of Vietnam">Democratic Republic of Vietnam</a> (DRV) in Hanoi and the DRV controlled almost the entire country of Vietnam. In June 1946, France declared Cochinchina a republic, separate from the northern and central parts. A victorious <a href="/wiki/National_Revolutionary_Army" title="National Revolutionary Army">Chinese Kuomintang army</a> arrived in Vietnam's northern part of parallel 17. The <a href="/wiki/French_Indochina_War" class="mw-redirect" title="French Indochina War">French Indochina War</a> began on 19 December 1946, with the French regaining control of Hanoi and many other cities.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/State_of_Vietnam" title="State of Vietnam">State of Vietnam</a> was created through co-operation between <a href="/wiki/Anti-communist" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-communist">anti-communist</a> Vietnamese and the French government on 14 June 1949. Former emperor <a href="/wiki/B%E1%BA%A3o_%C4%90%E1%BA%A1i" title="Bảo Đại">Bảo Đại</a> accepted the position of chief of state (<i>quốc trưởng</i>). This was known as the "Bảo Đại Solution". The colonial struggle in Vietnam became part of the global <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>. In 1950, China, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and other communist nations recognised the DRV while the United States and other non-communist states recognised the Bảo Đại government.
</p><p>In July 1954, France and the Việt Minh (later the <a href="/wiki/Vi%E1%BB%87t_C%E1%BB%99ng" class="mw-redirect" title="Việt Cộng">Việt Cộng</a>) agreed at the <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Conference_(1954)" class="mw-redirect" title="Geneva Conference (1954)">Geneva Conference</a> that the <a href="/wiki/State_of_Vietnam" title="State of Vietnam">State of Vietnam</a> would rule the territory south of the <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_Demilitarized_Zone" title="Vietnamese Demilitarized Zone">17th parallel</a>, pending unification on the basis of supervised elections in 1956. At the time of the conference, it was expected that the South would continue to be a French dependency. However, South Vietnamese Premier <a href="/wiki/Ng%C3%B4_%C4%90%C3%ACnh_Di%E1%BB%87m" class="mw-redirect" title="Ngô Đình Diệm">Ngô Đình Diệm</a>, who preferred American sponsorship to French, rejected the agreement. When Vietnam was divided, 800,000 to 1 million North Vietnamese, mainly (but not exclusively) Roman Catholics, sailed south as part of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Passage_to_Freedom" title="Operation Passage to Freedom">Operation Passage to Freedom</a> due to a fear of religious persecution in the North.
</p>
<h3><span id="1955.E2.80.931963"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1955–1963">1955–1963</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1955–1963">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ng%C3%B4_%C4%90%C3%ACnh_Di%E1%BB%87m" class="mw-redirect" title="Ngô Đình Diệm">Ngô Đình Diệm</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/1955_State_of_Vietnam_referendum" title="1955 State of Vietnam referendum">1955 State of Vietnam referendum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Saigon_(1955)" title="Battle of Saigon (1955)">Battle of Saigon (1955)</a></div>
<p>In July 1955, Diệm announced in a broadcast that South Vietnam would not participate in the elections specified in the Geneva accords.<sup id="cite_ref-Cheng1997p11_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cheng1997p11-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> As Saigon's delegation did not sign the Geneva accords, it was not bound by it.<sup id="cite_ref-Cheng1997p11_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cheng1997p11-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He also said the communist government in the North created conditions that made a fair election impossible in that region.  This view was supported by the <a href="/wiki/International_Control_Commission" title="International Control Commission">International Control Commission</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> in the circumstances prevailing in 1955 and 1956 – anarchy of the Sects and of the retiring Việt Minh in the South, the 1956 campaign of terror from Hanoi's land reform and resultant <a href="/wiki/Quynh_Luu_Uprising" class="mw-redirect" title="Quynh Luu Uprising">peasant uprising</a> around <a href="/wiki/Vinh" title="Vinh">Vinh</a> in the North.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Diệm held a referendum on 23 October 1955 to determine the future of the country. He asked voters to approve a republic, thus removing <a href="/wiki/B%E1%BA%A3o_%C4%90%E1%BA%A1i" title="Bảo Đại">Bảo Đại</a> as head of state. The poll was supervised by his younger brother, <a href="/wiki/Ng%C3%B4_%C4%90%C3%ACnh_Nhu" title="Ngô Đình Nhu">Ngô Đình Nhu</a>. Diệm was credited with 98 percent of the votes. In many districts, there were more votes to remove Bảo Đại than there were registered voters (e.g., in Saigon, 133% of the registered population reportedly voted to remove Bảo Đại). His American advisors had recommended a more modest winning margin of "60 to 70 percent". Diệm, however, viewed the election as a test of authority.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> On 26 October 1955, Diệm declared himself the president of the newly proclaimed Republic of Vietnam.<sup id="cite_ref-historyplace_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-historyplace-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The French, who needed troops to fight in <a href="/wiki/Algerian_War" title="Algerian War">Algeria</a>, completely withdrew from Vietnam by April 1956.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Diệm attempted to stabilise South Vietnam by defending against Việt Cộng activities. He launched an anti-communist denunciation campaign (To Cong) against remnants of the communist Việt Cộng. He acted against criminal factions by launching military campaigns against three powerful main sects; the <a href="/wiki/Cao_%C4%90%C3%A0i" class="mw-redirect" title="Cao Đài">Cao Đài</a>, <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B2a_H%E1%BA%A3o" title="Hòa Hảo">Hòa Hảo</a> and the <a href="/wiki/B%C3%ACnh_Xuy%C3%AAn" title="Bình Xuyên">Bình Xuyên</a> organised crime syndicate whose military strength combined amounted to approximately 350,000 soldiers.
</p><p>Throughout this period, the level of US aid and political support increased. In spite of this, a 1961 <a href="/wiki/National_Intelligence_Estimate" title="National Intelligence Estimate">US intelligence estimate</a> reported that "one-half of the entire rural region south and southwest of Saigon, as well as some areas to the north, are under considerable Communist control. Some of these areas are in effect <a href="/wiki/Failed_state" title="Failed state">denied to all government authority</a> not immediately backed by substantial armed force. The Việt Cộng's strength encircles Saigon and has recently begun to move closer in the city."<sup id="cite_ref-auto_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The report, later excerpted in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon_Papers" class="mw-redirect" title="The Pentagon Papers">The Pentagon Papers</a></i>, continued:
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>Many feel that [Diem] is unable to rally the people in the fight against the Communists because of his reliance on virtual one-man rule, his tolerance of corruption extending even to his immediate entourage, and his refusal to relax a rigid system of public controls.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<h3><span id="1963.E2.80.931973"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1963–1973">1963–1973</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1963–1973">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1967_Elections.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/1967_Elections.jpg/170px-1967_Elections.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/1967_Elections.jpg/255px-1967_Elections.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/1967_Elections.jpg/340px-1967_Elections.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2448" data-file-height="3264" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1967_Elections.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A woman casting her ballot in the 1967 Elections in the Republic of Vietnam</div></div></div>
<p>The Diệm government lost support among the populace, and from the <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy#Presidency" title="John F. Kennedy">Kennedy administration</a>, due to its repression of Buddhists and military defeats by the Việt Cộng. Notably, the <a href="/wiki/Hu%E1%BA%BF_Ph%E1%BA%ADt_%C4%90%E1%BA%A3n_shootings" title="Huế Phật Đản shootings">Huế Phật Đản shootings</a> of 8 May 1963 led to the <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_crisis" title="Buddhist crisis">Buddhist crisis</a>, provoking widespread protests and <a href="/wiki/Civil_resistance" title="Civil resistance">civil resistance</a>. Diệm was <a href="/wiki/Arrest_and_assassination_of_Ngo_Dinh_Diem" title="Arrest and assassination of Ngo Dinh Diem">overthrown in a coup</a> on 1 November 1963 with the tacit approval of the US.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Diệm's removal and assassination set off a period of political instability and declining legitimacy of the Saigon government. General <a href="/wiki/D%C6%B0%C6%A1ng_V%C4%83n_Minh" title="Dương Văn Minh">Dương Văn Minh</a> became president, but he was <a href="/wiki/1964_South_Vietnamese_coup" title="1964 South Vietnamese coup">ousted in January 1964</a> by General <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_Kh%C3%A1nh" title="Nguyễn Khánh">Nguyễn Khánh</a>. <a href="/wiki/Phan_Kh%E1%BA%AFc_S%E1%BB%ADu" title="Phan Khắc Sửu">Phan Khắc Sửu</a> was named head of state, but power remained with a junta of generals led by Khánh, which soon fell to infighting. Meanwhile, the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Tonkin_incident" title="Gulf of Tonkin incident">Gulf of Tonkin incident</a> of 2 August 1964 led to a dramatic increase in direct American participation in the war, with nearly 200,000 troops deployed by the end of the year. Khánh sought to capitalize on the crisis with the <a href="/wiki/V%C5%A9ng_T%C3%A0u_Charter" title="Vũng Tàu Charter">Vũng Tàu Charter</a>, a new constitution that would have curtailed civil liberties and concentrated his power, but was forced to back down in the face of widespread protests and strikes. Coup attempts followed <a href="/wiki/September_1964_South_Vietnamese_coup_attempt" title="September 1964 South Vietnamese coup attempt">in September</a> and February, the latter resulting in Air Marshall <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_Cao_K%E1%BB%B3" title="Nguyễn Cao Kỳ">Nguyễn Cao Kỳ</a> becoming prime minister and <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_V%C4%83n_Thi%E1%BB%87u" title="Nguyễn Văn Thiệu">Nguyễn Văn Thiệu</a> becoming nominal head of state.
</p><p>Kỳ and Thieu functioned in those roles until 1967, bringing much-desired stability to the government. They imposed censorship and suspended civil liberties, and intensified anticommunist efforts. Under pressure from the US, they held elections for president and the legislature in 1967, Thiệu being elected president with 34% of the vote in a widely criticised poll.
</p><p>On 31 January 1968, the NVA and the Việt Cộng broke the traditional truce accompanying the <a href="/wiki/T%E1%BA%BFt" title="Tết">Tết</a> (Lunar New Year) holiday. The so-called <a href="/wiki/Tet_Offensive" title="Tet Offensive">Tet Offensive</a> failed to spark a national uprising, and was militarily disastrous. By bringing the war to South Vietnam's cities, however, and by demonstrating the continued strength of communist forces, it marked a turning point in US support for the government in South Vietnam. The new administration of <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> introduced a policy of <a href="/wiki/Vietnamization" title="Vietnamization">Vietnamization</a> to reduce US combat involvement. Thiệu used the aftermath of the Tet Offensive to sideline Kỳ, his chief rival, and ran for re-election unopposed in 1971.
</p>
<h3><span id="1973.E2.80.931975"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1973–1975">1973–1975</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1973–1975">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Scene_of_Viet_Cong_terrorist_bombing_in_Saigon,_Republic_of_Vietnam.,_1965.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Scene_of_Viet_Cong_terrorist_bombing_in_Saigon%2C_Republic_of_Vietnam.%2C_1965.jpg/240px-Scene_of_Viet_Cong_terrorist_bombing_in_Saigon%2C_Republic_of_Vietnam.%2C_1965.jpg" decoding="async" width="240" height="190" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Scene_of_Viet_Cong_terrorist_bombing_in_Saigon%2C_Republic_of_Vietnam.%2C_1965.jpg/360px-Scene_of_Viet_Cong_terrorist_bombing_in_Saigon%2C_Republic_of_Vietnam.%2C_1965.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Scene_of_Viet_Cong_terrorist_bombing_in_Saigon%2C_Republic_of_Vietnam.%2C_1965.jpg/480px-Scene_of_Viet_Cong_terrorist_bombing_in_Saigon%2C_Republic_of_Vietnam.%2C_1965.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2626" data-file-height="2076" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Scene_of_Viet_Cong_terrorist_bombing_in_Saigon,_Republic_of_Vietnam.,_1965.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Scene of a <a href="/wiki/Viet_Cong" title="Viet Cong">Việt Cộng</a> bombing in a residential area of <a href="/wiki/Saigon" class="mw-redirect" title="Saigon">Saigon</a>, 1965</div></div></div>
<p>In accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Peace_Accords" title="Paris Peace Accords">Paris Peace Accords</a> signed with <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnam" title="North Vietnam">North Vietnam</a> on 27 January 1973, US military forces withdrew from South Vietnam. North Vietnam was allowed to continue supplying communist troops in the South, but only to the extent of replacing materials that were consumed.
</p><p>The communist leaders had expected that the ceasefire terms would favour their side. But as Saigon began to roll back the Việt Cộng, they found it necessary to adopt a new strategy, hammered out at a series of meetings in Hanoi in March 1973, according to the memoirs of <a href="/wiki/Tr%E1%BA%A7n_V%C4%83n_Tr%C3%A0" title="Trần Văn Trà">Trần Văn Trà</a>. As the Việt Cộng's top commander, Trà participated in several of these meetings. A plan to improve logistics was prepared so that the <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnamese_army" class="mw-redirect" title="North Vietnamese army">North Vietnamese army</a> would be able to launch a massive invasion of the South, projected for 1976, before <a href="/wiki/Army_of_the_Republic_of_Vietnam" title="Army of the Republic of Vietnam">Saigon's army</a> could be fully trained. A gas pipeline would be built from North Vietnam to Việt Cộng headquarters in <a href="/wiki/L%E1%BB%99c_Ninh" title="Lộc Ninh">Lộc Ninh</a>, about 60 miles (97&#160;km) north of Saigon.
</p><p>On 15 March 1973, US President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> implied that the US would intervene militarily if the communist side violated the ceasefire. Public reaction was unfavorable, and on 4 June 1973 the <a href="/wiki/US_Senate" class="mw-redirect" title="US Senate">US Senate</a> passed the <a href="/wiki/Case%E2%80%93Church_Amendment" title="Case–Church Amendment">Case–Church Amendment</a> to prohibit such intervention. The <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">oil price shock of October 1973</a> caused significant damage to the South Vietnamese economy. A spokesman for Thiệu admitted in a TV interview that the government was being "overwhelmed" by the inflation caused by the oil shock, while an American businessman living in Saigon stated after the oil shock that attempting to make money in South Vietnam was "like making love to a corpse".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> One consequence of the inflation was the South Vietnamese government had increasing difficulty in paying its soldiers. The Việt Cộng resumed offensive operations, and by January 1974 it had recaptured the territory that it had lost earlier. After two clashes that left 55 South Vietnamese soldiers dead, President Thieu announced on 4 January that the war had restarted and that the Paris Peace Accord was no longer in effect. There were over 25,000 South Vietnamese casualties during the ceasefire period.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In August 1974, Nixon was forced to resign as a result of the <a href="/wiki/Watergate_scandal" title="Watergate scandal">Watergate scandal</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/US_Congress" class="mw-redirect" title="US Congress">US Congress</a> voted to reduce assistance to South Vietnam from $1&#160;billion a year to $700&#160;million. By this time, the <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh_trail" title="Ho Chi Minh trail">Ho Chi Minh trail</a>, once an arduous mountain trek, had been upgraded into a drivable highway with gasoline stations.
</p><p>In December 1974, the communists of North Vietnam <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ph%C6%B0%E1%BB%9Bc_Long" title="Battle of Phước Long">launched an invasion first at Phuoc Long</a> to test two things: how much strengths and weaknesses the ARVN soldiers preparedness on the elevated terrain combat and the U.S military response on PAVN invasion. With the U.S unable to send soldiers to South Vietnam, ARVN soldiers in Phuoc Long couldn't defend any longer the siege around the districts near provincial capital Phuoc Long. PAVN soldiers, with a lot of ammunition and supplies, successfully captured many of the districts around the provincial capital of Phuoc Long weakening ARVN resistance in stronghold areas. President Thieu later abandoned Phuoc Long in early January 1975. As a result, Phuoc Long was the first provincial capital to fall to North Vietnamese communists.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1975, the communists of North Vietnam launched an offensive in the South, which became known as the <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh_Campaign" class="mw-redirect" title="Ho Chi Minh Campaign">Ho Chi Minh Campaign</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Army_of_the_Republic_of_Vietnam" title="Army of the Republic of Vietnam">Army of the Republic of Vietnam</a> unsuccessfully attempted a defence and counterattack. It had few remaining operational tanks and artillery pieces, as well as a shortage of spare parts and ammunition. The NVA had a vastly greater supply of new equipment and ammunition. As a consequence, South Vietnamese President <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_V%C4%83n_Thi%E1%BB%87u" title="Nguyễn Văn Thiệu">Nguyễn Văn Thiệu</a> was forced to withdraw key army units from the <a href="/wiki/Central_Highlands_(Vietnam)" title="Central Highlands (Vietnam)">Central Highlands</a>, which exacerbated an already perilous military situation and undercut the confidence of the ARVN soldiers in their leadership.
</p><p>The retreat became a rout. The cities of <a href="/wiki/Hu%E1%BA%BF" title="Huế">Huế</a>, <a href="/wiki/Da_Nang" title="Da Nang">Da Nang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Da_Lat" title="Da Lat">Da Lat</a> in central Vietnam quickly fell, and the North Vietnamese advanced southwards. As the military situation deteriorated, ARVN troops started deserting. By early April, South Vietnam had lost almost 3/5th of the land.
</p><p>Thiệu requested aid from US President <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Gerald Ford</a>, but the <a href="/wiki/US_Senate" class="mw-redirect" title="US Senate">US Senate</a> would not release extra money to provide aid to South Vietnam, and had already passed laws to prevent further involvement in Vietnam. In desperation, Thiệu recalled <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_Cao_K%E1%BB%B3" title="Nguyễn Cao Kỳ">Nguyễn Cao Kỳ</a> from retirement as a military commander, but resisted calls to name his old rival prime minister.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fall_of_Saigon:_April_1975">Fall of Saigon: April 1975</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Fall of Saigon: April 1975">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Saigon" title="Fall of Saigon">Fall of Saigon</a></div>
<p>In 1974 China attacked South Vietnam in the <a href="/wiki/Paracel_Islands" title="Paracel Islands">Paracel Islands</a>, further weakening South Vietnam's army. Morale was low in South Vietnam as the PAVN quickly advanced.   <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_V%C4%83n_Thi%E1%BB%87u" title="Nguyễn Văn Thiệu">Nguyễn Văn Thiệu</a> resigned on 21 April 1975, and fled to <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>. He nominated his Vice President <a href="/wiki/Tr%E1%BA%A7n_V%C4%83n_H%C6%B0%C6%A1ng" title="Trần Văn Hương">Trần Văn Hương</a> as his successor. A last-ditch defense was made by the <a href="/wiki/18th_Division_(South_Vietnam)" title="18th Division (South Vietnam)">ARVN 18th Division</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Xu%C3%A2n_L%E1%BB%99c" title="Battle of Xuân Lộc">Battle of Xuân Lộc</a> led by Major General <a href="/wiki/L%C3%AA_Minh_%C4%90%E1%BA%A3o" title="Lê Minh Đảo">Lê Minh Đảo</a>. After only one week in office, Trần Văn Hương handed over the presidency to General <a href="/wiki/D%C6%B0%C6%A1ng_V%C4%83n_Minh" title="Dương Văn Minh">Dương Văn Minh</a> ("Big Minh"). Minh was seen as a more conciliatory figure toward the North, and it was hoped he might be able to negotiate a more favourable settlement to end the war. The North, however, was not interested in negotiations, and its tanks rolled into <a href="/wiki/Saigon" class="mw-redirect" title="Saigon">Saigon</a> largely unopposed which led to the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Saigon" title="Fall of Saigon">Fall of Saigon</a>. Acting President Minh <a href="/wiki/Unconditional_surrender" title="Unconditional surrender">unconditionally surrendered</a> the capital city of Saigon and the rest of South Vietnam to <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnam" title="North Vietnam">North Vietnam</a> on 30 April 1975.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the hours leading up to the surrender, the United States undertook a massive evacuation of its embassy in Saigon, <a href="/wiki/Operation_Frequent_Wind" title="Operation Frequent Wind">Operation Frequent Wind</a>. The evacuees included US government personnel as well as high-ranking members of the ARVN and other South Vietnamese who were seen as potential targets for persecution by the Communists. Many of the evacuees were taken directly by helicopter to multiple <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carriers</a> waiting off the coast. An iconic image of the evacuation is the widely seen footage of empty <a href="/wiki/UH-1_Iroquois" class="mw-redirect" title="UH-1 Iroquois">Huey</a> helicopters being jettisoned over the side of the carriers, to provide more room on the ship's deck for more evacuees to land. The evacuation was forced to stop by the <a href="/wiki/US_Navy" class="mw-redirect" title="US Navy">US Navy</a>. All the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">Marines</a> and diplomats were evacuated, but thousands of South Vietnamese citizens waited in vain at the US embassy compound, and one block away at the former USAID and CIA office space in the Pittman Apartment House on 22 Gia Long Street atop the roof for helicopters that never came.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mekong_Delta:_The_Last_Region">Mekong Delta: The Last Region</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mekong Delta: The Last Region">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the Mekong Delta, <a href="/wiki/C%E1%BA%A7n_Th%C6%A1" title="Cần Thơ">Can Tho</a> and most of the provinces were intact and strongly kept control by aggressive ARVN soldiers. Many of ARVN soldiers were strong morale on the last days of South Vietnam to prevent VC taking over any provincial capital. Despite VC skirmishes attacks, many of ARVN units manage to defend many important places for days until President Minh ordered ARVN soldiers to disband. When Saigon was about to fall to communists, Brigadier General Le Van Hung planned a secret map places in jungles and military bases that would continue prolong counterattack against VC for few months until PAVN/VC reach a ceasefire hoping to keep several places for South Vietnam. There Hung sent remaining ARVN soldiers and officers to secret places to stabilize across Mekong places. However, when President Duong Van Minh surrender, many of Can Tho residents oppose a strong resistance against outnumbered VC soldiers. Summoning to say goodbye to ARVN soldiers, staffs, and his family, Le Van Hung committed suicide at his bedroom.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> On the same day, Brigadier General Tran Van Hai, who defend strongly to maintain control in <a href="/wiki/M%E1%BB%B9_Tho" title="Mỹ Tho">Mỹ Tho</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C4%90%E1%BB%8Bnh_T%C6%B0%E1%BB%9Dng_Province" title="Định Tường Province">Định Tường</a> province, committed suicide in <a href="/wiki/%C4%90%E1%BB%93ng_T%C3%A2m_Base_Camp" title="Đồng Tâm Base Camp">Dong Tam Base</a>. The next morning, Major General <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_Khoa_Nam" title="Nguyễn Khoa Nam">Nguyen Khoa Nam</a>, who direct military operations to protect whole Mekong Delta, committed suicide at IV Corps headquarter in Can Tho. Remaining ARVN soldiers in Mekong Delta later surrender to VC shortly after three ARVN generals suicide.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_the_United_States">Relationship with the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Relationship with the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ngo_Dinh_Diem_at_Washington_-_ARC_542189.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Ngo_Dinh_Diem_at_Washington_-_ARC_542189.jpg/220px-Ngo_Dinh_Diem_at_Washington_-_ARC_542189.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Ngo_Dinh_Diem_at_Washington_-_ARC_542189.jpg/330px-Ngo_Dinh_Diem_at_Washington_-_ARC_542189.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Ngo_Dinh_Diem_at_Washington_-_ARC_542189.jpg/440px-Ngo_Dinh_Diem_at_Washington_-_ARC_542189.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2961" data-file-height="2356" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ngo_Dinh_Diem_at_Washington_-_ARC_542189.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>US President <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a> and Secretary of State <a href="/wiki/John_Foster_Dulles" title="John Foster Dulles">John Foster Dulles</a> greet President <a href="/wiki/Ng%C3%B4_%C4%90%C3%ACnh_Di%E1%BB%87m" class="mw-redirect" title="Ngô Đình Diệm">Ngô Đình Diệm</a> of South Vietnam in Washington, 8 May 1957.</div></div></div>
<p>The Geneva Accords promised elections in 1956 to determine a national government for a united Vietnam. In 1957, independent observers from India, Poland, and Canada representing the <a href="/wiki/International_Control_Commission" title="International Control Commission">International Control Commission</a> (ICC) stated that fair, unbiased elections were not possible, reporting that neither South nor North Vietnam had honored the armistice agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After promising not to do so during the 1964 election campaign, in 1965 President <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a> decided to send in much larger numbers of combat troops, and conflict steadily escalated. The NLF ceased to be an effective fighting organization after the <a href="/wiki/Tet_Offensive" title="Tet Offensive">Tet Offensive</a> in 1968 and the war was largely taken over by regular army units of North Vietnam. Following American withdrawal from the war in 1973, the South Vietnamese government continued fighting until its <a href="/wiki/Unconditional_surrender" title="Unconditional surrender">unconditional surrender</a> to the Việt Cộng on 30 April 1975, the day of the <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Saigon" title="Fall of Saigon">surrender of Saigon</a>. North Vietnam controlled South Vietnam under military <a href="/wiki/Military_occupation" title="Military occupation">occupation</a>, while the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Revolutionary_Government_of_the_Republic_of_South_Vietnam" title="Provisional Revolutionary Government of the Republic of South Vietnam">Provisional Revolutionary Government of the Republic of South Vietnam</a>, which had been proclaimed in June 1969 by the NLF, became the nominal government. The North Vietnamese quickly moved to marginalise non-communist members of the PRG and integrate South Vietnam into the communist North. The unified <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Republic_of_Vietnam" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialist Republic of Vietnam">Socialist Republic of Vietnam</a> was declared on 2 July 1976. The Embassy of the Republic of Vietnam in Washington donated 527 reels of South Vietnamese-produced film to the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a> during the embassy's closure following the Fall of Saigon, which are in the Library to this day.<sup id="cite_ref-IATH_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IATH-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/1955_State_of_Vietnam_referendum" title="1955 State of Vietnam referendum">1955 State of Vietnam referendum</a>; <a href="/wiki/South_Vietnamese_parliamentary_election,_1959" class="mw-redirect" title="South Vietnamese parliamentary election, 1959">South Vietnamese parliamentary election, 1959</a>; <a href="/wiki/1960_South_Vietnamese_coup_attempt" title="1960 South Vietnamese coup attempt">1960 South Vietnamese coup attempt</a>; <a href="/wiki/1962_South_Vietnamese_Independence_Palace_bombing" title="1962 South Vietnamese Independence Palace bombing">1962 South Vietnamese Independence Palace bombing</a>; <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_crisis" title="Buddhist crisis">Buddhist crisis</a>; <a href="/wiki/Hu%E1%BA%BF_Vesak_shootings" class="mw-redirect" title="Huế Vesak shootings">Huế Vesak shootings</a>; <a href="/wiki/X%C3%A1_L%E1%BB%A3i_Pagoda_raids" title="Xá Lợi Pagoda raids">Xá Lợi Pagoda raids</a>; <a href="/wiki/1963_South_Vietnamese_coup" title="1963 South Vietnamese coup">1963 South Vietnamese coup</a>; <a href="/wiki/Arrest_and_assassination_of_Ng%C3%B4_%C4%90%C3%ACnh_Di%E1%BB%87m" class="mw-redirect" title="Arrest and assassination of Ngô Đình Diệm">Arrest and assassination of Ngô Đình Diệm</a>; and <a href="/wiki/1964_South_Vietnamese_coup" title="1964 South Vietnamese coup">1964 South Vietnamese coup</a></div>
<p>South Vietnam went through many political changes during its short life. Initially, former Emperor <a href="/wiki/B%E1%BA%A3o_%C4%90%E1%BA%A1i" title="Bảo Đại">Bảo Đại</a> served as <a href="/wiki/Head_of_State" class="mw-redirect" title="Head of State">Head of State</a>. He was unpopular however, largely because monarchical leaders were considered collaborators during French rule and because he had spent his reign absent in France.
</p><p>In 1955, Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Ng%C3%B4_%C4%90%C3%ACnh_Di%E1%BB%87m" class="mw-redirect" title="Ngô Đình Diệm">Ngô Đình Diệm</a> held a <a href="/wiki/1955_State_of_Vietnam_referendum" title="1955 State of Vietnam referendum">referendum</a> to decide whether the State of Vietnam would remain a monarchy or become a republic. This referendum was blatantly rigged in favor of a republic. Not only did an implausible 98% vote in favor of deposing Bảo Đại, but over 380,000 more votes were cast than the total number of registered voters; in <a href="/wiki/Saigon" class="mw-redirect" title="Saigon">Saigon</a>, for instance, Diệm was credited with 133% of the vote. Diệm proclaimed himself the president of the newly formed Republic of Vietnam. Despite successes in politics, economics and social change in the first 5 years, Diệm quickly became a dictatorial leader. With the support of the United States government and the <a href="/wiki/CIA" class="mw-redirect" title="CIA">CIA</a>, ARVN officers led by General <a href="/wiki/D%C6%B0%C6%A1ng_V%C4%83n_Minh" title="Dương Văn Minh">Dương Văn Minh</a> staged a coup and killed him in 1963. The military held a brief interim military government until General <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_Kh%C3%A1nh" title="Nguyễn Khánh">Nguyễn Khánh</a> <a href="/wiki/1964_South_Vietnamese_coup" title="1964 South Vietnamese coup">deposed Minh</a> in a January 1964 coup. Until late 1965, multiple coups and changes of government occurred, with some civilians being allowed to give a semblance of civil rule overseen by a military junta.
</p><p>In 1965, the feuding civilian government voluntarily resigned and handed power back to the nation's military, in the hope this would bring stability and unity to the nation. An elected constituent assembly including representatives of all the branches of the military decided to switch the nation's system of government to a Semi-Presidential system. There was a <a href="/wiki/Bicameral" class="mw-redirect" title="Bicameral">bicameral</a> <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly" title="National Assembly">National Assembly</a> consisting of a <a href="/wiki/Senate" title="Senate">Senate</a> and a <a href="/wiki/House_of_Representatives" title="House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a>, which came into being in 1967. Military rule initially failed to provide much stability however, as internal conflicts and political inexperience caused various factions of the army to launch coups and counter-coups against one another, making leadership very tumultuous. The situation within the ranks of the military stabilised in mid-1965 when the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Vietnam_Air_Force" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of Vietnam Air Force">Republic of Vietnam Air Force</a> chief <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_Cao_K%E1%BB%B3" title="Nguyễn Cao Kỳ">Nguyễn Cao Kỳ</a> became Prime Minister, with General <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_V%C4%83n_Thi%E1%BB%87u" title="Nguyễn Văn Thiệu">Nguyễn Văn Thiệu</a> as the figurehead chief of state.  As Prime Minister, Kỳ consolidated control of the South Vietnamese government and ruled the country with an iron fist.<sup id="cite_ref-gen3231_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gen3231-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 1965, Kỳ's influence over the ruling military government was solidified when he forced civilian prime minister <a href="/wiki/Phan_Huy_Qu%C3%A1t" title="Phan Huy Quát">Phan Huy Quát</a> from power.<sup id="cite_ref-gen264_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gen264-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Often praising aspects of Western culture in public,<sup id="cite_ref-gen264_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gen264-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Ky was supported by the United States and its allied nations,<sup id="cite_ref-gen264_24-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gen264-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> though doubts began to circulate among Western officials by 1966 on whether or not Ky could maintain stability in South Vietnam.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> A repressive leader, Ky was greatly despised by his fellow countrymen.<sup id="cite_ref-gen3231_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gen3231-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In early 1966, protesters influenced by popular Buddhist monk <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%ADch_Tr%C3%AD_Quang" title="Thích Trí Quang">Thích Trí Quang</a> attempted an uprising in Quang's hometown of Da Nang.<sup id="cite_ref-gen3231_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gen3231-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The uprising was unsuccessful and Ky's repressive stance towards the nation's Buddhist population continued.<sup id="cite_ref-gen3231_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gen3231-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1967, South Vietnam held its first elections under the new system. Following the elections, however, it switched back to a presidential system. The military nominated Nguyễn Văn Thiệu as their candidate, and he was elected with a <a href="/wiki/Plurality_(voting)" title="Plurality (voting)">plurality</a> of the popular vote. Thieu quickly consolidated power much to the dismay of those who hoped for an era of more political openness. He was re-elected unopposed in 1971, receiving a suspiciously high 94% of the vote on an 87% turn-out. Thieu ruled until the final days of the war, resigning in April 1975. <a href="/wiki/D%C6%B0%C6%A1ng_V%C4%83n_Minh" title="Dương Văn Minh">Dương Văn Minh</a> was the nation's last president and unconditionally surrendered to the Communist forces a few days after assuming office.
</p><p>South Vietnam was formerly a member of <a href="/wiki/Agence_de_Coop%C3%A9ration_Culturelle_et_Technique" title="Agence de Coopération Culturelle et Technique">ACCT</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asian_Development_Bank" title="Asian Development Bank">Asian Development Bank</a> (ADB), <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> (IBRD), <a href="/wiki/International_Development_Association" title="International Development Association">International Development Association</a> (IDA), International Finance Corporation (IFC), <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">IMF</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_Telecommunications_Satellite_Organization" title="International Telecommunications Satellite Organization">International Telecommunications Satellite Organization</a> (Intelsat), <a href="/wiki/International_Criminal_Police_Organization" class="mw-redirect" title="International Criminal Police Organization">Interpol</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">IOC</a>, <a href="/wiki/ITU" class="mw-redirect" title="ITU">ITU</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red_Cross" class="mw-redirect" title="Red Cross">League of Red Cross and Red Crescent Societies</a> (LORCS), <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Educational,_Scientific_and_Cultural_Organization" class="mw-redirect" title="United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization">UNESCO</a> and <a href="/wiki/Universal_Postal_Union" title="Universal Postal Union">Universal Postal Union</a> (UPU).
</p><p>The South Vietnamese government was regularly accused of holding large quantity of political prisoners, of which the exact number was a source of contention. <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">Amnesty International</a>, in a report in 1973, gave the estimation of number of South Vietnam's civilian prisoners ranging from 35,257 (as confirmed by Saigon) to 200,000 or more. Among them, approximately 22,000–41,000 were accounted "communist" political prisoners.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Robert F. Turner disputed the figure of 200,000, claiming the actual number to be "at the worst&#160;[...] a few hundred or so."<sup id="cite_ref-Turner_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turner-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Provisional_Revolutionary_Government">Provisional Revolutionary Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Provisional Revolutionary Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Revolutionary_Government_of_the_Republic_of_South_Vietnam" title="Provisional Revolutionary Government of the Republic of South Vietnam">Provisional Revolutionary Government of the Republic of South Vietnam</a></div>
<p>Following the surrender of Saigon to North Vietnamese and Việt Cộng forces on 30 April 1975, the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Revolutionary_Government_of_the_Republic_of_South_Vietnam" title="Provisional Revolutionary Government of the Republic of South Vietnam">Provisional Revolutionary Government of the Republic of South Vietnam</a> officially became the government of South Vietnam.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Merger_into_the_Socialist_Republic_of_Vietnam">Merger into the Socialist Republic of Vietnam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Merger into the Socialist Republic of Vietnam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Republic_of_Vietnam" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialist Republic of Vietnam">Socialist Republic of Vietnam</a></div>
<p>the 
On June 24, 1976, following the April 25, 1976 general election for the National Assembly, the <a href="/wiki/Provisional_Revolutionary_Government_of_the_Republic_of_South_Vietnam" title="Provisional Revolutionary Government of the Republic of South Vietnam">Provisional Revolutionary Government of the Republic of South Vietnam</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Vietnam" class="mw-redirect" title="Democratic Republic of Vietnam">Democratic Republic of Vietnam</a> were merged to become the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Republic_of_Vietnam" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialist Republic of Vietnam">Socialist Republic of Vietnam</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> which was held on 25 April 1976.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leaders">Leaders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Leaders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Leaders_of_South_Vietnam" title="Leaders of South Vietnam">Leaders of South Vietnam</a></div>
<ul><li>1946–47 <b>Autonomous Republic of <a href="/wiki/French_Cochinchina" title="French Cochinchina">Cochinchina</a></b> (<i>Chính phủ Cộng hoà Nam Kỳ tự trị</i>). The creation of this republic, during the <a href="/wiki/First_Indochina_War" title="First Indochina War">First Indochina War</a> (1946–1954), allowed <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> to evade a promise to recognise Vietnam as independent. The government was renamed in 1947 <i>Provisional Governenment of South Vietnam</i>, overtly stating its aim to reunite the whole country<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_V%C4%83n_Thinh" class="mw-redirect" title="Nguyễn Văn Thinh">Nguyễn Văn Thinh</a> (1946)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/L%C3%AA_V%C4%83n_Ho%E1%BA%A1ch" title="Lê Văn Hoạch">Lê Văn Hoạch</a> (1946–47)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nguyen_Van_Xuan" class="mw-redirect" title="Nguyen Van Xuan">Nguyễn Văn Xuân</a> (1947–48)</li></ul></li>
<li>1948–49 <b><a href="/wiki/Provisional_Central_Government_of_Vietnam" title="Provisional Central Government of Vietnam">Provisional Central Government of Vietnam</a></b> (<i>Chính phủ lâm thời Quốc gia Việt Nam</i>). This "pre-Vietnam" government prepared for a unified Vietnamese state, but the country's full reunification was delayed for a year because of the problems posed by Cochinchina's legal status.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_V%C4%83n_Xu%C3%A2n" title="Nguyễn Văn Xuân">Nguyễn Văn Xuân</a> (1948–49)</li></ul></li>
<li>1949–1955 <b><a href="/wiki/State_of_Vietnam" title="State of Vietnam">State of Vietnam</a></b> (<i>Quốc gia Việt Nam</i>). Internationally recognized in 1950. Roughly 60 percent of Vietnamese territory was actually physically controlled by the communist <a href="/wiki/Vi%E1%BB%87t_Minh" title="Việt Minh">Việt Minh</a>. Vietnam was partitioned at the <a href="/wiki/17th_parallel_north" title="17th parallel north">17th parallel</a> in 1954.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/B%E1%BA%A3o_%C4%90%E1%BA%A1i" title="Bảo Đại">Bảo Đại</a> (1949–1955). Abdicated as emperor (constitutional monarch) in 1945 following surrender of <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Japan" title="Empire of Japan">Imperial Japanese</a> occupying forces at the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, later serving as Head of State to 1955.</li></ul></li>
<li>1955–1975 <b>Republic of Vietnam</b> (<i>Việt Nam Cộng Hòa</i>). Fought <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> [Second Indochina War], (1959–75) against the Hanoi government of <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh" title="Ho Chi Minh">Ho Chi Minh</a>.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ng%C3%B4_%C4%90%C3%ACnh_Di%E1%BB%87m" class="mw-redirect" title="Ngô Đình Diệm">Ngô Đình Diệm</a> (1955–1963). Once highly lauded by America, he was ousted and assassinated in a US-backed <i>coup</i> in November 1963.</li>
<li>In 1963–1965, there were numerous coups and short-lived governments, several of which were headed by <a href="/wiki/D%C6%B0%C6%A1ng_V%C4%83n_Minh" title="Dương Văn Minh">Dương Văn Minh</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_Kh%C3%A1nh" title="Nguyễn Khánh">Nguyễn Khánh</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_V%C4%83n_Thi%E1%BB%87u" title="Nguyễn Văn Thiệu">Nguyễn Văn Thiệu</a> (1965–1975). Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Nguyen_Cao_Ky" class="mw-redirect" title="Nguyen Cao Ky">Nguyễn Cao Kỳ</a>, former charismatic maverick Air Force marshal was the top leader of the last of the military regimes in 1965–1967 before a US-backed civilian government was instituted, following a new constitution and elections in 1967, with Thieu elected president.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tr%E1%BA%A7n_V%C4%83n_H%C6%B0%C6%A1ng" title="Trần Văn Hương">Trần Văn Hương</a> (1975).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/D%C6%B0%C6%A1ng_V%C4%83n_Minh" title="Dương Văn Minh">Dương Văn Minh</a> (2nd time) (1975). Surrendered to invading Communists in Saigon's Presidential Palace when others abandoned their posts.</li></ul></li>
<li>1975–76 <b><a href="/wiki/Provisional_Revolutionary_Government_of_the_Republic_of_South_Vietnam" title="Provisional Revolutionary Government of the Republic of South Vietnam">Provisional Revolutionary Government of the Republic of South Vietnam</a></b> (<i>Chính phủ Cách mạng lâm thời Cộng hoà miền Nam Việt Nam</i>).</li></ul>
<dl><dd><ul><li><a href="/wiki/Hu%E1%BB%B3nh_T%E1%BA%A5n_Ph%C3%A1t" title="Huỳnh Tấn Phát">Huỳnh Tấn Phát</a> (1975–76)</li></ul></dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Army">Army</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Army">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Army_of_the_Republic_of_Vietnam" title="Army of the Republic of Vietnam">Army of the Republic of Vietnam</a></div><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Vietnam_Military_Forces" title="Republic of Vietnam Military Forces">Republic of Vietnam Military Forces</a></div>
<p>On 26 October 1956, the military was reorganised by the administration of President <a href="/wiki/Ng%C3%B4_%C4%90%C3%ACnh_Di%E1%BB%87m" class="mw-redirect" title="Ngô Đình Diệm">Ngô Đình Diệm</a> who established the Army of the Republic of Vietnam (ARVN, pronounced "arvin"). Early on, the focus of the army was combating the <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_warfare" title="Guerrilla warfare">guerrilla</a> fighters of the <a href="/wiki/Viet_Cong" title="Viet Cong">Việt Cộng</a>, or National Liberation Front, an insurgent movement supplied by North Vietnam. The United States, under <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">President Kennedy</a> sent advisors and a great deal of financial support to aid ARVN in combating the Việt Cộng. ARVN and President Diệm began to be criticised by the foreign press when the troops were used to crush southern religious groups like the <a href="/wiki/Cao_%C4%90%C3%A0i" class="mw-redirect" title="Cao Đài">Cao Đài</a> and <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B2a_H%E1%BA%A3o" title="Hòa Hảo">Hòa Hảo</a> as well as to raid <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_temple" title="Buddhist temple">Buddhist temples</a>, which Diệm claimed were harbouring Communist guerrillas.
</p><p>In 1963, <a href="/wiki/Ng%C3%B4_%C4%90%C3%ACnh_Di%E1%BB%87m" class="mw-redirect" title="Ngô Đình Diệm">Ngô Đình Diệm</a> was assassinated in a coup d'état carried out by ARVN officers led by <a href="/wiki/D%C6%B0%C6%A1ng_V%C4%83n_Minh" title="Dương Văn Minh">Dương Văn Minh</a> ('Big Minh'), supported by the CIA. In the confusion that followed Big Minh took power, but was only the first in a succession of ARVN generals to assume the presidency of South Vietnam in a period of intense political instability. During these years, the United States began taking full control of the war against the NLF and the role of the ARVN became less and less significant. They were also plagued by continuing problems of severe corruption among the officer corps. Although the US was highly critical of them, the ARVN continued to be entirely US armed and funded.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:CIDG_unit_training.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/CIDG_unit_training.jpg/220px-CIDG_unit_training.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="172" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/CIDG_unit_training.jpg/330px-CIDG_unit_training.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/CIDG_unit_training.jpg/440px-CIDG_unit_training.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2400" data-file-height="1874" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:CIDG_unit_training.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>CIDG Unit training</div></div></div>
<p>The ability of the <a href="/wiki/Army_of_the_Republic_of_Vietnam" title="Army of the Republic of Vietnam">Army of the Republic of Vietnam</a> to effectively root out the Việt Cộng counterinsurgency is questionable in this period. In 1963, at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ap_Bac" title="Battle of Ap Bac">Battle of Ap Bac</a>, some 1,400 ARVN troops alongside 10 US-piloted <a href="/wiki/Piasecki_H-21" title="Piasecki H-21">Piasecki H-21</a> and 5 US-piloted <a href="/wiki/Bell_UH-1_Iroquois" title="Bell UH-1 Iroquois">Bell UH-1 Iroquois</a> were defeated by 350 Việt Cộng guerrillas, more than 83 were KIA, 5 helicopters downed and 10 damaged. This would be pivotal as it demonstrated the ability of the Việt Cộng to counter helicopter assault with concentrated small-arms, recoil-less rifle and RPG fire. The <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dong_Xoai" class="mw-redirect" title="Battle of Dong Xoai">Battle of Dong Xoai</a> in 1965 was considered another defeat, as the Việt Cộng captured an area and ambushed a force deployed to recapture it before melting away when reinforcements arrived. These two tactics would form a basis for Việt Cộng strategy during the America intervention. Generals had also tended to be political appointees and corruption was rampant.
</p><p>Starting in 1969, President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Nixon</a> started the process of so-called "Vietnamization", withdrawing American forces and leaving the ARVN to fight the war against the North Vietnamese. Slowly, ARVN began to expand from its <a href="/wiki/Counter-insurgency" title="Counter-insurgency">counter-insurgency</a> role to become the primary ground defense against the <a href="/wiki/Viet_Cong" title="Viet Cong">Việt Cộng</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnamese" class="mw-redirect" title="North Vietnamese">North Vietnamese</a>. From 1969 to 1971 there were about 22,000 ARVN combat deaths per year. Starting in 1968, South Vietnam began calling up every available man for service in the ARVN, reaching a strength of a million soldiers by 1972. In 1970 they performed well in <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a> and were executing 3 times as many operations as they had during the American war period. However, the officer corps was still the biggest problem, and after the 1973 Paris Peace Accords, the ARVN lacked necessary military supplies and weapons as a result of a cutback of US financial aid and assistance.
</p><p>Relations with the public also remained poor as their only counter to Việt Cộng organising was to resurrect the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Hamlet_Program" title="Strategic Hamlet Program">Strategic Hamlet Program</a>, which many peasants resented. However, forced to carry the burden left by the Americans, the South Vietnamese army actually started to perform rather well, and in 1970 was winning the war against the Communists, though with continued American air support. The exhaustion of the North was becoming evident, and the Paris talks gave some hope of a negotiated peace, if not a victory for the North Vietnamese. Since 1973, the war shifted in favour of the Việt Cộng, who were better equipped, funded and aided by their communist allies, the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">USSR</a> and the China, than the South was by the Americans.
</p><p>The most crucial moment of truth for the ARVN came with General <a href="/wiki/V%C3%B5_Nguy%C3%AAn_Gi%C3%A1p" title="Võ Nguyên Giáp">Võ Nguyên Giáp</a>'s 1972 <a href="/wiki/Easter_Offensive" title="Easter Offensive">Easter Offensive</a>, the first all-out invasion of South Vietnam by the communists. It was code-named <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_Hu%E1%BB%87" title="Nguyễn Huệ">Nguyễn Huệ</a> after the Vietnamese emperor who defeated the Chinese in 1789. The assault combined infantry wave assaults, artillery and the first massive use of tanks by the North Vietnamese. ARVN took heavy losses, but to the surprise of many, managed to hold their ground.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Female_South_Vietnamese_Popular_Force_members_on_patrol_in_B%E1%BA%BFn_C%C3%A1t_District.JPEG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Female_South_Vietnamese_Popular_Force_members_on_patrol_in_B%E1%BA%BFn_C%C3%A1t_District.JPEG/170px-Female_South_Vietnamese_Popular_Force_members_on_patrol_in_B%E1%BA%BFn_C%C3%A1t_District.JPEG" decoding="async" width="170" height="190" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Female_South_Vietnamese_Popular_Force_members_on_patrol_in_B%E1%BA%BFn_C%C3%A1t_District.JPEG/255px-Female_South_Vietnamese_Popular_Force_members_on_patrol_in_B%E1%BA%BFn_C%C3%A1t_District.JPEG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Female_South_Vietnamese_Popular_Force_members_on_patrol_in_B%E1%BA%BFn_C%C3%A1t_District.JPEG/340px-Female_South_Vietnamese_Popular_Force_members_on_patrol_in_B%E1%BA%BFn_C%C3%A1t_District.JPEG 2x" data-file-width="2690" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Female_South_Vietnamese_Popular_Force_members_on_patrol_in_B%E1%BA%BFn_C%C3%A1t_District.JPEG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>South Vietnamese <a href="/wiki/South_Vietnamese_Popular_Force" title="South Vietnamese Popular Force">Self-Defense Force</a> of Kien Dien on patrol</div></div></div>
<p>US President Nixon dispatched more bombers to provide air support for ARVN when it seemed that South Vietnam was about to be overrun. In desperation, President <a href="/wiki/Nguy%E1%BB%85n_V%C4%83n_Thi%E1%BB%87u" title="Nguyễn Văn Thiệu">Nguyễn Văn Thiệu</a> fired the incompetent General Hoàng Xuân Lãm and replaced him with ARVN's best commander, General <a href="/wiki/Ng%C3%B4_Quang_Tr%C6%B0%E1%BB%9Fng" title="Ngô Quang Trưởng">Ngô Quang Trưởng</a>. He gave the order that all deserters would be executed and pulled enough forces together so that the North Vietnamese army failed to take <a href="/wiki/Hu%E1%BA%BF" title="Huế">Huế</a>. Finally, largely as a result of <a href="/wiki/Operation_Linebacker" title="Operation Linebacker">US air and naval support</a>, as well as determination by ARVN soldiers, the Easter Offensive was halted.
</p><p>After the signing of the Paris Peace Accords in 1973, all US armed forces withdrew from South Vietnam and theoretically the war officially ended, however clashes between ARVN and Việt Cộng forces continued.
</p><p>In 1975, the North Vietnamese again invaded the South. Lacking US air support, the ARVN could not hold them back. City after city fell to the Communists with ARVN soldiers joining the civilians trying to flee south. The North called this the "<a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh_Campaign" class="mw-redirect" title="Ho Chi Minh Campaign">Ho Chi Minh Campaign</a>". All resistance crumbled. Faced with few viable options, the South tried to form a coalition government that would be palatable to the Communists, one that favoured negotiated peace and neutrality. The new coalition government was headed by General <a href="/wiki/D%C6%B0%C6%A1ng_V%C4%83n_Minh" title="Dương Văn Minh">Dương Văn Minh</a> (Big Minh), one of the organisers of the coup in November 1963, with the full support of the CIA and President Kennedy, that killed President Ngô Đình Diệm. General <a href="/wiki/Cao_V%C4%83n_Vi%C3%AAn" title="Cao Văn Viên">Cao Văn Viên</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Colonel" title="Colonel">Colonel</a> and Commander of the Airborne Brigade, had been captured and held by the Big Minh faction and threatened with execution unless he ordered his troops to join the coup. He refused and was held captive until the end of the coup and was released only because of his close friendship with one of the coup leaders.
</p><p>Because the new coalition government would be headed by Big Minh, General Vien immediately submitted his resignation to then President of South Vietnam <a href="/wiki/Tr%E1%BA%A7n_V%C4%83n_H%C6%B0%C6%A1ng" title="Trần Văn Hương">Trần Văn Hương</a>, who succeeded President Thieu as President. President Huong, knowing the 1963 coup history, granted General Vien's resignation request, (Vien had submitted his resignation to President Thieu many times and had always been turned down). General Vien then escaped to the US as a civilian once his resignation was effective and formalised.
</p><p>The situation in South Vietnam further deteriorated, except on Mekong Delta. The ARVN tried to defend <a href="/wiki/Xu%C3%A2n_L%E1%BB%99c" class="mw-redirect" title="Xuân Lộc">Xuân Lộc</a>, their last line of defence before Saigon. General Le Minh Dao defended there for two weeks to prevent PAVN soldiers from overrun provincial capital. The ARVN forces were greatly outnumbered by the advancing North Vietnamese army. Xuân Lộc was taken and on 30 April 1975, initiated the Fall of Saigon. The North Vietnamese army captured the city, placing the Việt Cộng flag over the <a href="/wiki/Independence_Palace" title="Independence Palace">Independence Palace</a>. General <a href="/wiki/D%C6%B0%C6%A1ng_V%C4%83n_Minh" title="Dương Văn Minh">Dương Văn Minh</a>, recently appointed president by Trần Văn Hương, <a href="/wiki/Unconditional_surrender" title="Unconditional surrender">unconditionally surrendered</a> the city and government bringing the Republic of Vietnam and also the Army of the Republic of Vietnam to an end.
</p><p>In the Mekong Delta, most of the ARVN forces were intact and aggressive with an extensive military operations to prevent VC from taking over any regional districts. Weeks before South Vietnam ceased to exist, VC soldiers made a skirmishes attacks in random places but ARVN soldiers managed to repulsed VC attacks at targeted roads and provincial capitals. Despite VC captured several outposts and attacks any road routes, ARVN soldiers defended really well significantly to hold on many regional capitals until President Duong Van Minh from the radio ordered Armed Forces of Republic of Vietnam to surrender and disperse. Major General Nguyen Khoa Nam, who plan to continue counterattack after Saigon capitulated, decided to follow President's order and shortly disbanded military units. General Nam commit suicide at IV Corps headquarter on May 1, 1975. Although ARVN military operations in Mekong Delta ceased to exist, many of ARVN soldiers defended strongly well with great intelligence to stop VC from taking over important places. It was consider the last defense of stronghold Mekong Delta should South Vietnamese government retreat to Can Tho.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media">Media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>There were four AM and one FM radio stations, all of them owned by government (VTVN), named Radio Vietnam. One of them was designated as a nationwide civilian broadcast, another was for military service and the other two stations included a French language broadcast station and foreign language station broadcasting in Chinese, English, Khmer and Thai. Radio Vietnam started its operation in 1955 under then president <a href="/wiki/Ngo_Dinh_Diem" title="Ngo Dinh Diem">Ngo Dinh Diem</a>, and ceased operation on 30 April 1975, with the broadcast of surrender by <a href="/wiki/Duong_Van_Minh" class="mw-redirect" title="Duong Van Minh">Duong Van Minh</a> during the Fall of Saigon. The radio stations across the former South were later reused by the communist regime to broadcast their state-run radio service.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Television was introduced to South Vietnam on 7 February 1966 with black-and-white FCC system. Covering major cities in South Vietnam, started with a one-hour broadcast per day then increased to six hours in the evening during the 1970s. There were two main channels:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/THVN" class="mw-redirect" title="THVN">THVN</a>-TV (Truyền hình Việt Nam-TV) on Channel 9, featuring Vietnamese-language shows, news and special announcements from Saigon. This entirely Vietnamese language channel catered to the Vietnamese populace.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/American_Forces_Network#Vietnam" title="American Forces Network">AFVN-TV</a> on Channel 11, operated by <a href="/wiki/American_Forces_Network" title="American Forces Network">Armed Forces Radio and Television Service (now American Forces Network)</a>, catered to US troops in South Vietnam. Broadcasting entirely in English, it relayed popular US-made shows like <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ed_Sullivan_Show" title="The Ed Sullivan Show">The Ed Sullivan Show</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tonight_Show_Starring_Johnny_Carson" title="The Tonight Show Starring Johnny Carson">The Tonight Show Starring Johnny Carson</a></i>, and various sports games like the <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a>. It also broadcast news and special announcements from the American government and military commanders.</li></ul>
<p>Both channels used an airborne transmission relay system from airplanes flying at high altitudes, called <a href="/wiki/Stratovision" title="Stratovision">Stratovision</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Newspapers">Newspapers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Newspapers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Writing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Christian_Science_Monitor" title="The Christian Science Monitor">The Christian Science Monitor</a></i> in 1970, Dan Sutherland remarked: "Under its new press law, South Vietnam now has one of the freest presses in Southeast Asia, and the daily paper with the biggest circulation here happens to be sharply critical of President Thieu&#160;... since the new press law was promulgated nine months ago, the government has not been able to close down <i>Tin Sang</i> or any other newspaper among the more than 30 now being published in Saigon."<sup id="cite_ref-Turner_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Turner-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Provinces">Provinces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Provinces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/w/index.php?title=Provinces_of_the_Republic_of_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Provinces of the Republic of Vietnam (page does not exist)">Provinces of the Republic of Vietnam</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Southvietmap.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Southvietmap.jpg/220px-Southvietmap.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="299" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Southvietmap.jpg/330px-Southvietmap.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Southvietmap.jpg/440px-Southvietmap.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1025" data-file-height="1393" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Southvietmap.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of South Vietnam.</div></div></div>
<p>South Vietnam's capital was <a href="/wiki/Saigon" class="mw-redirect" title="Saigon">Saigon</a> which was renamed <a href="/wiki/Ho_Chi_Minh_City" title="Ho Chi Minh City">Hồ Chí Minh City</a> on 1 May 1975 after <a href="/wiki/Unconditional_surrender" title="Unconditional surrender">unconditionally surrendering</a> to the North.
</p><p>Before surrendering, the South was divided into forty-four provinces (<i>tỉnh</i>, singular and plural).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The South was divided into coastal lowlands, the mountainous Central Highlands (Cao-nguyen Trung-phan) and the <a href="/wiki/Mekong_Delta" title="Mekong Delta">Mekong Delta</a>.
South Vietnam's time zone was one hour ahead of North Vietnam, belonging to the UTC+8 time zone with the same time as the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">Mainland China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">Western Australia</a>.
</p><p>Apart from the mainland, the Republic of Vietnam also administered parts of the <a href="/wiki/Paracels" class="mw-redirect" title="Paracels">Paracels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spratly_Islands" title="Spratly Islands">Spratly Islands</a>. China <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Paracel_Islands" title="Battle of the Paracel Islands">seized control</a> of the Paracels in 1974 after the South Vietnam navy attempted an assault on PRC-held islands.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_the_Republic_of_Vietnam" title="Economy of the Republic of Vietnam">Economy of the Republic of Vietnam</a></div>
<p>South Vietnam maintained a <a href="/wiki/Capitalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Capitalist">capitalist</a> <a href="/wiki/Free-market_economy" class="mw-redirect" title="Free-market economy">free-market economy</a> with ties to the West. It established an airline under the Head of State Bảo Đại named <a href="/wiki/Air_Vietnam" title="Air Vietnam">Air Vietnam</a>. The economy was greatly assisted by American aid and the presence of large numbers of Americans in the country between 1961 and 1973. Electrical production increased fourteen-fold between 1954 and 1973 while industrial output increase by an average of 6.9 percent annually.<sup id="cite_ref-Kim_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kim-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> During the same period, rice output increased by 203 percent and the number of students in university increased from 2,000 to 90,000.<sup id="cite_ref-Kim_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kim-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> US aid peaked at $2.3&#160;billion in 1973, but dropped to $1.1&#160;billion in 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiest_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiest-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">Inflation</a> rose to 200 percent as the country suffered economic shock due the decrease of American aid as well as the oil price shock of October 1973.<sup id="cite_ref-Wiest_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiest-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The unification of Vietnam in 1976 was followed by the imposition of North Vietnam's <a href="/wiki/Centrally_planned_economy" class="mw-redirect" title="Centrally planned economy">centrally planned economy</a> in the South. The country made no significant economic progress for the next twenty years. After the break-up of the Soviet Union and the end of Soviet aid, the leadership of Vietnam accepted the need for change. Their occupation armies were withdrawn from <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">Laos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>. Afterward, the country introduced economic reforms that created a market economy in the mid-1990s. The government remains a collective dictatorship under the close control of the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Vietnam" title="Communist Party of Vietnam">Communist Party</a>.
</p><p>A 2017 study in the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_History_(journal)" title="Diplomatic History (journal)">Diplomatic History</a></i> found that South Vietnamese economic planners sought to model the South Vietnamese economy on Taiwan and South Korea, which were perceived as successful examples of how to modernize developing economies.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1970 about 90% of population was <a href="/wiki/Kinh" class="mw-redirect" title="Kinh">Kinh</a> (Viet), and 10% was <a href="/wiki/Hoa_people" title="Hoa people">Hoa</a> (Chinese), <a href="/wiki/Degar" class="mw-redirect" title="Degar">Montagnard</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_people" title="French people">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Khmer_Krom" title="Khmer Krom">Khmer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cham_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Cham people">Cham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_(mixed_ancestry)" title="Eurasian (mixed ancestry)">Eurasians</a> and others.
</p><p>The Vietnamese language was the primary official language and was spoken by the majority of the population. Despite the end of French colonial rule, the French language still maintained a strong presence in South Vietnam where it was used in administration, education (especially at the secondary and higher levels), trade and diplomacy. The ruling elite population of South Vietnam was known to speak French as its primary language.<sup id="cite_ref-k_early_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-k_early-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> With US involvement in the Vietnam War, the English language was also later introduced to the armed forces and became a secondary diplomatic language. Languages spoken by minority groups included Chinese, Khmer and other languages spoken by Montagnard groups.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The religion of the majority of the population was Buddhism influenced by <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">Confucian philosophy</a>, which was practiced by about 80% of the population.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cultural life was strongly influenced by China until French domination in the 18th century. At that time, the traditional culture began to acquire an overlay of Western characteristics. Many families had three generations living under one roof. The emerging South Vietnamese middle class and youth in the 1960s became increasingly more westernised, and followed American cultural and social trends, especially in music, fashion and social attitudes in major cities like Saigon.
</p>
