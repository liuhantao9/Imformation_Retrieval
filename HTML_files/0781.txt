	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Geriatrics</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Geriatrics</b>, or <b>geriatric medicine</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is a specialty that focuses on health care of <a href="/wiki/Elderly_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Elderly people">elderly people</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It aims to promote <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">health</a> by <a href="/wiki/Prevention_(medical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Prevention (medical)">preventing</a> and treating <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">diseases</a> and <a href="/wiki/Disabilities" class="mw-redirect" title="Disabilities">disabilities</a> in <a href="/wiki/Older_adults" class="mw-redirect" title="Older adults">older adults</a>. There is no set age at which patients may be under the care of a <b>geriatrician</b>, or <b>geriatric physician</b>, a physician who specializes in the care of elderly people. Rather, this decision is determined by the individual patient's needs, and the availability of a specialist. It is important to note the difference between geriatrics, the care of aged people, and <a href="/wiki/Gerontology" title="Gerontology">gerontology</a>, which is the study of the <a href="/wiki/Aging" class="mw-redirect" title="Aging">aging</a> process itself. The term <i>geriatrics</i> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> γέρων <i>geron</i> meaning "old man", and ιατρός <i>iatros</i> meaning "healer". However, geriatrics is sometimes called <b>medical gerontology</b>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scope">Scope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geriatrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Scope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nursing_home.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Nursing_home.JPG/300px-Nursing_home.JPG" decoding="async" width="300" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Nursing_home.JPG/450px-Nursing_home.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Nursing_home.JPG/600px-Nursing_home.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3504" data-file-height="2336" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nursing_home.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Old man at a <a href="/wiki/Nursing_home" class="mw-redirect" title="Nursing home">nursing home</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Differences_between_adult_and_geriatric_medicine">Differences between adult and geriatric medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geriatrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Differences between adult and geriatric medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Geriatrics differs from standard adult <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">medicine</a> because it focuses on the unique needs of the elderly person. The aged body is different physiologically from the younger adult body, and during old age, the decline of various organ systems becomes manifest. Previous health issues and lifestyle choices produce a different constellation of diseases and symptoms in different people. The appearance of symptoms depends on the remaining healthy reserves in the <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organs</a>. Smokers, for example, consume their respiratory system reserve early and rapidly.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid_871704_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid_871704-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Geriatricians distinguish between <a href="/wiki/Disease" title="Disease">diseases</a> and the effects of normal <a href="/wiki/Aging" class="mw-redirect" title="Aging">aging</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Renal_impairment" class="mw-redirect" title="Renal impairment">renal impairment</a> may be a part of aging, but <a href="/wiki/Renal_failure" class="mw-redirect" title="Renal failure">renal failure</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urinary_incontinence" title="Urinary incontinence">urinary incontinence</a> are not. Geriatricians aim to treat diseases that are present and achieve healthy aging. Geriatricians focus on achieving the patient's highest priorities in the context of multiple chronic conditions, and on preserving function.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Increased_complexity">Increased complexity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geriatrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Increased complexity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The decline in physiological reserve in organs makes the elderly develop some kinds of diseases and have more complications from mild problems (such as <a href="/wiki/Dehydration" title="Dehydration">dehydration</a> from a mild <a href="/wiki/Gastroenteritis" title="Gastroenteritis">gastroenteritis</a>). Multiple problems may compound: A mild <a href="/wiki/Fever" title="Fever">fever</a> in elderly persons may cause confusion, which may lead to a fall and to a fracture of the <a href="/wiki/Femur_neck" title="Femur neck">neck of the femur</a> ("broken hip").
</p><p>Elderly people require specific attention to <a href="/wiki/Medications" class="mw-redirect" title="Medications">medications</a>. Elderly people particularly are subjected to <a href="/wiki/Polypharmacy" title="Polypharmacy">polypharmacy</a> (taking multiple medications). Some elderly people have multiple medical disorders; some have self-prescribed many <a href="/wiki/Herbal_medication" class="mw-redirect" title="Herbal medication">herbal medications</a> and <a href="/wiki/Over-the-counter_drug" title="Over-the-counter drug">over-the-counter drugs</a>. This polypharmacy may increase the risk of <a href="/wiki/Drug_interaction" title="Drug interaction">drug interactions</a> or <a href="/wiki/Adverse_drug_reaction" title="Adverse drug reaction">adverse drug reactions</a>. In one study, it was found that prescription and nonprescription medications were commonly used together among older adults, with nearly 1 in 25 individuals potentially at risk for a major drug-drug interaction.<sup id="cite_ref-qato_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-qato-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Drug_metabolism" title="Drug metabolism">Drugs metabolites</a> are excreted mostly by the <a href="/wiki/Kidneys" class="mw-redirect" title="Kidneys">kidneys</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>, which may be impaired in the elderly, necessitating medication adjustment.
</p><p>The presentation of disease in elderly persons may be vague and non-specific, or it may include <a href="/wiki/Delirium" title="Delirium">delirium</a> or falls. (<a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">Pneumonia</a>, for example, may present with low-grade <a href="/wiki/Fever" title="Fever">fever</a> and confusion, rather than the high fever and cough seen in younger people.) Some elderly people may find it hard to describe their <a href="/wiki/Symptoms" class="mw-redirect" title="Symptoms">symptoms</a> in words, especially if the disease is causing confusion, or if they have <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_impairment" class="mw-redirect" title="Cognitive impairment">cognitive impairment</a>. Delirium in the elderly may be caused by a minor problem such as <a href="/wiki/Constipation" title="Constipation">constipation</a> or by something as serious and life-threatening as a <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">heart attack</a>. Many of these problems are treatable, if the root cause can be discovered.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geriatric_giants">Geriatric giants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geriatrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geriatric giants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The so-called <b>geriatric giants</b> are the major categories of impairment that appear in elderly people, especially as they begin to fail. These include immobility, instability, <a href="/wiki/Urinary_incontinence" title="Urinary incontinence">incontinence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">impaired intellect/memory</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Impaired_vision" class="mw-redirect" title="Impaired vision">Impaired vision</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hearing_loss" title="Hearing loss">hearing loss</a> are common chronic problems among older people. Hearing problems can lead to social isolation, depression, and dependence as the person can no longer talk to other people, receive information over the telephone, or engage in simple transactions, such as talking to a person at a bank or store. Vision problems lead to falls from tripping over unseen objects, medicine being taken incorrectly because the written instructions could not be read, and finances being mismanaged.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Practical_concerns">Practical concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geriatrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Practical concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Functional abilities, independence and <a href="/wiki/Quality_of_life" title="Quality of life">quality of life</a> issues are of great concern to geriatricians and their patients. Elderly people generally want to live independently as long as possible, which requires them to be able to engage in <a href="/wiki/Self-care" title="Self-care">self-care</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Activities_of_daily_living" title="Activities of daily living">activities of daily living</a>. A geriatrician may be able to provide information about <a href="/wiki/Elder_care" class="mw-redirect" title="Elder care">elder care</a> options, and refers people to <a href="/wiki/Home_care" class="mw-redirect" title="Home care">home care</a> services, <a href="/wiki/Skilled_nursing_facilities" class="mw-redirect" title="Skilled nursing facilities">skilled nursing facilities</a>, <a href="/wiki/Assisted_living_facilities" class="mw-redirect" title="Assisted living facilities">assisted living facilities</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hospice" title="Hospice">hospice</a> as appropriate.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Frailty_syndrome" title="Frailty syndrome">Frail elderly</a> people may choose to decline some kinds of medical care, because the risk-benefit ratio is different. For example, frail elderly women routinely stop <a href="/wiki/Screening_mammogram" class="mw-redirect" title="Screening mammogram">screening mammograms</a>, because <a href="/wiki/Breast_cancer" title="Breast cancer">breast cancer</a> is typically a slowly growing disease that would cause them no pain, impairment, or loss of life before they would die of other causes. Frail people are also at significant risk of post-surgical complications and the need for extended care, and an accurate prediction—based on validated measures, rather than how old the patient's face looks—can help older patients make fully informed choices about their options. Assessment of older patients before elective surgeries can accurately predict the patients' recovery trajectories.<sup id="cite_ref-Frailty_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frailty-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> One frailty scale uses five items: unintentional weight loss, <a href="/wiki/Muscle_weakness" title="Muscle weakness">muscle weakness</a>, exhaustion, low physical activity, and slowed walking speed. A healthy person scores 0; a very frail person scores 5. Compared to non-frail elderly people, people with intermediate frailty scores (2 or 3) are twice as likely to have post-surgical complications, spend 50% more time in the hospital, and are three times as likely to be discharged to a skilled nursing facility instead of to their own homes.<sup id="cite_ref-Frailty_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Frailty-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Frail elderly patients (score of 4 or 5) who were living at home before the surgery have even worse outcomes, with the risk of being discharged to a nursing home rising to twenty times the rate for non-frail elderly people.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geriatrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Cite_check plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Text_document_with_red_question_mark.svg" class="image"><img alt="Text document with red question mark.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Text_document_with_red_question_mark.svg/40px-Text_document_with_red_question_mark.svg.png" decoding="async" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Text_document_with_red_question_mark.svg/60px-Text_document_with_red_question_mark.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Text_document_with_red_question_mark.svg/80px-Text_document_with_red_question_mark.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="48" data-file-height="48" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>possibly contains inappropriate or misinterpreted <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:CITE" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:CITE">citations</a> that do not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:V" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:V">verify</a> the text</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Geriatrics&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by checking for citation inaccuracies.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">September 2010</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>One of the eight branches of the traditional Indian system of medicine, <a href="/wiki/Ayurveda" title="Ayurveda">Ayurveda</a>, is <i>jara</i> or <i>rasayana</i>, similar to geriatrics. <a href="/wiki/Charaka" title="Charaka">Charaka</a> described the fatigue and physical exhaustion caused by premature aging as the result of a poor diet. The <i><a href="/wiki/Charaka_Samhita" title="Charaka Samhita">Charaka Samhita</a></i> recommends that elderly patients avoid excessive physical or mental strain and consume a light but nutritious diet.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A number of physicians in the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine Empire</a> studied geriatrics, with doctors like <a href="/wiki/A%C3%ABtius_of_Amida" title="Aëtius of Amida">Aëtius of Amida</a> evidently specializing in the field. <a href="/wiki/Alexander_of_Tralles" title="Alexander of Tralles">Alexander of Tralles</a> viewed the process of aging as a natural and inevitable form of <a href="/wiki/Marasmus" title="Marasmus">marasmus</a>, caused by the loss of moisture in body tissue. The works of Aëtius describe the mental and physical symptoms of aging. <a href="/wiki/Theophilus_Protospatharius" title="Theophilus Protospatharius">Theophilus Protospatharius</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joannes_Actuarius" title="Joannes Actuarius">Joannes Actuarius</a> also discussed the topic in their medical works. Byzantine physicians typically drew on the works of <a href="/wiki/Oribasius" title="Oribasius">Oribasius</a> and recommended that elderly patients consume a diet rich in foods that provide "heat and moisture". They also recommended frequent bathing, massaging, rest, and low-intensity exercise regimens.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Canon_of_Medicine" title="The Canon of Medicine">The Canon of Medicine</a></i>, written by <a href="/wiki/Avicenna" title="Avicenna">Avicenna</a> in 1025, the author was concerned with how "old folk need plenty of sleep" and how their bodies should be <a href="/wiki/Anointing" title="Anointing">anointed</a> with <a href="/wiki/Oil" title="Oil">oil</a>, and recommended <a href="/wiki/Exercise" title="Exercise">exercises</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Walking" title="Walking">walking</a> or <a href="/wiki/Equestrianism" title="Equestrianism">horse-riding</a>. Thesis III of the <i>Canon</i> discussed the <a href="/wiki/Diet_(nutrition)" title="Diet (nutrition)">diet</a> suitable for <a href="/wiki/Old_age" title="Old age">old people</a>, and dedicated several sections to elderly patients who become <a href="/wiki/Constipation" title="Constipation">constipated</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Islamic_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic medicine">Arab</a> physician <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Al-Jazzar" class="mw-redirect" title="Ibn Al-Jazzar">Algizar</a> (circa 898–980) wrote a book on the medicine and health of the elderly.<sup id="cite_ref-Al_Jazzar_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Al_Jazzar-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> He also wrote a book on <a href="/wiki/Sleep_disorder" title="Sleep disorder">sleep disorders</a> and another one on <a href="/wiki/Forgetting" title="Forgetting">forgetfulness</a> and how to strengthen <a href="/wiki/Memory" title="Memory">memory</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and a treatise on causes of <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">mortality</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Al_Jazzar_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Al_Jazzar-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template"><span style="white-space: nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot"><span title="&#160;Dead link since June 2012">dead link</span></a></i>&#93;</span></sup> Another Arab physician in the 9th century, <a href="/wiki/Ishaq_ibn_Hunayn" title="Ishaq ibn Hunayn">Ishaq ibn Hunayn</a> (died 910), the son of Nestorian Christian scholar <a href="/wiki/Hunayn_Ibn_Ishaq" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunayn Ibn Ishaq">Hunayn Ibn Ishaq</a>, wrote a <i>Treatise on Drugs for Forgetfulness</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>George Day published the <i>Diseases of Advanced Life</i> in 1849, one of the first publications on the subject of geriatric medicine.<sup id="cite_ref-History_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The first modern geriatric hospital was founded in Belgrade, Serbia, in 1881 by doctor <a href="/wiki/Laza_Lazarevi%C4%87" title="Laza Lazarević">Laza Lazarević</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term <i>geriatrics</i> was proposed in 1909 by Dr. <a href="/wiki/Ignatz_Leo_Nascher" title="Ignatz Leo Nascher">Ignatz Leo Nascher</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> former Chief of Clinic in the <a href="/wiki/Mount_Sinai_Hospital,_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Mount Sinai Hospital, New York">Mount Sinai Hospital</a> Outpatient Department (New York City) and a "father" of geriatrics in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Modern geriatrics in the United Kingdom began with the "mother"<sup id="cite_ref-mother_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mother-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> of geriatrics, Dr. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marjorie_Warren&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Marjorie Warren (page does not exist)">Marjorie Warren</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-History_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-History-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Warren emphasized that rehabilitation was essential to the care of older people. Using her experiences as a physician in a London Workhouse infirmary, she believed that merely keeping older people fed until they died was not enough; they needed diagnosis, treatment, care, and support. She found that patients, some of whom had previously been bedridden, were able to gain some degree of independence with the correct assessment and treatment.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The practice of geriatrics in the UK is also one with a rich multi-disciplinary history. It values all the professions, not just medicine, for their contributions in optimizing the well-being and independence of older people.
</p><p>Another innovator of British geriatrics is Bernard Isaacs, who described the "giants" of geriatrics mentioned above: immobility and instability, <a href="/wiki/Urinary_incontinence" title="Urinary incontinence">incontinence</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">impaired intellect</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Isaacs asserted that, if examined closely enough, all common problems with older people relate to one or more of these giants.
</p><p>The care of older people in the UK has been advanced by the implementation of the National Service Frameworks for Older People, which outlines key areas for attention.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geriatrician_training">Geriatrician training</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geriatrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Geriatrician training">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_of_America">United States of America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geriatrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: United States of America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, geriatricians are <a href="/wiki/Primary-care_physician" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary-care physician">primary-care physicians</a> (<a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Osteopathic_Medicine" title="Doctor of Osteopathic Medicine">D.O</a>. or <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Medicine" title="Doctor of Medicine">M.D.</a>) who are board-certified in either <a href="/wiki/Family_medicine" title="Family medicine">family medicine</a> or <a href="/wiki/Internal_medicine" title="Internal medicine">internal medicine</a> and who have also acquired the additional training necessary to obtain the Certificate of Added Qualifications (CAQ) in geriatric medicine. Geriatricians have developed an expanded expertise in the aging process, the impact of aging on illness patterns, drug therapy in seniors, health maintenance, and rehabilitation. They serve in a variety of roles including hospital care, long-term care, home care, and terminal care. They are frequently involved in ethics consultations to represent the unique health and diseases patterns seen in seniors. The model of care practiced by geriatricians is heavily focused on working closely with other disciplines such as nurses, <a href="/wiki/Clinical_pharmacy" title="Clinical pharmacy">pharmacists</a>, therapists, and social workers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geriatrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United Kingdom, most geriatricians are hospital physicians, whereas others focus on community geriatrics in particular. Although originally a distinct clinical specialty, it has been integrated as a specialization of general medicine since the late 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Most geriatricians are, therefore, accredited for both. Unlike in the United States, geriatric medicine is a major specialty in the United Kingdom and are the single most numerous internal medicine specialists.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geriatrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, there are two pathways that can be followed in order to work as a physician in a geriatric setting.
</p>
<ol><li>Doctors of Medicine (M.D.) can complete a three-year core internal medicine residency program, followed by two years of specialized geriatrics residency training. This pathway leads to certification, and possibly fellowship after several years of supplementary academic training, by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Physicians_and_Surgeons_of_Canada" title="Royal College of Physicians and Surgeons of Canada">Royal College of Physicians and Surgeons of Canada</a>.</li>
<li>Doctors of Medicine can opt for a two-year residency program in family medicine and complete a one-year enhanced skills program in <a href="/wiki/Care_of_the_elderly" class="mw-redirect" title="Care of the elderly">care of the elderly</a>. This post-doctoral pathway is accredited by the <a href="/wiki/College_of_Family_Physicians_of_Canada" title="College of Family Physicians of Canada">College of Family Physicians of Canada</a>.</li></ol>
<p>Many universities across Canada also offer gerontology training programs for the general public, such that <a href="/wiki/Nurses" class="mw-redirect" title="Nurses">nurses</a> and other health care professionals can pursue further education in the discipline in order to better understand the process of aging and their role in the presence of older patients and residents.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geriatrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In India, Geriatrics is a relatively new speciality offering. A three year post graduate residency (M.D) training can be joined for after completing the 5.5 year undergraduate training of <a href="/wiki/MBBS" class="mw-redirect" title="MBBS">MBBS</a> (Bachelor of Medicine and Bachelor of Surgery). Unfortunately, only four major institutes provide M.D in Geriatric Medicine and subsequent training. Training in some institutes are exclusive in the Department of Geriatric medicine, with rotations in Internal medicine, medical subspecialties etc. but in certain institutions, are limited to 2-year training in Internal medicine and subspecialities followed by one year of exclusive training in Geriatric Medicine.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Minimum_geriatric_competencies">Minimum geriatric competencies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geriatrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Minimum geriatric competencies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In July 2007, the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_American_Medical_Colleges" title="Association of American Medical Colleges">Association of American Medical Colleges</a> (AAMC) and the <a href="/wiki/John_A._Hartford_Foundation" title="John A. Hartford Foundation">John A. Hartford Foundation</a><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> hosted a <a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Consensus_Conference_on_Competencies_in_Geriatric_Education&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="National Consensus Conference on Competencies in Geriatric Education (page does not exist)">National Consensus Conference on Competencies in Geriatric Education</a> where a consensus was reached on minimum competencies (learning outcomes) that graduating medical students needed to assure competent care by new interns to older patients. Twenty-six (26) Minimum Geriatric Competencies in eight content domains were endorsed by the <a href="/wiki/American_Geriatrics_Society" title="American Geriatrics Society">American Geriatrics Society</a> (AGS), the <a href="/wiki/American_Medical_Association" title="American Medical Association">American Medical Association</a> (AMA), and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Association_of_Directors_of_Geriatric_Academic_Programs&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Association of Directors of Geriatric Academic Programs (page does not exist)">Association of Directors of Geriatric Academic Programs</a> (ADGAP). The domains are: cognitive and behavioral disorders; medication management; self-care capacity; falls, balance, gait disorders; atypical presentation of disease; palliative care; hospital care for elders, and health care planning and promotion. Each content domain specifies three or more observable, measurable competencies.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geriatrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hospital_Elder_Life_Program">Hospital Elder Life Program</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geriatrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Hospital Elder Life Program">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Perhaps the most pressing issue facing geriatrics is the treatment and prevention of delirium.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This is a condition in which hospitalized elderly patients become confused and disoriented when confronted with the uncertainty and confusion of a hospital stay. The health of the patient will decline as a result of delirium and can increase the length of hospitalization and lead to other health complications. The treatment of delirium involves keeping the patient mentally stimulated and oriented to reality, as well as providing specialized care in order to ensure that her/his needs are being met.
</p><p>The Hospital Elder Life Program (HELP) is an model of hospital care developed at the Yale University School of Medicine. It is designed to prevent delirium and functional decline among elderly individuals in the hospital inpatient setting. HELP uses a core team of interdisciplinary staff and targeted intervention protocols to improve patients' outcomes and to provide cost-effective care. Unique to the program is the use of specially trained volunteers who carry out the majority of the non-clinical interventions.
</p><p>In up to 40% of the cases, incident delirium can be prevented. To that end, HELP promotes interventions designed to maintain cognitive and physical functioning of older adults throughout the hospitalization, maximize patients' independence at discharge, assist with the transition from hospital to home and prevent unplanned hospital readmissions. Customized interventions include daily visitors; therapeutic activities to provide mental stimulation; daily exercise and walking assistance; sleep enhancement; nutritional support and hearing and vision protocols.
</p><p>HELP has been replicated in over 63 hospitals across the world. Although the majority of the sites are based in the United States located in 25 different states, there is a growing international presence. International sites include: Australia, Canada, the Netherlands, Taiwan and the United Kingdom.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pharmacology">Pharmacology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geriatrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Pharmacology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pharmacological constitution and regimen for older people is an important topic, one that is related to changing and differing physiology and psychology.
</p><p>Changes in physiology with aging may alter the absorption, the effectiveness and the side effect profile of many drugs. These changes may occur in oral protective reflexes (dryness of the mouth caused by diminished salivary glands), in the gastrointestinal system (such as with delayed emptying of solids and liquids possibly restricting speed of absorption), and in the distribution of drugs with changes in <a href="/wiki/Body_fat" class="mw-redirect" title="Body fat">body fat</a> and muscle and drug elimination.
</p><p>Psychological considerations include the fact that elderly persons (in particular, those experiencing substantial memory loss or other types of cognitive impairment) are unlikely to be able to adequately monitor and adhere to their own scheduled pharmacological administration. One study (Hutchinson et al., 2006) found that 25% of participants studied admitted to skipping doses or cutting them in half. Self-reported noncompliance with adherence to a medication schedule was reported by a striking one-third of the participants. Further development of methods that might possibly help monitor and regulate dosage administration and scheduling is an area that deserves attention.
</p><p>Another important area is the potential for improper administration and use of potentially inappropriate medications, and the possibility of errors that could result in dangerous drug interactions. Polypharmacy is often a predictive factor (Cannon et al., 2006). Research done on home/community health care found that "nearly 1 of 3 medical regimens contain a potential medication error" (Choi et al., 2006).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Perception_of_aging_and_cosmeceuticals">Perception of aging and cosmeceuticals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geriatrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Perception of aging and cosmeceuticals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beauty standards are constantly evolving over decades due to increased perception of esthetics.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Because of that, cosmeceutical industry is expanding and gradually becoming a part of many people’s personal care routine. Cosmeceutical is currently the fastest growing beauty industry with more than $42 billion in 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The demand for cosmeceutical is growing, especially in Asia. Korea is currently in the forefront of research and development in creating the newest cosmeceutical products with anti-ageing and anti-melanogenic effects including ingredients such as snail secretions, botanical extract, green tea and red ginseng.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Cryptomphalus aspersa secretion (or brown garden snail secretion) has been found to have antioxidant properties, increase skin cell proliferation, as well as increasing extracellular protein such as collagen and fibronectin (important proteins for cell proliferation).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chrysanthemum indicum has been shown to have anti-inflammatory effects, it also has been shown to decrease UV-induced skin thickening as well as photodamage-induced wrinkling. Given the antioxidant / anti-inflammatory properties, this floral extract is highly sought after ingredient for cosmeceutical in Asia, especially for the skin brightening property <sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Green tea and ginkgo biloba extracts (together, FBlend) have been found to enhance the epidermis thickness of the skin, moisturizing effects as well as increasing skin cell renewal <sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Panax ginseng (red ginseng) has been found to have anti-inflammatory and antiaging effects. Red ginseng extract has also studied for its antiwrinkle effects in mice models. Scientists have found that oral supplementation of red ginseng decreases overall skin wrinkling while protects the skin from UV-induced dryness and damage <sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethical_and_medico-legal_issues">Ethical and medico-legal issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Geriatrics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Ethical and medico-legal issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Elderly persons sometimes cannot make decisions for themselves. They may have previously prepared a <a href="/wiki/Power_of_attorney" title="Power of attorney">power of attorney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Advance_directives" class="mw-redirect" title="Advance directives">advance directives</a> to provide guidance if they are unable to understand what is happening to them, whether this is due to long-term dementia or to a short-term, correctable problem, such as <a href="/wiki/Delirium" title="Delirium">delirium</a> from a fever.
</p><p>Geriatricians must respect the patients' privacy while seeing that they receive appropriate and necessary services. More than most specialties, they must consider whether the patient has the legal <a href="/wiki/Moral_responsibility" title="Moral responsibility">responsibility</a> and <a href="/wiki/Competence_(law)" title="Competence (law)">competence</a> to understand the facts and make decisions. They must support <a href="/wiki/Informed_consent" title="Informed consent">informed consent</a> and resist the temptation to manipulate the patient by withholding information, such as the dismal prognosis for a condition or the likelihood of recovering from surgery at home.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Elder_abuse" title="Elder abuse">Elder abuse</a> is the physical, financial, emotional, sexual, or other type of abuse of an older dependent. Adequate training, services, and support can reduce the likelihood of elder abuse, and proper attention can often identify it. For elderly people who are unable to care for themselves, geriatricians may recommend <a href="/wiki/Legal_guardianship" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal guardianship">legal guardianship</a> or <a href="/wiki/Conservatorship" title="Conservatorship">conservatorship</a> to care for the person or the estate.
</p><p>Elder abuse occurs increasingly when caregivers of elderly relatives suffer from mental illness. These instances of abuse can be prevented by engaging these individuals with mental illness in mental health treatment. Additionally, interventions aimed at decreasing elder reliance on relatives may help decrease conflict and abuse. Family education and support programs conducted by mental health professionals may also be beneficial for elderly patients to learn how to set limits with relatives with psychiatric disorders without causing conflict that leads to abuse.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
