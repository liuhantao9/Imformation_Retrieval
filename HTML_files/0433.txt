	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Indian Arrival Day</h1>

<p><b>Indian Arrival Day</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Holiday" title="Holiday">holiday</a> celebrated on various days in the nations of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> and the island nation of <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>, usually commemorating the arrival of people from the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a> to that nation as <a href="/wiki/Indentured_labour" class="mw-redirect" title="Indentured labour">indentured labour</a> brought by European colonial authorities and their agents.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="By_country">By country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Arrival_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: By country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Grenada">Grenada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Arrival_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Grenada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 100th anniversary of the first arrival of Indians in Grenada was commemorated in 1957. This was the first ever commemoration of the day in Grenada. The next commemoration would occur more than five decades later in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> On 29 April 2009, the Government of Grenada declared that the 1st of May would officially be designated as Indian Arrival Day and observed annually alongside the existing <a href="/wiki/Labour_Day" title="Labour Day">Labour Day</a>. The date was already a public holiday in Grenada, on account of Labour Day. The Government also announced that Boucherie Road, the road leading to the site of the arrival of the <i>Maidstone</i>, would be officially renamed Maidstone Road to honour the arrival of Indians in Grenada.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The renaming was officially carried out in a ceremony at 10:30 AM at La Fortune Junction, St. Patrick on 2 May 2009. Governor General Sir Carlyle Glean unveiled a granite plaque commemorating the arrival of the first Indians in Grenada. The plaque bears the inscription, "On 1st May 1857, in this bay the sailing vessel “Maidstone” anchored and landed 287 passengers having left India three months earlier, with 304 passengers. Between the years 1857 and 1890 other ships anchored in this and other bays bringing a total of 3,200 persons from India to work as agricultural indenture labourers in Grenada. This monument is dedicated to those who became the genesis of the Indo-Grenadian population of our nation”.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Guyana">Guyana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Arrival_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Guyana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> the holiday is celebrated on May commemorating the first arrival of indentured labourers from <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> to the country, on May 5, 1838.  On this day, the workers arrived in Guyana to work in sugar plantations. Their descendants today comprise 44 percent of Guyana's population of over 750,000.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jamaica">Jamaica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Arrival_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Jamaica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1995, the Government of <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> proclaimed May 10 Indian Heritage Day in recognition of the Indians’ contribution to the social and economic development of the country. The arrival of the Indians more than 170 years ago is commemorated in stamps.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mauritius">Mauritius</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Arrival_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mauritius">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a>, the holiday is celebrated on November 2 to commemorate the arrival of Indian labourers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saint_Lucia">Saint Lucia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Arrival_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Saint Lucia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Indian Diaspora of St. Lucia, an association promoting Indo-Saint Lucian heritage, organized the first Indian Arrival Day celebrations in Saint Lucia on 6 May 2013. The association is campaigning for the Saint Lucian government to officially declare the 6th of May as Indian Arrival Day.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Many other Caribbean nations observe Indian Arrival Day annually to commemorate the date when the first Indians arrived in their respective countries.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Saint_Vincent_and_the_Grenadines">Saint Vincent and the Grenadines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Arrival_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Saint Vincent and the Grenadines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Parliament of Saint Vincent and the Grenadines enacted an Act of Parliament on 26 March 2007 officially declaring the 1st of June as Indian Arrival Day. The first official commemoration of the event was held on 1 June that year.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The day is marked annually by a re-enactment of the landing of Indians at Indian Bay, Kingstown, followed by a procession to Heritage Square. Several Indian cultural events are also held to mark the occasion.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The first International Indian Diaspora Conference was held for the first time on 1-3 June 2012. It was organized by the St. Vincent and the Grenadines Chapter of the Global Organization of People of Indian Origin International (GOPIO-SVG), in partnership with the SVG Indian Heritage Foundation, and under the patronage of the SVG Ministry of Tourism and Culture. This was the first international conference for the Indian diaspora held in the country. Similar conferences had been held in other Caribbean nations since 1975.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Government of the Saint Vincent and the Grenadines officially designated the 7th of October as Indian Heritage Day.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Suriname">Suriname</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Arrival_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Suriname">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>, Indian Arrival Day is celebrated on June 5.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trinidad_and_Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Arrival_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Trinidad and Tobago">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a> Indian Arrival Day is celebrated on May 30th. Trinidad and Tobago was the first country to start this holiday.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Arrival_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Indian Arrival Day was first celebrated in Skinner Park, <a href="/wiki/San_Fernando,_Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="San Fernando, Trinidad and Tobago">San Fernando</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, as the East Indian Centenary on May 30, 1945<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> which marked the hundredth anniversary of the coming of Indians to Trinidad. The Acting Governor representing the Government of the United Kingdom attended indicating the significance of the observance. Other local dignitaries who addressed the large crowd included Timothy Roodal, George Fitzpatrick, <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Cola_Rienzi" title="Adrian Cola Rienzi">Adrian Cola Rienzi</a>, and Murli J. Kirpalani. Greetings were also read from <a href="/wiki/Mahatma_Gandhi" title="Mahatma Gandhi">Mahatma Gandhi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lord_Wavell" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Wavell">Lord Wavell</a>, and Colonel Stanley, the Secretary of State for the Colonies.
</p><p>After the 1945 Centenary extravaganza, however, the celebration of the anniversary of May 30 gradually declined. By the 1950s, the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Trinidadian_and_Tobagonian" title="Indo-Trinidadian and Tobagonian">Indians</a> who were on the <a href="/wiki/Fatel_Razack" title="Fatel Razack">Fatel Razack</a> as immigrants to Trinidad and Tobago were brought as "<a href="/wiki/Coolie" title="Coolie">coolie</a>" <a href="/wiki/Indian_indenture_system" title="Indian indenture system">indentured laborers</a>. By the early seventies only the <a href="/wiki/Indo-Trinidadian_and_Tobagonian" title="Indo-Trinidadian and Tobagonian">Indian</a> group was the <a href="/wiki/Hindu" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> Divine Life Society of the Chaguanas was staging an annual procession and ceremony under the name Indian Emigration Day.
</p><p>Indian Emigration Day, as it was called then, had been celebrated by various organizations after 1945 with limited success. By 1973 the latest was organized by the Divine Life Society, which had organized small annual processions in <a href="/wiki/Chaguanas" title="Chaguanas">Chaguanas</a> for Indian Emigration Day. By the late seventies even that small remembrance was dwindling.
</p><p>In 1976 the Indian Revival and Reform Association (IRRA) was formed. They were concerned about racism against Indians and were interested in developing ideas, writing pamphlets to bring about an Indian revival and renewed pride in Indian heritage and Indian culture. The IRRA wanted to preserve the good things about Indian heritage and reform the ones that were no longer useful or relevant. The anniversary of the coming of Indians to Guyana was one of the good things that came to the IRRA notice.
</p><p>In 1977 IRRA formed committee was established to revive the memory of the coming of Indians to Trinidad on May 30, 1845 – Indian Emigration Day.  The initial historic Committee comprised Anand Rameshwar Singh, Khalique Khan, Ramdath Jagessar, Rajiv Sieunarine, Azamudeen "Danny" Jang, Michael Sankar and Rajesh Harricharan. The following year Rajnie Ramlakhan, Anand Maharaj and Ashok Gobin joined in the group's celebration.
</p><p>The first active step was taken in early 1978, when they produced and distributed an onepage pamphlet with the title “Indian Emigration Day May 30, 1978”. It gave a brief account of the coming of Indians in 1845, and the importance of the event. The names of the first pioneers on the Fatel Razack were listed, and there was a short description of the achievements of Indians in Trinidad since 1845. The Trinidad Express carried a press release, and the Trinidad Guardian printed an article by Kusha Haracksingh on the voyage of the Fatel Razack. Mastana Bahar dedicated a show to Indian Emigration Day. San Fernando Secondary School organized a celebration.
</p><p>A major turning point occurred in 1979 when the group expanded by approaching the <a href="/wiki/Sanatan_Dharma_Maha_Sabha" title="Sanatan Dharma Maha Sabha">Sanatan Dharma Maha Sabha</a>. The SDMS Secretary General <a href="/wiki/Satnarayan_Maharaj" title="Satnarayan Maharaj">Satnarayan Maharaj</a> receptive to the idea, agreed to organize a major celebration at Lakshmi Girls College on May 27, 1979.  IRRA and the SDMS in discussions agreed that Indians were no longer emigrants to Trinidad and Tobago, but citizens who had arrived 134 years before. Coming out of this discussion the name was then changed to Indian Arrival Day.
</p><p>The 1979 celebration was a great success and included the presence of some of the original immigrants born in <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British India</a>. Government ministers Sham and Kamal Mohammed were there, as was the Indian High Commissioner and <a href="/wiki/Presbyterian_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Presbyterian Church">Presbyterian Church</a> moderator Idris Hamid. The event was widely covered by the local media and immediately knew about the event.  In fact it took just two years and an idea to revive the memory of the Arrival of Indians in Trinidad and Tobago to awaken the entire Indian community in Trinidad and Tobago.
</p><p>Subsequent years the Hindu Seva Sangh and other smaller groups approached the IRRA for guidance in developing in various communities throughout Trinidad and Tobago. By 1980 Indian Arrival Day celebrations were held at the Spring Village, <a href="/wiki/Cedros,_Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Cedros, Trinidad and Tobago">Cedros</a>, <a href="/wiki/Couva" title="Couva">Couva</a>, <a href="/wiki/Penal" title="Penal">Penal</a>, <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="San Juan, Trinidad and Tobago">San Juan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chaguanas" title="Chaguanas">Chaguanas</a>, and many other parts of the country. The National Joint Action Committee, the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>, other churches, libraries, <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">masjids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hindu_temple" title="Hindu temple">mandirs</a>, and schools at this point joined in the observances. In a remarkably short time, Indian Arrival Day was sweeping through the country. By 1985 there were more than 10 significant Indian Arrival Day celebrations taking place. The <a href="/wiki/Hindu" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindu">Hindu</a> community took the lead in the development of the celebrations.
</p><p>In 1991 Members of <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Parliament of Trinidad and Tobago">Parliament</a> Trevor Sudama and Raymond Pallackdarrysingh first introduced to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Representatives_(Trinidad_and_Tobago)" title="House of Representatives (Trinidad and Tobago)">House of Representatives</a> the concept that Indian Arrival Day should be made a national public holiday. This call to make Indian Arrival Day a public holiday continued to be unheeded until 1995 Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Manning" title="Patrick Manning">Patrick Manning</a> declared that the 150th Anniversary would be a public holiday called Indian Arrival Day, but thereafter the holiday will be called Arrival Day. The 1995 celebrations surpassed the 1945 celebrations with the <a href="/wiki/Sanatan_Dharma_Maha_Sabha" title="Sanatan Dharma Maha Sabha">Sanatan Dharma Maha Sabha</a> having major celebrations in Trinidad and Tobago
</p><p>In 1996 Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Basdeo_Panday" title="Basdeo Panday">Basdeo Panday</a> declared that 30 May would be known as Indian Arrival Day and not Arrival Day.  However, in the birthplace of Indian Arrival Day – Trinidad and Tobago – there is a lobby to remove the word 'Indian' from the name. In the 1990s the <a href="/wiki/Sanatan_Dharma_Maha_Sabha" title="Sanatan Dharma Maha Sabha">Sanatan Dharma Maha Sabha</a> expanded the Indian Arrival Day celebrations and dubbed May as "Indian Month" which ends on Indian Arrival Day.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_observances_in_other_countries">Similar observances in other countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indian_Arrival_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Similar observances in other countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since its establishment in Trinidad and Tobago, Indian Arrival Day has given rise to similar celebrations in <a href="/wiki/Grenada" title="Grenada">Grenada</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>. However, there is no similar celebration in <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">Fiji</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">Réunion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">Seychelles</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>,  or <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, which are also home to somewhat large Indian-origin populations.
</p>
