<p><b>Country rock</b> is a subgenre of <a href="/wiki/Popular_music" title="Popular music">popular music</a>, formed from the fusion of <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a>. It was developed by rock musicians who began to record country-flavored records in the late 1960s and early 1970s. These musicians recorded rock records using country themes, vocal styles, and additional instrumentation, most characteristically <a href="/wiki/Pedal_steel_guitar" title="Pedal steel guitar">pedal steel guitars</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bogdanov2002C-Rock_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov2002C-Rock-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Country rock began with artists like <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Byrds" title="The Byrds">the Byrds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitty_Gritty_Dirt_Band" title="Nitty Gritty Dirt Band">Nitty Gritty Dirt Band</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Flying_Burrito_Brothers" title="The Flying Burrito Brothers">the Flying Burrito Brothers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gram_Parsons" title="Gram Parsons">Gram Parsons</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and others, reaching its greatest popularity in the 1970s with artists such as <a href="/wiki/Emmylou_Harris" title="Emmylou Harris">Emmylou Harris</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Eagles_(band)" title="Eagles (band)">Eagles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linda_Ronstadt" title="Linda Ronstadt">Linda Ronstadt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Nesmith" title="Michael Nesmith">Michael Nesmith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poco" title="Poco">Poco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pure_Prairie_League" title="Pure Prairie League">Pure Prairie League</a>. Country rock also influenced artists in other genres, including <a href="/wiki/The_Band" title="The Band">the Band</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Grateful_Dead" title="Grateful Dead">Grateful Dead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Creedence_Clearwater_Revival" title="Creedence Clearwater Revival">Creedence Clearwater Revival</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">the Rolling Stones</a>, and <a href="/wiki/George_Harrison" title="George Harrison">George Harrison</a>'s solo work,<sup id="cite_ref-Bogdanov2002C-Rock_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov2002C-Rock-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> as well as playing a part in the development of <a href="/wiki/Southern_rock" title="Southern rock">Southern rock</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Country_rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">Rock and roll</a> has usually been seen as a combination of <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_and_blues" title="Rhythm and blues">rhythm and blues</a> and country music, a fusion particularly evident in 1950s <a href="/wiki/Rockabilly" title="Rockabilly">rockabilly</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> There has also been cross-pollination throughout the history of both genres; however, the term “country-rock” is used generally to refer to the wave of rock musicians of the late 1960s and early 1970s who began recording rock songs with country themes, vocal styles, and additional instrumentation, most characteristically <a href="/wiki/Pedal_steel_guitar" title="Pedal steel guitar">pedal steel guitars</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bogdanov2002C-Rock_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov2002C-Rock-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> John Einarson states, that "[f]rom a variety of perspectives and motivations, these musicians either played rock &amp; roll attitude, or added a country feel to rock, or folk, or bluegrass, there was no formula".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Country_rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Country_rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gram_Parsons.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Gram_Parsons.jpg/220px-Gram_Parsons.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Gram_Parsons.jpg/330px-Gram_Parsons.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Gram_Parsons.jpg/440px-Gram_Parsons.jpg 2x" data-file-width="645" data-file-height="482" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gram_Parsons.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Gram_Parsons" title="Gram Parsons">Gram Parsons</a> in 1972</div></div></div>
<p>Country influences can be heard on rock records through the 1960s, including <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">the Beatles</a>' 1964 recordings "<a href="/wiki/I%27ll_Cry_Instead" title="I&#39;ll Cry Instead">I'll Cry Instead</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Baby%27s_in_Black" title="Baby&#39;s in Black">Baby's in Black</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/I_Don%27t_Want_to_Spoil_the_Party" title="I Don&#39;t Want to Spoil the Party">I Don't Want to Spoil the Party</a>", <a href="/wiki/The_Byrds" title="The Byrds">the Byrds'</a> 1965 cover version of <a href="/wiki/Porter_Wagoner" title="Porter Wagoner">Porter Wagoner</a>'s "Satisfied Mind", on <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">the Rolling Stones</a> "High and Dry" (1966), as well as <a href="/wiki/Buffalo_Springfield" title="Buffalo Springfield">Buffalo Springfield</a>'s "Go and Say Goodbye" (1966) and "Kind Woman" (1968).<sup id="cite_ref-Bogdanov2002C-Rock_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov2002C-Rock-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> According to <i>The Encyclopedia of Country Music</i>, the Beatles' "I Don't Want to Spoil the Party", their cover of the <a href="/wiki/Buck_Owens" title="Buck Owens">Buck Owens</a> country hit "<a href="/wiki/Act_Naturally" title="Act Naturally">Act Naturally</a>" and their 1965 album <i><a href="/wiki/Rubber_Soul" title="Rubber Soul">Rubber Soul</a></i> can all be seen "with hindsight" as examples of country rock.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 1966, as many rock artists moved increasingly towards expansive and experimental <a href="/wiki/Psychedelia" title="Psychedelia">psychedelia</a>, Bob Dylan spearheaded the back-to-basics <a href="/wiki/Roots_revival" title="Roots revival">roots revival</a> when he went to Nashville to record the album <i><a href="/wiki/Blonde_on_Blonde" title="Blonde on Blonde">Blonde on Blonde</a></i>, using notable local musicians like <a href="/wiki/Charlie_McCoy" title="Charlie McCoy">Charlie McCoy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wolff2000_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wolff2000-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> This, and the subsequent more clearly country-influenced albums, <i><a href="/wiki/John_Wesley_Harding_(album)" title="John Wesley Harding (album)">John Wesley Harding</a></i> (1967) and <i><a href="/wiki/Nashville_Skyline" title="Nashville Skyline">Nashville Skyline</a></i> (1969), have been seen as creating the genre of <a href="/wiki/Country_folk" title="Country folk">country folk</a>, a route pursued by a number of, largely acoustic, folk musicians.<sup id="cite_ref-Wolff2000_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wolff2000-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Dylan's lead was also followed by the Byrds, who were joined by <a href="/wiki/Gram_Parsons" title="Gram Parsons">Gram Parsons</a> in 1968. Parsons had mixed <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a> with <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a> and <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk</a> to create what he called "Cosmic American Music".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Earlier in the year Parsons had released <i><a href="/wiki/Safe_at_Home" title="Safe at Home">Safe at Home</a></i> (although the principal recording for the album had taken place in mid-1967) with the <a href="/wiki/International_Submarine_Band" title="International Submarine Band">International Submarine Band</a>, which made extensive use of <a href="/wiki/Pedal_steel" class="mw-redirect" title="Pedal steel">pedal steel</a> and is seen by some as the first true country-rock album.<sup id="cite_ref-Bogdanov2002C-Rock_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov2002C-Rock-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The result of Parsons' brief tenure in the Byrds was <i><a href="/wiki/Sweetheart_of_the_Rodeo" title="Sweetheart of the Rodeo">Sweetheart of the Rodeo</a></i> (1968), generally considered one of the finest and most influential recordings in the genre.<sup id="cite_ref-Bogdanov2002C-Rock_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov2002C-Rock-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The Byrds continued for a brief period in the same vein, but Parsons left soon after the album was released to be joined by another ex-Byrds member <a href="/wiki/Chris_Hillman" title="Chris Hillman">Chris Hillman</a> in forming <a href="/wiki/The_Flying_Burrito_Brothers" title="The Flying Burrito Brothers">the Flying Burrito Brothers</a>. Over the next two years they recorded the albums <i><a href="/wiki/The_Gilded_Palace_of_Sin" title="The Gilded Palace of Sin">The Gilded Palace of Sin</a></i> (1969) and <i><a href="/wiki/Burrito_Deluxe" title="Burrito Deluxe">Burrito Deluxe</a></i> (1970), which helped establish the respectability and parameters of the genre, before Parsons departed to pursue a solo career.<sup id="cite_ref-Bogdanov2002C-Rock_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov2002C-Rock-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expansion">Expansion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Country_rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Expansion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Emmylou_Harris_2006_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Emmylou_Harris_2006_2.jpg/220px-Emmylou_Harris_2006_2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="190" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Emmylou_Harris_2006_2.jpg/330px-Emmylou_Harris_2006_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Emmylou_Harris_2006_2.jpg/440px-Emmylou_Harris_2006_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="599" data-file-height="518" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Emmylou_Harris_2006_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Emmylou_Harris" title="Emmylou Harris">Emmylou Harris</a>, the "Queen of country rock", playing in <a href="/wiki/Rotterdam" title="Rotterdam">Rotterdam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> (2006)</div></div></div>
<p>Country rock was a particularly popular style in the California music scene of the late 1960s, and was adopted by bands including Hearts and Flowers, <a href="/wiki/Poco" title="Poco">Poco</a> (formed by <a href="/wiki/Richie_Furay" title="Richie Furay">Richie Furay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jim_Messina_(musician)" title="Jim Messina (musician)">Jim Messina</a>, formerly of the Buffalo Springfield) and <a href="/wiki/New_Riders_of_the_Purple_Sage" title="New Riders of the Purple Sage">New Riders of the Purple Sage</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bogdanov2002C-Rock_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov2002C-Rock-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Some folk-rockers followed the Byrds into the genre, among them the <a href="/wiki/Beau_Brummels" class="mw-redirect" title="Beau Brummels">Beau Brummels</a><sup id="cite_ref-Bogdanov2002C-Rock_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov2002C-Rock-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Nitty_Gritty_Dirt_Band" title="Nitty Gritty Dirt Band">Nitty Gritty Dirt Band</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> A number of performers also enjoyed a renaissance by adopting country sounds, including: the Beatles, who re-explored elements of country in their later albums, like "Rocky Raccoon" and "Don't Pass Me By" from their eponymous "<i><a href="/wiki/The_Beatles_(album)" title="The Beatles (album)">White Album</a></i>" (1968),<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and "Octopus's Garden" from <i><a href="/wiki/Abbey_Road" title="Abbey Road">Abbey Road</a></i> (1969);<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Everly_Brothers" class="mw-redirect" title="Everly Brothers">Everly Brothers</a>, whose <i>Roots</i> album (1968) is usually considered some of their finest work; former <a href="/wiki/Teen_idol" title="Teen idol">teen idol</a> <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Nelson" title="Ricky Nelson">Ricky Nelson</a> who became the frontman for the Stone Canyon Band; <a href="/wiki/John_Fogerty" title="John Fogerty">John Fogerty</a>, who left <a href="/wiki/Creedence_Clearwater_Revival" title="Creedence Clearwater Revival">Creedence Clearwater Revival</a> behind for the country sounds of <a href="/wiki/The_Blue_Ridge_Rangers" title="The Blue Ridge Rangers">the Blue Ridge Rangers</a>(1972);<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mike_Nesmith" class="mw-redirect" title="Mike Nesmith">Mike Nesmith</a>, who had experimented with country sounds while with <a href="/wiki/The_Monkees" title="The Monkees">the Monkees</a>, formed the <a href="/wiki/First_National_Band" class="mw-redirect" title="First National Band">First National Band</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-TMT_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TMT-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Neil_Young" title="Neil Young">Neil Young</a> who moved in and out of the genre throughout his career.<sup id="cite_ref-Bogdanov2002C-Rock_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov2002C-Rock-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> One of the few acts to successfully move from the country side towards rock were the <a href="/wiki/Bluegrass_music" title="Bluegrass music">bluegrass</a> band <a href="/wiki/The_Dillards" title="The Dillards">the Dillards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bogdanov2002C-Rock_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov2002C-Rock-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peak">Peak</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Country_rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Peak">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dickey_Betts_Pistoia_Blues_Festival_2008.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Dickey_Betts_Pistoia_Blues_Festival_2008.jpg/170px-Dickey_Betts_Pistoia_Blues_Festival_2008.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Dickey_Betts_Pistoia_Blues_Festival_2008.jpg/255px-Dickey_Betts_Pistoia_Blues_Festival_2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Dickey_Betts_Pistoia_Blues_Festival_2008.jpg/340px-Dickey_Betts_Pistoia_Blues_Festival_2008.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="3888" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dickey_Betts_Pistoia_Blues_Festival_2008.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Dickey_Betts" title="Dickey Betts">Dickey Betts</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Allman_Brothers_Band" class="mw-redirect" title="Allman Brothers Band">Allman Brothers Band</a>, brought elements of country rock into the band during the 1970s</div></div></div>
<p>The greatest commercial success for country rock came in the 1970s, with the <a href="/wiki/Doobie_Brothers" class="mw-redirect" title="Doobie Brothers">Doobie Brothers</a> mixing in elements of R&amp;B, <a href="/wiki/Emmylou_Harris" title="Emmylou Harris">Emmylou Harris</a> (a former backing singer for Parsons) becoming the "Queen of country-rock" and <a href="/wiki/Linda_Ronstadt" title="Linda Ronstadt">Linda Ronstadt</a> creating a highly successful pop-oriented brand of the genre.<sup id="cite_ref-Tawa2005_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tawa2005-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pure_Prairie_League" title="Pure Prairie League">Pure Prairie League</a>, formed in Ohio in 1969 by <a href="/wiki/Craig_Fuller" title="Craig Fuller">Craig Fuller</a>, had both critical and commercial success with 5 straight Top 40 LP releases,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> including <i><a href="/wiki/Bustin%27_Out" title="Bustin&#39; Out">Bustin' Out</a></i> (1972), acclaimed by Allmusic critic Richard Foss as "an album that is unequaled in country-rock"<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Two_Lane_Highway" title="Two Lane Highway">Two Lane Highway</a></i>, described by <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> as "a worthy companion to the likes of the Byrds' <i>Sweetheart of the Rodeo</i> and other gems of the genre".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Former members of Ronstadt's backing band went on to form the <a href="/wiki/Eagles_(band)" title="Eagles (band)">Eagles</a> (two members of band were from the Flying Burrito Brothers and Poco), who emerged as one of the most successful rock acts of all time, producing albums that included <i><a href="/wiki/Desperado_(Eagles_album)" title="Desperado (Eagles album)">Desperado</a></i> (1973) and <i><a href="/wiki/Hotel_California_(Eagles_album)" title="Hotel California (Eagles album)">Hotel California</a></i> (1976).<sup id="cite_ref-Tawa2005_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tawa2005-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> However, the principal country rock influence in the Eagles came from <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Leadon" title="Bernie Leadon">Bernie Leadon</a>, formerly of the Flying Burrito Brothers, and the Eagles are perceived as shifting towards hard rock after he left the band in late 1975. The <a href="/wiki/Ozark_Mountain_Daredevils" class="mw-redirect" title="Ozark Mountain Daredevils">Ozark Mountain Daredevils</a> had hit singles “If You Wanna Get To Heaven” (1974) and "Jackie Blue" (1975), the latter of which peaked at #3 on the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Billboard Hot 100</a> in 1975. The Belammy Brothers had the hit "Let Your Love Flow"(1976). In 1979, the Southern rock <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Daniels_Band" class="mw-redirect" title="Charlie Daniels Band">Charlie Daniels Band</a> moved to a more country direction, released a song with strong bluegrass influence, "<a href="/wiki/The_Devil_Went_Down_to_Georgia" title="The Devil Went Down to Georgia">The Devil Went Down to Georgia</a>", and the song crossed over and became a hit on the pop chart.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Country_rock&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Outside its handful of stars, country rock's greatest significance was on artists in other genres, including <a href="/wiki/The_Band" title="The Band">the Band</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grateful_Dead" title="Grateful Dead">Grateful Dead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Creedence_Clearwater_Revival" title="Creedence Clearwater Revival">Creedence Clearwater Revival</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">the Rolling Stones</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Harrison" title="George Harrison">George Harrison</a>'s solo work.<sup id="cite_ref-Bogdanov2002C-Rock_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov2002C-Rock-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It also played a part in the development of <a href="/wiki/Southern_rock" title="Southern rock">Southern rock</a>, which, although largely derived from <a href="/wiki/Blues_rock" title="Blues rock">blues rock</a>, had a distinct southern lilt, and it paved the way for parts of the <a href="/wiki/Alternative_country" title="Alternative country">alternative country</a> movement.<sup id="cite_ref-Bogdanov2002C-Rock_1-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov2002C-Rock-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The genre declined in popularity in the late 1970s, but some established artists, including Neil Young, have continued to record country-tinged rock into the twenty-first century. Country rock has survived as a cult force in Texas, where acts including <a href="/wiki/The_Flatlanders" title="The Flatlanders">the Flatlanders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joe_Ely" title="Joe Ely">Joe Ely</a>, <a href="/wiki/Butch_Hancock" title="Butch Hancock">Butch Hancock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimmie_Dale_Gilmore" title="Jimmie Dale Gilmore">Jimmie Dale Gilmore</a> and California-based Richard Brooker, have collaborated and recorded.<sup id="cite_ref-Bogdanov2002C-Rock_1-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov2002C-Rock-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Other performers have produced occasional recordings in the genre, including <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Costello" title="Elvis Costello">Elvis Costello</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Almost_Blue" title="Almost Blue">Almost Blue</a></i> (1981)<sup id="cite_ref-Bogdanov2002C-Rock_1-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bogdanov2002C-Rock-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Robert_Plant" title="Robert Plant">Robert Plant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alison_Krauss" title="Alison Krauss">Alison Krauss</a> collaboration <i><a href="/wiki/Raising_Sand" title="Raising Sand">Raising Sand</a></i>, which was one of the most commercially successful albums of 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-top50_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-top50-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
