<p><b>Barry John Sheerman</b> (born 17 August 1940) is a British <a href="/wiki/Labour_Co-operative" class="mw-redirect" title="Labour Co-operative">Labour Co-operative</a> <a href="/wiki/Politician" title="Politician">politician</a> who has been the <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">Member of Parliament</a> (MP) for <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Huddersfield (UK Parliament constituency)">Huddersfield</a> since the <a href="/wiki/1979_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="1979 United Kingdom general election">1979 general election</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Sheerman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sheerman was born on 17 August 1940 in <a href="/wiki/Sunbury-on-Thames" title="Sunbury-on-Thames">Sunbury-on-Thames</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">Middlesex</a> and went to Hampton Grammar School (which became the independent <a href="/wiki/Hampton_School" title="Hampton School">Hampton School</a> in 1975) on <i>Hanworth Road</i> in <a href="/wiki/Hampton,_London" title="Hampton, London">Hampton</a> then <a href="/wiki/Kingston_College_(England)" title="Kingston College (England)">Kingston Technical College</a>. He was educated at the <a href="/wiki/London_School_of_Economics" title="London School of Economics">London School of Economics</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">BSc</a> Economics 1965) and at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a> (MSc 1967). He became a lecturer at the <a href="/wiki/Swansea_University" title="Swansea University">University of Wales, Swansea</a> in 1966 and remained there until his election to parliament in 1979.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parliamentary_career">Parliamentary career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Sheerman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Parliamentary career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sheerman unsuccessfully contested <a href="/wiki/Taunton_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Taunton (UK Parliament constituency)">Taunton</a> in the <a href="/wiki/October_1974_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="October 1974 United Kingdom general election">October 1974 election</a>, and became the MP for Huddersfield East from 1979 to 1983 and for <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Huddersfield (UK Parliament constituency)">Huddersfield</a> since the <a href="/wiki/1983_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="1983 United Kingdom general election">1983 general election</a>. Sheerman has held the Huddersfield seats since, with his majority as low as 3,955 in 1983 and as high as 15,848 in the <a href="/wiki/1997_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="1997 United Kingdom general election">1997 general election</a>. In the most recent general election in <a href="/wiki/2017_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="2017 United Kingdom general election">2017</a>, Sheerman's majority was increased to 12,005 with a swing of 4.7% to Labour.
</p><p>From 1983 to 1988, Sheerman was the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> spokesperson on education and employment; for <a href="/wiki/Home_Affairs" class="mw-redirect" title="Home Affairs">Home Affairs</a> (as Shadow Deputy <a href="/wiki/Home_Secretary" title="Home Secretary">Home Secretary</a>) from 1988 to 1992; and for Disabled People's Rights from 1992 to 1994. He was  Chair of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a> Education and Skills <a href="/wiki/Select_Committee_(Westminster_System)" class="mw-redirect" title="Select Committee (Westminster System)">select committee</a> from 2001 to 2010, renamed the <a href="/wiki/Children,_Schools_and_Families_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="Children, Schools and Families Committee">Children, Schools and Families Committee</a> in 2007. Under his chairmanship, the Committee was often critical of government policy. Sheerman warned the government not to "lose their nerve" over reforming secondary education exam system back in 2005,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and in 2006 said it was "naive" to allocate local school places through parental choice, with lottery selection being the best way to avoid "bloody awful" schools existing as a side effect of parents pushing for their children to study elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> During Sheerman's chairmanship, the select committee produced reports on subjects such as home education, education outside the classroom, and young people not in education employment or training (NEETs).
</p><p>Sheerman consistently voted for the Iraq war,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and has nearly always voted to block subsequent independent investigations into the war, with the most recent such vote in 2016.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> He is a member of <a href="/wiki/Labour_Friends_of_Israel" title="Labour Friends of Israel">Labour Friends of Israel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He is Chair of the Labour Forum for Criminal Justice and of the Cross-Party Advisory Group on Preparation for European Monetary Union. Outside parliament, he is Chair of the National Educational Research and Development Trust, and a trustee of the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://yorkshirechildrenscentre.org.uk/">National Children's Centre</a>. His political interests are listed as trade, industry, finance, <a href="/wiki/Further_education" title="Further education">further education</a>, education, economy, the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. His recreations include walking, biography and films. In 1993, Sheerman co-wrote, with Isaac Kramnick, a biography of the Labour intellectual <a href="/wiki/Harold_Laski" title="Harold Laski">Harold Laski</a>.
</p><p>In June 2009, Sheerman called for a secret ballot of the <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Labour_Party" title="Parliamentary Labour Party">Parliamentary Labour Party</a> on whether <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Brown" title="Gordon Brown">Gordon Brown</a> should continue in office as prime minister. This followed widespread criticism of Brown's performance and the resignation of Cabinet member <a href="/wiki/James_Purnell" title="James Purnell">James Purnell</a>. Sheerman later reassured his local party chairman that he had not directly called for Brown's resignation.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sheerman called for a London catering company to employ "English workers" in a <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> exchange on 23 April 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The comments reached the national press. In response Sheerman said the objection to him speaking out was "pernicious political correctness".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He is founder and chairman of <a href="/wiki/Policy_Connect" title="Policy Connect">Policy Connect</a>, a cross-party, not-for-profit based in London, where he regularly chairs seminar events and research inquiries. He is also chair and co-chair of a number of official All-Party Parliamentary Groups, including the <a href="/wiki/All-Party_Parliamentary_Carbon_Monoxide_Group" title="All-Party Parliamentary Carbon Monoxide Group">All-Party Parliamentary Carbon Monoxide Group</a>, the All-Party Parliamentary Manufacturing Group, and the Bullying All-Party Group. Since 2012, Sheerman has led the Schools to Work Commission, the Labour Party's policy review on the transition from education to employment.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2015, Sheerman caused controversy when he argued that lowering the voting age to 16, by reducing childhood, might raise the risk of sexual abuse.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He supported <a href="/wiki/Owen_Smith" title="Owen Smith">Owen Smith</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2016_Labour_Party_(UK)_leadership_election" class="mw-redirect" title="2016 Labour Party (UK) leadership election">2016 Labour Party (UK) leadership election</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sheerman voted for the triggering of Article 50 (the trigger for leaving the EU). Kirklees, which his constituency falls within, saw 55% of its residents vote in the 2016 EU referendum to leave the EU. In October 2017, Sheerman appeared on television and announced that he believed that remain vote came mostly from the better educated people in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Barry_Sheerman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Barry Sheerman married Pamela Elizabeth Brenchley in 1965 in north <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a>, with whom he has one son (born in 1978) and three daughters (born in 1970, 1972 and 1981). He lives in a flat in <a href="/wiki/Almondbury" title="Almondbury">Almondbury</a>, Huddersfield.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
