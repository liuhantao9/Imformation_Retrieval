<p>A <b>tropical climate</b> in the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a> is a non-<a href="/wiki/Arid" title="Arid">arid</a> climate in which all twelve months have mean temperatures of warmer than 18&#160;°C (64&#160;°F). In <a href="/wiki/Tropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical">tropical</a> climates there are often only two seasons: a wet season and a dry season, which in the case of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_savanna_climate" title="Tropical savanna climate">Aw/As</a> climates, the wet season may run from the high-sun half of the year (Which runs from April to September in the Northern Hemisphere, and from October to March in the Southern Hemisphere) or the low-sun half of the year (Which runs from October to March in the Northern Hemisphere, and from April to September in the Southern Hemisphere). Tropical climates are typically frost-free, and changes in the solar angle are small. In tropical climates, the temperature remains relatively constant (hot) throughout the year. Sunlight is intense.
</p>

<p>Within the tropical climate zone there are three distinct varieties based on precipitation:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_rain_forest_climate">Tropical rain forest climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_climate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tropical rain forest climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_rain_forest_climate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tropical rain forest climate (page does not exist)">Tropical rain forest climate</a></div>
<p>Tropical rain forest climates (AF) normally occur close to the <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>, typically within 10 or 15 degrees north and south latitude of the equator. This climate is dominated by the <a href="/wiki/Doldrums" class="mw-redirect" title="Doldrums">doldrums</a> or <a href="/w/index.php?title=Inter-tropical_Convergence_Zone&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Inter-tropical Convergence Zone (page does not exist)">Inter-tropical Convergence Zone</a>, and area of sinking air, calm winds, and frequent rainfall. Seasonal rainfall is heavy, often more than 200 cm (80 inches) annually. In tropical rain forest climates, there is either no dry season or at most two dry months. All 12 months have average precipitation of at least 60&#160;mm (2.4&#160;in). 
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Amazon_basin" title="Amazon basin">Amazon basin</a> of South America, the <a href="/wiki/Congo_Basin" title="Congo Basin">Congo Basin</a> of equatorial Africa and oceanic margins of parts of tropical West Africa have tropical rainforests climates. The insular and peninsular area of <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> (Malaysia, Thailand, Philippines, New Guinea. Indonesia, etc.) comprises the most extensive region with tropical rainforest climate. Eastern Central America and parts of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Caribbean islands">Caribbean islands</a>, the Atlantic margins of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> island, and southern <a href="/wiki/Ceylon" class="mw-redirect" title="Ceylon">Ceylon</a> comprise smaller areas with tropical rainforest climate.

<p>Some of the places with this climate are indeed uniformly and monotonously wet throughout the year (e.g., the northwest <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific</a> coast of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a> to <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>; see, for instance, <a href="/wiki/Andagoya" title="Andagoya">Andagoya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>), but in many cases, the period of higher sun and longer days is distinctly wettest (as at <a href="/wiki/Palembang" title="Palembang">Palembang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>) or the time of lower sun and shorter days may have more rain (as at <a href="/wiki/Sitiawan" title="Sitiawan">Sitiawan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>).
</p><p>(<b>Note</b>. The term <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">aseasonal</a> refers to the lack in the tropical zone of large differences in daylight hours and mean monthly (or daily) temperature throughout the year. Annual cyclic changes occur in the tropics, but not as predictable as those in the temperate zone, albeit unrelated to temperature, but to water availability whether as rain, mist, soil, or groundwater. Plant response (e. g., <a href="/wiki/Phenology" title="Phenology">phenology</a>), animal (feeding, migration, reproduction, etc.), and human activities (plant sowing, harvesting, hunting, fishing, etc.) are tuned to this 'seasonality'. Indeed, in tropical South America and Central America, the 'rainy season' (and the 'high water season') is called <a href="/wiki/Rainy_season" class="mw-redirect" title="Rainy season"><i> Invierno</i></a> or <i>inverno</i>, though it could occur in the Northern Hemisphere summer; likewise, the 'dry season' (and 'low water season') is called <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season"><i>Verano</i></a> or <i>verão</i>, and can occur in the Northern Hemisphere winter).
</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Tropical_monsoon_climate" title="Tropical monsoon climate">Tropical monsoon climate</a></div>

</div>
<p>Sometimes <i>As</i> is used in place of <i>Aw</i> if the dry season occurs during the high-sun half of the year, which runs from April to September in the northern hemisphere, and from October to March in the southern hemisphere. This is the case in parts of <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">Hawaii</a>, parts of the Caribbean, East Africa, areas of South Asia around the <a href="/wiki/Laccadive_Sea" title="Laccadive Sea">Laccadive Sea</a> and the Brazilian Northeastern Coast. In most places that have tropical wet and dry climates (Aw), however, the dry season occurs during the time of low-sun half of the year, which runs in opposition to As climates.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Intertropical_Convergence_Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropical_climate&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Intertropical Convergence Zone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Omega-500-july-era40-1979.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Omega-500-july-era40-1979.png/350px-Omega-500-july-era40-1979.png" decoding="async" width="350" height="184" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Omega-500-july-era40-1979.png/525px-Omega-500-july-era40-1979.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Omega-500-july-era40-1979.png/700px-Omega-500-july-era40-1979.png 2x" data-file-width="717" data-file-height="376" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Omega-500-july-era40-1979.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a> vertical velocity at 500 hPa, July average in units of <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">pascals</a> per second. Ascent (negative values) is <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">equator</a>; descent (positive values) is more diffuse.</div></div></div>
<p>Because of the <a href="/wiki/Effect_of_sun_angle_on_climate" class="mw-redirect" title="Effect of sun angle on climate">effect of sun angle on climate</a> most areas within the tropics are hot year-round, with diurnal variations in temperature exceeding seasonal variations. Seasonal variations in tropical climate are dominated by changes in precipitation, which are in turn largely influenced by the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rain_belt" title="Tropical rain belt">tropical rain belt</a> or <a href="/wiki/Intertropical_Convergence_Zone" title="Intertropical Convergence Zone">Intertropical Convergence Zone</a> (ITCZ), a portion of the <a href="/wiki/Hadley_cell" title="Hadley cell">Hadley cell</a>. The ITCZ is shown, for July average, in the graphic. Areas of ascending air have heavy rainfall; areas of descending air are dry. The ITCZ somewhat follows the solar equator throughout the year, but with geographical variations, and in some areas (India) is heavily influenced by local large-scale <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">monsoons</a>.
</p>
