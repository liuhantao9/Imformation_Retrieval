<p>The <b>Clean Air Act of 1963</b> (<a href="/wiki/Title_42_of_the_United_States_Code" title="Title 42 of the United States Code">42 U.S.C.</a> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/42/7401">§ 7401</a>) is a <a href="/wiki/United_States_federal_law" class="mw-redirect" title="United States federal law">United States federal law</a> designed to control <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> on a national level.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It is one of the United States' first and most influential modern <a href="/wiki/Environmental_law" title="Environmental law">environmental laws</a>, and one of the most comprehensive <a href="/wiki/Air_quality_law" title="Air quality law">air quality laws</a> in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> As with many other major <a href="/wiki/United_States_environmental_law" title="United States environmental law">U.S. federal environmental statutes</a>, it is administered by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">U.S. Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA), in coordination with state, local, and tribal governments.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Its implementing regulations are codified at <a href="/wiki/Title_40_of_the_Code_of_Federal_Regulations" title="Title 40 of the Code of Federal Regulations">40 C.F.R.</a> Sub-chapter C, Parts 50-97.
</p><p>The 1955 <a href="/wiki/Air_Pollution_Control_Act" title="Air Pollution Control Act">Air Pollution Control Act</a> was the first U.S. federal legislation that pertained to <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a>; it also provided funds for federal government research of air pollution.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The first federal legislation to actually pertain to "controlling" air pollution was the Clean Air Act of 1963.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The 1963 act accomplished this by establishing a federal program within the U.S. Public Health Service and authorizing research into techniques for monitoring and controlling air pollution.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was first amended in 1965, by the <a href="/wiki/Motor_Vehicle_Air_Pollution_Control_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Motor Vehicle Air Pollution Control Act">Motor Vehicle Air Pollution Control Act</a>, which authorized the federal government to set required <a href="/wiki/Automobile_emissions_control" class="mw-redirect" title="Automobile emissions control">standards for controlling the emission of pollutants</a> from certain automobiles, beginning with the 1968 models. A second amendment, the Air Quality Act of 1967, enabled the federal government to increase its activities to investigate enforcing interstate air pollution transport, and, for the first time, to perform far-reaching ambient monitoring studies and stationary source inspections. The 1967 act also authorized expanded studies of air pollutant emission inventories, ambient monitoring techniques, and control techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-CAA-history_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAA-history-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> While only six states had air pollution programs in 1960, all 50 states had air pollution programs by 1970 due to the federal funding and legislation of the 1960s.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Amendments approved in 1970 greatly expanded the federal mandate, requiring comprehensive federal and state regulations for both <a href="/wiki/Major_stationary_source" title="Major stationary source">stationary (industrial) pollution sources</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mobile_source_air_pollution" title="Mobile source air pollution">mobile sources</a>. It also significantly expanded federal <a href="/wiki/Environmental_law#Enforcement" title="Environmental law">enforcement</a>. Also, EPA was established on December 2, 1970 for the purpose of consolidating pertinent federal research, monitoring, standard-setting and enforcement activities into one agency that ensures environmental protection.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA-history_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-history-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Distillations_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Distillations-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Further amendments were made in 1990 to address the problems of <a href="/wiki/Acid_rain" title="Acid rain">acid rain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion" title="Ozone depletion">ozone depletion</a>, and toxic air pollution, and to establish a national permit program for stationary sources, and increased enforcement authority. The amendments also established new auto gasoline reformulation requirements, set <a href="/wiki/Reid_vapor_pressure" title="Reid vapor pressure">Reid vapor pressure</a> (RVP) standards to <a href="/wiki/Evaporative_emission#Evaporative_emissions_control" class="mw-redirect" title="Evaporative emission">control Evaporative emissions</a> from gasoline, and mandated new gasoline formulations sold from May to September in many states. Reviewing his tenure as EPA Administrator under President <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">George H. W. Bush</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_K._Reilly" title="William K. Reilly">William K. Reilly</a> characterized passage of the 1990 amendments to the Clean Air Act as his most notable accomplishment.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Clean Air Act was the first major <a href="/wiki/Environmental_law" title="Environmental law">environmental law</a> in the United States to include a provision for <a href="/wiki/Citizen_suit" title="Citizen suit">citizen suits</a>. Numerous state and local governments have enacted similar legislation, either implementing federal programs or filling in locally important gaps in federal programs.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Part_A:_Air_Quality_and_Emissions_Limitations">Part A: Air Quality and Emissions Limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Part A: Air Quality and Emissions Limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>This section of the act declares that protecting and enhancing the nation's air quality promotes <a href="/wiki/Environmental_health" title="Environmental health">public health</a>. The law encourages to prevent regional air pollution and control programs. It also provides technical and financial assistance for preventing air pollution at both state and local governments. Additional sub chapters cover cooperation, research, investigation, training, and other activities. Grants for air pollution planning and control programs, and interstate air quality agencies and program cost limitations are also included in this section.<sup id="cite_ref-Clean_Air_Act_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clean_Air_Act-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The act mandates air quality control regions, designated as attainment vs non-attainment. Non-attainment areas do not meet national standards for primary or secondary ambient air quality. Attainment areas meet these standards, while unclassified areas cannot be classified based on the available information.<sup id="cite_ref-Clean_Air_Act_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clean_Air_Act-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Air quality criteria, national primary and secondary ambient air quality standards, state implementation plans and performance standards for new stationary sources are covered in Part A as well. The list of hazardous air pollutants that the act establishes includes compounds of <a href="/wiki/Acetaldehyde" title="Acetaldehyde">Acetaldehyde</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chloroform" title="Chloroform">chloroform</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phenol" title="Phenol">Phenol</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Selenium" title="Selenium">selenium</a>. The list also includes mineral fiber emissions from manufacturing or processing glass, rock or slag fibers as well as radioactive atoms. The list can periodically be modified. The act lists unregulated radioactive pollutants such as <a href="/wiki/Cadmium" title="Cadmium">cadmium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arsenic" title="Arsenic">arsenic</a>, and poly cyclic organic matter and it mandates listing them if they will cause or contribute to air pollution that endangers public health, under section 7408 or 7412.<sup id="cite_ref-Clean_Air_Act_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clean_Air_Act-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The remaining sub-chapters cover smokestack heights, state plan adequacy, and estimating emissions of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compounds" class="mw-redirect" title="Volatile organic compounds">volatile organic compounds</a>, and oxides of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> from area and mobile sources. Measures to prevent unemployment or other economic disruption include using local <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> or coal derivatives to comply with implementation requirements. The final sub chapter in this act focuses on land use authority.<sup id="cite_ref-Clean_Air_Act_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clean_Air_Act-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Part_B:_Ozone_Protection">Part B: Ozone Protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Part B: Ozone Protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Because of advances in the atmospheric chemistry, this section was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)#Title_VI_-_Stratospheric_Ozone_Protection" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Title VI</a> when the law was amended in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This change in the law reflected significant changes in scientific understanding of <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> formation and depletion. Ozone absorbs <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">UVC light</a> and shorter wave <a href="/wiki/UVB" class="mw-redirect" title="UVB">UVB</a>, and lets through UVA, which is largely harmless to people. Ozone exists naturally in the <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">stratosphere</a>, not the troposphere. It is laterally distributed because it is destroyed by strong sunlight, so there is more ozone at the poles. Ozone is created when O<sub>2</sub> comes in contact with photons from solar radiation. Therefore, a decrease in the intensity of solar radiation also results in a decrease in the formation of ozone in the stratosphere. This exchange is known as the Chapman mechanism:
</p>
<dl><dd>O<sub>2</sub> + UV photon → 2 O (note that atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> as O is highly unstable)</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>O + O<sub>2</sub> + M → O<sub>3</sub> (O<sub>3</sub> is Ozone) + M</dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>M represents a third molecule, needed to carry off the excess energy of the collision of O + O<sub>2</sub>.</i></dd></dl>
<p>Atmospheric <a href="/wiki/Freon" title="Freon">freon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbons" class="mw-redirect" title="Chlorofluorocarbons">chlorofluorocarbons</a> (CFCs) contribute to ozone depletion (Chlorine is a catalytic agent in ozone destruction). Following discovery of the ozone hole in 1985, the 1987 <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a> successfully implemented a plan to replace CFCs and was viewed by some environmentalists as an example of what is possible for the future of environmental issues, if the political will is present.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Part_C_-_Prevention_of_Significant_Deterioration_of_Air_Quality">Part C - Prevention of Significant Deterioration of Air Quality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Part C - Prevention of Significant Deterioration of Air Quality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Clean Air Act requires permits to build or add to major stationary sources of air pollution. This permitting process, known as New Source Review (NSR), applies to sources in areas that meet <a href="/wiki/National_Ambient_Air_Quality_Standards" title="National Ambient Air Quality Standards">air quality standards</a> as well as areas that are unclassified.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Permits in attainment or unclassified areas are referred to as Prevention of Significant Deterioration (PSD) permits, while permits for sources located in nonattainment areas are referred to as non attainment area (NAA) permits.<sup id="cite_ref-NSR_Workshop_Manual_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NSR_Workshop_Manual-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The fundamental goals of the PSD program are to:
</p>
<ol><li>prevent new non-attainment areas by ensuring economic growth in harmony with existing clean air;</li>
<li>protect public health and welfare from any adverse effects;</li>
<li>preserve and enhance the air quality in national parks and other areas of special natural recreational, scenic, or historic value.<sup id="cite_ref-NSR_Workshop_Manual_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NSR_Workshop_Manual-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>3</span></sup></li></ol>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Part_D:_Plan_Requirements_for_Non-attainment_Areas">Part D: Plan Requirements for Non-attainment Areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Part D: Plan Requirements for Non-attainment Areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Under the Clean Air Act states are required to submit a plan for non-attainment areas to reach attainment status as soon as possible but in no more than five years, based on the severity of the air pollution and the difficulty posed by obtaining cleaner air.
</p><p>The plan must include:
</p>
<ul><li>an inventory of all pollutants</li>
<li>permits</li>
<li>control measures, means and techniques to reach standard qualifications</li>
<li>contingency measures</li></ul>
<p>The plan must be approved or revised if required for approval, and specify whether local governments or the state will implement and enforce the various changes. Achieving attainment status makes a request for reevaluation possible. It must include a plan for maintenance of air quality.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Title_II:_Emission_Standards_for_Moving_Sources">Title II: Emission Standards for Moving Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Title II: Emission Standards for Moving Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Part_A:_Motor_Vehicle_Emission_and_Fuel_Standards">Part A: Motor Vehicle Emission and Fuel Standards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Part A: Motor Vehicle Emission and Fuel Standards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Sub-chapters of Title II cover state standards and grants, prohibited acts and actions to restrain violations, as well as a study of <a href="/wiki/Exhaust_gas" title="Exhaust gas">emissions</a> from non road vehicles (other than locomotives) to determine whether they cause or contribute to air pollution. <a href="/wiki/Motorcycles" class="mw-redirect" title="Motorcycles">Motorcycles</a> are treated in the same way as automobiles under the emission standards for new motor vehicles or motor vehicle engines. The last few sub chapters deal with high altitude performance adjustments, motor vehicle compliance program fees, prohibition on production of engines requiring <a href="/wiki/Leaded_gasoline" class="mw-redirect" title="Leaded gasoline">leaded gasoline</a> and urban bus standards.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This part of the bill was extremely controversial the time it was passed. The automobile industry argued that it could not meet the new standards.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Senators expressed concern about impact on the economy. However the stricter standards led to the creation of the catalytic converter, which was a revolutionary development. (Coincidentally, these converters didn't work well with leaded gas, which contributed to the swift removal of lead from gasoline that was also recognized for having adverse health effects.)<sup id="cite_ref-:3_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Specific new emissions standards for moving sources passed years later.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Part_B:_Aircraft_Emission_Standards">Part B: Aircraft Emission Standards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Part B: Aircraft Emission Standards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Many volatile organic compounds (VOCs) are emitted over airports and affect the air quality in the region. VOCs include <a href="/wiki/Benzene" title="Benzene">benzene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Formaldehyde" title="Formaldehyde">formaldehyde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Butadienes" class="mw-redirect" title="Butadienes">butadienes</a> which are known to cause health problems such as birth defects, <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">cancer</a> and skin irritation. Hundreds of tons of emissions from aircraft, ground support equipment, heating systems, and shuttles and passenger vehicles are released into the air, causing <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">smog</a>. Therefore, major cities such as <a href="/wiki/Seattle" title="Seattle">Seattle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denver" title="Denver">Denver</a>, and <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> require a <a href="/wiki/Climate_Action_Plan" class="mw-redirect" title="Climate Action Plan">Climate Action Plan</a> as well as a <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse" title="Greenhouse">greenhouse</a> gas inventory. Additionally, federal programs such as the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.faa.gov/airports/environmental/vale/">Voluntary Airport Low Emissions Program (VALE)</a> are working to offset costs for programs that reduce emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-Sustainability_Trends_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainability_Trends-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Title II sets emission standards for airlines and aircraft engines and adopts standards set by the <a href="/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organization" title="International Civil Aviation Organization">International Civil Aviation Organization</a> (ICAO). However aircraft <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> emission standards have not been established by either ICAO nor the EPA.<sup id="cite_ref-Aircraft_Emissions_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Aircraft_Emissions-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> It is the responsibility of the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_Transportation" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of Transportation">Secretary of Transportation</a>, after consultation with the Administrator, to prescribe regulations that comply with <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.gpo.gov/fdsys/pkg/USCODE-2013-title42/html/USCODE-2013-title42-chap85-subchapII-partB-sec7571.htm">42 U.S. Code § 7571</a> (Establishment of standards) and ensure the necessary inspections take place.<sup id="cite_ref-Title_II_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Title_II-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Part_C:_Clean_Fuel_Vehicles">Part C: Clean Fuel Vehicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Part C: Clean Fuel Vehicles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Trucks and automobiles play a large role in deleterious air quality. Harmful chemicals such as <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbons" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrocarbons">hydrocarbons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> are released from motor vehicles. Some of these also react with sunlight to produce <a href="/wiki/Photochemical_smog" class="mw-redirect" title="Photochemical smog">Photochemical</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Biodiesel_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Biodiesel-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> These harmful substances change the climate, alter ocean pH and include toxins that may cause cancer, birth defects or respiratory illness.
Motor vehicles increased in the 1990s since approximately 58 percent of households owned two or more vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-Biodiesel_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Biodiesel-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The Clean Fuel Vehicle programs focused on alternative fuel use and petroleum fuels that met low emission vehicle (LEV) levels. Compressed <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethanol" title="Ethanol">ethanol</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">methanol</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> liquefied <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> gas and electricity are examples of cleaner alternative fuel. Programs such as the California Clean Fuels Program and pilot program are increasing demand that for new fuels to be developed to reduce harmful emissions.<sup id="cite_ref-Biodiesel_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Biodiesel-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The California pilot program incorporated under this section focuses on pollution control in <a href="/wiki/Ozone" title="Ozone">ozone</a> non-attainment areas. The provisions apply to light-duty trucks and light-duty vehicles in <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>. The state also requires that clean alternative fuels for sale at numerous locations with sufficient geographic distribution for convenience. Production of clean-fuel vehicles isn't mandated except as part of the California pilot program.<sup id="cite_ref-Clean_Air_Act_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clean_Air_Act-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Title_III:_General_Provisions">Title III: General Provisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Title III: General Provisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Under the law prior to 1990, EPA was required to construct a list of Hazardous Air Pollutants as well as health-based standards for each one. There were 187 air pollutants listed and the source from which they came. The EPA was given ten years to generate technology-based emission standards. Title III is considered a second phase, allowing the EPA to assess lingering risks after the enactment of the first phase of emission standards. Title III also enacts new standards with regard to the protection of public health.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A citizen may file a lawsuit to obtain compliance with an emission standard issued by the EPA or by a state, unless there is an ongoing enforcement action being pursued by EPA or the appropriate state agency.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Title_IV:_Noise_Pollution">Title IV: Noise Pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Title IV: Noise Pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This title pre-dates the <i>Clean Air Act</i>. With the passage of the <i>Clean Air Act</i>, it became codified as Title IV. However, another Title IV was enacted in the 1990 amendments. The second Title IV was then appended to this Title IV as Title IV-A (see below).
</p><p>This title established the EPA Office of Noise Abatement and Control to reduce <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise pollution</a> in urban areas, to minimize noise-related impacts on psychological and physiological effects on humans, effects on wildlife and property (including values), and other noise-related issues. The agency was also assigned to run experiments to study the effects of noise.
</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Noise_Control_Act" title="Noise Control Act">Noise Control Act</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Title_IV-A:_Acid_Deposition_Control">Title IV-A: Acid Deposition Control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Title IV-A: Acid Deposition Control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This title was added as part of the 1990 amendments. It addresses the issue of acid rain, which is caused by <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxides" class="mw-redirect" title="Nitrogen oxides">nitrogen oxides</a> (<span class="chemf nowrap">NO<span style="display:inline-block; margin-bottom:-0.3em; vertical-align:-0.4em; line-height:1.2em; font-size:70%; text-align:left;"><br /> <i>x</i> </span></span>) and <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> (SO<sub>2</sub>) emissions from electric power plants powered by fossil fuels, and other industrial sources. The 1990 amendments gave industries more pollution control options including switching to low-sulfur coal and/or adding devices that controlled the harmful emissions. In some cases plants had to be closed down to prevent the dangerous chemicals from entering the atmosphere.<sup id="cite_ref-Stratosphere_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stratosphere-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Title IV-A mandated a two-step process to reduce SO<sub>2</sub> emissions. The first stage required more than 100 electric generating facilities larger than 100 megawatts to meet a 3.5 million ton SO<sub>2</sub> emission reduction by January 1995. The second stage gave facilities larger than 75 megawatts a January 2000 deadline.<sup id="cite_ref-Acid_Deposition_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Acid_Deposition-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Title_V:_Permits">Title V: Permits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Title V: Permits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 1990 amendments authorized a national operating permit program, covering thousands of large industrial and commercial sources.<sup id="cite_ref-Permits_and_Enforcement._27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Permits_and_Enforcement.-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> It required large businesses to address pollutants released into the air, measure their quantity, and have a plan to control and minimize them as well as to periodically report. This consolidated requirements for a facility into a single document.<sup id="cite_ref-Permits_and_Enforcement._27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Permits_and_Enforcement.-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In non-attainment areas, permits were required for sources that emit as little as 50, 25, or 10 tons per year of VOCs depending on the severity of the region’s non-attainment status.<sup id="cite_ref-Clean_Air_Act_Summary_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clean_Air_Act_Summary-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most permits are issued by state and local agencies.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> If the state does not adequately monitor requirements, the EPA may take control. The public may request to view the permits by contacting the EPA. The permit is limited to no more than five years and requires a renewal.<sup id="cite_ref-Clean_Air_Act_Summary_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clean_Air_Act_Summary-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Title_VI:_Stratospheric_Ozone_Protection">Title VI: Stratospheric Ozone Protection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Title VI: Stratospheric Ozone Protection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Starting in 1990, Title VI mandated regulations regarding the use and production of chemicals that harm the Earth’s stratospheric <a href="/wiki/Ozone_layer" title="Ozone layer">ozone layer</a>. This ozone layer protects against harmful <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">ultraviolet</a> B sunlight linked to several medical conditions including cataracts and skin cancer.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The ozone-destroying chemicals were classified into two groups, Class I and Class II. Class I consists of substances, including chlorofluorocarbons, that have an <a href="/wiki/Ozone_depletion_potential" title="Ozone depletion potential">ozone depletion potential</a> (ODP) (HL) of 0.2 or higher. Class II lists substances, including hydro chlorofluorocarbons, that are known to or may be detrimental to the stratosphere. Both groups have a timeline for phase-out:
</p>
<ul><li>For Class I substances, no more than seven years after being added to the list and</li>
<li>For Class II substances no more than ten years.<sup id="cite_ref-Stratosphere_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stratosphere-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Title VI establishes methods for preventing harmful chemicals from entering the stratosphere in the first place, including recycling or proper disposal of chemicals and finding substitutes that cause less or no damage.<sup id="cite_ref-Stratosphere_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stratosphere-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The Significant New Alternatives Policy (SNAP) Program is EPA's program to evaluate and regulate substitutes for the ozone-depleting chemicals that are being phased out under the stratospheric ozone protection provisions of the Clean Air Act.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Over 190 countries signed the <a href="/wiki/Montreal_Protocol" title="Montreal Protocol">Montreal Protocol</a> in 1987, agreeing to work to eliminate or limit the use of chemicals with ozone-destroying properties.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Cleanup plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-Cleanup" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png" decoding="async" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/60px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/80px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="48" data-file-height="48" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section may <b>require <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Cleanup" title="Wikipedia:Cleanup">cleanup</a></b> to meet Wikipedia's <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style">quality standards</a>. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation">Legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> passed the first legislation to address air pollution with the 1955 Air Pollution Control Act that provided funds to the U.S. Public Health service, but did not formulate pollution regulation.<sup id="cite_ref-encyc-jacob_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyc-jacob-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> However, the Clean Air Act in 1963 created a research and regulatory program in the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Public_Health_Service" class="mw-redirect" title="Public Health Service">Public Health Service</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The Act authorized development of <a href="/wiki/Emission_standard" title="Emission standard">emission standards</a> for <a href="/wiki/Major_stationary_source" title="Major stationary source">stationary sources</a>, but not <a href="/wiki/Mobile_source_air_pollution" title="Mobile source air pollution">mobile sources of air pollution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jacobson_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobson-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>211</span></sup> The 1967 <b>Air Quality Act</b> mandated enforcement of interstate air pollution standards and authorized ambient monitoring studies and stationary source inspections.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA_History_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA_History-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the Clean Air Act Extension of 1970, Congress greatly expanded the federal mandate by requiring comprehensive federal and state regulations for both industrial and mobile sources.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The law established four new regulatory programs:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/National_Ambient_Air_Quality_Standards" title="National Ambient Air Quality Standards">National Ambient Air Quality Standards</a> (NAAQS). EPA was required to promulgate national standards for six criteria pollutants: <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide" title="Nitrogen dioxide">nitrogen dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">particulate matter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Photochemical_smog" class="mw-redirect" title="Photochemical smog">photochemical oxidants</a>. (Some of the criteria pollutants were revised in subsequent legislation.)<sup id="cite_ref-Jacobson_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobson-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>213</span></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/State_Implementation_Plan" title="State Implementation Plan">State Implementation Plans</a> (SIPs)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/New_Source_Performance_Standard" title="New Source Performance Standard">New Source Performance Standards</a> (NSPS); and</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/National_Emissions_Standards_for_Hazardous_Air_Pollutants" title="National Emissions Standards for Hazardous Air Pollutants">National Emissions Standards for Hazardous Air Pollutants</a> (NESHAPs).</li></ul>
<p>The 1970 law is sometimes called the "Muskie Act" because of the central role Maine Senator <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Muskie" title="Edmund Muskie">Edmund Muskie</a> played in drafting the bill.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>To implement the strict new Clean Air Act of 1970, during his first term as EPA Administrator <a href="/wiki/William_Ruckelshaus" title="William Ruckelshaus">William Ruckelshaus</a> spent 60% of his time on the automobile industry, whose emissions were to be reduced 90% under the new law. Senators had been frustrated at the industry’s failure to cut emissions under previous, weaker air laws.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Clean Air Act Amendments of 1977 required Prevention of Significant Deterioration (PSD) of air quality for areas attaining the NAAQS and added requirements for non-attainment areas.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1990 Clean Air Act added regulatory programs for control of acid deposition (acid rain) and stationary source operating permits. The provisions aimed at reducing sulfur dioxide emissions included a cap-and-trade program, which gave power companies more flexibility in meeting the law's goals compared to earlier iterations of the Clean Air Act.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>  The amendments moved considerably beyond the original criteria pollutants, expanding the NESHAP program with a list of 189 hazardous air pollutants to be controlled within hundreds of source categories, according to a specific schedule.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The NAAQS program was also expanded. Other new provisions covered stratospheric ozone protection, increased enforcement authority and expanded research programs.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_Clean_Air_Act">History of the Clean Air Act</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: History of the Clean Air Act">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">Introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clean_Air_Act_Signing.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Clean_Air_Act_Signing.jpg/220px-Clean_Air_Act_Signing.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="125" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Clean_Air_Act_Signing.jpg/330px-Clean_Air_Act_Signing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Clean_Air_Act_Signing.jpg/440px-Clean_Air_Act_Signing.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="1135" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clean_Air_Act_Signing.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>President <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a> signing the 1967 Air Quality Act in the East Room of <a href="/wiki/The_White_House" class="mw-redirect" title="The White House">the White House</a>, November 21, 1967.</div></div></div>
<p>The legal authority for federal programs regarding air pollution control is based on the 1990 Clean Air Act Amendments (1990 CAAA). These are the latest in a series of amendments made to the Clean Air Act (CAA), often referred to as "the Act." This legislation modified and extended federal legal authority provided by the earlier Clean Air Acts of 1963 and 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-CAA-history_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAA-history-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1955 <a href="/wiki/Air_Pollution_Control_Act" title="Air Pollution Control Act">Air Pollution Control Act</a> was the first federal legislation involving air pollution; it authorized $3 million per year to the U.S. Public Health Service for five years to fund federal level air pollution research, air pollution control research, and technical and training assistance to the states. Subsequently, the act was extended for four years in 1959 with funding levels at $5 million per year. The act was then amended in 1960 and 1962. Although the 1955 act brought the air pollution issue to the federal level, no federal regulations were formulated. Control and prevention of air pollution was instead delegated to state and local agencies.<sup id="cite_ref-encyc-jacob_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-encyc-jacob-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Clean Air Act of 1963 was the first federal legislation regarding air pollution control. It established a federal program within the U.S. Public Health Service and authorized research into techniques for monitoring and controlling air pollution. In 1967, the <a href="/wiki/Air_quality_law" title="Air quality law">Air Quality Act</a> was enacted in order to expand federal government activities. In accordance with this law, enforcement proceedings were initiated in areas subject to interstate air pollution transport. As part of these proceedings, the federal government for the first time conducted extensive ambient monitoring studies and stationary source inspections.
</p><p>The Air Quality Act of 1967 also authorized expanded studies of air pollutant emission inventories, ambient monitoring techniques, and control techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-CAA-history_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAA-history-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Clean_Air_Act_of_1970">Clean Air Act of 1970</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Clean Air Act of 1970">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <i>Clean Air Act of 1970</i> (1970 CAA) authorized the development of comprehensive federal and state regulations to limit emissions from both stationary (industrial) sources and mobile sources. Four major regulatory programs affecting stationary sources were initiated:
</p>
<ul><li>the National Ambient Air Quality Standards [NAAQS (pronounced "knacks")],</li>
<li>State Implementation Plans (SIPs),</li>
<li>New Source Performance Standards (NSPS),</li>
<li>and National Emission Standards for Hazardous Air Pollutants (NESHAPs).</li></ul>
<p>Enforcement authority was substantially expanded. This very important legislation was adopted at approximately the same time as the <i><a href="/wiki/National_Environmental_Policy_Act" title="National Environmental Policy Act">National Environmental Policy Act</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-CAA-history_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAA-history-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Distillations_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Distillations-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Clean_Air_Act_Amendments_of_1977">Clean Air Act Amendments of 1977</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Clean Air Act Amendments of 1977">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Major amendments were added to the <i>Clean Air Act</i> in 1977 (1977 CAAA). The 1977 Amendments primarily concerned provisions for the Prevention of Significant Deterioration (PSD) of air quality in areas attaining the NAAQS. The 1977 CAAA also contained requirements pertaining to sources in non-attainment areas for NAAQS. A non-attainment area is a geographic area that does not meet one or more of the federal air quality standards. Both of these 1977 CAAA established major permit review requirements to ensure attainment and maintenance of the NAAQS.<sup id="cite_ref-CAA-history_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAA-history-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> These amendments also included the adoption of an offset trading policy originally applied to Los Angeles in 1974 that enables new sources to offset their emissions by purchasing extra reductions from existing sources.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Clean_Air_Act_Amendments_of_1990">Clean Air Act Amendments of 1990</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Clean Air Act Amendments of 1990">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Another set of major amendments to the Clean Air Act occurred in 1990 (1990 CAAA). The 1990 CAAA substantially increased the authority and responsibility of the federal government. New regulatory programs were authorized for control of acid deposition (acid rain)<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> and for the issuance of stationary source operating permits. The NESHAPs were incorporated into a greatly expanded program for controlling toxic air pollutants. The provisions for attainment and maintenance of NAAQS were substantially modified and expanded. Other revisions included provisions regarding stratospheric ozone protection, increased enforcement authority, and expanded research programs.<sup id="cite_ref-CAA-history_7-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAA-history-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Milestones">Milestones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Milestones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Some of the principal milestones in the evolution of the Clean Air Act are as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-CAA-history_7-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CAA-history-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>The Air Pollution Control Act</i> of 1955
</p>
<ul><li>First federal air pollution legislation</li>
<li>Funded research on scope and sources of air pollution</li></ul>
<p><i>Clean Air Act</i> of 1963
</p>
<ul><li>Authorized a national program to address air pollution</li>
<li>Authorized research into techniques to minimize air pollution</li></ul>
<p><i>Air Quality Act</i> of 1967
</p>

</p><p>In 1997 EPA tightened the NAAQS regarding permissible levels of the <a href="/wiki/Tropospheric_ozone" title="Tropospheric ozone">ground-level ozone</a> that make up <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">smog</a> and the fine airborne <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">particulate matter</a> that makes up <a href="/wiki/Soot" title="Soot">soot</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt062697_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt062697-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cnn110700_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn110700-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The decision came after months of public review of the proposed new standards, as well as long and fierce internal discussion within the <a href="/wiki/Clinton_administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Clinton administration">Clinton administration</a>, leading to the most divisive environmental debate of that decade.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt062597_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt062597-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
The new regulations were challenged in the courts by industry groups as a violation of the <a href="/wiki/Nondelegation_doctrine" title="Nondelegation doctrine">U.S. Constitution's nondelegation principle</a> and eventually landed in the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Supreme Court of the United States">Supreme Court of the United States</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-cnn110700_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cnn110700-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> whose 2001 unanimous ruling in <i><a href="/wiki/Whitman_v._American_Trucking_Ass%27ns,_Inc." title="Whitman v. American Trucking Ass&#39;ns, Inc.">Whitman v. American Trucking Ass'ns, Inc.</a></i> largely upheld EPA's actions.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Clean Air Act (CAA or Act) directs the <a href="/wiki/EPA" class="mw-redirect" title="EPA">EPA</a> to establish national ambient air quality standards (NAAQS) for pollutants at levels that will protect public health. The <a href="/wiki/EPA" class="mw-redirect" title="EPA">EPA</a> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Lung_Association" title="American Lung Association">American Lung Association</a> promoted the <a href="/wiki/2011_Cross_State_Air_Pollution_Rule" class="mw-redirect" title="2011 Cross State Air Pollution Rule">2011 Cross State Air Pollution Rule</a> (CSAPR) as a means of controlling ozone and fine particle emissions. It was claimed that this prevented up 400,000 asthma cases and saved two millions workdays and schooldays that otherwise would have been lost to respiratory illnesses. Cities and power companies sued the EPA over the law in EPA v. EME Homer City Generation, but the case was eventually decided in favor of the EPA.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Roles_of_the_federal_government_and_states">Roles of the federal government and states</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Roles of the federal government and states">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 1970 Clean Air Act required states to develop State Implementation Plans for how they would meet new national ambient air quality standards by 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Although the 1990 Clean Air Act is a federal law covering the entire country, the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">states</a> do much of the work to carry out the Act. The EPA has allowed the individual states to elect responsibility for compliance with and regulation of the CAA within their own borders in exchange for funding. For example, a state air pollution agency holds a hearing on a permit application by a <a href="/wiki/Power_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Power plant">power</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chemical_plant" title="Chemical plant">chemical plant</a> or fines a company for violating air pollution limits. However, election is not mandatory and in some cases states have chosen to not accept responsibility for enforcement of the act and force the EPA to assume those duties.
</p><p>In order to take over compliance with the CAA the states must write and submit a state implementation plan (<a href="/wiki/State_Implementation_Plan" title="State Implementation Plan">SIP</a>) to the EPA for approval. A state implementation plan is a collection of the <a href="/wiki/Regulations" class="mw-redirect" title="Regulations">regulations</a> a state will use to clean up polluted areas. The states are obligated to notify the public of these plans, through hearings that offer opportunities to comment, in the development of each state implementation plan. The SIP becomes the state's legal guide for local enforcement of the CAA. For example, <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a> law requires compliance with the Federal CAA through the SIP.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The SIP delegates permitting and enforcement responsibility to the state Department of Environmental Management (RI-DEM).
</p><p>The federal law recognizes that states should lead in carrying out the Clean Air Act, because <a href="/wiki/Pollution_control" class="mw-redirect" title="Pollution control">pollution control</a> problems often require special understanding of local industries, geography, housing patterns, etc. However, states are not allowed to have weaker <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a> controls than the national minimum criteria set by EPA. EPA must approve each SIP, and if a SIP isn't acceptable, EPA can take over CAA enforcement in that state. For example, California was unable to meet the new standards set by the Clean Air Act of 1970, which led to a lawsuit and a federal state implementation plan for the state.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The United States government, through the EPA, assists the states by providing scientific <a href="/wiki/Research" title="Research">research</a>, expert studies, <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a> designs, and money to support clean air programs.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_planning_organization" title="Metropolitan planning organization">Metropolitan planning organizations</a> must approve all federally funded transportation projects in a given urban area. If the MPO's plans do not, <a href="/wiki/Federal_Highway_Administration" title="Federal Highway Administration">Federal Highway Administration</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Transit_Administration" title="Federal Transit Administration">Federal Transit Administration</a> have the authority to withhold funds if the plans do not conform with federal requirements, including air quality standards.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> In 2010, the EPA directly fined the <a href="/wiki/San_Joaquin_Valley" title="San Joaquin Valley">San Joaquin Valley</a> Air Pollution Control District $29 million for failure to meet ozone standards, resulting in fees for county drivers and businesses. This was the results of a federal appeals court case that required the EPA to continue enforce older, stronger standards,<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> and spurred debate in Congress over amending the Act.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most notably, the law prevents states from setting standards that are more strict than the federal standards, but carves out a special exemption for California due to its past issues with smog pollution in the metropolitan areas. In practice, when California's environmental agencies decide on new vehicle emission standards, they submit these to the EPA for approval under this waiver, with the most recent approval in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> The California standard was adopted by twelve other states, and it based on the <i>de facto</i> standard that automobile manufactures have subsequently built towards, as to avoid having to develop multiple lines of emission systems in their vehicles for different states. However, in September 2019, President <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> revoked this waiver, arguing that the stricter emissions have made cars too expensive, and by removing them, will make vehicles safer. EPA's <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Wheeler" class="mw-disambig" title="Andrew Wheeler">Andrew Wheeler</a> also stated that while the agency respects federalism, they could not allow one state to dictate standards for the entire nation. California's governor <a href="/wiki/Gavin_Newsom" title="Gavin Newsom">Gavin Newsom</a> considered the move part of Trump's "political vendetta" against California and stated his intent to sue the federal government.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Twenty-three states, along with the District of Columbia and the cities of New York City and Los Angeles joined California in a federal lawsuit challenging the administration's decision.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Interstate_air_pollution">Interstate air pollution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Interstate air pollution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Air pollution often travels from its source in one state to another state. In many <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_areas" class="mw-redirect" title="Metropolitan areas">metropolitan areas</a>, people live in one state and work or shop in another; air pollution from cars and trucks may spread throughout the <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/interstate" class="extiw" title="wikt:interstate">interstate</a> area. The 1990 Clean Air Act provides for interstate commissions on air pollution control, which are to develop regional strategies for cleaning up air pollution. The 1990 amendments include other provisions to reduce interstate air pollution.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Acid_Rain_Program" title="Acid Rain Program">Acid Rain Program</a>, created under Title IV of the Act, authorizes <a href="/wiki/Emissions_trading" title="Emissions trading">emissions trading</a> to reduce the overall cost of controlling emissions of <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leak_detection_and_repair">Leak detection and repair</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Leak detection and repair">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Act requires industrial facilities to implement a Leak Detection and Repair (LDAR) program to monitor and audit a facility's fugitive emissions of <a href="/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound" title="Volatile organic compound">volatile organic compounds</a> (VOC). The program is intended to identify and repair components such as valves, pumps, compressors, <a href="/wiki/Flange" title="Flange">flanges</a>, connectors and other components that may be leaking. These components are the main source of the fugitive VOC emissions.
</p><p>Testing is done manually using a portable vapor analyzer that read in parts per million (ppm). Monitoring frequency, and the leak threshold, is determined by various factors such as the type of component being tested and the chemical running through the line. Moving components such as pumps and agitators are monitored more frequently than non-moving components such as flanges and screwed connectors. The regulations require that when a leak is detected the component be repaired within a set number of days. Most facilities get 5 days for an initial repair attempt with no more than 15 days for a complete repair. Allowances for delaying the repairs beyond the allowed time are made for some components where repairing the component requires shutting process equipment down.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Application_to_greenhouse_gas_emissions">Application to greenhouse gas emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Application to greenhouse gas emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Regulation_of_greenhouse_gases_under_the_Clean_Air_Act" title="Regulation of greenhouse gases under the Clean Air Act">Regulation of greenhouse gases under the Clean Air Act</a></div>

The EPA's auto emission standards for greenhouse gas emissions issued in 2010 and 2012 are intended to cut emissions from targeted vehicles by half, double fuel economy of passenger cars and light-duty trucks by 2025 and save over $4 billion barrels of oil and $1.7 trillion for consumers. The agency has also proposed a two-phase program to reduce greenhouse gas emissions for medium and heavy duty trucks and buses.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clean_Air_Act_and_environmental_justice">Clean Air Act and environmental justice</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Clean Air Act and environmental justice">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By promoting pollution reduction, the Clean Air Act can help reduce heightened exposure to air pollution among communities of color and low-income communities.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Environmental researcher Dr. Marie Lynn Miranda notes that African American populations are “consistently over represented” in areas with the poorest air quality.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Dense populations of low-income and minority communities inhabit the most polluted areas across the United States, which is considered to exacerbate health problems among these populations.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> High levels of exposure to air pollution is linked to several health conditions, including asthma, cancer, premature death, and infant mortality, each of which disproportionately impact communities of color and low-income communities.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> The pollution reduction achieved by the Clean Air Act is associated with a decline in each of these conditions and can promote environmental justice for communities that are disproportionately impacted by air pollution and diminished health status.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clean_Air_Act_violations">Clean Air Act violations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Clean Air Act violations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The EPA analyzes violators of the Clean Air Act and addresses the violators accordingly. For companies or parties that do not comply with the act monetary penalties can be cited. Per day the EPA could fine civil administrators $37,500 per day, with a maximum of about 8 days; unless otherwise mandated by the EPA. For a field citation which is against federal facilities which are not abiding by EPA standards can get fines up to $7,500 per day.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Major cases of Clean Air Act violations include:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_emissions_scandal" title="Volkswagen emissions scandal">Volkswagen emissions scandal</a> (2015), <a href="/wiki/Diesel_emissions_scandal" title="Diesel emissions scandal">etc.</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Caterpillar_Inc.#Clean_Air_Act_violation" title="Caterpillar Inc.">Caterpillar</a> and five other manufacturers violated diesel engine emission standards (consent decree, July 1999)<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Alleged violations by Hyundai and Kia which resulted in a total $100 million in civil penalties paid to the United States and to the California Air Resources Board.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">Effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A 2017 study found that the Clean Air Act of 1970 led to an over 10 percent reduction in pollution ("ambient TSP levels") in counties that exceeded the pollution thresholds set by the Act in the three years after the regulation went into effect. The study found that this regulation-induced reduction in air pollution has caused affected workers to work more and earned one percent more in annual earnings. The authors estimate that cumulative lifetime income gain for each affected individual is approximately $4,300 in present value terms.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition, because air quality across the United States improved; it is estimated 205,000 premature deaths and millions of other respiratory complications were prevented which resulted in an economic savings of $50 trillion versus the $523 billion invested to meet the Clean Air Act standard.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mobile sources including automobiles, trains, and boat engines have become 99% cleaner for pollutants like hydrocarbons, carbon monoxide, nitrogen oxides, and particle emissions since the 1970s. The allowable emissions of volatile organic chemicals, carbon monoxide, nitrogen oxides, and lead from individual cars have also been reduced by more than 90%, resulting in decreased national emissions of these pollutants despite a more than 400% increase in total miles driven yearly.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the 1980s, 1/4th of ground level ozone has been cut, mercury emissions have been cut by 80%, and since the change from leaded gas to unleaded gas 90% of atmospheric lead pollution has been reduced.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2018 study found that the Clean Air Act contributed to the 60% decline in pollution emissions by the manufacturing industry between 1990 and 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Future_challenges">Future challenges</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Future challenges">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Climate change poses a challenge to the management of conventional air pollutants in the United States due to warmer, dryer summer conditions that can lead to increased air stagnation episodes. Prolonged droughts that may contribute to wildfires would also result in regionally high levels of air particles.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2017, some US cities still don’t meet all national ambient air quality standards. It is likely that tens of thousands of premature deaths are still being caused by fine-particle pollution and ground-level ozone pollution.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Air pollution is not bound to a nation. Often, air coming into the U.S. contains pollution from upwind countries, making it harder to meet air quality standards. In turn, air that travels downwind of the U.S. likely has pollutants in it already. Addressing this may require international negotiations of reductions of pollutants in the originating countries.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This also relates to the challenge of climate change.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Diane Katz, a research fellow for the <a href="/wiki/Heritage_Foundation" class="mw-redirect" title="Heritage Foundation">Heritage Foundation</a>, criticized the validity of the Clean Air Act by stating, "The largest proportion of economic benefit is based on the value of avoiding premature mortality. Yet the valuation of this benefit ranks among the most significant uncertainties in the study, according to the researchers. Alternative estimates they cite would lower the benefit calculation by up to 22 percent."<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> The Clean Air Act is a constant battle between current economic benefits versus future cost benefits of both health of the nation, and economy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
