<p><b>Choate Rosemary Hall</b> (often known as <b>Choate</b>; <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/tʃ/: &#39;ch&#39; in &#39;China&#39;">tʃ</span><span title="/oʊ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;code&#39;">oʊ</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>) is a <a href="/wiki/Private_school" title="Private school">private</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mixed-sex_education" title="Mixed-sex education">co-educational</a>, <a href="/wiki/College-preparatory_school" title="College-preparatory school">college-preparatory</a> <a href="/wiki/Boarding_school" title="Boarding school">boarding school</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wallingford,_Connecticut" title="Wallingford, Connecticut">Wallingford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, United States. It took its present name and began a co-educational system with the merger in 1971 of two <a href="/wiki/Single-sex_education" title="Single-sex education">single-sex schools</a>; The Choate School (founded in 1896 in Wallingford) and <a href="/wiki/Rosemary_Hall_(Greenwich,_Connecticut)" title="Rosemary Hall (Greenwich, Connecticut)">Rosemary Hall</a> (founded in 1890 in Wallingford, moved later to <a href="/wiki/Greenwich,_Connecticut" title="Greenwich, Connecticut">Greenwich</a>). At the merger, the Wallingford campus was enlarged with a complex of modernist buildings on its eastern edge to accommodate the students from Rosemary Hall.
</p><p>The school has educated generations of the upper-class New England establishment and the American political elite, and it has introduced many programs to diversify the student population, including the introduction of a free education for families whose income is $75,000 or less. It is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Eight_Schools_Association" title="Eight Schools Association">Eight Schools Association</a>, begun informally in 1973–74 and formalized in 2006, when former Choate headmaster Edward Shanahan was appointed its first president.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It is also a member of the <a href="/wiki/Ten_Schools_Admissions_Organization" class="mw-redirect" title="Ten Schools Admissions Organization">Ten Schools Admissions Organization</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Choate_Rosemary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Choate_Rosemary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Squire_Stanley_House_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Squire_Stanley_House_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg/290px-Squire_Stanley_House_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg" decoding="async" width="290" height="218" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Squire_Stanley_House_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg/435px-Squire_Stanley_House_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Squire_Stanley_House_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg/580px-Squire_Stanley_House_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Squire_Stanley_House_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Squire Stanley House, built 1690–1750 and visited in 1775 by George Washington. The Choate School began here in 1896. It is now a third form (first-year) girls dormitory.</div></div></div>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><i>See also: <a href="/wiki/Rosemary_Hall_(Greenwich,_Connecticut)" title="Rosemary Hall (Greenwich, Connecticut)">Rosemary Hall</a> for its history before the 1971 re-affiliation with Choate.</i></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>The schools that would eventually become Choate Rosemary Hall were begun by members of two prominent New England families, the Choates and Atwaters.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Rosemary Hall was founded in 1890 by Mary Atwater Choate at Rosemary Farm in Wallingford, her girlhood home and the summer residence of Mary and her husband, <a href="/wiki/William_Gardner_Choate" title="William Gardner Choate">William Gardner Choate</a>. Mary, an alumna of <a href="/wiki/Miss_Porter%27s_School" title="Miss Porter&#39;s School">Miss Porter's School</a>, was the great-granddaughter of Caleb Atwater (1741–1832), a Connecticut merchant magnate who supplied the American forces during the Revolutionary War. In 1775 General <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> visited the Atwater store in Wallingford en route to assuming command of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a>. On that occasion, Washington took tea with Judge Oliver Stanley at the "Red House" (built 1690–1750), now Squire Stanley House on the Choate campus.
</p><p>In 1878 Mary Atwater Choate had co-founded a vocational organization for Civil War widows, the New York Exchange for Women's Work, prototype of many such exchanges across the country (it survived until 2003).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In 1889 Mary planned a new institution on the same principle of female self-sufficiency and she advertised in <i>The New York Times</i> for a headmistress to run a school that would train girls in the "domestic arts." The advertisement was answered by Caroline Ruutz-Rees (1865–1954), a 25-year-old Briton teaching in New Jersey.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On October 3, 1890, the <i>New Haven Morning News</i> reported: "The opening of Rosemary Hall took place at Wallingford yesterday ... at the beautiful Rosemary Farms, which have been the property of Mrs. Choate's family for five generations. ... Rev. <a href="/wiki/Edward_Everett_Hale" title="Edward Everett Hale">Edward Everett Hale</a> addressed the school girls in his inimitable way, at once attractive and helpful. 'Never forget,' said he, 'that it is a great art to do what you do well. If you limp, limp well, and if you dance, dance well'."
</p><p>The eight arriving girls occupied the original school building, "old Atwater House" (built 1758), located at the northwest corner of Christian and Elm streets, where new Atwater House now stands. They also had the use of "Atwater homestead" (built 1774, now known as Homestead), which stands at the center of the present day campus, on the northeast corner of Christian and Elm streets.
</p>

<p>Caroline Ruutz-Rees (pronounced "roots-reece," the first syllable rhyming with "foot"), headmistress of Rosemary Hall until 1938, was a figure of extraordinary personality and influence, a militant feminist and suffragist of national prominence. On the Wallingford golf course she wore bloomers, which shocked the locals, and on buggy rides to Wallingford station she carried a pistol.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> She quickly changed Rosemary Hall's mission from "domestic arts" to that of a contemporary boys school.
</p><p>The Choate School was founded by William and Mary Choate in 1896. William Gardner Choate (1830–1921), <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard</a> class of 1852, was U.S. District Judge for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_Southern_District_of_New_York" title="United States District Court for the Southern District of New York">Southern Circuit of New York</a> from 1878 to 1881, and afterward a partner of Shipman, Barlow, Laroque, and Choate. He was a national authority on admiralty, railroad, bankruptcy, and corporation law.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Like his younger, more famous brother, he was a prominent clubman (Harvard and The Century). That brother was <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Hodges_Choate" title="Joseph Hodges Choate">Joseph Hodges Choate</a>, lawyer, prosecutor of the <a href="/wiki/Tweed_Ring" class="mw-redirect" title="Tweed Ring">Tweed Ring</a>, and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Ambassador_to_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Ambassador to the United Kingdom">Ambassador to the Court of St. James's</a>.
</p><p>William and Mary Choate invited Mark Pitman (1830–1905), their tenant in the aforementioned Red House, to start a boys' school under their sponsorship. Pitman, <a href="/wiki/Bowdoin_College" title="Bowdoin College">Bowdoin</a> class of 1859, was 66 years old, a widower, and had been principal of Woolsey School in New Haven, Connecticut, since 1872.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He accepted the Choates' offer, not least perhaps because it would provide employment for his unmarried adult daughters, Leila, Elizabeth, and Helen. Six boys entered the new school in fall term 1896, their average age about ten. Four of the six lived in Red House with the Pitmans, and Red House (now Squire Stanley House) has remained a dormitory to the present day.
</p><p>Pitman taught Latin, English, history, and science; Elizabeth taught art, Helen piano, and Leila was writing teacher and school nurse. Mary Choate's physician brother, Huntington Atwater, taught crafts and was school doctor. There was no formal relationship with the Choates' other foundation, Rosemary Hall, a hundred yards to the east on Christian Street, but there were coeducational audiences for plays and recitals and Mary Choate hosted dances at the Homestead.
</p><p>In 1897 the boys school erected Choate House across the street from Red House, the first purpose-built institutional building (and John F. Kennedy's dormitory in 1931-2). It contained recitation rooms, an infirmary, a dining room, and housing for fifteen boys. In 1899 Choate House was venue for the first "Junior Dance", but a year later the Rosemary girls would depart for a seventy-one year absence.
</p>

<p>The official history of Choate Rosemary Hall, written by Tom Generous, says that the rift between Caroline Ruutz-Rees and Mary Choate, proponents of two very different sorts of feminism, was public knowledge as early as 1896. In that year the two women did not share the lectern at Prize Day, and local newspapers published "denials" of a rumor that Ruutz-Rees would leave the school.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> But by 1900 the headmistress and her educational style had acquired influential champions among the students' parents and two of them, residents of Greenwich, Connecticut, joined forces to effect the removal of the school to their town.
</p><p>Shipping magnate Nathaniel Witherell donated 5 acres (20,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) of land in the Rock Ridge section of Greenwich. Julian Curtiss gathered a group of investors and established a joint stock corporation funded through the sale of six-percent bonds. Ruutz-Rees was the chief shareholder. The Greenwich residence of Rosemary Hall began in fall term 1900, when 57 girl students moved into the Main Building, known as "The School", a U-shaped shingled house on Zaccheus Mead Lane.
</p><p>In Wallingford, Mark Pitman died on December 3, 1905. Until 1908 Sumner Blakemore was titular headmaster, but the school was effectively the domain of the three Pitman sisters. At the 1908 graduation ceremony the Japanese Consul General watched his countryman Noyobu Masuda give the valedictory address. Then Judge Choate introduced the man who would assume the headmastership in the fall, George St. John, and his wife, Clara Seymour St. John. She was a <a href="/wiki/Bryn_Mawr_College" title="Bryn Mawr College">Bryn Mawr</a> alumna, member of a well-connected Connecticut family, sister of future (1937–1951) Yale president <a href="/wiki/Charles_Seymour" title="Charles Seymour">Charles Seymour</a>, and descendant of Yale president (1740–1766) <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Clap" title="Thomas Clap">Thomas Clap</a>.
</p><p>George Clair St. John (1877–1966), Harvard class of 1902, aged 31 in the fall of 1908, had grown up on a farm in Hoskins Station, Connecticut. He was an ordained Episcopal priest. He had taught at <a href="/wiki/The_Hill_School_(Pennsylvania)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Hill School (Pennsylvania)">The Hill School</a> in Pennsylvania and <a href="/wiki/Ransom_Everglades_School" title="Ransom Everglades School">Adirondack-Florida School</a>, and was teaching at <a href="/wiki/Hackley_School" title="Hackley School">Hackley School</a> in Tarrytown, N.Y., when Samuel Dutton of <a href="/wiki/Teachers_College,_Columbia_University" title="Teachers College, Columbia University">Columbia Teachers College</a> recruited him for the headmastership. St. John "knew, long before I read Mr. Dutton's letter, that I wanted some day to have a school of my own. In my thought about it, Dean Briggs was my first text."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> This was <a href="/wiki/LeBaron_Russell_Briggs" title="LeBaron Russell Briggs">LeBaron Russell Briggs</a>, dean of men at Harvard when St. John was there and afterward dean of the faculty until 1925. Briggs, St. John wrote, "fathered the whole college", and the St. Johns too would serve <i>in loco parentis</i>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dining_hall_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Dining_hall_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.JPG/320px-Dining_hall_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.JPG" decoding="async" width="320" height="240" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Dining_hall_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.JPG/480px-Dining_hall_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Dining_hall_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.JPG/640px-Dining_hall_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dining_hall_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The dining hall in Hill House west wing</div></div></div>
<p>Their first move to secure parental powers was decisive. In September 1909, as the official history tells it, the St. Johns "signed with the Choates an 'Agreement to Lease and Purchase.' Under its terms, the younger couple rented the school, its property and reputation, for five years at a sum equal to 11 percent of The Choate School's net income per year. ... Less than twenty months later, on May 12, 1911, St. John reported to the trustees that he had purchased the title to the school by acquiring mortgages of roughly $41,000. He resold the title in turn to The Choate School, Incorporated, for $23,000 cash and $38,000 in stock ... For all intents and purposes, The Choate School belonged to George St. John."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In his first quarter-century as headmaster, St. John created much of Choate as it is regarded today. Of the Georgian brick and stone campus, he had built by 1932 Hill House, West Wing, the Gymnasium, Memorial House, the Chapel, the Library, the Winter Exercise Building, and Archbold Infirmary, which was the largest school infirmary in the country. He grew the enrollment from 35 to 505 boys and the faculty from 5 to 64 masters. In the decade following the First World War (classes of 1918 to 1928) Choate sent 412 of its 618 graduates to Yale, Princeton, and Harvard, according to a table published in <i>The Choate News</i> in fall term 1928.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>George St. John belonged to the generation of legendary, long-serving headmasters who shaped the New England prep school, chief among whom were <a href="/wiki/Endicott_Peabody" title="Endicott Peabody">Endicott Peabody</a> of Groton, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Boyden" title="Frank Boyden">Frank Boyden</a> of Deerfield, <a href="/wiki/Horace_Dutton_Taft" title="Horace Dutton Taft">Horace Dutton Taft</a> of Taft, Frederick Sill of Kent, Samuel Drury of St. Paul's, Alfred Stearns of Andover, <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Perry" title="Lewis Perry">Lewis Perry</a> of Exeter, and George Van Santvoord of Hotchkiss.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Rev. George St. John of Choate was succeeded in 1947 by his son, the Rev. Seymour St. John '31 (1912–2006), and the "St. John dynasty" was continued to 1973. Seymour was Yale class of 1935 and was ordained after graduating from <a href="/wiki/Virginia_Theological_Seminary" title="Virginia Theological Seminary">Virginia Theological Seminary</a> in 1942. During his time as head he built as many buildings as his father had built, greatly broadened the curriculum, raised the national profile of the school, and made it more progressive (<a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Roosevelt" title="Eleanor Roosevelt">Eleanor Roosevelt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norman_Thomas" title="Norman Thomas">Norman Thomas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/William_Sloane_Coffin" title="William Sloane Coffin">William Sloane Coffin</a> were regular speakers) and cosmopolitan (Russian, Near Eastern, and Afro-American studies centers were founded, and Russian, Chinese, and Arabic courses were begun). St. John was a longtime advocate of coeducation and initiated the Choate-Rosemary contacts. At his death, headmaster Edward Shanahan told the <i>New York Times</i>, "The merger demanded an enormous expenditure of resources by Choate because the building of a new campus was to occur within the footprints of its property. Seymour was central to the decision to expand those resources."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="JFK.2C_the_Muckers.2C_and_.22Ask_not.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="JFK,_the_Muckers,_and_&quot;Ask_not&quot;">JFK, the Muckers, and "Ask not"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Choate_Rosemary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: JFK, the Muckers, and &quot;Ask not&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1931 <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> entered Choate as a third form (9th grade) student, following his older brother <a href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Kennedy,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph P. Kennedy, Jr.">Joe Jr</a>, who was a star athlete at the school. Jack Kennedy—sickly, underweight, and nicknamed Rat Face by his schoolfellows—spent his first two years at Choate in his brother's shadow, and compensated for it with rebellious behavior that attracted a coterie. He named his group The Muckers Club, which had thirteen members—Kennedy and twelve disciples. (Among these was Kennedy's lifelong inseparable friend <a href="/wiki/Lem_Billings" title="Lem Billings">Kirk LeMoyne "Lem" Billings</a>, who kept an apartment in the White House during JFK's presidency.)<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As Headmaster of Choate, George St. John's chapel talks included a recurring phrase that was widely remembered by Choate alumni. In 2008, school archivist Judy Donald found the phrase in St. John's notebooks, and in 2011 journalist <a href="/wiki/Chris_Matthews" title="Chris Matthews">Chris Matthews</a> published the discovery in his biography of JFK. St. John's phrase (borrowed from Harvard dean <a href="/wiki/LeBaron_Russell_Briggs" title="LeBaron Russell Briggs">LeBaron Briggs</a>) was, "The youth who loves his alma mater will always ask not 'What can she do for me?' but 'What can I do for her?'" Speculation that this was the original of JFK's inaugural address "Ask not" goes back at least as far as a 1966 <i>Time</i> magazine article, and several of JFK's Choate contemporaries noticed the echo in 1961.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy graduated from Choate in 1935. In senior class polling for the yearbook (of which he was business manager), Kennedy was voted Most Likely to Succeed.
</p>

Fall term, first interscholastic football season, record of 2–1.</li>

"Admiral" Austin Meeks '16 arrives to coach crew to regional dominance in the 1920s and 1930s; by 1933 Choate rows eight shells.</li>

<li>1923: 322 boys, 40 masters. Brown-Massie House bought.</li>
<li>1924: 339 boys, 43 masters. Creation of "The Choate School Chapel Foundation"; it attracts 624 donors. Completion of the Chapel; designed by <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Adams_Cram" title="Ralph Adams Cram">Ralph Adams Cram</a> and constructed by Choate staff; Andrew Mellon's donation was $25,000. Choate Farm is bought and the school dairy built.</li>
<li>1925: <a href="/wiki/Paul_Mellon" title="Paul Mellon">Paul Mellon</a> graduates. Woodhouse is bought. 361 boys, 45 masters.</li>
<li>1926: Spring term, Andrew Mellon Library is completed; Mellon gave $150,000 of its $200,000 cost. Fall term, 401 boys, 50 masters.</li>
<li>1927: 426 boys, 57 masters. The Deaconage bought.</li>
<li>1928: 452 boys, 57 masters. John Ed Wilfong starts the nursery. Completion of John D. Archbold Infirmary.</li>
<li>1929: <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fitzgerald" title="Robert Fitzgerald">Robert Fitzgerald</a> graduates. Fall term, 497 boys, 62 masters. The Hall is built. Foundation name changed to "The Choate School Chapel and Library Foundation"; it owns the Chapel, Library, Memorial House, Archbold, and the Hall.</li>
<li>1930: <a href="/wiki/Alan_Lomax" title="Alan Lomax">Alan Lomax</a> graduates. Mahlon Thatcher Track and Fields built, the gift of Mr. and Mrs. Mahlon Thatcher in memory of their son Mahlon Jr, a fourth former who had died in a riding accident. Fall term, 509 boys, 64 masters. Soccer team begins. First appearance of the band at a football game.</li>
<li>1931: 505 boys, 64 masters. Munson House bought. Baseball begins a six-season record of 57–6.</li>
<li>1932: January, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph P. Kennedy">Joseph P. Kennedy</a> P'35 arranges the first showing of a movie. Winter Exercise Building (now Johnson Athletic Center) is built entirely by Choate staff and cost $275,000, one-third of which was given by Mr. and Mrs. Mahlon Thatcher; the Winter Ex was the final project of building supervisor Henry Raymond Stone, who died in 1931. The Headmaster, an insatiable Savoyard, commands the first of yearly <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_and_Sullivan" title="Gilbert and Sullivan">Gilbert and Sullivan</a> productions (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Mikado" title="The Mikado">The Mikado</a></i>) that continue to his retirement year 1947 (<i><a href="/wiki/Patience_(opera)" title="Patience (opera)">Patience</a></i>).</li>
<li>1933: 475 boys, 59 masters. Homestead and Red Cottage bought, Homestead from Hunt Atwater '03, nephew of Mary Atwater Choate.</li>
<li>1934: 459 boys, 58 masters. Edsall House bought.</li>
<li>1935: <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> graduates. 466 boys, 57 masters. 1690 House bought. January 12–13, <a href="/wiki/Gertrude_Stein" title="Gertrude Stein">Gertrude Stein</a> visits campus and her lecture is published in <i>The Choate Literary Magazine</i>.</li>
<li>1936: <a href="/wiki/Alan_Jay_Lerner" title="Alan Jay Lerner">Alan Jay Lerner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Avery_Dulles" title="Avery Dulles">Avery Dulles</a> graduate. Fall term, 490 boys, 62 masters. Chapel House and New Old South are bought, the gift of Clinton P. Knight, Jr. <a href="/wiki/Dudley_Fitts" title="Dudley Fitts">Dudley Fitts</a> and his former student <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fitzgerald" title="Robert Fitzgerald">Robert Fitzgerald</a> '29 publish translations of <i>Alcestis of Euripides</i> (1936) and <i>Antigone of Sophocles</i> (1939).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>1937: 501 boys, 64 masters. Alumni Boathouse built; 400 donors. The Choate School, Incorporated, transfers its property to the Foundation. Combination House is created from the joining of the relocated Brown and Middle cottages.</li></ul>

<ul><li>1938: Paul Mellon Science Hall (now Mellon Humanities Center) is built, the gift of <a href="/wiki/Paul_Mellon" title="Paul Mellon">Paul Mellon</a> '25. Red House (now Squire Stanley) bought from the Atwater family. "The Golden Blues" swing band begins and will last 30 years.</li>
<li>1939: April, first issue of <i>The Choate Alumni Bulletin</i> is published; Dan D. Coyle '34 is editor.</li>
<li>1940: Fathers Association begins.</li>
<li>1941: Mothers Association begins.</li>
<li>1943: Alumni Fund council begins. March, <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Boston_Braves" title="History of the Boston Braves">Boston Braves</a> move into campus residence to use the Winter Ex for spring training; they return in March 1944.</li>
<li>1944: Fiftieth Anniversary Campaign for endowment begins.</li>
<li>1945: The Maiyeros <i>a cappella</i> group begun by music master Duncan Phyfe '38.</li>
<li>1946: <a href="/wiki/Edward_Albee" title="Edward Albee">Edward Albee</a> graduates; his first published play, <i>Schism</i>, appears in the Commencement issue of <i>The Choate Literary Magazine</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Dedication of Wilken Field in memory of Ray Theodore "Ted" Wilken '40. September 27–29, celebration of Choate's Fiftieth Anniversary, among the speakers were <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> '35, the presidents of Yale (St. John's brother-in-law), Princeton, and Williams, and the heads of Andover, Deerfield, Groton, St. Paul's, Hotchkiss, Lawrenceville, Taft, Loomis, Westminster, and Milton.</li>
<li>1947: 533 boys. Trustees elect Seymour St. John '31 headmaster; he is installed June 11 and begins his 26-year tenure. Richard R. Higgins succeeds George St. John as president of the Foundation. Logan Munroe House is built, the gift of Charles A. Munroe P'33 in memory of his son.</li>
<li>1948: 550 boys. Nichols House is built, the gift of Charles Walter Nichols, Jr. '29, and named in memory of his father, trustee 1947–57.</li>
<li>1950: First Project Day, brought on by a storm that downed trees; now called Community Day.</li>
<li>1952: <a href="/wiki/Adlai_Stevenson_II" title="Adlai Stevenson II">Adlai Stevenson</a> '18 is the Democratic Presidential nominee, and again in 1956.</li>
<li>1953: December 5, dedication of the outdoor Courtenay Hemenway Rink.</li>
<li>1957: Wheeler House is bought; named for Frank Wheeler, teacher and director of studies 1916–52. Fall term, Johannes van Straalen begins teaching Russian a month before the launch of <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a>. October, dedication of George and Clara St. John Hall; it cost $562,000.</li>
<li>1959: <i>Forty Years at School</i> by headmaster emeritus George St. John is published by Henry Holt and Company.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li>1960: April 17, the History Club is addressed by <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Peace_Prize" title="Nobel Peace Prize">Nobel Peace Prize</a> laureate <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Bunche" title="Ralph Bunche">Ralph Bunche</a>, whose son Ralph Jr. '61 is the school's first African-American student. Pitman, Atwater, and Mead houses are built, the latter named for George Jackson Mead, class of 1911, trustee 1947–57. In November, <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John Kennedy</a> '35 is elected President; in a straw poll only 18 percent of students vote for him.</li>
<li>1961: January 20, Kennedy takes office; his inaugural address "Ask not" phrase is sometimes said to derive from George St. John's "Ask not what your school can do for you; ask what you can do for your school."<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>1962: May 5, dedication of new wing of Library, with <a href="/wiki/Robert_Frost" title="Robert Frost">Robert Frost</a> reading his Kennedy inaugural poem, "The Gift Outright." Spencer House (originally '62 House) is built; in 1982 it will be renamed in memory of James Spencer, chemistry and physics teacher 1962–82.</li>
<li>1963: <a href="/wiki/Michael_Douglas" title="Michael Douglas">Michael Douglas</a> graduates. Quantrell House is built; named in memory of Ernest E. Quantrell P'42, trustee 1942–61.</li>
<li>1964: In the summer, Alberto "Tico" Carrero '66 wins the U.S. under-16 tennis championship; his varsity record in three years is 36–0.</li>
<li>1965: <a href="/wiki/John_Dos_Passos" title="John Dos Passos">John Dos Passos</a> '11 attends 50th anniversary celebration of <i>The Lit</i>. Fall term, sixth formers in the history class of Jack McCune (future headmaster of St. Alban's) are the guests of Secretary of State Dean Rusk in his office; they challenge him on the Vietnam War. McCook House is completed, named for Anson T. McCook, trustee 1911–62; the dormitory and its twin, Clinton Knight House, were designed by architect Frank Winder '39.</li>
<li>1966: January, headmaster emeritus George St. John dies, aged 88. Spring term, Ralph Metcalfe, Jr. '66, son of the Olympic gold-medalist and Illinois Congressman, is the top high school hammer thrower in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Clinton Knight House is completed, named for Clinton P. Knight Jr. '12, trustee 1938–56. Fall term, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Mellon" title="Paul Mellon">Paul Mellon</a> '25 offers $1.5 million to build a facility for theater, music, and visual arts; his vision would be realized for $5 million in 1972.</li>
<li>1967: Winter term, dedication of Remsen Arena, the gift of the family of William C. S. Remsen '39. Varsity hockey is 17–0. Steele Hall is built, named for George Steele. The Chapel is enlarged by extension to the south. The Hall is enlarged by widening to the south. Baseball, coached by Tom Yankus '52, begins an eight-season league record of 54–2. Afro-American Union is formed. Fall term, in Greenwich, Connecticut, Rosemary Hall (RH) headmistress Alice McBee urges trustees of that school to consider "official affiliation" with a boys school, in light of declining enrollment and financial difficulty.</li></ul>

<ul><li>1968: In January, <a href="/wiki/Duke_Ellington" title="Duke Ellington">Duke Ellington</a> Orchestra plays in the Chapel. January 8, headmaster St. John hosts a private meeting in Wallingford with RH Board chair Charles Stetson, and the feasibility of relocating RH is discussed. January 26, Choate trustees vote to allow continuing discussions with RH. June, the CS and RH boards make a formal agreement, with the understanding that RH would relocate. September 24, St. John and McBee hold a press conference in St. John Hall and issue a press release dated September 26 which states that "a brand new school would be built for Rosemary Hall on Choate Land, and the combined institutions would provide 'coordinate' secondary education starting in September 1971."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>1969: Spring term, in response to student unrest St. John appoints English master Malcolm Manson '57 to run "Metanoia", a six-week lecture program that replaces Thursday classes; <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Mead" title="Margaret Mead">Margaret Mead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alvin_Toffler" title="Alvin Toffler">Alvin Toffler</a> are the first two speakers. First African-American master, Charles O. Todman Jr., is appointed to teach history and start the Afro-American Studies Center. Dress code no longer requires jacket and tie in the classroom. In November St. John allows about 50 students to attend the peace march in Washington, D.C.</li>
<li>1969: January, architect <a href="/wiki/James_Polshek" title="James Polshek">James Polshek</a> is selected to design the Wallingford RH campus; his budget will eventually be $6.4 million. On Prize Day, Seymour St. John is the graduation speaker in Greenwich.</li>
<li>1970: Spring term, 20 RH sixth formers have an advance guard residence on the CS campus; they live in Nichols House; Meg Colgate '70 is made interim editor of <i>The Choate News</i>. The book <i>A World of Our Own</i> by Peter Prescott '53, son of Orville Prescott of the <i>New York Times</i>, examines the political turmoil in the school year 1967–68.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Fall term, chapel attendance is no longer compulsory. Zurn Organ is installed in the Chapel, given in memory of John Henry Zurn '43.</li>
<li>1971: 568 boys. Elizabeth Winslow Loomis begins two years as RH headmistress; she had been head of upper school at The Lenox School (now <a href="/wiki/Birch_Wathen_Lenox_School" title="Birch Wathen Lenox School">Birch Wathen Lenox School</a>) in Manhattan and a former RH teacher and dean. June 3, the last Prize Day in Greenwich, Choate head Seymour St. John is the graduation speaker. The Greenwich campus is bought by <a href="/wiki/Daycroft_School" title="Daycroft School">Daycroft School</a>, which moves from its own Greenwich campus on Rock Ridge Road. August, the last two Wallingford campus buildings, Bronfman Library and Macquire Gymnasium, are completed. The school and 223 girls relocate.</li>
<li>1972: May 12–14, dedication of Paul Mellon Arts Center ("PMAC"), the gift entire of Paul Mellon '25; it cost more than $5 million; three-day celebration includes May 12 dedication speech by Edward Albee '46, a production of his <i><a href="/wiki/Zoo_Story" class="mw-redirect" title="Zoo Story">Zoo Story</a></i>, performances by the Boston Pops and Victor Borge, and official May 13 dedication of the RH campus. Fall term, all-school sit-down meals end.</li></ul>

<ul><li>1973: Spring term, RH yearbook <i>The Answer Book</i> is merged into <i>The Brief</i>. July 1, Seymour St. John resigns as headmaster. Wallingford Symphony Orchestra is formed by Philip Ventre, with the PMAC as its home. Lewis House is bought, named for Robert E. Lewis, teacher 1917–47. Fall term, CS and RH adopt uniform grading system. October 4, Charles F. Dey is installed as first "president and principal of The Choate School and Rosemary Hall." Dey was formerly an associate dean at <a href="/wiki/Dartmouth_College" title="Dartmouth College">Dartmouth</a> and had taught history at <a href="/wiki/Phillips_Academy" title="Phillips Academy">Andover</a>. He appoints RH academic dean and classics teacher Joanne Sullivan as dean and head of RH, and former <a href="/wiki/St._Paul%27s_School_(Concord,_New_Hampshire)" title="St. Paul&#39;s School (Concord, New Hampshire)">St. Paul's School</a> teacher Richard Aiken as dean and head of CS.</li>
<li>1974: June, the two boards of trustees are merged, with Elizabeth Hyde Brownell '21 as chair. RH newspaper <i>The Wild Boar</i> is merged into <i>The Choate News</i>. The students Creative Arts Committee is formed; an arts credit is added to graduation requirements.</li>
<li>1976: <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Lee_Curtis" title="Jamie Lee Curtis">Jamie Lee Curtis</a> graduates. February 18, fire damages the north section of the Winter Ex. December 1, rebuilt Winter Ex opens and is named for Worthington Johnson '32, who led fundraising that would secure $4 million in two years. Winter term, squash and riflery become coed sports.</li>
<li>1977: May, first joint Prize Day. Fall term, first joint registration, orientation, and Convocation. CS Student Council and RH Committee unofficially merge. Football, coached by Doug James, begins a five-season record of 32–7–1. Bobette Reed is first African-American admissions officer. <a href="/wiki/Adlai_Stevenson_II" title="Adlai Stevenson II">Adlai E. Stevenson</a> '18 Lecture Series is inaugurated by <a href="/wiki/Allard_K._Lowenstein" title="Allard K. Lowenstein">Allard Lowenstein</a>.</li>
<li>1978: May 28, the first joint graduation on Archbold Lawn, with single diploma of "Choate Rosemary Hall." Fall term, football beats Deerfield 9–3 on a 99-yard, two-play drive, and Bob Galvin's "Miracle Catch" with no time left on the clock.</li>
<li>1979: March, John Joseph Activities Center opens in the made-over Gymnasium, the gift of F. Morgan and Barbara Olin Taylor. Headmistress emeritus Eugenia Baker Jessup dies. Alumni Weekend, Larry Hart Pool opens as a coed facility, the gift of Larry Hart '32. November 1–4, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Crucible" title="The Crucible">The Crucible</a></i> is performed and its author <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Miller" title="Arthur Miller">Arthur Miller</a> lectures on <a href="/wiki/McCarthyism" title="McCarthyism">McCarthyism</a>.</li>
<li>1980: Spring term, the Last Hurrah begins with the merger of Festivities and The Mid. The cross is removed from the Chapel.</li>
<li>1981: Official merger of the student governments as CRH Student Council. Spring term, Joanne Sullivan heads an <i>ad hoc</i> committee that recommends increased emphasis on lab sciences and computer science, and curriculum mandates that might include economics, psychology, philosophy, religious studies, and the arts.</li>
<li>1982: 76 students of color, more than twice as many as the year before. Faculty approves new curriculum based on 1981 Sullivan committee recommendations. Richardson House is bought; given its present name in 1998 for Elfrida Richardson, RH organist and choirmistress 1916–59.</li></ul>

<li>1986: In the Chapel "The Creation" altar tapestry is hung, the gift of RH class of 1921, woven by Sylvia Heyden.</li>
<li>1987: Squire Stanley is moved 300 feet (91&#160;m) back from Christian Street to help reveal the west facade of Mellon Science Hall (now Mellon Humanities Center).</li>
<li>1988: January, agreement signed with Russia's Moscow School #18 for an annual four-week exchange of five students and one leader; Russian group arrives in April, Choate group to Moscow in September. Pierce House is named for Charles Pierce, admissions director and teacher 1945–77. Walsh House is named for Donald D. Walsh, teacher 1928–59.</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sexual_abuse_scandal">Sexual abuse scandal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Choate_Rosemary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sexual abuse scandal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2017, it acknowledged a decades-long sex abuse scandal, with multiple victims being subjected to abuse from faculty members.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ChoateBoardOfTrustees-KestenbaumReport-2017_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ChoateBoardOfTrustees-KestenbaumReport-2017-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academics">Academics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Choate_Rosemary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Academics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Choate's curriculum includes elective and interdisciplinary courses, from astronomy and architecture to printmaking and post-modernism to digital video and development economics.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> There are more than 300 courses in the curriculum, which has requirements in community service and in contemporary global studies. All disciplines have honors courses. As of 2017, Choate no longer offers AP (Advanced Placement) courses.
</p><p>A two-year intensive Science Research Program includes mentored laboratory work during the summer at universities in the United States and abroad. The Capstone Program allows sixth form (senior) students to explore an area of the curriculum in depth. Working under a faculty adviser, students take at least five courses that focus on a curricular theme, culminating in a substantial final project. During the college application process, Choate's College Counseling Office highlights Capstone Project participation in letters of recommendation.
</p><p>The performing and visual arts are supported by the resources of the Paul Mellon Arts Center. Among extracurricular arts clubs are five <i><a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">a cappella</a></i> groups; <a href="/wiki/Step_dance" title="Step dance">step dance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slam_poetry" class="mw-redirect" title="Slam poetry">slam poetry</a>, hip hop, and rap groups; improv, musical theater, and instrumental ensembles of all sizes; photography and film-making clubs; and supporting publications for the arts, fashion, and culture. The Arts Concentration Program provides students with individually tailored instruction and class scheduling.
</p><p>The Senior Project Program provides on- or off-campus internships in academic research, visual art, and the performing arts. Other specialized programs include American Studies, creative writing, economics, FBLA, mathematics, philosophy, psychology, religion, debate, and the Fed Challenge. The 2011–12 academic year saw the introduction of an Arabic and Middle Eastern Studies Program (AMES).<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Choate's Office of Global Studies supports study-abroad and other international initiatives. One-third of Choate students participate in programs in China, France, Japan, Spain, and Jordan.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Paul_Mellon_Humanities_Center_2_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Paul_Mellon_Humanities_Center_2_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg/290px-Paul_Mellon_Humanities_Center_2_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg" decoding="async" width="290" height="218" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Paul_Mellon_Humanities_Center_2_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg/435px-Paul_Mellon_Humanities_Center_2_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Paul_Mellon_Humanities_Center_2_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg/580px-Paul_Mellon_Humanities_Center_2_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Paul_Mellon_Humanities_Center_2_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Paul Mellon Humanities Center, built 1938, designed by Charles Fuller</div></div></div>
<p>The Kohler Environmental Center, designed by <a href="/wiki/Robert_A.M._Stern" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert A.M. Stern">Robert A.M. Stern Architects</a>, opened in 2012 and is located on a 268-acre site in the northeast quadrant of the campus. It has been described as "the first teaching, research and residential environmental center in U.S. secondary education."<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> It can house up to 20 students and two faculty members and supports the curricular Environmental Immersion Program.
</p><p>In February 2015 the school opened the Lanphier Center for Mathematics and Computer Science, a $17 million, 35,000-square foot campus hub for information technology, applied mathematics, and robotics. The center, designed by architect <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Pelli" title="César Pelli">César Pelli</a>'s firm <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Pelli" title="César Pelli">Pelli Clarke Pelli</a>, contains laboratories, classrooms, a lecture hall, and common spaces. It composes a U-shaped courtyard centering on a giant weeping beech tree, and is linked by a footbridge spanning Science Center Pond to the Icahn Center for Science.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Choate's <a href="/wiki/National_Fed_Challenge" title="National Fed Challenge">Fed Challenge</a> team was the 2009 national champion and has won the New England District Championship in 12 of the past 13 years.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (May 2015)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In the 2012 <a href="/wiki/American_Mathematics_Competitions" title="American Mathematics Competitions">American Mathematics Competitions</a> (AMC 12), Choate's team finished first in the nation, with the highest combined score of all 2600 participating schools.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Choate chamber orchestra performed at the White House in December 2009 and the school's symphony orchestra toured Europe in 2010 and 2011, giving concerts in ten countries. The festival and chamber choruses performed at St. Patrick's Day mass at <a href="/wiki/St._Peter%27s_Basilica" title="St. Peter&#39;s Basilica">St. Peter's Basilica</a> in Rome in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The Maiyeros, an <i><a href="/wiki/A_cappella" title="A cappella">a cappella</a></i> group, performed at <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a> in 2008. Choate orchestras and choral groups toured East Asia in 2000, 2005, 2007, and 2014. The June 2014 tour comprised concerts in Seoul, Hong Kong, and Macau, at the Great Wall at <a href="/wiki/Juyong_Pass" title="Juyong Pass">Ju Yong Guan</a>, and in ensemble with the Concert Band of <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_Palace_(China)" title="Children&#39;s Palace (China)">Beijing Children's Palace</a>. Choate orchestras have also performed at <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Center_for_the_Performing_Arts" title="Lincoln Center for the Performing Arts">Lincoln Center</a> in New York, <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Hall" title="Carnegie Hall">Carnegie Hall</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_R._Guggenheim_Museum" title="Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum">Guggenheim Museum</a>. The school's student-operated radio station, <a href="/wiki/WWEB" title="WWEB">WWEB</a>, is FCC-licensed and has been broadcasting since 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012 Choate became the first among its peer preparatory schools to require that all faculty and students own an iPad. The fall term that year saw the beginning of full integration of the tablet's capabilities into the syllabus. Choate's director of academic technology discussed Choate's iPad program in an August 2012 article in <i>US News</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Statistical_profile">Statistical profile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Choate_Rosemary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Statistical profile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Seymour_St._John_Chapel_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Seymour_St._John_Chapel_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg/220px-Seymour_St._John_Chapel_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Seymour_St._John_Chapel_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg/330px-Seymour_St._John_Chapel_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Seymour_St._John_Chapel_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg/440px-Seymour_St._John_Chapel_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2448" data-file-height="3264" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Seymour_St._John_Chapel_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Seymour St. John Chapel, built 1924, designed by Ralph Adams Cram</div></div></div>
<p>Choate enrolls 846 students, has 120.4 <a href="/wiki/Full-time_equivalent" title="Full-time equivalent">full-time equivalent</a> teaching staff, and a student-teacher ratio of 7.0 for the 2015-2016 school year.<sup id="cite_ref-NCES_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NCES-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Financial aid totaling more than $10 million was awarded to 32 percent of the student body, the average award being $38,000 for boarders and $26,000 for day students.<sup id="cite_ref-choate.edu_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-choate.edu-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> A variety of named scholarship endowments support students of various profiles, including the Paul Mellon scholarships (science and math), the Gakio-Walton (Africa, the Middle East, India), the Glendorn (southwestern U.S. states), the Wallingford (local residence), and many others.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the fiscal year ending June 2014 Choate's endowment was $356.7 million. Among member institutions of the <a href="/wiki/Eight_Schools_Association" title="Eight Schools Association">Eight Schools Association</a> Choate led in gifts from parents, with $2.1 million. Total gifts received in that fiscal year were $28.9 million.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> In November 2006 the school inaugurated a capital campaign with a target of $200 million and by the campaign's close in 2011 gifts and pledges of $220 million had been secured.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Protestant, Catholic, Jewish, and Muslim chaplains serve Choate's campus ministry. Services include Christian fellowship, Protestant evening services, Roman Catholic Mass, Buddhist meditation, Hillel, Spiritual Alternatives, Reflections Program, and other student worship groups.<sup id="cite_ref-choate.edu_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-choate.edu-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The school fields 81 interscholastic athletic teams in 32 sports. Choate's historical archrival in athletic competition is <a href="/wiki/Deerfield_Academy" title="Deerfield Academy">Deerfield Academy</a>. The final weekend of the fall season is Deerfield Day (at Deerfield it's called Choate Day), when the two schools compete in every sport.
</p><p>On July 1, 2011, Dr. Alex Curtis succeeded Edward Shanahan as headmaster of Choate. Shanahan had served as head for twenty years from 1991, when he arrived from <a href="/wiki/Dartmouth_College" title="Dartmouth College">Dartmouth College</a>. Curtis was selected by unanimous vote of the Choate board on November 4, 2010. Before Choate, Curtis was headmaster of <a href="/wiki/Morristown-Beard_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Morristown-Beard School">Morristown-Beard School</a> in New Jersey for seven years.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings_and_facilities">Buildings and facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Choate_Rosemary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Buildings and facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:422px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Memorial_House_(dorm)_1_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Memorial_House_%28dorm%29_1_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg/420px-Memorial_House_%28dorm%29_1_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg" decoding="async" width="420" height="315" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Memorial_House_%28dorm%29_1_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg/630px-Memorial_House_%28dorm%29_1_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Memorial_House_%28dorm%29_1_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg/840px-Memorial_House_%28dorm%29_1_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Memorial_House_(dorm)_1_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Georgian ensemble on the west campus: Andrew Mellon Library, George Steele Hall, Paul Mellon Humanities Center, and Memorial House</div></div></div>
<p>The 458-acre (1.85&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) campus contains 121 buildings in a variety of architectural styles. <a href="/wiki/Georgian_Revival" class="mw-redirect" title="Georgian Revival">Georgian Revival</a> predominates (examples by famed traditionalist architect <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Adams_Cram" title="Ralph Adams Cram">Ralph Adams Cram</a> and by <a href="/wiki/Polhemus_%26_Coffin" title="Polhemus &amp; Coffin">Polhemus &amp; Coffin</a>), but there are also eighteenth- and nineteenth-century houses and dramatic modernist structures (examples by <a href="/wiki/I.M._Pei" class="mw-redirect" title="I.M. Pei">I.M. Pei</a> and by <a href="/wiki/James_Polshek" title="James Polshek">James Polshek</a>).
</p><p>Principal buildings are in Georgian red brick, often with classical porticoes that were, by design, the unifying architectural feature of the early building phase.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Of this type are, in chronological order:
</p>
<ul><li>Hill House: Built 1911, designed by Francis Waterman; administration offices, with dormitory above.</li>
<li>West Wing: Built 1914; adjoining Hill House, a dining hall, with dormitory above.</li>
<li>Former Student Activities Center (formerly Gymnasium): Built 1917 as a gym, renovated 1979; currently unused, with a connector to the Larry Hart Pool.</li>
<li>Memorial House: Built 1921; dormitory on the northwest campus, designed to mirror Hill House on the southwest campus.</li>
<li>Seymour St. John Chapel (formerly St. Andrew's Chapel): Built 1924, designed by <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Adams_Cram" title="Ralph Adams Cram">Ralph Adams Cram</a>; recently the filming location for commercials of the Royal Bank of Scotland.</li>
<li>Andrew Mellon Library: Built 1925, designed by Edward Mellon; given by U.S. Treasury Secretary <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Mellon" title="Andrew Mellon">Andrew Mellon</a> P'25; Library special collections include correspondence and memorabilia of John F. Kennedy '35, Adlai Stevenson '18, Edward Albee '46, Caresse Crosby 1910 and other alumni, the Haffenreffer Collection of early American documents and autographs, and collections related to <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hardy" title="Thomas Hardy">Thomas Hardy</a> and other writers.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Archbold: Built 1928, designed by Ralph Adams Cram; its scale on the northeast campus mirrors those of Hill House and Memorial House on the west campus; formerly the largest school infirmary in the country, it now houses the visitor center and admissions office, with dormitory above.</li>
<li>Hall: Built 1929; adjoining West Wing, a cavernous dining hall, with dormitory above.</li>
<li>Paul Mellon Humanities Center (formerly Paul Mellon Science Hall): Built 1938, designed by Charles Fuller; houses the digital video lab and the departments of English, History, Philosophy, Religion, and Social Sciences.</li>
<li>Logan Munroe House: Built 1947; dormitory forming an ensemble with Memorial House, Nichols House, and Pitman House, linked by "Mem Circle" on the northwest campus.</li>
<li>Nichols House: Built 1948, designed by <a href="/wiki/Polhemus_%26_Coffin" title="Polhemus &amp; Coffin">Polhemus &amp; Coffin</a>; dormitory.</li>
<li>George and Clara St. John Hall: Built 1957; departments of Mathematics and Computer Science, demolished 2015.</li>
<li>Pitman House: Built 1960; dormitory.</li>
<li>Library new wing: Built 1963; dedication poem read by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Frost" title="Robert Frost">Robert Frost</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Steele Hall: Built 1967; departments of foreign languages.</li>
<li>Tenney House and Bernhard House: Built 2008, designed by <a href="/wiki/Centerbrook_Architects_%26_Planners" title="Centerbrook Architects &amp; Planners">Centerbrook Architects &amp; Planners</a>; it follows the <a href="/wiki/Residential_college" title="Residential college">residential college</a> model, the houses flank a courtyard and have a connector archway.</li>
<li>St. John Hall: Built 2016, current student center, houses Choate Store, Tuck Shop, video game room, pool and ping pong tables, study rooms, newsroom, lockers for day students.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Paul_Mellon_Arts_Center_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall,_Wallingford,_Connecticut.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Paul_Mellon_Arts_Center_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall%2C_Wallingford%2C_Connecticut.JPG/280px-Paul_Mellon_Arts_Center_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall%2C_Wallingford%2C_Connecticut.JPG" decoding="async" width="280" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Paul_Mellon_Arts_Center_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall%2C_Wallingford%2C_Connecticut.JPG/420px-Paul_Mellon_Arts_Center_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall%2C_Wallingford%2C_Connecticut.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Paul_Mellon_Arts_Center_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall%2C_Wallingford%2C_Connecticut.JPG/560px-Paul_Mellon_Arts_Center_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall%2C_Wallingford%2C_Connecticut.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1920" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Paul_Mellon_Arts_Center_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall,_Wallingford,_Connecticut.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Paul Mellon Arts Center, designed by I.M. Pei</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Carl_C._Icahn_Center_for_Science_2_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Carl_C._Icahn_Center_for_Science_2_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg/280px-Carl_C._Icahn_Center_for_Science_2_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg" decoding="async" width="280" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Carl_C._Icahn_Center_for_Science_2_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg/420px-Carl_C._Icahn_Center_for_Science_2_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Carl_C._Icahn_Center_for_Science_2_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg/560px-Carl_C._Icahn_Center_for_Science_2_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Carl_C._Icahn_Center_for_Science_2_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Carl C. Icahn Center for Science, designed by I.M. Pei</div></div></div>
<p>The I. M. Pei-designed buildings on campus are:
</p>
<ul><li>Paul Mellon Arts Center ("PMAC"): Built 1972, the gift of Paul Mellon; it was prototype for the Pei-designed East Building of the <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery_of_Art" title="National Gallery of Art">National Gallery of Art</a> in Washington, which was also a Paul Mellon benefaction; with a 775-seat <a href="/wiki/Proscenium" title="Proscenium">proscenium</a> theater designed by George Izenour, a <a href="/wiki/Black_box_theater" title="Black box theater">black box theater</a>, a recital hall, film studio, exhibition galleries, fine arts studios, music practice rooms, associated dressing rooms, and classrooms; the PMAC is also the home of the Wallingford Symphony Orchestra and a frequent venue for touring companies.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Icahn Center for Science (formerly Paul Mellon Science Center): Built 1989, the gift of Paul Mellon, renamed in 2001 following a gift from <a href="/wiki/Carl_C._Icahn" class="mw-redirect" title="Carl C. Icahn">Carl C. Icahn</a>; with 22 classrooms, laboratories, conservatory, and auditorium; departments of Physics, Chemistry, and Biology.</li></ul>
<p>Other large-scale buildings and athletic facilities include:
</p>
<ul><li>Kohler Environmental Center: Built 2012, designed by <a href="/wiki/Robert_A.M._Stern" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert A.M. Stern">Robert A.M. Stern Architects</a>; described as "the first teaching, research and residential environmental center in U.S. secondary education."</li>
<li>Lanphier Center: Built 2013-15, designed by <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Pelli" title="César Pelli">César Pelli</a>'s firm <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Pelli" title="César Pelli">Pelli Clarke Pelli</a>; in large part the gift of Cameron and Edward Lanphier '74; a 35,000-square foot center for applied mathematics, robotics, and information technology; its U-shape forms a courtyard centering on a giant weeping beech tree; a footbridge spanning Science Center Pond connects the Lanphier Center to the Icahn Center for Science.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> The building houses study rooms, a café, robotics lab, design lab, and math classrooms.</li>
<li>The Rosemary Hall campus: Built 1971, designed by <a href="/wiki/James_Polshek" title="James Polshek">James Polshek</a>; a complex on the heights of the northeast campus comprising, among other buildings, Bronfman, originally a library, now the Learning Community Day Care Center; Macquire Gymnasium; and Brownell, a former classroom building which now houses the offices of Alumni Relations, Development, and Information Technology. The girls dormitories were removed in the early 2000s.</li>
<li>Sally Hart Lodge and Alumni Center: Built 1850; the alumni center, guest house, and "hotel" of the school.</li>
<li>Pratt Health Center: The infirmary, staffed by 24-hour duty nurses and a pediatrician resident on campus; with the offices of Counseling and Community Service.</li>
<li>Clinton Knight House and McCook House: Built 1966, designed by Frank Winder '39; twin white-brick dormitories, each forming a quadrangle with central skylight atrium.</li>
<li>Worthington Johnson Athletic Center: Built 1932, designed by <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Augustus_Coffin" class="mw-redirect" title="Lewis Augustus Coffin">Lewis Augustus Coffin</a>; north wing added in 2002, designed by Herbert Newman and Partners; originally called the Winter Exercise Building ("Winter Ex") and renamed in 1976; an enormous building whose central room was used by the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Braves_(baseball)" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Braves (baseball)">Boston Braves</a> in 1943 and 1944 as their spring training infield; the WJAC contains two basketball courts, international squash courts, wrestling room, volleyball courts, suspended indoor track, ergometric room for crew, fitness and training rooms, and dance and aerobics studios.</li>
<li>Remsen Arena: Built 1967, renovated 2006; ice hockey facility among whose recent alumnae are four women's Team USA Olympians.</li>
<li>Larry Hart Pool: Built 1978, designed by Jeter, Cook &amp; Jepson; a 25-meter, 8-lane, solar-heated pool, with electronic timing system and underwater windows.</li>
<li>Hunt Tennis Center: Built 1995; in a terraced garden setting, with clubhouse, coaches' offices, and 22 tennis courts, six of which are all-weather USTA regulation.</li>
<li>Shanahan Field: Built 2010; a complex of side-by-side artificial surface fields, in landscaping and with permanent seating and lights for night events.</li>
<li>Bruce and Lueza Gelb Track: Built 2008; an 8-lane synthetic track with adjacent facilities for jumping and throwing events.</li>
<li>Sylvester Boathouse: Built 1985; on Lake Quonnipaug, the crew race course.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extracurricular_activities">Extracurricular activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Choate_Rosemary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Extracurricular activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Athletics">Athletics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Choate_Rosemary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Athletics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Andrew_Mellon_Library_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Andrew_Mellon_Library_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.JPG/280px-Andrew_Mellon_Library_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.JPG" decoding="async" width="280" height="210" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Andrew_Mellon_Library_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.JPG/420px-Andrew_Mellon_Library_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Andrew_Mellon_Library_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.JPG/560px-Andrew_Mellon_Library_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Andrew_Mellon_Library_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Andrew Mellon Library, built 1925</div></div></div>
<p>Choate competes in sports against schools from all over New England and adjacent states. Teams are fielded at the levels of varsity, junior varsity, and thirds sections. There are 32 different sports and 81 teams in interscholastic competition.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Intramural programs include aerobics, dance, senior weight training, yoga, winter running, rock climbing, fitness and conditioning, and senior volleyball.
</p><p>From 2007 to 2016 Choate has won New England championships in football, boys' and girls' ice hockey, girls' soccer, boys' golf, boys' crew, and in girls' swimming, volleyball, and water polo. In that same period, Choate has won Founders League championships in boys' and girls' squash, in boys' cross country, golf, softball, and tennis, and in girls' volleyball.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The athletic directors of Choate and the other members of the <a href="/wiki/Eight_Schools_Association" title="Eight Schools Association">Eight Schools Association</a> compose the Eight Schools Athletic Council, which organizes sports events and tournaments among ESA schools.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> Choate is also a member of the <a href="/wiki/New_England_Preparatory_School_Athletic_Council" title="New England Preparatory School Athletic Council">New England Preparatory School Athletic Council</a> (NEPSAC) and the Founders League, which comprises private schools located mainly in Connecticut.
</p>

<h3><span id="Choate.E2.80.93Deerfield_rivalry"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Choate–Deerfield_rivalry">Choate–Deerfield rivalry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Choate_Rosemary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Choate–Deerfield rivalry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Choate Rosemary Hall and Deerfield Academy have had a long-standing rivalry. The final weekend of the fall sports season is Deerfield Day (at Deerfield it's called "Choate Day"), when the two schools compete in every sport at varsity and sub-varsity levels. The tradition began in 1922 with an exchange of letters between Deerfield head <a href="/wiki/Frank_Boyden" title="Frank Boyden">Frank Boyden</a> and Choate head George St. John. Since then, busloads (in the early years, trainloads) of students have made the 80-mile journey along the Connecticut River valley to cheer their teams on the rival's campus.
</p><p>In the days leading up to the event, rallies and activities are held, led by Choate's Boar Pen cheering section and Deerfield's Captain Deerfield. At Choate there's the igniting of a fire-breathing dragon of sophisticated construction. At Deerfield there's a bonfire topped by a burning C, and ritual encircling of the school seal so that Choate teams won't tread on it. In recent years, alumni clubs of the two schools have met at venues around the world, from London to Los Angeles, to watch live-streaming of the varsity football game.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Similar boarding school traditions include the <a href="/wiki/Andover%E2%80%93Exeter_rivalry" class="mw-redirect" title="Andover–Exeter rivalry">Andover–Exeter rivalry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hotchkiss_School#Hotchkiss–Taft_rivalry" title="Hotchkiss School">Hotchkiss–Taft rivalry</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historic_cricket_match">Historic cricket match</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Choate_Rosemary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Historic cricket match">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a> match that Rosemary Hall hosted in Wallingford in 1893 against Mrs. Hazen's School of Pelham Manor, N.Y., has been described as "the first interscholastic girls sporting event in American history."<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> (Girls <i>intramural</i> sports had, of course, existed, but the Rosemary cricket match is the earliest discoverable <i>extramural</i> item.) The dating to 1893 occurs in the official history of Choate, published in 1997. Other discussions of the event give an inferential date of 1895 or earlier, referencing newspaper articles in 1896 that imply a well-established rivalry. An 1898 wire story titled "Women and Cricket" said that the Rosemary–Hazen series was four years old. Whatever the date of the first match, journalistic treatment of the novelty was "over-publicized and mildly sensationalized", according to a standard <a href="/wiki/History_of_American_cricket" class="mw-redirect" title="History of American cricket">history of American cricket</a> published in 1998. "Bareheaded and wearing sweaters and short skirts", reported <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, "daughters of some of the most prominent men in the country defied the cold, wintry wind." The <i><a href="/wiki/Baltimore_Morning_Herald" title="Baltimore Morning Herald">Baltimore Morning Herald</a></i> under the headline "Girls Play Cricket" noted that "the Pelham girls' skirts were some two or three inches longer than those of their opponents." In November 1896 the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yale_Medical_Journal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Yale Medical Journal (page does not exist)">Yale Medical Journal</a></i> carried a notice that Yale Med student Frederick Hulseberg had been "engaged to coach the Rosemary Hall cricket team this fall", thereby becoming the first coach of women's cricket in America.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stengel_and_the_Braves">Stengel and the Braves</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Choate_Rosemary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Stengel and the Braves">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During World War II, baseball commissioner <a href="/wiki/Kenesaw_Mountain_Landis" title="Kenesaw Mountain Landis">Kenesaw Mountain Landis</a>, under orders from the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Defense_Mobilization" title="Office of Defense Mobilization">Office of Defense Transportation</a>, told major league team owners to find spring training venues close to home. In Boston, the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Red_Sox" title="Boston Red Sox">Red Sox</a> chose Tufts University and the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Boston_Braves" title="History of the Boston Braves">Braves</a> at first considered <a href="/wiki/Phillips_Academy" title="Phillips Academy">Phillips Academy (Andover)</a>. But a sports editor at <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i> was a Choate alumnus, and he persuaded the Braves to look at Choate's Winter Exercise Building (now the Johnson Athletic Center), one of the largest glass-roofed, cage-netted rooms in the country, and easily able to accommodate a baseball infield. The Braves moved into Choate dormitories on March 21, 1943 and again in March 1944.
</p><p>Their roster included <a href="/wiki/Warren_Spahn" title="Warren Spahn">Warren Spahn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Sain" title="Johnny Sain">Johnny Sain</a> ("Spahn and Sain and pray for rain"), and manager <a href="/wiki/Casey_Stengel" title="Casey Stengel">Casey Stengel</a>. The journalistic opportunity afforded by Stengel in a prep school setting was not lost, and a famous wire-service photo shows him dressed in mortarboard and gown "lecturing" his players. His nickname "The Perfessor" first appears at this time, as does a fine example of Stengelese: asked to comment on Choate's facilities, he said, "Excellent workouts in that there cage you just saw, which is a honey in all my years to the present time, if I may be permitted to drop into the vernacular."<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditions">Traditions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Choate_Rosemary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Traditions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Choate Rosemary Hall's traditional events include:
</p>
<ul><li>Matriculation Ceremony at the start of school, in which each student signs a pledge card of commitment "to personal growth, to integrity, to self-discipline, and to caring for others."</li>
<li>Cake Race is held on the last day of fall classes. Students and staff throng the cross country course for a 5K race, with winners in each form awarded a cake.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:292px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Archbold_(dorm)_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Archbold_%28dorm%29_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg/290px-Archbold_%28dorm%29_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg" decoding="async" width="290" height="218" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Archbold_%28dorm%29_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg/435px-Archbold_%28dorm%29_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Archbold_%28dorm%29_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg/580px-Archbold_%28dorm%29_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Archbold_(dorm)_-_Choate_Rosemary_Hall.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Archbold, built 1928, designed by Ralph Adams Cram. The facade and lawn as shown are set up for the annual graduation ceremony.</div></div></div>
<ul><li>Festival of Lessons and Carols is based on a service held in <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_Chapel,_Cambridge" title="King&#39;s College Chapel, Cambridge">King's College Chapel, Cambridge</a>. The yuletide candlelight event in Seymour St. John Chapel includes Bible readings, music by Choate choruses, and instrumental groups, and carol-singing by the congregation.</li>
<li>Physics Phlotilla takes place in spring term. Students gather on the banks of the Science Center Pond to watch a competition among makeshift cardboard boats. The principles of buoyancy are tested, and many craft are sunk. Students must sail their boats the length of the pond and there are prizes for speed and creative design. There is also a sub-category for best suit of armour constructed from cardboard.</li>
<li>First Hurrah is the final social event for the fifth form students hosted by the sixth formers. It marks 100 more days till the end of school.</li>
<li>Last Hurrah is the final social event for sixth formers. Traditionally sixth formers enjoy a reception, dinner, ballroom dance, and swing dance competition.</li>
<li>Incendium is held in early April after sixth formers have received their college application decisions. Rejection letters are tossed into fire pits on the Class of 2008 Patio, and pinatas blazoned with the names of colleges are cathartically destroyed.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Garden Party, a Rosemary Hall tradition, takes place in spring term. Sixth form girls invite a fifth form (junior class) girl and a faculty member. They exchange flowers, take pictures, and pass on Rosemarian traditions to the rising senior girls. A slideshow is then presented. In response to Garden Party, Choate boys have created a "Parden Garty" and are now included in the slideshow portion of the event.</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gertrude_Stein_and_The_Lit">Gertrude Stein and <i>The Lit</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Choate_Rosemary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Gertrude Stein and The Lit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1935 <a href="/wiki/Gertrude_Stein" title="Gertrude Stein">Gertrude Stein</a> gave a series of talks in the United States that included, on January 12 and 13, a visit to Choate. Fortunately for literary posterity, her audience included both a stenographer and <a href="/wiki/Dudley_Fitts" title="Dudley Fitts">Dudley Fitts</a>, critic, translator, and longtime teacher of Greek and Latin at Choate. Stein's public speaking style was extemporary, and Fitts recognized the permanent value of a stenographic transcript. After an exchange of letters, Stein authorized Fitts to oversee publication of her talk in <i>The Choate Literary Magazine</i>. On February 5, he sent her the page proofs, saying, "I had to restore a great deal of the lecture from memory. I hope I didn't spoil anything. Sorry to have missed entirely what you said about the noun." (Fitts's typescript and the Fitts-Stein correspondence are now at Yale.)<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Stein's essay appeared in the February 1935 issue of <i>The Lit</i> with the title "How Writing Is Written." It has many times since been anthologized and given academic treatment, with the Choate text unaltered. It "occupies a unique place in Stein's corpus as a social text that carries the marks of its particular occasion ... direct address to her audience (around sixty boys, as well as faculty and some former students)."<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> Stein's two-day stay at Choate was her first exposure to a private school, if we can believe her statement in <i>Everybody's Autobiography</i> (published 1937). She wrote, "It was the first time I had ever seen such a school. When I was brought up in East Oakland we all went to public school ... The boys from twelve to sixteen listened really listened [<i>sic</i>] to everything I had to say ... I had been much struck by the Choate school literary magazine which did have extraordinary good writing in it."<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Edward_Albee_and_The_Lit">Edward Albee and <i>The Lit</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Choate_Rosemary_Hall&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Edward Albee and The Lit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1944 <a href="/wiki/Edward_Albee" title="Edward Albee">Edward Albee</a> transferred from <a href="/wiki/Valley_Forge_Military_Academy_and_College" title="Valley Forge Military Academy and College">Valley Forge Military Academy</a> to Choate, with admissions director Frank Wheeler making a prescient note, "I have a feeling he will distinguish himself in literature."<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> His brilliance was quickly recognized and mentored by such teachers as John Joseph, Charles Rice, and Sandy Lehmann. He was made managing editor of <i>The Lit</i> and for two years a large part of its content, in all genres, was supplied by him. Incidents from Albee's time at Choate are reworked for his plays, including the famous "bergin and water" speech in <i><a href="/wiki/Who%27s_Afraid_of_Virginia_Woolf" class="mw-redirect" title="Who&#39;s Afraid of Virginia Woolf">Who's Afraid of Virginia Woolf</a></i> and the "walking crow" speech in <i><a href="/wiki/Tiny_Alice" title="Tiny Alice">Tiny Alice</a></i>, which reproduces almost verbatim English teacher Lehmann's criticism of a sonnet Albee wrote for <i>The Lit</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> The May 1946 Commencement issue of <i>The Lit</i> contained Albee's first published play, a long one-acter called <i>Schism</i>. Another play written at Choate, <i>Each In His Own Way</i>, went unpublished and forgotten until 1996, when a classmate preparing for their 50th reunion found it in a scrapbook.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
