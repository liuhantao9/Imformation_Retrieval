<p>The <b>National Archives and Records Administration</b> (<b>NARA</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Independent_agencies_of_the_United_States_government" title="Independent agencies of the United States government">independent agency</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_government" class="mw-redirect" title="United States government">United States government</a> charged with the preservation and documentation of government and historical records. It is also tasked with increasing public access to those documents which make up the National Archive.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> NARA is officially responsible for maintaining and publishing the legally authentic and authoritative copies of <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Congress" class="mw-redirect" title="Acts of Congress">acts of Congress</a>, <a href="/wiki/Presidential_directives" class="mw-redirect" title="Presidential directives">presidential directives</a>, and federal regulations. NARA also transmits votes of the <a href="/wiki/Electoral_College_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Electoral College (United States)">Electoral College</a> to Congress.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Archives_and_Records_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_the_National_Archives_and_Records_Administration" title="Organization of the National Archives and Records Administration">Organization of the National Archives and Records Administration</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Archivist_of_the_United_States" title="Archivist of the United States">Archivist of the United States</a> is the chief official overseeing the operation of the National Archives and Records Administration. The Archivist not only maintains the official documentation of the passage of <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_amendment#United_States" title="Constitutional amendment">amendments</a> to the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Constitution">U.S. Constitution</a> by state legislatures, but has the authority to declare when the constitutional threshold for passage has been reached, and therefore when an act has become an amendment.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Office_of_the_Federal_Register" title="Office of the Federal Register">Office of the Federal Register</a> publishes the <i><a href="/wiki/Federal_Register" title="Federal Register">Federal Register</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Code_of_Federal_Regulations" title="Code of Federal Regulations">Code of Federal Regulations</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/United_States_Statutes_at_Large" title="United States Statutes at Large">United States Statutes at Large</a></i>, among others. It also administers the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Electoral_College" title="United States Electoral College">Electoral College</a>.
</p><p>The National Historical Publications and Records Commission (NHPRC)—the agency's grant-making arm—awards funds to state and local governments, public and private archives, colleges and universities, and other nonprofit organizations to preserve and publish historical records.  Since 1964, the NHPRC has awarded some 4,500 grants.
</p><p>The Office of Government Information Services (OGIS) is a <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_Information_Act_(United_States)" title="Freedom of Information Act (United States)">Freedom of Information Act</a> (FOIA) resource for the public and the government. Congress has charged NARA with reviewing FOIA policies, procedures and compliance of Federal agencies and to recommend changes to FOIA. NARA's mission also includes resolving FOIA disputes between Federal agencies and requesters.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Archives_and_Records_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ArchivesRotunda.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/ArchivesRotunda.jpg/220px-ArchivesRotunda.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/ArchivesRotunda.jpg/330px-ArchivesRotunda.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/ArchivesRotunda.jpg/440px-ArchivesRotunda.jpg 2x" data-file-width="796" data-file-height="526" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ArchivesRotunda.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rotunda of the National Archives Building</div></div></div>
<p>Originally, each branch and agency of the U.S. government was responsible for maintaining its own documents, which often resulted in the loss and destruction of records. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> created the National Archives Establishment in 1934 to centralize federal record keeping, with the <a href="/wiki/Archivist_of_the_United_States" title="Archivist of the United States">Archivist of the United States</a> serving as chief administrator. <a href="/wiki/R.D.W._Connor" class="mw-redirect" title="R.D.W. Connor">R.D.W. Connor</a> was chosen to be the first leader of the organization.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After a recommendation by the first <a href="/wiki/Hoover_Commission" title="Hoover Commission">Hoover Commission</a> in 1949, the National Archives was placed within the newly formed <a href="/wiki/General_Services_Administration" title="General Services Administration">General Services Administration</a> (GSA). NARA was officially given its independence from the GSA with the passing of the Records Administration Act of 1984, thus giving birth to the institution we have today.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 1978, millions of feet of news reels were destroyed in a fire at an offsite location in Suitland, Maryland.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The reels, made of exceptionally flammable nitrate material, had been donated previously by Universal Pictures and were stored in special vaults to protect against fires. In total over 12.6 million feet of film was destroyed.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2006, it was revealed by the Archivist of the United States in a public hearing that a memorandum of understanding between NARA and various government agencies existed to "reclassify", i.e., withdraw from public access, certain documents in the name of national security, and to do so in a manner such that researchers would not be likely to discover the process (the <a href="/wiki/U.S._reclassification_program" title="U.S. reclassification program">U.S. reclassification program</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-secret_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-secret-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> An audit indicated that more than one third withdrawn since 1999 did not contain sensitive information.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  The program was originally scheduled to end in 2007.
</p><p>In 2010, <a href="/wiki/Executive_Order_13526" title="Executive Order 13526">Executive Order 13526</a> created the National Declassification Center<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> to coordinate <a href="/wiki/Declassification" title="Declassification">declassification</a> practices across agencies, provide secure document services to other agencies, and review records in NARA custody for declassification.
</p><p>The National Archives found itself under public scrutiny when it initially agreed, in 2017, to requests from the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Immigration_and_Customs_Enforcement" title="U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement">U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement</a> agency (ICE) to allow certain documents be scheduled for destruction.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Records">Records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Archives_and_Records_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>NARA's holdings are classed into "record groups" reflecting the governmental department or agency from which they originated. Records include paper documents, microfilm, still pictures, motion pictures, and electronic media.
</p><p>Archival descriptions of the permanent holdings of the federal government in the custody of NARA are stored in the National Archives Catalog.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The archival descriptions include information on traditional paper holdings, electronic records, and artifacts.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  As of December 2012, the catalog consisted of about 10 <a href="/wiki/1000000000_(number)" class="mw-redirect" title="1000000000 (number)">billion</a> logical data records describing 527,000 artifacts and encompassing 81% of NARA's records.<sup id="cite_ref-ARC_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ARC-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> There are also 922,000 digital copies of already digitized materials.<sup id="cite_ref-ARC_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ARC-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most records at NARA are in the <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a>, as works of the federal government are excluded from <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> protection. However, records from other sources may still be protected by copyright or donor agreements.<sup id="cite_ref-copyright_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-copyright-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Executive_Order_13526" title="Executive Order 13526">Executive Order 13526</a> directs originating agencies to declassify documents if possible before shipment to NARA for long-term storage,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> but NARA also stores some <a href="/wiki/Classified_document" class="mw-redirect" title="Classified document">classified documents</a> until they can be <a href="/wiki/Declassified" title="Declassified">declassified</a>.  Its <a href="/wiki/Information_Security_Oversight_Office" title="Information Security Oversight Office">Information Security Oversight Office</a> monitors and sets policy for the U.S. government's security classification system.
</p><p>Many of NARA's most requested records are frequently used for <a href="/wiki/Genealogy" title="Genealogy">genealogy</a> research. This includes <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a> records from 1790 to 1940, ships' passenger lists, and <a href="/wiki/Naturalization" title="Naturalization">naturalization</a> records.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Archival_Recovery_Teams">Archival Recovery Teams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Archives_and_Records_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Archival Recovery Teams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities_and_exhibition_spaces">Facilities and exhibition spaces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Archives_and_Records_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Facilities and exhibition spaces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/National_Archives_facilities" title="National Archives facilities">National Archives facilities</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:National_Archives_DC_2007s.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/National_Archives_DC_2007s.jpg/220px-National_Archives_DC_2007s.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="139" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/National_Archives_DC_2007s.jpg/330px-National_Archives_DC_2007s.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/National_Archives_DC_2007s.jpg/440px-National_Archives_DC_2007s.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2298" data-file-height="1452" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:National_Archives_DC_2007s.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/National_Archives_Building" title="National Archives Building">National Archives Building</a> from <a href="/wiki/Constitution_Avenue" title="Constitution Avenue">Constitution Avenue</a></div></div></div>
<p>The most well known facility of the National Archives and Records Administration is the <a href="/wiki/National_Archives_Building" title="National Archives Building">National Archives Building</a> (informally known as "Archives I"), located north of the <a href="/wiki/National_Mall" title="National Mall">National Mall</a> on <a href="/wiki/Constitution_Avenue" title="Constitution Avenue">Constitution Avenue</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>. A sister facility, known as the <a href="/wiki/National_Archives_at_College_Park" title="National Archives at College Park">National Archives at College Park</a> ("Archives II") was opened 1994 near the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Maryland,_College_Park" title="University of Maryland, College Park">University of Maryland, College Park</a>.  The <a href="/wiki/Washington_National_Records_Center" title="Washington National Records Center">Washington National Records Center</a> (WNRC), also located in the Washington, D.C., metropolitan area, is a large warehouse facility where federal records that are still under the control of the creating agency are stored.  Federal government agencies pay a yearly fee for storage at the facility. In accordance with federal records schedules, documents at WNRC are transferred to the legal custody of the National Archives after a certain time; this usually involves a relocation of the records to College Park. Temporary records at WNRC are either retained for a fee or destroyed after retention times have elapsed.  WNRC also offers research services and maintains a small research room.
</p><p>Across the United States, the National Archives maintains both research facilities and additional federal records centers (FRCs). In many cases, the research rooms of regional archives are located at the same site as the federal records center, which are inaccessible to the public.
</p><p>In April 2019 an unknown person set fire to an exterior wall of the archives building using a homemade incendiary device before firefighters were able to extinguish the flames.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Public.E2.80.93private_partnerships"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Public–private_partnerships">Public–private partnerships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Archives_and_Records_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Public–private partnerships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In an effort to make its holdings more widely available and more easily accessible, the National Archives began entering into public–private partnerships in 2006. A joint venture with <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> will digitize and offer NARA video online. When announcing the agreement, Archivist Allen Weinstein said that this pilot program is
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote" style="font-size: 100%;"><p>...&#160;an important step for the National Archives to achieve its goal of becoming an archive without walls. Our new strategic plan emphasizes the importance of providing access to records anytime, anywhere. This is one of many initiatives that we are launching to make our goal a reality. For the first time, the public will be able to view this collection of rare and unusual films on the Internet.<sup id="cite_ref-google_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>On January 10, 2007, the National Archives and Fold3.com (formerly Footnote)<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> launched a pilot project to digitize historic documents from the National Archives holdings. Allen Weinstein explained that this partnership would "allow much greater access to approximately 4.5 million pages of important documents that are currently available only in their original format or on microfilm" and "would also enhance NARA's efforts to preserve its original records."<sup id="cite_ref-footnote_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-footnote-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 2007, the National Archives announced it would make copies of its collection of <a href="/wiki/Universal_Newsreel" title="Universal Newsreel">Universal Newsreels</a> from 1929 to 1967 available for purchase through <a href="/wiki/CreateSpace" title="CreateSpace">CreateSpace</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Amazon_(company)" title="Amazon (company)">Amazon.com</a> subsidiary. During the announcement, Weinstein noted that the agreement would "...&#160;reap major benefits for the public-at-large and for the National Archives." Adding, "While the public can come to our College Park, Maryland, research room to view films and even copy them at no charge, this new program will make our holdings much more accessible to millions of people who cannot travel to the Washington, D.C. area." The agreement also calls for CreateSpace partnership to provide the National Archives with digital reference and preservation copies of the films as part of NARA's preservation program.<sup id="cite_ref-create_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-create-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In May 2008, the National Archives announced a five-year agreement to digitize selected records including the complete U.S. Federal Census Collection, 1790–1930, passenger lists from 1820 to 1960 and World War I and World War II draft registration cards.<sup id="cite_ref-ancestry_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ancestry-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The partnership agreement allows for exclusive use of the digitized records by <a href="/wiki/Ancestry.com" title="Ancestry.com">Ancestry.com</a> for a 5-year embargo period whereupon the digital records will be turned over to the National Archives.<sup id="cite_ref-ancestryagr_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ancestryagr-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Social_media">Social media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Archives_and_Records_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Social media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The National Archives currently utilizes <a href="/wiki/Social_media" title="Social media">social media</a> and <a href="/wiki/Web_2.0" title="Web 2.0">Web 2.0</a> technologies in an attempt to communicate better with the public.<sup id="cite_ref-social_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-social-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On June 18, 2009, the National Archives announced the launching of a YouTube channel "to showcase popular archived films, inform the public about upcoming events around the country, and bring National Archives exhibits to the people."<sup id="cite_ref-youtube_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-youtube-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Also in 2009, the National Archives launched a Flickr photostream to share portions of its photographic holdings with the general public.<sup id="cite_ref-flickr_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-flickr-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> A new teaching-with-documents Web site premiered in 2010 and was developed by the education team.  The site<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> features 3,000 documents, images, and recordings from the holdings of the Archives. It also features lesson plans and tools for creating new classroom activities and lessons.
</p><p>In 2011, the National Archives initiated a WikiProject on the English Wikipedia to expand collaboration in making its holdings widely available through Wikimedia.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_crimes">Notable crimes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Archives_and_Records_Administration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Notable crimes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>In 1963, Robert Bradford Murphy and his wife, Elizabeth Irene Murphy were arrested and sentenced to ten years in prison for stealing documents from several federal depositories, including the National Archives.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In 1987, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Mount#Controversy" title="Charles Mount">Charles Merrill Mount</a> was arrested and sentenced to five years in prison for stealing 400 documents from the National Archives<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>.</li>
<li>In 2002, Shawn Aubitz pleaded guilty to stealing dozens of documents and photographs from the National Archives during the 90's.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2005, <a href="/wiki/Sandy_Berger#Unauthorized_removal_and_destruction_of_classified_material" title="Sandy Berger">Sandy Berger</a> was charged with an unauthorized removal of documents from the National Archives; sentenced to 100 hours of community service and fined $50,000.</li>
<li>In 2005, Howard Harner was sentenced to two years in prison and fined $10,000 after stealing 100 documents from the National Archives.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2006, Denning McTague was sentenced to 15 months in prison and fined $3,000 after stealing 164 documents from the National Archives.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2011, Leslie Waffen was sentenced to 18 months in prison after stealing 955 recordings from the National Archives.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2011, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_P._Lowry" title="Thomas P. Lowry">Thomas Lowry</a> was permanently banned from the National Archives after he confessed to altering the date on a presidential pardon signed by <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2011, Barry Landau and Jason Savedoff were arrested and sentenced to seven-and-a-half years in prison for stealing ten thousand documents from the National Archives.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2018, Antonin DeHays was arrested for multiple thefts of military artifacts and records from the National Archive during the mid to late 2010s.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
