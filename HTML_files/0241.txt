<p><b>Dutch West India Company</b> (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i lang="nl">Geoctroyeerde Westindische Compagnie</i>, <small>Dutch pronunciation:&#32;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Dutch" title="Help:IPA/Dutch">[ɣəʔɔktroːˈjeːrdə ʋɛstˈɪndisə kɔmpɑˈɲi]</a></span> or <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i lang="nl"><b>WIC</b></i>; English: <span lang="en">Chartered West India Company</span>) was a <a href="/wiki/Chartered_company" title="Chartered company">chartered company</a> (known as the "WIC") of <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> <a href="/wiki/Merchant" title="Merchant">merchants</a> as well as foreign investors. Among its founders was <a href="/wiki/Willem_Usselincx" title="Willem Usselincx">Willem Usselincx</a> (1567–1647).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> On 3 June 1621, it was granted a <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Charter_of_the_Dutch_West_India_Company" class="extiw" title="wikisource:Charter of the Dutch West India Company">charter</a> for a trade monopoly in the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_West_Indies" class="mw-redirect" title="Dutch West Indies">Dutch West Indies</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_Seven_United_Netherlands" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of the Seven United Netherlands">Republic of the Seven United Netherlands</a> and given jurisdiction over Dutch participation in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">Atlantic slave trade</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, and <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. The area where the company could operate consisted of <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a> (between the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">Tropic of Cancer</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cape_of_Good_Hope" title="Cape of Good Hope">Cape of Good Hope</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>, which included the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a> and the eastern part of <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">New Guinea</a>. The intended purpose of the charter was to eliminate competition, particularly Spanish or Portuguese, between the various trading posts established by the merchants. The company became instrumental in the largely ephemeral <a href="/wiki/Dutch_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Dutch colonization of the Americas">Dutch colonization of the Americas</a> (including <a href="/wiki/New_Netherland" title="New Netherland">New Netherland</a>) in the seventeenth century. From 1624 to 1654, in the context of the <a href="/wiki/Dutch-Portuguese_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Dutch-Portuguese War">Dutch-Portuguese War</a>, the WIC held Portuguese territory in northeast Brazil, but they were ousted from <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Brazil" title="Dutch Brazil">Dutch Brazil</a> following fierce resistance.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After several reversals, WIC reorganized and a new charter was granted in 1675, largely on the strength in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">Atlantic slave trade</a>.  This "New" version lasted for more than a century, until after the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Anglo-Dutch_War" title="Fourth Anglo-Dutch War">Fourth Anglo-Dutch War</a>, during which it lost most its assets.
</p>

<p>When the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_India_Company" title="Dutch East India Company">Dutch East India Company</a> (VOC) was founded in 1602, some traders in Amsterdam did not agree with its mono politics. With help from <a href="/wiki/Petrus_Plancius" title="Petrus Plancius">Petrus Plancius</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Dutch_people" title="Dutch people">Dutch</a>-<a href="/wiki/Flemish_people" title="Flemish people">Flemish</a> <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">astronomer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">cartographer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clergyman" class="mw-redirect" title="Clergyman">clergyman</a>, they sought for a northeastern or northwestern access to Asia to circumvent the VOC monopoly. In 1609, English explorer <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hudson" title="Henry Hudson">Henry Hudson</a>, in employment of the VOC, landed on the coast of <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> and sailed up what is now known as the Hudson River in his quest for the <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Passage" title="Northwest Passage">Northwest Passage</a> to Asia. However, he failed to find a passage. Consequently, in 1615 <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Le_Maire" title="Isaac Le Maire">Isaac Le Maire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Blommaert" title="Samuel Blommaert">Samuel Blommaert</a>, assisted by others, focused on finding a south-westerly route around South America's <a href="/wiki/Tierra_del_Fuego" title="Tierra del Fuego">Tierra del Fuego</a> archipelago in order to circumvent the monopoly of the VOC.
</p><p>One of the first sailors who focused on trade with Africa was <a href="/wiki/Balthazar_de_Moucheron" title="Balthazar de Moucheron">Balthazar de Moucheron</a>. The trade with <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> offered several possibilities to set up trading posts or factories, an important starting point for negotiations. It was Blommaert, however, who stated that, in 1600, eight companies sailed on the coast of Africa, competing with each other for the supply of copper, from the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Loango" title="Kingdom of Loango">Kingdom of Loango</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Pieter van den Broecke was employed by one of these companies. In 1612, a Dutch fortress was built in <a href="/wiki/Mouree" class="mw-redirect" title="Mouree">Mouree</a> (present day Ghana), along the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Gold_Coast" title="Dutch Gold Coast">Dutch Gold Coast</a>.
</p><p>Trade with the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, for salt, sugar and tobacco, was hampered by Spain and delayed because of peace negotiations. Spain offered peace on condition that the Dutch Republic would withdraw from trading with Asia and America. Spain refused to sign the peace treaty if a West Indian Company would be established. At this time, the <a href="/wiki/Eighty_Years%27_War" title="Eighty Years&#39; War">Dutch War of Independence (1568–1648)</a> between Spain and the Dutch Republic was occurring. <a href="/wiki/Grand_Pensionary" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Pensionary">Grand Pensionary</a> <a href="/wiki/Johan_van_Oldenbarnevelt" title="Johan van Oldenbarnevelt">Johan van Oldenbarnevelt</a> offered to only suspend trade with the West in exchange for the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Years%27_Truce" title="Twelve Years&#39; Truce">Twelve Years' Truce</a>. The result was that, during a few years, the company sailed under a foreign flag in South America. However, ten years later, <a href="/wiki/Stadtholder" title="Stadtholder">Stadtholder</a> <a href="/wiki/Maurice_of_Orange" class="mw-redirect" title="Maurice of Orange">Maurice of Orange</a>, proposed to continue the war with Spain, but also to distract attention from Spain to the Republic. In 1619, his opponent <a href="/wiki/Johan_van_Oldenbarnevelt" title="Johan van Oldenbarnevelt">Johan van Oldenbarnevelt</a> was beheaded, and when two years later the truce expired, the West Indian Company was established.
</p><p>The West India Company received its charter from the States-General in 1621, but its foundation had been suggested much earlier in the 17th century only to be delayed by the conclusion of the <a href="/wiki/Twelve_Years%27_Truce" title="Twelve Years&#39; Truce">Twelve Years' Truce</a> between Spain and the United Provinces in 1609.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_West_India_Company">The West India Company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_West_India_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The West India Company">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Willem_Usselinx_(1567-na_1647)._Koopman_en_stichter_van_de_West_Indische_Compagnie_Rijksmuseum_SK-A-1675.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Willem_Usselinx_%281567-na_1647%29._Koopman_en_stichter_van_de_West_Indische_Compagnie_Rijksmuseum_SK-A-1675.jpeg/220px-Willem_Usselinx_%281567-na_1647%29._Koopman_en_stichter_van_de_West_Indische_Compagnie_Rijksmuseum_SK-A-1675.jpeg" decoding="async" width="220" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Willem_Usselinx_%281567-na_1647%29._Koopman_en_stichter_van_de_West_Indische_Compagnie_Rijksmuseum_SK-A-1675.jpeg/330px-Willem_Usselinx_%281567-na_1647%29._Koopman_en_stichter_van_de_West_Indische_Compagnie_Rijksmuseum_SK-A-1675.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Willem_Usselinx_%281567-na_1647%29._Koopman_en_stichter_van_de_West_Indische_Compagnie_Rijksmuseum_SK-A-1675.jpeg/440px-Willem_Usselinx_%281567-na_1647%29._Koopman_en_stichter_van_de_West_Indische_Compagnie_Rijksmuseum_SK-A-1675.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="2061" data-file-height="2500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Willem_Usselinx_(1567-na_1647)._Koopman_en_stichter_van_de_West_Indische_Compagnie_Rijksmuseum_SK-A-1675.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Willem_Usselincx" title="Willem Usselincx">Willem Usselincx</a>, founder of the Dutch West India Company</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Delaware_Bay_Vinckeboons_14.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Delaware_Bay_Vinckeboons_14.jpg/220px-Delaware_Bay_Vinckeboons_14.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Delaware_Bay_Vinckeboons_14.jpg/330px-Delaware_Bay_Vinckeboons_14.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Delaware_Bay_Vinckeboons_14.jpg/440px-Delaware_Bay_Vinckeboons_14.jpg 2x" data-file-width="8382" data-file-height="5888" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Delaware_Bay_Vinckeboons_14.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Swaanendael Colony along the Delaware</div></div></div>
<p>The <i>Dutch West India Company</i> was organized similarly to the Dutch East India Company (VOC). Like the VOC, the WIC company had five offices, called chambers (<i>kamers</i>), in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rotterdam" title="Rotterdam">Rotterdam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hoorn" title="Hoorn">Hoorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middelburg" title="Middelburg">Middelburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Groningen_(city)" class="mw-redirect" title="Groningen (city)">Groningen</a>, of which the chambers in Amsterdam and Middelburg contributed most to the company. The board consisted of 19 members, known as the Heeren XIX (the Nineteen Gentlemen).<sup id="cite_ref-WDL2_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WDL2-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The institutional structure of the WIC followed the federal structure, which entailed extensive discussion for any decision, with regional representation: 8 from Amsterdam; 4 from Zeeland, 2 each from the Northern Quarter (Hoorn and Enkhuizen), the Maas (Rotterdam and Dordrecht), the region of Groningen, and one representative from the States General. Each region had its own chamber and board of directors.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The validity of the charter was set at 24 years.
</p><p>Only in 1623 was funding arranged, after several bidders were put under pressure. The <a href="/wiki/States_General_of_the_Netherlands" title="States General of the Netherlands">States General of the Netherlands</a> and the VOC pledged one million <a href="/wiki/Guilders" class="mw-redirect" title="Guilders">guilders</a> in the form of capital and subsidy. Although Iberian writers said that <a href="/wiki/Crypto-Jews" class="mw-redirect" title="Crypto-Jews">crypto-Jews</a> or <a href="/wiki/Marranos" class="mw-redirect" title="Marranos">Marranos</a> played an important role in the formation of both the VOC and the WIC, research has shown that initially they played a minor role, but expanded during the period of the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Brazil" title="Dutch Brazil">Dutch in Brazil</a>. Emigrant <a href="/wiki/Calvinists" class="mw-redirect" title="Calvinists">Calvinists</a> from the Spanish Netherlands did make significant investments in the WIC.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Investors did not rush to put their money in the company in 1621, but the States-General urged municipalities and other institutions to invest. Explanations for the slow investment by individuals were that shareholders had "no control over the directors' policy and the handling of ordinary investors' money," that it was a "racket" to provide "cushy posts for the directors and their relatives, at the expense of ordinary shareholders."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The VOC directors invested money in the WIC, without consulting their shareholders, causing dissent among a number of shareholders.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In order to attract foreign shareholders, the WIC offered equal standing to foreign investors with Dutch, resulting in shareholders from France, Switzerland, and Venice. A translation of the original 1621 charter appeared in English, <i>Orders and Articles granted by the High and Mightie Lords the States General of the United Provinces concerning the erecting of a West-Indies Companie, Anno Dom. MDCXII</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> by 1623, the capital for the WIC at 2.8 million florins was not as great the VOC's original capitalization of 6.5 million, but it was still a substantial sum. The WIC had 15 ships to carry trade and plied the west African coast and Brazil.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike the VOC, the WIC had no right to deploy military troops. When the Twelve Years' Truce in 1621 was over, the Republic had a free hand to re-wage war with Spain. A <i><a href="/wiki/Groot_Desseyn" title="Groot Desseyn">Groot Desseyn</a></i> ("grand design") was devised to seize the Portuguese colonies in Africa and the Americas, so as to dominate the sugar and slave trade. When this plan failed, <a href="/wiki/Privateering" class="mw-redirect" title="Privateering">privateering</a> became one of the major goals within the WIC. The arming of merchant ships with guns and soldiers to defend themselves against Spanish ships was of great importance. On almost all ships in 1623, 40 to 50 soldiers were stationed, possibly to assist in the <a href="/wiki/Maritime_hijacking" class="mw-redirect" title="Maritime hijacking">hijacking</a> of enemy ships.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> It is unclear whether the first expedition was the expedition by Jacques l'Hermite to the coast of <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, set up by Stadtholder Maurice with the support of the States General and the VOC.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Piet_Hein.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Piet_Hein.jpg/220px-Piet_Hein.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="284" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Piet_Hein.jpg/330px-Piet_Hein.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Piet_Hein.jpg/440px-Piet_Hein.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="2578" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Piet_Hein.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Piet_Hein_(Netherlands)" class="mw-redirect" title="Piet Hein (Netherlands)">Piet Heyn</a>, WIC admiral who captured the Spanish silver fleet in 1628.</div></div></div>
<p>The company was initially a dismal failure, in terms of its expensive early projects, and its directors shifted emphasis from conquest of territory to pursue plunder of shipping. The most spectacular success for the WIC was <a href="/wiki/Piet_Hein_(Netherlands)" class="mw-redirect" title="Piet Hein (Netherlands)">Piet Heyn</a>'s seizure of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_silver_fleet" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish silver fleet">Spanish silver fleet</a>, which carried silver from Spanish colonies to Spain. He had also seized a consignment of sugar from Brazil and a galleon from Honduras with cacao, indigo, and other valuable goods. <a href="/wiki/Privateering" class="mw-redirect" title="Privateering">Privateering</a> was its most profitable activity in the late 1620s.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Despite Heyn's success at plunder, the company's directors realized that it was not a basis to build long-term profit, leading them to renew their attempts to seize Iberian territory in the Americas. They decided their target was Brazil.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There were conflicts between directors from different areas of The Netherlands, with Amsterdam less supportive of the company. Non-maritime cities, including Haarlem, Leiden, and Gouda, along with Enkhuizen and Hoorn were enthusiastic about seizing territory. They sent a fleet to Brazil, capturing <a href="/wiki/Olinda" title="Olinda">Olinda</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pernambuco" title="Pernambuco">Pernambuco</a> in 1630 in their initial foray to create a <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Brazil" title="Dutch Brazil">Dutch Brazil</a>, but could not hold them due to a strong Portuguese resistance.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Company ships continued privateering in the Caribbean, as well seizing vital land resources, particularly salt pans.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The company's general lack of success saw their shares plummet and the Dutch and The Spanish renewed truce talks in 1633.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1629 the WIC gave permission to a number of investors in <a href="/wiki/New_Netherland" title="New Netherland">New Netherlands</a> to found <a href="/wiki/Patroonships" class="mw-redirect" title="Patroonships">patroonships</a>, enabled by the <a href="/wiki/Charter_of_Freedoms_and_Exemptions" title="Charter of Freedoms and Exemptions">Charter of Freedoms and Exemptions</a> which was ratified by the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_States-General&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Dutch States-General (page does not exist)">Dutch States-General</a> on June 7, 1629. The patroonships were created to help populate the colony, by providing investors grants providing land for approximately 50 people and "upwards of 15 years old", per grant, mainly in the region of New Netherland.<sup id="cite_ref-WDL2_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WDL2-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WDL_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WDL-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Patroon investors could expand the size of their land grants as large as 4 miles, "along the shore or along one bank of a navigable river..." <a href="/wiki/Rensselaerswyck" class="mw-redirect" title="Rensselaerswyck">Rensselaerswyck</a> was the most successful Dutch West India Company patroonship.<sup id="cite_ref-WDL2_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WDL2-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The New Netherland area, which included <a href="/wiki/New_Amsterdam" title="New Amsterdam">New Amsterdam</a>, covered parts of present-day <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Delaware" title="Delaware">Delaware</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WDL2_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WDL2-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Other settlements were established on the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Antilles" title="Netherlands Antilles">Netherlands Antilles</a>, and in South America, in <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Brazil" title="Dutch Brazil">Dutch Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>. In Africa, posts were established on the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Gold_Coast" title="Dutch Gold Coast">Gold Coast</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Slave_Coast" title="Dutch Slave Coast">Slave Coast</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Benin" title="Benin">Benin</a>), and briefly in <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">Angola</a>. It was a neo-<a href="/wiki/Feudal_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal system">feudal system</a>, where patrons were permitted considerable powers to control the overseas colony. In the Americas, <a href="/wiki/Fur" title="Fur">fur</a> (North America) and sugar (South America) were the most important trade goods, while African settlements traded the enslaved (mainly destined for the plantations on the Antilles and Suriname), gold, and ivory.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline">Decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_West_India_Company&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cidade_mauricia.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Cidade_mauricia.jpg/200px-Cidade_mauricia.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="126" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Cidade_mauricia.jpg/300px-Cidade_mauricia.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Cidade_mauricia.jpg/400px-Cidade_mauricia.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="314" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cidade_mauricia.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Recife" title="Recife">Recife</a> or Mauritsstad&#160;– capital of <a href="/wiki/Nieuw_Holland" class="mw-redirect" title="Nieuw Holland">Nieuw Holland</a></div></div></div>
<p>In North America, the settlers <a href="/wiki/Albert_Burgh" title="Albert Burgh">Albert Burgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Blommaert" title="Samuel Blommaert">Samuel Blommaert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Godijn" class="mw-redirect" title="Samuel Godijn">Samuel Godijn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johannes_de_Laet" class="mw-redirect" title="Johannes de Laet">Johannes de Laet</a> had little success with populating the colony of New Netherland, and to defend themselves against local Amerindians. Only <a href="/wiki/Kiliaen_van_Rensselaer_(merchant)" title="Kiliaen van Rensselaer (merchant)">Kiliaen Van Rensselaer</a> managed to maintain his settlement in the north along the Hudson. Samuel Blommaert secretly tried to secure his interests with the founding of the colony of <a href="/wiki/New_Sweden" title="New Sweden">New Sweden</a> on behalf of Sweden on the <a href="/wiki/Delaware_River" title="Delaware River">Delaware</a> in the south. The main focus of the WIC now went to Brazil.
</p><p>Only in 1630 did the West India Company manage to conquer a part of Brazil. In 1630, the colony of <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Brazil" title="Dutch Brazil">New Holland</a> (capital Mauritsstad, present-day <a href="/wiki/Recife" title="Recife">Recife</a>) was founded, taking over <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> possessions in Brazil. In the meantime, the war demanded so many of its forces that the Company had to operate under a permanent threat of bankruptcy.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In fact, the WIC went bankrupt in 1636 and all attempts at rehabilitation were doomed to failure.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pakhuiswic.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Pakhuiswic.JPG/220px-Pakhuiswic.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Pakhuiswic.JPG/330px-Pakhuiswic.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Pakhuiswic.JPG/440px-Pakhuiswic.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2980" data-file-height="2218" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pakhuiswic.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Warehouse of the WIC in Amsterdam</div></div></div>
<p>Because of the ongoing war in Brazil, the situation for the WIC in 1645, at the end of the charter, was very bad. An attempt to compensate the losses of the WIC with the profits of the VOC failed because the directors of the VOC did not want to.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Merging the two companies was not feasible. Amsterdam was not willing to help out, because it had too much interest in peace and healthy trade relations with Portugal. This indifferent attitude of Amsterdam was the main cause of the slow, half-hearted policy, which would eventually lead to losing the colony.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In 1647 the Company made a restart using 1.5 million guilders, capital of the VOC. The States General took responsibility for the warfare in Brazil.
</p><p>Due to the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> the seizing of Spanish ships was no longer allowed. Many merchants from Amsterdam and <a href="/wiki/Zeeland" title="Zeeland">Zeeland</a> decided to work with marine and merchants from <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gl%C3%BCckstadt" title="Glückstadt">Glückstadt</a> (then <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a>), <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and other countries. In 1649, the WIC obtained a monopoly on gold and enslaved Africans in the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Accra" title="Accra">Accra</a> (present-day <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a>). In 1662 there were contacts with the owners of the <a href="/wiki/Asiento" title="Asiento">Asiento</a>, which were obliged to deliver 24,000 enslaved Africans.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In 1663 and 1664 the WIC sold more enslaved Africans than the Portuguese and English together.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first West India Company suffered a long agony, and its end in 1674 was painless.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The reason that the WIC could drag on for twenty years was due to its valuable <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West African</a> possessions, due to its slaves.
</p>

<p>When the WIC could not repay its debts in 1674, the company was dissolved. But because of high demand for trade with the West (mainly <a href="/wiki/Slave_trade" class="mw-redirect" title="Slave trade">slave trade</a>), and the fact that still many colonies existed, it was decided to establish the Second Chartered West India Company (also called New West India Company) in 1675. This new company had the same trade area as the first. All ships, fortresses, etc. were taken over by the new company. The number of directors was reduced from 19 to 10, and the number of governors from 74 to 50. The new WIC had a capital that was slightly more than <span class="nowrap">6 million</span> guilders around 1679, which was largely supplied by the Amsterdam Chamber.
</p><p>From 1694 until 1700, the WIC waged a long conflict against the Eguafo Kingdom along the Gold Coast, present-day <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Komenda_Wars" title="Komenda Wars">Komenda Wars</a> drew in significant numbers of neighbouring African kingdoms and led to replacement of the gold trade with enslaved Africans.
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Anglo-Dutch_War" title="Fourth Anglo-Dutch War">Fourth Anglo-Dutch War</a>, it became apparent that the Dutch West India Company was no longer capable of defending its own colonies, as <a href="/wiki/Sint_Eustatius" title="Sint Eustatius">Sint Eustatius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berbice" title="Berbice">Berbice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Essequibo_(colony)" title="Essequibo (colony)">Essequibo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demerara" title="Demerara">Demerara</a>, and some forts on the Dutch Gold Coast were rapidly taken by the British. In 1791, the company's stock was bought by the Dutch government, and on 1 January 1792, all territories previously held by the Dutch West India Company reverted to the rule of the States General of the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">Dutch Republic</a>. Around 1800 there was an attempt to create a third West Indian Company, without any success.
</p>
