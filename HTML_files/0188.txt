	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Dual naming</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Dual naming</b> is the adoption of an official place name that combines two earlier names, usually to resolve a disagreement over which of the two individual names is most appropriate. Sometimes the two individual names are from different languages. In several countries dual naming is used where a native people and a colonial settler community have used different names, for example the <a href="/wiki/Denali_naming_dispute" class="mw-redirect" title="Denali naming dispute">Denali naming dispute</a> in the US.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, a dual naming policy is used to name landmarks that are of significance to the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australians" title="Indigenous Australians">Indigenous Australians</a>, but for which the most common name is European.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  For example, the landmark with the indigenous name <i><a href="/wiki/Uluru" title="Uluru">Uluru</a></i> and European name <i>Ayers Rock</i> is now officially named <i>Uluru / Ayers Rock</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Similarly, <a href="/wiki/List_of_dual_place_names_in_New_Zealand" title="List of dual place names in New Zealand">some places in New Zealand</a> have dual <a href="/wiki/Maori_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Maori language">Maori</a> and <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> names, such as <a href="/wiki/Aoraki/Mount_Cook" class="mw-redirect" title="Aoraki/Mount Cook">Aoraki/Mount Cook</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NZGB_frameworks_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NZGB_frameworks-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The practice of officially giving certain New Zealand places dual names began in the 1920s,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> but dual names have become much more common in the 1990s and 2000s, in part due to <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Waitangi_claims_and_settlements" title="Treaty of Waitangi claims and settlements">Treaty of Waitangi settlements</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NZGB_frameworks_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NZGB_frameworks-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>"Derry/Londonderry" has been used unofficially to circumvent the <a href="/wiki/Derry/Londonderry_name_dispute" title="Derry/Londonderry name dispute">Derry/Londonderry name dispute</a>, in which <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationalism" title="Irish nationalism">Irish nationalists</a> used "Derry" and <a href="/wiki/Ulster_unionism" class="mw-redirect" title="Ulster unionism">Ulster unionists</a> use "Londonderry" for the <a href="/wiki/Derry" title="Derry">city</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_Londonderry" title="County Londonderry">county</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. The "Derry stroke Londonderry" spoken form of this has in turn engendered the city's nickname "Stroke City".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, the city of Cluj was renamed <a href="/wiki/Cluj-Napoca" title="Cluj-Napoca">Cluj-Napoca</a> for political reasons in the 1970s, as the communist government wanted to emphasize the city's Roman origins.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another example of the phenomenon can be seed in the name of the capital of the <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">Basque Country</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vitoria-Gasteiz" title="Vitoria-Gasteiz">Vitoria-Gasteiz</a>. This combines the city's <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> name of Vitoria and <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">Basque</a> name of Gasteiz.
</p><p>The official names of bilingual areas of <a href="/wiki/Alsace" title="Alsace">Alsace</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> also apply. For instance, the German and French Swiss town of <a href="/wiki/Biel/Bienne" title="Biel/Bienne">Biel/Bienne</a> is the combination of its German name (Biel) and its French name (Bienne).
</p><p>In Finland, many towns have two names, one in Finnish and one in Swedish (the two official languages of the country). The two names are considered equally correct but are not used as a formal duality of names.
</p><p>A special problem occurs when the landmark lies on the border between two (or more) countries, for example <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a> has several different locally-used names.
</p>
