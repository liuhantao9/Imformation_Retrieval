<p>The <b>Wayana</b> (alternate names: <b>Ajana</b>, <b>Uaiana</b>, <b>Alucuyana</b>, <b>Guaque</b>, <b>Ojana</b>, <b>Orcocoyana</b>, <b>Pirixi</b>, <b>Urukuena</b>, <b>Waiano</b> etc.) are a <a href="/wiki/Carib_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Carib languages">Carib-speaking</a> people located in the south-eastern part of the <a href="/wiki/Guiana_highlands" class="mw-redirect" title="Guiana highlands">Guiana highlands</a>, a region divided between <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surinam" class="mw-redirect" title="Surinam">Surinam</a>, and <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>. In 1980, when the last census took place, the Wayana numbered some 1,500 individuals, of which 150 in <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, among the <a href="/wiki/Apalai" class="mw-redirect" title="Apalai">Apalai</a>, 400 in <a href="/wiki/Surinam" class="mw-redirect" title="Surinam">Surinam</a>, and 1,000 in <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>, along the <a href="/wiki/Maroni_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Maroni River">Maroni River</a>. About half of them still speak <a href="/wiki/Wayana_language" title="Wayana language">their original language</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wayana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to both oral tradition and descriptions by 20th century European explorers, the Wayana emerged fairly recently as a distinctive group; contemporary Wayana are considered an amalgation of smaller ethnic groups such as the Upului, Opagwana, and Kukuyana.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoven200659_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoven200659-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In the eighteenth century, the ancestors of the Wayana lived along the <a href="/wiki/Paru_River" title="Paru River">Paru</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jari_River" title="Jari River">Jari</a> rivers in contemporary Brazil, and along the upper tributaries of the <a href="/wiki/Oyapock_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Oyapock River">Oyapock</a> river, which nowadays forms the border between French Guiana and Brazil.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoven200663_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoven200663-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the late 18th century, the ancestors of the Wayana were involved in an almost continuous military struggle with <a href="/wiki/Tupi_people" title="Tupi people">Tupi peoples</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/Wayampi" title="Wayampi">Wayampi</a>, which drove them across the <a href="/wiki/Tumuk_Humak_Mountains" title="Tumuk Humak Mountains">Tumuk Humak Mountains</a> to the upper tributaries of the <a href="/wiki/Litani_(Maroni_tributary)" title="Litani (Maroni tributary)">Litani</a> river.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoven200663,_67_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoven200663,_67-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Around the same time, the <a href="/wiki/Aluku" title="Aluku">Aluku</a> maroons, who had fled plantations in Suriname, were driven up the Litani river by Dutch colonial forces aided by <a href="/wiki/Ndyuka_people" title="Ndyuka people">Ndyuka</a> maroons, who had settled for peace with the colonial authorities in return for military assistance against "incursions" from new maroon groups. From that moment on, an intensive trade relationship developed between the Wayana and the Aluku.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoven200677_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoven200677-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Over time, the Wayana migrated with the Aluku further downstream the Litani and Lawa rivers to end up in their contemporary position. In 1865, the Ndyuka <a href="/wiki/Granman" title="Granman">granman</a> Alabi invited a Wayana group still living along the Paru river in Brazil to join them along the <a href="/wiki/Tapanahony_River" title="Tapanahony River">Tapanahony</a> river in Suriname, probably inspired by the arrangement with the Wayana that the Aluku had. This particular group still lives in villages along the Tapanahony and Palumeu rivers.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoven200677_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoven200677-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society_and_culture">Society and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wayana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Society and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wayana society is characterized by a rather low degree of <a href="/wiki/Social_stratification" title="Social stratification">social stratification</a>. Villages often comprise not more than one <a href="/wiki/Extended_family" title="Extended family">extended family</a> and are rather loosely linked to their neighbouring villages by kinship ties, marital exchanges, shared rituals and trade. Missionaries and representatives of the state have only partially succeeded in grouping the Wayana together in larger settlements, and despite the fact that the Wayana are not as nomadic as before, villages are by no means permanent, and are often abandoned after the death of a leader.<sup id="cite_ref-PIB_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PIB-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Villages are often led by a <a href="/wiki/Shaman" class="mw-redirect" title="Shaman">shaman</a> or <i>pïyai</i>, who mediate Wayana contact with the world of spirits and deities, act as healers, and who are consulted in matters concerning hunting and fishing. Many Wayana villages still feature a community house or <i>tukusipan</i>.
</p>
<h3><span id=".C3.8Bputop"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Ëputop">Ëputop</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wayana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ëputop">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Coming_of_age" title="Coming of age">Coming of age</a> was for a long time associated with a ritual called <i>ëputop</i> or <i>maraké</i>, in which a wicker frame full of stinging ants or wasps was applied to the bodies of adolescent boys and girls, who emerged from the ceremony as adult men and women. While older Wayana still to a degree define their Wayanahood by the number of <i>ëputop</i> they underwent during their lifetime, many younger Wayana reject the necessity of undergoing <i>ëputop</i> to become a valued member of society. As a result, few <i>ëputop</i> ceremonies occur today.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoven2006147-156_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoven2006147-156-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> One of the more recent <i>ëputop</i> ceremonies took place in 2004 in the village of <a href="/wiki/Talhuwen" title="Talhuwen">Talhuwen</a>, organized by Aïmawale Opoya, grandson of Wayana leader Janomalë, in consultation with French film director Jean-Philippe Isel, who made a documentary about the ritual.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoven2006154_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoven2006154-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Blada.com_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blada.com-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Télérama.fr_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Télérama.fr-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In spite of its demise, <i>ëputop</i> was listed on the inventory of <a href="/wiki/Intangible_cultural_heritage" title="Intangible cultural heritage">intangible cultural heritage</a> drawn up by the French <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Culture_(France)" title="Ministry of Culture (France)">Ministry of Culture</a> in 2011.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_organisation">Political organisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wayana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Political organisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before contact with missionaries and state representatives, the Wayana did not recognise a form of leadership that transcended the village level. The Surinamese, French, and Brazilian states preferred to centralise their dealings with the Wayana, however, and for this purpose installed captains, head captains and <a href="/wiki/Granman" title="Granman">granman</a> among the Wayana leaders. As the concept of a paramount chief goes against Wayana ideas of political organisation, the authority of these chiefs beyond their own villages is often limited.<sup id="cite_ref-PIB2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PIB2-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoven243_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoven243-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Suriname, <a href="/wiki/Kananoe_Apetina" title="Kananoe Apetina">Kananoe Apetina</a> was made "head captain" of the Wayana on the Tapanahony river in 1937, while Janomalë was made "head captain" of the Wayana on the Lawa and Litani rivers in 1938. After the death of Janomalë in 1958, Anapaikë was installed as his successor, and served as the leader of the Wayana on the Surinamese side of the Lawa river until he died in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoven168_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoven168-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Kananu Apetina died in 1975 and was succeeded by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aptuk_Noewahe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Aptuk Noewahe (page does not exist)">Aptuk Noewahe</a>, who is currently recognised by the Surinamese government as the <a href="/wiki/Granman" title="Granman">granman</a> of all Wayana in Suriname. The current head captain on the Lawa river is Ipomadi Pelenapïn, who was installed in August 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBoven243_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBoven243-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The current <a href="/wiki/Granman" title="Granman">granman</a> of the Wayana in French Guiana is Amaipotï, son of first granman Twenkë, who resides in the village of <a href="/wiki/Kulumuli" title="Kulumuli">Kulumuli</a>.
</p>
