<p><b>Gillan</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> band formed in 1978 by <a href="/wiki/Deep_Purple" title="Deep Purple">Deep Purple</a> vocalist <a href="/wiki/Ian_Gillan" title="Ian Gillan">Ian Gillan</a>. Gillan was one of the <a href="/wiki/Hard_rock" title="Hard rock">hard rock</a> bands to make a significant impact and commercial success in the United Kingdom during the early 1980s, with 1 gold and 4 silver albums. They sold over 10 million LP's.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gillan_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1978:_The_Ian_Gillan_new_band">1978: The Ian Gillan new band</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gillan_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1978: The Ian Gillan new band">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In July 1978 Ian Gillan had become dissatisfied with the <a href="/wiki/Jazz_fusion" title="Jazz fusion">jazz fusion</a> style of his band <a href="/wiki/Ian_Gillan_Band" title="Ian Gillan Band">Ian Gillan Band</a> and dissolved it, retaining only keyboard player <a href="/wiki/Colin_Towns" title="Colin Towns">Colin Towns</a>, and formed this new band entitled Gillan. He added Steve Byrd on guitar, <a href="/wiki/Liam_Genockey" title="Liam Genockey">Liam Genockey</a> on drums and <a href="/wiki/John_McCoy_(musician)" title="John McCoy (musician)">John McCoy</a> on bass, and initially pursued a <a href="/wiki/Progressive_rock" title="Progressive rock">progressive rock</a> direction, releasing their eponymous debut in September 1978, although they could only get a record deal in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>. This recording has subsequently become more widely available as <i><a href="/wiki/Gillan_(album)" title="Gillan (album)">The Japanese Album</a></i> as a CD re-issue by RPM Records in 1994. However, the RPM CD issue replaces the original opening instrumental "Second Sight" with another instrumental, "Street Theatre". Genocky was unable to commit to the band beyond the recording of the album and the band's live debut at the <a href="/wiki/Reading_and_Leeds_Festivals" title="Reading and Leeds Festivals">Reading Festival</a> in 1978.  He was replaced for the subsequent tour by <a href="/wiki/Pete_Barnacle" title="Pete Barnacle">Pete Barnacle</a>.
</p><p>At Christmas 1978, Ian Gillan turned down an offer from <a href="/wiki/Ritchie_Blackmore" title="Ritchie Blackmore">Ritchie Blackmore</a> to join <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_(English_band)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rainbow (English band)">Rainbow</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> but Blackmore did make a guest appearance for Gillan at their Christmas show. It was the first time Ian Gillan and Blackmore had performed together since 1973.
</p>
<h3><span id="1979.E2.80.931981:_The_Glory_Era"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1979–1981:_The_Glory_Era">1979–1981: The Glory Era</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gillan_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1979–1981: The Glory Era">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The album was sufficiently successful to attract more attention and in 1979 the band secured a European deal with Acrobat Records. Before a new album was recorded, Byrd was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Torm%C3%A9" title="Bernie Tormé">Bernie Tormé</a> and Barnacle by drummer <a href="/wiki/Mick_Underwood" title="Mick Underwood">Mick Underwood</a>, Ian Gillan's former colleague in <a href="/wiki/Episode_Six" title="Episode Six">Episode Six</a>. Torme's "screaming guitar" sound fundamentally altered the dynamics and Gillan took a more <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">heavy metal</a> direction. This line-up's first album was released as <i><a href="/wiki/Mr._Universe_(album)" title="Mr. Universe (album)">Mr. Universe</a></i> and contained many re-worked songs from <i>The Japanese Album</i>. The album went straight into the UK album charts but stalled as Acrobat Records went bankrupt. This led to a multi-album deal with <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Records" title="Virgin Records">Virgin Records</a>.
</p><p>Meanwhile, in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> a version of <i>Mr. Universe</i> was released with a different track selection to avoid repeating the <i>Gillan</i> tracks used on the <i>Mr. Universe</i> album. Several of the alternative tracks are included on the <i><a href="/wiki/Gillan_(album)" title="Gillan (album)">Japanese Album</a></i> release.
</p><p>The band caught the rise of the <a href="/wiki/New_wave_of_British_heavy_metal" title="New wave of British heavy metal">NWOBHM</a> at just the right time and the group gained popularity in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.
</p><p>In 1980 Gillan reached the peak of their success, releasing the album <i><a href="/wiki/Glory_Road_(album)" title="Glory Road (album)">Glory Road</a></i>, with initial copies containing the free album <i>For Gillan Fans Only</i>. However, the band remained unknown in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> and were unable to raise any interest there despite a long and difficult 1980 tour there.
</p><p>By 1981 the band members had become disgruntled that their European and Japanese success was not translating into increased financial rewards,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and after the <i><a href="/wiki/Future_Shock_(Gillan_album)" title="Future Shock (Gillan album)">Future Shock</a></i> album, whilst on tour in Germany, Torme left just before the band were due to fly back to the UK to appear on <a href="/wiki/Top_of_the_Pops" title="Top of the Pops">Top of the Pops</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1982:_Struggles_and_final_shows">1982: Struggles and final shows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gillan_(band)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1982: Struggles and final shows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tormé was replaced by <a href="/wiki/White_Spirit_(band)" title="White Spirit (band)">White Spirit</a> guitarist <a href="/wiki/Janick_Gers" title="Janick Gers">Janick Gers</a> (who would later join <a href="/wiki/Iron_Maiden" title="Iron Maiden">Iron Maiden</a>) and this line-up released the live/studio double album <i><a href="/wiki/Double_Trouble_(Gillan_album)" title="Double Trouble (Gillan album)">Double Trouble</a></i> at the end of 1981. In August 1982, the final album <i><a href="/wiki/Magic_(Gillan_album)" title="Magic (Gillan album)">Magic</a></i> followed. By this time, tension over money had reached fever pitch and Ian Gillan needed time to have nodes removed from his vocal cords.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> After the <i>Magic</i> tour, the band performed a final show at the Wembley Arena on 17 December, and then Ian Gillan dissolved the group while he underwent surgery. He then accepted an offer to front <a href="/wiki/Black_Sabbath" title="Black Sabbath">Black Sabbath</a> to the incredulity of the Gillan band members, particularly McCoy, and the acrimony remains to the present day.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="https://www.loudersound.com/features/was-gillans-magic-a-hidden-concept-album-about-the-end-of-the-band">[1]</a>
</p><p>McCoy subsequently released compilations of studio out-takes to which he had the rights, known as <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Gillan_Tapes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Gillan Tapes (page does not exist)">The Gillan Tapes</a></i>, and ensured that revenues were distributed fairly amongst the band.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Bernie Tormé and John McCoy collaborated on the GMT band project, releasing two albums in 2006 and 2009.
</p><p>According to at least one interview, Gillan did not have nodes removed as British doctors recommended, as he was afraid that would adversely affect his voice.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> A German doctor diagnosed infected, enlarged tonsils with nodes on them, and Gillan had a history of that. He then had a complete tonsillectomy instead. Prior to surgery, he asked the German doctor if the surgery would affect his voice. The doctor said "Yes it will make it better, since the sound was having to come around the tonsils before then".
</p>
