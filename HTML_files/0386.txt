	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Tughra</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:249px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tugra_Mahmuds_II.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/96/Tugra_Mahmuds_II.gif" decoding="async" width="247" height="188" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="247" data-file-height="188" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption">The tughra of <a href="/wiki/Sultan" title="Sultan">Sultan</a> <a href="/wiki/Mahmud_II" title="Mahmud II">Mahmud II</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a>. It reads in <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>  "<span style="color:#990000;">Mahmud</span> <span style="color:#000099;">Khan</span> <span style="color:#009900;">son of</span> <span style="color:#999900;">Abdülhamid</span>, <span style="color:#990099;">forever</span> <span style="color:#009999;">victorious</span>".  Written out:  <span class="nowrap"><span lang="ar" title="Arabic language text"><span style="color:#990000">محمود</span> <span style="color:#000099">خان</span> <span style="color:#009900">بن</span> <span style="color:#999900">عبد الحميد</span> <span style="color:#009999">مظفر</span> <span style="color:#990099">دائماً</span></span></span> (<i><span style="color:#990000">Mahmūd</span> <span style="color:#000099">Ḫān</span> <span style="color:#009900">bin</span> <span style="color:#999900">Abdulhamīd</span> <span style="color:#009999">muẓaffar</span> <span style="color:#990099">dāʾimān</span></i>).</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>tughra</b> (<a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turkish_language" title="Ottoman Turkish language">Ottoman Turkish</a>: <span lang="ota" dir="rtl">طغرا</span>&#8206;, <small>romanized:&#160;</small><i lang="ota-Latn" title="Ottoman Turkish-language romanization">tuğrâ</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Calligraphy" title="Calligraphy">calligraphic</a> <a href="/wiki/Monogram" title="Monogram">monogram</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seal_(device)" class="mw-redirect" title="Seal (device)">seal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Signature" title="Signature">signature</a> of a sultan that was affixed to all official documents and correspondence. It was also carved on his seal and stamped on the coins minted during his reign. Very elaborate decorated versions were created for important documents that were also works of art in the tradition of <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_illumination" title="Ottoman illumination">Ottoman illumination</a>, such as the example of <a href="/wiki/Suleiman_the_Magnificent" title="Suleiman the Magnificent">Suleiman the Magnificent</a> in the gallery below.
</p><p>The tughra was designed at the beginning of the sultan's reign and drawn by the court <a href="/wiki/Calligrapher" class="mw-redirect" title="Calligrapher">calligrapher</a> or <i><a href="/wiki/Nisanci" class="mw-redirect" title="Nisanci">nişancı</a></i> on written documents. The first tughra belonged to <a href="/wiki/Orhan_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Orhan I">Orhan I</a> (1284–1359), the second ruler of the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman Empire</a> and it evolved until it reached the classical form in the tughra of Sultan <a href="/wiki/Suleiman_the_Magnificent" title="Suleiman the Magnificent">Suleiman the Magnificent</a> (1494–1566).<sup id="cite_ref-bm_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bm-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Tughras served a purpose similar to the <a href="/wiki/Cartouche" title="Cartouche">cartouche</a> in ancient Egypt or the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Cypher" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Cypher">Royal Cypher</a> of British monarchs. Every Ottoman sultan had his own individual tughra.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Visual_elements_of_a_tughra">Visual elements of a tughra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tughra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Visual elements of a tughra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2><p>
The tughra has a characteristic form, two loops on the left side, three vertical lines in the middle,  stacked writing on the bottom and two extensions to the right. Each of these elements has a specific meaning, and together they make up the form that is easily recognizable as a tughra. </p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tugra_decomposed.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Tugra_decomposed.png/220px-Tugra_decomposed.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="158" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e2/Tugra_decomposed.png 1.5x" data-file-width="304" data-file-height="218" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tugra_decomposed.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Visual Elements of a Tughra</div></div></div>
<p>The name of the sultan is written out in the bottom section, called a <i>sere</i>. Depending on the period, this name can be as simple as <a href="/wiki/Orhan_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Orhan I">Orhan</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Osman_I" title="Osman I">Osman</a> in the first tughra in 1326. In later periods honorifics and prayers are also added to the name of the tughra holder and his father.
</p><p>The loops to the left of the tughra are called <i>beyze</i>, from Arabic meaning <i>egg</i>. Some interpretations of tughra design claim that the beyzes are supposed to symbolize the two seas the sultans held sway over: the outer larger loop signifying the Mediterranean and the inner, smaller loop signifying the Black Sea.
</p><p>The vertical lines on the top of the tughra are called <i>tuğ</i>, or flagstaff. The three tugs signify independence. The S-shaped lines crossing the tugs are called <i>zülfe</i> and they, together with the tops of the tugs that also look to the right, signify that the winds blow from the east to the west, the traditional movement of the Ottomans.
</p><p>The lines to the right of the tughra are called <i>hançer</i> and signify a sword, symbol of power and might.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_tughras">Other tughras</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tughra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other tughras">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the tughra is largely identified with the Ottoman Sultans, they have also sometimes been used in other states, such as the <a href="/wiki/Qajar_dynasty" title="Qajar dynasty">Qajar dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Safavid_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Safavid Empire">Safavid Empire</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Khanate_of_Kazan" title="Khanate of Kazan">Khanate of Kazan</a>. Later, tughras were used among the <a href="/wiki/Tatars" title="Tatars">Tatars</a> of <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Russia" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Russia">Imperial Russia</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:201px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Naser_al-Din_Shah_stamp.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c8/Naser_al-Din_Shah_stamp.jpg" decoding="async" width="199" height="205" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="199" data-file-height="205" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Naser_al-Din_Shah_stamp.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tughra of <a href="/wiki/Naser_al-Din_Shah_Qajar" title="Naser al-Din Shah Qajar">Naser al-Din Shah Qajar</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mogultughra.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Mogultughra.jpg/175px-Mogultughra.jpg" decoding="async" width="175" height="123" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a2/Mogultughra.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="251" data-file-height="176" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mogultughra.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The official imperial Tughra of the <a href="/wiki/Mughal_Empire" title="Mughal Empire">Mughal Empire</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Mughal_Emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Mughal Emperor">Mughal Emperors</a> are also known to have used calligraphic symbols, alongside the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottomans</a>, the Mughal "Tughra" was circular in shape with three points at its tip, beside the calligraphic signature of the emperor.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research#Primary,_secondary_and_tertiary_sources" title="Wikipedia:No original research"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable secondary sources. (November 2011)">non-primary source needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Afghan currency notes from 1919 to 1936 had the tughra present as well. Pakistan had the tughra on it coins from 1947 till 1974; both of these are present in the State Bank Museum in Karachi. The nawab of Bahawalpur and the Nizam of Hyderabad had tugras on their coinage as well. The flowing lines could symbolize the wide reach of Suleyman’s rule and his future conquests. It could also signify the spread of Islam to other realms beyond the Ottoman Empire.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:An_imperial_order,_farman,_from_the_time_of_Shah_%27Alam_II,_dated_1776.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/An_imperial_order%2C_farman%2C_from_the_time_of_Shah_%27Alam_II%2C_dated_1776.jpg/150px-An_imperial_order%2C_farman%2C_from_the_time_of_Shah_%27Alam_II%2C_dated_1776.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="352" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/An_imperial_order%2C_farman%2C_from_the_time_of_Shah_%27Alam_II%2C_dated_1776.jpg/225px-An_imperial_order%2C_farman%2C_from_the_time_of_Shah_%27Alam_II%2C_dated_1776.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/An_imperial_order%2C_farman%2C_from_the_time_of_Shah_%27Alam_II%2C_dated_1776.jpg/300px-An_imperial_order%2C_farman%2C_from_the_time_of_Shah_%27Alam_II%2C_dated_1776.jpg 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="1201" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:An_imperial_order,_farman,_from_the_time_of_Shah_%27Alam_II,_dated_1776.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Firman issued by the <a href="/wiki/Mughal_Emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Mughal Emperor">Mughal Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Shah_Alam_II" title="Shah Alam II">Shah Alam II</a>; containing the official <a href="/wiki/Mughal_Empire" title="Mughal Empire">Mughal</a> "tughra" in <a href="/wiki/Black" title="Black">black</a> ink alongside the calligraphic signature of <a href="/wiki/Shah_Alam_II" title="Shah Alam II">Shah Alam II</a> in <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">red</a>.</div></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-imperial_interpretations">Post-imperial interpretations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tughra&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post-imperial interpretations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are modern artists of <a href="/wiki/Calligraphy" title="Calligraphy">calligraphy</a> that use the characteristic tughra form today. Examples are the tughras of Russian president <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Putin" title="Vladimir Putin">Vladimir Putin</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Akihito" title="Akihito">Akihito</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Japan" title="Emperor of Japan">Emperor of Japan</a>, created by artist Vladimir Popov.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
