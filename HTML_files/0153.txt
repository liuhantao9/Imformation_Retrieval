	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Dougla</h1>

<p><b>Dougla</b> (also <b>Dugla</b> or <b>Dogla</b>) is a word used by people especially in <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> to describe people who are of mixed <a href="/wiki/Afro-Caribbean" title="Afro-Caribbean">African</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indo-Caribbean" class="mw-redirect" title="Indo-Caribbean">Indian</a> descent.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_etymology">Origins and etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dougla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins and etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word originated from <b>doogala</b> (<b>दुगला</b>), which is a <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Hindustani" title="Caribbean Hindustani">Caribbean Hindustani</a> word that may mean "many", "much" or "a mix".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Some of the connotations of the word such as <a href="/wiki/Illegitimacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Illegitimacy">bastard</a>, illegitimate and son of a whore are secondary and limited to sections of <a href="/wiki/North_India" title="North India">North India</a> where the term may have originated.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Originally, in the West Indies the word was only used for Afro-Indo <a href="/wiki/Multiracial" title="Multiracial">mixed race</a>, despite its origin as a word used to describe inter-caste mixing.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dougla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are sporadic records of Indo-Euro interracial relationships, both forced and unforced, before any ethnic mixing of the African and Indian variety. Women were a minority among earlier Indian migrants. Many did not take the <a href="/wiki/Indian_indenture_system" title="Indian indenture system">voyage across the Atlantic</a> for several reasons, among them the fear of <a href="/wiki/Exploitation_of_labour" title="Exploitation of labour">exploitation</a> and the assumption that they were unfit for labor.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Socio-religious practice played a part as <a href="/wiki/Purusharthas" class="mw-redirect" title="Purusharthas">religious practices</a> are paramount to the <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hindu</a> religion and preservation of the religion and culture was of extreme importance to the indentured laborers. Association with those outside the community who engaged in <a href="/wiki/Adharma" title="Adharma">Adharmic</a> practices was considered to compromise the purity of the race, religion and culture, seen as necessary for survival in the foreign land.
</p><p>The second reason was socio-economic. The <a href="/wiki/Indian_Arrival_Day" title="Indian Arrival Day">arrival</a> of Indians to the <a href="/wiki/British_West_Indies" title="British West Indies">British Caribbean</a> was not meant to be permanent. For most of the Indian immigrants, the aim was to gain material wealth under contract, then return to their respective homelands. The Dougla represented the postponement and deferral of that goal if not rendering it completely impossible, being a living symbol of departure from cultural custom <a href="/wiki/Jatis" class="mw-redirect" title="Jatis">jatis</a>.
</p><p>The third reason was <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a>. Trinidad, as well as other territories in the Caribbean, had a dynamic of <a href="/wiki/Power_(sociology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Power (sociology)">power</a> based on the <a href="/wiki/Pigmentocracy" class="mw-redirect" title="Pigmentocracy">colour of one's skin</a>. This reinforced the rules by which Indo society functioned in excluding the Dougla. Other Indo-based types of miscegenation (Indo-Chinese (<a href="/wiki/Chindian" title="Chindian">Chindian</a>), Indo-<a href="/wiki/Island_Caribs" title="Island Caribs">Carib</a>) tended to identify as one of the older, unmixed ethnic strains on the island: Afro, Indo or Euro or <a href="/wiki/Passing_(sociology)" title="Passing (sociology)">passing as one of them</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Trinidad_culture">In Trinidad culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dougla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In Trinidad culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One <a href="/wiki/Calypso_music" title="Calypso music">calypsonian</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mighty_Dougla" title="Mighty Dougla">Mighty Dougla</a> (Clatis Ali), described the predicament of Douglas in the 1960s:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><div class="poem">
<p>If they sending Indians to India<i><br /></i>
<i>And Africans back to Africa</i><br />
<i>Well somebody please just tell me</i><br />
<i>Where they sending poor me?</i><br />
<i>I am neither one nor the other</i><br />
<i>Six of one, half a dozen of the other</i><br />
<i>So if they sending all these people back home for true</i><br />
<i>They got to split me in two</i>
</p>
</div>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Throughout_the_Caribbean">Throughout the Caribbean</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dougla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Throughout the Caribbean">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The biggest population of Dougla peoples, second (and if not on par), with those in Trinidad and Tobago are those in <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>. <a href="/wiki/Afro-Guyanese" title="Afro-Guyanese">Afro-Guyanese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indo-Guyanese" title="Indo-Guyanese">Indo-Guyanese</a> make up half of the Guyanese population, and Douglas number 15% of the country's demographics.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/French_West_Indies" title="French West Indies">French West Indies</a> (<a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a>), mixed Afro-Indian people used to be called Batazendien or Chapé-Coolie, those who have escaped the disagreeable Indian condition by becoming hybrid.
</p><p>In the French West Indies they are now treated in a more positive way by other categories of the population and no longer face the cruel existential dilemma of post-slavery times. The uncommon phenomenon of mutual acceptance and cultural exchange now attained, called by some "the Guadeloupe Model", has widely contributed to the rare harmony of the multiracial French West Indian communities.
</p>
