<p><b>Sir Thomas Innes of Learney</b> <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Knight_Grand_Cross_of_the_Royal_Victorian_Order" class="mw-redirect" title="Knight Grand Cross of the Royal Victorian Order">GCVO</a>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Writer_to_the_Signet" class="mw-redirect" title="Writer to the Signet">WS</a></span> (1893–1971) was <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lyon" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Lyon">Lord Lyon</a> from 1945 to 1969, after having been <a href="/wiki/Carrick_Pursuivant" title="Carrick Pursuivant">Carrick Pursuivant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Albany_Herald" title="Albany Herald">Albany Herald</a> in the 1920s and 1930s. He was a very active Lord Lyon, strongly promoting his views of what his office was through his writings and pronouncements in <a href="/wiki/Court_of_the_Lord_Lyon" title="Court of the Lord Lyon">his Court</a>. In 1950, he convinced the <a href="/wiki/Scots_Law_Times" title="Scots Law Times">Scots Law Times</a> to start publishing the decisions made in Lyon Court. By ruling on uncontested petitions, he was able to expound many of his theories in court but not under review of his superior court, and get them published in the judicial record. His treatise, <i>Scots Heraldry</i>, was first published in 1934 when he was Carrick Pursuivant; then a second, enlarged edition came out in 1956, and it has practically eclipsed earlier works on the subject. Following his retirement as Lord Lyon in 1969, he was appointed <a href="/wiki/Marchmont_Herald" title="Marchmont Herald">Marchmont Herald</a>.
</p><p>Innes of Learney's writings contain a number of theories which, at a time when English armorial law had come to dominate even Scottish heraldry, may have seemed quite novel, despite his claims that they were grounded in Scotland's feudal past. Most notable is the claim that a grant of arms in Scotland confers what he calls "noblesse" and equates with <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobility</a> in the original sense, namely basic untitled nobility possessed by everyone noble, from Gentleman to Duke – though the word is nowadays generally taken to mean exclusively <a href="/wiki/The_Peerage" class="mw-redirect" title="The Peerage">the Peerage</a>, which is why the French word <a href="/wiki/Noblesse" title="Noblesse">noblesse</a> seemed to him a better term. There are also other claims, such as his right to decide disputes over chiefships of clans or branches of clans, his right to decide disputes of <a href="/wiki/Order_of_precedence" title="Order of precedence">precedence</a>, his right to confer nobility to non-physical persons such as corporations or associations, etc. These rights are still (2007) being exercised by the Court of the Lord Lyon. As a jurist, in 'Scots Heraldry' and in his revision of Adam's <i>The Clans, Septs and Regiments of the Scottish Highlands</i> as well as in <i>The Tartans of the Clans and Families of Scotland</i> he offers evidence from ancient legal documents as well as more recent parliament and court decisions to support his position.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
