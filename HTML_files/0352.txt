	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Drainage basin</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

<p>A <b>drainage basin</b> is any area of land where <a href="/wiki/Precipitation" title="Precipitation">precipitation</a> collects and drains off into a common outlet, such as into a <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bay" title="Bay">bay</a>, or other <a href="/wiki/Body_of_water" title="Body of water">body of water</a>. The drainage basin includes all the <a href="/wiki/Surface_water" title="Surface water">surface water</a> from <a href="/wiki/Surface_runoff" title="Surface runoff">rain runoff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Snowmelt" title="Snowmelt">snowmelt</a>, and nearby streams that run downslope towards the shared outlet, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> underneath the earth's surface.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Drainage basins connect into other drainage basins at lower elevations in a <a href="/wiki/Strahler_number" title="Strahler number">hierarchical pattern</a>, with smaller <b>sub-drainage basins</b>, which in turn drain into another common outlet.<sup id="cite_ref-whatis_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whatis-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Other terms used interchangeably with <i>drainage basin</i> are <b>catchment area</b>, <b>catchment basin</b>, <b>drainage area</b>, <b>river basin</b>, <b>water basin</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and <b>impluvium</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  In <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, the term <i><b>watershed</b></i> is commonly used to mean a drainage basin, though in other English-speaking countries, it is used only in its original sense, that of a <a href="/wiki/Drainage_divide" title="Drainage divide">drainage divide</a>.
</p><p>In a closed drainage basin, or <a href="/wiki/Endorheic_basin" title="Endorheic basin">endorheic basin</a>, the water converges to a single point inside the basin, known as a <a href="/wiki/Sink_(geography)" title="Sink (geography)">sink</a>, which may be a permanent lake, a <a href="/wiki/Dry_lake" title="Dry lake">dry lake</a>, or a point where surface water is <a href="/wiki/Losing_stream" title="Losing stream">lost underground</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-virginia_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-virginia-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The drainage basin acts as a <a href="/wiki/Funnel" title="Funnel">funnel</a> by collecting all the water within the area covered by the basin and channelling it to a single point. Each drainage basin is separated topographically from adjacent basins by a perimeter, the <a href="/wiki/Drainage_divide" title="Drainage divide">drainage divide</a>, making up a succession of higher geographical features (such as a <a href="/wiki/Ridge" title="Ridge">ridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hill" title="Hill">hill</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountains</a>) forming a barrier.
</p><p>Drainage basins are similar but not identical to <a href="/wiki/Hydrological_code" title="Hydrological code">hydrologic units</a>, which are drainage areas delineated so as to nest into a multi-level hierarchical <a href="/wiki/Drainage_system_(geomorphology)" title="Drainage system (geomorphology)">drainage system</a>. Hydrologic units are defined to allow multiple inlets, outlets, or sinks. In a strict sense, all drainage basins are hydrologic units but not all hydrologic units are drainage basins.<sup id="cite_ref-virginia_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-virginia-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean_basins">Ocean basins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drainage_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ocean basins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following is a list of the major ocean basins:
</p>
<ul><li>About 48.7% of the world's land drains to the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, surface water drains to the Atlantic via the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lawrence_River" title="Saint Lawrence River">Saint Lawrence River</a> and <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> basins, the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">Eastern Seaboard</a> of the United States, the <a href="/wiki/Maritimes" class="mw-redirect" title="Maritimes">Canadian Maritimes</a>, and most of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a>. Nearly all of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> east of the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> also drains to the Atlantic, as does most of <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a> and the greatest portion of western <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">Sub-Saharan Africa</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara" title="Western Sahara">Western Sahara</a> and part of <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>. The two major <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_sea_(oceanography)" title="Mediterranean sea (oceanography)">mediterranean seas</a> of the world also flow to the Atlantic:
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> basin includes most of the U.S. interior between the <a href="/wiki/Appalachian_Mountains" title="Appalachian Mountains">Appalachian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rocky_Mountains" title="Rocky Mountains">Rocky Mountains</a>, a small part of the Canadian provinces of <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>, eastern <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>, the islands of the Caribbean and the Gulf, and a small part of northern South America.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">Mediterranean Sea</a> basin includes much of <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, east-central <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a> (through the <a href="/wiki/Nile_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Nile River">Nile River</a>), <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">Southern</a>, Central, and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">Eastern Europe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, and the coastal areas of <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>.</li></ul></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">Arctic Ocean</a> drains most of <a href="/wiki/Western_Canada" title="Western Canada">Western</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Canada" title="Northern Canada">Northern Canada</a> east of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Divide_of_the_Americas" title="Continental Divide of the Americas">Continental Divide</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/North_Dakota" title="North Dakota">North Dakota</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Dakota" title="South Dakota">South Dakota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a> in the United States, the north shore of the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Scandinavian peninsula">Scandinavian peninsula</a> in Europe, central and northern Russia, and parts of <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">Kazakhstan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, which totals to about 17% of the world's land.</li>
<li>Just over 13% of the land in the world drains to the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. Its basin includes much of China, eastern and southeastern Russia, Japan, the <a href="/wiki/Korean_Peninsula" title="Korean Peninsula">Korean Peninsula</a>, most of Indochina, Indonesia and Malaysia, the Philippines, all of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific Islands">Pacific Islands</a>, the northeast coast of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, and Canada and the United States west of the Continental Divide (including most of Alaska), as well as western Central America and South America west of the Andes.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>'s drainage basin also comprises about 13% of Earth's land. It drains the eastern coast of Africa, the coasts of the <a href="/wiki/Red_Sea" title="Red Sea">Red Sea</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>, Burma, and most of Australia.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">Southern Ocean</a> drains <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>. Antarctica comprises approximately eight percent of the Earth's land.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Largest_river_basins">Largest river basins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drainage_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Largest river basins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The five largest river basins (by area), from largest to smallest, are the basins of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon</a> (7M km<sup>2</sup>), the <a href="/wiki/Congo_River" title="Congo River">Congo</a> (4M km<sup>2</sup>), the <a href="/wiki/Nile_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Nile River">Nile</a> (3.4M km<sup>2</sup>), the <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Río de la Plata">Río de la Plata</a> (3.2M km<sup>2</sup>), and the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi</a> (3M km<sup>2</sup>). The three rivers that drain the most water, from most to least, are the Amazon, <a href="/wiki/Ganga_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Ganga River">Ganga</a>, and Congo rivers.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Endorheic_drainage_basins">Endorheic drainage basins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drainage_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Endorheic drainage basins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Uureg_Nuur.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Uureg_Nuur.jpg/220px-Uureg_Nuur.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="229" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Uureg_Nuur.jpg/330px-Uureg_Nuur.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Uureg_Nuur.jpg/440px-Uureg_Nuur.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3066" data-file-height="3186" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Uureg_Nuur.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Endorheic_basin" title="Endorheic basin">Endorheic basin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a></div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Endorheic_basin" title="Endorheic basin">Endorheic basin</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Endorheic_basin" title="Endorheic basin">Endorheic drainage basins</a> are inland basins that do not drain to an ocean. Around 18% of all land drains to endorheic lakes or seas or sinks. The largest of these consists of much of the interior of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, which drains into the <a href="/wiki/Caspian_Sea" title="Caspian Sea">Caspian Sea</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Aral_Sea" title="Aral Sea">Aral Sea</a>, and numerous smaller lakes. Other endorheic regions include the <a href="/wiki/Great_Basin" title="Great Basin">Great Basin</a> in the United States, much of the <a href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" class="mw-redirect" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a>, the drainage basin of the <a href="/wiki/Okavango_River" title="Okavango River">Okavango River</a> (<a href="/wiki/Kalahari_Basin" title="Kalahari Basin">Kalahari Basin</a>), highlands near the <a href="/wiki/African_Great_Lakes" title="African Great Lakes">African Great Lakes</a>, the interiors of <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Peninsula" title="Arabian Peninsula">Arabian Peninsula</a>, and parts in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a>. Some of these, such as the Great Basin, are not single drainage basins but collections of separate, adjacent closed basins.
</p><p>In endorheic <a href="/wiki/Bodies_of_water" class="mw-redirect" title="Bodies of water">bodies of standing water</a> where evaporation is the primary means of water loss, the water is typically more saline than the oceans. An extreme example of this is the <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">Dead Sea</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Importance">Importance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drainage_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Importance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geopolitical_boundaries">Geopolitical boundaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drainage_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Geopolitical boundaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Drainage basins have been historically important for determining territorial boundaries, particularly in regions where trade by water has been important. For example, the <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> crown gave the <a href="/wiki/Hudson%27s_Bay_Company" title="Hudson&#39;s Bay Company">Hudson's Bay Company</a> a monopoly on the <a href="/wiki/Fur_trade" title="Fur trade">fur trade</a> in the entire <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Bay" title="Hudson Bay">Hudson Bay</a> basin, an area called <a href="/wiki/Rupert%27s_Land" title="Rupert&#39;s Land">Rupert's Land</a>. <a href="/wiki/Bioregionalism" title="Bioregionalism">Bioregional</a> political organization today includes agreements of states (e.g., international <a href="/wiki/Treaty" title="Treaty">treaties</a> and, within the US, <a href="/wiki/Interstate_compact" title="Interstate compact">interstate compacts</a>) or other political entities in a particular drainage basin to manage the body or bodies of water into which it drains. Examples of such interstate compacts are the <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes_Commission" title="Great Lakes Commission">Great Lakes Commission</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tahoe_Regional_Planning_Agency" title="Tahoe Regional Planning Agency">Tahoe Regional Planning Agency</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydrology">Hydrology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drainage_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hydrology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ohiorivermap.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ohiorivermap.png/220px-Ohiorivermap.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ohiorivermap.png/330px-Ohiorivermap.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b4/Ohiorivermap.png/440px-Ohiorivermap.png 2x" data-file-width="467" data-file-height="469" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ohiorivermap.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Drainage basin of the <a href="/wiki/Ohio_River" title="Ohio River">Ohio River</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> drainage basin</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">hydrology</a>, the drainage basin is a logical unit of focus for studying the movement of water within the <a href="/wiki/Hydrological_cycle" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydrological cycle">hydrological cycle</a>, because the majority of water that discharges from the basin outlet originated as <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_(meteorology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Precipitation (meteorology)">precipitation</a> falling on the basin. A portion of the water that enters the <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a> system beneath the drainage basin may flow towards the outlet of another drainage basin because groundwater flow directions do not always match those of their overlying drainage network. Measurement of the discharge of water from a basin may be made by a <a href="/wiki/Stream_gauge" title="Stream gauge">stream gauge</a> located at the basin's outlet.
</p><p>Rain gauge data is used to measure total precipitation over a drainage basin, and there are different ways to interpret that data. If the gauges are many and evenly distributed over an area of uniform precipitation, using the <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic_mean" title="Arithmetic mean">arithmetic mean</a> method will give good results. In the <a href="/wiki/Thiessen_polygon" class="mw-redirect" title="Thiessen polygon">Thiessen polygon</a> method, the drainage basin is divided into polygons with the rain gauge in the middle of each polygon assumed to be representative for the rainfall on the area of land included in its polygon. These polygons are made by drawing lines between gauges, then making perpendicular bisectors of those lines form the polygons. The <a href="/wiki/Isohyet" class="mw-redirect" title="Isohyet">isohyetal</a> method involves contours of equal precipitation are drawn over the gauges on a map. Calculating the area between these curves and adding up the volume of water is time consuming.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Isochrone_map" title="Isochrone map">Isochrone maps</a> can be used to show the time taken for runoff water within a drainage basin to reach a lake, reservoir or outlet, assuming constant and uniform effective rainfall.<sup id="cite_ref-hydrolearthsystsci_bell_1998_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hydrolearthsystsci_bell_1998-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-subramanya_2008_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-subramanya_2008-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-unesco_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unesco-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-websters_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-websters-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geomorphology">Geomorphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drainage_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Geomorphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Drainage basins are the principal hydrologic unit considered in <a href="/wiki/Fluvial" class="mw-redirect" title="Fluvial">fluvial</a> <a href="/wiki/Geomorphology" title="Geomorphology">geomorphology</a>. A drainage basin is the source for water and <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">sediment</a> that moves from higher elevation through the river system to lower elevations as they reshape the channel forms.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drainage_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ecology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mississippi_River_basin.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/bd/Mississippi_River_basin.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="222" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="222" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mississippi_River_basin.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi River</a> drains the largest area of any <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> river, much of it <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> regions. Agricultural runoff and other water pollution that flows to the outlet is the cause of the <a href="/wiki/Dead_zone_(ecology)" title="Dead zone (ecology)">hypoxic, or dead zone</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Drainage basins are important in <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>. As water flows over the ground and along rivers it can pick up nutrients, sediment, and <a href="/wiki/Water_pollution" title="Water pollution">pollutants</a>. With the water, they are transported towards the outlet of the basin, and can affect the ecological processes along the way as well as in the receiving water source.
</p><p>Modern use of artificial fertilizers, containing nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium, has affected the mouths of drainage basins. The minerals are carried by the drainage basin to the mouth, and may accumulate there, disturbing the natural mineral balance. This can cause <a href="/wiki/Eutrophication" title="Eutrophication">eutrophication</a> where plant growth is accelerated by the additional material.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resource_management">Resource management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drainage_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Resource management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Watershed_management" title="Watershed management">Watershed management</a></div>
<p>Because drainage basins are coherent entities in a hydro-logical sense, it has become common to manage water resources on the basis of individual basins. In the <a href="/wiki/U.S._state" title="U.S. state">U.S. state</a> of <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a>, governmental entities that perform this function are called "<a href="/wiki/Watershed_district_(Minnesota)" title="Watershed district (Minnesota)">watershed districts</a>". In New Zealand, they are called catchment boards. Comparable community groups based in Ontario, Canada, are called <a href="/wiki/Conservation_authority_(Canada)" class="mw-redirect" title="Conservation authority (Canada)">conservation authorities</a>. In North America, this function is referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Watershed_management" title="Watershed management">watershed management</a>".
In <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, the National Policy of Water Resources, regulated by Act n° 9.433 of 1997, establishes the drainage basin as the territorial division of Brazilian water management.
</p><p>When a river basin crosses at least one political border, either a border within a nation or an international boundary, it is identified as a <a href="/wiki/Transboundary_river" title="Transboundary river">transboundary river</a>. Management of such basins becomes the responsibility of the countries sharing it. <a href="/wiki/Nile_Basin_Initiative" title="Nile Basin Initiative">Nile Basin Initiative</a>, <a href="/wiki/OMVS" class="mw-redirect" title="OMVS">OMVS</a> for <a href="/wiki/Senegal_River" title="Senegal River">Senegal River</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mekong_River_Commission" title="Mekong River Commission">Mekong River Commission</a> are a few examples of arrangements involving management of shared river basins.
</p><p>Management of shared drainage basins is also seen as a way to build lasting peaceful relationships among countries.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Catchment_factors">Catchment factors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drainage_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Catchment factors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The catchment is the most significant factor determining the amount or likelihood of <a href="/wiki/Flooding" class="mw-redirect" title="Flooding">flooding</a>.
</p><p>Catchment factors are: <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">topography</a>, shape, size, <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> type, and <a href="/wiki/Land_use" title="Land use">land use</a> (paved or <a href="/wiki/Roof" title="Roof">roofed</a> areas). Catchment topography and shape determine the time taken for <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">rain</a> to reach the river, while catchment size, soil type, and development determine the amount of water to reach the river.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Topography">Topography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drainage_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Topography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Generally, topography plays a big part in how fast runoff will reach a river. Rain that falls in steep <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountainous</a> areas will reach the primary river in the drainage basin faster than flat or lightly sloping areas (e.g., &gt; 1% gradient).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shape">Shape</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drainage_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Shape">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shape will contribute to the speed with which the runoff reaches a river. A long thin catchment will take longer to drain than a circular catchment.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Size">Size</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drainage_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Size">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Size will help determine the amount of water reaching the river, as the larger the catchment the greater the potential for flooding. It is also determined on the basis of length and width of the drainage basin.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soil_type">Soil type</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drainage_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Soil type">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Soil type will help determine how much water reaches the river. Certain soil types such as <a href="/wiki/Sand" title="Sand">sandy</a> soils are very free-draining, and rainfall on sandy soil is likely to be absorbed by the ground. However, soils containing <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> can be almost impermeable and therefore rainfall on clay soils will run off and contribute to flood volumes. After prolonged rainfall even free-draining soils can become <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/saturated" class="extiw" title="wikt:saturated">saturated</a>, meaning that any further rainfall will reach the river rather than being absorbed by the ground. If the surface is impermeable the precipitation will create surface run-off which will lead to higher risk of flooding; if the ground is permeable, the precipitation will infiltrate the soil.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Land_use">Land use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drainage_basin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Land use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Land use can contribute to the volume of water reaching the river, in a similar way to clay soils. For example, rainfall on roofs, <a href="/wiki/Sidewalk" title="Sidewalk">pavements</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">roads</a> will be collected by rivers with almost no absorption into the <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">groundwater</a>.
</p>
