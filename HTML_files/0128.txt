	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Akurio language</h1>

<p><b>Akurio,</b> also known as <b>Akuriyó,</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Dead_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Dead language">dead</a> <a href="/wiki/Cariban_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Cariban language">Cariban language</a> that was used by the <a href="/wiki/Akurio_people" title="Akurio people">Akurio people</a> in <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> until the late <a href="/wiki/20th_century" title="20th century">20th century</a>, when the group began using the <a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%ADo_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Trío language">Trío language</a>. Akuriyo does not have a <a href="/wiki/Writing_system" title="Writing system">writing system</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status">Status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akurio_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The last native speaker is believed to have died in the first decade of the 2000s, at which time only 10 people were estimated to have Akuriyó as a second language. By 2012, only two semi-speakers remained.<sup id="cite_ref-ethno_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ethno-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sepi Akuriyó, one of the last surviving speakers of Akuriyó, went missing on December 2, 2018, when a small plane carrying 8 people disappeared during a flight over the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_rainforest" title="Amazon rainforest">Amazon rainforest</a>. A search and rescue operation was called off after two weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
