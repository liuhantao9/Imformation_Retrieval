<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_personal_names" title="Ancient Greek personal names">Ancient Greeks generally had a single name</a>, often qualified with a patronymic, a clan or tribe, or a place of origin. Married women were identified by the name of their husbands, not their fathers.
</p><p>Hereditary family names or surnames began to be used by elites in the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Byzantine</a> period. Well into the 9th century, they were rare. But by the 11th and 12th centuries, elite families often used family names.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Family names came from placenames, nicknames, or occupations.<sup id="cite_ref-odfn_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-odfn-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is not clear when stable family surnames became widely used. Though elite families often had stable family names, many of the "last names" used by Greeks into the 19th century were either patronymics or nicknames. It is also possible that family names were simply not recorded because <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Ottoman</a> administrative practice preferred patronymics, and did not require surnames.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 19th century, patronymic surnames became common.
</p><p>For personal names, from the first century CE until the nineteenth century CE, <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">pagan</a> names from <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">antiquity</a> were mostly replaced by names from <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> scriptures and tradition. With the <a href="/wiki/Modern_Greek_Enlightenment" title="Modern Greek Enlightenment">Modern Greek Enlightenment</a> and the development of <a href="/wiki/Greek_nationalism" title="Greek nationalism">Greek nationalism</a>, names from antiquity became popular again.<sup id="cite_ref-Mackridge_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mackridge-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Family names may be patronymic in origin or else based on occupation, location, or personal characteristic. These origins are often indicated by prefixes or suffixes. Traditionally a woman used a feminine version of her father's family name, replacing it with a feminine version of her husband's family name on marriage. In modern Greece, a woman keeps her father's family name for life but may use a husband's name. 
</p><p>In official documents in modern Greece, people are given three names: a given name, a patronymic and a family name. 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Given_names">Given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Until the late 18th century, almost all Christian Greeks were named for <a href="/wiki/Saint#Eastern_Orthodoxy" title="Saint">Orthodox saints</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New</a> Testaments and early <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> tradition. Since then, names of both deities and mortals from antiquity have been popular as well.<sup id="cite_ref-Mackridge_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mackridge-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Male names usually end in -ας, -ης, and -ος, but sometimes ancient forms are also used. Female names almost always end in -α and -η, though a few end in -ώ with -ου being possible.
</p><p>Since antiquity, there has been a strong tradition of naming the first son after the paternal grandfather and the second after the maternal grandfather.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> This results in a continuation of names in the family line.
</p><p>There is a strong clustering of first names by locality according to patron saints, famous churches or monasteries. Examples include:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Spyridon</i> and <i>Spyridoula</i> in <a href="/wiki/Corfu" title="Corfu">Corfu</a>,</li>
<li><i>Gerasimos</i> in <a href="/wiki/Kefalonia" class="mw-redirect" title="Kefalonia">Kefalonia</a>,</li>
<li><i>Dionysia</i> and <i>Dionysios</i> in <a href="/wiki/Zakynthos" title="Zakynthos">Zakynthos</a>,</li>
<li><i>Andreas</i> and <i>Andriana</i> in <a href="/wiki/Patras" title="Patras">Patras</a>,</li>
<li><i>Markella</i> and <i>Markos</i> in Chios</li>
<li><i>Savvas</i> among families from <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>,</li>
<li><i>Emmanuel</i> or <i>Manolis</i>, <i>Joseph</i> or <i>Sifis</i>, <i>Manousos</i>, and <i>Mēnas</i> in <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>.</li>
<li><i>Tsambikos</i> or <i>Tsampika</i>/<i>Mika</i> in <a href="/wiki/Rhodes" title="Rhodes">Rhodes</a>.</li></ul>
<p>When Greek names are used in other languages, they are sometimes rendered phonetically, such as <i>Eleni</i> for Ἑλένη, and sometimes by their equivalents, like <i>Helen</i> in English or <i>Hélène</i> in French. In the United States, there are also <a href="/wiki/Anglicization_of_names#Greek_given_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglicization of names">conventional anglicizations</a> based on phonetic similarity rather than etymology, for example James or Jimmy for Δημήτρης/Dimitris (nickname Ντίμης/Dimi, hence Jimmy), when the English name James is a derivative of Ἰάκωβος/ Iakovos.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_names"><span id="Greek_surnames"></span>Family names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Family names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Greek family names are most commonly patronymics but may also be based on occupation, personal characteristics or location. The <a href="/wiki/Feminine_gender" class="mw-redirect" title="Feminine gender">feminine</a> version is usually the <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">genitive</a> of the family name of the woman's father or husband; so, for example, Mr. Yannatos and Mrs. <a href="/wiki/Savina_Yannatou" title="Savina Yannatou">Yannatou</a>.
</p><p>As a result of their codification in the Modern Greek state, surnames have <a href="/wiki/Katharevousa" title="Katharevousa">Katharevousa</a> forms even though Katharevousa is no longer the official standard. Thus, the Ancient Greek name Eleutherios forms the Modern Greek proper name Lefteris. In the past, people in speaking used the family name followed by the given name, so John Eleutherios was called Leftero-giannis. In modern practice he is called Giannis Eleftheriou, where Giannis is the popular form of the formal Ioannis but Eleftheriou is an archaic genitive. For women the surname is usually a Katharevousa genitive of a male name, whereas back in Byzantine times there were separate feminine forms of male surnames, such as <i>Palaiologína</i> for <i>Palaiológos</i> which nowadays would be <i>Palaiológou</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the past, women would change their surname on first marrying to that of their husband in the genitive case, so marking the change of dependence to husband from father. In early Modern Greek society, women were named with <i>-aina</i> as a feminine suffix on the husband's given name, for example "Giorgaina" signifying "wife of George". Nowadays, a woman's surname does not change upon marriage but she can use the husband's surname socially. Children usually receive the paternal surname, though some children receive the maternal surname in addition or exclusively.<sup id="cite_ref-oxnames_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oxnames-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In official documents, the father's name in the genitive will be inserted between a person's first and last names. For example, if John Papadopoulos has a daughter named Mary and a son named Andrew, they will be referred to as <i>María Ioánnou Papadopoúlou</i> and <i>Andréas Ioánnou Papadópoulos</i>. If Mary then marries George Demetriádes, she may retain her original name or choose to be called <i>María Geōrgíou Demetriádou</i>.  If she is widowed, she will revert to her father's patronymic but retain her husband's surname to become <i>María Ioánnou Demetriádou</i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_family_names">Examples of family names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Examples of family names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_prefixes">Common prefixes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Common prefixes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em;">
<ul><li><i>Archi-</i>: meaning "superior" or "boss".</li>
<li><i>Chondro-</i>: meaning "fat".</li>
<li><i>Gero-</i>: meaning "old" or "wise".</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Hajji" title="Hajji">Hadji</a>-</i>: the Arabic honorific for one who has made the <a href="/wiki/Hadj" class="mw-redirect" title="Hadj">Hadj</a> or pilgrimage, used in the case of Christians for a voyage to Jerusalem, for example "<a href="/wiki/Vassilis_Hatzipanagis" class="mw-redirect" title="Vassilis Hatzipanagis">Hatzipanagis</a>".</li>
<li><i>Kara-</i>: the Greek word for "cart" and the Turkish word for "black",<sup id="cite_ref-davis_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-davis-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (May 2014)">failed verification</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> for example "<a href="/wiki/Dimitrios_Karatasos" title="Dimitrios Karatasos">Karatasos</a>".</li>
<li><i>Konto-</i>: meaning "short".</li>
<li><i>Makro-</i>: meaning "tall" or "long".</li>
<li><i>Mastro-</i>: meaning "artisan" or "workman".</li>
<li><i>Palaio-</i>: meaning "old" or "wise".</li>
<li><i>Papa-</i>: indicating descent from a <i>papas</i>, a priest. So <i><a href="/wiki/Lambros_Papakostas" class="mw-redirect" title="Lambros Papakostas">Papakostas</a></i> is the "son of Kostas, the priest".</li></ul>
</div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_suffixes">Common suffixes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Greek_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Common suffixes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em;">
<ul><li><b>-akis</b> (-άκης): associated primarily with <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a> (except <a href="/wiki/Anogeia" title="Anogeia">Anogeia</a>)  and the Aegean Islands, it is a diminutive, such as Giorgos becoming <i>Giorgakis</i> for the <i>young Giorgos</i>. Examples are: "<a href="/wiki/Konstantinos_Mitsotakis" title="Konstantinos Mitsotakis">Mitsotakis</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Mikis_Theodorakis" title="Mikis Theodorakis">Theodorakis</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Olympia_Dukakis" title="Olympia Dukakis">Doukakis</a>".This suffix was also very common for <a href="/wiki/Cretan_Turks" title="Cretan Turks">Cretan Turks</a> up until they were officially changed with the <a href="/wiki/Surname_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Surname Law">Surname Law</a>. This suffix was introduced in the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>-akos</b> (-ᾶκος): mainly from <a href="/wiki/Laconia" title="Laconia">Laconia</a>, particularly among <a href="/wiki/Maniots" title="Maniots">Maniots</a> from the Laconian part of the <a href="/wiki/Mani_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Mani peninsula">Mani peninsula</a>.' Examples are: "<a href="/wiki/Stavros_Xarhakos" class="mw-redirect" title="Stavros Xarhakos">Xarhakos</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Sotiris_Kyrgiakos" class="mw-redirect" title="Sotiris Kyrgiakos">Kyrgiakos</a>".</li>
<li><b>-oulis</b> (-ούλης): mainly from <a href="/wiki/Thessalia" class="mw-redirect" title="Thessalia">Thessalia</a>, it is a diminutive, which is also used as a diminutive for place names in the region such as <a href="/wiki/Giannouli" title="Giannouli">Giannouli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Damasouli" title="Damasouli">Damasouli</a> .' Examples are: "Katsioulis" and "<a href="/wiki/Kostas_Giannoulis" title="Kostas Giannoulis">Giannoulis</a>".</li>
<li><b>-as</b> (-ᾶς): from <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(Greece)" title="Macedonia (Greece)">Macedonia</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Epirus" title="Epirus">Epirus</a>. Examples are: "<a href="/wiki/Pavlos_Melas" title="Pavlos Melas">Melas</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Pyrros_Dimas" title="Pyrros Dimas">Dimas</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Alexis_Zorbas" class="mw-redirect" title="Alexis Zorbas">Zorbas</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Moustakas" title="Moustakas">Moustakas</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Zappas" class="mw-redirect" title="Zappas">Zappas</a>". However, the surname <a href="/wiki/Doukas" title="Doukas"><i>Dukas</i></a> or <i>Doukas</i> derives from the Latin title <i><a href="/wiki/Dux" title="Dux">dux</a></i> or <i>duke</i>.</li>
<li><b>-atos</b> (-ᾶτος): (from <a href="/wiki/Cephalonia" title="Cephalonia">Cephalonia</a>), of <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">Venetian</a> derivation. <sup id="cite_ref-Corriere_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Corriere-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Examples are: "<a href="/wiki/Grigoris_Georgatos" title="Grigoris Georgatos">Georgatos</a>", "<a href="/wiki/George_Pan_Cosmatos" class="mw-redirect" title="George Pan Cosmatos">Cosmatos</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Andrew_Manatos" title="Andrew Manatos">Manatos</a>".</li>
<li><b>-eas</b> (-εας): mainly among Maniots from the <a href="/wiki/Messenia" class="mw-redirect" title="Messenia">Messenian</a> part of the <a href="/wiki/Mani_peninsula" class="mw-redirect" title="Mani peninsula">Mani peninsula</a>. Examples are: "<a href="/wiki/Elias_Koteas" title="Elias Koteas">Koteas</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Nikolaos_Georgeas" title="Nikolaos Georgeas">Georgeas</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Angelos_Charisteas" title="Angelos Charisteas">Charisteas</a>".</li>
<li><b>-elis</b> (-έλης) and <b>-ilis</b> (-ιλής): from the Turkish suffixes for agent, possession and origin, common in western Asia Minor, <a href="/wiki/Mytiline" class="mw-redirect" title="Mytiline">Mytiline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lemnos" title="Lemnos">Lemnos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Imbros" title="Imbros">Imbros</a>. Examples are: Myrsilis, Katselis, Papadelis, Manelis.</li>
<li><b>-allis</b> (-άλλης) and <b>-ellis</b> (-έλλης): both found especially in the <a href="/wiki/Dodecanese" title="Dodecanese">Dodecanese</a>, mainly <a href="/wiki/Rhodes" title="Rhodes">Rhodes</a>. Examples are "<a href="/wiki/Nick_Gallis" class="mw-redirect" title="Nick Gallis">Georgallis</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Maria_Kanellis" title="Maria Kanellis">Kanellis</a>".</li>
<li><b>-idis</b> or <b>-ides</b> and <b>-iadis</b> or <b>iades</b> (-ίδης/-ιάδης): meaning 'son of' or 'descendant of'. The suffix -idis (often transliterated -ides in English and French) is the oldest in use. <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a>, for example, was also referred to as <a href="/wiki/Cronides" class="mw-redirect" title="Cronides">Cronides</a> ("son of Cronus"). <i>-idis</i> was the most common suffix in <a href="/wiki/Byzantium" title="Byzantium">Byzantium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bithynia" title="Bithynia">Bithynia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Thrace" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine Thrace">Byzantine Thrace</a>, being also used by <a href="/wiki/Pontic_Greeks" title="Pontic Greeks">Pontic Greeks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caucasus_Greeks" title="Caucasus Greeks">Caucasus Greeks</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Pontic_Alps" class="mw-redirect" title="Pontic Alps">Pontic Alps</a>, northeast <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">Georgia</a>, the former <a href="/wiki/Kars_Oblast" title="Kars Oblast">Kars Oblast</a>, and sometimes in <a href="/wiki/Epirus" title="Epirus">Epirus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Corfu" title="Corfu">Corfu</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Aegean_islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Aegean islands">Aegean islands</a>. Examples include: "<a href="/wiki/James_Stavridis" class="mw-redirect" title="James Stavridis">Stavridis</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Anthony_Koutoufides" title="Anthony Koutoufides">Koutoufides</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Phil_Angelides" title="Phil Angelides">Angelidis</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Nikos_Sampson" title="Nikos Sampson">Georgiadis</a>".</li>
<li><b>-lis</b> (-λής). Turkish suffix for "of" a place, like the Greek suffixes -tis and -otis. Examples are: "<a href="/wiki/Kostas_Karamanlis" title="Kostas Karamanlis">Karamanlis</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Dionysios_Kasdaglis" title="Dionysios Kasdaglis">Kasdaglis</a>".</li>
<li><b>-opoulos</b> (-όπουλος): meaning "descendant of", originated from the <a href="/wiki/Peloponnese" title="Peloponnese">Peloponnese</a> but has become very widespread. Examples are: "<a href="/wiki/Nikitaras" title="Nikitaras">Stamatelopoulos</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Tassos_Papadopoulos" title="Tassos Papadopoulos">Papadopoulos</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Jim_Gianopoulos" class="mw-redirect" title="Jim Gianopoulos">Gianopoulos</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Spiro_Agnew" title="Spiro Agnew">Anagnostopoulos</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Taki_Theodoracopulos" title="Taki Theodoracopulos">Theodorakopoulos</a>". It can also indicate ethnic origin, such as <i>Frangopoulos</i> (Φραγκόπουλος) meaning "son of a Frank", <i>Persopoulos</i> (Περσόπουλος) meaning "son of a Persian", <i>Servopoulos</i> (Σερβόπουλος) meaning "son of a Serb" and <i>Voulgaropoulos</i> (Βουλγαρόπουλος) meaning "son of a Bulgarian".</li>
<li><b>-oglou</b> (-όγλου): from the Turkish <i>-oğlu</i> meaning "son of", seen in families from <a href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" class="mw-redirect" title="Asia Minor">Asia Minor</a>. Examples are: "<a href="/wiki/Georgios_Tsolakoglou" title="Georgios Tsolakoglou">Tsolakoglou</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Christos_Ardizoglou" title="Christos Ardizoglou">Ardizoglou</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Patsatzoglou" class="mw-redirect" title="Patsatzoglou">Patsatzoglou</a>".</li>
<li><b>-ou</b> (-ου): a genitive mainly from <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>. Examples are: "<a href="/wiki/Grigoris_Afxentiou" title="Grigoris Afxentiou">Afxentiou</a>", "<a href="/wiki/George_Economou_(poet)" title="George Economou (poet)">Economou</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Michalis_Konstantinou" title="Michalis Konstantinou">Konstantinou</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Constantinos_Christoforou" title="Constantinos Christoforou">Christoforou</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/George_Gregoriou" title="George Gregoriou">Gregoriou</a>".</li>
<li><b>-tis, -otis</b> (-της, -ώτης): meaning "of" a place. Examples are "<a href="/wiki/Nikolaos_Politis" title="Nikolaos Politis">Politis</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Dionisis_Chiotis" title="Dionisis Chiotis">Chiotis</a>" from <a href="/wiki/Chios" title="Chios">Chios</a>.</li>
<li><b>-tzis, -tsis</b> (-τζής, -τσής) and feminine (-τζή, -τσή): Turkish suffix to signify a profession, like the English <i>-er</i> in <i>Baker</i> or <i>Butcher</i>. Examples are: "<a href="/wiki/Hrisopiyi_Devetzi" class="mw-redirect" title="Hrisopiyi Devetzi">Devetzi</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Stavros_Kouyioumtzis" title="Stavros Kouyioumtzis">Kouyioumtzis</a>".</li></ul>
