<p><b>Henry Cabot Lodge Jr.</b> (July 5, 1902 – February 27, 1985), sometimes referred to as <b>Henry Cabot Lodge II</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-Dynasty_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dynasty-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> was a Republican <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senator</a> from <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Ambassadors_from_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Ambassadors from the United States">United States ambassador</a>. He was the <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> nominee for <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1960_United_States_presidential_election" title="1960 United States presidential election">1960 presidential election</a> alongside incumbent Vice President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>. The Republican ticket lost to Democrats <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a>.
</p><p>Born in <a href="/wiki/Nahant,_Massachusetts" title="Nahant, Massachusetts">Nahant, Massachusetts</a>, Lodge was the grandson of Senator <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cabot_Lodge" title="Henry Cabot Lodge">Henry Cabot Lodge</a> and the great-grandson of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State" title="United States Secretary of State">Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Theodore_Frelinghuysen" title="Frederick Theodore Frelinghuysen">Frederick Theodore Frelinghuysen</a>. After graduating from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, Lodge won election to the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_House_of_Representatives" title="Massachusetts House of Representatives">Massachusetts House of Representatives</a>. He defeated Democratic Governor <a href="/wiki/James_Michael_Curley" title="James Michael Curley">James Michael Curley</a> in 1936 to represent Massachusetts in the United States Senate. He resigned from the Senate in 1944 to serve in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Lodge remained in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Reserve" title="United States Army Reserve">Army Reserve</a> after the war and eventually rose to the rank of major general. In 1946, Lodge defeated incumbent Democratic Senator <a href="/wiki/David_I._Walsh" title="David I. Walsh">David I. Walsh</a> to return to the Senate.
</p><p>He led the <a href="/wiki/Draft_Eisenhower_movement" title="Draft Eisenhower movement">Draft</a> <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Eisenhower</a> movement before the <a href="/wiki/1952_United_States_presidential_election" title="1952 United States presidential election">1952 election</a> and served as Eisenhower's campaign manager, ensuring that his candidate triumphed at the <a href="/wiki/1952_Republican_National_Convention" title="1952 Republican National Convention">1952 Republican National Convention</a>. Eisenhower defeated Democratic nominee <a href="/wiki/Adlai_Stevenson_II" title="Adlai Stevenson II">Adlai Stevenson II</a> in the general election, but Lodge lost his own <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Massachusetts,_1952" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Senate election in Massachusetts, 1952">re-election campaign</a> to <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>. Lodge was named as <a href="/wiki/United_States_Ambassador_to_the_United_Nations" title="United States Ambassador to the United Nations">ambassador to the United Nations</a> in 1953 and became a member of Eisenhower's <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_the_United_States" title="Cabinet of the United States">Cabinet</a>. Vice President <a href="/wiki/Richard_M._Nixon" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard M. Nixon">Richard M. Nixon</a> chose Lodge as his running mate in the 1960 presidential election, but the Republican ticket lost the election.
</p><p>In 1963, President Kennedy appointed Lodge to the position of Ambassador to South Vietnam, where Lodge supported the <a href="/wiki/1963_South_Vietnamese_coup" title="1963 South Vietnamese coup">1963 South Vietnamese coup</a>. He continued to represent the United States in various countries under President <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a>, President Nixon, and President <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Gerald Ford</a>. Lodge led the U.S. delegation that signed the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Peace_Accords" title="Paris Peace Accords">Paris Peace Accords</a> with <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnam" title="North Vietnam">North Vietnam</a>, leading to the end of the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>. He died in <a href="/wiki/Beverly,_Massachusetts" title="Beverly, Massachusetts">Beverly, Massachusetts</a> in 1985.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Cabot_Lodge_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lodge was born in <a href="/wiki/Nahant,_Massachusetts" title="Nahant, Massachusetts">Nahant, Massachusetts</a>. His father was <a href="/wiki/George_Cabot_Lodge" title="George Cabot Lodge">George Cabot Lodge</a>, a poet, through whom he was a grandson of Senator <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cabot_Lodge" title="Henry Cabot Lodge">Henry Cabot Lodge</a>, great-great-grandson of Senator <a href="/wiki/Elijah_H._Mills" title="Elijah H. Mills">Elijah H. Mills</a>, and great-great-great-grandson of Senator <a href="/wiki/George_Cabot" title="George Cabot">George Cabot</a>. Through his mother, Mathilda Elizabeth Frelinghuysen (Davis), he was a great-grandson of Senator <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Theodore_Frelinghuysen" title="Frederick Theodore Frelinghuysen">Frederick Theodore Frelinghuysen</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and a great-great-grandson of Senator <a href="/wiki/John_Davis_(Massachusetts_governor)" title="John Davis (Massachusetts governor)">John Davis</a>. He had two siblings: <a href="/wiki/John_Davis_Lodge" title="John Davis Lodge">John Davis Lodge</a> (1903–1985), also a politician, and Helena Lodge de Streel (1905-1998).<sup id="cite_ref-JohnDavisLodge_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JohnDavisLodge-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lodge attended <a href="/wiki/St._Albans_School_(Washington,_D.C.)" title="St. Albans School (Washington, D.C.)">St. Albans School</a> and graduated from <a href="/wiki/Middlesex_School" title="Middlesex School">Middlesex School</a>. In 1924, he graduated <i>cum laude</i> from <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>, where he was a member of the <a href="/wiki/Hasty_Pudding_Club" title="Hasty Pudding Club">Hasty Pudding</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fox_Club_(Harvard)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fox Club (Harvard)">Fox Club</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hcl_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hcl-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career">Political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Cabot_Lodge_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lodge worked in the newspaper business from 1924–1931. He was elected in 1932, and served in the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_House_of_Representatives" title="Massachusetts House of Representatives">Massachusetts House of Representatives</a> from 1933 to 1936.<sup id="cite_ref-HenryCabotLodgeJr_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HenryCabotLodgeJr-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_U.S._Senate_career">First U.S. Senate career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Cabot_Lodge_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First U.S. Senate career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 1936, Lodge was elected to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a>, defeating Democrat <a href="/wiki/James_Michael_Curley" title="James Michael Curley">James Michael Curley</a>.  He served from January 1937 to February 1944.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Cabot_Lodge_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lodge served with distinction during the war, rising to the rank of <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_colonel_(United_States)" title="Lieutenant colonel (United States)">lieutenant colonel</a>. During the war he saw two tours of duty. The first was in 1942 while he was also serving as a U.S. Senator. The second was in 1944–5 after he resigned from the Senate.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The first period was a continuation of Lodge's longtime service as an Army Reserve Officer. Lodge was a major in the <a href="/wiki/1st_Armored_Division_(United_States)" title="1st Armored Division (United States)">1st Armored Division</a>. That tour ended in July 1942, when President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> ordered congressmen serving in the military to resign one of the two positions, and Lodge, who chose to remain in the Senate, was ordered by Secretary of War <a href="/wiki/Henry_Stimson" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Stimson">Henry Stimson</a> to return to Washington. During this brief service, he led a squadron of American tankers at <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Gazala" title="Battle of Gazala">Gazala</a>; they were the first Americans to engage German troops on land in the war.<sup id="cite_ref-services_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-services-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After returning to Washington and winning re-election in November 1942, Lodge went to observe allied troops serving in Egypt and Libya,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and in that position, he was on hand for the British retreat from <a href="/wiki/Tobruk" title="Tobruk">Tobruk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-services_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-services-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lodge served the first year of his new Senate term but then resigned his Senate seat on February 3, 1944 in order to return to active duty,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> the first U.S. Senator to do so since the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He saw action in Italy and France.
</p><p>In the fall of 1944, Lodge single-handedly captured a four-man German patrol.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the end of the war, in 1945, he used his knowledge of the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French language</a> and culture, gained from attending school in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> to aid <a href="/wiki/Jacob_L._Devers" title="Jacob L. Devers">Jacob L. Devers</a>, the commander of the <a href="/wiki/Sixth_United_States_Army_Group" title="Sixth United States Army Group">Sixth United States Army Group</a>, to coordinate activities with the <a href="/wiki/First_Army_(France)#1944–1945" class="mw-redirect" title="First Army (France)">French First Army</a> commander, <a href="/wiki/Jean_de_Lattre_de_Tassigny" title="Jean de Lattre de Tassigny">Jean de Lattre de Tassigny</a>, and then carry out surrender negotiations with German forces in western <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Lodge was decorated with the French <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Honour" title="Legion of Honour">Legion of Honor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Croix_de_guerre_1939%E2%80%931945" class="mw-redirect" title="Croix de guerre 1939–1945">Croix de Guerre</a> with palm.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> His American decorations included the <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Merit" title="Legion of Merit">Legion of Merit</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Star_Medal" title="Bronze Star Medal">Bronze Star Medal</a>.
</p><p>After the war, Lodge returned to Massachusetts and resumed his political career. He continued his status as an Army Reserve officer and rose to the rank of major general.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_Senate_and_the_drafting_of_Eisenhower">Return to Senate and the drafting of Eisenhower</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Cabot_Lodge_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Return to Senate and the drafting of Eisenhower">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1946 Lodge defeated Democratic Senator <a href="/wiki/David_I._Walsh" title="David I. Walsh">David I. Walsh</a> and returned to the Senate. He soon emerged as a spokesman for the moderate, internationalist wing of the Republican Party. In late 1951, Lodge <a href="/wiki/Draft_Eisenhower" class="mw-redirect" title="Draft Eisenhower">helped persuade</a> General <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a> to run for the Republican presidential nomination. When Eisenhower finally consented, Lodge served as his campaign manager and played a key role in helping Eisenhower to <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)_presidential_primaries,_1952" class="mw-redirect" title="Republican Party (United States) presidential primaries, 1952">win the nomination</a> over Senator <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Taft" title="Robert A. Taft">Robert A. Taft</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, the candidate of the party's conservative faction.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In March 1950, Lodge sat on a subcommittee of the Government Operations Committee, chaired by Democratic Senator <a href="/wiki/Millard_Tydings" title="Millard Tydings">Millard Tydings</a>, which looked into Senator <a href="/wiki/Joseph_McCarthy" title="Joseph McCarthy">Joseph McCarthy</a>'s list of possibly communist State Department employees. Lodge argued in hearings that Tydings demonized McCarthy and whitewashed McCarthy's supposed discovery of security leaks at the State Department.  Lodge told Tydings:
</p>
<blockquote><p>Mr. Chairman, this is the most unusual procedure I have seen in all the years I have been here. Why cannot the senator from Wisconsin get the normal treatment and be allowed to make his statement in his own way, ... and not be pulled to pieces before he has had a chance to offer one single consecutive sentence. ... I do not understand what kind of game is being played here.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>In July 1950, the record of the committee hearing was printed, and Lodge was outraged to find that 35 pages were not included.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Lodge noted that his objections to the conduct of the hearing and his misgivings about the inadequacy of vetting suspected traitors were missing,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and that the edited version read as if all committee members agreed that McCarthy was at fault and that there was no Communist infiltration of the State Department.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Lodge stated "I shall not attempt to characterize these methods of leaving out of the printed text parts of the testimony and proceedings ... because I think they speak for themselves."  Lodge soon fell out with McCarthy and joined the effort to reduce McCarthy's influence.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the fall of 1952, Lodge found himself fighting in a tight race for re-election with <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>, then a US Representative. His efforts in helping Eisenhower caused Lodge to neglect his own campaign. In addition, some of Taft's supporters in Massachusetts defected from Lodge to the Kennedy campaign out of anger over Lodge's support of Eisenhower.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In November 1952 <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_election_in_Massachusetts,_1952" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Senate election in Massachusetts, 1952">Lodge was defeated by Kennedy</a>; Lodge received 48.5% of the vote to Kennedy's 51.5%. It was neither the first nor the last time a Lodge faced a Kennedy in a Massachusetts election: in 1916 <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cabot_Lodge" title="Henry Cabot Lodge">Henry Cabot Lodge</a> had defeated Kennedy's grandfather <a href="/wiki/John_F._Fitzgerald" title="John F. Fitzgerald">John F. Fitzgerald</a> for the same Senate seat, and Lodge's son, <a href="/wiki/George_C._Lodge" title="George C. Lodge">George</a>, was defeated in his bid for the seat by Kennedy's brother <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Ted</a> in the 1962 election for John F. Kennedy's unexpired term.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ambassador_to_United_Nations">Ambassador to United Nations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Cabot_Lodge_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ambassador to United Nations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lodge was named <a href="/wiki/United_States_Ambassadors_to_the_United_Nations" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Ambassadors to the United Nations">U.S. ambassador</a> to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> by President Eisenhower in February 1953, with his office elevated to Cabinet-level rank. In contrast to <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cabot_Lodge" title="Henry Cabot Lodge">his grandfather</a> (who had been a principal opponent of the UN's predecessor, the <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a>), Lodge was supportive of the UN as an institution for promoting peace. As he famously said about it, "This organization is created to prevent you from going to hell. It isn't created to take you to heaven."<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Since then, no one has even approached his record of seven and a half  years as ambassador to the UN. During his time as UN Ambassador, Lodge supported the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> policies of the Eisenhower Administration, and often engaged in debates with the UN representatives of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>.
</p><p>During the CIA-sponsored overthrowing of the legitimate Guatemalan government, when Britain and France became concerned about the US being involved in the aggression, Lodge (as US Ambassador to the United Nations) threatened to withdraw US support to Britain on Egypt and Cyprus, and to France on Tunisia and Morocco, unless they backed the US in their action.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> When the government was overthrown, the <a href="/wiki/United_Fruit_Company" title="United Fruit Company">United Fruit Company</a>, of which Lodge was a significant stockholder,<sup id="cite_ref-Cohen186_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen186-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> re-established itself in Guatemala. The episodes tainted an otherwise distinguished career and painted Lodge as a face of US imperialism and exceptionalism.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1959, he escorted Soviet leader <a href="/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev" title="Nikita Khrushchev">Nikita Khrushchev</a> on a highly publicized tour of the United States.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1960_Vice_Presidential_campaign">1960 Vice Presidential campaign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Cabot_Lodge_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1960 Vice Presidential campaign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lodge left the UN ambassadorship turning over his seat to Deputy Chief Jerry Wadsworth during <a href="/wiki/1960_U.S._presidential_election" class="mw-redirect" title="1960 U.S. presidential election">the election of 1960</a> to run for <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a> on the Republican ticket headed by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>, against Lodge's old foe, John F. Kennedy. Before choosing Lodge, Nixon had also considered <a href="/wiki/Philip_Willkie" title="Philip Willkie">Philip Willkie</a> of <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Wendell_L._Willkie" class="mw-redirect" title="Wendell L. Willkie">Wendell L. Willkie</a>; U.S. Representative <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Gerald Ford</a> of <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>; and U.S. Senator <a href="/wiki/Thruston_B._Morton" class="mw-redirect" title="Thruston B. Morton">Thruston B. Morton</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>. Nixon finally settled on Lodge in the mistaken hope that Lodge's presence on the ticket would force Kennedy to divert time and resources to securing his Massachusetts base, but Kennedy won his home state handily. Nixon also felt that the name Lodge had made for himself in the United Nations as a foreign policy expert would prove useful against the relatively inexperienced Kennedy. Nixon and Lodge lost the election in a razor-thin vote.
</p><p>The choice of Lodge proved to be questionable. He did not carry his home state for Nixon. Also, some conservative Republicans charged that Lodge had cost the ticket votes, particularly in the South, by his pledge (made without Nixon's approval) that if elected, Nixon would name at least one African American to a <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_(government)" title="Cabinet (government)">Cabinet</a> post. He suggested <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Bunche" title="Ralph Bunche">Ralph Bunche</a> as a "wonderful idea".<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cabotlodgekennedymeeting1961.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Cabotlodgekennedymeeting1961.jpg/220px-Cabotlodgekennedymeeting1961.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="172" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Cabotlodgekennedymeeting1961.jpg/330px-Cabotlodgekennedymeeting1961.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Cabotlodgekennedymeeting1961.jpg/440px-Cabotlodgekennedymeeting1961.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="783" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cabotlodgekennedymeeting1961.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>President John F. Kennedy meets with Director General of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Institute" title="Atlantic Institute">Atlantic Institute</a>, Henry Cabot Lodge, in the Oval Office, White House, Washington, D.C., 1961.</div></div></div>
<p>Between 1961 and 1962, Lodge was the first director-general of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Institute" title="Atlantic Institute">Atlantic Institute</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ambassador_to_South_Vietnam">Ambassador to South Vietnam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Cabot_Lodge_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Ambassador to South Vietnam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Xa_Loi_Pagoda_raids" class="mw-redirect" title="Xa Loi Pagoda raids">Xa Loi Pagoda raids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cable_243" title="Cable 243">Cable 243</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krulak_Mendenhall_mission" class="mw-redirect" title="Krulak Mendenhall mission">Krulak Mendenhall mission</a>, <a href="/wiki/McNamara_Taylor_mission" class="mw-redirect" title="McNamara Taylor mission">McNamara Taylor mission</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Arrest_and_assassination_of_Ngo_Dinh_Diem" title="Arrest and assassination of Ngo Dinh Diem">Arrest and assassination of Ngo Dinh Diem</a></div>
<p>Kennedy appointed Lodge to the position of Ambassador to South Vietnam, which he held from 1963 to 1964. The new ambassador quickly determined that <a href="/wiki/Ngo_Dinh_Diem" title="Ngo Dinh Diem">Ngo Dinh Diem</a>, President of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Vietnam" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of Vietnam">Republic of Vietnam</a>, was both inept and corrupt and that South Vietnam was headed for disaster unless Diem reformed his administration or was replaced.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
While Diem was eventually assassinated and his government toppled in a <a href="/wiki/1963_South_Vietnamese_coup" title="1963 South Vietnamese coup">November 1963 coup d'état</a>, the coup sparked a rapid succession of leaders in South Vietnam, each unable to rally and unify their people and in turn overthrown by someone new. These frequent changes in leadership caused political instability in the South, since no strong, centralized and permanent government was in place to govern the nation, while the Viet Minh stepped up their infiltration of the Southern populace and their pace of attacks in the South. Having supported the coup against President Diem, Lodge then realized it had caused the situation in the region to deteriorate, and he suggested to the State Department that South Vietnam should be made to relinquish its independence and become a protectorate of the United States (like the former status of the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>) so as to bring governmental stability. The alternatives, he warned, were either increased military involvement by the U.S. or total abandonment of South Vietnam by America.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Walking_for_President.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Walking_for_President&quot;">"Walking for President"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Cabot_Lodge_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: &quot;Walking for President&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1964RepublicanPresidentialPrimaries.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/1964RepublicanPresidentialPrimaries.svg/300px-1964RepublicanPresidentialPrimaries.svg.png" decoding="async" width="300" height="186" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/1964RepublicanPresidentialPrimaries.svg/450px-1964RepublicanPresidentialPrimaries.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/1964RepublicanPresidentialPrimaries.svg/600px-1964RepublicanPresidentialPrimaries.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="959" data-file-height="593" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1964RepublicanPresidentialPrimaries.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Republican primaries results by state <div>  &#32; <table class="multicol" role="presentation" style="border-collapse: collapse; padding: 0; border: 0; background:transparent; width:100%;"><tbody><tr> <td style="width: 50%;text-align: left; vertical-align: top;"> <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #c1c1c1; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;No primary held</div> <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column"><span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #423121; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">&#160;</span>&#160;<a href="/wiki/John_W._Byrnes" title="John W. Scranton">William W. Scranton</a></div> &#32; </td></tr></tbody></table></div> Lodge won three primaries as a "write-in" candidate without making any public appearances.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1964, Lodge, while still Ambassador to South Vietnam, was the surprise <a href="/wiki/Write-in_candidate" title="Write-in candidate">write-in</a> victor in the Republican <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire_primary" title="New Hampshire primary">New Hampshire primary</a>, defeating declared presidential candidates <a href="/wiki/Barry_Goldwater" title="Barry Goldwater">Barry Goldwater</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Rockefeller" title="Nelson Rockefeller">Nelson Rockefeller</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> His entire campaign was organized by a small band of political amateurs working independently of the ambassador, who, believing they had little hope of winning him any delegates, did nothing to aid their efforts. However, when they scored the New Hampshire upset, Lodge, along with the press and Republican party leaders, suddenly began to seriously consider his candidacy. Many observers remarked on the situation's similarity to 1952, when Eisenhower had unexpectedly defeated Senator <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Taft" title="Robert A. Taft">Robert A. Taft</a>, then leader of the Republican Party's conservative faction. However, Lodge (who refused to become an open candidate) did not fare as well in later primaries, and Goldwater ultimately won the presidential nomination.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_career">Later career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Cabot_Lodge_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Later career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He was re-appointed <a href="/wiki/Ambassador" title="Ambassador">ambassador</a> to South Vietnam by President <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a> in 1965, and served thereafter as <a href="/wiki/Ambassador_at_Large" class="mw-redirect" title="Ambassador at Large">Ambassador at Large</a> (1967–1968) and Ambassador to <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> (1968–1969). In 1969, when his former running mate Richard M. Nixon finally became President, he was appointed by President Nixon to serve as head of the American delegation at the Paris peace negotiations, and he served occasionally as <a href="/wiki/Holy_See-United_States_relations" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy See-United States relations">personal representative of the President</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> from 1970 to 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Henry_Cabot_Lodge_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cabotlodgefam.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Cabotlodgefam.jpg/220px-Cabotlodgefam.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="177" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Cabotlodgefam.jpg/330px-Cabotlodgefam.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Cabotlodgefam.jpg/440px-Cabotlodgefam.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="515" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cabotlodgefam.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Henry Cabot Lodge and family</div></div></div>
<p>In 1926, Lodge married Emily Esther Sears. They had two children: <a href="/wiki/George_C._Lodge" title="George C. Lodge">George Cabot Lodge II</a> (b. 1927) and Henry Sears Lodge (1930-2017).<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> George was in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_federal_civil_service" title="United States federal civil service">federal civil service</a> and is now a well-published professor emeritus at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School" title="Harvard Business School">Harvard Business School</a>. Henry married Elenita Ziegler of <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and was a former sales executive.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1966 he was elected an honorary member of the Massachusetts <a href="/wiki/Society_of_the_Cincinnati" title="Society of the Cincinnati">Society of the Cincinnati</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lodge died in 1985 after a long illness and was interred in the <a href="/wiki/Mount_Auburn_Cemetery" title="Mount Auburn Cemetery">Mount Auburn Cemetery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Watertown,_Massachusetts" title="Watertown, Massachusetts">Watertown, Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Two years after Lodge's death, Sears married Forrester A. Clark. She died in 1992 of <a href="/wiki/Lung_cancer" title="Lung cancer">lung cancer</a> and is interred near her first husband in the Cabot Lodge family <a href="/wiki/Columbarium" title="Columbarium">columbarium</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
