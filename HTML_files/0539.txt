<p><b>Berry Brow</b> is a semi-rural village in <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, England, situated about 2 miles (3&#160;km) south of <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>. It lies on the eastern bank of the <a href="/wiki/River_Holme" title="River Holme">Holme Valley</a> and partially straddles the <a href="/wiki/A616_road" title="A616 road">A616 road</a> to <a href="/wiki/Honley" title="Honley">Honley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Penistone" title="Penistone">Penistone</a>.
</p>

<p>The village has a <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a> <a href="/wiki/Primary_education#England" title="Primary education">infants' and nursery school</a>, some shops and a <a href="/wiki/Berry_Brow_railway_station" title="Berry Brow railway station">railway platform</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Penistone_Line" title="Penistone Line">Penistone Line</a>. It lies between <a href="/wiki/Armitage_Bridge" title="Armitage Bridge">Armitage Bridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taylor_Hill,_Huddersfield" title="Taylor Hill, Huddersfield">Taylor Hill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newsome" title="Newsome">Newsome</a>. Berry Brow is served by two <a href="/wiki/Public_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Public house">public houses</a> (The Railway and The Golden Fleece) and a <a href="/wiki/Social_clubs" class="mw-redirect" title="Social clubs">liberal club</a>. The site of a third public house, The Black Bull, was converted into an <a href="/wiki/Cuisine_of_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Cuisine of India">Indian restaurant</a> in 1994, and received planning permission to expand capacity in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the bottom of the <a href="/wiki/Valley" title="Valley">valley</a> are two high rise buildings, built in the 1960s in an attempt to modernise the village.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> These reached public notoriety in the 1980s when they were found to contain high levels of <a href="/wiki/Asbestos" title="Asbestos">asbestos</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, which had been built into the fabric of the building, under the regulations in force at that time. A request by Kirklees Council for money from central Government to assist with the predicted £1 million cost of removing the asbestos was rejected in February 1991.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Expecting to take approximately 6 months to complete the task of removing the asbestos, <a href="/wiki/Kirklees" title="Kirklees">Kirklees Council</a> temporarily re-housed the tenants in other areas, the majority of whom consisted of mature and elderly residents<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. As the 6 months dragged on into first two then three years, the tenants were offered more permanent housing elsewhere. The two blocks were eventually refurbished at a cost in excess of £6,000,000<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. They were then used to house younger single people and <a href="/wiki/Asylum_seeker" title="Asylum seeker">asylum seekers</a> from other countries.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> As part of the refurbishment a 'State-of-the Art' CCTV security system was installed in 2009 to cover both buildings, as well as other locations in <a href="/wiki/Dewsbury" title="Dewsbury">Dewsbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Batley" title="Batley">Batley</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Berry_Brow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The oldest part of the village is Deadmanstone. Originally known as Dudmanstone, Dudmanstone House is situated above Berry Brow.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In 1584 the estate was recorded as belonging to the Lockwood family:
</p>
<blockquote><p>Thomas Lockwood holdeth a messuage called Dudmanstone, now made into two&#160;; two gardens&#160;; one little croft, called Tenter Croft&#160;; two closes, called Cockshutts&#160;; two closes, called Ouroyds&#160;; one close, called Sykes&#160;; third part of one called William Croft&#160;; one little meadow, called Calf Croft&#160;; one other meadow, called the Lime Croft&#160;; four closes called the Lees, &amp;c. One house, called the Forward House&#160;; one garden and one close to the same belonging. One house called Budge Royd. One house and one garden in the tenure of one Shaw&#160;; and one meadow to the same adjoining. One messuage, called Stirley&#160;; one garden and one croft to the same belonging.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
