<p><i><b>Brosimum</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> of plants in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Moraceae" title="Moraceae">Moraceae</a>, native to <a href="/wiki/Tropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical">tropical</a> regions of the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>.
</p><p>The breadnut (<i><a href="/wiki/Brosimum_alicastrum" title="Brosimum alicastrum">B. alicastrum</a></i>) was used by the <a href="/wiki/Maya_civilization" title="Maya civilization">Maya civilization</a> for its edible nut. The dense vividly colored scarlet wood of <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brosimum_paraense&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brosimum paraense (page does not exist)">B. paraense</a></i> is used for decorative woodworking.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brosimum_guianense&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brosimum guianense (page does not exist)">B. guianese</a></i>, or <a href="/w/index.php?title=Brosimum_guianense&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Brosimum guianense (page does not exist)">snakewood</a>, has a mottled snake-skin pattern, and is among the densest woods, with a very high stiffness; it was the wood of choice for making of bows for musical instruments of the <a href="/wiki/Violin_family" title="Violin family">violin family</a> until the late 18th century, when it was replaced by the more easily worked brazilwood (<i><a href="/wiki/Caesalpinia_echinata" class="mw-redirect" title="Caesalpinia echinata">Caesalpinia echinata</a></i>). Plants of this genus are otherwise used for <a href="/wiki/Timber" class="mw-redirect" title="Timber">timber</a>, building materials, and in a cultural context.
</p>
