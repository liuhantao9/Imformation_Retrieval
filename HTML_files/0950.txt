<p>The <b>SS <i>Athenia</i></b> was a steam turbine <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_crossing" title="Transatlantic crossing">transatlantic</a> passenger liner built in Glasgow in 1923 for the Anchor-Donaldson Line, which later became the Donaldson Atlantic Line. She worked between the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and the east coast of <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> until September 1939, when a <a href="/wiki/Torpedo" title="Torpedo">torpedo</a> from  <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Submarine" title="Submarine">submarine</a> <a href="/wiki/German_submarine_U-30_(1936)" title="German submarine U-30 (1936)"><i>U-30</i></a> sank her in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Approaches" title="Western Approaches">Western Approaches</a>.
</p><p>The <i>Athenia</i> was the first <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a> ship to be sunk by <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Germany</a> during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, and the incident accounted for the Donaldson Line's greatest single loss of life at sea. 117 civilian passengers and crew were killed with the sinking condemned as a <a href="/wiki/War_crime" title="War crime">war crime</a>. The dead included 28 <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">US</a> citizens, leading Germany to fear that the US might react by joining the war on the side of the UK and <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. Wartime German authorities denied that one of their vessels had sunk the ship, and a German admission of responsibility did not come until 1946.
</p><p>She was the second Donaldson ship of that name to be torpedoed and sunk off <a href="/wiki/Inishtrahull" title="Inishtrahull">Inishtrahull</a> by a German submarine; the earlier <a href="/wiki/SS_Athenia_(1903)" title="SS Athenia (1903)"><i>Athenia</i></a> was similarly attacked in 1918.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Athenia_(1922)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Fairfield_Shipbuilding_and_Engineering_Company" title="Fairfield Shipbuilding and Engineering Company">Fairfield Shipbuilding and Engineering Company</a> of <a href="/wiki/Govan" title="Govan">Govan</a> in Glasgow built <i>Athenia</i>, launching her on 28 January 1922 and completing her in 1923. She measured 13,465&#160;<a href="/wiki/Gross_register_tonnage" title="Gross register tonnage">GRT</a> and 8,118&#160;<a href="/wiki/Net_register_tonnage" title="Net register tonnage">NRT</a>, was  526.3&#160;ft (160.4&#160;m) long <a href="/wiki/Length_between_perpendiculars" title="Length between perpendiculars">between perpendiculars</a> by 66.4&#160;ft (20.2&#160;m) <a href="/wiki/Beam_(nautical)" title="Beam (nautical)">beam</a> and had a <a href="/wiki/Hull_(watercraft)#Metrics" title="Hull (watercraft)">depth</a> of 38.1&#160;ft (11.6&#160;m). She had six <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine#Marine_propulsion" title="Steam turbine">steam turbines</a> driving twin <a href="/wiki/Propeller" title="Propeller">screws</a> <i>via</i> double reduction gearing, giving her a speed of 15 knots (28&#160;km/h).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELloyd&#39;s_Register_1930_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELloyd&#39;s_Register_1930-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> She had capacity for 516 cabin class passengers and 1,000 in 3rd class.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> By 1930 her navigation equipment included wireless <a href="/wiki/Direction_finding" title="Direction finding">direction finding</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELloyd&#39;s_Register_1930_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELloyd&#39;s_Register_1930-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and by 1934 this had been augmented with an <a href="/wiki/Echo_sounding" title="Echo sounding">echo sounding</a> device and a <a href="/wiki/Gyrocompass" title="Gyrocompass">gyrocompass</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Athenia_(1922)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Athenia</i> was built for Anchor-Donaldson Line, which was a joint venture between <a href="/wiki/Anchor_Line_(steamship_company)" title="Anchor Line (steamship company)">Anchor Line</a> and Donaldson Line. Fairfield built a <a href="/wiki/Sister_ship" title="Sister ship">sister ship</a>, <a href="/wiki/SS_Letitia" title="SS Letitia"><i>Letitia</i></a>, which was launched in October 1924 and was completed in 1925. <i>Athenia</i> and <i>Letitia</i> were the two largest ships in Donaldson's various fleets. The ships worked Anchor-Donaldson's trans-Atlantic route linking <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Liverpool" title="Port of Liverpool">Liverpool</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> with <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Quebec" title="Port of Quebec">Quebec</a> and <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Montreal" title="Port of Montreal">Montreal</a> in summer and to <a href="/wiki/Halifax,_Nova_Scotia" title="Halifax, Nova Scotia">Halifax</a> in winter. After the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Pier_21" title="Pier 21">Pier 21</a> immigration complex in Halifax in 1928, <i>Athenia</i> became a more frequent caller at Halifax, making over 100 trips to Halifax with immigrants.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In 1935 Anchor Line went into liquidation and Donaldson Line bought most of its assets.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In 1936 Donaldson was reconstituted as Donaldson Atlantic Line.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Loss">Loss</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Athenia_(1922)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Loss">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SS_Athenia_fsa_8d37075.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/SS_Athenia_fsa_8d37075.jpg/220px-SS_Athenia_fsa_8d37075.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="276" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/SS_Athenia_fsa_8d37075.jpg/330px-SS_Athenia_fsa_8d37075.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/76/SS_Athenia_fsa_8d37075.jpg 2x" data-file-width="416" data-file-height="522" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SS_Athenia_fsa_8d37075.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Workers painting <i>Athenia</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s stern, summer 1937</div></div></div>
<p>On 1 September 1939 <i>Athenia</i>, commanded by <a href="/wiki/Sea_captain" title="Sea captain">Captain</a> James Cook, left Glasgow for Montreal via Liverpool and <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>. She carried 1,103 passengers, including about 500 Jewish refugees, 469 Canadians, 311 US citizens and 72 UK subjects, and 315 crew.<sup id="cite_ref-Johnmeyer_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnmeyer-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Despite clear indications that war would break out any day, she departed Liverpool at 13:00 hrs on 2 September without recall, and on the evening of the 3rd was 60 nautical miles (110&#160;km) south of <a href="/wiki/Rockall" title="Rockall">Rockall</a> and 200 nautical miles (370&#160;km) northwest of <a href="/wiki/Inishtrahull" title="Inishtrahull">Inishtrahull</a>, Ireland, when she was sighted by the <a href="/wiki/German_submarine_U-30_(1936)" title="German submarine U-30 (1936)">German submarine&#160;<i>U-30</i></a> commanded by <i><a href="/wiki/Oberleutnant_zur_See" title="Oberleutnant zur See">Oberleutnant</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Fritz-Julius_Lemp" title="Fritz-Julius Lemp">Fritz-Julius Lemp</a> around 16:30. Lemp later claimed that the fact that she was a darkened ship steering a zigzag course which seemed to be well off the normal shipping routes made him believe she was either a <a href="/wiki/Troopship" title="Troopship">troopship</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Q-ship" title="Q-ship">Q-ship</a> or an <a href="/wiki/Armed_merchantman#Armed_merchant_cruisers" title="Armed merchantman">armed merchant cruiser</a>. <i>U-30</i> tracked the <i>Athenia</i> for three hours until eventually, at 19:40, when both vessels were between Rockall and <a href="/wiki/Tory_Island" title="Tory Island">Tory Island</a>, Lemp ordered two torpedoes to be fired. One exploded on <i>Athenia</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s port side in her engine room, and she began to settle by the stern.
</p><p>Several ships, including the <a href="/wiki/E-class_destroyer" class="mw-redirect" title="E-class destroyer">E-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyer</a> <a href="/wiki/HMS_Electra_(H27)" title="HMS Electra (H27)">HMS&#160;<i>Electra</i></a>, responded to <i>Athenia</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s <a href="/wiki/Distress_signal" title="Distress signal">distress signal</a>. <i>Electra</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s commander, Lt. Cdr. Sammy A. Buss, was senior officer present and took charge. He sent the <a href="/wiki/E_and_F_class_destroyer" class="mw-redirect" title="E and F class destroyer">F-class</a> <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyer</a> <a href="/wiki/HMS_Fame_(H78)" title="HMS Fame (H78)">HMS&#160;<i>Fame</i></a> on an anti-submarine sweep of the area, while <i>Electra</i>, another E-class destroyer, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Escort_(H66)" title="HMS Escort (H66)">HMS&#160;<i>Escort</i></a>, the Swedish <a href="/wiki/Yacht" title="Yacht">yacht</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Rover_(yacht)" title="Rover (yacht)">Southern Cross</a></i>, the 5,749&#160;<a href="/wiki/Gross_register_tonnage" title="Gross register tonnage">GRT</a> Norwegian <a href="/wiki/Tanker_(ship)" title="Tanker (ship)">tanker</a> MS <i>Knute Nelson</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and the US <a href="/wiki/Cargo_ship" title="Cargo ship">cargo ship</a> <a href="/wiki/SS_City_of_Flint_(1919)" title="SS City of Flint (1919)"><i>City of Flint</i></a>, rescued survivors. Between them they rescued about 981 passengers and crew. The German liner <a href="/wiki/SS_Bremen_(1929)" class="mw-redirect" title="SS Bremen (1929)">SS&#160;<i>Bremen</i></a>, en route from New York to <a href="/wiki/Murmansk" title="Murmansk">Murmansk</a>, also received <i>Athenia</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s distress signal, but ignored it.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrennecke200315–16_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrennecke200315–16-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <i>City of Flint</i> took 223 survivors to <a href="/wiki/Pier_21" title="Pier 21">Pier 21</a> at <a href="/wiki/Halifax,_Nova_Scotia" title="Halifax, Nova Scotia">Halifax</a>, and <i>Knute Nelson</i> landed 450 at <a href="/wiki/Galway" title="Galway">Galway</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sinking_of_Ss_Athenia,_September_1939_HU51008.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Sinking_of_Ss_Athenia%2C_September_1939_HU51008.jpg/220px-Sinking_of_Ss_Athenia%2C_September_1939_HU51008.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="158" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Sinking_of_Ss_Athenia%2C_September_1939_HU51008.jpg/330px-Sinking_of_Ss_Athenia%2C_September_1939_HU51008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Sinking_of_Ss_Athenia%2C_September_1939_HU51008.jpg/440px-Sinking_of_Ss_Athenia%2C_September_1939_HU51008.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3463" data-file-height="2480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sinking_of_Ss_Athenia,_September_1939_HU51008.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Survivors in one of <i>Athenia</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s lifeboats alongside <a href="/wiki/SS_City_of_Flint_(1919)" title="SS City of Flint (1919)"><i>City of Flint</i></a></div></div></div>
<p><i>Athenia</i> remained afloat for more than 14 hours, until she finally sank stern first at 10:40 the next morning. Of the 1,418 aboard, 98 passengers<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and 19 crew members were killed.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Many died in the engine room and aft stairwell, where the torpedo hit.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPadfield19967_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPadfield19967-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The British crews were famous for putting the passengers' lives before their own<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (April 2017)">dubious</span></a>&#32;<span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:SS_Athenia_(1922)#Dubious" title="Talk:SS Athenia (1922)">discuss</a></span></i>&#93;</sup>, and were expertly trained to handle such "events"; nonetheless, about 50 people died when one of the lifeboats was crushed in the propeller of the <i>Knute Nelson</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlair199667_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlair199667-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> 
No. 5A lifeboat came alongside the empty tanker and tied up, against advice, astern of No 12 lifeboat.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Only 15 feet (5&#160;m) separated the life boat from the tanker's exposed propeller. Once No. 12 lifeboat was emptied it was cast adrift and began to sink. This fact was reported to the bridge of <i>Knute Nelson</i>. For some reason the ship's <a href="/wiki/Engine_order_telegraph" title="Engine order telegraph">engine order telegraph</a> was then set to full ahead. 5A lifeboat's mooring line or "warp" parted under the stress, causing the lifeboat to be pulled back into the revolving propeller.
</p><p>There was a second accident at about 05:00 hrs when No. 8 lifeboat capsized in a heavy sea below the stern of the yacht <i>Southern Cross</i>, killing 10 people. Three passengers were crushed to death while trying to transfer from lifeboats to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> destroyers. Other deaths were due to falling overboard from <i>Athenia</i> and her lifeboats, or to injuries and exposure.  Ultimately, all deaths were the result of the U-boat violating orders and torpedoing a merchant passenger liner.
</p><p>54 dead were Canadian and 28 were US citizens, which led to German fears that the incident would bring the US into the war.<sup id="cite_ref-Johnmeyer_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnmeyer-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Athenia_(1922)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="noviewer thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px"><div style="position:relative;width:240px;border:1px solid lightgray"><a href="/wiki/File:Oceans_around_British_Isles_satellite_image_location_map.jpg" class="image" title="SS Athenia (1922) is located in Oceans around British Isles"><img alt="SS Athenia (1922) is located in Oceans around British Isles" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Oceans_around_British_Isles_satellite_image_location_map.jpg/240px-Oceans_around_British_Isles_satellite_image_location_map.jpg" decoding="async" width="240" height="290" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Oceans_around_British_Isles_satellite_image_location_map.jpg/360px-Oceans_around_British_Isles_satellite_image_location_map.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Oceans_around_British_Isles_satellite_image_location_map.jpg/480px-Oceans_around_British_Isles_satellite_image_location_map.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1654" data-file-height="1998" /></a><div style="position:absolute;top:41.333%;left:4.333%"><div style="position:absolute;left:-4px;top:-4px;line-height:0"><img alt="SS Athenia (1922)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Red_pog.svg/8px-Red_pog.svg.png" decoding="async" title="SS Athenia (1922)" width="8" height="8" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Red_pog.svg/12px-Red_pog.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Red_pog.svg/16px-Red_pog.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="64" data-file-height="64" /></div></div></div><div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Oceans_around_British_Isles_satellite_image_location_map.jpg" title="File:Oceans around British Isles satellite image location map.jpg"> </a></div>Approximate position of <i>Athenia</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s wreck</div></div></div>
<p>It was not until the Nuremberg Trials after the War that the truth of the U-boat sinking of the SS <i>Athenia</i> finally came out.  The sinking was given dramatic publicity throughout the English-speaking world.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilliams200317_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilliams200317-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The front pages of many newspapers ran photographs of the lost ship along with headlines about the UK's declaration of war. For example, the <i><a href="/wiki/Halifax_Herald" class="mw-redirect" title="Halifax Herald">Halifax Herald</a></i> for 4 September 1939 had a banner across its front page announcing "LINER ATHENIA IS TORPEDOED AND SUNK" with, in the center of the page, "EMPIRE AT WAR" in outsized red print.
</p><p>A Canadian girl, 10-year-old Margaret Hayworth,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> was among the casualties, and was one of the first Canadians to be killed by enemy action. Newspapers widely publicised the story, proclaiming "Ten-Year-Old Victim of Torpedo" as "Canadians Rallying Point", and set the tone for their coverage of the rest of the war. One thousand people met the train that brought her body back to <a href="/wiki/Hamilton,_Ontario" title="Hamilton, Ontario">Hamilton, Ontario</a>, and there was a public funeral attended by the mayor of Hamilton, the city council, the Lieutenant-Governor, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Edward_Matthews" title="Albert Edward Matthews">Albert Edward Matthews</a>, Premier <a href="/wiki/Mitchell_Hepburn" title="Mitchell Hepburn">Mitchell Hepburn</a>, and the entire Ontario cabinet.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHoughton200375–76_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoughton200375–76-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When <a href="/wiki/Erich_Raeder" title="Erich Raeder">Grand Admiral Raeder</a> first heard of the sinking of the <i>Athenia</i>, he made inquiries and was told that no U-boat was nearer than 75&#160;mi (121&#160;km) to the location of the sinking. He therefore told the US chargé d'affaires in good faith that the German Navy had not been responsible. When, on 27 September, <i>U-30</i> returned to Wilhelmshaven, Lemp reported to <a href="/wiki/Karl_D%C3%B6nitz" title="Karl Dönitz">Admiral Dönitz</a> that he had sunk the <i>Athenia</i> in error. Dönitz at once sent Lemp to Berlin, where he explained the incident to Raeder. In turn, Raeder reported to Hitler, who decided that the incident should be kept secret for political reasons. Raeder decided against court-martialling Lemp because he considered that he had made an understandable mistake, and the log of the <i>U-30</i>, which was seen by many people, was altered to sustain the official denials<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>.
</p><p>A month later the <i><a href="/wiki/V%C3%B6lkischer_Beobachter" title="Völkischer Beobachter">Völkischer Beobachter</a></i>, the Nazi party's official newspaper, published an article which blamed the loss of the <i>Athenia</i> on the UK, accusing <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a>, then First Lord of the Admiralty, of sinking the ship to turn neutral opinion against <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Germany</a>. Raeder claimed not to have known about this previous to publication and said that if he had known about it, he would have prevented its appearing.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavidson1997381_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavidson1997381-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the US, 60 per cent of respondents to a <a href="/wiki/Gallup_poll" class="mw-redirect" title="Gallup poll">Gallup poll</a> believed the Germans were responsible, despite their initial claims that the <i>Athenia</i> had been sunk by the UK for propaganda purposes, with only 9 per cent believing otherwise. Some anti-interventionists called for restraint while at the same time expressing their abhorrence of the sinking. <a href="/wiki/Boake_Carter" title="Boake Carter">Boake Carter</a> described it as a criminal act.
</p><p>Some were not completely convinced that Germany was in fact responsible. <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Hoover" title="Herbert Hoover">Herbert Hoover</a> expressed his doubts, saying, "It is such poor tactics that I cannot believe that even the clumsy Germans would do such a thing", while <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">North Carolina</a> senator <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rice_Reynolds" title="Robert Rice Reynolds">Robert Rice Reynolds</a> denied that Germany had any motive to sink the <i>Athenia</i>. At best, he said, such an action "could only further inflame the world, and particularly America, against Germany, with no appreciable profits from the sinking." He added that Britain could have had a motive – "to infuriate the American people".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDoenecke200368_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDoenecke200368-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was not until January 1946, during the case against Admiral Raeder at the <a href="/wiki/Nuremberg_trials" title="Nuremberg trials">Nuremberg trials</a>, that a statement by Admiral Dönitz was read in which he finally admitted that <i>Athenia</i> had been torpedoed by <i>U-30</i> and that every effort had been made to cover it up. Lemp, who claimed he had mistaken her for an armed merchant cruiser, took the first steps to conceal the facts by omitting to make an entry in the submarine's log, and swearing his crew to secrecy.
</p><p>After <i>Athenia</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s sinking, <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_theory" title="Conspiracy theory">conspiracy theories</a> were circulated by pro-<a href="/wiki/Axis_powers" title="Axis powers">Axis</a> and anti-British circles. For example, one editor in Boston's <i>Italian News</i> suggested the ship had been sunk by British mines and blamed on German U-boats to draw America into the war.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The claims were unfounded.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cargo">Cargo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Athenia_(1922)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cargo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A cargo of 888 tons was taken on in Glasgow, 472 tons of which were building bricks. Other items included granite <a href="/wiki/Curling#Curling_stone" title="Curling">curling rocks</a> from Scotland, textbooks for the <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Toronto" title="Education in Toronto">Toronto school system</a>, a number of sealed steel boxes containing new clothes purchased in Europe by tourists, and <a href="/wiki/Watercolor_painting" title="Watercolor painting">watercolor paintings</a> by passenger and English illustrator Winifred Walker, intended for her planned book, <i>Shakespeare's Flowers</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Carroll2012_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carroll2012-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Caulfield_1958_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Caulfield_1958-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Stevenson_1979_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stevenson_1979-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 4 September 1939, curling stone manufacturer Andrew Kay &amp; Co. sent a cablegram to its sales representative in Toronto stating, "We now learn that the Athenia was this morning sunk off the coast of Scotland, and we regret that the finest consignment of curling stones that have ever yet left our factory has gone with it."  According to James Wyllie, secretary and director of <a href="/wiki/Kays_of_Scotland" title="Kays of Scotland">Kays of Scotland</a> (as the company is now known) in 2018, three bills of lading for this shipment included 48 pairs of <a href="/wiki/Ailsa_Craig#Curling_stones" title="Ailsa Craig">Blue Hone Ailsa curling stones</a> for the London, Ontario Curling Club, 41 pairs of Blue Hone Ailsa curling stones for the <a href="/wiki/High_Park_Club" title="High Park Club">Toronto High Park Curling Club</a>, and 50 pairs of Red Hone Ailsa curling stones for the Lindsay Curling Club.  This is a total of 278 Andrew Kay &amp; Co. Excelsior Ailsa curling stones with handles and cases weighing nearly six tons with a 1939 value of <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">£</a>585.12 (equivalent to £35,633 in 2018).<sup id="cite_ref-MacTavish_2018_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MacTavish_2018-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wreck_discovery">Wreck discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Athenia_(1922)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wreck discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2017, the <a href="/wiki/Oceanographer" class="mw-redirect" title="Oceanographer">oceanographer</a> and marine archaeologist <a href="/wiki/David_Mearns" title="David Mearns">David Mearns</a> found a wreck he believes to be <i>Athenia</i>. Mearns located the wreck on <a href="/wiki/Rockall_Bank" class="mw-redirect" title="Rockall Bank">Rockall Bank</a> using <a href="/wiki/Sonar" title="Sonar">sonar</a> imagery that was scanned by the <a href="/wiki/Geological_Survey_of_Ireland" title="Geological Survey of Ireland">Geological Survey of Ireland</a> to map the sea floor. He stated "Can I go into a court of law and say, '100%, that's Athenia?' No. But barring a photograph I can say in my expert opinion there's a very, very high probability that that's <i>Athenia</i>. Everything fits."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legality_of_sinking">Legality of sinking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Athenia_(1922)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legality of sinking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As <i>Athenia</i> was an unarmed passenger ship, the attack violated the <a href="/wiki/Hague_Conventions_of_1899_and_1907" title="Hague Conventions of 1899 and 1907">Hague conventions</a> and the <a href="/wiki/London_Naval_Treaty" title="London Naval Treaty">London Naval Treaty</a> of 1930 that allowed all warships, including submarines, to stop and search merchant vessels, but forbade capture as <a href="/wiki/Prize_(law)" title="Prize (law)">prize</a> or sinking unless the ship was carrying contraband or engaged in military activity.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarwood201520_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarwood201520-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Even if this was the case, and if it was decided to sink their ship, it was required that passengers and crew must be transferred to a "place of safety" as a priority. Although Germany had not signed the 1930 treaty, the German 1936 Prize Rules (<i>Prisenordnung</i>) binding their naval commanders copied most of its restrictions.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarwood201520_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarwood201520-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Lemp of <i>U-30</i> did none of these things, choosing instead to fire without warning.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Memorials">Memorials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Athenia_(1922)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Memorials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lost British members of <i>Athenia's</i> crew are commemorated at the <a href="/wiki/Tower_Hill_Memorial" title="Tower Hill Memorial">Tower Hill Memorial</a> in London. Canadian crew who died are listed at the Halifax Memorial (Sailor's Memorial) at <a href="/wiki/Point_Pleasant_Park" title="Point Pleasant Park">Point Pleasant Park</a> in <a href="/wiki/Halifax,_Nova_Scotia" title="Halifax, Nova Scotia">Halifax, Nova Scotia</a><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> as well as by special plaque for Hannah Baird, a Canadian stewardess who died in the sinking and who is commemorated in a memorial to female merchant mariners in <a href="/wiki/Langford,_British_Columbia" title="Langford, British Columbia">Langford, British Columbia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=SS_Athenia_(1922)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>No movie has been made of the full story of the sinking, but the film <i><a href="/wiki/Arise,_My_Love" title="Arise, My Love">Arise, My Love</a></i> (1940), directed by <a href="/wiki/Mitchell_Leisen" title="Mitchell Leisen">Mitchell Leisen</a> and starring <a href="/wiki/Claudette_Colbert" title="Claudette Colbert">Claudette Colbert</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ray_Milland" title="Ray Milland">Ray Milland</a>, had a sequence involving the torpedoing of the liner.
</p><p>The song <i>Rollerskate Skinny</i>, written by <a href="/wiki/Rhett_Miller" title="Rhett Miller">Rhett Miller</a> and performed by his band <a href="/wiki/The_Old_97%27s" class="mw-redirect" title="The Old 97&#39;s">The Old 97's</a>, mentions <i>Athenia</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s sinking.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/John_Dickson_Carr" title="John Dickson Carr">John Dickson Carr</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Man_Who_Could_Not_Shudder" title="The Man Who Could Not Shudder">The Man Who Could Not Shudder</a></i>, Dr Fell announces the end of story by showing his audience a newspaper bearing headline "LINER ATHENIA: FULL LIST OF VICTIMS". He means to say that the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> has begun and the truth of the mystery is now unlikely to surface.
</p><p>The sinking of the SS <i>Athenia</i> is also mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Alyson_Richman" title="Alyson Richman">Alyson Richman</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_Wife" class="mw-redirect" title="The Lost Wife">The Lost Wife</a></i> about pre-war Prague and how the dreams of two young lovers are shattered when they are separated by the Nazi invasion, their endurance and experiences during World War II and the Holocaust only to find one another again decades later in the United States.
</p><p>Recent extensive research concerning the incident appears in Cay Rademacher's 2009 book <i>Drei Tage im September – die letzte Fahrt der Athenia, 1939</i> published by MareVerlag of Hamburg.
</p><p>In the novel by Norman Collins, "London Belongs to Me", he describes the sinking of the SS <i>Athenia</i> as war breaks out across Europe.  As a result of the sinking, Londoners are in no doubt war has started, and start bracing themselves for what's to come.
</p><p>The sinking of the <i>Athenia</i> also forms part of the beginning in the movie <a href="/wiki/U_47_%E2%80%93_Kapit%C3%A4nleutnant_Prien" title="U 47 – Kapitänleutnant Prien">U 47 – Kapitänleutnant Prien</a> (1958).
</p><p>A graphic firsthand account of the sinking and rescue appears as the first chapter of <a href="/wiki/James_A._Goodson" title="James A. Goodson">James A. Goodson</a>’s autobiographical account of his wartime experiences as a fighter ace.<sup id="cite_ref-Goodson2016_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goodson2016-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
