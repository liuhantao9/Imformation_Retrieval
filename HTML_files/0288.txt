<p>In <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a>, <b>unicameralism</b> (Latin <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">uni</i>, one + <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">camera</i>, chamber) is the practice of having one legislative or <a href="/wiki/Legislative_chamber" title="Legislative chamber">parliamentary chamber</a>. Thus, a <i>unicameral parliament</i> or <i>unicameral legislature</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislature</a> which consists of one chamber or house.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concept">Concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unicameralism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Unicameral legislatures exist when there is no widely perceived need for <a href="/wiki/Multicameralism" title="Multicameralism">multicameralism</a>. Many multicameral legislatures were created to give separate voices to different sectors of society. Multiple chambers allowed for guaranteed representation of different social classes (as in the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a> or the <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">French</a> <a href="/wiki/French_States-General" class="mw-redirect" title="French States-General">States-General</a>), ethnic or regional interests, or subunits of a <a href="/wiki/Federation" title="Federation">federation</a>. Where these factors are unimportant, in unitary states with limited regional autonomy, unicameralism often prevails. Sometimes, as in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, this comes about through the <a href="/wiki/List_of_abolished_upper_houses" title="List of abolished upper houses">abolition of one of the two chambers</a>, or, as in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, through the merger of the two chambers into a single one, while in others a second chamber has never existed.
</p><p>Unicameral legislatures are also common in official <a href="/wiki/Communist_state" title="Communist state">Communist states</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People&#39;s Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>. Similarly, many formerly Communist states, such as <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moldova" title="Moldova">Moldova</a> and <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, have retained their unicameral legislatures, though others, such as <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, adopted bicameral legislatures. Both the former <a href="/wiki/Russian_SFSR" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian SFSR">Russian SFSR</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Union_of_Soviet_Socialist_Republics" class="mw-redirect" title="Union of Soviet Socialist Republics">Union of Soviet Socialist Republics</a> (USSR) were bicameral. The two chambers were the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_of_Nationalities" title="Soviet of Nationalities">Soviet of Nationalities</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_of_the_Union" title="Soviet of the Union">Soviet of the Union</a>. The Russian Federation retained bicameralism after the dissolution of the USSR and the transition from existing socialism to capitalism.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The principal advantage of a unicameral system is more efficient lawmaking, as the legislative process is much simpler and there is no possibility of <a href="/wiki/Gridlock_(politics)" title="Gridlock (politics)">deadlock</a>. Proponents of unicameralism have also argued that it reduces costs, even if the number of legislators stay the same, since there are fewer institutions to maintain and support it.
</p><p>The main weakness of a unicameral system can be seen as the lack of restraint on the <a href="/wiki/Majority" title="Majority">majority</a>, particularly noticeable in <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_system" title="Parliamentary system">parliamentary systems</a> where the leaders of the parliamentary majority also dominate the <a href="/wiki/Executive_(government)" title="Executive (government)">executive</a>. There is also the risk that important sectors of society may not be adequately represented.
</p>

<p>Approximately half of the world's <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign states</a> are currently unicameral, including both the most populous (the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People&#39;s Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a>) and the least populous (the <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">Vatican City</a>).
</p><p>Many <a href="/wiki/Subnational_entity" class="mw-redirect" title="Subnational entity">subnational entities</a> have unicameral legislatures. These include the state of <a href="/wiki/Nebraska" title="Nebraska">Nebraska</a> and territories of <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">Guam</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Virgin_Islands" title="United States Virgin Islands">Virgin Islands</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the Chinese <a href="/wiki/Special_administrative_regions_of_China" title="Special administrative regions of China">special administrative regions</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australian</a> state of <a href="/wiki/Queensland" title="Queensland">Queensland</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">Northern Territory</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Capital_Territory" title="Australian Capital Territory">Australian Capital Territory</a>, a majority of the <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Argentina" title="Provinces of Argentina">provinces of Argentina</a>, all of the <a href="/wiki/Provinces_and_territories_of_Canada" title="Provinces and territories of Canada">provinces and territories</a> in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, all of the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">German states</a>, all of the <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_Italy" title="Regions of Italy">regions of Italy</a>, all of the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_autonomous_communities" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish autonomous communities">Spanish autonomous communities</a>, both the <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_regions_of_Portugal" class="mw-redirect" title="Autonomous regions of Portugal">autonomous regions of Portugal</a>, most of the <a href="/wiki/States_and_union_territories_of_India" title="States and union territories of India">states and union territories of India</a> and all of the <a href="/wiki/States_of_Brazil" title="States of Brazil">states of Brazil</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the devolved <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Parliament" title="Scottish Parliament">Scottish Parliament</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/London_Assembly" title="London Assembly">London Assembly</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Assembly" title="Northern Ireland Assembly">Northern Ireland Assembly</a> are also unicameral.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unicameralism_in_the_United_States">Unicameralism in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unicameralism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Unicameralism in the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Within <a href="/wiki/U.S._states" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. states">U.S. states</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nebraska" title="Nebraska">Nebraska</a> is currently the only state with a unicameral legislature; after a statewide vote, it changed from bicameral to unicameral in 1937.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> A 2018 study found that efforts to adopt unicameralism in Ohio and Missouri failed due to rural opposition.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> There was a fear in rural communities that unicameralism would diminish their influence in state government.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Local government legislatures of counties, cities, or other political subdivisions within states are usually unicameral and have limited lawmaking powers compared to their state and federal counterparts.
</p><p>In 1999, Governor <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Ventura" title="Jesse Ventura">Jesse Ventura</a> proposed converting the <a href="/wiki/Minnesota_Legislature" title="Minnesota Legislature">Minnesota Legislature</a> into a single unicameral chamber.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Although debated, the idea was never adopted.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/U.S._territory" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. territory">U.S. territory</a> of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> held a non-binding <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_unicameralism_referendum" class="mw-redirect" title="Puerto Rican unicameralism referendum">referendum in 2005</a>. Voters approved changing its <a href="/wiki/Legislative_Assembly_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Legislative Assembly of Puerto Rico">Legislative Assembly</a> to a unicameral body by 456,267 votes in favor (83.7%) versus 88,720 against (16.3%).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> If both the territory's <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_House_of_Representatives" class="mw-redirect" title="Puerto Rico House of Representatives">House of Representatives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Senate" class="mw-redirect" title="Puerto Rico Senate">Senate</a> had approved by a &#x200b;<span class="frac nowrap"><sup>2</sup>&#8260;<sub>3</sub></span> vote the specific amendments to the <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="Puerto Rico Constitution">Puerto Rico Constitution</a> that are required for the change to a unicameral legislature, another referendum would have been held in the territory to approve such amendments. If those constitutional changes had been approved, Puerto Rico could have switched to a unicameral legislature as early as 2015.
</p><p>On June 9, 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Maine_House_of_Representatives" title="Maine House of Representatives">Maine House of Representatives</a> voted to form a unicameral legislature, but the measure did not pass the Senate.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Because of <a href="/wiki/Legislative_gridlock" class="mw-redirect" title="Legislative gridlock">legislative gridlock</a> in 2009, former Congressman <a href="/wiki/Rick_Lazio" title="Rick Lazio">Rick Lazio</a>, a prospective candidate for governor, has proposed that <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a> adopt unicameralism.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The United States as a whole was subject to a unicameral Congress during the years 17811788, when the <a href="/wiki/Articles_of_Confederation" title="Articles of Confederation">Articles of Confederation</a> were in effect.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unicameralism_in_the_Philippines">Unicameralism in the Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unicameralism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Unicameralism in the Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though the current <a href="/wiki/Congress_of_the_Philippines" title="Congress of the Philippines">Congress of the Philippines</a> is bicameral, the country experienced unicameralism in 1898 and 1899 (during the <a href="/wiki/First_Philippine_Republic" title="First Philippine Republic">First Philippine Republic</a>), from 1935 to 1941  ( the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_the_Philippines" title="Commonwealth of the Philippines">Commonwealth</a> era) and from 1943 to 1944 (during the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_occupation_of_the_Philippines" title="Japanese occupation of the Philippines">Japanese occupation</a>). Under the 1973 Constitution, the legislative body was called <a href="/wiki/Batasang_Pambansa" title="Batasang Pambansa">Batasang Pambansa</a>, which functioned also a unicameral legislature within a <a href="/wiki/Semi-presidential_system" title="Semi-presidential system">semi-presidential system</a> form of government until 1986.
</p><p>The ongoing process of amending or revising the <a href="/wiki/1987_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="1987 Constitution">current Constitution</a> and form of government is popularly known as <a href="/wiki/Charter_Change" class="mw-redirect" title="Charter Change">Charter Change</a>. A shift to a unicameral parliament was included in the proposals of the constitutional commission created by former President <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Macapagal-Arroyo" class="mw-redirect" title="Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo">Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Unlike in the United States, senators in the <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_the_Philippines" title="Senate of the Philippines">Senate of the Philippines</a> are elected not per district and state but nationally; the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Unitary_state" title="Unitary state">unitary state</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The Philippine government's decision-making process, relative to the United States, is more rigid, highly centralised, much slower and susceptible to <a href="/wiki/Political_gridlock" class="mw-redirect" title="Political gridlock">political gridlock</a>. As a result, the trend for unicameralism as well as other political system reforms are more contentious in the Philippines.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While Congress is bicameral, all local legislatures are unicameral: the <a href="/wiki/ARMM_Regional_Legislative_Assembly" title="ARMM Regional Legislative Assembly">ARMM Regional Legislative Assembly</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sangguniang_Panlalawigan" title="Sangguniang Panlalawigan">Sangguniang Panlalawigan</a> (Provincial Boards), <a href="/wiki/Sangguniang_Panlungsod" title="Sangguniang Panlungsod">Sangguniang Panlungsod</a> (City Councils), <a href="/wiki/Sangguniang_Bayan" title="Sangguniang Bayan">Sangguniang Bayan</a> (Municipal Councils), <a href="/wiki/Sangguniang_Barangay" class="mw-redirect" title="Sangguniang Barangay">Sangguniang Barangay</a> (<a href="/wiki/Barangay" title="Barangay">Barangay</a> Councils) and the <a href="/wiki/Sangguniang_Kabataan" title="Sangguniang Kabataan">Sangguniang Kabataan</a> (Youth Councils).
</p>
