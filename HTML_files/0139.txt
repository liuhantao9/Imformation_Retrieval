<p><b>Ndyuka</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/dʒ/: &#39;j&#39; in &#39;jam&#39;">dʒ</span><span title="/uː/: &#39;oo&#39; in &#39;goose&#39;">uː</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span>, also called <b>Aukan</b>, <i>Okanisi, Ndyuka tongo</i>, <b>Aukaans</b>, <i>Businenge Tongo</i> (considered by some to be pejorative), <b>Eastern Maroon Creole</b>, or <i>Nenge</i> is a <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">creole language</a> of <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>, spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Ndyuka_people" title="Ndyuka people">Ndyuka people</a>. The speakers are one of six <a href="/wiki/Maroon_(people)" title="Maroon (people)">Maroon peoples</a> (formerly called "Bush Negroes") in the Republic of <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> and one of the Maroon peoples in <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>. Most of the 25 to 30 thousand speakers live in the interior of the country, which is a part of the country covered with <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">tropical rainforests</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">Ethnologue</a> lists <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.ethnologue.com/show_family.asp?subid=2806-16">two related languages</a> under the name <i>Ndyuka</i>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">Phonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ndyuka_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Phonology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ndyuka is based on <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> vocabulary, with influence from <a href="/wiki/African_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="African languages">African languages</a> in its grammar and sounds. For example, the difference between <i>na</i> ("is") and <i>ná</i> ("isn't") is <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">tone</a>; words can start with consonants such as <i>mb</i> and <i>ng</i>, and some speakers use the consonants <i><a href="/wiki/Voiceless_labial-velar_plosive" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiceless labial-velar plosive">kp</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Voiced_labial-velar_plosive" class="mw-redirect" title="Voiced labial-velar plosive">gb</a></i>. (For other Ndyuka speakers, these are pronounced <i>kw</i> and <i>gw</i>, respectively. For example, the word "to leave" is <i>gwé</i> or <i>gbé</i>, from English "go away".)  There are also influences from  <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> and other languages.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">Orthography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ndyuka_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orthography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Modern <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthography</a> differs from an older <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>-based orthography in substituting <i>u</i> for <i>oe</i> and <i>y</i> for <i>j</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Digraph_(orthography)" title="Digraph (orthography)">digraphs</a> <i>ty</i> and <i>dy</i> are pronounced somewhat like the English <i><a href="/wiki/Ch_(digraph)" title="Ch (digraph)">ch</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/J" title="J">j</a></i>, respectively. Tone is infrequently written, but it is required for words such as <i>ná</i> ("isn't"). The syllabic <a href="/wiki/Afaka_script" class="mw-redirect" title="Afaka script">Afaka script</a> was devised for Ndyuka in 1908.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">Dialects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ndyuka_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dialects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Ndyuka language has three dialects: proper Ndyuka (or <i>Okanisi</i>), <a href="/wiki/Aluku_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Aluku language">Aluku</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Paramaccan_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Paramaccan language">Paramaccan</a>, which are ethnically distinct.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Kwinti is distinct enough linguistically to be considered a separate language, but it is sometimes included as well under the name <i>Ndyuka</i>.
</p><p>Ndyuka was also a basis of the <a href="/wiki/Ndyuka-Tiriy%C3%B3_Pidgin" title="Ndyuka-Tiriyó Pidgin">Ndyuka-Tiriyó Pidgin</a>.
</p>

<p>Here is an example of Ndyuka text, and its translation into English (showing the similitarities as well as the lexical evolution), adapted from <i>Languages of the Guianas (SIL Publications)</i>:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>En so den be abaa na a líba, dísi wi kai Kawína Líba. Di den abaa de, den abaa teke gwe na opu fu Kawína. En so den be waka langa langa gwe te na Mama Ndyuka ede, pe wi kai Mama Ndyuka.
</p></blockquote>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>And so they crossed the river, which we call "Kawina [Commewijne] River". Having crossed it, they went way upstream along the Commewijne. Thus they travelled a long, long way, clear to the upper Tapanahony, the place we call "Mama Ndyuka".
</p></blockquote>
<p>The language bears some similarity to <a href="/wiki/Twi" title="Twi">Twi</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Akan_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Akan languages">Akan languages</a> spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Akan_people" title="Akan people">Akan people</a> of Ghana.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. </p>
