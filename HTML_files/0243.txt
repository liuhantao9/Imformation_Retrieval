	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">C. R. Boxer</h1>

<b>Charles Ralph Boxer</b> <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_British_Academy" title="Fellow of the British Academy">FBA</a> (8 March 1904 at <a href="/wiki/Sandown" title="Sandown">Sandown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a> – 27 April 2000 at <a href="/wiki/St._Albans" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Albans">St. Albans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire" title="Hertfordshire">Hertfordshire</a>) was a historian of Dutch and Portuguese maritime and colonial history. In Hong Kong he was the chief spy of the <a href="/wiki/MI6" class="mw-redirect" title="MI6">British army intelligence</a> in the tumultuous years leading up to <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, but it is his lead role in one of the most flamboyantly public love stories of the 1940s, his romance with <a href="/wiki/Emily_Hahn" title="Emily Hahn">Emily Hahn</a>, author and one of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i>'s most prolific contributors, that accounts for most of this fame.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C._R._Boxer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Charles Ralph Boxer was born on the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a> in 1904. On his father's side, he was a descendant of an illustrious British family that had served in command positions in every British war since the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>. Boxer's father Colonel Hugh Edward Richard Boxer served in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Lincolnshire_Regiment" title="Royal Lincolnshire Regiment">Lincolnshire Regiment</a> and had been killed at the <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_Ypres" title="Second Battle of Ypres">Second Battle of Ypres</a> in 1915. While his father's family may have been of Huguenot origin, the family of his mother, Jane Patterson, hailed from Scotland. Her forebears became successful <a href="/wiki/Pastoral_farming" title="Pastoral farming">pastoralists</a> in 19th century <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a> and in Australia.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education_and_military_career">Education and military career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C._R._Boxer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Education and military career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Charles Boxer was educated at <a href="/wiki/Wellington_College,_Berkshire" title="Wellington College, Berkshire">Wellington College</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Military_College,_Sandhurst" title="Royal Military College, Sandhurst">Royal Military College, Sandhurst</a>, Boxer was gazetted a second lieutenant in the Lincolnshire Regiment in 1923 and served in that regiment for twenty-four years until 1947.  He served in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, then, following language and intelligence training, Charles Boxer was seconded to the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Army" title="Imperial Japanese Army">Imperial Japanese Army</a> in 1930 for three years as part of an exchange of Japanese and English officers. He was assigned to the 38th Infantry Regiment based at <a href="/wiki/Nara,_Nara" title="Nara, Nara">Nara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nara_Prefecture" title="Nara Prefecture">Nara Prefecture</a>, Japan. At the same time, he was assigned to the non-commissioned officers school at <a href="/wiki/Toyohashi" title="Toyohashi">Toyohashi</a>. His housekeeper concubine was a northerner from <a href="/wiki/Hakodate" title="Hakodate">Hakodate</a> on the island of <a href="/wiki/Hokkaido" title="Hokkaido">Hokkaido</a>. In 1933, he qualified as an official interpreter in the Japanese language. It was in Japan that he expanded his interest in Portuguese imperial history, concentrating his attention on the first disastrous experiment of European incursion into Japan and its catastrophic ending when <a href="/wiki/Tokugawa_shogunate" title="Tokugawa shogunate">Tokugawa</a> closed off the country to outside influence in the 1640s. The Japanese <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion" title="Crucifixion">crucified</a> hundreds of Christian missionaries and converts and for good measure executed a delegation of anxious envoys sent out from the Portuguese enclave of <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a> to make it entirely clear to the European outsiders that they meant what they said. This was the subject of Boxer's book <i>The Christian Century of Japan</i>. Boxer also took up the traditional Japanese sport of <a href="/wiki/Kendo" title="Kendo">kendo</a>, becoming one of only four British nationals recorded to have done this up until that time. Joining the regimental team he became proficient in the art to the level of being awarded the rank of <a href="/wiki/Dan_(rank)#In_Japanese_martial_arts" title="Dan (rank)">nidan</a>. He would later use his skill as a method of subterfuge in his profession as a spy when he was sent to <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> in 1936. On visits to the occupied territories he would often have a kendo bout, eat, drink <a href="/wiki/Scotch_whisky" title="Scotch whisky">scotch</a> and then pump the various Japanese officers and officials that he was socialising with for information in the true nature of a secret service agent.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Boxer returned to <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> for a two-year posting from 1935–36 to the military intelligence section of the <a href="/wiki/War_Office" title="War Office">War Office</a>. Posted to Hong Kong in 1936, he served as a General Staff Officer 3rd grade (GSO3) with British troops in China at Hong Kong, doing intelligence work. Between 1937 and 1941, Boxer, promoted from captain to major, became one of the key members of the <a href="/wiki/Far_East_Combined_Bureau" title="Far East Combined Bureau">Far East Combined Bureau</a>, a British intelligence organisation that extended from <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a> to <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>. By 1940, most of its Hong Kong office had been transferred to Singapore, leaving Boxer as the army's chief intelligence officer in the colony. In 1940, he was advanced to General Staff Officer 2nd grade (GSO2). Wounded in action during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hong_Kong" title="Battle of Hong Kong">Japanese attack on Hong Kong</a> on 8 December 1941, he was taken by the Japanese as a prisoner of war and remained in captivity until 1945. After his release, Boxer returned to Japan in February 1946 as a member of the British <a href="/wiki/Far_Eastern_Commission" title="Far Eastern Commission">Far Eastern Commission</a>, a post that he served until the next year. During his military career, Boxer published 86 publications on Far Eastern history with a particular focus on the 16th and 17th centuries.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_career">Academic career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C._R._Boxer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Academic career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Visayans_3.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Visayans_3.png/220px-Visayans_3.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Visayans_3.png/330px-Visayans_3.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Visayans_3.png/440px-Visayans_3.png 2x" data-file-width="495" data-file-height="660" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Visayans_3.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Visayan_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Visayan people">Visayan</a> kadatuan (royal) and his wife, wearing the distinctive color of his class (red), <a href="/wiki/Boxer_Codex" title="Boxer Codex">Boxer Codex</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>As a major in the British Army, Boxer had resigned from the service in 1947, when <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_London" title="King&#39;s College London">King's College London</a> offered him its ″<a href="/wiki/Cam%C3%B5es" class="mw-redirect" title="Camões">Camões</a> Chair of Portuguese″, a post founded and co-funded by <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>, and, at the time, the only such chair in the English-speaking world. During this period, the <a href="/wiki/School_of_Oriental_and_African_Studies" class="mw-redirect" title="School of Oriental and African Studies">School of Oriental and African Studies</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a> also appointed him as its first Professor of the History of the Far East, serving in that post for two years from 1951 to 1953.
</p><p>On retiring from the University of London in 1967, Boxer took up a visiting professorship at <a href="/wiki/Indiana_University" title="Indiana University">Indiana University</a>, where he also served as an advisor to the <a href="/wiki/Lilly_Library" title="Lilly Library">Lilly Library</a> located on its campus in <a href="/wiki/Bloomington,_Indiana" title="Bloomington, Indiana">Bloomington, Indiana</a>. From 1969 to 1972, Boxer held a personal chair in the history of European Overseas Expansion at <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>.
</p><p>Charles R. Boxer died at the age of 96. <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Maxwell" title="Kenneth Maxwell">Kenneth Maxwell</a> wrote after his death: ″To generations of historians of the Portuguese-speaking world C.R. Boxer was a true colossus. His highly original, pithy, and path-breaking books, monographs, and articles flowed forth with seeming effortlessness. Boxer’s works covered the history of early European intrusions into Japan and China during the sixteenth century, and splendid accounts of the opulence and decline of <a href="/wiki/Goa" title="Goa">Goa</a>, seat of Portugal's empire in Asia. In over 350 publications, all of the highest order of scholarship, Boxer wrote on sixteenth-century naval warfare in the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a>, the tribulations of the maritime trading route between Europe and Asia, a sparkling overview of Brazil during the eighteenth century in the age of gold strikes and frontier expansion, magnificent syntheses of both Dutch and Portuguese colonial history, as well as many pioneering comparative studies of local municipal institutions in Asia, Africa, and South America, race relations, and social mores. Famously in the 1960s at the height of <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Colonial_War" title="Portuguese Colonial War">Portugal's colonial wars</a> in Africa, he took on the "<a href="/wiki/Lusotropicalism" title="Lusotropicalism">Luso-tropicalist</a>" propaganda of the <a href="/wiki/Estado_Novo_(Portugal)" title="Estado Novo (Portugal)">Salazar dictatorship</a> by unravelling its roots in <a href="/wiki/Gilberto_Freyre" title="Gilberto Freyre">Gilberto Freyre</a>'s assertion of Portuguese colonial <a href="/wiki/Non-racialism" title="Non-racialism">non-racialism</a> and was thoroughly vilified for it by the regime and its apologists.″
<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=C._R._Boxer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He was married to Ursula Norah Anstice Tulloch, a woman commonly called the most beautiful in Hong Kong, when he met and had an affair with <a href="/wiki/Emily_Hahn" title="Emily Hahn">Emily Hahn</a>, the <i>New Yorker'</i>s China correspondent, who herself was involved with one of China's leading intellectuals, <a href="/wiki/Zau_Sinmay" class="mw-redirect" title="Zau Sinmay">Zau Sinmay</a>. In 1945, he married Hahn, with whom he had two daughters, Carola and <a href="/wiki/Amanda_Boxer" title="Amanda Boxer">Amanda Boxer</a>.
</p>

<p><b>Bibliographies</b>
