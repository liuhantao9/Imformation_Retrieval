<p><b>Karen Spärck Jones</b> <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_British_Academy" title="Fellow of the British Academy">FBA</a> (26 August 1935 – 4 April 2007) was a British computer scientist who was responsible for the concept of <a href="/wiki/Tf%E2%80%93idf" title="Tf–idf">inverse document frequency</a>, a technology that underlies most modern <a href="/wiki/Search_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Search engine">search engines</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tait_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tait-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In 2019, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> published her belated obituary in its series <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2018/obituaries/overlooked.html">Overlooked</a>, calling her "a pioneer of computer science for work combining statistics and linguistics, and an advocate for women in the field."<sup id="cite_ref-NYTobit_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karen_Sp%C3%A4rck_Jones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Karen Ida Boalth Spärck Jones was born in <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>, Yorkshire, England. Her father was Owen Jones, a lecturer in chemistry, and her mother was Ida Spärck, a Norwegian who moved to Britain during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.  They left Norway on one of the last boats out after the German invasion in 1940. Spärck Jones was educated at a grammar school in Huddersfield and then from 1953 to 1956 at <a href="/wiki/Girton_College,_Cambridge" title="Girton College, Cambridge">Girton College, Cambridge</a>, reading History, with an additional final year in Moral Sciences (philosophy). She briefly became a school teacher, before moving into Computer Science. During her career in Computer Science, she campaigned hard for more women to enter computing. She was married to fellow Cambridge computer scientist <a href="/wiki/Roger_Needham" title="Roger Needham">Roger Needham</a> until his death in 2003. She died 4 April 2007 at <a href="/wiki/Willingham,_Cambridgeshire" title="Willingham, Cambridgeshire">Willingham</a> in Cambridgeshire.<sup id="cite_ref-odnb_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-odnb-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Karen_Sp%C3%A4rck_Jones&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Spärck Jones worked at the Cambridge Language Research Unit from the late 1950s,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> then at <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University_Computer_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="Cambridge University Computer Laboratory">Cambridge University Computer Laboratory</a> from 1974, and retired in 2002, holding the post of Professor of Computers and Information, which she was awarded in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-odnb_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-odnb-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Prior to 1999 she was employed on a series of short-term contracts.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTobit_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> She continued to work in the Computer Laboratory until shortly before her death. 
</p><p>Her main research interests, since the late 1950s, were <a href="/wiki/Natural_language_processing" title="Natural language processing">natural language processing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Information_retrieval" title="Information retrieval">information retrieval</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-doi10.1108/eb026526_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-doi10.1108/eb026526-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> One of her most important contributions was the concept of <a href="/wiki/Inverse_document_frequency" class="mw-redirect" title="Inverse document frequency">inverse document frequency</a> (IDF) weighting in information retrieval, which she introduced in a 1972 paper.<sup id="cite_ref-doi10.1108/eb026526_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-doi10.1108/eb026526-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-idf_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-idf-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> IDF is used in most search engines today, usually as part of the <a href="/wiki/Tf-idf" class="mw-redirect" title="Tf-idf">tf-idf</a> weighting scheme.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1982 she became involved in the <a href="/wiki/Alvey" title="Alvey">Alvey</a> Programme.<sup id="cite_ref-NYTobit_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTobit-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  
</p><p>There is an annual <a href="/wiki/British_Computer_Society" title="British Computer Society">British Computer Society</a> lecture named in her honour.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In August 2017, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Huddersfield" title="University of Huddersfield">University of Huddersfield</a> renamed one of its campus buildings in her honour. Formerly known as Canalside West, the Spärck Jones building houses the University's School of Computing and Engineering.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<li>2013: <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eugene_Agichtein&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Eugene Agichtein (page does not exist)">Eugene Agichtein</a></li>
