<p><b>Lieutenant</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;nb 1&#93;</a></sup> (abbreviated <b>Lt</b>, <b>LT</b>, <b>LT(N)</b>, <b>Lt(N)</b>, <b>Lieut</b> and <b>LEUT</b>, depending on nation) is a <a href="/wiki/Commissioned_officer" class="mw-redirect" title="Commissioned officer">commissioned officer</a> rank in many nations' <a href="/wiki/Navy" title="Navy">navies</a>. It is typically the most senior of junior officer ranks. In most navies, the rank's insignia may consist of two medium gold braid stripes, the uppermost stripe featuring an <a href="/wiki/Executive_curl" title="Executive curl">executive curl</a> in many <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth of Nations</a>; or three stripes of equal or unequal width.
</p><p>The now immediately senior rank of <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_commander" title="Lieutenant commander">lieutenant commander</a> was formerly a senior naval lieutenant rank. Many navies also use a subordinate rank of <a href="/wiki/Sub-lieutenant" title="Sub-lieutenant">sub-lieutenant</a>. The appointment of "first lieutenant" in many navies is held by a senior lieutenant.
</p><p>A navy lieutenant ranks higher than an <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant#Army_ranks" title="Lieutenant">army lieutenant</a>; the navy rank of lieutenant is a <a href="/wiki/Ranks_and_insignia_of_NATO" title="Ranks and insignia of NATO">NATO OF-2</a> (US grade O-3) and ranks with an <a href="/wiki/Captain_(armed_forces)" title="Captain (armed forces)">army captain</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lieutenant_(navy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From at least 1580,<sup id="cite_ref-RNM_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RNM-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> the lieutenant on a ship had been the officer immediately subordinate to the captain. Before the <a href="/wiki/English_Restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="English Restoration">English Restoration</a>, lieutenants were appointed by their captains, and this inevitably led to abuses and to the widespread appointment of men of insufficient qualification. In 1677, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Pepys" title="Samuel Pepys">Samuel Pepys</a>, while he was <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_and_Financial_Secretary_to_the_Admiralty" title="Parliamentary and Financial Secretary to the Admiralty">Chief Secretary to the Admiralty</a>, introduced the first examination for lieutenant,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and thereafter their seniority was dated from the passing of this examination.
</p><p>A lieutenant was numbered by his seniority within the ship on which he served, so that a frigate (which was entitled to three) would have a first, a second, and a third lieutenant. A <a href="/wiki/First-rate" title="First-rate">first-rate ship</a> was entitled to six, and they were numbered accordingly. At first, a lieutenant's commission was given only for the particular ship in which he served, but after the loss of <a href="/wiki/HMS_Wager_(1739)" title="HMS Wager (1739)">HMS <i>Wager</i></a> in 1741 and the <a href="/wiki/Wager_Mutiny" title="Wager Mutiny">subsequent mutiny</a>, the Royal Navy changed its policy and lieutenants were given more general commissions upon passing their examination.<sup id="cite_ref-RNM_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RNM-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the early days of the naval rank, some lieutenants could be very junior indeed, while others could be on the cusp of promotion to captain; those lieutenants ranged across present-day army ranks from a second lieutenant through to a lieutenant colonel. As the rank structure of navies stabilized, and the ranks of commander, lieutenant commander, and sub-lieutenant were introduced, the rank of naval lieutenant became less wide-ranging and is today the equivalent of an army captain (NATO OF-2 or US O-3).
</p><p>In the United States Navy, promotion to lieutenant is governed by Department of Defense policies derived from the <a href="/wiki/Defense_Officer_Personnel_Management_Act" title="Defense Officer Personnel Management Act">Defense Officer Personnel Management Act</a> of 1980. DOPMA guidelines suggest that 95% of <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_(junior_grade)" title="Lieutenant (junior grade)">lieutenants (junior grade)</a> should be promoted to lieutenant after serving a minimum of two years at the lower rank.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rank_insignia">Rank insignia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lieutenant_(navy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rank insignia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The insignia of a lieutenant in many navies, including the Royal Navy,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> consists of two medium <a href="/wiki/Gold_braid" class="mw-redirect" title="Gold braid">gold braid</a> stripes (top stripe with loop) on a <a href="/wiki/Navy_blue" title="Navy blue">navy blue</a> or black background. This pattern was copied by the United States Navy and various air forces for their equivalent ranks grades, except that the executive curl is removed (see <a href="/wiki/Flight_lieutenant" title="Flight lieutenant">flight lieutenant</a>).
</p>

<h2><span id=".22First_lieutenant.22_in_naval_usage"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;First_lieutenant&quot;_in_naval_usage">"First lieutenant" in naval usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lieutenant_(navy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: &quot;First lieutenant&quot; in naval usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <b>first lieutenant</b> (1st Lt or 1LT) in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> and other Commonwealth navies, is a post or appointment, rather than a rank. Historically, the lieutenants in a ship were ranked in accordance with seniority, with the most senior being termed the first lieutenant and acting as the <a href="/wiki/Second-in-command" title="Second-in-command">second-in-command</a>. Although lieutenants are no longer numbered by seniority, the post of "first lieutenant" remains.
</p><p>In minor war vessels, <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frigate" title="Frigate">frigates</a>, the first lieutenant (either a lieutenant or lieutenant commander) is second in command, <a href="/wiki/Executive_officer" title="Executive officer">executive officer</a> (XO) and head of the executive branch; in larger ships, where a commander of the warfare specialisation is appointed as the executive officer, a first lieutenant (normally a lieutenant commander) is appointed as his deputy. The post of first lieutenant in a <a href="/wiki/Shore_establishment" class="mw-redirect" title="Shore establishment">shore establishment</a> carries a similar responsibility to that of the first lieutenant of a <a href="/wiki/Capital_ship" title="Capital ship">capital ship</a>.
</p><p>In the U.S. Navy or U.S. Coast Guard, the billet of first lieutenant describes the officer in charge of the <a href="/wiki/Deck_department" title="Deck department">deck department</a> or division, depending on the size of the ship. In smaller ships that have only a single deck division, the billet is typically filled by an ensign; while in larger ships, with a deck department consisting of multiple subordinate divisions, the billet may be filled by a lieutenant commander. On submarines and smaller Coast Guard cutters, the billet of first lieutenant may be filled by a <a href="/wiki/Petty_officer" title="Petty officer">petty officer</a>.
</p>
