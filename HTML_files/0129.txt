<p><b>Arawak</b> (Arowak/Aruák), also known as <b>Lokono</b> (<b>Lokono Dian</b>, literally 'people's talk' by its speakers), is an <a href="/wiki/Arawakan_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arawakan language">Arawakan language</a> spoken by the <a href="/wiki/Lokono" title="Lokono">Lokono</a> (Arawak) people of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> in eastern <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>, and <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is the eponymous language of the Arawakan language family.
</p><p>Lokono has an <a href="/wiki/Active%E2%80%93stative" class="mw-redirect" title="Active–stative">active–stative</a> syntax.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arawak_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lokono is a critically endangered language.<sup id="cite_ref-:02_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:02-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The Lokono language is most commonly spoken in South America. Some specific countries where this language is spoken include Guyana, Suriname, French Guiana, and Venezuela.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The percentage of living fluent speakers with active knowledge of the language is estimated to be 5% of the ethnic population.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> There are small communities of semi-speakers who have varying degrees of comprehension and fluency in Lokono that keep the language alive..<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It is estimated that there are around 2,500 remaining speakers (including fluent and semi-fluent speakers).<sup id="cite_ref-:4_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The decline in the use of Lokono as a language of communication is due to its lack of transmission from older speakers to the next generation. The language is not being passed to young children, as they are taught to speak the official languages of their countries. The oldest generation of speakers are around the age of 70 years of age of older.<sup id="cite_ref-:02_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:02-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arawak_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Lokono language is part of the larger Arawakan language family spoken by indigenous people in South and Central America along with the Caribbean.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It spans four countries of Central America — Belize, Honduras, Guatemala, Nicaragua — and eight of South America — Bolivia, Guyana, French Guiana, Surinam, Venezuela, Colombia, Peru, Brazil (and also formerly Argentina and Paraguay). With about 40 extant languages, it is the largest language family in Latin America.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arawak_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Arawak</i> is a tribal name in reference to the main crop food, the cassava root. It is commonly known as <i>Manioc.</i> The cassava root is a popular staple to millions in South America, Asia and Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-:8_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:8-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> It is a woody shrub grown in tropical or subtropical regions. The speakers of the Arawak language also identify themselves as, <i>Lokono</i>, which translates to "the people" . The Arawak language within itself is known as, <i>Lokono Dian</i>, "the people's speech".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_Names">Alternative Names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arawak_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Alternative Names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Alternative names of the same language include Arawák, Arahuaco, Aruak, Arowak, Arawac, Araguaco, Aruaqui, Arwuak, Arrowukas, Arahuacos, Locono, and Luccumi.<sup id="cite_ref-:102_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:102-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_Distribution">Geographic Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arawak_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geographic Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Lokono is an Arawakan language most commonly found to be spoken in eastern Venezuela, Guyana, Suriname and French Guiana. It was also formerly spoken on Caribbean islands such as Barbados and other neighboring countries. There are approximately 2,500 native speakers today. The following are regions where Arawak has been found spoken by native speakers.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">Vocabulary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arawak_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Vocabulary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender">Gender</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arawak_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Gender">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Arawak language there are two distinct genders of masculine and feminine. They are used in cross referencing affixes, in demonstratives, in nominalization and in personal pronouns. Typical pronominal genders for example are feminine and non-feminine. The markers go back to Arawak third person singular cross referencing&#160;: feminine <i>-(r)u,</i> masculine <i>-(r)i</i><sup id="cite_ref-:102_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:102-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Number">Number</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arawak_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Number">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Arawak Languages do distinguish singular and plural, however plural is optional unless the referent is a person. Markers used are <i>*-na/-ni</i> (animate/human plural) and <i>*-pe</i> (inanimate/animate non-human plural)<sup id="cite_ref-:102_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:102-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Possession">Possession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arawak_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Possession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Arawak nouns are fragmented into inalienably and alienably possessed. Inalienably crossed nouns include things such as body parts, terms for kinship and common nouns like food selections. Deverbal nominalization belong to that grouping. Both forms of possession  are marked with prefixes (A/Sa) . Inalienably possessed nouns have what is known as an "unpossessed" form (also known as 'absolute') marked with the suffix <i>*-tfi or *-hV.</i> Alienably possessed nouns take one of the suffixes <i>*-ne/ni, *-te, *-re, *i/e, or *-na.</i> All suffixes used as nominalizers.<sup id="cite_ref-:12_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Negation">Negation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arawak_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Negation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Arawak languages have a negative prefix <i>ma-</i> and attributive-relative prefix <i>ka-.</i> An example of the use is <i>ka-witi-w</i> (a woman with good eyes) and <i>ma-witti-w (a woman with bad eyes aka. blind) .</i>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_System">Writing System</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arawak_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Writing System">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Arawak language system has an alphabetical system similar to the Roman Alphabet with some minor changes and new additions to letters. The letters in brackets under each alphabetical letter is the IPA symbol for each letter.<sup id="cite_ref-:13_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:13-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
