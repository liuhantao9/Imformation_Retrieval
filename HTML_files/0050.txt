<p>The <b><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Music_industry" title="Music industry">music industry</a> standard <a href="/wiki/Record_chart" title="Record chart">record chart</a> in the United States for songs, published weekly by <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> magazine. Chart rankings are based on sales (physical and <a href="/wiki/Music_download" title="Music download">digital</a>), <a href="/wiki/Airplay" title="Airplay">radio play</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Music_streaming" class="mw-redirect" title="Music streaming">online streaming</a> in the United States.
</p><p>The weekly tracking period for sales was initially Monday to Sunday when <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_Holdings_PLC" class="mw-redirect" title="Nielsen Holdings PLC">Nielsen</a> started tracking sales in 1991, but was changed to Friday to Thursday in July 2015. This tracking period also applies to compiling online streaming data. Radio airplay, which, unlike sales figures and streaming, is readily available on a real-time basis, is tracked on a Monday to Sunday cycle (previously Wednesday to Tuesday).<sup id="cite_ref-tracking_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tracking-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> A new chart is compiled and officially released to the public by <i>Billboard</i> on Tuesdays.
</p><p>The first number one song of the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100 was "<a href="/wiki/Poor_Little_Fool" title="Poor Little Fool">Poor Little Fool</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Ricky_Nelson" title="Ricky Nelson">Ricky Nelson</a>, on August 4, 1958. As of the issue for the week ending on September 28, 2019, the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100 has had 1,089 different number one entries. The chart's current number-one song is "<a href="/wiki/Truth_Hurts_(song)" title="Truth Hurts (song)">Truth Hurts</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Lizzo" title="Lizzo">Lizzo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<p>Prior to 1955, <i>Billboard's</i> lead popularity chart was the <i>Honor Roll of Hits</i>, established in 1945. This chart ranked the most popular songs comprised by record and sheet sales, disk jockey, and juke box performances as determined by <i>Billboard'</i>s weekly nationwide survey.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> At the start of the rock era in 1955, there were three charts that measured songs by individual metrics:<sup id="cite_ref-NPR_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><i>Best Sellers in Stores</i> was the first <i>Billboard</i> chart, established in 1940. This chart ranked the biggest selling singles in retail stores, as reported by merchants surveyed throughout the country (20 to 50 positions).</li>
<li><i>Most Played by Jockeys</i> was <i>Billboard's</i> original airplay chart. It ranked the most played songs on United States radio stations, as reported by radio disc jockeys and radio stations (20 to 25 positions).</li>
<li><i>Most Played in Jukeboxes</i> ranked the most played songs in <a href="/wiki/Jukebox" title="Jukebox">jukeboxes</a> across the United States (20 positions). This was one of the main outlets of measuring song popularity with the younger generation of music listeners, as many radio stations resisted adding <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock and roll</a> music to their playlists for many years.</li></ul>
<p>Although officially all three charts had equal "weight" in terms of their importance, Billboard Magazine considers the <i>Best Sellers in Stores</i> chart when referencing a song's performance prior to the creation of the Hot 100.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> On the week ending November 12, 1955, <i>Billboard</i> published <b>The Top 100</b> for the first time. <i>The Top 100</i> combined all aspects of a single's performance (sales, airplay and jukebox activity), based on a point system that typically gave sales (purchases) more weight than radio airplay. The <i>Best Sellers In Stores</i>, <i>Most Played by Jockeys</i> and <i>Most Played in Jukeboxes</i> charts continued to be published concurrently with the new <i>Top 100</i> chart.
</p><p>On June 17, 1957, <i>Billboard</i> discontinued the <i>Most Played in Jukeboxes</i> chart, as the popularity of jukeboxes waned and radio stations incorporated more and more rock-oriented music into their playlists. The week ending July 28, 1958 was the final publication of the <i>Most Played by Jockeys</i> and <i>Top 100</i> charts, both of which had <a href="/wiki/Perez_Prado" class="mw-redirect" title="Perez Prado">Perez Prado</a>'s instrumental version of "<a href="/wiki/Patricia_(Perez_Prado_song)" title="Patricia (Perez Prado song)">Patricia</a>" ascending to the top.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>On August 4, 1958, <i>Billboard</i> premiered one main all-genre singles chart: the <i>Hot 100</i>. The Hot 100 quickly became the industry standard and <i>Billboard</i> discontinued the <i>Best Sellers In Stores</i> chart on October 13, 1958.
</p><p>The <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100 is still the standard by which a song's popularity is measured in the United States. The Hot 100 is ranked by radio airplay audience impressions as measured by Nielsen BDS, sales data compiled by <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_SoundScan" title="Nielsen SoundScan">Nielsen Soundscan</a> (both at retail and digitally) and streaming activity provided by online music sources.<sup id="cite_ref-NPR_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPR-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are several component charts that contribute to the overall calculation of the Hot 100. The most significant ones are:
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Hot_100_Airplay" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot 100 Airplay">Hot 100 Airplay</a></i>: <i>(per Billboard)</i> approximately 1,000 stations, "composed of <a href="/wiki/Adult_contemporary" class="mw-redirect" title="Adult contemporary">adult contemporary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_R%26B" title="Contemporary R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">gospel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latin_music_(genre)" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin music (genre)">Latin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_music" title="Christian music">Christian</a> formats, digitally monitored twenty-four hours a day, seven days a week. Charts are ranked by number of gross audience impressions, computed by cross-referencing exact times of radio airplay with <a href="/wiki/Arbitron" class="mw-redirect" title="Arbitron">Arbitron</a> listener data."</li>
<li><i>Hot Singles Sales</i>: <i>(per Billboard)</i> "the top selling singles compiled from a national sample of retail store, mass merchant and internet sales reports collected, compiled, and provided by <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_SoundScan" title="Nielsen SoundScan">Nielsen SoundScan</a>." The chart is released weekly and measures sales of physical commercial singles. With the decline in sales of physical singles in the US, many songs that become number one on this chart often do not even chart on the Hot 100.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Digital_Songs" title="Digital Songs">Digital Songs</a></i>: Digital sales are tracked by Nielsen SoundScan and are included as part of a title's sales points.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Streaming_Songs" title="Streaming Songs">Streaming Songs</a></i>: a collaboration between <i>Billboard</i>, Nielsen SoundScan and <a href="/wiki/National_Association_of_Recording_Merchandisers" class="mw-redirect" title="National Association of Recording Merchandisers">National Association of Recording Merchandisers</a> which measures the top streamed radio songs, on-demand songs and videos on leading online music services.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Compilation">Compilation</span></h2>
<p>The tracking week for sales and streaming begins on Friday and ends on Thursday, while the radio play tracking-week runs from Monday to Sunday. A new chart is compiled and officially released to the public by <i>Billboard</i> on Tuesday. Each chart is post-dated with the "week-ending" issue date four days after the charts are refreshed online (i.e., the following Saturday).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> For example:
</p>
<ul><li>Friday, January 1 – sales tracking-week begins, streaming tracking-week begins</li>
<li>Monday, January 4 – airplay tracking-week begins</li>
<li>Thursday, January 7 – sales tracking-week ends, streaming tracking-week ends</li>
<li>Sunday, January 10 – airplay tracking-week ends</li>
<li>Tuesday, January 12 – new chart released, with issue post-dated Saturday, January 16</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Policy_changes">Policy changes</span></h2>
<p>The methods and policies by which this data is obtained and compiled have changed many times throughout the chart's history.
</p><p>Although the advent of a singles music chart spawned chart historians and chart-watchers and greatly affected <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">pop culture</a> and produced countless bits of trivia, the main purpose of the Hot 100 is to aid those within the music industry: to reflect the popularity of the "product" (the singles, the albums, etc.) and to track the trends of the buying public. <i>Billboard</i> has (many times) changed its methodology and policies to give the most precise and accurate reflection of what is popular. A very basic example of this would be the ratio given to sales and airplay. During the Hot 100's early history, singles were the leading way by which people bought music. At times, when singles sales were robust, more weight was given to a song's retail points than to its radio airplay.
</p><p>As the decades passed, the recording industry concentrated more on album sales than singles sales. Musicians eventually expressed their creative output in the form of full-length albums rather than singles, and by the 1990s many record companies stopped releasing singles altogether (see <i>Album Cuts</i>, below). Eventually, a song's airplay points were weighted more so than its sales. <i>Billboard</i> has adjusted the sales/airplay ratio many times to more accurately reflect the true popularity of songs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Double-sided_singles">Double-sided singles</span></h3>
<p><i>Billboard</i> has also changed its Hot 100 policy regarding "two-sided singles" several times. The pre-Hot 100 chart "Best Sellers in Stores" listed popular A- and-B-sides together, with the side that was played most often (based on its other charts) listed first. One of the most notable of these, but far from the only one, was <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">Elvis Presley</a>'s "Don't Be Cruel" / "Hound Dog". During the Presley single's chart run, top billing was switched back and forth between the two sides several times. But on the concurrent "Most Played in Juke Boxes", "Most Played by Jockeys" and the "Top 100", the two songs were listed separately, as was true of all songs. With the initiation of the Hot 100 in 1958, A- and-B-sides charted separately, as they had on the former Top 100.
</p><p>Starting with the Hot 100 chart for the week ending November 29, 1969, this rule was altered; if both sides received significant airplay, they were listed together. This started to become a moot point by 1972, as most major record labels solidified a trend they had started in the 1960s by putting the same song on both sides of the singles provided to radio.
</p><p>More complex issues began to arise as the typical A-and-B-side format of singles gave way to <a href="/wiki/12_inch_singles" class="mw-redirect" title="12 inch singles">12 inch singles</a> and maxi-singles, many of which contained more than one B-side. Further problems arose when, in several cases, a B-side would eventually overtake the A-side in popularity, thus prompting record labels to release a new single, featuring the former B-side as the A-side, along with a "new" B-side.
</p><p>The inclusion of album cuts on the Hot 100 put the double-sided hit issues to rest permanently.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Album_cuts">Album cuts</span></h3>
<p>As many Hot 100 chart policies have been modified over the years, one rule always remained constant: songs were not eligible to enter the Hot 100 unless they were available to purchase as a single. However, on December 5, 1998, the Hot 100 changed from being a "singles" chart to a "songs" chart.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> During the 1990s, a growing trend in the music industry was to promote songs to radio without ever releasing them as singles. It was claimed by major record labels that singles were cannibalizing album sales, so they were slowly phased out. During this period, accusations began to fly of chart manipulation as labels would hold off on releasing a single until airplay was at its absolute peak, thus prompting a top ten or, in some cases, a number one debut. In many cases, a label would delete a single from its catalog after only one week, thus allowing the song to enter the Hot 100, make a high debut and then slowly decline in position as the one-time production of the retail single sold out.
</p><p>It was during this period that several popular mainstream hits never charted on the Hot 100, or charted well after their airplay had declined. During the period that they were not released as singles, the songs were not eligible to chart. Many of these songs dominated the <i>Hot 100 Airplay</i> chart for extended periods of time:
</p>
<ul><li>1995 <a href="/wiki/The_Rembrandts" title="The Rembrandts">The Rembrandts</a>: "<a href="/wiki/I%27ll_Be_There_for_You_(The_Rembrandts_song)" title="I&#39;ll Be There for You (The Rembrandts song)">I'll Be There for You</a>" (number one for eight weeks)</li>
<li>1996 <a href="/wiki/No_Doubt" title="No Doubt">No Doubt</a>: "<a href="/wiki/Don%27t_Speak" title="Don&#39;t Speak">Don't Speak</a>" (number one for 16 weeks)</li>
<li>1997 <a href="/wiki/Sugar_Ray" title="Sugar Ray">Sugar Ray</a> featuring <a href="/wiki/Super_Cat" title="Super Cat">Super Cat</a>: "<a href="/wiki/Fly_(Sugar_Ray_song)" title="Fly (Sugar Ray song)">Fly</a>" (number one for six weeks)</li>
<li>1997 <a href="/wiki/Will_Smith" title="Will Smith">Will Smith</a>: "<a href="/wiki/Men_in_Black_(song)" title="Men in Black (song)">Men in Black</a>" (number one for four weeks)</li>
<li>1997 <a href="/wiki/The_Cardigans" title="The Cardigans">The Cardigans</a>: "<a href="/wiki/Lovefool" title="Lovefool">Lovefool</a>" (number two for eight weeks)</li>
<li>1998 <a href="/wiki/Natalie_Imbruglia" title="Natalie Imbruglia">Natalie Imbruglia</a>: "<a href="/wiki/Torn_(Ednaswap_song)#Natalie_Imbruglia_version" title="Torn (Ednaswap song)">Torn</a>" (number one for 11 weeks)</li>
<li>1998 <a href="/wiki/Goo_Goo_Dolls" title="Goo Goo Dolls">Goo Goo Dolls</a>: "<a href="/wiki/Iris_(Goo_Goo_Dolls_song)" class="mw-redirect" title="Iris (Goo Goo Dolls song)">Iris</a>" (number one for 18 weeks)</li></ul>
<p>As debate and conflicts occurred more and more often, <i>Billboard</i> finally answered the requests of music industry artists and insiders by including airplay-only singles (or "album cuts") in the Hot 100.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="EPs">EPs</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">Extended play</a> (EP) releases were listed by <i>Billboard</i> on the Hot 100 and in pre-Hot 100 charts (Top 100) until the mid-to-late 1960s. With the growing popularity of albums, it was decided to move EPs (which typically contain four to six tracks) from the Hot 100 to the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a>, where they are included to this day.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Digital_downloads_and_online_streaming">Digital downloads and online streaming</span></h3>
<p>Since February 12, 2005, the <i>Billboard</i> Hot 100 tracks paid digital downloads from such internet services as <a href="/wiki/ITunes_Store" title="ITunes Store">iTunes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Musicmatch" class="mw-redirect" title="Musicmatch">Musicmatch</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rhapsody_(online_music_service)" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhapsody (online music service)">Rhapsody</a>. <i>Billboard</i> initially started tracking downloads in 2003 with the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Digital_Tracks" title="Hot Digital Tracks">Hot Digital Tracks</a> chart. However, these downloads did not count towards the Hot 100 and that chart (as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Hot_Digital_Songs" class="mw-redirect" title="Hot Digital Songs">Hot Digital Songs</a>) counted each version of a song separately (the chart still exists today along with Hot Digital Songs). This was the first major overhaul of the Hot 100's chart formula since December 1998.
</p><p>The change in methodology has shaken up the chart considerably, with some songs debuting on the chart strictly with robust online sales and others making drastic leaps. In recent years, several songs have been able to achieve 80-to-90 position jumps in a single week as their digital components were made available at online music stores. Since 2006, the all-time record for the biggest single-week upward movement was broken nine times.
</p><p>In the issue dated August 11, 2007, Billboard began incorporating weekly data from <a href="/wiki/Streaming_media" title="Streaming media">streaming media</a> and on-demand services into the Hot 100. The first two major companies to provide their statistics to Nielsen BDS on a weekly basis were <a href="/wiki/AOL" title="AOL">AOL</a> Music and <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!_Music" title="Yahoo! Music">Yahoo! Music</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> On March 24, 2012, Billboard premiered its <a href="/wiki/On-Demand_Songs" title="On-Demand Songs">On-Demand Songs</a> chart, and its data was incorporated into the equation that compiles the Hot 100.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> This was expanded to a broader <a href="/wiki/Streaming_Songs" title="Streaming Songs">Streaming Songs</a> chart in January 2013, which ranks web radio streams from services such as <a href="/wiki/Spotify" title="Spotify">Spotify</a>, as well as on-demand audio titles.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In February 2013, U.S. views for a song on <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> were added to the Hot 100 formula. "<a href="/wiki/Harlem_Shake_(song)" title="Harlem Shake (song)">Harlem Shake</a>" was the first song to reach number one after the changes were made.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The Hot 100 formula starting 2013 generally incorporates sales (35–45%), airplay (30–40%) and streaming (20–30%), and the precise percentage can change from week to week.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Remixes">Remixes</span></h3>
<p>A growing trend in the early first decade of the 21st century was to issue a song as a "remix" that was so drastically different in structure and lyrical content from its original version that it was essentially a whole new song. Under normal circumstances, airplay points from a song's album version, "radio" mix and/or <a href="/wiki/Dance_music" title="Dance music">dance music</a> remix, etc. were all combined and factored into the song's performance on the Hot 100, as the structure, lyrics and melody remained intact. Criticisms began when songs were being completely re-recorded to the point that they no longer resembled the original recording. The first such example of this scenario is <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Lopez" title="Jennifer Lopez">Jennifer Lopez</a>' "<a href="/wiki/I%27m_Real_(Jennifer_Lopez_song)" title="I&#39;m Real (Jennifer Lopez song)">I'm Real</a>". Originally entering the Hot 100 in its album version, a "remix" was issued in the midst of its chart run that featured rapper <a href="/wiki/Ja_Rule" title="Ja Rule">Ja Rule</a>. This new version proved to be far more popular than
the album version and the track was propelled to number one.
</p><p>To address this issue, Billboard now separates airplay points from a song's original version and its remix, if the remix is determined to be a "new song". Since administering this new chart rule, several songs have charted twice, normally credited as "Part 1" and "Part 2". The remix rule is still in place.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recurrents">Recurrents</span></h3>
<p><i>Billboard</i>, in an effort to allow the chart to remain as current as possible and to give proper representation to new and developing artists and tracks, has (since 1991) removed titles that have reached certain criteria regarding its current rank and number of weeks on the chart. Recurrent criteria have been modified several times and currently (as of 2015<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Billboard_Hot_100&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>), a song is permanently moved to "recurrent status" if it has spent 20 weeks on the Hot 100 and fallen below position number 50. Additionally, descending songs are removed from the chart if ranking below number 25 after 52 weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Exceptions are made to re-releases and sudden resurgence in popularity of tracks that have taken a very long time to gain mainstream success. These rare cases are handled on a case-by-case basis and ultimately determined by <i>Billboard</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s chart managers and staff.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Adjustment_of_tracking_week">Adjustment of tracking week</span></h3>
<p><i>Billboard</i> altered its tracking-week for sales, streaming and radio airplay in order to conform to a new Global Release Date, which now falls on Fridays in all major-market territories (United States product was formerly released on Tuesdays prior to June 2015). This modified tracking schedule took effect in the issue dated July 25, 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-tracking_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tracking-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Year-end_charts">Year-end charts</span></h2>
<p><i>Billboard</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s "chart year" runs from the first week of December to the final week in November. This altered calendar allows for <i>Billboard</i> to calculate <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Year-End" title="Billboard Year-End">year-end charts</a> and release them in time for its final print issue in the last week of December.
</p><p>Prior to Nielsen SoundScan, year-end singles charts were calculated by an inverse-point system based solely on a song's performance on the Hot 100 (for example, a song would be given one point for a week spent at position 100, two points for a week spent at position 99 and so forth, up to 100 points for each week spent at number one). Other factors including the total weeks a song spent on the chart and at its peak position were calculated into its year-end total.
</p><p>After <i>Billboard</i> began obtaining sales and airplay information from Nielsen SoundScan, the year-end charts are now calculated by a very straightforward cumulative total of yearlong sales, streaming, and airplay points. This gives a more accurate picture of any given year's most popular tracks, as the points accrued by one song during its week at number one in March might be less than those accrued by another song reaching number three in January. Songs at the peak of their popularity at the time of the November/December chart-year cutoff many times end up ranked on the following year's chart as well, as their cumulative points are split between the two chart-years, but often are ranked lower than they would have been had the peak occurred in a single year.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_media">Use in media</span></h2>
<p>The Hot 100 served for many years as the data source for the weekly radio countdown show <i><a href="/wiki/American_Top_40" title="American Top 40">American Top 40</a></i>. This relationship ended on November 30, 1991, as <i>American Top 40</i> started using the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100_Airplay" class="mw-redirect" title="Billboard Hot 100 Airplay">airplay-only side of the Hot 100</a> (then called <b>Top 40 Radio Monitor</b>). The ongoing splintering of <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_hit_radio" title="Contemporary hit radio">Top 40 radio</a> in the early 1990s led stations to lean into specific formats, meaning that practically no station would play the wide array of genres that typically composed each weekly Hot 100 chart.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_charts">Similar charts</span></h2>
<p>A new chart, the <a href="/wiki/Pop_100" title="Pop 100">Pop 100</a>, was created by <i>Billboard</i> in February 2005 to answer criticism that the Hot 100 was biased in favor of <a href="/wiki/Rhythmic_Top_40" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhythmic Top 40">rhythmic</a> songs, as throughout most of its existence, the Hot 100 was seen predominantly as a pop chart. It was discontinued in June 2009 due to the charts becoming increasingly similar.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Hot_100" title="Canadian Hot 100">Canadian Hot 100</a> was launched June 16, 2007. Like the Hot 100 chart, it uses sales and airplay tracking compiled by Nielsen SoundScan and <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_Broadcast_Data_Systems" title="Nielsen Broadcast Data Systems">BDS</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Japan_Hot_100" class="mw-redirect" title="Japan Hot 100">Japan Hot 100</a> was launched in the issue dated May 31, 2008, using the same methodologies as the Hot 100 charts for the U.S. and Canada, using sales and airplay data from SoundScan Japan and radio tracking service Plantech.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
