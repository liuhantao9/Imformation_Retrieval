<p>A <b>record producer</b> or <b>music producer</b> oversees and manages the <a href="/wiki/Sound_recording" class="mw-redirect" title="Sound recording">sound recording</a> and production of a band or performer's <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a>, which may range from recording one <a href="/wiki/Song" title="Song">song</a> to recording a lengthy <a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept album</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> A producer has many, varying roles during the recording process.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> They may gather musical ideas for the project, collaborate with the artists to select <a href="/wiki/Cover_tune" class="mw-redirect" title="Cover tune">cover tunes</a> or original songs by the artist/group, work with artists and help them to improve their songs, lyrics or arrangements.
</p><p>A producer may also:
</p>
<ul><li>Select <a href="/wiki/Session_musician" title="Session musician">session musicians</a> to play <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_section" title="Rhythm section">rhythm section</a> <a href="/wiki/Accompaniment" title="Accompaniment">accompaniment</a> parts or solos</li>
<li>Co-write<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Propose changes to the song arrangements</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vocal_coach" title="Vocal coach">Coach</a> the singers and musicians in the studio</li></ul>
<p>The producer typically supervises the entire process from preproduction, through to the <a href="/wiki/Sound_recording" class="mw-redirect" title="Sound recording">sound recording</a> and <a href="/wiki/Audio_mixing_(recorded_music)" title="Audio mixing (recorded music)">mixing</a> stages, and, in some cases, all the way to the <a href="/wiki/Audio_mastering" class="mw-redirect" title="Audio mastering">audio mastering</a> stage. The producer may perform these roles themselves, or help select the engineer, and provide suggestions to the engineer. The producer may also pay session musicians and engineers and ensure that the entire project is completed within the record label's budget.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Function">Function</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Record_producer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Function">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <i>record producer</i> or <i>music producer</i> has a very broad role in overseeing and managing the recording and production of a band or performer's <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a>. A producer has many roles that may include, but are not limited to, gathering ideas for the project, composing the music for the project, selecting songs or <a href="/wiki/Session_musician" title="Session musician">session musicians</a>, proposing changes to the song arrangements, coaching the artist and musicians in the studio, controlling the recording sessions, and supervising the entire process through <a href="/wiki/Audio_mixing_(recorded_music)" title="Audio mixing (recorded music)">audio mixing (recorded music)</a> and, in some cases, to the <a href="/wiki/Audio_mastering" class="mw-redirect" title="Audio mastering">audio mastering</a> stage. Producers also often take on a wider <a href="/wiki/Entrepreneur" class="mw-redirect" title="Entrepreneur">entrepreneurial</a> role, with responsibility for the budget, schedules, contracts, and negotiations.
</p><p>For <a href="/wiki/Grammy" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammy">Grammy</a> qualification <a href="/wiki/The_Recording_Academy" title="The Recording Academy">The Recording Academy</a> definition of a producer is:<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>The person who has overall creative and technical control of the entire recording project, and the individual recording sessions that are part of that project. He or she is present in the recording studio or at the location recording and works directly with the artist and engineer. The producer makes creative and aesthetic decisions that realize both the artist's and label's goals in the creation of musical content. Other duties include, but are not limited to; keeping budgets and schedules, adhering to deadlines, hiring musicians, singers, studios and engineers, overseeing other staffing needs and editing (Classical projects).
</p></blockquote>
<p>Writer Chris Deville explains it, "Sometimes a producer functions like a creative consultant — someone who helps a band achieve a certain aesthetic, or who comes up with the perfect violin part to complement the vocal melody, or who insists that a chorus should actually be a bridge. Other times a producer will build a complete piece of music from the ground up and present the finished product to a vocalist, like Metro Boomin supplying Future with readymade beats or Jack Antonoff letting Taylor Swift add lyrics and melody to an otherwise-finished “Out Of The Woods.”<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The artist of an album may not be a record producer or music producer for his/her album. While both contribute creatively, the official credit of "record producer" may depend on the record contract. <a href="/wiki/Christina_Aguilera" title="Christina Aguilera">Christina Aguilera</a>, for example, did not receive record producer credits until many albums into her career.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 2010s, the producer role is sometimes divided among up to three different individuals: <b><a href="/wiki/Music_executive" title="Music executive">executive producer</a></b>, vocal producer and <b>music producer</b>. An executive producer oversees project finances, a vocal producer (also known as a vocal arranger) oversees the vocal production, and a music producer oversees the creative process of recording and mixings.
</p><p>The music producer is also often a competent arranger, composer, musician or songwriter who can bring fresh ideas to a project. As well as making any songwriting and arrangement adjustments, the producer often selects and/or collaborates with the mixing engineer, who takes the raw recorded tracks and edits and modifies them with hardware and software tools to create a stereo or surround sound "mix" of all the individual voices sounds and instruments, which is in turn given further adjustment by a mastering engineer for the various distribution media. The producer also oversees the recording engineer who concentrates on the technical aspects of recording.
</p><p>Noted producer <a href="/wiki/Phil_Ek" title="Phil Ek">Phil Ek</a> described his role as "the person who creatively guides or directs the process of making a record", like a director would a movie. Indeed, in <a href="/wiki/Bollywood_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Bollywood music">Bollywood music</a>, the designation is actually <i>music director.</i> The music producer's job is to create, shape, and mold a piece of music. The scope of responsibility may be one or two songs or an artist's entire album – in which case the producer will typically develop an overall vision for the album and how the various songs may interrelate.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Record_producer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the beginning of the record industry, the producer role was technically limited to record, in one shot, artists performing live.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The immediate predecessors to record producers were the <a href="/wiki/Artists_and_repertoire" title="Artists and repertoire">artists and repertoire</a> executives of the late 1920s and 1930s who oversaw the "pop" product and often led session orchestras. That was the case of <a href="/wiki/Ben_Selvin" title="Ben Selvin">Ben Selvin</a> at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nathaniel_Shilkret" title="Nathaniel Shilkret">Nathaniel Shilkret</a> at <a href="/wiki/Victor_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="Victor Records">Victor Records</a> and Bob Haring at <a href="/wiki/Brunswick_Records" title="Brunswick Records">Brunswick Records</a>. By the end of the 1930s, the first professional recording studios not owned by the major companies were established, essentially separating the roles of <a href="/wiki/Artists_and_repertoire" title="Artists and repertoire">artists and repertoire</a> (A&amp;R) man and producer, although it wouldn't be until the late 1940s when the term "producer" became widely used in the industry.
</p><p>The role of producers changed progressively over the 1950s and 1960s due to technology. The development of <a href="/wiki/Multitrack_recording" title="Multitrack recording">multitrack recording</a> caused a major change in the recording process. Before multitracking, all the elements of a song (lead vocals, backup vocals, <a href="/wiki/Rhythm_section" title="Rhythm section">rhythm section</a> instrument <a href="/wiki/Accompaniment" title="Accompaniment">accompaniment</a>, solos and orchestral parts) had to be performed simultaneously. All of these singers and musicians had to be assembled in a large studio where the performance was 
recorded. With multitrack recording, the "bed tracks" (rhythm section accompaniment parts such as the <a href="/wiki/Bassline" title="Bassline">bassline</a>, drums, and rhythm guitar could be recorded first, and then the vocals and solos could be added later, using as many "takes" (or attempts) as necessary. It was no longer necessary to get all the players in the studio at the same time. A pop band could record their backing tracks one week, and then a <a href="/wiki/Horn_section" title="Horn section">horn section</a> could be brought in a week later to add horn shots and punches, and then a <a href="/wiki/String_section" title="String section">string section</a> could be brought in a week after that.
</p><p>Multitrack recording had another profound effect on music production: it enabled producers and audio engineers to create new sounds that would be impossible in a live performance style ordering. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_rock" title="Psychedelic rock">psychedelic rock</a> sound effects of the 1960s, e.g. playing back the sound of recorded instruments backward changing the tape to produce unique sound effects. During the same period, the instruments of popular music began to shift from the acoustic instruments of <a href="/wiki/Traditional_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Traditional music">traditional music</a> (<a href="/wiki/Piano" title="Piano">piano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Upright_bass" class="mw-redirect" title="Upright bass">upright bass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_guitar" title="Acoustic guitar">acoustic guitar</a>, strings, brass and wind instruments) to <a href="/wiki/Electric_piano" title="Electric piano">electric piano</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_organ" class="mw-redirect" title="Electronic organ">electronic organ</a>, <a href="/wiki/Synthesizer" title="Synthesizer">synthesizer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electric_bass" class="mw-redirect" title="Electric bass">electric bass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electric_guitar" title="Electric guitar">electric guitar</a>. These new instruments were electric or electronic, and thus they used <a href="/wiki/Instrument_amplifier" title="Instrument amplifier">instrument amplifiers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Speaker_enclosure" class="mw-redirect" title="Speaker enclosure">speaker enclosures</a> (speaker cabinets) to create sound.
</p><p>Electric and electronic instruments and amplifiers enabled performers and producers to change the tone and sound of instruments to produce unique electric sounds that would be impossible to achieve with acoustic instruments and live performers, such as having a singer do her own backup vocals or having a guitarist play 15 layers of backing parts to her own solo.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>New technologies like multitracking changed the goal of recording: A producer could blend together multiple takes and edit together different sections to create the desired sound. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Jazz_fusion" title="Jazz fusion">jazz fusion</a> Bandleader-composer <a href="/wiki/Miles_Davis" title="Miles Davis">Miles Davis</a>' album <i><a href="/wiki/Bitches_Brew" title="Bitches Brew">Bitches Brew</a></i>, the producer cut and edited sections together from extensive improvisation sessions.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MFQ_with_Phil_Spector.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/05/MFQ_with_Phil_Spector.jpg/220px-MFQ_with_Phil_Spector.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="168" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/05/MFQ_with_Phil_Spector.jpg/330px-MFQ_with_Phil_Spector.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/0/05/MFQ_with_Phil_Spector.jpg 2x" data-file-width="393" data-file-height="300" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MFQ_with_Phil_Spector.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Phil_Spector" title="Phil Spector">Phil Spector</a> producing <a href="/wiki/Modern_Folk_Quartet" title="Modern Folk Quartet">Modern Folk Quartet</a>, 1966</div></div></div>
<p>Producers like <a href="/wiki/Phil_Spector" title="Phil Spector">Phil Spector</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Martin" title="George Martin">George Martin</a> were soon creating recordings that were, in practical terms, almost impossible to realize in live performance. Producers became creative figures in the studio.  Other examples includes <a href="/wiki/Joe_Meek" title="Joe Meek">Joe Meek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Teo_Macero" title="Teo Macero">Teo Macero</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Wilson" title="Brian Wilson">Brian Wilson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Biddu" title="Biddu">Biddu</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Brian_Wilson_1976_crop.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Brian_Wilson_1976_crop.jpg/170px-Brian_Wilson_1976_crop.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="232" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Brian_Wilson_1976_crop.jpg/255px-Brian_Wilson_1976_crop.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/70/Brian_Wilson_1976_crop.jpg 2x" data-file-width="297" data-file-height="405" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Brian_Wilson_1976_crop.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Brian_Wilson" title="Brian Wilson">Brian Wilson</a> at a <a href="/wiki/Mixing_board" class="mw-redirect" title="Mixing board">mixing board</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brother_Studios" title="Brother Studios">Brother Studios</a>, 1976</div></div></div>
<p>Another related phenomenon in the 1960s was the emergence of the performer-producer. As pop acts like The Beatles, <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">The Rolling Stones</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Beach_Boys" title="The Beach Boys">The Beach Boys</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Kinks" title="The Kinks">The Kinks</a> gained expertise in studio recording techniques, many of these groups eventually took over as (frequently uncredited) producers of their own work. Many recordings by acts such as <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">The Rolling Stones</a>, The Kinks and <a href="/wiki/The_Who" title="The Who">The Who</a> are officially credited to their various producers at the time, but a number of these performers have since asserted that many of their recordings in this period were, either wholly self-produced (e.g. The Rolling Stones' Decca recordings) or collaborations between the group and their recording engineer (e.g. The Small Faces' <a href="/wiki/Immediate_Records" title="Immediate Records">Immediate</a> recordings, which were made with <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Studios" title="Olympic Studios">Olympic Studios</a> engineer <a href="/wiki/Glyn_Johns" title="Glyn Johns">Glyn Johns</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;nb 1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Beach Boys are probably the best example of the trend of artists becoming producers – within two years of the band's commercial breakthrough, group leader Brian Wilson had taken over from his father Murry, and he was the sole producer of all their recordings between 1963 and 1967. Alongside The Beatles and Martin, Wilson also pioneered many production innovations – by 1964 he had developed Spector's techniques to a new level of sophistication, using multiple studios and multiple "takes" of instrumental and vocal components to capture the best possible combinations of sound and performance, and then using tape editing extensively to assemble a perfect composite performance from these elements.
</p><p>At the end of the 20th century, digital recording and producing tools and widespread availability of relatively affordable computers with music software made music producing more accessible.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="American_women_and_record_producing">American women and record producing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Record_producer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: American women and record producing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to a 2018 study covering  by the <a href="/wiki/USC_Annenberg_School_for_Communication_and_Journalism" title="USC Annenberg School for Communication and Journalism">USC Annenberg</a> Inclusion Initiative, "The ratio of male to female producers across 300 popular songs is 49 to 1."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2019, <a href="/wiki/The_Recording_Academy" title="The Recording Academy">The Recording Academy</a>'s Task Force on Diversity and Inclusion announced the "Producer &amp; Engineer Inclusion Initiative." This initiative asks musicians, record labels, studios and others to consider at least two women for each producer or engineer position. Major artists, producers and organizations have signed on including <a href="/wiki/Taylor_Swift" title="Taylor Swift">Taylor Swift</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lady_Gaga" title="Lady Gaga">Lady Gaga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quincy_Jones" title="Quincy Jones">Quincy Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Jam" title="Pearl Jam">Pearl Jam</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Legend" title="John Legend">John Legend</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pharrell_Williams" title="Pharrell Williams">Pharrell Williams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pink_(singer)" title="Pink (singer)">Pink</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cardi_B" title="Cardi B">Cardi B</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maroon_5" title="Maroon 5">Maroon 5</a> and over 200 others.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2019, record producer <a href="/wiki/Linda_Perry" title="Linda Perry">Linda Perry</a> was nominated for a Grammy for Producer of the Year, Non-Classical. She was the first woman in over 15 years to be nominated for the award. When asked about the disparity between male and female record producer by Billboard, she attributed it to many women not being interested in record production.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Previous Grammy nominees for Producer of the Year include <a href="/wiki/Lauren_Christy" title="Lauren Christy">Lauren Christy</a> (2004), <a href="/wiki/Sheryl_Crow" title="Sheryl Crow">Sheryl Crowe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lauryn_Hill" title="Lauryn Hill">Lauryn Hill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Janet_Jackson" title="Janet Jackson">Janet Jackson</a>. None have won the award.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the classical music field, <a href="/wiki/Judith_Sherman" title="Judith Sherman">Judith Sherman</a> has won Grammy for Producer of the Year, Classical, five times and has been nominated twelve times. Anthony Tommasini, a music critic for The New York Times is quoted as stating, "In the struggling field of classical recording, it's the producers who take the real risks and make things happen."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Wilma_Cozart_Fine" title="Wilma Cozart Fine">Wilma Cozart Fine</a> produced hundreds of recordings for Mercury Records.
</p><p>Producer Wendy Page describes being a record producer, "The difficulties are usually very short-lived. Once people realize that you can do your job, sexism tends to lower its ugly head. I tend to create a happy studio 'family' where everyone is glad to be there, especially the artist. Good communication and diplomacy usually sort any little problems out."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The path to record producing for many female singer-songwriters is through self-producing their own albums. Major artists who are "record producers" (on their own albums) include <a href="/wiki/Taylor_Swift" title="Taylor Swift">Taylor Swift</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mariah_Carey" title="Mariah Carey">Mariah Carey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beyonc%C3%A9" title="Beyoncé">Beyonce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toni_Braxton" title="Toni Braxton">Toni Braxton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lady_Gaga" title="Lady Gaga">Lady Gaga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pink_(singer)" title="Pink (singer)">P!nk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adele" title="Adele">Adele</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lauryn_Hill" title="Lauryn Hill">Lauren Hill</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Missy_Elliott" title="Missy Elliott">Missy Elliott</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equipment_and_technology">Equipment and technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Record_producer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Equipment and technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Audio_mixer_faders.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Audio_mixer_faders.jpg/250px-Audio_mixer_faders.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="375" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Audio_mixer_faders.jpg/375px-Audio_mixer_faders.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Audio_mixer_faders.jpg/500px-Audio_mixer_faders.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="3456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Audio_mixer_faders.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mixing console.</div></div></div>
<p>There are numerous technologies utilized by record producers. In modern-day recordings, recording and mixing tasks are commonly centralized within computers using <a href="/wiki/Digital_audio_workstation" title="Digital audio workstation">digital audio workstations</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Pro_Tools" title="Pro Tools">Pro Tools</a>, <a href="/wiki/Logic_Pro" title="Logic Pro">Logic Pro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ableton_Live" title="Ableton Live">Ableton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steinberg_Cubase" title="Steinberg Cubase">Cubase</a>, and <a href="/wiki/FL_Studio" title="FL Studio">FL Studio</a>, which all are often used with third party virtual studio technology <a href="/wiki/Virtual_Studio_Technology" title="Virtual Studio Technology">plugins</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Logic Pro and Pro Tools are considered the industry standard <a href="/wiki/Digital_audio_workstation" title="Digital audio workstation">DAWs</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> However, there is also the main mixer, outboard effects gear, <a href="/wiki/MIDI" title="MIDI">MIDI</a> controllers, and the recording device itself.
</p><p>While most music production is done using sophisticated software, some musicians and producers prefer the sound of older analog technology. Professor Albin Zak claims that the increased automation of both newer processes and newer instruments reduces the level of control and manipulation available to musicians and producers.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Studio_application">Studio application</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Record_producer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Studio application">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Production has changed drastically over the years with advancing technology. While the producer's role has changed, their duties continue to require a broad knowledge of the recording process.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Tracking is the act of recording audio to a <a href="/wiki/Digital_audio_workstation" title="Digital audio workstation">DAW (digital audio workstation)</a> or in some cases to tape. Even though digital technologies have widely supplanted the use of tape in studios, the older term "track" is still used in the 2010s. Tracking audio is primarily the role of the <a href="/wiki/Audio_engineer" title="Audio engineer">audio engineer</a>. Producers work side by side with the artists while they play or sing their part and coach them on how to perform it and how to get the best technical accuracy (e.g., intonation). In some cases, the producer will even sing a backup vocal or play an instrument.
</p><p>Many artists are also beginning to produce and write their own music.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<li id="cite_note-10"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-10">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Similarly, although The Beatles' productions were credited to George Martin throughout their recording career, many sources now attest that Lennon and McCartney in particular had an increasing influence on the production process as the group's career progressed, and especially after the band retired from touring in 1966. In an extreme example of this, Martin actually went on a two-week vacation as The Beatles were recording <i><a href="/wiki/The_Beatles_(album)" title="The Beatles (album)">The White Album</a></i>; production of several completed tracks on the album were credited to The Beatles on internal paperwork at Abbey Road Studios, although the released LP gave sole production credit to Martin.</span>
