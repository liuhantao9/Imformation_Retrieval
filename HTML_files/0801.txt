	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Gunshot wound</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>A <b>gunshot wound</b> (<b>GSW</b>) is <a href="/wiki/Physical_trauma" class="mw-redirect" title="Physical trauma">physical trauma</a> due to a <a href="/wiki/Ammunition" title="Ammunition">bullet</a> from a <a href="/wiki/Firearm" title="Firearm">firearm</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pt2019_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pt2019-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Damage may include <a href="/wiki/Bleeding" title="Bleeding">bleeding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Broken_bones" class="mw-redirect" title="Broken bones">broken bones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organ</a> damage, infection of the wound, or loss of the ability to move part of the body.<sup id="cite_ref-ADAM2019_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADAM2019-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Damage depends on the part of the body hit, the path the bullet follows through the body, and the type and speed of the bullet.<sup id="cite_ref-Pt2019_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pt2019-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Long term complications can include <a href="/wiki/Lead_poisoning" title="Lead poisoning">lead poisoning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Post_traumatic_stress_disorder" class="mw-redirect" title="Post traumatic stress disorder">post traumatic stress disorder</a> (PTSD).<sup id="cite_ref-Ortho2019_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ortho2019-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ADAM2019_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ADAM2019-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Factors that determine rates of firearm violence vary by country.<sup id="cite_ref-GBD2016_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GBD2016-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> These factors may include the <a href="/wiki/Illegal_drug_trade" title="Illegal drug trade">illegal drug trade</a>, access to firearms, <a href="/wiki/Substance_misuse" class="mw-redirect" title="Substance misuse">substance misuse</a> including alcohol, <a href="/wiki/Mental_health" title="Mental health">mental health</a> problems, <a href="/wiki/Firearm_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Firearm law">firearm laws</a>, and social and economic differences.<sup id="cite_ref-GBD2016_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GBD2016-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bra2016_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bra2016-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Where guns are more common, altercations more often end in death.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Before management begins it should be verified the area is safe.<sup id="cite_ref-Bre2017_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bre2017-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This is followed by stopping major bleeding, than assessing and supporting the <a href="/wiki/ABC_(medicine)" title="ABC (medicine)">airway, breathing, and circulation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bre2017_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bre2017-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Firearm laws, particularly <a href="/wiki/Background_checks" class="mw-redirect" title="Background checks">background checks</a> and permit to purchase, decrease the risk of death from firearms.<sup id="cite_ref-Lee2017_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lee2017-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Safer <a href="/wiki/Gun_safe" title="Gun safe">firearm storage</a> may decrease the risk of firearm related deaths in children.<sup id="cite_ref-San2016_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-San2016-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015 about a million gunshot wounds occurred from interpersonal violence.<sup id="cite_ref-GBD2015Pre_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GBD2015Pre-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Firearms, globally in 2016, resulted in 251,000 deaths up from 209,000 in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-GBD2016_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GBD2016-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Of these deaths 161,000 (64%) were the result of assault, 67,500 (27%) were the result of <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a>, and 23,000 were accidents.<sup id="cite_ref-GBD2016_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GBD2016-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In the United States, guns result in about 40,000 deaths in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-AIM2019_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIM2019-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Firearm related deaths are most common in males between the ages of 20 to 24 years.<sup id="cite_ref-GBD2016_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GBD2016-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Economic costs due to gunshot wounds have been estimated at $US 140 billion a year in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Signs_and_symptoms">Signs and symptoms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gunshot_wound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Signs and symptoms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Trauma from a gunshot wound varies widely based on the bullet, velocity, mass, entry point, trajectory, and affected anatomy. Gunshot wounds can be particularly devastating compared to other <a href="/wiki/Penetrating_trauma" title="Penetrating trauma">penetrating injuries</a> because the trajectory and fragmentation of bullets can be unpredictable after entry. Additionally, gunshot wounds typically involve a large degree of nearby tissue disruption and destruction due to the physical effects of the projectile correlated with the bullet velocity classification.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The immediate damaging effect of a gunshot wound is typically severe <a href="/wiki/Bleeding" title="Bleeding">bleeding</a>, and with it the potential for <a href="/wiki/Hypovolemia" title="Hypovolemia">hypovolemic shock</a>, a condition characterized by inadequate delivery of oxygen to vital organs. In the case of traumatic hypovolemic shock, this failure of adequate oxygen delivery is due to blood loss, as blood is the means of delivering oxygen to the body's constituent parts. Devastating effects can result when a bullet strikes a vital organ such as the heart, lungs or liver, or damages a component of the central nervous system such as the spinal cord or brain. 
</p><p>Common causes of death following gunshot injury include <a href="/wiki/Bleeding" title="Bleeding">bleeding</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">low oxygen</a> caused by <a href="/wiki/Pneumothorax" title="Pneumothorax">pneumothorax</a>, catastrophic injury to the heart and major blood vessels, and damage to the brain or central nervous system. Non-fatal gunshot wounds frequently have mild to severe long-lasting effects, typically some form of major disfigurement such as <a href="/wiki/Amputation" title="Amputation">amputation</a> due to a severe <a href="/wiki/Bone_fracture" title="Bone fracture">bone fracture</a>, and may cause permanent disability.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pathophysiology">Pathophysiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gunshot_wound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pathophysiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The degree of tissue disruption caused by a <a href="/wiki/Projectile" title="Projectile">projectile</a> is related to the <a href="/wiki/Penetrating_trauma#Cavitation" title="Penetrating trauma">cavitation</a> the projectile creates as it passes through tissue. A bullet with sufficient energy will have a cavitation effect in addition to the penetrating track injury. As the bullet passes through the tissue, initially crushing then lacerating, the space left forms a cavity; this is called the <a href="/wiki/Penetrating_trauma#Permanent_cavitation" title="Penetrating trauma">permanent cavity</a>. Higher-velocity bullets create a pressure wave that forces the tissues away, creating not only a permanent cavity the size of the caliber of the bullet but also a <a href="/wiki/Penetrating_trauma#Temporary_cavitation" title="Penetrating trauma">temporary cavity</a> or secondary cavity, which is often many times larger than the bullet itself.<sup id="cite_ref-jtacs20160601_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jtacs20160601-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  The temporary cavity is the radial stretching of tissue around the bullet's wound track, which momentarily leaves an empty space caused by high pressures surrounding the projectile that accelerate material away from its path.<sup id="cite_ref-forensic200909_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forensic200909-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The extent of cavitation, in turn, is related to the following characteristics of the projectile:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">Kinetic energy</a>: KE = 1/2<i>mv</i><sup>2</sup> (where <i>m</i> is <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">mass</a> and <i>v</i> is <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">velocity</a>). This helps to explain why wounds produced by projectiles of higher mass and/or higher velocity produce greater tissue disruption than projectiles of lower mass and velocity. The velocity of the bullet is a more important determinant of tissue injury. Although both mass and velocity contribute to the overall energy of the projectile, the energy is proportional to the mass while proportional to the <i>square</i> of its velocity. As a result, for a constant velocity, if the mass is doubled, the energy is doubled; however, if the velocity of the bullet is doubled, the energy increases four times. The initial velocity of a bullet is largely dependent on the firearm. The US military commonly uses 5.56-mm bullets, which have a relatively low mass as compared with other bullets; however, the speed of these bullets is relatively fast. As a result, they produce a larger amount of kinetic energy, which is transmitted to the tissues of the target.<sup id="cite_ref-jtacs20160601_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jtacs20160601-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-injury20150701_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-injury20150701-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The size of the temporary cavity is approximately proportional to the kinetic energy of the bullet and depends on the resistance of the tissue to stress.<sup id="cite_ref-forensic200909_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forensic200909-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Muzzle_energy" title="Muzzle energy">Muzzle energy</a>, which is based on muzzle velocity, is often used for ease of comparison.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yaw_(rotation)" title="Yaw (rotation)">Yaw</a> Handgun bullets will generally travel in a relatively straight line or make one turn if a bone is hit. Upon travel through deeper tissue, high-energy rounds may become unstable as they decelerate, and may tumble (pitch and yaw) as the energy of the projectile is absorbed, causing stretching and tearing of the surrounding tissue.<sup id="cite_ref-jtacs20160601_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jtacs20160601-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fragmentation_(weaponry)" title="Fragmentation (weaponry)">Fragmentation</a> Most commonly, bullets do not fragment, and secondary damage from fragments of shattered bone is a more common complication than bullet fragments.<sup id="cite_ref-jtacs20160601_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jtacs20160601-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">Diagnosis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gunshot_wound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Diagnosis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gunshot_wound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Gunshot wounds are classified according to the speed of the projectile:
</p>
<ul><li>Low-velocity: &lt; 1,100&#160;ft/s (340&#160;m/s)</li>
<li>Medium-velocity: 1,100&#160;ft/s (340&#160;m/s) to 2,000&#160;ft/s (610&#160;m/s)</li>
<li>High-velocity:  2,000&#160;ft/s (610&#160;m/s) to 3,500&#160;ft/s (1,100&#160;m/s)</li>
<li>Hyper velocity: &gt; 3,500&#160;ft/s (1,100&#160;m/s)</li></ul>
<p>Bullets from <a href="/wiki/Handgun" title="Handgun">handguns</a> are sometimes less than 1,000&#160;ft/s (300&#160;m/s) but with modern pistol loads, they usually are slightly above 1,000&#160;ft/s (300&#160;m/s), while bullets from most modern <a href="/wiki/Rifle" title="Rifle">rifles</a> exceed 2,500&#160;ft/s (760&#160;m/s). One recently developed class of firearm projectiles is the hyper-velocity bullet, such cartridges are usually either wildcats made for achieving such high speed or purpose built factory ammunition with the same goal in mind. Examples of hyper velocity cartridges include the <a href="/wiki/.220_Swift" title=".220 Swift">.220 Swift</a>, <a href="/wiki/.17_Remington" title=".17 Remington">.17 Remington</a> and <a href="/wiki/.17_Mach_IV" title=".17 Mach IV">.17 Mach IV</a> cartridges. The US military commonly uses <a href="/wiki/5.56x45mm_NATO" class="mw-redirect" title="5.56x45mm NATO">5.56mm</a> bullets, which have a relatively low mass as compared with other bullets (40-62 grains); however, the speed of these bullets is relatively fast (Approximately 2,800&#160;ft/s (850&#160;m/s), placing them in the high velocity category). As a result, they produce a larger amount of kinetic energy, which is transmitted to the tissues of the target.<sup id="cite_ref-jtacs20160601_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jtacs20160601-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> However, one must remember that high kinetic energy does not necessarily equate to high stopping power, as incapacitation usually results from remote wounding effects such as bleeding, rather than raw energy transfer. High energy does indeed result in more tissue disruption, which plays a role in incapacitation, but other factors such as wound size and shot placement play as big of, if not a bigger role in stopping power and thus, effectiveness. <a href="/wiki/Muzzle_velocity" title="Muzzle velocity">Muzzle velocity</a> does not consider the effect of aerodynamic drag on the flight of the bullet for the sake of ease of comparison.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention">Prevention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gunshot_wound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Prevention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Medical organizations in the United States recommend criminal background check before people can buy a gun and those who are guilty of crimes of violence should be prevented from buying them.<sup id="cite_ref-AIM2019_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIM2019-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Safe storage of firearms is also recommended as is better mental health care and removal of guns from those at risk of suicide.<sup id="cite_ref-AIM2019_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIM2019-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In an effort to prevent <a href="/wiki/Mass_shootings" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass shootings">mass shootings</a> greater regulations on guns that can rapidly fire many bullets is recommended.<sup id="cite_ref-AIM2019_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIM2019-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Management">Management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gunshot_wound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Initial assessment for a gunshot wound is approached in the same way as other acute trauma using the <a href="/wiki/Advanced_trauma_life_support" title="Advanced trauma life support">advanced trauma life support</a> (ATLS) protocol.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> These include:
</p>
<ul><li>A) <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_tract" title="Respiratory tract">Airway</a> - Assess and protect airway and potentially the cervical spine</li>
<li>B) <a href="/wiki/Breathing" title="Breathing">Breathing</a> - Maintain adequate ventilation and oxygenation</li>
<li>C) Circulation - Assess for and control bleeding to maintain organ perfusion including <a href="/wiki/Focused_assessment_with_sonography_for_trauma" title="Focused assessment with sonography for trauma">focused assessment with sonography for trauma (FAST)</a></li>
<li>D) Disability - Perform basic neurological exam including <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Coma_Scale" title="Glasgow Coma Scale">Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)</a></li>
<li>E) Exposure - Expose entire body and search for any missed injuries, entry points, and exit points while maintaining body temperature</li></ul>
<p>Depending on the extent of injury, management can range from urgent surgical intervention to observation. As such, any history from the scene such as gun type, shots fired, shot direction and distance, blood loss on scene, and pre-hospital vitals signs can be very helpful in directing management. Unstable people with signs of bleeding that cannot be controlled during the initial evaluation require immediate <a href="/wiki/Exploratory_surgery" title="Exploratory surgery">surgical exploration</a> in the operating room.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Otherwise, management protocols are generally dictated by anatomic entry point and anticipated trajectory.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neck">Neck</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gunshot_wound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Neck">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PNI_Protocol.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/PNI_Protocol.jpg/310px-PNI_Protocol.jpg" decoding="async" width="310" height="129" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/PNI_Protocol.jpg/465px-PNI_Protocol.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/PNI_Protocol.jpg/620px-PNI_Protocol.jpg 2x" data-file-width="968" data-file-height="404" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PNI_Protocol.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Penetrating neck injury protocol.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>A gunshot wound to the neck can be particularly dangerous because of the high number of vital anatomical structures contained within a small space. The neck contains the <a href="/wiki/Larynx" title="Larynx">larynx</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trachea" title="Trachea">trachea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pharynx" title="Pharynx">pharynx</a>, <a href="/wiki/Esophagus" title="Esophagus">esophagus</a>, vasculature (<a href="/wiki/Common_carotid_artery" title="Common carotid artery">carotid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subclavian_artery" title="Subclavian artery">subclavian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vertebral_artery" title="Vertebral artery">vertebral arteries</a>; <a href="/wiki/Jugular_vein" title="Jugular vein">jugular</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brachiocephalic_vein" title="Brachiocephalic vein">brachiocephalic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vertebral_vein" title="Vertebral vein">vertebral veins</a>; thyroid vessels), and nervous system anatomy (<a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cranial_nerves" title="Cranial nerves">cranial nerves</a>, peripheral nerves, <a href="/wiki/Sympathetic_trunk" title="Sympathetic trunk">sympathetic chain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brachial_plexus" title="Brachial plexus">brachial plexus</a>). Gunshots to the neck can thus cause severe bleeding, airway compromise, and nervous system injury.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Initial assessment of a gunshot wound to the neck involves non-probing inspection of whether the injury is a penetrating neck injury (PNI), classified by violation of the <a href="/wiki/Platysma_muscle" title="Platysma muscle">platsyma</a> muscle.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> If the platsyma is intact, the wound is considered superficial and only requires local wound care. If the injury is a PNI, surgery should be consulted immediately while the case is being managed. Of note, wounds should not be explored on the field or in the emergency department given the risk of exacerbating the wound.
</p><p>Due to the advances in diagnostic imaging, management of PNI has been shifting from a "zone-based" approach, which uses anatomical site of injury to guide decisions, to a "no-zone" approach which uses a symptom-based algorithm.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The no-zone approach uses a hard signs and imaging system to guide next steps. Hard signs include airway compromise, unresponsive shock, diminished pulses, uncontrolled bleeding, expanding <a href="/wiki/Hematoma" title="Hematoma">hematoma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bruit" title="Bruit">bruits</a>/thrill, air bubbling from wound or extensive <a href="/wiki/Subcutaneous_emphysema" title="Subcutaneous emphysema">subcutaneous air</a>, stridor/hoarseness, neurological deficits.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> If any hard signs are present, immediate surgical exploration and repair is pursued alongside airway and bleeding control. If there are no hard signs, the person receives a <a href="/wiki/Computed_tomography_angiography" title="Computed tomography angiography">multi-detector CT angiography</a> for better diagnosis. A directed <a href="/wiki/Angiography" title="Angiography">angiography</a> or <a href="/wiki/Endoscopy" title="Endoscopy">endoscopy</a> may be warranted in a high-risk trajectory for the gunshot. A positive finding on CT leads to operative exploration. If negative, the person may be observed with local wound care.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chest">Chest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gunshot_wound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Chest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Important anatomy in the chest includes the <a href="/wiki/Thoracic_wall" title="Thoracic wall">chest wall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rib_cage" title="Rib cage">ribs</a>, spine, spinal cord, <a href="/wiki/Intercostal_nerves" title="Intercostal nerves">intercostal neurovascular bundles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">lungs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bronchus" title="Bronchus">bronchi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heart" title="Heart">heart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aorta" title="Aorta">aorta</a>, major vessels, esophagus, <a href="/wiki/Thoracic_duct" title="Thoracic duct">thoracic duct</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thoracic_diaphragm" title="Thoracic diaphragm">diaphragm</a>. Gunshots to the chest can thus cause severe bleeding (<a href="/wiki/Hemothorax" title="Hemothorax">hemothorax</a>), respiratory compromise (<a href="/wiki/Pneumothorax" title="Pneumothorax">pneumothorax</a>, hemothorax, <a href="/wiki/Pulmonary_contusion" title="Pulmonary contusion">pulmonary contusion</a>, tracheobronchial injury), cardiac injury (<a href="/wiki/Cardiac_tamponade" title="Cardiac tamponade">pericardial tamponade</a>), esophageal injury, and nervous system injury.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Initial workup as outlined in the Workup section is particularly important with gunshot wounds to the chest because of the high risk for direct injury to the lungs, heart, and major vessels. Important notes for the initial workup specific for chest injuries are as follows. In people with pericardial tamponade or tension pneumothorax, the chest should be evacuated or decompressed if possible prior to attempting <a href="/wiki/Tracheal_intubation" title="Tracheal intubation">tracheal intubation</a> because the positive pressure ventilation can cause <a href="/wiki/Hypotension" title="Hypotension">hypotention</a> or cardiovascular collapse.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Those with signs of a tension pneumothorax (asymmetric breathing, unstable blood flow, respiratory distress) should immediately receive a <a href="/wiki/Chest_tube" title="Chest tube">chest tube</a> (&gt; French 36) or needle decompression if chest tube placement is delayed.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> FAST exam should include extended views into the chest to evaluate for <a href="/wiki/Hemopericardium" title="Hemopericardium">hemopericardium</a>, pneumothorax, hemothorax, and <a href="/wiki/Peritoneal_fluid" title="Peritoneal fluid">peritoneal fluid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Those with cardiac tamponade, uncontrolled bleeding, or a persistent air leak from a chest tube all require surgery.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Cardiac tamponade can be identified on FAST exam. Blood loss warranting surgery is 1-1.5 L of immediate chest tube drainage or ongoing bleeding of 200-300 mL/hr.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Persistent air leak is suggestive of tracheobronchial injury which will not heal without surgical intervention.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Depending on the severity of the person's condition and if <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_arrest" title="Cardiac arrest">cardiac arrest</a> is recent or imminent, the person may require surgical intervention in the emergency department, otherwise known as an emergency department <a href="/wiki/Thoracotomy" title="Thoracotomy">thoracotomy</a> (EDT).<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, not all gunshot to the chest require surgery. Asymptomatic people with a normal <a href="/wiki/Chest_radiograph" title="Chest radiograph">chest X-ray</a> can be observed with a repeat exam and imaging after 6 hours to ensure no delayed development of pneumothorax or hemothorax.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> If a person only has a pneumothorax or hemothorax, a chest tube is usually sufficient for management unless there is large volume bleeding or persistent air leak as noted above.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_25-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Additional imaging after initial chest X-ray and ultrasound can be useful in guiding next steps for stable people. Common imaging modalities include chest <a href="/wiki/CT_scan" title="CT scan">CT</a>, formal <a href="/wiki/Echocardiography" title="Echocardiography">echocardiography</a>, angiography, <a href="/wiki/Esophagogastroduodenoscopy" title="Esophagogastroduodenoscopy">esophagoscopy</a>, esophagography, and <a href="/wiki/Bronchoscopy" title="Bronchoscopy">bronchoscopy</a> depending on the signs and symptoms.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Abdomen">Abdomen</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gunshot_wound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Abdomen">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:223px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Abdominal_Gunshot_Wound.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Abdominal_Gunshot_Wound.jpg/221px-Abdominal_Gunshot_Wound.jpg" decoding="async" width="221" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Abdominal_Gunshot_Wound.jpg/332px-Abdominal_Gunshot_Wound.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Abdominal_Gunshot_Wound.jpg/442px-Abdominal_Gunshot_Wound.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3472" data-file-height="2604" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Abdominal_Gunshot_Wound.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Abdominal gunshot wound</div></div></div>
<p>Important anatomy in the abdomen includes the <a href="/wiki/Stomach" title="Stomach">stomach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Small_intestine" title="Small intestine">small bowel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Large_intestine" title="Large intestine">colon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spleen" title="Spleen">spleen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pancreas" title="Pancreas">pancreas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kidney" title="Kidney">kidneys</a>, spine, diaphragm, descending aorta, and other abdominal vessels and nerves. Gunshots to the abdomen can thus cause severe bleeding, release of bowel contents, <a href="/wiki/Peritonitis" title="Peritonitis">peritonitis</a>, organ rupture, respiratory compromise, and neurological deficits.
</p><p>The most important initial evaluation of a gunshot wound to the abdomen is whether there is uncontrolled bleeding, inflammation of the <a href="/wiki/Peritoneum" title="Peritoneum">peritoneum</a>, or spillage of bowel contents. If any of these are present, the person should be transferred immediately to the operating room for <a href="/wiki/Laparotomy" title="Laparotomy">laparotomy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> If it is difficult to evaluate for those indications because the person is unresponsive or incomprehensible, it is up to the surgeon's discretion whether to pursue laparotomy, exploratory <a href="/wiki/Laparoscopy" title="Laparoscopy">laparoscopy</a>, or alternative investigative tools.
</p><p>Although all people with abdominal gunshot wounds were taken to the operating room in the past, practice has shifted in recent years with the advances in imaging to non-operative approaches in more stable people.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> If the person's vital signs are stable without indication for immediate surgery, imaging is done to determine the extent of injury.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ultrasound" title="Ultrasound">Ultrasound</a> (FAST) and help identify intra-abdominal bleeding and X-rays can help determine bullet trajectory and fragmentation.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> However, the best and preferred mode of imaging is high-resolution multi-detector CT (MDCT) with IV, oral, and sometimes rectal contrast.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_31-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Severity of injury found on imaging will determine whether the surgeon takes an operative or close observational approach.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Diagnostic_peritoneal_lavage" title="Diagnostic peritoneal lavage">Diagnostic peritoneal lavage</a> (DPL) has become largely obsolete with the advances in MDCT, with use limited to centers without access to CT to guide requirement for urgent transfer for operation.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_31-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extremities">Extremities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gunshot_wound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Extremities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shotgun_wound.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Shotgun_wound.JPG/220px-Shotgun_wound.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="115" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Shotgun_wound.JPG/330px-Shotgun_wound.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Shotgun_wound.JPG/440px-Shotgun_wound.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3745" data-file-height="1957" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shotgun_wound.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Acute penetrating trauma from a close-range shotgun blast injury to knee. Birdshot pellets are visible in the wound, within the shattered patella. The powder wad from the shotgun shell has been extracted from the wound, and is visible at the upper right of the image.</div></div></div>
<p>The four main components of extremities are <a href="/wiki/Bone" title="Bone">bones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blood_vessel" title="Blood vessel">vessels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nerve" title="Nerve">nerves</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Soft_tissue" title="Soft tissue">soft tissues</a>. Gunshot wounds can thus cause severe bleeding, <a href="/wiki/Bone_fracture" title="Bone fracture">fractures</a>, nerve deficits, and soft tissue damage. The Mangled Extremity Severity Score (MESS) is used to classify the severity of injury and evaluates for severity of skeletal and/or soft tissue injury, limb <a href="/wiki/Ischemia" title="Ischemia">ischemia</a>, shock, and age.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Depending on the extent of injury, management can range from superficial wound care to limb <a href="/wiki/Amputation" title="Amputation">amputation</a>.
</p><p>Vital sign stability and vascular assessment are the most important determinants of management in extremity injuries. As with other traumatic cases, those with uncontrolled bleeding require immediate surgical intervention.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> If surgical intervention is not readily available and direct pressure is insufficient to control bleeding, <a href="/wiki/Tourniquet" title="Tourniquet">tourniquets</a> or direct clamping of visible vessels may be used temporarily to slow active bleeding.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> People with hard signs of vascular injury also require immediate surgical intervention. Hard signs include active bleeding, expanding or pulsatile hematoma, bruit/thrill, absent distal pulses and signs of extremity ischemia.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For stable people without hard signs of vascular injury, an injured extremity index (IEI) should be calculated by comparing the blood pressure in the injured limb compared to an uninjured limb in order to further evaluate for potential vascular injury.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> If the IEI or clinical signs are suggestive of vascular injury, the person may undergo surgery or receive further imaging including CT angiography or conventional arteriography.
</p><p>In addition to vascular management, people must be evaluated for bone, soft tissue, and nerve injury. Plain films can be used for fractures alongside CTs for soft tissue assessment. Fractures must be <a href="/wiki/Debridement" title="Debridement">debrided</a> and stabilized, nerves repaired when possible, and soft tissue debrided and covered.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> This process can often require multiple procedures over time depending on the severity of injury.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epidemiology">Epidemiology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gunshot_wound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Epidemiology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_firearm-related_death_rate" title="List of countries by firearm-related death rate">List of countries by firearm-related death rate</a></div>
<p>In 2015 about a million gunshot wounds occurred from interpersonal violence.<sup id="cite_ref-GBD2015Pre_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GBD2015Pre-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Firearms, globally in 2016, resulted in 251,000 deaths up from 209,000 in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-GBD2016_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GBD2016-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Of these deaths 161,000 (64%) were the result of assault, 67,500 (27%) were the result of <a href="/wiki/Suicide" title="Suicide">suicide</a>, and 23,000 were accidents.<sup id="cite_ref-GBD2016_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GBD2016-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Firearm related deaths are most common in males between the ages of 20 to 24 years.<sup id="cite_ref-GBD2016_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GBD2016-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The countries with the greatest number of deaths from firearms are Brazil, the United States, Mexico, Colombia, Venezuela, and Guatemala which make up just over half the total.<sup id="cite_ref-GBD2016_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GBD2016-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In the United States in 2015 about half of the 44,000 people who died by suicide did so with a gun.<sup id="cite_ref-Hout2018_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hout2018-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2016, the countries with the highest rates of gun violence per capita were El Salvador, Venezuela, and Guatemala with 40.3, 34.8, and 26.8 violent gun deaths per 100,000 people respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The countries with the lowest rates of were Singapore, Japan, and South Korea with 0.03, 0.04, and 0.05 violent gun deaths per 100,000 people respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gunshot_wound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2016 about 893 people died due to gunshot wounds in Canada (2.1 per 100,000).<sup id="cite_ref-GBD2016_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GBD2016-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> About 80% were suicides, 12% were assaults, and 4% percent were an accident.<sup id="cite_ref-GC2019_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GC2019-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GC2019_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GC2019-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gunshot_wound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Gun_violence_in_the_United_States" title="Gun violence in the United States">Gun violence in the United States</a></div>
<p>In 2017 there were 39,773 deaths in the United States as a result gunshot wounds.<sup id="cite_ref-AIM2019_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIM2019-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Of these 60% were suicides, 37% were homicides, 1.4% were by law enforcement, 1.2% were accidents, and 0.9% were from an unknown cause.<sup id="cite_ref-AIM2019_14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIM2019-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> This is up from 37,200 deaths in 2016 due to a gunshot wound (10.6 per 100,000).<sup id="cite_ref-GBD2016_4-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GBD2016-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> With respect to those that pertain to interpersonal violence, it had the 31st highest rate in the world with 3.85 deaths per 100,000 people in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_38-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The majority of all homicides and suicides are firearm-related, and the majority of firearm-related deaths are the result of murder and suicide.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> When sorted by GDP, however, the United States has a much higher violent gun death rate compared to other developed countries, with over 10 times the number of firearms assault deaths than the next four highest GDP countries combined.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Gunshot violence is the third most costly cause of injury and the fourth most expensive form of hospitalization in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-scand20100617_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scand20100617-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gunshot_wound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Hieronymus_Brunschwig" title="Hieronymus Brunschwig">Hieronymus Brunschwig</a> argued that infection of gunshot wounds was a result of poisoning by <a href="/wiki/Gunpowder" title="Gunpowder">gunpowder</a>, which provided the rationale for <a href="/wiki/Cauterization" title="Cauterization">cauterizing</a> wounds. <a href="/wiki/Ambroise_Par%C3%A9" title="Ambroise Paré">Ambroise Paré</a> wrote in his 1545 book, <i>The Method of Curing Wounds Caused by Arquebus and Firearms,</i> that wounds should be sealed rather than cauterized.<sup id="cite_ref-historypmc_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-historypmc-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pruitt2006_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pruitt2006-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Hunter_(surgeon)" title="John Hunter (surgeon)">John Hunter</a> argued that infection was not caused by poisoning.
</p><p>Until the 1880s, the standard practice for treating a gunshot wound called for physicians to insert their <a href="/wiki/Asepsis" title="Asepsis">unsterilized</a> fingers into the wound to probe and locate the path of the bullet.<sup id="cite_ref-Schaffer_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schaffer-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Surgically opening abdominal cavities to repair gunshot wounds,<sup id="cite_ref-crane_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crane-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Germ_theory_of_disease" title="Germ theory of disease">germ theory</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Lister,_1st_Baron_Lister" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph Lister, 1st Baron Lister">Joseph Lister</a>'s technique for antiseptic surgery using diluted <a href="/wiki/Carbolic_acid" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbolic acid">carbolic acid</a>, first demonstrated in 1865, had not yet been accepted as standard practice. For example, sixteen doctors attended to President <a href="/wiki/James_A._Garfield" title="James A. Garfield">James A. Garfield</a> after he was shot in 1881, and most probed the wound with their fingers or dirty instruments.<sup id="cite_ref-eyewitness_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-eyewitness-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Historians agree that massive infection was a significant factor in <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_James_A._Garfield#Treatment_and_death" title="Assassination of James A. Garfield">Garfield's death</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Schaffer_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Schaffer-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At almost the same time, in <a href="/wiki/Tombstone,_Arizona" title="Tombstone, Arizona">Tombstone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arizona_Territory" title="Arizona Territory">Arizona Territory</a>, on 13 July 1881, <a href="/wiki/George_E._Goodfellow" title="George E. Goodfellow">George E. Goodfellow</a> performed the first <a href="/wiki/Laparotomy" title="Laparotomy">laparotomy</a> to treat an abdominal gunshot wound.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>M-9</span></sup> Goodfellow pioneered the use of sterile techniques in treating gunshot wounds,<sup id="cite_ref-cochise100_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cochise100-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> washing the person's wound and his hands with lye soap or whisky.<sup id="cite_ref-prescott_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prescott-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> He became America's leading authority on gunshot wounds<sup id="cite_ref-oldwest_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oldwest-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> and is credited as the United States' first civilian <a href="/wiki/Trauma_surgery" title="Trauma surgery">trauma surgeon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Today's surgeons consider any projectile with a velocity of 330&#160;m/s to be low or subsonic and practically all modern weapons have bullet speeds that are usually higher (for example, a 124 grain <a href="/wiki/9x19mm_Parabellum" class="mw-redirect" title="9x19mm Parabellum">9mm</a> bullet fired from a 4-inch barrel has a velocity of roughly 350&#160;m/s).  By contrast, mid-nineteenth-century handguns such as the Colt revolvers used during the American Civil War had muzzle velocities of just 230–260&#160;m/s and their powder and ball predecessors had velocities of 167&#160;m/s or less.  Many of these weapons were still in circulation in America during the 1860s.  Unlike today's high-velocity bullets, nineteenth-century balls produced almost little or no cavitation (a kinetic shockwave that causes serious tissue damage) and, being slower moving, they were liable to lodge in unusual locations at odds with their trajectory.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_R%C3%B6ntgen" title="Wilhelm Röntgen">Wilhelm Röntgen</a>'s discovery of <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-rays</a> in 1895 led to the use of radiographs to locate bullets in wounded soldiers.<sup id="cite_ref-historypmc_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-historypmc-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Survival rates for gunshot wounds improved among US military personnel during the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam Wars</a>, due in part to helicopter evacuation, along with improvements in resuscitation and battlefield medicine.<sup id="cite_ref-historypmc_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-historypmc-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Similar improvements were seen in US trauma practices during the <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Some military trauma care practices are disseminated by citizen soldiers who return to civilian practice.<sup id="cite_ref-historypmc_43-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-historypmc-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> One such practice is to transfer major trauma cases to an operating theater as soon as possible, to stop <a href="/wiki/Internal_bleeding" title="Internal bleeding">internal bleeding</a>. Within the United States, the survival rate for gunshot wounds has increased, leading to apparent declines in the gun death rate in states that have stable rates of gunshot hospitalizations.<sup id="cite_ref-JenaSun2014_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JenaSun2014-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gunshot_wound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Research into gunshot wounds is hampered by lack of funding. Federal-funded research into firearm injury, <a href="/wiki/Epidemiology" title="Epidemiology">epidemiology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gun_violence_in_the_United_States" title="Gun violence in the United States">violence</a>, and prevention is minimal. Pressure from the <a href="/wiki/National_Rifle_Association" title="National Rifle Association">National Rifle Association</a>, the gun lobby, and some gun owners, expressing concerns regarding increased government controls on freedom and guns, is highly effective in preventing related research.<sup id="cite_ref-jtacs20160601_17-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jtacs20160601-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
