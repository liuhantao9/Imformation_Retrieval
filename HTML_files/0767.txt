<p><b>Flemish</b> (<i>Vlaams</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Omniglot_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Omniglot-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> also called <b>Flemish Dutch</b> (<i>Vlaams-Nederlands</i>), <b>Belgian Dutch</b> (<i>Belgisch-Nederlands</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Dutch" title="Help:IPA/Dutch">[ˈbɛlɣis ˈneːdərlɑnts]</a></span>&#32;<span class="nowrap" style="font-size:85%">(<span class="unicode haudio"><span class="fn"><span style="white-space:nowrap;margin-right:.25em;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nl-Belgisch-Nederlands.ogg" title="About this sound"><img alt="About this sound" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="20" data-file-height="20" /></a></span><a href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c4/Nl-Belgisch-Nederlands.ogg" class="internal" title="Nl-Belgisch-Nederlands.ogg">listen</a></span></span>)</span>), or <b>Southern Dutch</b> (<i>Zuid-Nederlands</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Low_Franconian" class="mw-redirect" title="Low Franconian">Low Franconian</a> <a href="/wiki/Dialect_cluster" class="mw-redirect" title="Dialect cluster">dialect cluster</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch language</a>, as spoken in <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Historical_region" title="Historical region">historical region</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Flemings" class="mw-redirect" title="Flemings">Flemings</a>, the dominant <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic group</a> of the region.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Along with Flanders, it is also spoken to some extent in <a href="/wiki/French_Flanders" title="French Flanders">French Flanders</a> and the Dutch <a href="/wiki/Zeelandic_Flanders" title="Zeelandic Flanders">Zeelandic Flanders</a> by approximately 6.5 million people.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term <i>Flemish</i> itself has become ambiguous. Nowadays, it is used in at least five ways, depending on the context. These include:
</p>
<ol><li>An indication of Dutch written and spoken in <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a> including the Dutch <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">standard language</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Nonstandard_dialect" title="Nonstandard dialect">non-standardized dialects</a>, including intermediate forms between vernacular dialects and the standard. Some linguists avoid the term Flemish in this context and prefer the designation Belgian-Dutch or South-Dutch <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Dutch_in_Belgium" title="Dutch in Belgium">Dutch in Belgium</a></div></li>
<li>A synonym for the so-called intermediate language in <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a> region, the <i>Tussentaal</i></li>
<li>An indication of the <a href="/wiki/Nonstandard_dialect" title="Nonstandard dialect">non-standardized dialects</a> and <a href="/wiki/Regiolect" class="mw-redirect" title="Regiolect">regiolects</a> of <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a> region</li>
<li>An indication of the non-standardized dialects of only the former <a href="/wiki/County_of_Flanders" title="County of Flanders">County of Flanders</a>, ie the current provinces of <a href="/wiki/West_Flanders" title="West Flanders">West Flanders</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Flanders" title="East Flanders">East Flanders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zeelandic_Flanders" title="Zeelandic Flanders">Zeelandic Flanders</a> and <a href="/wiki/Frans-Vlaanderen" class="mw-redirect" title="Frans-Vlaanderen">Frans-Vlaanderen</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>An indication of the non-standardized <a href="/wiki/West_Flemish" title="West Flemish">West Flemish</a> dialects of the province of <a href="/wiki/West_Flanders" title="West Flanders">West Flanders</a>, the Dutch <a href="/wiki/Zeelandic_Flanders" title="Zeelandic Flanders">Zeelandic Flanders</a> and French <a href="/wiki/Frans-Vlaanderen" class="mw-redirect" title="Frans-Vlaanderen">Frans-Vlaanderen</a> <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/West_Flemish" title="West Flemish">West Flemish</a></div></li></ol>
<p><a href="/wiki/MultiTree" class="mw-redirect" title="MultiTree">MultiTree</a> considers Flemish to include the four principal Dutch dialects in the Flemish region (Flanders): <a href="/wiki/Brabantian" class="mw-redirect" title="Brabantian">Brabantian</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Flemish" title="East Flemish">East Flemish</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Flemish" title="West Flemish">West Flemish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limburgish" title="Limburgish">Limburgish</a> as well as three other dialects.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Glottolog" title="Glottolog">Glottolog</a> considers <a class="mw-selflink selflink">Flemish</a> to be a separate (regional) language, which includes the dialects of Antverpian (the dialect of the city of <a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a>), French Flemish, West Flemish, East Flemish and Limburgish.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Ethnologue considers Limburgish and West Flemish to be separate (regional) languages.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flemish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Languages_Benelux.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Languages_Benelux.PNG/220px-Languages_Benelux.PNG" decoding="async" width="220" height="861" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Languages_Benelux.PNG/330px-Languages_Benelux.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Languages_Benelux.PNG/440px-Languages_Benelux.PNG 2x" data-file-width="699" data-file-height="2737" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Languages_Benelux.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map showing the dialects spoken in the <a href="/wiki/Benelux" title="Benelux">Benelux</a>: many people in Flanders speak a dialect and the common Flemish, and understand spoken Dutch; in writing, the dialects are hardly used, while Flemish and Dutch are nearly identical in this regard</div></div></div><p>Dutch is the <a href="/wiki/National_language" title="National language">majority language</a> in northern Belgium, being used in written language by three-fifths of the population of Belgium. It is one of the three national languages of Belgium, together with <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> and <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, and is the only official language of the <a href="/wiki/Flemish_Region" title="Flemish Region">Flemish Region</a>.
</p><p>The various Dutch dialects spoken in Belgium contain a number of lexical and a good amount of grammatical features which distinguish them from the standard Dutch. Basic Dutch words can have a completely different meaning in Flemish or imply different context.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> As in the Netherlands, the pronunciation of Standard Dutch is affected by the native dialect of the speaker.
</p><p>All Dutch dialect groups spoken in Belgium are spoken in adjacent areas of the Netherlands as well. East Flemish forms a continuum with both Brabantic and West Flemish. Standard Dutch is primarily based on the <a href="/wiki/Hollandic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hollandic">Hollandic</a> dialect&#8201;<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> (spoken in the Western provinces of the Netherlands) and to a lesser extent on <a href="/wiki/Brabantian" class="mw-redirect" title="Brabantian">Brabantian</a>, which is the dominant dialect in <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>, as well as in the south of the Netherlands.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tussentaal">Tussentaal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flemish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tussentaal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The supra-regional, semi-standardized colloquial form (<a href="/wiki/Mesolect" class="mw-redirect" title="Mesolect">mesolect</a>) of Dutch spoken in Belgium uses the vocabulary and the sound inventory of the Brabantic dialects. It is often called <b><a href="/wiki/Tussentaal" class="mw-redirect" title="Tussentaal">Tussentaal</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;&#91;<a href="https://nl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tussentaal" class="extiw" title="nl:Tussentaal">nl</a>&#93;</span></b> ("in-between-language" or "intermediate language", intermediate between dialects and standard Dutch).<sup id="cite_ref-kul_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kul-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Despite its name, Brabantian is the dominant contributor to the Flemish Dutch <i>tussentaal</i>.  
</p><p>It is a rather informal <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">variety of speech</a>, which occupies an intermediate position between <a href="/wiki/Nonstandard_dialect" title="Nonstandard dialect">vernacular dialects</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">standard language</a>. It incorporates phonetic, lexical and grammatical elements not part of the standard language but drawn from local dialects.
</p><p>It is a relatively new phenomenon that has been gaining popularity during the past decades. Some linguists note that it seems to be undergoing a process of (limited) standardisation<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> or that it is evolving into a <a href="/wiki/Koin%C3%A9_language" title="Koiné language">koiné variety</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RysTaeldeman_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RysTaeldeman-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Tussentaal</i> is slowly gaining popularity in Flanders because it is used a lot in television dramas and comedies. Often, middle-class characters in a television series will be speaking <i>tussentaal</i>, lower-class characters use the dialect of the location where the show is set, and upper-class characters will speak Standard Dutch.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> That has given <i>tussentaal</i> the status of normalcy in Flanders. It is slowly being accepted by the general population, but it has met with objections from writers and academics who argue that it dilutes the usage of Standard Dutch.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <i>Tussentaal</i> is used in entertainment television but rarely in informative programmes (like the news), which normally use Standard Dutch.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Flemish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The English adjective <i>Flemish</i> (first attested as <i>flemmysshe</i>, c. 1325;<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> cf. <i>Flæming</i>, c. 1150),<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> meaning "from <a href="/wiki/County_of_Flanders" title="County of Flanders">Flanders</a>", was probably borrowed from <a href="/wiki/Old_Frisian" title="Old Frisian">Old Frisian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Old_Dutch" title="Old Dutch">Old Dutch</a> form is <i>flāmisk</i>, which becomes <i>vlamesc</i>, <i>vlaemsch</i> in <a href="/wiki/Middle_Dutch" title="Middle Dutch">Middle Dutch</a> and <i>Vlaams</i> in <a href="/wiki/Modern_Dutch" class="mw-redirect" title="Modern Dutch">Modern Dutch</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The word <i>Vlaams</i> itself is derived from <i>flâm</i>, <sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> an <a href="/wiki/Ingaevones" title="Ingaevones">Ingaevones</a> word, from the Germanic <i>flauma</i> (a cognate to the English <i>flow</i> and the Old German word <i>flaum</i>), which means 'flow or current'. The name <i>Vlaanderen</i> was formed from a stem <i>flâm-</i>, with a suffix <i>-ðr-</i> attached.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
