<p><b>Amapá</b> (<small>Portuguese pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Portuguese" title="Help:IPA/Portuguese">[ɐmɐˈpa]</a></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/States_of_Brazil" title="States of Brazil">state</a> located in the <a href="/wiki/North_Region,_Brazil" title="North Region, Brazil">northern region</a> of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>. It is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Brazilian_states_by_population" title="List of Brazilian states by population">second least populous state</a> and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Brazilian_states_by_area" title="List of Brazilian states by area">eighteenth largest by area</a>. Located in the far northern part of the country, Amapá is bordered clockwise by <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a> to the north, the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> to the east, <a href="/wiki/Par%C3%A1" title="Pará">Pará</a> to the south and west, and <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> to the northwest. The capital and largest city is <a href="/wiki/Macap%C3%A1" title="Macapá">Macapá</a>.
</p><p>In the colonial period the region was called <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Guiana" class="mw-redirect" title="Portuguese Guiana">Portuguese Guiana</a> and was part of <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portugal's</a> <a href="/wiki/State_of_Brazil" title="State of Brazil">State of Brazil</a>. Later, the region was distinguished from the other <a href="/wiki/The_Guianas" title="The Guianas">Guianas</a>. Amapá was once part of Pará, but became a separate territory in 1943, and a state in 1990.<sup id="cite_ref-b_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-b-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The dominant feature of the region, and 90 percent of its total area, is the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_Rainforest" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon Rainforest">Amazon Rainforest</a>. Unexplored forests occupy 70 percent of Amapá, and <a href="/wiki/Tumucumaque_Mountains_National_Park" title="Tumucumaque Mountains National Park">Tumucumaque Mountains National Park</a>, established in 2002, is the largest tropical forest park in the world. The estuary of the <a href="/wiki/Oyapock_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Oyapock River">River Oiapoque</a> is the northernmost point of coastal Brazil.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amap%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the colonial era from 1637 to 1654 the Amapá region was merged into the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Captaincy_of_Para&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Captaincy of Para (page does not exist)">Captaincy of Para</a>, this was the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Captaincy_of_Cabo_de_Norte&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Captaincy of Cabo de Norte (page does not exist)">Captaincy of Cabo de Norte</a>. The Amapá region had the highest population of any region of Brazil in the 16th century, with an estimated population of 7 million residents.<sup id="cite_ref-p_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In the early colonial period the Amapá region was a rich source of <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">lumber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Resin" title="Resin">resins</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Annatto" title="Annatto">annatto</a>, vegetable oils, and <a href="/wiki/Salted_fish" title="Salted fish">salted fish</a>, all of which were exported to Europe. The French established <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a> plantations in this period.
</p><p>With the discovery of mineral deposits the Amapá region was invaded by the <a href="/wiki/English_colonization_of_the_Americas" class="mw-redirect" title="English colonization of the Americas">English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dutch_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Dutch colonization of the Americas">Dutch</a>, who were ultimately repelled by the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Portuguese colonization of the Americas">Portuguese</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-p_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Utrecht" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Utrecht">Treaty of Utrecht</a> in 1713 established the boundaries between the <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Brazil" title="Colonial Brazil">colony of Brazil</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>, but these were not respected by the French. A fort was constructed at <a href="/w/index.php?title=S%C3%A3o_Jos%C3%A9_de_Macap%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="São José de Macapá (page does not exist)">São José de Macapá</a>, in present-day Macapá, as a base of Portuguese power in the region.<sup id="cite_ref-p_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In the 18th century, France retook control of the area. This international dispute continued until 1900.
</p><p>With the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> and the increasing value of <a href="/wiki/Natural_rubber" title="Natural rubber">rubber</a> on the international market during the 19th century, outsider population increased in Amapá and the territorial dispute with France was brought to a head. French settlers established the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Independent_Guyana" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of Independent Guyana">State of Counani</a> (1886-1891). The gold rush however brought in Brazilian interests who gained control of the territory, which led to clashes with the French. The dispute was sent for arbitration and on December 1, 1900, the <a href="/wiki/Arbitration_Commission_of_Geneva" class="mw-redirect" title="Arbitration Commission of Geneva">Arbitration Commission of Geneva</a> ceded possession of the territory to Brazil. It was incorporated into the state of <a href="/wiki/Par%C3%A1" title="Pará">Pará</a>, with the joint name of Araguari (named for the <a href="/wiki/Araguari_River_(Amap%C3%A1)" title="Araguari River (Amapá)">river of the same name</a>). It became the federal <a href="/wiki/Territory_(country_subdivision)" class="mw-redirect" title="Territory (country subdivision)">territory</a> of Amapá in 1943.
</p><p>The discovery of rich <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> deposits in <a href="/wiki/Serra_do_Navio" title="Serra do Navio">Serra do Navio</a> in 1945 revolutionized the local economy. Manganese remains the largest source of revenue in the state.<sup id="cite_ref-p_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Amapá did not achieve statehood until October 5, 1988, at the time of the promulgation of the new <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Brazil" title="Constitution of Brazil">Brazilian Constitution</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amap%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The State of Amapá possesses the lowest rate of loss of its original vegetation for any Brazilian state, estimated at only 2%. Most of the Amapá territory is covered with <a href="/wiki/Rainforest" title="Rainforest">rainforest</a>, while the remaining areas are covered with <a href="/wiki/Savannah" class="mw-redirect" title="Savannah">savannah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plain" title="Plain">plains</a>. On the Amapá coast, almost intact beaches mix with swamps, creating the largest representation of this biome in Brazil. This mixture of salt and fresh water is perfect for the food chain reproduction for several animal species. Pollution, however, is now a chronic problem in the state of Amapá. Mercury, which is used in the extraction of gold, is widely found in water sources and sewage systems in the population centers of Amapá.<sup id="cite_ref-p_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amap%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Amapá has an <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_climate" class="mw-redirect" title="Equatorial climate">equatorial climate</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_climate" title="Tropical climate">tropical climate</a> with no <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry season</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a>, it has a Tropical Monsoon climate, <i>Am</i>.  <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest" title="Tropical rainforest">Tropical rainforest</a> is the natural vegetation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amap%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Institute_of_Geography_and_Statistics" title="Brazilian Institute of Geography and Statistics">IBGE</a> of 2010, there were 668,689 people residing in the state, with a population density of 4.3/km². The population of Amapá is highly urbanized, with 93.7% of the population residing primarily in Macapá and <a href="/wiki/Santana,_Amap%C3%A1" title="Santana, Amapá">Santana</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-a_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-a-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Population_growth" title="Population growth">Population growth</a>: 5.7% (1991–2000); <a href="/wiki/House" title="House">Houses</a>: 144,000 (2006).
</p><p>The last PNAD (National Research for Sample of Domiciles) census revealed the following numbers: 429,000 <a href="/wiki/Pardo_Brazilians" title="Pardo Brazilians">Pardo</a> (<a href="/wiki/Multiracial#Brazil" title="Multiracial">Multiracial</a>) people (69.4%), 148,000 <a href="/wiki/White_Brazilian" class="mw-redirect" title="White Brazilian">White</a> people (24.0%), 40,000 <a href="/wiki/Afro-Brazilian" class="mw-redirect" title="Afro-Brazilian">Black</a> people (6.5%), 6,000 <a href="/wiki/Asian_Brazilian" class="mw-redirect" title="Asian Brazilian">Asian</a> or <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Brazil" title="Indigenous peoples in Brazil">Amerindian</a> people (1.0%).<sup id="cite_ref-s_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-s-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The majority of the residents of Amapá live in poverty. According to the <a href="/wiki/Funda%C3%A7%C3%A3o_Getulio_Vargas" class="mw-redirect" title="Fundação Getulio Vargas">Fundação Getulio Vargas</a> (FGV) 36.56% of the population live on only R$79 per month, which the foundation considers below a decent standard of living.<sup id="cite_ref-p_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amap%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Service_sector" class="mw-redirect" title="Service sector">service sector</a> is the largest component of <a href="/wiki/GDP" class="mw-redirect" title="GDP">GDP</a> at 87.6%, followed by the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_sector" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial sector">industrial sector</a> at 7.8%. <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">Agriculture</a> represents 4.6% of <a href="/wiki/GDP" class="mw-redirect" title="GDP">GDP</a> (2004). Amapá exports: <a href="/wiki/Wood" title="Wood">wood</a> 75.5%, <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">ores</a> 18.7%, <a href="/wiki/Heart_of_palm" title="Heart of palm">heart of palm</a> 5.5% (2002). Share of the Brazilian economy: 0.2% (2005).<sup id="cite_ref-p_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-p-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amap%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> is the official national language, and thus the primary language taught in schools. <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> is the second language taught in every school due to the proximity of <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>. A creole language called <a href="/wiki/Lanc-Patu%C3%A1_creole" class="mw-redirect" title="Lanc-Patuá creole">Lanc-Patuá</a> is spoken among some of the people.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Airport">International Airport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amap%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: International Airport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The state of Amapá will soon have a new airport. <a href="/wiki/Infraero" title="Infraero">Infraero</a> will start work<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (June 2018)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> on the new <a href="/wiki/Macap%C3%A1_International_Airport" title="Macapá International Airport">Macapá International Airport</a>, which will contain 17,000 square metres of space to serve 700,000 passengers a year. The current airport, with an area of 2,900 square metres, operates annually with 324,000 <a href="/wiki/Passenger" title="Passenger">passengers</a>. The work, scheduled for conclusion in 30 months, will enlarge the parking lot to 258 spots, compared to the current 80, and expand the total area from 2,400 to 11,000 square metres. The apron area will be increased from its current area of 21,000 square metres to 30,000 square metres, with capacity for four large <a href="/wiki/Airplane" title="Airplane">airplanes</a> and 30 small aircraft.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flag">Flag</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Amap%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Flag">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Amap%C3%A1" title="Flag of Amapá">Flag of Amapá</a></div>
<p>The flag was adopted by Decree No. 8 of 23 April 1984. The blue represents the sky over Amapá and justice, the green represents the native rainforest, and the yellow represents the Union and its natural resources. The black stands for the deceased who worked for the state, and the white for the will of the state to live in peace and stability. The symbol on the <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_vexillology" title="Glossary of vexillology">hoist-side</a> represents the Fortress of São José, out of which the state capital grew.<sup id="cite_ref-f_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-f-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Before 1984, the state had a red-white-red flag, based on the flag of <a href="/wiki/Par%C3%A1" title="Pará">Pará</a>, and similar to the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Peru" title="Flag of Peru">flag of Peru</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
