<p>The <b>A14</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Trunk_road" title="Trunk road">trunk road</a> in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, running 127 miles (204&#160;km) from the <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Felixstowe" title="Port of Felixstowe">Port of Felixstowe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a> to its western end at the <a href="/wiki/Catthorpe_Interchange" title="Catthorpe Interchange">Catthorpe Interchange</a>; a major intersection at the southern end of the <a href="/wiki/M6_motorway" title="M6 motorway">M6</a> and junction 19 of the <a href="/wiki/M1_motorway" title="M1 motorway">M1</a> in <a href="/wiki/Leicestershire" title="Leicestershire">Leicestershire</a>. The road forms part of the unsigned <a href="/wiki/International_E-road_network" title="International E-road network">Euroroutes</a> <a href="/wiki/European_route_E24" title="European route E24">E24</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_route_E30" title="European route E30">E30</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Route">Route</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A14_road_(England)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Route">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From the <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Felixstowe" title="Port of Felixstowe">Port of Felixstowe</a> the road heads west, bypassing <a href="/wiki/Ipswich" title="Ipswich">Ipswich</a> to the south via the <a href="/wiki/Orwell_Bridge" title="Orwell Bridge">Orwell Bridge</a> and to  <a href="/wiki/Stowmarket" title="Stowmarket">Stowmarket</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bury_St_Edmunds" title="Bury St Edmunds">Bury St Edmunds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newmarket,_Suffolk" title="Newmarket, Suffolk">Newmarket</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a> where it meets the <a href="/wiki/M11_motorway" title="M11 motorway">M11</a>, then past <a href="/wiki/St_Ives,_Cambridgeshire" title="St Ives, Cambridgeshire">St Ives</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huntingdon" title="Huntingdon">Huntingdon</a> and the junction with the <a href="/wiki/A1_road_(Great_Britain)" title="A1 road (Great Britain)">A1</a>, from there through <a href="/wiki/Kettering" title="Kettering">Kettering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northamptonshire" title="Northamptonshire">Northamptonshire</a>, ending at junction 19 of the M1 and the start of the M6.
</p><p>The road is a <a href="/wiki/Dual_carriageway" title="Dual carriageway">dual carriageway</a>, most with two lanes each way, but there are two dual three-lane sections: on the Newmarket bypass (between Junctions 36 and 38) where it <a href="/wiki/Concurrency_(road)" title="Concurrency (road)">runs concurrent</a> with the <a href="/wiki/A11_road_(Great_Britain)" class="mw-redirect" title="A11 road (Great Britain)">A11</a>, and a short stretch between the Girton Interchange and <a href="/wiki/Bar_Hill,_Cambridgeshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Bar Hill, Cambridgeshire">Bar Hill</a>. There are three <a href="/wiki/At-grade_intersection" class="mw-redirect" title="At-grade intersection">at-grade junctions</a>: with the B663 at <a href="/wiki/Bythorn" title="Bythorn">Bythorn</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a> (junction 15); at the Leighton Bromswold turn a few miles to the east (junction 17); and at the Dockspur Roundabout at the edge of <a href="/wiki/Felixstowe" title="Felixstowe">Felixstowe</a> (junction 60), although at the A14/A1 junction (junction 21) and at the A14/A141 junction (junction 23), the road's main route does have to negotiate a roundabout (in the case of the latter, the A14 southbound and a spur goes underneath the roundabout, as the A1 does at the former).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A14_road_(England)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The current A14 includes parts of the former A45 between Felixstowe and Cambridge, the A604 between Cambridge and Kettering, a short stretch of the former A6 west of Kettering, plus a new link road which was constructed in the early 1990s between there and the M1/M6 interchange at Catthorpe, Leicestershire. Prior to the current A14, the main route from <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Haven_ports" title="Haven ports">Haven ports</a> followed the M6, M1, the <a href="/wiki/A428_road" title="A428 road">A428</a> and <a href="/wiki/A45_road" title="A45 road">A45 road</a> via Coventry, Rugby, Northampton, Bedford, St Neots, Cambridge and then through all the towns on the A14 to Felixstowe. Prior to its use for the current route the A14 designation had been used for a section of road between the <a href="/wiki/A10_road_(England)" title="A10 road (England)">A10</a> at <a href="/wiki/Royston,_Hertfordshire" title="Royston, Hertfordshire">Royston</a> and the <a href="/wiki/A1_road_(Great_Britain)" title="A1 road (Great Britain)">A1</a> at <a href="/wiki/Alconbury" title="Alconbury">Alconbury</a> following part of the route of <a href="/wiki/Ermine_Street" title="Ermine Street">Ermine Street</a> which now, in most parts, is designated the <a href="/wiki/A1198_road" title="A1198 road">A1198</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/M45_motorway" title="M45 motorway">M45 motorway</a> was constructed in 1959 parallel to part of the old A45 in the Midlands. It opened on the same day as the <a href="/wiki/M1_motorway" title="M1 motorway">M1 motorway</a> and was soon one of the busiest sections of motorway. The M6 opened in the late 1960s and early 1970s, after which more traffic to the ports used the route from junction 1 of the M6 via the <a href="/wiki/A427_road" title="A427 road">A427</a> to Market Harborough followed by a short section of the <a href="/wiki/A6_road_(England)" title="A6 road (England)">A6</a> to Kettering and then the <a href="/wiki/A604_road" class="mw-redirect" title="A604 road">A604</a> to Cambridge before joining the old A45 to the ports as above.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The M45 now carries little traffic.
</p><p>The sections from Huntingdon east to the ports were upgraded first, starting with the Huntingdon bypass in 1973, followed by the Girton to Bar Hill section in 1975/76 and the Cambridge northern bypass and Cambridge/Newmarket section in 1976/77.<sup id="cite_ref-dates_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dates-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The Bar Hill to Huntington section opened in 1979 prior to the M11 which was fully opened in 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Ipswich southern bypass including the <a href="/wiki/Orwell_Bridge" title="Orwell Bridge">Orwell Bridge</a> opened in 1982.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The M1-A1 link road was constructed between 1989 and 1991 following a lengthy period of consultation. The first inquiry was in 1974 and then a series of inquiries for sections of the preferred route from September 1984 until June 1985, during which objections came from some 1,130 sources. Subsequent public inquiries were held regarding Supplementary Orders. The route close to the site of the Battle of Naseby was particularly difficult and was taken to the High Court.<sup id="cite_ref-hist_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hist-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The link was opened by <a href="/wiki/John_MacGregor,_Baron_MacGregor_of_Pulham_Market" title="John MacGregor, Baron MacGregor of Pulham Market">John MacGregor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transport_Secretary" class="mw-redirect" title="Transport Secretary">Transport Secretary</a> on 15 July 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Work to create a <a href="/wiki/Grade_separation" title="Grade separation">compact grade-separated</a> junction (Junction 45/Rougham) and to re-align a 2-mile (3.2&#160;km) stretch of carriageway was completed in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Vehicles over 7.5 tonnes traveling east were banned from using the outside lane on a dual 2-lane section on a 2-mile (3.2&#160;km) steep climb to Welford summit close to Junction 1 (A5199) from spring 2007; a similar scheme covered 2 miles (3.2&#160;km) of the westbound carriageway from Junction 2, including a particularly steep climb to Naseby summit. The bans are active between 6am and 8pm and are intended to reduce delays to other traffic from lorries attempting to pass on these climbs.<sup id="cite_ref-HA14233_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HA14233-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Between 2007 and 2008 a new section of two-lane dual carriageway was constructed at the Haughley Bends, one of Suffolk's most notorious accident blackspots,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> to rationalise access using a new <a href="/wiki/Grade_separation" title="Grade separation">grade-separated junction</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HA13030_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HA13030-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The road opened in the summer of 2008<sup id="cite_ref-HA13030_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HA13030-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> with some associated local works being completed early in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-HaughleyUpdate_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HaughleyUpdate-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Variable_Message_Sign" class="mw-redirect" title="Variable Message Sign">Variable Message Signs</a> (VMS), traffic queue detection loops and <a href="/wiki/Closed-circuit_television" title="Closed-circuit television">closed circuit TV</a> (CCTV) were installed at a cost of 58m euros during 2009 to 2010<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Both carriageways between Junction 52 (Claydon) and Junction 55 (Copdock) were refurbished during 2010 at a cost of £9 million. Work was being carried out a year earlier than scheduled as part of a <a href="/wiki/National_fiscal_policy_response_to_the_late_2000s_recession" class="mw-redirect" title="National fiscal policy response to the late 2000s recession">UK government’s fiscal stimulus package</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire_Guided_Busway" title="Cambridgeshire Guided Busway">Cambridgeshire Guided Busway</a> connecting <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huntingdon" title="Huntingdon">Huntingdon</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_Ives,_Cambridgeshire" title="St Ives, Cambridgeshire">St Ives</a>, which opened in 2011 was intended to remove 5.6% of traffic using that section of the A14 (rising to 11.1% with the new Park &amp; Ride sites), although as other traffic re-routes to the freed-up road space from other parts of the local road network, the net reduction is predicted to be 2.3%.<sup id="cite_ref-DFTrep7_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DFTrep7-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Felixstowe" title="Port of Felixstowe">Felixstowe and Nuneaton freight capacity scheme</a>, designed to take more lorry traffic off the A14 between the Port and the Midlands by increasing rail capacity and allowing the carriage of larger 'Hi-cube' <a href="/wiki/Intermodal_container" title="Intermodal container">shipping containers</a> by widening to the W10 <a href="/wiki/Loading_gauge" title="Loading gauge">loading gauge</a>, opened in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Junction 55 (<a href="/wiki/Copdock" title="Copdock">Copdock</a> interchange) to the south of Ipswich was signalisation in 2011, along with lengthening the off-slip from the <a href="/wiki/A1214" class="mw-redirect" title="A1214">A1214</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-libdem_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-libdem-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The section around Kettering between Junctions 7 and 9 was widened to three lanes between November 2013 and April 2015 at a cost of £42m.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After initially being shelved in 2010,<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_20100618_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_20100618-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Catthorpe_Interchange" title="Catthorpe Interchange">Catthorpe Interchange</a> at the road's Western terminus underwent a massive restructuring in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The redesigned junction was intended to allow free-flowing traffic movement between the A14, the southern terminus of the <a href="/wiki/M6_motorway" title="M6 motorway">M6 motorway</a> and Junction 19 of the <a href="/wiki/M1_motorway" title="M1 motorway">M1 motorway</a>. The first part opened in September 2016,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> and the upgrade was completed three months later.
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:A14traffic.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/A14traffic.jpg/220px-A14traffic.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/A14traffic.jpg/330px-A14traffic.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/17/A14traffic.jpg 2x" data-file-width="427" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:A14traffic.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A14 Traffic congestion near <a href="/wiki/Needham_Market" title="Needham Market">Needham Market</a></div></div></div><p>From the <a href="/wiki/A12_road_(Great_Britain)" class="mw-redirect" title="A12 road (Great Britain)">A12</a> west of <a href="/wiki/Ipswich" title="Ipswich">Ipswich</a> to the <a href="/wiki/M1_motorway" title="M1 motorway">M1</a>/<a href="/wiki/M6_motorway" title="M6 motorway">M6</a> junction, the A14 is part of (but not signed as) the <a href="/wiki/International_E-road_network" title="International E-road network">E-road</a> <a href="/wiki/European_route_E24" title="European route E24">E&#160;24</a>. From Ipswich to <a href="/wiki/Felixstowe" title="Felixstowe">Felixstowe</a> is part of <a href="/wiki/European_route_E30" title="European route E30">E&#160;30</a>.
</p><p>The numbering of the A14 is <a href="/wiki/Incorrectly_numbered_roads_in_Great_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Incorrectly numbered roads in Great Britain">inconsistent</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain_road_numbering_scheme" title="Great Britain road numbering scheme">national road numbering scheme</a>, as it begins in zone 5 and crosses through zone 6 on the way to zone 1 east of Huntingdon to Felixstowe. The road is concurrent with the <a href="/wiki/A12_road_(Great_Britain)" class="mw-redirect" title="A12 road (Great Britain)">A12 road</a> from the Seven Hills Interchange to the Copdock Interchange which forms the Ipswich Southern bypass and with the A11 road between junctions 36 and 38.
</p><p>The final 1.2 mile section of the A14 'spur' from the junction with the B1043 near Huntingdon to the A1(M) at Alconbury has many inconsistent designations. It is the only section of the original A14 (<a href="/wiki/A1198_road" title="A1198 road">A1198 road</a>) that still bears that A14 designation; it was renumbered A604 for a period prior to the construction of the current A14. The scheme page on the Highways Agency website for the 'A1(M) Alconbury to Peterborough' scheme carried out in 1996–1998 refers to it as the A14(M),<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> but the map page linked from that page marks it as the A604(M).<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The statutory instrument for the construction of the road in 1993 also refers to it as the A604(M).<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Neither the A14(M) nor the A604(M) designation is used on the ground; heading onto the A1(M) heading north there a bare 'motorway' sign just past the B1043 exit without any number (see photo in the top right of this page).<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> However, when heading south along the A1 it is signed as 'A14'.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Online mapping is also inconsistent – the Highway Agency mapping (which uses <a href="/wiki/Navteq" title="Navteq">Navteq</a> data) refers to it as the 'A14(M)',<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Bing_(search_engine)" title="Bing (search engine)">Bing</a> maps (which also uses <a href="/wiki/Navteq" title="Navteq">Navteq</a> mapping) shows it as motorway without any designation<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Yahoo_maps" class="mw-redirect" title="Yahoo maps">Yahoo maps</a> (which uses Navteq data) shows it as motorway and as the A1(M). <a href="/wiki/Google_maps" class="mw-redirect" title="Google maps">Google maps</a> (which uses <a href="/wiki/TeleAtlas" class="mw-redirect" title="TeleAtlas">TeleAtlas</a> data) shows it as a trunk road called A14.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>East of the Girton Interchange with the <a href="/wiki/M11_motorway" title="M11 motorway">M11</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, the A14 used to be the <a href="/wiki/A45_road_(Great_Britain)" class="mw-redirect" title="A45 road (Great Britain)">A45</a>, and much of the long-distance traffic further west had previously used the A45 route. The section between <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kettering" title="Kettering">Kettering</a> used to be the <a href="/wiki/A604_road" class="mw-redirect" title="A604 road">A604</a>, apart from a short section near Kettering that used to be part of the <a href="/wiki/A6_road_(Great_Britain)" class="mw-redirect" title="A6 road (Great Britain)">A6</a>. The road which was the A14 until the late 1980s is now the <a href="/wiki/A1198_road" title="A1198 road">A1198</a> between <a href="/wiki/Royston,_Hertfordshire" title="Royston, Hertfordshire">Royston, Hertfordshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Godmanchester" title="Godmanchester">Godmanchester</a>. North of Godmanchester the route of the original A14 became part of the A604, itself now part of the new A14 until it meets the <a href="/wiki/A1_road_(Great_Britain)" title="A1 road (Great Britain)">A1 road</a> near <a href="/wiki/Alconbury" title="Alconbury">Alconbury</a>, thus forming a 'spur' off the A14.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="A14_Cambridge_to_Huntingdon_Improvement_Scheme">A14 Cambridge to Huntingdon Improvement Scheme</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A14_road_(England)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: A14 Cambridge to Huntingdon Improvement Scheme">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="infobox vcard" style="width:22em"><caption>A14 Cambridge to Huntingdon Improvement Scheme</caption><tbody><tr><td colspan="2" style="text-align:center"><a href="/wiki/File:Proposed_A14_Ellington_to_Fen_Ditton_development.png" class="image"><img alt="Proposed A14 Ellington to Fen Ditton development.png" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Proposed_A14_Ellington_to_Fen_Ditton_development.png/200px-Proposed_A14_Ellington_to_Fen_Ditton_development.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="155" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Proposed_A14_Ellington_to_Fen_Ditton_development.png/300px-Proposed_A14_Ellington_to_Fen_Ditton_development.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Proposed_A14_Ellington_to_Fen_Ditton_development.png/400px-Proposed_A14_Ellington_to_Fen_Ditton_development.png 2x" data-file-width="538" data-file-height="416" /></a><div>A14 Ellington to Fen Ditton</div></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Location</th><td><a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Proposer</th><td><a href="/wiki/Highways_England" title="Highways England">Highways England</a></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Cost estimate</th><td>£1.2-1.8b</td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Start date</th><td>2017</td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Completion date</th><td>2020</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The plan, originally called the 'A14 Ellington to Fen Ditton' was to include widening from <a href="/wiki/Fen_Ditton" title="Fen Ditton">Fen Ditton</a> to <a href="/wiki/Fen_Drayton" title="Fen Drayton">Fen Drayton</a>,  broadly on the existing alignment and then on a new route from Fen Drayton to the south of the current road to the Brampton Interchange before tracking the A1 north to <a href="/wiki/Ellington,_Cambridgeshire" title="Ellington, Cambridgeshire">Ellington</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HA4215_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HA4215-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Highways Agency unveiled its plans in March 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Details of the preferred route for the Fen Drayton to Fen Ditton section were published in March 2007. The contract was awarded to Costain Skanska Joint Venture on 28 January 2008,<sup id="cite_ref-HA4215_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HA4215-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> which worked on detailed plans and the Highways Agency before publishing a draft order. The scheme was expected to open in stages between 2015 and 2016. The Highways Agency estimated that the Ellington-Fen Ditton widening would cost between £690 million and £1.2 billion, making it the most expensive scheme in their roads programme.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2009 the cost estimate had risen to £1.3b with work due to start in 2012 and being completed in winter 2015/2016.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Campaign_for_Better_Transport_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Campaign for Better Transport (UK)">Campaign for Better Transport</a> was opposed to the plans, listing their reasons for objection as the carbon emissions the road would induce, the cost, and its negative impact on non-car travel.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The coalition government suspended the scheme when it came into power, with <a href="/wiki/Philip_Hammond" title="Philip Hammond">Philip Hammond</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Transport" title="Secretary of State for Transport">Secretary of State for Transport</a>, suggesting that the scheme would be 'axed', and that the only way it would get built was as a <a href="/wiki/Toll_road" title="Toll road">toll road</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> That government money would not pay for the scheme was confirmed  at the end of October, when Roads Minister <a href="/wiki/Mike_Penning" title="Mike Penning">Mike Penning</a> said that the scheme was unaffordable and no longer offered acceptable value for money.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The scheme was officially cancelled in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-hechis_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hechis-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2011 the government announced an 'A14 Challenge' inviting people to present proposals for the route.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The revised scheme, now known as the 'A14 Cambridge to Huntingdon Improvement Scheme' involves widening of the A1 between Brampton and Alconbury, a new Huntingdon Southern Bypass, widening of the existing A14 from Swavesey to Milton, and a new local access road between Swavesey and Girton. The A14 viaduct in Huntingdon town centre will become redundant and will be demolished.  In November 2012 it was reported that the scheme might be back on a fast track to implementation<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> and it was mentioned in the June 2013 spending review.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The project was approved by the Secretary of State for Transport in May 2016. Work is due to be complete by March 2021 at a cost of £1.2-1.8b.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 2018, Highways England said it was to ask the Planning Inspectorate to consider giving the upgraded section of A14 motorway status, to be known as the A14(M).<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>   The Secretary of State for Transport was scheduled to make the final decision on whether the planned A14 upgrade will be given motorway status by the end of 2019.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Longer_term_plans">Longer term plans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A14_road_(England)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Longer term plans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Highways Agency has plans to increase capacity from Junctions 3 to 10 near Kettering 'in the longer term'<sup id="cite_ref-HA22936_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HA22936-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> and also to widen the road throughout <a href="/wiki/Northamptonshire" title="Northamptonshire">Northamptonshire</a> to "help cut the number of accidents and cope with the likely growth in traffic".<sup id="cite_ref-bbc4037663_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc4037663-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coach_services">Coach services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A14_road_(England)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Coach services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The A14 is no longer used by a coach service, <a href="/wiki/National_Express_Coaches" title="National Express Coaches">National Express</a> route 350 (Clacton on sea to Liverpool) between Copdock (J53) and Huntingdon (J26); 
National Express 305 (Liverpool to Southend-on-sea) and route 314 (Southport to Cambridge) follow the old A45 between Cambridge and Birmingham.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_incidents">Notable incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=A14_road_(England)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Notable incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>17 November 1998. A lorry collided with the petrol station between <a href="/wiki/Bar_Hill" title="Bar Hill">Bar Hill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lolworth" title="Lolworth">Lolworth</a>. The incident happened shortly after 11am and one person was killed and many others injured. The road was closed and there were huge tailbacks.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>26 July 2006. The A14 was closed for 24 hours near Newmarket when a van carrying acetylene gas canisters caught fire and the rescue services were advised by <a href="/wiki/British_Oxygen" class="mw-redirect" title="British Oxygen">British Oxygen</a> that they could remain unstable and would need 24 hours to cool. <a href="/wiki/Bomb_disposal" title="Bomb disposal">Bomb disposal</a> officers were called in and the <a href="/wiki/Red_Cross" class="mw-redirect" title="Red Cross">Red Cross</a> set up a centre in Newmarket for those who were stranded.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc5216482_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc5216482-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
