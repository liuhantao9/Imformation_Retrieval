<p><b>Roderick Charles Smallwood</b> (born 17 February 1950) is an English <a href="/wiki/Music_manager" class="mw-redirect" title="Music manager">music manager</a>, best known as the co-manager of the British <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">heavy metal</a> band <a href="/wiki/Iron_Maiden" title="Iron Maiden">Iron Maiden</a>. With his business partner, <a href="/wiki/Andy_Taylor_(music_entrepreneur)" title="Andy Taylor (music entrepreneur)">Andy Taylor</a>, whom he met while studying at <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College, Cambridge</a>, he founded the <a href="/wiki/Sanctuary_Records_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Sanctuary Records Group">Sanctuary Records Group</a> in 1979, which became the largest <a href="/wiki/Independent_record_label" title="Independent record label">independent record label</a> in the UK and the largest independent music management company in the world until its closure in 2007. Prior to managing Iron Maiden, Smallwood managed <a href="/wiki/Steve_Harley_%26_Cockney_Rebel" title="Steve Harley &amp; Cockney Rebel">Steve Harley &amp; Cockney Rebel</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rod_Smallwood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Smallwood was born and raised in Huddersfield, West Yorkshire, where he grew up listening to <a href="/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stones" title="The Rolling Stones">The Rolling Stones</a> on the radio and playing <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_73_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_73-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It was only when he began attending university that his musical interests expanded and he began listening to <a href="/wiki/Deep_Purple" title="Deep Purple">Deep Purple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pink_Floyd" title="Pink Floyd">Pink Floyd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grateful_Dead" title="Grateful Dead">Grateful Dead</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Doors" title="The Doors">The Doors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_73_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_73-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the autumn of 1968, Smallwood began studying architecture at <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College, Cambridge</a>, where he soon became involved in organising social events, such as the annual <a href="/wiki/First_and_Third_Trinity_Boat_Club_May_Ball" title="First and Third Trinity Boat Club May Ball">Trinity May Ball</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_73_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_73-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> for which he booked the "various acts".<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_74_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_74-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It was while undertaking these duties that he formed a friendship with fellow undergraduate and future business partner <a href="/wiki/Andy_Taylor_(music_entrepreneur)" title="Andy Taylor (music entrepreneur)">Andy Taylor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_74_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_74-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> From then on, Smallwood and Taylor worked on end-of-term events together and booked various musicians, including <a href="/wiki/Graham_Bond" title="Graham Bond">Graham Bond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Farlowe" title="Chris Farlowe">Chris Farlowe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bridget_St_John" title="Bridget St John">Bridget St John</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Martyn" title="John Martyn">John Martyn</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_76_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_76-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and, most notably, the <a href="/wiki/MC5" title="MC5">MC5</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_78_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_78-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Smallwood acquired most of these artists from a local booking agency, Horus Arts, whose boss, Barry Hawkins, gave Smallwood advice which led to his career as a booking agent.<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_77_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_77-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Shortly before his final exams in 1971, Smallwood dropped out of university and moved to Paris with his girlfriend,<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_80_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_80-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> stating that "it just seemed like the cool thing to do".<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_80_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_80-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> After three months in Paris, Smallwood undertook a job at a London booking agency, Gemini, in order to finance a trip to Morocco.<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_80_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_80-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Through his work with Gemini, Smallwood was offered a "£35 a week" job with rival agency <a href="/wiki/MAM_Records" title="MAM Records">MAM</a>, and therefore abandoned his trip to Morocco.<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_80_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_80-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> After 18 months with MAM, Smallwood began managing English rock act <a href="/wiki/Steve_Harley_%26_Cockney_Rebel" title="Steve Harley &amp; Cockney Rebel">Steve Harley &amp; Cockney Rebel</a>. Smallwood disliked working with Harley, whom he would later describe as "a pain - selfish, egotistical, obsessed" and thus "completely put me off management."<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_81_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_81-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Convinced by <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a>, Harley fired Trigram, his management agency, and Smallwood undertook the management of punk rock act <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Mundi" title="Gloria Mundi">Gloria Mundi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_82_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_82-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1979, Smallwood decided to return to university to obtain a law degree, where he was given a copy of <a href="/wiki/Iron_Maiden" title="Iron Maiden">Iron Maiden</a>'s <a href="/wiki/The_Soundhouse_Tapes" title="The Soundhouse Tapes">demo tape</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_83_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_83-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> After much deliberation, Smallwood eventually contacted <a href="/wiki/Steve_Harris_(musician)" title="Steve Harris (musician)">Steve Harris</a>, the band's bassist and founder member, and arranged two pub gigs for the group in west London, one at the <a href="/wiki/Windsor_Castle,_Maida_Vale" title="Windsor Castle, Maida Vale">Windsor Castle</a> in <a href="/wiki/Maida_Vale" title="Maida Vale">Maida Vale</a> and the other at <a href="/wiki/The_Swan,_Hammersmith" title="The Swan, Hammersmith">The Swan</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hammersmith" title="Hammersmith">Hammersmith</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_84_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_84-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Neither show went to plan- the first was cancelled after the band refused to play early,<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_84_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_84-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and the band had to perform the second without lead vocalist, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Di%27Anno" title="Paul Di&#39;Anno">Paul Di'Anno</a>, who was arrested for carrying a knife 30 minutes before the gig.<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_85_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_85-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In spite of this, Smallwood agreed to help the group, although, following his experiences with Steve Harley, he did not initially commit to becoming their full-time manager until he arranged the band's signing to EMI and publishers Zomba at the end of the year.<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_86_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_86-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Eventually joined by Andy Taylor in 1982,<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_230_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_230-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> the pair have managed the group ever since.
</p><p>After committing himself to managing Iron Maiden in October 1979, Smallwood decided to form his own management company, which he would name after Iron Maiden's second single, "<a href="/wiki/Sanctuary_(Iron_Maiden_song)" title="Sanctuary (Iron Maiden song)">Sanctuary</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Wall_115_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_115-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sanctuary_Records_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Sanctuary Records Group">Sanctuary Records Group</a> eventually expanded into the largest <a href="/wiki/Independent_record_label" title="Independent record label">independent record label</a> in the United Kingdom<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> until its closure in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Prior to this, in November 2006, Smallwood left Sanctuary and formed Phantom Music Management with Andy Taylor, which focuses solely on Iron Maiden.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The Iron Maiden <a href="/wiki/A-side_and_B-side" title="A-side and B-side">b-side</a> "Sheriff of <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>" was written by Iron Maiden about Rod Smallwood, and was released on the 1986 single "<a href="/wiki/Wasted_Years" title="Wasted Years">Wasted Years</a>". It likens Smallwood to the <a href="/wiki/Sheriff_of_Nottingham" title="Sheriff of Nottingham">Sheriff of Nottingham</a>, due to his notorious stinginess with money, and refers to his frequent complaining about a (then recent) move to <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>. Smallwood did not know about the song until the single was released.
</p>
