	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Equator</h1>

<p>The <b>equator</b> of a rotating <a href="/wiki/Spheroid" title="Spheroid">spheroid</a> (such as a <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">planet</a>) is the parallel (<a href="/wiki/Circle_of_latitude" title="Circle of latitude">circle of latitude</a>) at which latitude is defined to be 0°. It is the <a href="/wiki/Imaginary_line" title="Imaginary line">imaginary line</a> on the spheroid, equidistant from its <a href="/wiki/Geographical_pole" title="Geographical pole">poles</a>, dividing it into <a href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern hemisphere">northern</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_hemisphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Southern hemisphere">southern hemispheres</a>. In other words, it is the intersection of the spheroid with the <a href="/wiki/Plane_(mathematics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Plane (mathematics)">plane</a> <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">perpendicular</a> to its axis of <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">rotation</a> and midway between its <a href="/wiki/Geographical_pole" title="Geographical pole">geographical poles</a>.
</p><p>On <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>, <i>the equator</i> is about 40,075&#160;km (24,901&#160;mi) long, of which 78.8% lies across water and 21.3% over land. <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> is the country straddling the greatest length of the equatorial line across both land and sea.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name is derived from medieval Latin word <i>aequator</i>, in the phrase <i>circulus aequator diei et noctis,</i> meaning ‘circle equalizing day and night’, from the Latin word <i>aequare</i> meaning ‘make equal’.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> of the Earth's equator is, by definition, 0° (zero <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">degrees</a>) of arc. The equator is one of the five notable <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_latitude" title="Circle of latitude">circles of latitude</a> on Earth; the other four are both <a href="/wiki/Polar_circle" title="Polar circle">polar circles</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">Arctic Circle</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">Antarctic Circle</a>) and both <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical circles</a> (the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">Tropic of Cancer</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Tropic of Capricorn</a>). The equator is the only line of latitude which is also a <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">great circle</a>—that is, one whose <a href="/wiki/Plane_(mathematics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Plane (mathematics)">plane</a> passes through the center of the globe. The plane of Earth's equator, when projected outwards to the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">celestial sphere</a>, defines the <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">celestial equator</a>.
</p><p>In the cycle of Earth's <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">seasons</a>, the equatorial plane runs through the Sun twice per <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">year</a>: on the <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">equinoxes</a> in <a href="/wiki/March_equinox" title="March equinox">March</a> and <a href="/wiki/September_equinox" title="September equinox">September</a>. To a person on Earth, the Sun <a href="/wiki/Subsolar_point" title="Subsolar point">appears to travel above</a> the equator (or along the celestial equator) at these times. Light rays from the Sun's center are <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">perpendicular</a> to Earth's surface at the point of <a href="/wiki/Noon#Solar_noon" title="Noon">solar noon</a> on the equator.
</p>

<p>Locations on the equator experience the shortest <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">sunrises</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">sunsets</a> because the Sun's <a href="/wiki/Sun_path" title="Sun path">daily path</a> is nearly perpendicular to the <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">horizon</a> for most of the year. The length of <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">daylight</a> (sunrise to sunset) is almost constant throughout the year; it is about 14 minutes longer than nighttime due to <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">atmospheric refraction</a> and the fact that sunrise begins (or sunset ends) as the upper limb, not the center, of the Sun's disk contacts the horizon.
</p><p>Earth <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_bulge" title="Equatorial bulge">bulges slightly</a> at the equator; the "average" diameter of Earth is 12,750&#160;km (7,920&#160;mi), but the diameter at the equator is about 43&#160;km (27&#160;mi) greater than at the poles.<sup id="cite_ref-National_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-National-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sites near the equator, such as the <a href="/wiki/Guiana_Space_Centre" title="Guiana Space Centre">Guiana Space Centre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kourou" title="Kourou">Kourou</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>, are good locations for <a href="/wiki/Spaceport" title="Spaceport">spaceports</a> as they have a fastest <a href="/wiki/Rotational_speed" title="Rotational speed">rotational speed</a> of any latitude, 460 m/s. The added <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">velocity</a> reduces the fuel needed to launch spacecraft eastward (in the direction of Earth's rotation) to orbit, while simultaneously avoiding costly maneuvers to flatten <a href="/wiki/Inclination" class="mw-redirect" title="Inclination">inclination</a> during missions such as the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Program" class="mw-redirect" title="Apollo Program">Apollo moon landings</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Moonport_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Moonport-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geodesy">Geodesy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geodesy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Earth_ellipsoid" title="Earth ellipsoid">Earth ellipsoid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">Reference ellipsoid</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precise_location">Precise location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Precise location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The precise location of the equator is not truly fixed; the true equatorial plane is perpendicular to the <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">Earth's spin axis</a>, which <a href="/wiki/Polar_motion" title="Polar motion">drifts</a> about 9 metres (30&#160;ft) during a year. This effect must be accounted for in detailed <a href="/wiki/Geophysical" class="mw-redirect" title="Geophysical">geophysical</a> measurements.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exact_length">Exact length</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Exact length">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_circumference" title="Earth&#39;s circumference">Earth's circumference</a></div>
<p>The International Association of Geodesy (IAG) and the International Astronomical Union (IAU) have chosen to use an equatorial radius of 6,378.1366 kilometres (3,963.1903 mi) (codified as the IAU 2009 value).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This equatorial radius is also in the 2003 and 2010 IERS Conventions.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It is also the equatorial radius used for the IERS 2003 ellipsoid.  If it were really circular, the length of the equator would then be exactly 2π times the radius, namely 40,075.0142 kilometres (24,901.4594 mi). The <a href="/wiki/GRS_80" class="mw-redirect" title="GRS 80">GRS 80</a> (Geodetic Reference System 1980) as approved and adopted by the IUGG at its Canberra, Australia meeting of 1979 has an equatorial radius of 6,378.137 kilometres (3,963.191 mi). The <a href="/wiki/WGS_84" class="mw-redirect" title="WGS 84">WGS 84</a> (World Geodetic System 1984) which is a standard for use in cartography, geodesy, and satellite navigation including GPS, also has an equatorial radius of 6,378.137 kilometres (3,963.191 mi). For both GRS 80 and WGS 84, this results in a length for the equator of 40,075.0167 km (24,901.4609 mi). 
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Geographical_mile" title="Geographical mile">geographical mile</a> is defined as one <a href="/wiki/Arc-minute" class="mw-redirect" title="Arc-minute">arc-minute</a> of the equator, so it has different values depending on which radius is assumed. For example, by WSG-84, the distance is 1,855.3248 metres (6,087.024&#160;ft), while by IAU-2000, it is 1,855.3257 metres (6,087.027&#160;ft). This is a difference of less than one millimetre (0.039&#160;in) over the total distance (approximately 1.86 kilometres or 1.16 miles).
</p><p>The earth is commonly modeled as a <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">sphere</a> flattened 0.336% along its axis. This makes the equator 0.16% longer than a <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">meridian</a> (a great circle passing through the two poles). The IUGG standard meridian is, to the nearest millimetre, 40,007.862917 kilometres (24,859.733480&#160;mi), one arc-minute of which is 1,852.216 metres (6,076.82&#160;ft), explaining the <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">SI</a> standardization of the <a href="/wiki/Nautical_mile" title="Nautical mile">nautical mile</a> as 1,852 metres (6,076&#160;ft), more than 3 metres (9.8&#160;ft) less than the <a href="/wiki/Geographical_mile" title="Geographical mile">geographical mile</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea-level</a> surface of the Earth (the <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">geoid</a>) is irregular, so the actual length of the equator is not so easy to determine. <i><a href="/wiki/Aviation_week_and_space_technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Aviation week and space technology">Aviation Week and Space Technology</a></i> on 9 October 1961 reported that measurements using the <a href="/wiki/Transit_(satellite)" title="Transit (satellite)">Transit</a> IV-A satellite had shown the equatorial diameter from longitude 11° West to 169° East to be 1,000 feet (300&#160;m) greater than its diameter ninety degrees away.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<p>Despite its name, no part of <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">Equatorial Guinea</a> lies on the equator. However, its island of <a href="/wiki/Annob%C3%B3n_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Annobón Province">Annobón</a> is 155&#160;km (96&#160;mi) south of the equator, and the rest of the country lies to the north.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equatorial_seasons_and_climate">Equatorial seasons and climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Equatorial seasons and climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Seasons" class="mw-redirect" title="Seasons">Seasons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">Tropics</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Effect_of_sun_angle_on_climate" class="mw-redirect" title="Effect of sun angle on climate">Effect of sun angle on climate</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:268px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Seasons.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Seasons.svg/266px-Seasons.svg.png" decoding="async" width="266" height="170" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="266" data-file-height="170" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><br />Diagram of the seasons, depicting the situation at the December solstice. Regardless of the time of day (i.e. the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>’s rotation on its axis), the <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">North Pole</a> will be dark, and the <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">South Pole</a> will be illuminated; see also <a href="/wiki/Arctic_winter" class="mw-redirect" title="Arctic winter">arctic winter</a>. In addition to the density of <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_incidence_(optics)" title="Angle of incidence (optics)">incident</a> light, the <a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">dissipation</a> of light in the <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth&#39;s atmosphere">atmosphere</a> is greater when it falls at a shallow angle.</div></div></div>
<p>Seasons result from the tilt of the Earth's axis compared to the plane of its revolution around the Sun. Throughout the year the northern and southern hemispheres are alternately turned either toward or away from the sun depending on Earth's position in its orbit. The hemisphere turned toward the sun receives more sunlight and is in summer, while the other hemisphere receives less sun and is in winter (see <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">solstice</a>).
</p><p>At the <a href="/wiki/Equinoxes" class="mw-redirect" title="Equinoxes">equinoxes</a>, the Earth's axis is perpendicular to the sun rather than tilted toward or away, meaning that day and night are both about 12 hours long across the whole of the Earth.
</p><p>The equator lies mostly on the three largest <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">oceans</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">Pacific Ocean</a>. Near the equator there is little temperature change throughout the year, though there may be dramatic differences in rainfall and humidity. The terms summer, autumn, winter and spring do not generally apply. Lowlands around the equator generally have a <a href="/wiki/Tropical_rainforest_climate" title="Tropical rainforest climate">tropical rainforest climate</a>, also known as an equatorial climate, though cold currents cause some regions to have <a href="/wiki/Tropical_monsoon_climate" title="Tropical monsoon climate">tropical monsoon climates</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Dry_season" title="Dry season">dry season</a> in the middle of the year, and the <a href="/wiki/Somali_Current" title="Somali Current">Somali Current</a> generated by <a href="/wiki/Monsoon_of_South_Asia" title="Monsoon of South Asia">the Asian monsoon</a> due to continental heating via the high <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_Plateau" title="Tibetan Plateau">Tibetan Plateau</a> causes <a href="/wiki/Greater_Somalia" title="Greater Somalia">Greater Somalia</a> to have an arid climate despite its equatorial location.
</p><p>Average annual temperatures in equatorial lowlands are around 31&#160;°C (88&#160;°F) during the afternoon and 23&#160;°C (73&#160;°F) around sunrise. Rainfall is very high away from cold current upwelling zones, from 2,500 to 3,500&#160;mm (100 to 140&#160;in) per year. There are about 200 rainy days per year and average annual sunshine hours are around 2,000. Despite high year-round sea level temperatures, some higher altitudes such as the <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mount_Kilimanjaro" title="Mount Kilimanjaro">Mount Kilimanjaro</a> have glaciers. The highest point on the equator is at the elevation of 4,690 metres (15,387&#160;ft), at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Equator&amp;params=0_0_0_N_77_59_31_W_type:landmark_region:EC&amp;title=highest+point+on+the+equator"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">0°0′0″N</span> <span class="longitude">77°59′31″W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#xfeff; / &#xfeff;</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="vcard"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">0.00000°N 77.99194°W</span><span style="display:none">&#xfeff; / <span class="geo">0.00000; -77.99194</span></span><span style="display:none">&#xfeff; (<span class="fn org">highest point on the equator</span>)</span></span></span></a></span>, found on the southern slopes of <a href="/wiki/Volc%C3%A1n_Cayambe" class="mw-redirect" title="Volcán Cayambe">Volcán Cayambe</a> [summit 5,790 metres (18,996&#160;ft)] in <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>. This is slightly above the <a href="/wiki/Snow_line" title="Snow line">snow line</a> and is the only place on the equator where snow lies on the ground. At the equator the snow line is around 1,000 metres (3,300&#160;ft) <i>lower</i> than on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">Mount Everest</a> and as much as 2,000 metres (6,600&#160;ft) lower than the highest snow line in the world, near the <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">Tropic of Capricorn</a> on <a href="/wiki/Llullaillaco" title="Llullaillaco">Llullaillaco</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Line_crossing_ceremonies">Line crossing ceremonies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Equator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Line crossing ceremonies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Line-crossing_ceremony" title="Line-crossing ceremony">Line-crossing ceremony</a></div>
<p>There is a widespread maritime tradition of holding ceremonies to mark a sailor's first crossing of the equator. In the past, these ceremonies have been notorious for their brutality, especially in naval practice.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Milder line-crossing ceremonies, typically featuring <a href="/wiki/Neptune_(god)" class="mw-redirect" title="Neptune (god)">King Neptune</a>, are also held for passengers' entertainment on some civilian ocean liners and cruise ships.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
