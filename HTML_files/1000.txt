<p><b>James Vincent Forrestal</b> (February 15, 1892&#160;– May 22, 1949) was the last <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_(government)" title="Cabinet (government)">Cabinet</a>-level <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_the_Navy" title="United States Secretary of the Navy">United States Secretary of the Navy</a> and the first <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">United States Secretary of Defense</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Historical_Office_James_V._Forrestal_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Historical_Office_James_V._Forrestal-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He was nominated to be Undersecretary of the Navy by President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> in 1940 where he led the national effort for industrial mobilisation for the war effort during World War II. He was named Secretary of the Navy in May 1944, and the first Secretary of the newly created Defense Department in 1947 by Roosevelt's successor <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">Harry S. Truman</a>. 
</p><p>Forrestal was a supporter of naval <a href="/wiki/Carrier_battle_group" title="Carrier battle group">battle groups</a> centered on <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_carrier" title="Aircraft carrier">aircraft carriers</a>. As Secretary of Defense, he was often at odds with President Truman over national policy.
After an embarrassing political gaffe became public in 1949, in which it was revealed that Forrestal had met and negotiated for a cabinet position with Truman's opponent <a href="/wiki/Thomas_E._Dewey" title="Thomas E. Dewey">Thomas E. Dewey</a> in the 1948 Presidential election, he was forced to resign as Secretary of Defense by President Truman.  Shortly after his resignation he underwent medical care for depression, and died after falling from a sixteenth floor window of the hospital where he was being treated.
</p><p>In 1954, the world's first <a href="/wiki/Supercarrier" class="mw-redirect" title="Supercarrier">supercarrier</a> was named <a href="/wiki/USS_Forrestal_(CV-59)" title="USS Forrestal (CV-59)">USS&#160;<i>Forrestal</i></a> in his honor, as is the <a href="/wiki/James_V._Forrestal_Building" title="James V. Forrestal Building">James V. Forrestal Building</a>, which houses the headquarters of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">United States Department of Energy</a>. He is also the namesake of the Forrestal Lecture Series at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Academy" title="United States Naval Academy">United States Naval Academy</a>, which brings prominent military and civilian leaders to speak to the Brigade of <a href="/wiki/Midshipman" title="Midshipman">Midshipmen</a>, and of the <a href="/wiki/Princeton_Plasma_Physics_Laboratory" title="Princeton Plasma Physics Laboratory">James Forrestal Campus</a> of <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Plainsboro_Township,_New_Jersey" title="Plainsboro Township, New Jersey">Plainsboro Township, New Jersey</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_private_employment">Early life and private employment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Forrestal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and private employment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Forrestal was born in <a href="/wiki/Beacon,_New_York" title="Beacon, New York">Matteawan</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, (now part of <a href="/wiki/Beacon,_New_York" title="Beacon, New York">Beacon, New York</a>), the youngest son of James Forrestal, an <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish</a> immigrant who dabbled in politics. His mother, the former Mary Anne Toohey (herself the daughter of another Irish immigrant) raised him as a devout <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He was an amateur <a href="/wiki/Boxing" title="Boxing">boxer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DictAmBio_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DictAmBio-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> After graduating from high school at the age of 16, in 1908, he spent the next three years working for a trio of newspapers: the <i>Matteawan Evening Journal</i>, the <i>Mount Vernon Argus</i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Poughkeepsie,_New_York" title="Poughkeepsie, New York">Poughkeepsie</a> News Press</i>.
</p><p>Forrestal entered <a href="/wiki/Dartmouth_College" title="Dartmouth College">Dartmouth College</a> in 1911, but transferred to <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a> in his sophomore year. He served as an editor for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Princetonian" title="The Daily Princetonian">The Daily Princetonian</a></i>. The senior class voted him "Most Likely to Succeed", but he left just prior to completing work on a degree. Forrestal married the former Josephine Stovall (née Ogden), a <a href="/wiki/Vogue_(magazine)" title="Vogue (magazine)">Vogue</a> writer, in 1926. She eventually developed <a href="/wiki/Alcoholism" title="Alcoholism">alcohol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mental_illness" class="mw-redirect" title="Mental illness">mental problems</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Forrestal went to work as a <a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">bond</a> salesman for William A. Read and Company (later renamed <a href="/wiki/Dillon,_Read_%26_Co." title="Dillon, Read &amp; Co.">Dillon, Read &amp; Co.</a>) in 1916. When the USA entered <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, he enlisted in the Navy and ultimately became a <a href="/wiki/Military_aviation" title="Military aviation">Naval Aviator</a>, training with the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Flying_Corps" title="Royal Flying Corps">Royal Flying Corps</a> in Canada. During the final year of the war, Forrestal spent much of his time in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, at the office of Naval Operations while completing his flight training. He eventually reached the rank of <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant" title="Lieutenant">Lieutenant</a>. After the war, Forrestal returned to working in finance and made his fortune on Wall Street. He became a partner (1923), vice-president (1926), and president of the company (1937). He also acted as a publicist for the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a> committee in <a href="/wiki/Dutchess_County,_New_York" title="Dutchess County, New York">Dutchess County, New York</a> helping politicians from the area win elections at both the state and national level. One of those individuals aided by his work was a neighbor, <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>.
</p><p>By some accounts, Forrestal was a compulsive <a href="/wiki/Workaholic" title="Workaholic">workaholic</a>, skilled <a href="/wiki/Administrator_(business)" class="mw-redirect" title="Administrator (business)">administrator</a>, pugnacious, introspective, shy, philosophic, solitary, and <a href="/wiki/Emotional_security" title="Emotional security">emotionally insecure</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DictAmBio_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DictAmBio-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career">Political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Forrestal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secretary_of_the_Navy">Secretary of the Navy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Forrestal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Secretary of the Navy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> appointed Forrestal a <a href="/wiki/Special_Assistant_to_the_President" class="mw-redirect" title="Special Assistant to the President">special administrative assistant</a> on June 22, 1940. Six weeks later, he nominated him for the newly established position, Undersecretary of the Navy. In his nearly four years as undersecretary, Forrestal proved highly effective at mobilizing domestic <a href="/wiki/Industrial_production" title="Industrial production">industrial production</a> for the war effort. <a href="/wiki/Chief_of_Naval_Operations" title="Chief of Naval Operations">Chief of Naval Operations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Admiral" title="Admiral">Admiral</a> <a href="/wiki/Ernest_J._King" class="mw-redirect" title="Ernest J. King">Ernest J. King</a>, wanted to control logistics and procurement, but Forrestal prevailed.<sup id="cite_ref-DictAmBio_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DictAmBio-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 1942, to get a grasp on the reports for materiel his office was receiving, he made a tour of naval operations in the Southwest Pacific and a stop at Pearl Harbor. Returning to Washington, D.C., he made his report to President Roosevelt, Secretary of War <a href="/wiki/Henry_L._Stimson" title="Henry L. Stimson">Henry L. Stimson</a>, and the cabinet. In response to Forrestal's elevated request that materiel be sent immediately to the Southwest Pacific area, Stimson (who was more concerned with supplying <a href="/wiki/Operation_Torch" title="Operation Torch">Operation Torch</a> in North Africa), told Forrestal, "Jim, you've got a bad case of <a href="/wiki/Localitis" class="mw-redirect" title="Localitis">localitis</a>." Forrestal shot back in a heated manner, "Mr. Secretary, if the Marines on <a href="/wiki/Guadalcanal_Campaign" class="mw-redirect" title="Guadalcanal Campaign">Guadalcanal</a> were wiped out, the reaction of the country will give you a bad case of localitis in the seat of your pants".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He became <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_the_Navy" title="United States Secretary of the Navy">Secretary of the Navy</a> on May 19, 1944, after his immediate superior Secretary <a href="/wiki/Frank_Knox" title="Frank Knox">Frank Knox</a> died from a <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">heart attack</a>. Forrestal led the Navy through the closing year of the war and the painful early years of <a href="/wiki/Demobilization" title="Demobilization">demobilization</a> that followed. As Secretary, Forrestal introduced a policy of <a href="/wiki/Racial_integration" title="Racial integration">racial integration</a> in the Navy.
</p><p>Forrestal traveled to combat zones to see naval forces in action. He was in the <a href="/wiki/South_West_Pacific_theatre_of_World_War_II" title="South West Pacific theatre of World War II">South Pacific</a> in 1942, present at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kwajalein" title="Battle of Kwajalein">Battle of Kwajalein</a> in 1944, and (as Secretary) witnessed the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Iwo_Jima" title="Battle of Iwo Jima">Battle of Iwo Jima</a> in 1945. After five days of pitched battle, a detachment of Marines was sent to hoist the American flag on the 545-foot summit of Mount Suribachi on Iwo Jima. This was the first time in the war that the U.S. flag had flown on Japanese soil. Forrestal, who had just landed on the beach, claimed the historic flag as a souvenir. A second, larger flag was run up in its place, and this second flag-raising was the moment captured by Associated Press photographer <a href="/wiki/Joe_Rosenthal" title="Joe Rosenthal">Joe Rosenthal</a> in his <a href="/wiki/Raising_the_Flag_on_Iwo_Jima" title="Raising the Flag on Iwo Jima">famous photograph</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Forrestal, along with <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_War" title="United States Secretary of War">Secretary of War</a> <a href="/wiki/Henry_Stimson" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Stimson">Henry Stimson</a> and Under Secretary of State <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Grew" title="Joseph Grew">Joseph Grew</a>, in the early months of 1945, strongly advocated a softer policy toward Japan that would permit a negotiated armistice, a 'face-saving' surrender. Forrestal's primary concern was not the resurgence of a militarized Japan, but rather "the menace of Russian <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">Communism</a> and its attraction for decimated, destabilized societies in Europe and Asia," and, therefore, keeping the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> out of the war with Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-Hoopes_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hoopes-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> So strongly did he feel about this matter that he cultivated negotiation efforts that some regarded as approaching insubordination.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>His counsel on ending the war was finally followed, but not until the atomic bombs had been dropped on Hiroshima and Nagasaki. The day after the Nagasaki attack, the Japanese sent out a radio transmission saying that it was ready to accept the terms of the allies' <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Declaration" title="Potsdam Declaration">Potsdam Declaration</a>, "with the understanding that said declaration does not comprise any demand which prejudices the prerogatives of His Majesty as a sovereign ruler." That position still fell short of the U.S. "unconditional surrender" demand, retaining the sticking point that had held up the war's conclusion for months. Strong voices within the administration, including Secretary of State James Byrnes, counseled fighting on. At that point, "Forrestal came up with a shrewd and simple solution: Accept the offer and declare that it accomplishes what the Potsdam Declaration demanded. Say that the Emperor and the Japanese government will rule subject to the orders of the Supreme Commander for the Allied Powers. This would imply recognition of the Emperor while tending to neutralize American public passions against the Emperor. Truman liked this. It would be close enough to 'unconditional.'"<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the war, Forrestal urged Truman to take a hard line with the Soviets over Poland. He also strongly influenced the new Wisconsin Senator, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_McCarthy" title="Joseph McCarthy">Joseph McCarthy</a>, concerning infiltration of the government by Communists. Upon McCarthy's arrival in Washington in December 1946, Forrestal invited him to lunch. In McCarthy's words, "Before meeting Jim Forrestal I thought we were losing to international Communism because of incompetence and stupidity on the part of our planners. I mentioned that to Forrestal. I shall forever remember his answer. He said, 'McCarthy, consistency has never been a mark of stupidity. If they were merely stupid, they would occasionally make a mistake in our favor.' This phrase struck me so forcefully that I have often used it since."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secretary_of_Defense">Secretary of Defense</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Forrestal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Secretary of Defense">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1947, <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">Harry S. Truman</a> appointed him the first <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">United States Secretary of Defense</a>. Forrestal continued to advocate for complete racial integration of the <a href="/wiki/U.S._armed_forces" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. armed forces">services</a>, a policy eventually implemented in 1949.
</p><p>During private cabinet meetings with President Truman in 1946 and 1947, Forrestal had argued against <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_Palestine" class="mw-redirect" title="Partition of Palestine">partition of Palestine</a> on the grounds it would infuriate <a href="/wiki/Arab_countries" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab countries">Arab countries</a> who supplied oil needed for the <a href="/wiki/U.S._economy" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. economy">U.S. economy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Defense_(military)" class="mw-redirect" title="Defense (military)">national defense</a>. Instead, Forrestal favored a federalization plan for Palestine. Outside the White House, response to Truman's continued silence on the issue was immediate. President Truman received threats to cut off <a href="/wiki/Campaign_contributions" class="mw-redirect" title="Campaign contributions">campaign contributions</a> from wealthy donors, as well as <a href="/wiki/Hate_mail" title="Hate mail">hate mail</a>, including a letter accusing him of "preferring <a href="/wiki/Fascist" class="mw-redirect" title="Fascist">fascist</a> and Arab elements to the democracy-loving <a href="/wiki/Jewish_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewish people">Jewish people</a> of Palestine."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Appalled by the intensity and implied threats over the partition question, Forrestal appealed to Truman in two separate cabinet meetings not to base his decision on partition, whatever the outcome, on the basis of political pressure.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In his only known public comment on the issue, Forrestal stated to <a href="/wiki/J._Howard_McGrath" title="J. Howard McGrath">J. Howard McGrath</a>, <a href="/wiki/U.S._Senator" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Senator">Senator</a> from <a href="/wiki/Rhode_Island" title="Rhode Island">Rhode Island</a>:
</p>
<blockquote><p>...no group in this country should be permitted to influence our policy to the point it could endanger our national security.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Forrestal's statement soon earned him the active enmity of some congressmen and supporters of <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>. Forrestal was also an early target of the <a href="/wiki/Muckraking" class="mw-redirect" title="Muckraking">muckraking</a> columnist and broadcaster <a href="/wiki/Drew_Pearson_(journalist)" title="Drew Pearson (journalist)">Drew Pearson</a>, an opponent of <a href="/wiki/Foreign_policies" class="mw-redirect" title="Foreign policies">foreign policies</a> hostile to the Soviet Union, who began to regularly call for Forrestal's removal after President Truman named him Secretary of Defense.<sup id="cite_ref-Time1949_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Time1949-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Pearson told his own <a href="/wiki/Mentorship" title="Mentorship">protege</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Anderson_(columnist)" title="Jack Anderson (columnist)">Jack Anderson</a>, that he believed Forrestal was "the most dangerous man in America" and claimed that if he was not removed from office, he would "cause another world war."
</p><p>Upon taking office as Secretary of Defense, Forrestal was surprised to learn that the administration did not <a href="/wiki/Military_budget_of_the_United_States" title="Military budget of the United States">budget for defense</a> needs based on <a href="/wiki/Military_threat" title="Military threat">military threats</a> posed by enemies of the United States and its interests. According to historian <a href="/wiki/Walter_LaFeber" title="Walter LaFeber">Walter LaFeber</a>, Truman was known to approach defense budgetary requests in the abstract, without regard to defense response requirements in the event of conflicts with potential enemies.<sup id="cite_ref-LaFeber1993_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LaFeber1993-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The president would begin by subtracting from total receipts the amount needed for domestic needs and recurrent operating costs, with any surplus going to the defense budget for that year.<sup id="cite_ref-LaFeber1993_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LaFeber1993-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The Truman administration's willingness to slash conventional readiness needs for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">Navy</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">Marine Corps</a> soon caused fierce controversies within the upper ranks of their respective branches.<sup id="cite_ref-LaFeber1993_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LaFeber1993-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Blair2003_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blair2003-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the Reagan years, <a href="/wiki/Paul_Nitze" title="Paul Nitze">Paul Nitze</a> reflected upon the qualities which made a Secretary of Defense great: the ability to work with Congress, the ability for "big-time management," and an ability at war planning. Nitze felt that Forrestal was the only one who possessed all three qualities together.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war">Post-war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Forrestal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post-war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the close of World War II, millions of dollars of serviceable equipment had been scrapped or abandoned rather than having funds appropriated for its storage costs. New military equipment <i>en route</i> to operations in the Pacific theater was scrapped or simply tossed overboard.<sup id="cite_ref-Blair2003_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Blair2003-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Facing the wholesale <a href="/wiki/Demobilization" title="Demobilization">demobilization</a> of most of the US defense force structure, Forrestal resisted President Truman's efforts to substantially reduce defense appropriations,<sup id="cite_ref-Hess1972_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hess1972-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> but was unable to prevent a steady reduction in defense spending, resulting in major cuts not only in defense equipment stockpiles, but also in military readiness.
</p><p>By 1948, President Harry Truman had approved military budgets billions of dollars below what the services were requesting, putting Forrestal in the middle of a fierce tug-of-war between the President and the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Chiefs_of_Staff" title="Joint Chiefs of Staff">Joint Chiefs of Staff</a>. Forrestal was also becoming increasingly worried about the Soviet threat.<sup id="cite_ref-Chambers_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> His 18 months at Defense came at an exceptionally difficult time for the U.S. military establishment: Communist governments came to power in <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia" title="Czechoslovakia">Czechoslovakia</a> and China; the Soviets imposed a <a href="/wiki/Berlin_blockade" class="mw-redirect" title="Berlin blockade">blockade</a> on <a href="/wiki/West_Berlin" title="West Berlin">West Berlin</a> prompting the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Airlift" class="mw-redirect" title="Berlin Airlift">Berlin Airlift</a> to supply the city; the <a href="/wiki/1948_Arab%E2%80%93Israeli_War" title="1948 Arab–Israeli War">1948 Arab–Israeli War</a> followed the establishment of Israel; and negotiations were going on for the formation of <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a> recorded he was in agreement with Forrestal's theories on the dangers of Soviet and International communist expansion. Eisenhower recalled that Forrestal had been "the one man who, in the very midst of the war, always counseled caution and alertness in dealing with the Soviets." Eisenhower remembered on several occasions, while he was <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Allied_Commander" title="Supreme Allied Commander">Supreme Allied Commander</a>, he had been visited by Forrestal, who carefully explained his thesis that the Communists would never cease trying to destroy all <a href="/wiki/Representative_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Representative government">representative government</a>. Eisenhower commented in his personal diary on 11 June 1949, "I never had cause to doubt the accuracy of his judgments on this point."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Forrestal also opposed the unification of the military services proposed by the Truman officials. Even so, he helped develop the <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Act_of_1947" title="National Security Act of 1947">National Security Act of 1947</a> that created the <a href="/wiki/National_Military_Establishment" class="mw-redirect" title="National Military Establishment">National Military Establishment</a> (the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a> was not created as such until August 1949).<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> With the former <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_War" title="United States Secretary of War">Secretary of War</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_P._Patterson" title="Robert P. Patterson">Robert P. Patterson</a> retiring to private life, Forrestal was the next choice.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resignation_as_Secretary_of_Defense">Resignation as Secretary of Defense</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Forrestal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Resignation as Secretary of Defense">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Governor_of_New_York" title="Governor of New York">Governor of New York</a> <a href="/wiki/Thomas_E._Dewey" title="Thomas E. Dewey">Thomas E. Dewey</a> was expected to win the <a href="/wiki/1948_United_States_presidential_election" title="1948 United States presidential election">presidential elections of 1948</a>. Forrestal met with Dewey privately, and it was agreed he would continue as Secretary of Defense under a Dewey administration. Unwittingly, Forrestal would trigger a series of events that would not only undermine his already precarious position with President Truman but would also contribute to the loss of his job, his failing health, and eventual demise. Weeks before the election, Pearson published an exposé of the meetings between Dewey and Forrestal.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
In 1949, angered over Forrestal's continued opposition to his defense economization policies, and concerned about reports in the press over his mental condition, Truman abruptly asked Forrestal to resign. By March 31, 1949, Forrestal was out of a job.<sup id="cite_ref-Hess1972_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hess1972-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> He was replaced by <a href="/wiki/Louis_A._Johnson" title="Louis A. Johnson">Louis A. Johnson</a>, an ardent supporter of Truman's defense retrenchment policy.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Psychiatric_treatment">Psychiatric treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Forrestal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Psychiatric treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1949, exhausted from overwork, Forrestal entered psychiatric treatment. The attending psychiatrist, <a href="/wiki/Captain_(naval)" title="Captain (naval)">Captain</a> George N. Raines, was handpicked by the <a href="/wiki/Surgeon_General_of_the_United_States_Navy" title="Surgeon General of the United States Navy">Navy Surgeon General</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Regimen" title="Regimen">regimen</a> was as follows:
</p>
<ol><li>1st week: <a href="/wiki/Truth_drug" class="mw-redirect" title="Truth drug">narcosis</a> with <a href="/wiki/Amobarbital" title="Amobarbital">sodium amytal</a>.</li>
<li>2nd – 5th weeks: a regimen of <a href="/wiki/Insulin_shock_therapy" title="Insulin shock therapy">insulin sub-shock</a> combined with psycho-therapeutic interviews. According to Dr. Raines, the patient overreacted to the insulin much as he had to the amytal and this would occasionally throw him into a confused state with a great deal of agitation and confusion.</li>
<li>4th week: insulin administered only in stimulating doses; 10 units of insulin four times a day, morning, noon, afternoon and evening.</li></ol>
<p>According to Dr. Raines, "We considered <a href="/wiki/Electroconvulsive_therapy" title="Electroconvulsive therapy">electro-shock</a> but thought it better to postpone it for another 90 days. In reactive depression if electro-shock is used early and the patient is returned to the same situation from which he came there is grave danger of suicide in the immediate period after they return... so strangely enough we left out electro-shock to avoid what actually happened anyhow".<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Forrestal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although Forrestal told associates he had decided to resign, he was shattered when Truman abruptly asked for his resignation. His letter of resignation was tendered on March 28, 1949. On the day of Forrestal's resignation from office, he was reported to have gone into a strange daze and was flown on a Navy airplane to the estate of Under Secretary of State <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Lovett" title="Robert A. Lovett">Robert A. Lovett</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hobe_Sound,_Florida" title="Hobe Sound, Florida">Hobe Sound, Florida</a>, where Forrestal's wife, Josephine, was vacationing. Dr. <a href="/wiki/William_C._Menninger" title="William C. Menninger">William C. Menninger</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Menninger_Clinic" class="mw-redirect" title="Menninger Clinic">Menninger Clinic</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kansas" title="Kansas">Kansas</a> was consulted and he diagnosed "severe depression" of the type "seen in <a href="/wiki/Combat_stress_reaction" title="Combat stress reaction">operational fatigue</a> during the war". The Menninger Clinic had successfully treated similar cases during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, but Forrestal's wife, his friend and associate <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Eberstadt" title="Ferdinand Eberstadt">Ferdinand Eberstadt</a>, Dr. Menninger and Navy psychiatrist Captain Dr. George N. Raines decided to send the former Secretary of Defense to the <a href="/wiki/National_Naval_Medical_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="National Naval Medical Center">National Naval Medical Center</a> (NNMC) in <a href="/wiki/Bethesda,_Maryland" title="Bethesda, Maryland">Bethesda, Maryland</a>, where it would be possible to deny his mental illness.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> He was checked into NNMC five days later. The decision to house him on the 16th floor instead of the first floor was justified in the same way. Forrestal's condition was officially announced as "nervous and physical exhaustion"; his lead doctor, Captain Raines, diagnosed his condition as "depression" or "reactive depression".
</p><p>As a person who prized anonymity and once stated that his hobby was "obscurity", Forrestal and his policies had been the constant target of vicious personal attacks from columnists, including Drew Pearson and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Winchell" title="Walter Winchell">Walter Winchell</a>. Pearson's protégé, <a href="/wiki/Jack_Anderson_(columnist)" title="Jack Anderson (columnist)">Jack Anderson</a>, later asserted that Pearson "hectored Forrestal with innuendos and false accusations". <sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:US_Navy_030820-N-9593R-114_The_entrance_to_the_National_Naval_Medical_Center_in_Bethesda,_Maryland.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/US_Navy_030820-N-9593R-114_The_entrance_to_the_National_Naval_Medical_Center_in_Bethesda%2C_Maryland.jpg/220px-US_Navy_030820-N-9593R-114_The_entrance_to_the_National_Naval_Medical_Center_in_Bethesda%2C_Maryland.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="308" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/US_Navy_030820-N-9593R-114_The_entrance_to_the_National_Naval_Medical_Center_in_Bethesda%2C_Maryland.jpg/330px-US_Navy_030820-N-9593R-114_The_entrance_to_the_National_Naval_Medical_Center_in_Bethesda%2C_Maryland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/US_Navy_030820-N-9593R-114_The_entrance_to_the_National_Naval_Medical_Center_in_Bethesda%2C_Maryland.jpg/440px-US_Navy_030820-N-9593R-114_The_entrance_to_the_National_Naval_Medical_Center_in_Bethesda%2C_Maryland.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1501" data-file-height="2100" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:US_Navy_030820-N-9593R-114_The_entrance_to_the_National_Naval_Medical_Center_in_Bethesda,_Maryland.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/National_Naval_Medical_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="National Naval Medical Center">National Naval Medical Center</a></div></div></div>
<p>Forrestal seemed to be on the road to recovery, having regained 12 pounds (5.4&#160;kg) since his entry into the hospital. However, in the early morning hours of May 22, his body, clad only in the bottom half of a pair of pajamas, was found on a third-floor roof below the sixteenth-floor kitchen across the hall from his room.<sup id="cite_ref-Report_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Report-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ariwatch.com_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ariwatch.com-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Forrestal's alleged last written statement, touted in the contemporary press and later biographers as an implied suicide note, was part of a poem from <a href="/wiki/Winthrop_Mackworth_Praed" title="Winthrop Mackworth Praed">W.&#160;M. Praed's</a> translation of <a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">Sophocles</a>' tragedy <i><a href="/wiki/Ajax_(Sophocles)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ajax (Sophocles)">Ajax</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><div class="poem">
<p>Fair <a href="/wiki/Salamis_Island" title="Salamis Island">Salamis</a>, the billows' roar,<br />
Wander around thee yet,<br />
And sailors gaze upon thy shore<br />
Firm in the Ocean set.<br />
Thy son is in a foreign clime<br />
Where <a href="/wiki/Mount_Ida" title="Mount Ida">Ida</a> feeds her countless flocks,<br />
Far from thy dear, remembered rocks,<br />
Worn by the waste of time–<br />
Comfortless, nameless, hopeless save<br />
In the dark prospect of the yawning grave....<br />
Woe to the mother in her close of day,<br />
Woe to her desolate heart and temples gray,<br />
When she shall hear<br />
Her loved one's story whispered in her ear!<br />
"Woe, woe!" will be the cry–<br />
No quiet murmur like the tremulous wail<br />
Of the lone bird, the querulous <a href="/wiki/Nightingale" class="mw-redirect" title="Nightingale">nightingale</a>–
</p>
</div></blockquote>
<p>The official Navy review board, which completed hearings on May 31, waited until October 11, 1949, to release only a brief summary of its findings. The announcement, as reported on page 15 of the October 12 <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i>, stated only that Forrestal had died from his fall from the window. It did not say what might have caused the fall, nor did it make any mention of a bathrobe sash cord that had first been reported as tied around his neck.  The full report,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> was not released by the Department of the Navy until April 2004 in response to a Freedom of Information Act request by researcher, David Martin <sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>After full and mature deliberation, the board finds as follows:
</p><p>FINDING OF FACTS
</p>
<ol><li>That the body found on the ledge outside of room three eighty-four of building one of the National Naval Medical Center at one-fifty a.m. and pronounced dead at one fifty-five a.m., Sunday, May 22, 1949, was identified as that of the late James V. Forrestal, a patient on the Neuropsychiatric Service of the U. S. Naval Hospital, National Naval Medical Center, Bethesda, Maryland.</li>
<li>That the late James V. Forrestal died on or about May 22, 1949, at the National Naval Medical Center, Bethesda, Maryland, as a result of injuries, multiple, extreme, received incident to a fall from a high point in the tower, building one, National Naval Medical Center, Bethesda, Maryland.</li>
<li>That the behavior of the deceased during the period of his stay in the hospital preceding his death was indicative of a mental depression.</li>
<li>That the treatment and precautions in the conduct of the case were in agreement with accepted psychiatric practice and commensurate with the evident status of the patient at all times.</li>
<li>That the death was not caused in any manner by the intent, fault, negligence or inefficiency of any person or persons in the naval service or connected therewith.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>
</blockquote>
<p>James Forrestal is buried in Section 30 Lot 674 Grid X-39 of <a href="/wiki/Arlington_National_Cemetery" title="Arlington National Cemetery">Arlington National Cemetery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Forrestal_Diaries"><i>The Forrestal Diaries</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Forrestal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Forrestal Diaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Forrestal's diaries from 1944 to March 1949 were serialised in the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Herald_Tribune" title="New York Herald Tribune">New York Herald Tribune</a></i> in 1951, and published as a 581-page book <i>The Forrestal Diaries</i>, edited by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Millis" title="Walter Millis">Walter Millis</a> in October 1951. They were censored prior to publication.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Adam_Matthew_Publications_Ltd." class="mw-redirect" title="Adam Matthew Publications Ltd.">Adam Matthew Publications Ltd.</a> published a <a href="/wiki/Microform" title="Microform"> microfilm</a> of the complete and unexpurgated diaries in 2001, from the originals preserved in the <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University_Library" title="Princeton University Library">Seeley G Mudd Manuscript Library</a>, <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sscnet.ucla.edu_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sscnet.ucla.edu-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> An example of censorship is the removal of the following account of a conversation with Truman: "He referred to Hitler as an egomaniac. The result is we shall have a <a href="/wiki/Slav" class="mw-redirect" title="Slav">Slav</a> Europe for a long time to come. I don't think it is so bad."<sup id="cite_ref-sscnet.ucla.edu_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sscnet.ucla.edu-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Forrestal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/James_V._Forrestal_Building" title="James V. Forrestal Building">James V. Forrestal Building</a> in Washington, D.C., completed in 1969, is named for him.
</p><p>The J.&#160;V. Forrestal Elementary School at 125 Liberty Street in <a href="/wiki/Beacon,_New_York" title="Beacon, New York">Beacon, New York</a>, his hometown, is named for him.
Forrestal Elementary School in the Great Lakes military housing area is named for him.
</p><p>In the 1994 television movie <i><a href="/wiki/Roswell_(film)" title="Roswell (film)">Roswell</a></i>, Forrestal is portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Roche" title="Eugene Roche">Eugene Roche</a>. He is depicted as sitting on a commission concerning the <a href="/wiki/Roswell_UFO_incident" title="Roswell UFO incident">Roswell UFO incident</a> and advocating the eventual release of information to the public. The film treats his death and classified diary as highly suspicious.
</p><p>An opera concerning the conspiracy theories behind Forrestal's death, <i>Nightingale: The Last Days of James Forrestal</i> composed by Evan Hause with a libretto by Gary Heidt, premiered in New York City at the Present Company Theatorium on May 19, 2002.
</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Golden_Age_(comics)" title="The Golden Age (comics)">The Golden Age</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Elseworlds" title="Elseworlds">Elseworlds</a></i> "imaginary story" four-issue prestige format <a href="/wiki/Mini-series" class="mw-redirect" title="Mini-series">mini-series</a> by <a href="/wiki/James_Robinson_(comics)" class="mw-redirect" title="James Robinson (comics)">James Robinson</a> (writer) and <a href="/wiki/Paul_Smith_(comics)" title="Paul Smith (comics)">Paul Smith</a> (artist), Forrestal's death is shown to have been a murder. Forrestal is pushed from the window of his Bethesda Naval Hospital room by the Golden Age <a href="/wiki/Robotman_(Robert_Crane)" title="Robotman (Robert Crane)">Robotman</a>.
</p><p>In the PC game <i><a href="/wiki/Area_51_(2005_video_game)" title="Area 51 (2005 video game)">Area 51</a></i> one of the secret documents the player can collect talks about the <a href="/wiki/Majestic_12" title="Majestic 12">Majestic 12</a> initiative being threatened with "receiving the same punishment as his last secretary, Forrestal", implying the murder of Forrestal was to cover his operation from the public.
</p><p>In the anime OVA series <i><a href="/wiki/Mobile_Suit_Gundam_0083:_Stardust_Memory" title="Mobile Suit Gundam 0083: Stardust Memory">Mobile Suit Gundam 0083: Stardust Memory</a>,</i> a secret document is briefly viewable in the eighth episode that mentions the death of a Secretary Forrestal. It goes on to say that a "vacancy" was left due to his death until he was replaced by an unnamed general.
</p><p>In the 2002 <a href="/wiki/HBO" title="HBO">HBO</a> TV movie <i><a href="/wiki/Path_to_War" title="Path to War">Path to War</a></i>, Secretary of Defense Robert McNamara (portrayed by Alec Baldwin) hauntingly recounts the story of James Forrestal's dismissal and suicide to speechwriter Richard Goodwin (portrayed by James Frain).
</p><p>In the 2006 film <i><a href="/wiki/Flags_of_Our_Fathers_(film)" title="Flags of Our Fathers (film)">Flags of Our Fathers</a>,</i> Forrestal was played by <a href="/wiki/Michael_Cumpsty" title="Michael Cumpsty">Michael Cumpsty</a>.
</p><p>The story of James Forrestal is prominently featured in Chapter 4 of the <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Stone" title="Oliver Stone">Oliver Stone</a> popular documentary series <i><a href="/wiki/Oliver_Stone%27s_Untold_History_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Oliver Stone&#39;s Untold History of the United States">Oliver Stone's Untold History of the United States</a>,</i> which aired on Showtime in 2012–13.
</p><p>The later part of Forrestal's life, including his marriage and his death, is a large part of <i>Majic Man</i> by <a href="/wiki/Max_Allan_Collins" title="Max Allan Collins">Max Allan Collins</a>.
</p><p>In 2017, the final episode of  <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a> Miniseries  <i><a href="/wiki/Wormwood_(miniseries)" title="Wormwood (miniseries)">Wormwood</a></i> implies that Forrestal’s death may have been one of a series of deaths labeled as suicides, but may have been CIA “executions.”
</p>
