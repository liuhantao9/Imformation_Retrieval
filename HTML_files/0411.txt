	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Cariban languages</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">&#160;&#160;(Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Carib_languages&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Carib languages">Carib languages</a>)</span></div>

<p>The <b>Cariban languages</b> are an indigenous <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">language family</a> of <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. They are widespread across northernmost South America, from the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_River" title="Amazon River">Amazon River</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombian</a> <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">Andes</a> but also appear in central Brazil. Cariban languages are relatively closely related. There are two to three dozen of them, depending on what is considered a dialect. Most are still spoken but often by only a few hundred speakers; the only one with more than a few thousand is <a href="/wiki/Macushi_language" title="Macushi language">Macushi</a>, which has 30,000. The Cariban family is well known in the linguistic world partly because <a href="/wiki/Hixkaryana" class="mw-redirect" title="Hixkaryana">Hixkaryana</a>'s default <a href="/wiki/Word_order" title="Word order">word order</a> is <a href="/wiki/Object%E2%80%93verb%E2%80%93subject" title="Object–verb–subject">object–verb–subject</a>, which had previously not been thought to exist in human language.
</p><p>Some years prior to the arrival of the first <a href="/wiki/Spanish_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish people">Spanish</a> <a href="/wiki/Explorer" class="mw-redirect" title="Explorer">explorers</a>, Caribs invaded and occupied the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> and killed, displaced or assimilated the <a href="/wiki/Arawaks" class="mw-redirect" title="Arawaks">Arawaks</a> who had inhabited the islands. The resulting language was Carib in name but largely Arawak in substance. That was because the invading Carib men killed Arawak men and took Arawak wives, who then passed their language on to the children. For a time, Arawak was spoken by women and children and Carib by adult men, but the situation was unstable. As each generation of Carib-Arawak boys reached adulthood, they acquired less Carib until only basic vocabulary and a few grammatical elements were left. That "<a href="/wiki/Island_Carib_language" title="Island Carib language">Island Carib</a>" became extinct in the <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Antilles" title="Lesser Antilles">Lesser Antilles</a> in the 1920s but survives in the form of <a href="/wiki/Garifuna_language" title="Garifuna language">Garífuna</a>, or "Black Carib," in <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>. The gender distinction has dwindled to only a handful of words. <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">Dominica</a> is the only island in the eastern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> to retain some of its <a href="/wiki/Pre-Columbian" class="mw-redirect" title="Pre-Columbian">pre-Columbian</a> population, the <a href="/wiki/Island_Carib_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Island Carib people">Carib</a> Indians, about 3,000 of whom live on the island's east coast.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_division">Family division</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cariban_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family division">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Cariban languages are closely related, and in many cases where a language is more distinct, this is due to influence from neighboring languages rather than an indication that it is not closely related. Kaufman 2007 says, "Except for Opon, Yukpa, Pimenteira and Palmela (and possibly Panare), the Cariban languages are not very diverse phonologically and lexically (though more so than Romance, for example)."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Inline_citation#When_you_must_use_inline_citations" title="Wikipedia:Inline citation"><span title="The text near this tag needs a citation. (October 2014)">This quote needs a citation</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Previous_classifications">Previous classifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cariban_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Previous classifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Good data has been collected around ca. 2000 on most Cariban languages; classifications prior to that time (including Kaufman 2007, which relies on them) are unreliable. 
Several such classifications are seen; the one shown here divides Cariban into seven branches. A traditional geographic classification into northern and southern branches is cross referenced with (N) or (S) after each language.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Galibi_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Galibi language">Galibi</a></b> [<a href="/wiki/Kali%C3%B1a" class="mw-redirect" title="Kaliña">Kaliña</a>] (N)</li>
<li><b>Guiana Carib</b> (Taranoan):
<ul><li>Trio: <a href="/wiki/Tiriy%C3%B3_language" title="Tiriyó language">Tiriyó</a>–<a href="/wiki/Akuriy%C3%B3_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Akuriyó language">Akuriyó</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salum%C3%A1_language" title="Salumá language">Salumá</a> (N), <a href="/wiki/Carijona_language" title="Carijona language">Carijona</a>–<a href="/wiki/Hian%C3%A1koto_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Hianákoto language">Hianákoto</a> (S)</li>
<li>Kashuyana: <a href="/wiki/Sikiana_language" title="Sikiana language">Sikiana</a> (N), <a href="/wiki/Kaxui%C3%A2na_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Kaxuiâna language">Kaxuiâna</a> [Warikyana] <i>(†)</i> (S)</li>
<li>Waiwai: <a href="/wiki/Hixkaryana_language" title="Hixkaryana language">Hixkaryána</a> (S), <a href="/wiki/Waiwai_language" title="Waiwai language">Waiwai</a> (N)
<dl><dd>Kaufman breaks this up into its constituent branches</dd></dl></li></ul></li>
<li><b>North Amazonian Carib</b>:
<ul><li>Yawaperi: <a href="/wiki/Atruah%C3%AD_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Atruahí language">Atruahí</a> [Atrowari, Waimiri] (N)</li>
<li>Pemong: <a href="/wiki/Macushi_language" title="Macushi language">Macushi</a>–<a href="/wiki/Pemon_language" title="Pemon language">Pemon</a> [Arekuna], <a href="/wiki/Akawaio_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Akawaio language">Akawaio</a>–<a href="/wiki/Patamona_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Patamona language">Patamona</a> (= Kapong, Ingariko) (N)</li>
<li>Paravilyana: <a href="/wiki/Pawishiana_language" title="Pawishiana language">Pawishiana</a> <i>(†)</i>
<dl><dd>Kaufman breaks this up into its constituent branches, adding <a href="/wiki/Purukot%C3%B3_language" title="Purukotó language">Purukotó</a> <i>(†)</i> to Pemong; <a href="/wiki/Boanar%C3%AD_language" title="Boanarí language">Boanarí</a> <i>(†)</i> to Atruahí; <a href="/wiki/Paravilyana_language" title="Paravilyana language">Paravilyana</a> <i>(†)</i> and <a href="/wiki/Sapar%C3%A1_language" title="Sapará language">Sapará</a> <i>(†)</i> to Pawishiana</dd></dl></li></ul></li>
<li><b>Central Carib</b>:
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Wayana_language" title="Wayana language">Wayana</a>–<a href="/wiki/Apala%C3%AD_language" title="Apalaí language">Apalaí</a> (N)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Maquiritari_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Maquiritari language">Maquiritari</a> [Dekwana] (S)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mapoyo_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Mapoyo language">Mapoyo</a>–<a href="/wiki/Yabarana_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Yabarana language">Yabarana</a>–<a href="/wiki/P%C3%A9mono_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Pémono language">Pémono</a> (N)
<dl><dd>Kaufman adds <a href="/wiki/Chaima_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Chaima language">Chaima</a> [Kumaná]; <a href="/wiki/Arakaj%C3%BA_language" title="Arakajú language">Arakajú</a> <i>(†)</i> (to Wayana); <a href="/wiki/Yao_language_(Carib)" class="mw-redirect" title="Yao language (Carib)">Yao</a> <i>(†)</i> and <a href="/wiki/Tiverikoto_language" title="Tiverikoto language">Tiverikoto</a> <i>(†)</i>; <a href="/wiki/Wajumar%C3%A1_language" title="Wajumará language">Wajumará</a> <i>(†)</i> (to Makiritare)</dd>

<li><b>South Amazonian Carib</b>:
<ul><li>Bakairi: <a href="/wiki/Bakair%C3%AD_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Bakairí language">Bakairí</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kuik%C3%BAro_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Kuikúro language">Kuikúro</a> [Kalapálo, Amonap], <a href="/wiki/Matipuhy_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Matipuhy language">Matipuhy</a> [Nahukwa] <i>(†)</i> (S)</li>
<li>Arara: <a href="/wiki/Txik%C3%A3o_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Txikão language">Txikão</a> [Ikpeng, Chikaon], <a href="/wiki/Par%C3%A1_Ar%C3%A1ra_language" title="Pará Arára language">Arára</a> [Pará] (N)
<dl><dd>To Arara Kaufman adds extinct <a href="/wiki/Juma_language_(Carib)" title="Juma language (Carib)">Juma</a> <i>(†)</i>, <a href="/wiki/Apiak%C3%A1-Apingi_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Apiaká-Apingi language">Apiaká-Apingi</a> <i>(†)</i>, <a href="/wiki/Yarum%C3%A1_language_(Carib)" title="Yarumá language (Carib)">Yarumá</a> <i>(†)</i></dd></dl></li></ul></li>
<li><b>Yukpa</b>:
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Japrer%C3%ADa_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Japrería language">Japrería</a> (N)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yukpa_language" title="Yukpa language">Yukpa</a> (N)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Coyaima_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Coyaima language">Coyaima</a> (N) <i>(†)</i></li></ul></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Panare_language" title="Panare language">Panare</a></b> (N)</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Opon_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Opon language">Opon</a></b> [Opón-Karare] <i>(†)</i></li></ul>

</p><p>The extinct <a href="/wiki/Patagon_language_(Peru)" class="mw-redirect" title="Patagon language (Peru)">Patagón de Perico</a> language of northern Peru also appears to have been a Cariban language, perhaps close to Carijona. <a href="/wiki/Yao_language_(Carib)" class="mw-redirect" title="Yao language (Carib)">Yao</a> is so poorly attested that Gildea believes it may never be classified.
</p>
<h3><span id="Gildea_.282012.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Gildea_(2012)">Gildea (2012)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cariban_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Gildea (2012)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of Gildea (2012), there had not yet been time to fully reclassify the Cariban languages based on the new data. The list here is therefore tentative, though an improvement over the one above; the most secure branches are listed first, and only two of the extinct languages are addressed.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Parukotoan</b>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Sikiana_language" title="Sikiana language">Katxúyana</a> (Shikuyana, <i>(†)</i> Warikyana)</li>
<li>Waiwai: <a href="/wiki/Waiwai_language" title="Waiwai language">Waiwai</a> (Wabui, Tunayana), <a href="/wiki/Hixkaryana_language" title="Hixkaryana language">Hixkaryana</a></li></ul></li>
<li><b>Pekodian</b>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Bakair%C3%AD_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Bakairí language">Bakairí</a> (apparently including <a href="/wiki/Kuikuro_language" title="Kuikuro language">Kuikuro</a>)</li>
<li>Arara: <a href="/wiki/Par%C3%A1_Ar%C3%A1ra_language" title="Pará Arára language">Arara</a> (Parirí), <a href="/wiki/Ikpeng_language" title="Ikpeng language">Ikpéng</a> (Txikão)</li></ul></li>
<li><b>Venezuelan Carib</b>
<ul><li>Pemóng–Panare
<ul><li>Pemóng: <a href="/wiki/Kap%C3%B3ng_language" title="Kapóng language">Kapóng</a> (Akawaio, Patamuna, Ingarikó), <a href="/wiki/Macushi_language" title="Macushi language">Makushi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pemon_language" title="Pemon language">Pemón</a> (Taurepang, Kamarakóto, Arekuna)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Panare_language" title="Panare language">Panare</a></li></ul></li>
<li>Mapoyo–Tamanaku
<ul><li><i>(†)</i> <a href="/wiki/Cumanagota_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Cumanagota language">Kumaná</a> (Chaima, Cumanagota)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mapoyo-Yabarana_language" title="Mapoyo-Yabarana language">Mapoyo-Yawarana</a> (Mapoyo, Wanai, Yawarana, Pémono)</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic_relations">Genetic relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cariban_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Genetic relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Cariban languages share irregular morphology with the <a href="/wiki/G%C3%AA_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Gê languages">Ge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tupi_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Tupi languages">Tupi</a> families, and Ribeiro connects them all in a <a href="/wiki/Je%E2%80%93Tupi%E2%80%93Carib" class="mw-redirect" title="Je–Tupi–Carib">Je–Tupi–Carib</a> family.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Meira, Gildea, &amp; Hoff (2010) note that likely morphemes in proto-Tupian and proto-Cariban are good candidates for being cognates, but that work so far is insufficient to make definitive statements.
</p>
