<p><b>Horbury</b> is a town<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Wakefield" title="City of Wakefield">City of Wakefield</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_district" class="mw-redirect" title="Metropolitan district">metropolitan district</a> of <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, England and part of the <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire_Urban_Area" title="West Yorkshire Urban Area">West Yorkshire Urban Area</a>. <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">Historically</a> in the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>, it is situated north of the <a href="/wiki/River_Calder,_West_Yorkshire" title="River Calder, West Yorkshire">River Calder</a> about three miles (5&#160;km) south west of <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a> and two miles (3&#160;km) to the south of <a href="/wiki/Ossett" title="Ossett">Ossett</a>. It includes the outlying areas of <b>Horbury Bridge</b> and <b>Horbury Junction</b>. At the 2001 census the Horbury and South Ossett ward of <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Metropolitan_District_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Wakefield Metropolitan District Council">Wakefield Metropolitan District Council</a> had a population of 10,002.<sup id="cite_ref-KS01_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KS01-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> At the 2011 census the population was 15,032.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  Old industries include woollens, engineering and building wagons for the railways but the area is now largely residential and retail.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponymy">Toponymy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Toponymy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The name Horbury is attested in 1086 as <i>(H)orberie</i>. It is derived from <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>horu</i> 'dirty land' and <i>burh</i> (in its dative form <i>byrig</i>), which translates as 'filthy fortification' or 'stronghold on muddy land'.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMills1998186_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMills1998186-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Other spellings include Orberie, Horbiry and Horberie.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWalker1966294_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWalker1966294-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The name possibly referred to a fortification near an old fording point of the River Calder.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Manor">Manor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Manor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The settlement predates the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> of 1086 in which Horbury and <a href="/wiki/Crigglestone" title="Crigglestone">Crigglestone</a>, on the south side of the River Calder, were the only parts of the Manor of Wakefield not described as "waste". The survey recorded about 40 people and four ox-drawn ploughs in 'Orberie' and 'Crigeston' combined. About 400 acres (1.6&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of land were in cultivation and much woodland.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWalker196643_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWalker196643-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Horbury had a church dating from about 1106 which was a daughter church of the church in Wakefield and possibly replaced an earlier <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_architecture" title="Anglo-Saxon architecture">Saxon church</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArundale195143_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArundale195143-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Norman_architecture" title="Norman architecture">Norman</a> church had a tower, <a href="/wiki/Nave" title="Nave">nave</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chancel" title="Chancel">chancel</a>.
</p><p>The Manor of Wakefield was given by the crown to the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Surrey#Earls_of_Surrey_or_Warenne" title="Earl of Surrey">Earls Warenne</a> in 1106 and was held by them until 1359.
Sir Robert de Horbiry and Sir John de Horbiry were stewards to the Earl de Warenne, who granted Sir John the village of Horbury and its lands for life.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWalker196694_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWalker196694-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  After the death of Sir John de Horbury in 1306, it became one of the constituent 'graveships' of the Manor of Wakefield.
</p><p>The oldest surviving house in the town is Horbury Hall in Church Street, built by Ralph Amyas, deputy steward of the Manor of Wakefield. It has been dated by <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">dendrochronology</a> to 1474. Other old buildings include the tithe barn.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArundale195112_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArundale195112-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Theand in Horbury was divided into three great fields, Northfield, Southfield and Westfield, and remains of <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> <a href="/wiki/Ridge_and_furrow" title="Ridge and furrow">ridge and furrow</a> of strip cultivation are visible in Carr Lodge Park.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Horbury_Bridge">Horbury Bridge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Horbury Bridge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Horbury_Bridge_over_the_River_Calder_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789103.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Horbury_Bridge_over_the_River_Calder_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789103.jpg/220px-Horbury_Bridge_over_the_River_Calder_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789103.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Horbury_Bridge_over_the_River_Calder_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789103.jpg/330px-Horbury_Bridge_over_the_River_Calder_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789103.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Horbury_Bridge_over_the_River_Calder_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789103.jpg/440px-Horbury_Bridge_over_the_River_Calder_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789103.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Horbury_Bridge_over_the_River_Calder_-_geograph.org.uk_-_789103.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bridge over the <a href="/wiki/River_Calder,_West_Yorkshire" title="River Calder, West Yorkshire">River Calder</a></div></div></div>
<p>A wooden bridge spanned the River Calder on the road from Wakefield to <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a> in the 15th century. Money for its upkeep was left in local wills dated 1404 and 1492, a custom that continued into the 16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECudworth20007_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECudworth20007-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> A stone-arched bridge that replaced the wooden structure in the 17th century lasted until it partly collapsed in 1918. A new bridge was completed in 1930 and was repaired in 1991 at a cost of £2 million.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECudworth20008_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECudworth20008-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Horbury Bridge was flooded after heavy rain in 1946.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECudworth200011_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECudworth200011-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_history">Industrial history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Industrial history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wool spinning and cloth manufacture were important originally as <a href="/wiki/Cottage_industry" class="mw-redirect" title="Cottage industry">cottage industries</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tde_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tde-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> At the start of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> steam engines were installed at Race's Mill in Dudfleet and Foster's Mill on Engine Lane in 1795.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArundale195119_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArundale195119-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Resistance to the implementation of new textile machinery and the factory system was shown when <a href="/wiki/Luddites" class="mw-redirect" title="Luddites">Luddites</a>, who blamed the new factories for depriving weavers from earning a living in a time of widespread hunger and poverty, destroyed Fosters Mill.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArundale195119_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArundale195119-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESale1996120_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESale1996120-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Albion Mills and Millfield Mills were built in the 1870s. <a href="/wiki/William_Sykes_(businessman)" title="William Sykes (businessman)">William Sykes's</a> sports goods works which became part of <a href="/wiki/Slazenger" title="Slazenger">Slazengers</a> was established at about this time.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArundale195121_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArundale195121-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Slazenger had four factories which produced sports equipment. Since the factories closed the name is preserved in Slazengers Sports and Social Club, which has facilities and floodlit grounds for many different sporting activities.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Charles_Roberts_Works.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Charles_Roberts_Works.JPG/220px-Charles_Roberts_Works.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="162" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Charles_Roberts_Works.JPG/330px-Charles_Roberts_Works.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Charles_Roberts_Works.JPG/440px-Charles_Roberts_Works.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2057" data-file-height="1515" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Charles_Roberts_Works.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Charles Roberts Works</div></div></div>
<p>Charles Roberts (1831–1892) who described himself as a joiner, moved the Buffer and Wagon Works he had established on Ings Road, Wakefield to a site at Horbury Junction in 1873. Between 1901 and 1956 the company built 110,000 railway wagons of varying types and by 1945 <a href="/wiki/Charles_Roberts_and_Co." class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Roberts and Co.">Charles Roberts and Co.</a>'s works covered 45 acres including the adjacent site of the Horbury Junction Iron Company which it had taken over in 1923. During the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a> the firm was among the first to employ women who were employed to forge shell covers.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodall197397_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodall197397-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, the wagon works was used for armament manufacture, and made 1,300 <a href="/wiki/Churchill_tanks" class="mw-redirect" title="Churchill tanks">Churchill tanks</a>, half a million naval shells and one and a half million trench mortar bombs.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodall197398_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodall197398-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The Horbury Junction Iron Company site was used to build tram bodies for Blackpool and Sheffield trams.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodall197399_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodall197399-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The works later became owned by <a href="/wiki/Procor" title="Procor">Procor</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Inc." title="Bombardier Inc.">Bombardier Inc.</a>. The last vehicles constructed at the site were <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Voyager" title="Bombardier Voyager">Bombardier Voyager</a> trains, the plant closed in 2005; the engineering company Eddison &amp; Wanless now occupies the site.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1905, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Sutcliffe_(engineer)" title="Richard Sutcliffe (engineer)">Richard Sutcliffe</a> (1849–1930), who had worked as part-time manager at Hartley Bank Colliery across the valley in <a href="/wiki/Netherton,_Wakefield" title="Netherton, Wakefield">Netherton</a>, opened his Universal Works on the site of the old dye house mill on the Horbury-Wakefield boundary in 1905 and started to manufacture conveyor belts and mining machinery. In 1972 the company employed 742 people at its Horbury site.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWoodall197395_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWoodall197395-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Historically" class="mw-redirect" title="Historically">Historically</a> Horbury was a <a href="/wiki/Chapelry" title="Chapelry">chapelry</a> in the parish of <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a>, in the lower division of the <a href="/wiki/Wapentake" class="mw-redirect" title="Wapentake">Wapentake</a> of <a href="/wiki/Agbrigg_and_Morley" title="Agbrigg and Morley">Agbrigg and Morley</a> and part of the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tde_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tde-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Following the implementation of the <a href="/wiki/Poor_Law_Amendment_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Poor Law Amendment Act">Poor Law Amendment Act</a> of 1834, Horbury became one of the 17 constituent parishes of the Wakefield <a href="/wiki/Poor_Law_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Poor Law Union">Poor Law Union</a> formed in 1837.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Horbury Urban District Council built the town hall. Its foundation stone was laid on Wednesday 30 July 1902 by Joshua Harrop. The architects and builders were Henry Fallas &amp; Sons of Horbury.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Horbury encompasses the neighbourhoods of <b>Horbury Bridge</b>, named after the crossing of the <a href="/wiki/River_Calder,_West_Yorkshire" title="River Calder, West Yorkshire">River Calder</a> and <b>Horbury Junction</b>, named after the railway junction. It covers an area of 1,162 acres (470&#160;ha).<sup id="cite_ref-tde_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tde-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The River Calder flows generally west to east in a wide valley across the south of the town alongside the <a href="/wiki/Calder_and_Hebble_Navigation" title="Calder and Hebble Navigation">Calder and Hebble Navigation</a> which made the river navigable to <a href="/wiki/Sowerby_Bridge" title="Sowerby Bridge">Sowerby Bridge</a>. The town centre is on a hill on the north side of the River Calder and most of the land slopes towards the river. The A642 Wakefield to <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a> road bypasses the town to the south of the town centre with a branch road to Horbury Junction. The <a href="/wiki/B6128_road" class="mw-redirect" title="B6128 road">B6128</a> goes through the town centre and connects with <a href="/wiki/Ossett" title="Ossett">Ossett</a> to the north. The M1 motorway passes to the east of the town.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A network of local buses, coordinated by <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire_Passenger_Transport_Executive" class="mw-redirect" title="West Yorkshire Passenger Transport Executive">West Yorkshire Passenger Transport Executive</a> (WYPTE) connects Horbury with Wakefield, Ossett, and <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>. The <a href="/wiki/M1_motorway" title="M1 motorway">M1 motorway</a> to the east of the town is accessed at junctions 39 at Durkar and 40 at Ossett.
</p><p>Proposals were made for a railway through Horbury in the 1830s and an Act of Parliament was passed for the <a href="/wiki/Manchester_and_Leeds_Railway" title="Manchester and Leeds Railway">Manchester and Leeds Railway</a> engineered by <a href="/wiki/George_Stephenson" title="George Stephenson">George Stephenson</a> in 1836.  Horbury has had three stations, <a href="/wiki/Horbury_and_Ossett_railway_station" title="Horbury and Ossett railway station"><i>Horbury and Ossett</i></a> at Horbury Bridge of 1840, <i>Horbury Junction</i> on Green Lane of 1853 and Millfield Road at Horbury Junction opened in 1927. All are closed.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECudworth20047_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECudworth20047-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Horbury Town School on Tithe Barn Street was enlarged in 1789 but it is not known when it was built. By 1870 there were 113 pupils paying fees of 3d to 6d weekly. It closed in 1886.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArundale195152_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArundale195152-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The "Gaskell School" was built on New Road in 1842 by Daniel Gaskell of Lupset Hall. It was used until 1887.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArundale195156_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArundale195156-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> St Peter's Church of England School was founded in 1849 close to the church. It was replaced by the present school in the early 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBell199820_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBell199820-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> In September 2010 St Peter's Junior School amalgamated with Clifton Infant School to form a new primary school. A new building is proposed to be built on the current St Peter's site with a completion date of September 2012.
</p><p>Horbury Council School catering for all age groups on Northfield Lane was opened in 1913 becoming Horbury County Secondary Modern in 1952. The infant school remained on the site and the junior school moved to the Wesleyan School at Horbury Junction. In 1962 secondary pupils moved to a new school. The Northfield Lane school became Horbury County Junior School and Horbury County Infant School. After a fire in 2000 and a £1m upgrade the schools amalgamated, becoming Horbury Primary School in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Horbury_Academy" title="Horbury Academy">Horbury Academy</a> (formerly Horbury School) caters for pupils aged 11 to 16 in a new building completed in 2009 on the same site as the old one.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Peter_and_St_Leonard_Horbury_-_geograph.org.uk_-_344703.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/St_Peter_and_St_Leonard_Horbury_-_geograph.org.uk_-_344703.jpg/220px-St_Peter_and_St_Leonard_Horbury_-_geograph.org.uk_-_344703.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/St_Peter_and_St_Leonard_Horbury_-_geograph.org.uk_-_344703.jpg/330px-St_Peter_and_St_Leonard_Horbury_-_geograph.org.uk_-_344703.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/St_Peter_and_St_Leonard_Horbury_-_geograph.org.uk_-_344703.jpg/440px-St_Peter_and_St_Leonard_Horbury_-_geograph.org.uk_-_344703.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Peter_and_St_Leonard_Horbury_-_geograph.org.uk_-_344703.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/St_Peter_and_St_Leonard%27s_Church,_Horbury" title="St Peter and St Leonard&#39;s Church, Horbury">St Peter and St Leonard's Church</a></div></div></div>
<p>Horbury had a <a href="/wiki/Chapel_of_ease" title="Chapel of ease">chapel of ease</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Cathedral" title="Wakefield Cathedral">Church of All Saints</a> in Wakefield, from before the time of the Domesday Book. The chapel was replaced by a <a href="/wiki/Norman_architecture" title="Norman architecture">Norman</a> chapel with a nave and tower that stood until it was replaced by the present church in 1790. <a href="/wiki/St_Peter_and_St_Leonard%27s_Church,_Horbury" title="St Peter and St Leonard&#39;s Church, Horbury">St Peter and St Leonard's Church</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Parish_church" title="Parish church">parish church</a>, was designed by <a href="/wiki/John_Carr_(architect)" title="John Carr (architect)">John Carr</a>, the Horbury born architect who built it in the Georgian neo-classical style between 1790 and 1794 at a cost to himself of £8,000. He is buried in a vault beneath the north aisle.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_John%27s_Church,_Horbury_Bridge,_Horbury_-_geograph.org.uk_-_787420.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/St_John%27s_Church%2C_Horbury_Bridge%2C_Horbury_-_geograph.org.uk_-_787420.jpg/220px-St_John%27s_Church%2C_Horbury_Bridge%2C_Horbury_-_geograph.org.uk_-_787420.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/St_John%27s_Church%2C_Horbury_Bridge%2C_Horbury_-_geograph.org.uk_-_787420.jpg/330px-St_John%27s_Church%2C_Horbury_Bridge%2C_Horbury_-_geograph.org.uk_-_787420.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/St_John%27s_Church%2C_Horbury_Bridge%2C_Horbury_-_geograph.org.uk_-_787420.jpg/440px-St_John%27s_Church%2C_Horbury_Bridge%2C_Horbury_-_geograph.org.uk_-_787420.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_John%27s_Church,_Horbury_Bridge,_Horbury_-_geograph.org.uk_-_787420.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>St John's Church, Horbury Bridge</div></div></div>
<p>The foundation of St John's Church at Horbury Bridge was in a mission meeting in a room in what is now the hairdressers in 1864. Funds were raised and the church was built with stone from Horbury Quarry in 1884. The curate, <a href="/wiki/Sabine_Baring-Gould" title="Sabine Baring-Gould">Sabine Baring-Gould</a>, wrote the hymn "<a href="/wiki/Onward_Christian_Soldiers" class="mw-redirect" title="Onward Christian Soldiers">Onward Christian Soldiers</a>" in 1865 for the Whitsun procession to Horbury Church.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBell199828_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBell199828-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Another mission was set up at Horbury Junction in 1887 and St Mary's Church was built in 1893.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBell199830_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBell199830-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Methodist" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodist">Methodist</a> society was established in Horbury in about 1746, meeting for worship in a private house in Cluntergate. In 1765 the congregation built a chapel seating perhaps 200, also in Cluntergate, adjacent to the house which later became the Working Men's Club. In 1824 a branch of Methodism called the New Connexion erected a chapel in Northgate; the Society was short-lived but the building survives to this day - as a private house. The Primitive Methodists built their first chapel in Horbury in 1841 in High Street, and four years later the Wesleyans built a chapel on the adjoining plot to replace the one in Cluntergate. The Free Methodists built a small chapel in Queen Street in 1857 which served them until 1900 when they built a larger one at the bottom of Bank Street which was bought by Nettleton's in 1958.
</p><p>The Primitive Methodists built a larger chapel in 1875 on the site of their 1841 building, and the Wesleyans similarly replaced their 1845 chapel in 1884. The Wesleyans had established a mission in Horbury Junction in 1878 and built a chapel there in 1887.
</p><p>In 1958, many years after Methodist Union, the congregations of the former Wesleyan and Primitive churches amalgamated using the 1884 Wesleyan building for worship and the Primitive's Sunday school for other activities (The congregation of the Horbury Junction chapel joined them in 1969).
</p><p>In 2012 the 1884 building, having been found to be unsafe, was demolished and a new church was constructed further back from the High Street and connected to the old Sunday School. The new building was formally opened on 10th September 2016. 
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Salvation_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="Salvation Army">Salvation Army</a> corps former headquarters on Peel Street is now disused, the congregation moving to the Leeds Road, Gawthorpe, Ossett premises. The former Tithe Barn Christian Centre, Westfield Road, is now similarly disused.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_services">Public services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Public services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Horbury Library on Westfield Road was built in 1905 with a donation from the <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Carnegie" title="Andrew Carnegie">Carnegie</a> foundation. It celebrated its centenary in 2005 with the making of a community tapestry, which now hangs in the library.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
Horbury is policed by the West Yorkshire Police force from Ossett Police Station and is within the DA, Wakefield division, which covers the whole district.<sup id="cite_ref-WYP_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WYP-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Fire_service_in_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Fire service in the United Kingdom">statutory emergency fire and rescue service</a> is provided by the <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire_Fire_and_Rescue_Service" title="West Yorkshire Fire and Rescue Service">West Yorkshire Fire and Rescue Service</a> from Wakefield Fire Station.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Hospital services are provided by the Mid Yorkshire Hospitals NHS Trust. <a href="/wiki/Mid_Yorkshire_Hospitals_NHS_Trust" class="mw-redirect" title="Mid Yorkshire Hospitals NHS Trust">Pinderfields Hospital</a> is the nearest hospital with an <a href="/wiki/Accident_and_Emergency" class="mw-redirect" title="Accident and Emergency">Accident and Emergency</a> department. Community health services, including <a href="/wiki/General_Practitioner" class="mw-redirect" title="General Practitioner">GPs</a>, district and community nurses, dentists and pharmacists, are co-ordinated by Wakefield District <a href="/wiki/Primary_Care_Trust" class="mw-redirect" title="Primary Care Trust">Primary Care Trust</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Waste_management" title="Waste management">Waste management</a> is co-ordinated by the <a href="/wiki/Local_authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Local authority">local authority</a>. Horbury's <a href="/wiki/Distribution_Network_Operator" class="mw-redirect" title="Distribution Network Operator">Distribution Network Operator</a> for electricity is <a href="/wiki/CE_Electric_UK" class="mw-redirect" title="CE Electric UK">CE Electric</a> via <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Electricity" title="Yorkshire Electricity">Yorkshire Electricity</a>. <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Water" title="Yorkshire Water">Yorkshire Water</a> manages Wakefield's <a href="/wiki/Drinking_water" title="Drinking water">drinking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Waste_water" class="mw-redirect" title="Waste water">waste water</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_people">Notable people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Horbury&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Notable people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Architect John Carr was born in Horbury in 1723. His nephew bought Sunroyd House in 1789 and renamed it "Carr Lodge".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWalker1966295_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWalker1966295-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Horbury Bridge is known as the home of <i><a href="/wiki/Onward,_Christian_Soldiers" title="Onward, Christian Soldiers">Onward, Christian Soldiers</a></i>, the hymn written by <a href="/wiki/Sabine_Baring-Gould" title="Sabine Baring-Gould">Sabine Baring-Gould</a>. <a href="/wiki/Stan_Barstow" title="Stan Barstow">Stan Barstow</a>, author of <i>Joby</i> and <i>A Kind of Loving</i>, was born in Horbury. In his autobiography, he said that Ossett and Horbury were the "border country" where the north-west of the coalfield merges with the south-east of the wool towns. <a href="/wiki/William_Baines" title="William Baines">William Baines</a>, born in 1899, was a pianist and prolific composer who died in 1922 of tuberculosis; he composed two hundred works, the majority for solo piano.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Two children from Horbury, <a href="/wiki/Deaths_of_Christianne_and_Robert_Shepherd" title="Deaths of Christianne and Robert Shepherd"> Christianne and Robert Shepherd</a>, died of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a> poisoning while on holiday in Corfu in October 2006. Their deaths resulted in changes to health and safety policies in the travel industry.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_News_2_November_2015_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_News_2_November_2015-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> A memorial garden was opened at Horbury Primary School, which they both attended.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_News_4_February_2010_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_News_4_February_2010-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
