<p>The <b>overseas departments and regions of France</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">département et régions d’outre-mer</i> or <i>DROM</i>) are <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">departments of France</a> which are outside <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_France" title="Metropolitan France">metropolitan France</a>, the European part of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>. They have nearly the same political status as metropolitan departments, although special constitutional provisions allow them greater autonomy and they are excluded from certain domestic statistics, such as the unemployment rate.
</p><p>As integral parts of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, overseas departments are represented in the <a href="/wiki/French_National_Assembly" class="mw-redirect" title="French National Assembly">National Assembly</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_Senate" class="mw-redirect" title="French Senate">Senate</a>, and <a href="/wiki/French_Economic_and_Social_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="French Economic and Social Council">Economic and Social Council</a>, vote to elect members of the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> (MEP), and also use the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a> as their currency.  
The overseas departments and regions are not the same as the <a href="/wiki/Overseas_collectivities" class="mw-redirect" title="Overseas collectivities">overseas collectivities</a>, which have a semi-autonomous status.
</p><p>Each overseas department is the sole department in its own <b>overseas region</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">région d'outre-mer</i>) with powers identical to the <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_France" title="Regions of France">regions</a> of metropolitan France. Because of the one-to-one correspondence, informal usage does not distinguish the two, and the French media uses the term <i lang="fr">département d’outre-mer</i> (<i>DOM</i>) almost exclusively.
</p><p>Since March 2011, the five overseas departments and regions of France are:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a> in the Caribbean;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mayotte" title="Mayotte">Mayotte</a> and <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">Réunion</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a>, off the coast of Africa.</li></ul>
<p>Guadeloupe and Réunion each have separate departmental and regional councils, while in Mayotte, Guiana and Martinique, the two layers of government are <a href="/wiki/Single_territorial_collectivity" title="Single territorial collectivity">consolidated</a> so one body wields both sets of powers. The overseas departments acquired these additional powers in 1982, when France's <a href="/wiki/Decentralization" title="Decentralization">decentralisation</a> policy dictated that they be given elected <a href="/wiki/Regional_council_(France)" title="Regional council (France)">regional councils</a> and other regional powers, however the term "overseas region" was only introduced with the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_amendments_under_the_Fifth_French_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="Constitutional amendments under the Fifth French Republic">French constitutional amendment</a> of 28 March 2003.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Overseas_department_and_region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>'s earliest, short-lived attempt at setting up overseas departments was after <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_the_Republic_of_Venice" title="Fall of the Republic of Venice">conquest</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">Republic of Venice</a> in 1797, when the hitherto Venetian <a href="/wiki/Ionian_islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Ionian islands">Ionian islands</a> fell to the <a href="/wiki/French_Directory" title="French Directory">French Directory</a> and were organised as the departments of <i><a href="/wiki/Mer-%C3%89g%C3%A9e" title="Mer-Égée">Mer-Égée</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Ithaque" title="Ithaque">Ithaque</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Corcyre" title="Corcyre">Corcyre</a></i>. In 1798 the Russian Admiral <a href="/wiki/Fyodor_Ushakov" title="Fyodor Ushakov">Fyodor Ushakov</a> evicted the French from these islands, and though France <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Tilsit" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Tilsit">regained them</a> in 1807, the three departments were not revived.
</p><p>Under the 1947 <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_France" title="Constitution of France">Constitution</a> of the <a href="/wiki/French_Fourth_Republic" title="French Fourth Republic">Fourth Republic</a>, the <a href="/wiki/French_colonial_empire" title="French colonial empire">French colonies</a> of <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">Algeria</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, and <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">Réunion</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">Indian Ocean</a> were defined as overseas departments. Algeria became independent in 1962 while the others are still French departments.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:EU_OCT_and_OMR_map_en.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/EU_OCT_and_OMR_map_en.png/220px-EU_OCT_and_OMR_map_en.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="135" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/EU_OCT_and_OMR_map_en.png/330px-EU_OCT_and_OMR_map_en.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/EU_OCT_and_OMR_map_en.png/440px-EU_OCT_and_OMR_map_en.png 2x" data-file-width="2206" data-file-height="1358" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:EU_OCT_and_OMR_map_en.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of the European Union in the world with <a href="/wiki/Special_member_state_territories_and_the_European_Union" title="Special member state territories and the European Union">overseas countries and territories and outermost regions</a></div></div></div>
<p>Since 1982, following the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Cabinet of France">French government</a>'s policy of <a href="/wiki/Decentralisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Decentralisation">decentralisation</a>, overseas departments have elected regional councils with powers similar to those of the <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_France" title="Regions of France">regions</a> of metropolitan France. As a result of a constitutional revision that occurred in 2003, these regions are now to be called "overseas regions"; indeed the new wording of the Constitution gives no precedence to the terms overseas department or overseas region, though the latter is still virtually unused by the French media.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Overseas_collectivity" title="Overseas collectivity">overseas collectivity</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Pierre_and_Miquelon" title="Saint Pierre and Miquelon">Saint Pierre and Miquelon</a> was an overseas department from 1976 to 1985. All five of France's overseas departments have between 200,000 and a million people each, whereas Saint Pierre and Miquelon has only about 6,000, and the smaller collectivity unit therefore seemed more appropriate for the islands.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Following a yes vote in a <a href="/wiki/2009_Mahoran_status_referendum" title="2009 Mahoran status referendum">referendum</a> held on 29 March 2009, the former overseas colectivity of <a href="/wiki/Mayotte" title="Mayotte">Mayotte</a> became an overseas department on 31 March 2011.
