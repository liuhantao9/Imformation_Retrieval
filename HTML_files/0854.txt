<p>The <b>state funeral of John F. Kennedy</b>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_Presidents_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Presidents of the United States">35th</a> <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a>, took place in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, during the three days that followed <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="Assassination of John F. Kennedy">his assassination</a> on Friday, November 22, 1963, in <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The body of <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">President Kennedy</a> was brought back to Washington soon after his death and was placed in the <a href="/wiki/East_Room" title="East Room">East Room of the White House</a> for 24 hours.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EastRoom_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EastRoom-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> On the Sunday after <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="Assassination of John F. Kennedy">the assassination</a>, his flag-draped <a href="/wiki/Coffin" title="Coffin">coffin</a> was carried on a horse-drawn <a href="/wiki/Caisson_(military)" class="mw-redirect" title="Caisson (military)">caisson</a> to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">U.S. Capitol</a> to <a href="/wiki/Lying_in_state" title="Lying in state">lie in state</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Throughout the day and night, hundreds of thousands lined up to view the guarded casket.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Representatives from over 90 countries attended the <a href="/wiki/State_funeral" title="State funeral">state funeral</a> on Monday, November 25.<sup id="cite_ref-Funeral_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Funeral-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> After the <a href="/wiki/Requiem_Mass" class="mw-redirect" title="Requiem Mass">Requiem Mass</a> at <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_St._Matthew_the_Apostle" class="mw-redirect" title="Cathedral of St. Matthew the Apostle">St. Matthew's Cathedral</a>, the late president was buried at <a href="/wiki/Arlington_National_Cemetery" title="Arlington National Cemetery">Arlington National Cemetery</a> in Virginia.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Preparations_for_the_state_funeral">Preparations for the state funeral</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_funeral_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Preparations for the state funeral">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> was assassinated in Dallas, his body was flown back to <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and taken to <a href="/wiki/National_Naval_Medical_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="National Naval Medical Center">Bethesda Naval Hospital</a> for the autopsy.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> At the same time, military authorities began making arrangements for a <a href="/wiki/State_funeral" title="State funeral">state funeral</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Army <a href="/wiki/Major_general_(United_States)" title="Major general (United States)">Major General</a> <a href="/wiki/Philip_C._Wehle" title="Philip C. Wehle">Philip C. Wehle</a>, the commanding general of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Military_District_of_Washington" title="United States Army Military District of Washington">Military District of Washington</a> (MDW) (CG MDW), and retired Army Colonel Paul C. Miller, chief of ceremonies and special events at the MDW, planned the funeral.<sup id="cite_ref-Planning_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Planning-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>They headed to the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> and worked with the president's brother-in-law, <a href="/wiki/Sargent_Shriver" title="Sargent Shriver">Sargent Shriver</a>, also director of the Peace Corps, and Ralph Dungan, an aide to the president.<sup id="cite_ref-Planning_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Planning-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Because Kennedy had no funeral plan in place, much of the planning rested with the CG MDW.<sup id="cite_ref-Planning_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Planning-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="Speaker of the United States House of Representatives">House Speaker</a> <a href="/wiki/John_W._McCormack" title="John W. McCormack">John W. McCormack</a> said that the president's body would be brought back to the White House to lie in the East Room the following day and then taken to the Capitol to lie in state in the rotunda all day Sunday.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The day after the assassination, the new president, <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a>, issued <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Proclamation_3561" class="extiw" title="s:Proclamation 3561">Presidential Proclamation 3561</a>, declaring Monday to be a <a href="/wiki/National_day_of_mourning" title="National day of mourning">national day of mourning</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-AssociatedPress-Page40_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AssociatedPress-Page40-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and only essential emergency workers to be at their posts.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> He read the proclamation over a nationwide radio and television broadcast at 4:45&#160;p.m. from the <a href="/wiki/Roosevelt_Room" title="Roosevelt Room">Fish Room</a> (currently known as the Roosevelt Room) at the White House.<sup id="cite_ref-AssociatedPress-Page40_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AssociatedPress-Page40-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several elements of the state funeral paid tribute to Kennedy's service in the Navy during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Navy_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Navy-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> They included a member of the Navy bearing the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_President_of_the_United_States" title="Flag of the President of the United States">presidential flag</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Navy_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Navy-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> the playing of the Navy Hymn, "<a href="/wiki/Eternal_Father,_Strong_to_Save" title="Eternal Father, Strong to Save">Eternal Father, Strong to Save</a>," and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Academy" title="United States Naval Academy">Naval Academy</a> Glee Club performing at the White House.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The hymn " Oh God of Loveliness" was played as the casket was brought down the Capitol steps; "The Barren Rocks of Aden" as it was brought to the White House, and "<a href="/wiki/Ave_Maria" class="mw-redirect" title="Ave Maria">Ave Maria</a>" when it arrived at St. Matthew's Cathedral.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="White_House_repose">White House repose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_funeral_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: White House repose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JFKeastRoomnov23%2763.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/JFKeastRoomnov23%2763.jpg/250px-JFKeastRoomnov23%2763.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="196" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/JFKeastRoomnov23%2763.jpg/375px-JFKeastRoomnov23%2763.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/JFKeastRoomnov23%2763.jpg/500px-JFKeastRoomnov23%2763.jpg 2x" data-file-width="647" data-file-height="506" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JFKeastRoomnov23%2763.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">United States President</a> <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> <a href="/wiki/Lying_in_repose" title="Lying in repose">lies in repose</a> in the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> <a href="/wiki/East_Room" title="East Room">East Room</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>After the autopsy at <a href="/wiki/National_Naval_Medical_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="National Naval Medical Center">Bethesda Naval Hospital</a>, Kennedy's body was prepared for burial by embalmers from Gawler's Funeral Home in Washington, who performed the embalming and cosmetic restoration procedures at Bethesda.<sup id="cite_ref-AssociatedPress-Page31_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AssociatedPress-Page31-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WhiteHouse_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WhiteHouse-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Mossman&amp;Stark-Page_190_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mossman&amp;Stark-Page_190-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Then, Kennedy's body was put in a new mahogany casket in place of the bronze casket used to transport the body from Dallas. The bronze casket had been damaged in transit, and was later disposed of by the Air Force in the Atlantic Ocean so that it would not "fall into the hands of sensation seekers".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>President Kennedy's body was returned to the White House at about 4:30&#160;a.m. on Saturday, November 23.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The motorcade bearing the remains was met at the White House gate by a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">U.S. Marine Corps</a> <a href="/wiki/Honor_guard" class="mw-redirect" title="Honor guard">honor guard</a>, which escorted it to the North Portico. The pallbearers bore the casket to the <a href="/wiki/East_Room" title="East Room">East Room</a> where, 18 years earlier, the body of <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and nearly one hundred years earlier, the body of <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a> had lain.<sup id="cite_ref-Mossman&amp;Stark-Page_190_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mossman&amp;Stark-Page_190-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy's casket was placed on a <a href="/wiki/Catafalque" title="Catafalque">catafalque</a> previously used for the funerals of the <a href="/wiki/Tomb_of_the_Unknowns" class="mw-redirect" title="Tomb of the Unknowns">Unknown Soldiers</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> at Arlington.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Initially, the casket was to be open for public viewing, but <a href="/wiki/Jacqueline_Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacqueline Kennedy">Jacqueline Kennedy</a> declared that the casket would be kept closed for the viewing and funeral. The shot to Kennedy's head left a gaping wound,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and religious leaders said that a closed casket minimized morbid concentration on the body.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mrs. Kennedy, still wearing the blood-stained raspberry-colored suit she wore in Dallas, had not left the side of her husband's body since his death.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Only after the casket was placed in the East Room, draped with black crepe,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> did she retire to her private quarters.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kennedy's body lay in repose in the East Room for 24 hours,<sup id="cite_ref-EastRoom_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EastRoom-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> attended by an honor guard including troops from the <a href="/wiki/3rd_US_Infantry_Regiment_(The_Old_Guard)" class="mw-redirect" title="3rd US Infantry Regiment (The Old Guard)">3rd Infantry</a> and from the <a href="/wiki/Special_Forces_(United_States_Army)" class="mw-redirect" title="Special Forces (United States Army)">Army's Special Forces</a> (Green Berets).<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GreenBerets_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GreenBerets-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The Special Forces troops had been brought hurriedly from <a href="/wiki/Fort_Bragg" title="Fort Bragg">Fort Bragg</a> in North Carolina, at the request of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Attorney_General" title="United States Attorney General">U.S. Attorney General</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a>, who was aware of his brother's particular interest in them.<sup id="cite_ref-GreenBerets_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GreenBerets-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mrs. Kennedy requested two Catholic priests to remain with the body until the official funeral. A call was made to <a href="/wiki/The_Catholic_University_of_America" class="mw-redirect" title="The Catholic University of America">The Catholic University of America</a>, and Msgr. Robert Paul Mohan and <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_V._Hartke" title="Gilbert V. Hartke">Fr. Gilbert Hartke</a>, two prominent Washington, D.C., priests, were immediately dispatched for the task.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>  A solemn <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">Mass</a> was celebrated for family by Fr. M. Frank Ruppert of St. Matthew's Cathedral Parish in the East Room at 10:30&#160;a.m. on Saturday, November 23.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> After the Mass, other family members, friends, and other government officials came at specified times to pay their respects to President Kennedy.<sup id="cite_ref-Respects_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Respects-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> This included former U.S. Presidents <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">Truman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Eisenhower</a>. The other surviving former U.S. president at the time, <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Hoover" title="Herbert Hoover">Herbert Hoover</a>, was too ill to attend the state funeral,<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> and was represented by his sons, <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Hoover_Jr." title="Herbert Hoover Jr.">Herbert Hoover Jr.</a>, who also attended the funeral, and <a href="/wiki/Allan_Hoover" title="Allan Hoover">Allan Hoover</a>, who went to the services in the rotunda.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/President%27s_Park" title="President&#39;s Park">Lafayette Park</a>, across the street from the White House, crowds stood in the rain, keeping a vigil and paying quiet respects.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> It rained all day in Washington, befitting the mood of the nation.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lying_in_state">Lying in state</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_funeral_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lying in state">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JFK_casket_in_Capitol_rotunda,_1963.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/JFK_casket_in_Capitol_rotunda%2C_1963.png/250px-JFK_casket_in_Capitol_rotunda%2C_1963.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="201" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/JFK_casket_in_Capitol_rotunda%2C_1963.png/375px-JFK_casket_in_Capitol_rotunda%2C_1963.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/JFK_casket_in_Capitol_rotunda%2C_1963.png/500px-JFK_casket_in_Capitol_rotunda%2C_1963.png 2x" data-file-width="2962" data-file-height="2378" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JFK_casket_in_Capitol_rotunda,_1963.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">United States President</a> <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a> placing a wreath before the flag-draped casket of <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">President Kennedy</a>, during funeral services held in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol_Rotunda" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Capitol Rotunda">United States Capitol Rotunda</a>, November 24, 1963.</div></div></div>
<p>On Sunday afternoon about 300,000 people watched a <a href="/wiki/Limbers_and_caissons_(military)" class="mw-redirect" title="Limbers and caissons (military)">horse-drawn caisson</a>, which had borne the body of <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Tomb_of_the_Unknowns" class="mw-redirect" title="Tomb of the Unknowns">Unknown Soldier</a>, carry Kennedy's flag-covered casket down the White House drive, past parallel rows of soldiers bearing the flags of the 50 states of the Union, then along <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Avenue_(Washington,_D.C.)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pennsylvania Avenue (Washington, D.C.)">Pennsylvania Avenue</a> to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">Capitol</a> Rotunda to lie in state.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The only sounds on Pennsylvania Avenue as the cortège made its way to the Capitol were the sounds of the muffled drums and the clacking of horses' hooves, including the <a href="/wiki/Riderless_horse" title="Riderless horse">riderless (caparisoned) horse</a> <a href="/wiki/Black_Jack_(horse)" title="Black Jack (horse)">Black Jack</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Throngs_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Throngs-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The widow, holding her two children by the hand, led the public mourning for the country.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Widow_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Widow-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> In the rotunda, Mrs. Kennedy and her daughter <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Kennedy" title="Caroline Kennedy">Caroline</a> knelt beside the casket, which rested on the <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_catafalque" class="mw-redirect" title="Lincoln catafalque">Lincoln catafalque</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Three-year-old <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_Jr." title="John F. Kennedy Jr.">John Jr.</a> was briefly taken out of the rotunda so as not to disrupt the service.<sup id="cite_ref-Widow_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Widow-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Mrs. Kennedy maintained her composure as her husband was taken to the Capitol to lie in state, as well as during the memorial service.<sup id="cite_ref-Widow_55-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Widow-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Brief eulogies were delivered inside the rotunda by Senate Majority Leader <a href="/wiki/Mike_Mansfield" title="Mike Mansfield">Mike Mansfield</a> of <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">Montana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chief_Justice_of_the_United_States" title="Chief Justice of the United States">Chief Justice</a> <a href="/wiki/Earl_Warren" title="Earl Warren">Earl Warren</a>, and Speaker McCormack.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kennedy was the first president in more than 30 years to lie in state in the rotunda,<sup id="cite_ref-PresidentsLyingInState_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PresidentsLyingInState-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> and the first Democrat to lie in state at the Capitol.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_viewing">Public viewing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_funeral_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Public viewing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the only public viewing, thousands lined up in near-freezing temperatures to view the casket.<sup id="cite_ref-Rotunda_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rotunda-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Over the span of 18 hours, 250,000 people, some waiting for as long as 10 hours in a line up to 10 persons wide that stretched 40 blocks,<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> personally paid their respects as Kennedy's body lay in state.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol_Police" title="United States Capitol Police">United States Capitol Police</a> officers politely reminded mourners to keep moving along in two lines that passed on either side of the casket and exited the building on the west side facing the National Mall.<sup id="cite_ref-Throngs_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Throngs-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rotunda_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rotunda-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The original plan was for the rotunda to close at 9:00&#160;p.m. and reopen for an hour at 9:00 the next morning.<sup id="cite_ref-AssociatedPressPage91_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AssociatedPressPage91-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Because of long lines police and military authorities decided to keep the doors open.<sup id="cite_ref-AssociatedPressPage91_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AssociatedPressPage91-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> At 9:00&#160;p.m., when the rotunda was supposed to close, both Jacqueline Kennedy and Robert F. Kennedy returned to the rotunda again.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> More than half the mourners came to the rotunda after 2:45&#160;a.m., by which time 115,000 had already visited.<sup id="cite_ref-Throngs_53-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Throngs-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Military officials doubled the lines, first to two abreast, then to four abreast.<sup id="cite_ref-Rotunda_60-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rotunda-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/NBC_News" title="NBC News">NBC</a> broadcast uninterrupted coverage of the people passing through the Capitol rotunda during the overnight hours.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> While anchoring the <i><a href="/wiki/Today_(NBC_program)" class="mw-redirect" title="Today (NBC program)">Today</a></i> show from an NBC Washington studio the next day, <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Downs" title="Hugh Downs">Hugh Downs</a> said that the numbers made it "the greatest and most solemn wake in history."<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/CBS_News" title="CBS News">CBS</a> Washington correspondent <a href="/wiki/Roger_Mudd" title="Roger Mudd">Roger Mudd</a> said of the numbers: "This outpouring of affection and sympathy for the late president is probably the most majestic and stately ceremony the American people can perform."<sup id="cite_ref-Stately_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stately-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jersey_Joe_Walcott" title="Jersey Joe Walcott">Jersey Joe Walcott</a>, a former heavyweight boxing champion, passed by the bier at 2:30&#160;a.m.<sup id="cite_ref-Stately_69-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stately-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> and agreed with Mudd, saying of Kennedy, "He was a great man."<sup id="cite_ref-FinalRest_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FinalRest-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arrival_of_dignitaries">Arrival of dignitaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_funeral_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Arrival of dignitaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JFK_funeral_dignitaries.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/JFK_funeral_dignitaries.png/350px-JFK_funeral_dignitaries.png" decoding="async" width="350" height="162" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/JFK_funeral_dignitaries.png/525px-JFK_funeral_dignitaries.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/JFK_funeral_dignitaries.png/700px-JFK_funeral_dignitaries.png 2x" data-file-width="1357" data-file-height="628" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JFK_funeral_dignitaries.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nations that attended the funeral (blue) or whose dignitaries arrived too late (pink), but attended <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a>'s reception on November 25.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_dignitaries_at_the_state_funeral_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="List of dignitaries at the state funeral of John F. Kennedy">List of dignitaries at the state funeral of John F. Kennedy</a></div>
<p>As Kennedy lay in state, foreign dignitaries—including heads of state and government and members of royal families—started to arrive in Washington to attend the state funeral on Monday.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State" title="United States Secretary of State">Secretary of State</a> <a href="/wiki/Dean_Rusk" title="Dean Rusk">Dean Rusk</a> and other <a href="/wiki/State_Department" class="mw-redirect" title="State Department">State Department</a> personnel went to both of Washington's commercial airports to personally greet foreign dignitaries.<sup id="cite_ref-WorldLeaders_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WorldLeaders-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some of the dignitaries that arrived on Sunday to attend the funeral included <a href="/wiki/List_of_First_Deputies_of_the_Soviet_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="List of First Deputies of the Soviet Union">Soviet First Deputy Premier</a> <a href="/wiki/Anastas_Mikoyan" title="Anastas Mikoyan">Anastas Mikoyan</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/President_of_France" title="President of France">French President</a> <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle" title="Charles de Gaulle">Charles de Gaulle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Canada" title="Prime Minister of Canada">Canadian Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Lester_B._Pearson" title="Lester B. Pearson">Lester B. Pearson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Philip,_Duke_of_Edinburgh" title="Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh">the Duke of Edinburgh</a> (representing Queen <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a>), <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">British Prime Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Sir_Alec_Douglas-Home" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Alec Douglas-Home">Sir Alec Douglas-Home</a>, <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">Irish President</a> <a href="/wiki/%C3%89amon_de_Valera" title="Éamon de Valera">Éamon de Valera</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Ethiopia" title="Emperor of Ethiopia">Ethiopian Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Haile_Selassie" title="Haile Selassie">Haile Selassie</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Frederica_of_Hanover" title="Frederica of Hanover">Queen Frederica of the Hellenes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/King_Baudouin_I" class="mw-redirect" title="King Baudouin I">King Baudouin I</a> of the Belgians were just some of the other members of royalty attending. Some law enforcement officials, including <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Police_Department_of_the_District_of_Columbia" title="Metropolitan Police Department of the District of Columbia">MPDC</a> Chief Robert V. Murray, later said that it was the biggest security nightmare they ever faced.<sup id="cite_ref-WorldLeaders_72-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WorldLeaders-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Funeral">Funeral</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_funeral_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Funeral">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As people were viewing the casket, military authorities held meetings at the White House, at MDW headquarters, and at <a href="/wiki/Arlington_National_Cemetery" title="Arlington National Cemetery">Arlington National Cemetery</a> to plan Monday's events.<sup id="cite_ref-Meetings_78-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meetings-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> First, they decided that the public viewing should end at 9:00&#160;a.m. EST and that the ceremonies would begin at 10:30&#160;a.m. EST.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MossmanAndStark-Page205_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MossmanAndStark-Page205-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>  On Sunday evening the State Department issued its Official Narrative of Events for November 25:
</p>
<dl><dd>'AT THE WHITE HOUSE<i></i></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>1.  Approximately 50 minutes after leaving from the front of the Rotunda, the Marine element of march, which directly precedes the Joint Chiefs of Staff and the Caisson, will have arrived on Pennsylvania Avenue directly opposite of the Northeast gate of the White House.  At that point, one platoon of the Marine company will detach itself from the main body and enter the Northeast gate and will be followed by all elements in their rear, i.e., the Joints Chiefs of Staff, the Personal Flag, the Caparisoned Horse and the car bearing Mrs. Kennedy and the Attorney General.</i></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>2.  The column that has entered the White House grounds will proceed on the drive, NOT passing under, but keeping to the right of the portico, until the head of the platoon of Marines reaches the Northwest gate, which is on Pennsylvania Avenue.  There it will be halted.  Mrs. Kennedy's car will pull under the portico roof and halt at the front steps of the White House, at which point she will dismount with the Attorney General.  Upon her leaving the car, it should be backed up 10 or 15 yards.</i></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>3.  It is presumed Mrs. Kennedy will mount the steps with the Attorney General and be met by members of the Presidential staff.</i></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>4.  The entire driveway in the White House grounds will be cordoned by Navy enlisted men, bearing State and Territorial Flags.  They will render the appropriate honors upon the passage of the Caisson.</i></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>5. It is presumed that the Presidential Military Aides will remain in column behind the Joint Chiefs of Staff.  If the Aides decide they wish to enter the White House, it is their responsibility to position themselves back in the column prior to Mrs. Kennedy's leaving the White House to follow the Caisson on foot.</i></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>6.  It is assumed that the foreign dignitaries have been formed in the proper column inside of the White House, extending from the East Room down the hall to the North Entrance.  When Mrs. Kennedy mounts the steps and has been met by the Presidential staff, she will be told by a member of that staff when the entire group within the White House is ready to move off. Mrs.  Kennedy is then free to move down the steps and to the left along the drive towards the Northwest gate on foot.</i></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>7.  On descending to the driveway Mrs. Kennedy will find the Caisson followed by the Caparisoned Horse some 20 - 30 yards to the left and on the driveway.  As she approaches the Caisson, the Marine platoon, halted at the Northeast gate, will move the column off so that Mrs.  Kennedy will not have to halt behind the Caparisoned Horse.  The normal distance for marching behind the Caparison Horse is approximately 10 to 15 yards.  The dignitaries, already formed at the White House, will move out immediately behind Mrs. Kennedy.  It is presumed that Mrs. Kennedy will be escorted by her brother-in-law, the Attorney General.  It is further presumed that the other mourners and dignitaries on foot will keep about a five (5) yard distance in rear of Mrs. Kennedy and her escort.</i></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>8.  The mourners behind Mrs. Kennedy and her escort should maintain a five or six man front.</i></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>9.  As the Marine Platoon moves out of the Northwest gate the Black Watch will move in following the Marine Platoon and immediately in front of the Joints Chiefs of Staff.</i></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>10.  The Naval Academy Choir, positioned on the lawn opposite the North portico, will begin to sing as the Caisson enters the Northeast gate.  They will sing three hymns only.</i><sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>Unlike Sunday's procession, which was led by only the muffled drum corps,<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> Monday's was expanded to include other military units.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MilitaryUnits_85-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MilitaryUnits-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> Military officials also agreed to requests from Kennedy's widow, Jacqueline Kennedy.<sup id="cite_ref-Meetings_78-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meetings-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> They agreed that the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Band" title="United States Marine Band">Marine Band</a> should lead the <a href="/wiki/Funeral_procession" title="Funeral procession">funeral procession</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Meetings_78-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meetings-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> which would include two foreign military units&#8212;10 pipers from the <a href="/wiki/Black_Watch" title="Black Watch">Scottish Black Watch</a> (Royal Highland Regiment) marching from the White House to <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_St._Matthew_the_Apostle" class="mw-redirect" title="Cathedral of St. Matthew the Apostle">St. Matthew's Roman Catholic Cathedral</a>, a group of 26 <a href="/wiki/Defence_Forces_(Ireland)" title="Defence Forces (Ireland)">Irish Defence Forces</a> cadets&#8212;at the request of Mrs. Kennedy&#8212;performing silent drill at the grave site, and placement of an eternal flame at the grave.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> The cadets came from the  <a href="/wiki/Curragh_Camp" title="Curragh Camp">Curragh Camp</a>, County Kildare, Republic of Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-MilitaryUnits_85-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MilitaryUnits-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> The cadets traveled with Irish President Éamon de Valera, and together they paid tribute to Kennedy's Irish ancestry.<sup id="cite_ref-MilitaryUnits_85-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MilitaryUnits-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Approximately one million people lined the route of the funeral procession, from the Capitol back to the White House, then to St. Matthew's Cathedral, and finally to Arlington National Cemetery.<sup id="cite_ref-Millions_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Millions-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Silence_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Silence-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> Millions more followed the funeral on television.<sup id="cite_ref-Millions_88-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Millions-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> Those who watched the funeral on television were the only ones who saw the ceremony in its entirety.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> The three networks, <a href="/wiki/ABC_News" title="ABC News">ABC</a>, CBS, and NBC, used at least 50 cameras for the joint coverage in order to allow viewers to follow the proceedings in their entirety from the Capitol to Arlington.<sup id="cite_ref-Cameras_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cameras-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> In addition, the networks'  Washington bureau chiefs (Bob Fleming at ABC, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Monroe_(journalist)" title="Bill Monroe (journalist)">Bill Monroe</a> at NBC, and Bill Small at CBS) moved correspondents and cameras to keep them ahead of the cortège.<sup id="cite_ref-Cameras_93-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cameras-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The day's events began at 8:25&#160;a.m.,<sup id="cite_ref-Timeline_95-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Timeline-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> when the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Police_Department_of_the_District_of_Columbia" title="Metropolitan Police Department of the District of Columbia">MPDC</a> cut off the line of mourners waiting to get into the rotunda. They did so because a large group tried to break into the line and the MPDC were not able to sort out those who had already been in line, many of whom had waited for five hours.<sup id="cite_ref-Rotunda_60-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rotunda-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup> Thirty-five minutes, later, the doors closed, ending the lying in state; the last visitors passed through at 9:05&#160;a.m.<sup id="cite_ref-Timeline_95-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Timeline-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At 10:00&#160;a.m., both houses of Congress met to pass resolutions expressing sorrow.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senate</a>, Maine Republican Senator <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Chase_Smith" title="Margaret Chase Smith">Margaret Chase Smith</a> laid a single rose on the desk Kennedy had occupied when in the Senate.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Earlier that morning the State Department also issued the following directive:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>Funeral Services of John Fitzgerald Kennedy, late President of the United States – 25 November 1963</i></dd>
<dd><i>Procession departs the Capitol at 11:00am</i></dd>
<dd><i>Mrs. Kennedy and the Attorney General in an automobile will join the military formation to proceed to the White House.</i></dd>
<dd><i>Members of Congress who will participate in the funeral ceremonies (except "The Leadership") proceed directly to St. Matthews Cathedral to be seated by 11:45am.</i></dd>
<dd><i>The military formation will proceed down Pennsylvania Avenue past the White House and pause in the intersection of 17th and Pennsylvania.  In the meantime, the caisson will have entered the North Gate and proceed to the North Portico followed by Mrs. Kennedy and the Attorney General.  They will leave the automobile at this point and be joined by other dignitaries who will proceed with them on foot behind the caisson.  The caisson will move forth and the procession will proceed to St. Matthew's via Connecticut Avenue.</i></dd>
<dd><i>The following will assemble at the White House at 11:15am preparatory to joining Mrs. Kennedy and Family on the walk from the White House to the Cathedral:</i></dd>
<dd><i>The President</i></dd>
<dd><i>Chiefs of State</i></dd>
<dd><i>Heads of Government and Chiefs of Special Delegations.</i></dd>
<dd><i>The Chief Justice</i></dd>
<dd><i>Former Presidents</i></dd>
<dd><i>Justices of the Supreme Court</i></dd>
<dd><i>Members of the Cabinet</i></dd>
<dd><i>Congressional Leadership</i></dd>
<dd><i>The Joint Chiefs of Staff</i></dd>
<dd><i>Personal Assistants to President Kennedy</i></dd>
<dd><i>Close friends</i></dd>
<dd><i>Those that are not in the procession but who have been invited to attend the requiem Mass at the Cathedral should proceed directly to the Cathedral and be in their place at approximately 11:45am.</i></dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>Upon the conclusion of the ceremonies at the Cathedral, those attending the Mass enter their cars and join the procession from the Cathedral to Arlington National Cemetery in the following order of precedence:</i></dd>
<dd><i>Mrs. Kennedy and Members of the immediate family</i></dd>
<dd><i>The President and his Party</i></dd>
<dd><i>Chiefs of State, Heads of Government and Chiefs of Special Delegations.</i></dd>
<dd><i>The Chief Justice and the Supreme Court</i></dd>
<dd><i>Members of the Cabinet</i></dd>
<dd><i>Leadership of the Senate</i></dd>
<dd><i>Governors of the States and Territories</i></dd>
<dd><i>Leadership of the House of Representatives</i></dd>
<dd><i>Joints Chiefs of Staff</i></dd>
<dd><i>Personal Staff of President Kennedy</i></dd>
<dd><i>Close Friends of the Family</i></dd>
<dd><i>Others attending the funeral mass are invited to Arlington Cemetery</i></dd>
<dd><i>The procession upon arrival at the site of interment will halt and passengers will leave their automobiles and proceed to the grave site. After the interment ceremonies, those participating will return to their cars and return to the city.</i> <sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>

Kennedy</a> seen moving down the White House drive on the way to <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_St._Matthew_the_Apostle_(Washington,_D.C.)" title="Cathedral of St. Matthew the Apostle (Washington, D.C.)">St. Matthew's Cathedral</a> on November 25, 1963. A <a href="/wiki/Color_guard" class="mw-redirect" title="Color guard">color guard</a> holding the presidential colors, the <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_President_of_the_United_States" title="Flag of the President of the United States">flag of the President of the United States</a>, and the riderless horse "Black Jack", follow behind.</div></div></div>
<p>After Jacqueline Kennedy and her brothers-in-laws, Attorney General Robert Kennedy and Massachusetts Democratic Senator <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Ted Kennedy</a>, visited the rotunda, the coffin was carried out onto the caisson.<sup id="cite_ref-MossmanAndStark-Page205_80-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MossmanAndStark-Page205-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> At 10:50, the caisson left the Capitol.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup> Ten minutes later, the procession began,<sup id="cite_ref-Timeline_95-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Timeline-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> making its way back to the White House. As the procession reached the White House, all the military units except for the Marine company turned right off Pennsylvania Avenue and onto 17th Street.<sup id="cite_ref-Mossman-Stark-Page206_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mossman-Stark-Page206-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup> A platoon of the Marine company turned in the northeast gate and led the cortege into the North Portico.<sup id="cite_ref-Mossman-Stark-Page206_102-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mossman-Stark-Page206-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the White House, the procession resumed on foot for roughly 0.9 miles (1.4&#160;km) to St. Matthew's Cathedral, led by Jacqueline Kennedy and the late president's brothers, Robert and Edward (Ted) Kennedy. They walked the same route that John F. Kennedy and Jacqueline Kennedy often used when going to Mass at the cathedral.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> This also marked the first time that a first lady walked in her husband's funeral procession.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup> The two Kennedy children rode in a limousine behind their mother and uncles.<sup id="cite_ref-KennedyChildren_105-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KennedyChildren-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup> The rest of the Kennedy family, apart from the president's father, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Kennedy,_Sr." class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph P. Kennedy, Sr.">Joseph P. Kennedy, Sr.</a>, who was ill,<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup> waited at the cathedral.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The new president <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a>, his wife <a href="/wiki/Lady_Bird_Johnson" title="Lady Bird Johnson">Lady Bird</a>, and their two daughters <a href="/wiki/Luci_Baines_Johnson" title="Luci Baines Johnson">Luci</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lynda_Bird_Johnson" class="mw-redirect" title="Lynda Bird Johnson">Lynda</a> also marched in the procession.<sup id="cite_ref-FinalRest_70-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FinalRest-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> Johnson had been advised not to do so because of the potential risk in the wake of Kennedy's assassination.<sup id="cite_ref-LetterToJohnson_108-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LetterToJohnson-108">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup> Johnson recounted his experiences in his memoirs, saying, "I remember marching behind the caisson to St. Matthew's Cathedral. The muffled rumble of drums set up a heartbreaking echo."<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Merle_Miller" title="Merle Miller">Merle Miller</a> quoted him as having said, "Walking in the procession was especially one of the most difficult decisions I had to make," but it was something he "could do, should do, would do, and did so." When he moved into the Oval Office the next day, there was a letter from Mrs. Kennedy on his desk, thanking him for walking in the procession.<sup id="cite_ref-LetterToJohnson_108-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LetterToJohnson-108">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Not since the <a href="/wiki/Funeral_of_Edward_VII" class="mw-redirect" title="Funeral of Edward VII">funeral of Britain's King Edward VII</a>, in 1910, had there been such a large gathering of presidents, prime ministers, and royalty at a state funeral.<sup id="cite_ref-NBC_News_1966_140_110-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NBC_News_1966_140-110">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup> In all, 220 foreign dignitaries, including 19 heads of state and government, and members of royal families from 92 countries, five international agencies, and the papacy attended the funeral.<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">&#91;112&#93;</a></sup> Most of the dignitaries passed unnoticed, following respectfully behind the former first lady and the Kennedy family during the relatively short walk to the cathedral along Connecticut Avenue.<sup id="cite_ref-Funeral_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Funeral-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As the dignitaries marched, there was a heavy security presence because of concerns for the potential assassination of so many world leaders,<sup id="cite_ref-113" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-113">&#91;113&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-114" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-114">&#91;114&#93;</a></sup> the greatest being for French President <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle" title="Charles de Gaulle">Charles de Gaulle</a>, who had specific threats against his life.<sup id="cite_ref-deGaulle_115-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deGaulle-115">&#91;115&#93;</a></sup> Under Secretary of State <a href="/wiki/George_Wildman_Ball" class="mw-redirect" title="George Wildman Ball">George Ball</a> manned the operations center at the <a href="/wiki/State_Department" class="mw-redirect" title="State Department">State Department</a> with the goal of ensuring that no incident occurred.<sup id="cite_ref-116" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-116">&#91;116&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>NBC transmitted coverage of the procession from the White House to the cathedral by satellite to twenty-three countries, including Japan and the Soviet Union,<sup id="cite_ref-Cameras_93-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cameras-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> allowing hundreds of millions on both sides of the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Curtain" title="Iron Curtain">Iron Curtain</a> in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> to watch the funeral.<sup id="cite_ref-117" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-117">&#91;117&#93;</a></sup> Satellite coverage ended when the coffin went into the cathedral.<sup id="cite_ref-Telstar_118-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telstar-118">&#91;118&#93;</a></sup> In the Soviet Union, their commentators said that "the grief of the Soviet people mingles with the grief of the American people."<sup id="cite_ref-Telstar_118-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telstar-118">&#91;118&#93;</a></sup> There was no coverage in East Germany, where television audiences had only a soccer match to watch.<sup id="cite_ref-Telstar_118-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Telstar-118">&#91;118&#93;</a></sup> In Ireland, coverage of the funeral was broadcast live by the television service, Teilifís Éireann to the Irish audience via Telstar satellite. The Irish audience were only able to see the 25 minutes that showed President Kennedy's coffin being brought to St. Matthew's Cathedral. The Irish television audiences also did not see all of the footage live but Michael O'Hehir's audio commentary remained available to them throughout. In Britain, coverage of the funeral was broadcast by both B.B.C and I.T.V who also screened the funeral live via Telstar, BBC's leading news commentator, Richard Dimbleby gave live commentary of the funeral on B.B.C, while ITN journalist and broadcaster, Alastair Burnet gave live commentary of the funeral on I.T.V.
</p><p>The widow, wearing a black veil, led the way up the steps of the cathedral holding the hands of her two children, with <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="John F. Kennedy, Jr.">John Jr.</a>, whose third birthday fell on the day of his father's funeral,<sup id="cite_ref-KennedyChildren_105-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KennedyChildren-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup> on her left, and <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Kennedy" title="Caroline Kennedy">Caroline</a> on her right.<sup id="cite_ref-NBC_News_1966_140_110-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NBC_News_1966_140-110">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup> Because of the funeral and the day of mourning, the widow postponed John Jr.'s birthday party until December 5, the last day the family was in the White House.<sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119">&#91;119&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Funeral_Mass_at_cathedral">Funeral Mass at cathedral</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_funeral_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Funeral Mass at cathedral">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>About 1,200 invited guests attended the funeral Mass in the cathedral.<sup id="cite_ref-Martyred_120-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martyred-120">&#91;120&#93;</a></sup> The Archbishop of Boston, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Cushing" title="Richard Cushing">Richard Cardinal Cushing</a>, celebrated the funeral Mass at the cathedral where Kennedy, a practicing Catholic, often worshipped.<sup id="cite_ref-121" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-121">&#91;121&#93;</a></sup> Cardinal Cushing was a close friend of the family who had witnessed and blessed the marriage of Senator Kennedy and Jacqueline Bouvier in 1953.<sup id="cite_ref-AssociatedPress-Page94_122-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AssociatedPress-Page94-122">&#91;122&#93;</a></sup> He had also baptized two of their children, given the invocation at President Kennedy's inauguration, and officiated at the recent funeral of their infant son, <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Bouvier_Kennedy" title="Patrick Bouvier Kennedy">Patrick Bouvier Kennedy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-AssociatedPress-Page94_122-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AssociatedPress-Page94-122">&#91;122&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the request of the First Lady, the Requiem Mass was a <a href="/wiki/Pontifical_Mass" class="mw-redirect" title="Pontifical Mass">Pontifical</a> <a href="/wiki/Requiem" title="Requiem">Requiem</a> <a href="/wiki/Low_Mass" title="Low Mass">Low Mass</a><sup id="cite_ref-NBCNews-Page126_123-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NBCNews-Page126-123">&#91;123&#93;</a></sup>—that is, a simplified version of the Mass, with the Mass recited or spoken and not sung.<sup id="cite_ref-NBCNews-Page126_123-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NBCNews-Page126-123">&#91;123&#93;</a></sup> Two months later, Cardinal Cushing offered a pontifical <a href="/wiki/Solemn_Mass" title="Solemn Mass">Solemn High</a> Requiem Mass at Holy Cross Cathedral in Boston, with the city's orchestra and choir singing Mozart's Requiem setting.<sup id="cite_ref-124" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-124">&#91;124&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There was no formal eulogy at the funeral Mass.<sup id="cite_ref-Hannan_125-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hannan-125">&#91;125&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-126" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-126">&#91;126&#93;</a></sup> However, the Roman Catholic <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_Bishop" class="mw-redirect" title="Auxiliary Bishop">Auxiliary Bishop</a> of Washington, the Most Reverend <a href="/wiki/Philip_M._Hannan" class="mw-redirect" title="Philip M. Hannan">Philip M. Hannan</a>, decided to read selections from Kennedy's writings and speeches.<sup id="cite_ref-Hannan_125-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hannan-125">&#91;125&#93;</a></sup> Bishop Hannan had been asked to speak by Mrs. Kennedy. The readings included a passage from the third chapter of <i><a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastes" title="Ecclesiastes">Ecclesiastes</a></i>: "There is an appointed time for everything...a time to be born and a time to die...a time to love and a time to hate...a time of war and a time of peace."<sup id="cite_ref-AssociatedPress-Page94_122-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AssociatedPress-Page94-122">&#91;122&#93;</a></sup> He then concluded his remarks by reading Kennedy's entire <a href="/wiki/Inaugural_address_of_John_F._Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Inaugural address of John F. Kennedy">Inaugural Address</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-127" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-127">&#91;127&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Jacqueline Kennedy requested that Luigi Vena sing <a href="/wiki/Georges_Bizet" title="Georges Bizet">Georges Bizet</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Agnus_Dei" class="mw-redirect" title="Agnus Dei">Agnus Dei</a>", as he had at her wedding to John F. Kennedy ten years prior. Instead, he was told to sing <a href="/wiki/Pie_Jesu" title="Pie Jesu">Pie Jesu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franz_Schubert" title="Franz Schubert">Franz Schubert</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Ave_Maria_(Schubert)" title="Ave Maria (Schubert)">Ave Maria</a></i> after the <a href="/wiki/Offertory" title="Offertory">offertory</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-128" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-128">&#91;128&#93;</a></sup> For a few moments, she lost her composure and sobbed as this music filled the cathedral.<sup id="cite_ref-Funeral_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Funeral-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Burial">Burial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=State_funeral_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Burial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kennedy_salute.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/d1/Kennedy_salute.gif" decoding="async" width="180" height="249" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="180" data-file-height="249" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kennedy_salute.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="John F. Kennedy, Jr.">John F. Kennedy, Jr.</a> salutes his father's coffin while standing next to <a href="/wiki/Jacqueline_Kennedy_Onassis" title="Jacqueline Kennedy Onassis">Jacqueline Kennedy</a>, who is holding <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Kennedy" title="Caroline Kennedy">Caroline Kennedy</a>'s hand; <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Ted Kennedy</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Attorney_General" title="United States Attorney General">Attorney General</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a> are seen behind them.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_Eternal_Flame" title="John F. Kennedy Eternal Flame">John F. Kennedy Eternal Flame</a></div>
<p>The casket was borne again by caisson on the final leg to <a href="/wiki/Arlington_National_Cemetery" title="Arlington National Cemetery">Arlington National Cemetery</a> for burial.<sup id="cite_ref-AssociatedPress-Page96_129-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AssociatedPress-Page96-129">&#91;129&#93;</a></sup> Moments after the casket was carried down the front steps of the cathedral, Jacqueline Kennedy whispered to her son, after which he saluted his father's coffin;<sup id="cite_ref-130" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-130">&#91;130&#93;</a></sup> the image, taken by photographer <a href="/wiki/Stan_Stearns" title="Stan Stearns">Stan Stearns</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-131" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-131">&#91;131&#93;</a></sup> became an iconic representation of the 1960s. The children were deemed to be too young to attend the final burial service, so this was the point where the children said goodbye to their father.<sup id="cite_ref-132" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-132">&#91;132&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Virtually everyone else followed the caisson in a long line of black limousines passing by the <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Memorial" title="Lincoln Memorial">Lincoln Memorial</a> and crossing the <a href="/wiki/Potomac_River" title="Potomac River">Potomac River</a>. Many of the military units did not participate in the burial service and left just after crossing the Potomac.<sup id="cite_ref-133" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-133">&#91;133&#93;</a></sup> Because the line of cars taking the foreign dignitaries was long, the last cars carrying the dignitaries left St. Matthew's as the procession entered the cemetery.<sup id="cite_ref-AssociatedPress-Page96_129-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AssociatedPress-Page96-129">&#91;129&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-134" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-134">&#91;134&#93;</a></sup> The burial services had already begun when the last car arrived.<sup id="cite_ref-Martyred_120-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martyred-120">&#91;120&#93;</a></sup> Security guards walked beside the cars carrying the dignitaries, with the one carrying the French president having the most&#8212;10.<sup id="cite_ref-Silence_89-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Silence-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-deGaulle_115-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-deGaulle-115">&#91;115&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A detachment of 26 cadets from the <a href="/wiki/Defence_Forces_(Ireland)" title="Defence Forces (Ireland)">Irish Defense Forces</a>, performed, at the request of Jackie Kennedy, a silent solemn  graveside drill known as the Queen Anne Drill. This is the first, and only, time that a foreign army has been invited to deliver honors at the graveside of a US President.<sup id="cite_ref-135" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-135">&#91;135&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-136" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-136">&#91;136&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the end of the burial services, the widow lit an <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_Eternal_Flame" title="John F. Kennedy Eternal Flame">eternal flame</a> to burn continuously over his grave.<sup id="cite_ref-Martyred_120-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Martyred-120">&#91;120&#93;</a></sup> At 3:34&#160;p.m. EST, the casket containing his remains was lowered into the Wilbert "Triune" grave vault, a 3,000&#160;lb air- and water-tight copper-lined concrete burial receptacle in the Arlington ground, as "Kennedy slipped out of mortal sight&#8212;out of sight but not out of heart and mind."<sup id="cite_ref-AssociatedPress-Page96_129-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AssociatedPress-Page96-129">&#91;129&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy thus became only the second president to be buried at Arlington, after <a href="/wiki/William_Howard_Taft" title="William Howard Taft">William Howard Taft</a>, which meant that, at that time, the two most recent presidents to lie in state in the Capitol rotunda were buried at Arlington.<sup id="cite_ref-PresidentsLyingInState_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PresidentsLyingInState-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SecondPresident_137-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SecondPresident-137">&#91;137&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy was buried at Arlington exactly two weeks to the day he last visited there, when he came for <a href="/wiki/Veterans_Day" title="Veterans Day">Veterans Day</a> observances.<sup id="cite_ref-SecondPresident_137-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SecondPresident-137">&#91;137&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-138" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-138">&#91;138&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
