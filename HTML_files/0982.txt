<p><b>St John's College</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Colleges_of_the_University_of_Cambridge" title="Colleges of the University of Cambridge">constituent college</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a> (the full, formal name of the college is the <b>College of St John the Evangelist in the University of Cambridge</b>)<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> founded by the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Tudor" title="House of Tudor">Tudor</a> matriarch <a href="/wiki/Lady_Margaret_Beaufort" title="Lady Margaret Beaufort">Lady Margaret Beaufort</a>. In constitutional terms, the college is a charitable corporation established by a charter dated 9 April 1511. The aims of the college, as specified by its statutes, are the promotion of education, religion, learning and research.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It is one of the larger <a href="/wiki/Oxbridge" title="Oxbridge">Oxbridge</a> colleges in terms of student numbers. For 2018, St. John‚Äôs was ranked 9th of 29 colleges in the <a href="/wiki/Tompkins_Table" title="Tompkins Table">Tompkins Table</a> (the annual league table of Cambridge colleges) with over 30% of its students earning <a href="/wiki/British_undergraduate_degree_classification#Degree_classification" title="British undergraduate degree classification">First-class honours</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The college's alumni comprise the winners of 11 Nobel Prizes (including physicists <a href="/wiki/Paul_Dirac" title="Paul Dirac">Paul Dirac</a> and <a href="/wiki/Max_Born" title="Max Born">Max Born</a>, the latter having been affiliated with the college in the 1930's), seven prime ministers and 12 archbishops of various countries, at least two princes and three <a href="/wiki/Saints" class="mw-redirect" title="Saints">Saints</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-joh.cam.ac.uk_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-joh.cam.ac.uk-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The Romantic poet <a href="/wiki/William_Wordsworth" title="William Wordsworth">William Wordsworth</a> studied at St John's, as did <a href="/wiki/William_Wilberforce" title="William Wilberforce">William Wilberforce</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Clarkson" title="Thomas Clarkson">Thomas Clarkson</a>, the two abolitionists who led the movement that brought slavery to an end in the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>. <a href="/wiki/Prince_William" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince William">Prince William</a> was affiliated with the college while undertaking a university-run course in estate management in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>St John's is well known for its <a href="/wiki/Choir_of_St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="Choir of St John&#39;s College, Cambridge">choir</a>, its members' success in a wide variety of inter-collegiate sporting competitions and its annual <a href="/wiki/May_Ball" class="mw-redirect" title="May Ball">May Ball</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Apostles" title="Cambridge Apostles">Cambridge Apostles</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University_Moral_Sciences_Club" title="Cambridge University Moral Sciences Club">Cambridge University Moral Sciences Club</a> were both founded by members of the college. The <a href="/wiki/Oxford_and_Cambridge_Boat_Race" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford and Cambridge Boat Race">Oxford and Cambridge Boat Race</a> tradition furthermore began with a St John's student, and the college boat club, <a href="/wiki/Lady_Margaret_Boat_Club" title="Lady Margaret Boat Club">Lady Margaret Boat Club</a>, is the oldest in the university. In 2011, the college celebrated its quincentenary, an event marked by a visit of <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a> and <a href="/wiki/Prince_Philip,_Duke_of_Edinburgh" title="Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh">Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:BeaufortLadyM_CU_SJ_170sm.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/BeaufortLadyM_CU_SJ_170sm.jpg/180px-BeaufortLadyM_CU_SJ_170sm.jpg" decoding="async" width="180" height="272" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/BeaufortLadyM_CU_SJ_170sm.jpg/270px-BeaufortLadyM_CU_SJ_170sm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/BeaufortLadyM_CU_SJ_170sm.jpg/360px-BeaufortLadyM_CU_SJ_170sm.jpg 2x" data-file-width="834" data-file-height="1260" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:BeaufortLadyM_CU_SJ_170sm.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hall portrait of the foundress <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Beaufort,_Countess_of_Richmond_and_Derby" class="mw-redirect" title="Margaret Beaufort, Countess of Richmond and Derby">Margaret Beaufort, Countess of Richmond and Derby</a> by <a href="/wiki/Rowland_Lockey" title="Rowland Lockey">Rowland Lockey</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge_by_Loggan_1690.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge_by_Loggan_1690.jpg/220px-St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge_by_Loggan_1690.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="268" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge_by_Loggan_1690.jpg/330px-St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge_by_Loggan_1690.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge_by_Loggan_1690.jpg/440px-St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge_by_Loggan_1690.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3078" data-file-height="3754" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge_by_Loggan_1690.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Engraving of St John's College, <a href="/wiki/David_Loggan" title="David Loggan">David Loggan</a>, c. 1685</div></div></div>
<p>The site was originally occupied by the Hospital of St John the Evangelist, probably founded around 1200.<sup id="cite_ref-VictoriaV2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VictoriaV2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The hospital infirmary was located where the east end of the current chapel now stands.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> By 1470 <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Rotherham" title="Thomas Rotherham">Thomas Rotherham</a> Chancellor of the university, extended to the hospital the privileges of membership of the university.<sup id="cite_ref-VictoriaV2_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VictoriaV2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> This led to St. John's House, as it was then known, being conferred the status of a college.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> By the early 16th century the hospital was dilapidated and suffering from a lack of funds. <a href="/wiki/Lady_Margaret_Beaufort" title="Lady Margaret Beaufort">Lady Margaret Beaufort</a>, having endowed <a href="/wiki/Christ%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="Christ&#39;s College, Cambridge">Christ's College</a> sought to found a new college, and chose the hospital site at the suggestion of <a href="/wiki/Saint_John_Fisher" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint John Fisher">John Fisher</a>, her chaplain and <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Rochester" title="Bishop of Rochester">Bishop of Rochester</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-VictoriaV2_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VictoriaV2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> However, Lady Margaret died without having mentioned the foundation of St John's in her will, and it was largely the work of Fisher that ensured that the college was founded. He had to obtain the approval of King <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII of England</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pope_Julius_II" title="Pope Julius II">Pope</a> through the intermediary <a href="/wiki/Polydore_Vergil" title="Polydore Vergil">Polydore Vergil</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Ely" title="Bishop of Ely">Bishop of Ely</a> to suppress the religious hospital, by which time held only a Master and three Augustinian brethren, and convert it to a college.
</p><p>The college received its charter on 9 April 1511. Further complications arose in obtaining money from the estate of Lady Margaret to pay for the foundation and it was not until 22 October 1512 that a <a href="/wiki/Codicil_(will)" title="Codicil (will)">codicil</a> was obtained in the court of the Archbishop of Canterbury. In November 1512 the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Chancery" title="Court of Chancery">Court of Chancery</a> allowed Lady Margaret's executors to pay for the foundation of the college from her estates. When Lady Margaret's executors took over they found most of the old Hospital buildings beyond repair, but repaired and incorporated the Chapel into the new college. A kitchen and hall were added, and an imposing gate tower was constructed for the College Treasury. The doors were to be closed each day at dusk, sealing the monastic community from the outside world.
</p><p>Over the course of the following five hundred years, the college expanded westwards towards the <a href="/wiki/River_Cam" title="River Cam">River Cam</a>, and now has twelve courts, the most of any Oxford or Cambridge College. The first three courts are arranged in <a href="/wiki/Enfilade_(architecture)" title="Enfilade (architecture)">enfilade</a>.
</p><p>The college has retained its relationship with <a href="/wiki/Shrewsbury_School" title="Shrewsbury School">Shrewsbury School</a> since 1578, when the headmaster <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Ashton_(schoolmaster)" title="Thomas Ashton (schoolmaster)">Thomas Ashton</a> assisted in drawing up ordinances to govern the school. Under these rulings, the <a href="/wiki/List_of_mayors_of_Shrewsbury" title="List of mayors of Shrewsbury">borough bailiffs</a> (mayors after 1638) had power to appoint masters, along with Ashton's old college, St John's, having an academic veto. Since then, the appointment of Johnian academics to the Governing Body, and the historic awarding of 'closed' Shrewsbury Exhibitions, has continued. The current Master of St. John‚Äôs, <a href="/wiki/Chris_Dobson" title="Chris Dobson">Chris Dobson</a>, has remained an ex officio Governor of <a href="/wiki/Shrewsbury_School" title="Shrewsbury School">Shrewsbury</a> since 2007. <sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>St John's College first admitted women in October 1981, when K. M. Wheeler was admitted to the fellowship, along with nine female graduate students. The first women undergraduates arrived a year later.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings_and_grounds">Buildings and grounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Buildings and grounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Johns_main_gate.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/St_Johns_main_gate.jpg/220px-St_Johns_main_gate.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="303" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/St_Johns_main_gate.jpg/330px-St_Johns_main_gate.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/St_Johns_main_gate.jpg/440px-St_Johns_main_gate.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1316" data-file-height="1810" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Johns_main_gate.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Main Gate of St John's College on <a href="/wiki/St_John%27s_Street,_Cambridge" title="St John&#39;s Street, Cambridge">St John's Street</a>, decorated with the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">arms</a> of the foundress.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Great_Gate">The Great Gate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Great Gate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>St John's distinctive Great Gate follows the standard contemporary pattern employed previously at <a href="/wiki/Christ%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="Christ&#39;s College, Cambridge">Christ's College</a> and <a href="/wiki/Queens%27_College,_Cambridge" title="Queens&#39; College, Cambridge">Queens' College</a>. The gatehouse is crenelated and adorned with the arms of the foundress <a href="/wiki/Lady_Margaret_Beaufort" title="Lady Margaret Beaufort">Lady Margaret Beaufort</a>. Above these are displayed her ensigns, the Red Rose of Lancaster and Portcullis. The college arms are flanked by curious creatures known as <a href="/wiki/Yale_(mythical_creature)" title="Yale (mythical creature)">yales</a>, mythical beasts with elephants' tails, antelopes' bodies, goats' heads, and swivelling horns. Above them is a <a href="/wiki/Church_tabernacle" title="Church tabernacle">tabernacle</a> containing a <a href="/wiki/Socle_(architecture)" title="Socle (architecture)">socle</a> figure of St John the Evangelist, an Eagle at his feet and symbolic, poisoned chalice in his hands. The fan vaulting above is contemporary with tower, and may have been designed by William Swayne, a master mason of King's College Chapel.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Court">First Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>First Court is entered via the Great Gate, and is highly architecturally varied. First Court was converted from the hospital on the foundation of the college, and constructed between 1511 and 1520. Though it has since been gradually changed, the front (east) range is still much as it appeared when first erected in the 16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The south range was refaced between 1772‚Äì6 in the Georgian style by the local architect, James Essex, as part of an abortive attempt to modernise the entire court in the same fashion. The most dramatic alteration to the original, Tudor court, however, remains the Victorian amendment of the north range, which involved the demolition of the original mediaeval chapel and the construction of a new, far larger set of buildings in the 1860s. These included the Chapel, designed by Sir <a href="/wiki/George_Gilbert_Scott" title="George Gilbert Scott">George Gilbert Scott</a>, which includes in its interior some pieces saved from the original chapel. It is the tallest building in Cambridge. The alteration of the north range necessitated the restructuring of the connective sections of First Court; another bay window was added to enlarge the college's hall, and a new building constructed to the north of Great Gate. Parts of First Court were used as a prison in 1643 during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>. In April 2011, <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a> visited St John's college to inaugurate a new pathway in First Court, which passes close to the ruins of the Old Chapel.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dining_hall">Dining hall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dining hall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cam_formal_dinner_st_johns_hall_2005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Cam_formal_dinner_st_johns_hall_2005.jpg/220px-Cam_formal_dinner_st_johns_hall_2005.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="172" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Cam_formal_dinner_st_johns_hall_2005.jpg/330px-Cam_formal_dinner_st_johns_hall_2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Cam_formal_dinner_st_johns_hall_2005.jpg/440px-Cam_formal_dinner_st_johns_hall_2005.jpg 2x" data-file-width="632" data-file-height="495" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cam_formal_dinner_st_johns_hall_2005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The 16th-century dining hall has a <a href="/wiki/Hammerbeam_roof" title="Hammerbeam roof">hammerbeam roof</a></div></div></div>
<p>The college's hall has a fine hammerbeam roof, painted in black and gold and decorated with the armorial devices of its benefactors. The hall is lined to cill level with linenfold panelling which dates from 1528‚Äì9, and has a five-bay screen, surmounted by the Royal Arms. Above is a hexagonal louvre, dating to 1703. The room was extended from five to eight bays according to designs by <a href="/wiki/Sir_George_Gilbert_Scott" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir George Gilbert Scott">Sir George Gilbert Scott</a> in 1863. It has two bay windows, containing heraldic glass dating from the fifteenth to nineteenth centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-imagesofengland.org.uk_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-imagesofengland.org.uk-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In 1564, Queen Elizabeth rode into the college's Hall on horseback, during a state visit to Cambridge.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_Court">Second Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Second Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Second Court, built from 1598 to 1602, has been described as 'the finest <a href="/wiki/Tudor_period" title="Tudor period">Tudor</a> court in England'. Built atop the demolished foundations of an earlier, far smaller court, Second Court was begun in 1598 to the plans of <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Symons" title="Ralph Symons">Ralph Symons</a> of Westminster, and Gilbert Wigge of Cambridge. Their original architectural drawings are housed in the college's library, and are the oldest surviving plans for an Oxford or Cambridge college building.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> It was financed by the <a href="/wiki/Mary_Talbot,_Countess_of_Shrewsbury" title="Mary Talbot, Countess of Shrewsbury">Countess of Shrewsbury</a>, whose arms and statue stand above the court's western gatehouse. The court's Oriel windows are perhaps its most striking feature, though the dominating Shrewsbury Tower to the west is undoubtedly the most imposing. This gatehouse, built as a mirror image of the college's Great Gate, contains a statue of the benefactress <a href="/wiki/Mary_Talbot,_Countess_of_Shrewsbury" title="Mary Talbot, Countess of Shrewsbury">Mary Talbot, Countess of Shrewsbury</a>, added in 1671. Behind the <a href="/wiki/Oriel_window" title="Oriel window">Oriel window</a> of the north range lies the Long Gallery, a promenading room that was, prior to its segmentation, 148 feet long. In this room, the treaty between England and France was signed that established the marriage of King <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I of England</a> to Queen <a href="/wiki/Henrietta_Maria" class="mw-redirect" title="Henrietta Maria">Henrietta Maria</a>. In the 1940s, parts of the <a href="/wiki/D-day" class="mw-redirect" title="D-day">D-day</a> landings were planned there. Second Court is also home to the college's famous 'triple set', K6.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_College_Library">The College Library</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The College Library">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/St_John%27s_College_Old_Library,_Cambridge" title="St John&#39;s College Old Library, Cambridge">Old Library</a> was built in 1624, largely with funds donated by <a href="/wiki/John_Williams,_Bishop_of_Lincoln" class="mw-redirect" title="John Williams, Bishop of Lincoln">John Williams, Bishop of Lincoln</a>. Hearing of the college's urgent need for greater library space, Williams donated ¬£1,200 anonymously, later revealing his identity and donating a total of ¬£2,011 towards the library's total cost of ¬£3,000.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The Library's fine bay window overlooks the River Cam, and bears the letters ILCS on it, standing for Iohannes Lincolniensis Custos Sigilli, or John of Lincoln, Keeper of the Seal. The original intention of the college had been to construct an elegant, classical building supported by pillared porticos, but Bishop William insisted on a more traditional design. Thus, though the college lays claim to few examples of <a href="/wiki/Neoclassical_architecture" title="Neoclassical architecture">neo-classical</a> design, the college Library stands as one of the earliest examples of English <a href="/wiki/Neo-Gothic" class="mw-redirect" title="Neo-Gothic">neo-Gothic</a> architecture.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Third_Court">Third Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Third Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Third Court is entered through Shrewsbury Tower, which from 1765 to 1859 housed an observatory. Each of its ranges was built in a different style. Following the completion of the college library in 1624, the final sides of Third Court were added between 1669 and 1672, after the college had recovered from the trauma of the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>. The additions included a fine set of Dutch-gabled buildings backing onto the River Cam, and a 'window-with-nothing-behind-it' that was designed to solve the problem of connecting the windowed library with the remainder of the court.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kitchen_or_Wren_Bridge">Kitchen or Wren Bridge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Kitchen or Wren Bridge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bridge_over_the_River_Cam,_Cambridge,_2008-03-31.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Bridge_over_the_River_Cam%2C_Cambridge%2C_2008-03-31.jpg/220px-Bridge_over_the_River_Cam%2C_Cambridge%2C_2008-03-31.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Bridge_over_the_River_Cam%2C_Cambridge%2C_2008-03-31.jpg/330px-Bridge_over_the_River_Cam%2C_Cambridge%2C_2008-03-31.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Bridge_over_the_River_Cam%2C_Cambridge%2C_2008-03-31.jpg/440px-Bridge_over_the_River_Cam%2C_Cambridge%2C_2008-03-31.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bridge_over_the_River_Cam,_Cambridge,_2008-03-31.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View of Wren Bridge from the Backs</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bridge_of_Sighs,_St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge,_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Bridge_of_Sighs%2C_St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/220px-Bridge_of_Sighs%2C_St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Bridge_of_Sighs%2C_St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/330px-Bridge_of_Sighs%2C_St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/60/Bridge_of_Sighs%2C_St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/440px-Bridge_of_Sighs%2C_St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5680" data-file-height="3787" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bridge_of_Sighs,_St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge,_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>St John's College Bridge of Sighs</div></div></div>
<p>This was the first stone bridge erected at St John's college, continuing on from Kitchen lane. The crossing's chief distinction is the use of illusory <a href="/wiki/Intaglio_(sculpture)" class="mw-redirect" title="Intaglio (sculpture)">intaglio</a>; Wren's bridge is carved from a limestone <a href="/wiki/Monolith" title="Monolith">monolith</a> incised to give the appearance of masonry.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The crossing lies south of the Bridge of Sighs, and was a replacement for a wooden bridge that had stood on the site since the foundation's early days as a hospital. Though <a href="/wiki/Sir_Christopher_Wren" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Christopher Wren">Sir Christopher Wren</a> submitted designs for the bridge, it was eventually built on a different site by a local mason, Robert Grumbold, who also built Trinity College Library. As with the Library, Grumbold's work was based on Wren's designs, and the bridge has become known more famously as 'the Wren Bridge'.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kitchen_Court">Kitchen Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Kitchen Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This tiny court, formed within the walls of the old Kitchen Lane, is used as an outdoor dining area.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Bridge_of_Sighs">The Bridge of Sighs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The Bridge of Sighs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though it bears little resemblance to its namesake in Venice, the bridge connecting Third Court to New Court, originally known as New Bridge, is now commonly known as the <a href="/wiki/Bridge_of_Sighs_(Cambridge)" title="Bridge of Sighs (Cambridge)">Bridge of Sighs</a>. It is one of the most photographed buildings in Cambridge, and was described by the visiting Queen Victoria as "so pretty and picturesque".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> It is a single-span bridge of stone with highly decorative Neo-Gothic covered footwalk over with traceried openings. There is a three-bay arcade at the east end of the bridge. The architect was <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hutchinson" title="Henry Hutchinson">Henry Hutchinson</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Court">New Court</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: New Court">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 19th-century neo-Gothic New Court, probably one of the best known buildings in Cambridge, was the first major building built by any of the colleges on the west side of the river. Designed by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Rickman" title="Thomas Rickman">Thomas Rickman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hutchinson" title="Henry Hutchinson">Henry Hutchinson</a>, New Court was built between 1826 and 1831 to accommodate the college's rapidly increasing numbers of students. Despite the college's original intention to get the architects to build another copy of Second Court, plans were eventually accepted for a fashionably romantic building in the 'Gothic' style. It is a three-sided court of tall Gothic Revival buildings, closed on the fourth side by an open, seven-bayed cross-vaulted cloister and gateway. It is four storeys high, has battlements and is pinnacled. The main portal has a fan vault with a large octagonal pendant, and the interior of the main building retains many of its original features including ribbed plaster ceilings in the mock-Gothic style. Its prominent location (especially when seen from the river) and flamboyant design have led it to be nicknamed "The Wedding Cake". Hutchinson was suitably proud of his creation, and it is said that he once dashed up a staircase to reprimand an undergraduate for spoiling its symmetry by sitting too near one of its windows.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="College_Chapel">College Chapel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: College Chapel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:LeKeux_-_Cambridge,_c1840_-_St_John%27s_05,_Chapel_-_memorialsofcambr01wriguoft_0214.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/LeKeux_-_Cambridge%2C_c1840_-_St_John%27s_05%2C_Chapel_-_memorialsofcambr01wriguoft_0214.jpg/220px-LeKeux_-_Cambridge%2C_c1840_-_St_John%27s_05%2C_Chapel_-_memorialsofcambr01wriguoft_0214.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="327" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/LeKeux_-_Cambridge%2C_c1840_-_St_John%27s_05%2C_Chapel_-_memorialsofcambr01wriguoft_0214.jpg/330px-LeKeux_-_Cambridge%2C_c1840_-_St_John%27s_05%2C_Chapel_-_memorialsofcambr01wriguoft_0214.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/LeKeux_-_Cambridge%2C_c1840_-_St_John%27s_05%2C_Chapel_-_memorialsofcambr01wriguoft_0214.jpg/440px-LeKeux_-_Cambridge%2C_c1840_-_St_John%27s_05%2C_Chapel_-_memorialsofcambr01wriguoft_0214.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2092" data-file-height="3112" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:LeKeux_-_Cambridge,_c1840_-_St_John%27s_05,_Chapel_-_memorialsofcambr01wriguoft_0214.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Old College Chapel</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_John%27s_College_Chapel,_Cambridge,_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/St_John%27s_College_Chapel%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/220px-St_John%27s_College_Chapel%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="243" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/St_John%27s_College_Chapel%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/330px-St_John%27s_College_Chapel%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/St_John%27s_College_Chapel%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/440px-St_John%27s_College_Chapel%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4918" data-file-height="5432" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_John%27s_College_Chapel,_Cambridge,_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Inside St John's College Chapel, Cambridge</div></div></div>
<p>The Chapel of St John's College is entered by the northwest corner of First Court, and was constructed between 1866 and 1869 to replace the smaller, mediaeval chapel which dated back to the 13th century. When in 1861 the college's administration decided that a new building was needed, <a href="/wiki/Sir_George_Gilbert_Scott" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir George Gilbert Scott">Sir George Gilbert Scott</a> was selected as architect. He had recently finished work on the chapel at <a href="/wiki/Exeter_College,_Oxford" title="Exeter College, Oxford">Exeter College, Oxford</a>, and went about constructing the chapel of St John's College along similar lines, drawing inspiration from the Church of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Chapelle" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Chapelle">Saint Chapelle</a> in Paris.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_John%27s_College_Chapel_Court,_Cambridge,_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/St_John%27s_College_Chapel_Court%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/220px-St_John%27s_College_Chapel_Court%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/St_John%27s_College_Chapel_Court%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/330px-St_John%27s_College_Chapel_Court%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/St_John%27s_College_Chapel_Court%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/440px-St_John%27s_College_Chapel_Court%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6008" data-file-height="4016" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_John%27s_College_Chapel_Court,_Cambridge,_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>St John's College Chapel was designed by Sir <a href="/wiki/George_Gilbert_Scott" title="George Gilbert Scott">George Gilbert Scott</a></div></div></div>
<p>The benefactor Henry Hoare offered a downpayment of ¬£3000 to finance the chapel's construction, in addition to which he promised to pay ¬£1000 a year if a tower were added to Scott's original plans, which had included only a small <a href="/wiki/Fleche" class="mw-redirect" title="Fleche">fleche</a>. Work began, but Mr Hoare's death in a railway accident left the college ¬£3000 short of his expected benefaction. The tower was completed, replete with <a href="/wiki/Louver" title="Louver">louvres</a> but left without bells. It is based on <a href="/wiki/Pershore_Abbey" title="Pershore Abbey">Pershore Abbey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The tower is 163 feet (50&#160;m) high.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Chapel's antechamber contains statues of Margaret Beaufort and John Fisher. Inside the building is a stone-vaulted antechapel, at the end of which hangs a 'Deposition of the Cross' by <a href="/wiki/Anton_Rafael_Mengs" class="mw-redirect" title="Anton Rafael Mengs">Anton Rafael Mengs</a>, completed around 1777. The misericordes and panelling date from 1516, and were salvaged from the old chapel. The chapel contains some fifteenth-century glass, but most was cast by Clayton and Bell, Hardman, and Wailes, in around 1869.<sup id="cite_ref-imagesofengland.org.uk_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-imagesofengland.org.uk-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Freestanding statues and plaques commemorate college benefactors such as James Wood, Master 1815‚Äì39, as well as alumni including <a href="/wiki/William_Wilberforce" title="William Wilberforce">William Wilberforce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Clarkson" title="Thomas Clarkson">Thomas Clarkson</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Gilbert_(astronomer)" title="William Gilbert (astronomer)">William Gilbert</a>. The college tower can be climbed, and is accessed via a small door on First Court. However, this access was closed in 2016 for the duration that important structural repairs were carried out to the tower <a href="/wiki/Pinnacle" title="Pinnacle">Pinnacle</a>s and roof.
</p><p>The Chapel is surrounded on three sides by large <a href="/wiki/Church_tabernacle" title="Church tabernacle">tabernacles</a> which form part of the external buttresses. Each contains a statue of a prominent college alumnus, alumna or benefactor. The persons commemorated are, beginning with the buttress next to the transept on the south side:
</p>

<h3><span id="The_Master.27s_lodge_and_garden"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Master's_lodge_and_garden">The Master's lodge and garden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: The Master&#039;s lodge and garden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>St John's Master's lodge is located in a grassy clearing to the north of Third Court. It was built at the same time as the new chapel was being constructed, and has Tudor fittings, wainscot, portraits and other relics from the demolished north wing of First Court. It has a large garden, and in the winter its westmost rooms have excellent views of the college's old library, the River Cam, and the Bridge of Sighs. The architect was <a href="/wiki/Sir_George_Gilbert_Scott" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir George Gilbert Scott">Sir George Gilbert Scott</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings_and_courts_since_1900">Buildings and courts since 1900</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Buildings and courts since 1900">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Divinity_School,_St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge_-_01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Divinity_School%2C_St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge_-_01.jpg/220px-Divinity_School%2C_St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge_-_01.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Divinity_School%2C_St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge_-_01.jpg/330px-Divinity_School%2C_St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge_-_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Divinity_School%2C_St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge_-_01.jpg/440px-Divinity_School%2C_St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge_-_01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4194" data-file-height="2996" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Divinity_School,_St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge_-_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Old Divinity School</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge,_chapel_court_03.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge%2C_chapel_court_03.JPG/220px-St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge%2C_chapel_court_03.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge%2C_chapel_court_03.JPG/330px-St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge%2C_chapel_court_03.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge%2C_chapel_court_03.JPG/440px-St_John%27s_College%2C_Cambridge%2C_chapel_court_03.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1944" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge,_chapel_court_03.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chapel Court</div></div></div>
<p>Located to the west of the Chapel tower lies <b>Chapel Court</b>, which was constructed together with <b>North Court</b> and <b>Forecourt</b> in the 1930s to account for an increase in student numbers. North Court is located just north of Chapel Court and Forecourt is situated to the east, facing <a href="/wiki/St_John%27s_Street,_Cambridge" title="St John&#39;s Street, Cambridge">St John's Street</a>. The latter is used partly as a car park for fellows and night entrance to the college. All three courts were designed by the architect <a href="/wiki/Edward_Maufe" title="Edward Maufe">Edward Maufe</a>.
</p><p>Further increases in student numbers following WWII prompted the college to again increase the number of accommodation buildings. <b>Cripps Court</b> was built in the late 1960s to satisfy this demand. It is located just behind New Court and forms two courts (Upper &amp; Lower River Court). Designed by architects <a href="/wiki/Philip_Powell_(architect)" title="Philip Powell (architect)">Philip Powell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hidalgo_Moya" title="Hidalgo Moya">Hidalgo Moya</a>, the building is Grade II* listed<sup id="cite_ref-NHLE1393223_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHLE1393223-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> having received an award from the British Architectural Institution. It is considered an exemplar of late 20th-century architectural style and is named after its main benefactor, <a href="/wiki/Humphrey_Cripps" title="Humphrey Cripps">Humphrey Cripps</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> In 2014, the building went through an extensive refurbishment program, which saw renovated accommodation and structural repairs, including the cleaning of the <a href="/wiki/Portland_stone" title="Portland stone">Portland stone</a> which the building is made of.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>To the west of Cripps Court lies the <a href="/wiki/School_of_Pythagoras" title="School of Pythagoras">School of Pythagoras</a>. Built around 1200, it predates the college with 300 years and is  both the oldest secular building in Cambridge and the oldest building continuously in use by a university in Britain. The building now serves as the location for the <b>College Archives</b>. Next to the School of Pythagoras lies <a href="/wiki/Merton_Hall" class="mw-redirect" title="Merton Hall">Merton Hall</a>. From 1266 until 1959 both the School of Pythagoras and Merton Hall were property of <a href="/wiki/Merton_College,_Oxford" title="Merton College, Oxford">Merton College, Oxford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Merton Court is the college's eleventh and westernmost court.
</p><p>In 1987 the construction of the <b>Fisher Building</b> was completed. Named after cardinal <a href="/wiki/John_Fisher" title="John Fisher">John Fisher</a>, the building contains teaching rooms, conference facilities, and the student-run college cinema. It was designed by the architect <a href="/wiki/Peter_Boston" title="Peter Boston">Peter Boston</a>.
</p><p>Located directly opposite the college's Great Gate is <b>All Saints' Yard</b>. The complex is formed from the buildings of the so-called 'Triangle Site', a collection of structures owned by the college. An extensive renovation project finished in Michaelmas Term 2012 had a budget of approximately ¬£9.75 million. The centrepiece of the Yard is Corfield Court, named after the project's chief benefactor, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Corfield" title="Charles Corfield">Charles Corfield</a>. The site can be entered through one of two card-activated gates, or through the School of Divinity. The School of Divinity is the largest building on the site, and was built between 1878 and 1879 by <a href="/wiki/Basil_Champneys" title="Basil Champneys">Basil Champneys</a> for the University of Cambridge's Divinity Faculty on land leased by St John's College. Control of the building reverted to St John's when the Faculty of Divinity moved to a new building on the Sidgwick site in 2000.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="College_choirs">College choirs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: College choirs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Choir_of_St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="Choir of St John&#39;s College, Cambridge">Choir of St John's College, Cambridge</a></div>
<p>The Choir of St John's College has a tradition of religious music and has sung the daily services in the College Chapel since the 1670s. The services follow the cathedral tradition of the Church of England, <a href="/wiki/Evening_Prayer_(Book_of_Common_Prayer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Evening Prayer (Book of Common Prayer)">Evensong</a> being sung during Term six days a week and Sung <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">Eucharist</a> on Sunday mornings. The choir is currently directed by Mr Andrew Nethsingha, who has previously been Director of Music at Gloucester and Truro Cathedrals.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The boys of the choir are all educated and board at <a href="/wiki/St_John%27s_College_School" title="St John&#39;s College School">St John's College School</a>. During university vacations the choir carries out engagements elsewhere. Recent tours have taken it to places including the Netherlands, the US and Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The choir has an extensive discography of nearly 100 commercial releases dating back to the 1950s, when it was signed to the Decca/<a href="/wiki/Argo_Records_(UK)" title="Argo Records (UK)">Argo</a> label under <a href="/wiki/George_Guest" title="George Guest">George Guest</a>. The Choir has since had successful recording contracts with <a href="/wiki/Hyperion_Records" title="Hyperion Records">Hyperion Records</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chandos_Records" title="Chandos Records">Chandos Records</a>, resulting in many critical accolades including a <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_(magazine)" title="Gramophone (magazine)">Gramophone (magazine)</a> Editor's Choice selection for 2015's collection <i>The Call</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> In 2016 the choir signed to Signum Records on its own St John's College imprint.  The first recording of this new venture was a collection of music by the contemporary composer <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Harvey_(composer)" title="Jonathan Harvey (composer)">Jonathan Harvey (composer)</a> released in May 2016 to a number two position in the UK specialist classical charts. The imprint will also release non choral recordings by current and former members of the College.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The men of the choir, or choral scholars, also form their own close harmony group, The Gentlemen of St John's. Their repertoire spans the 15th century through to the modern day, and concert tours have taken them to Europe, the US and Japan. They provide a mixture of classical a cappella music and folksongs, as well as covers of recently chart hits and light-hearted entertainment, and host an annual Christmas concert and garden party.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The college also has a mixed-voice choir, St John's Voices, which was founded in 2013 to allow female members of the college to take part in the college's choral tradition. It comprises around 30 members, and has premiered 3 works thus far<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditions_and_legends">Traditions and legends</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Traditions and legends">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Consumption_of_Swan">Consumption of Swan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Consumption of Swan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fellows of St John's College are the only people outside the Royal Family legally allowed to eat unmarked <a href="/wiki/Mute_swan" title="Mute swan">mute swans</a>. Swan traps were originally built into the walls of the college alongside the river, but these are no longer used.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The Crown (the <a href="/wiki/British_monarch" class="mw-redirect" title="British monarch">British monarch</a>) retains the right to ownership of all unmarked mute swans in open water, but the Queen only exercises her ownership on certain stretches of the Thames and its surrounding tributaries. This ownership is shared with the Vintners' and Dyers' Companies, who were granted rights of ownership by the Crown in the fifteenth century, and was extended to the college via ancient <a href="/wiki/Royalist" title="Royalist">Royalist</a> ties.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ghosts">Ghosts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Ghosts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to popular legend, St John's College is inhabited by a number of ghosts. In 1706, four fellows exorcised some ghosts from a house opposite the college by the simple method of threatening to fire their pistols at the positions the moans and groans were coming from. Second court is apparently still haunted by the ghost of the former undergraduate and master, <a href="/wiki/James_Wood_(mathematician)" title="James Wood (mathematician)">James Wood</a>. Wood was so poor that he could not afford to light his room, and would often do his work in the well-lit stairway.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="New_Court.27s_Clock_Tower"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Court's_Clock_Tower">New Court's Clock Tower</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: New Court&#039;s Clock Tower">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_John%27s_College_New_Court_1,_Cambridge,_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/St_John%27s_College_New_Court_1%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/220px-St_John%27s_College_New_Court_1%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/St_John%27s_College_New_Court_1%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/330px-St_John%27s_College_New_Court_1%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/St_John%27s_College_New_Court_1%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg/440px-St_John%27s_College_New_Court_1%2C_Cambridge%2C_UK_-_Diliff.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6748" data-file-height="4305" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_John%27s_College_New_Court_1,_Cambridge,_UK_-_Diliff.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>St John's College New Court and blank clock tower face</div></div></div>
<p>New Court's central <a href="/wiki/Cupola" title="Cupola">cupola</a> has four blank clock-faces. These are subject to various apocryphal explanations. One legend maintains that a statute limiting the number of chiming clocks in Cambridge rendered the addition of a mechanism illegal. No such limitation is known to exist. More likely explanations include Hutchinson's fear that the installation of a clockface would spoil the building's symmetry, and that the college's financial situation in the early nineteenth century made completion impossible.
</p><p>Other legends explaining the absence of clockfaces claim that St John's College and its neighbour, Trinity College, were engaged in a race to build the final (or tallest) clocktower in Cambridge. Supposedly, whichever was finished first (or was tallest) would be permitted to house the 'final' chiming clock in Cambridge. Trinity's Tower was finished first (or, in another version of the same story, was made taller overnight by the addition of a wooden cupola), and its clock was allowed to remain. In truth, the completion of New Court and Trinity's Clock (which is in King Edward's Tower) was separated by nearly two centuries. Trinity's famous double-striking was installed in the seventeenth century by its then-Master, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bentley" title="Richard Bentley">Richard Bentley</a>, a former student of St John's, who dictated that the clock chime once for Trinity, and once for his alma mater, St John's.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="College_rivalry">College rivalry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: College rivalry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>St John's remains a great rival of <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College</a>, which is its main competitor in sports and academia. The rivalry can be traced to Henry VIII founding Trinity after having ordered the execution of Saint John Fisher, the man whose efforts had ensured the foundation of St John's. Over the years, numerous anecdotes and myths have arisen, involving students and fellows of both colleges alike. The rivalry is often cited as the reason why the older courts of Trinity have no "J" staircases, despite including other letters in alphabetical order (it should be mentioned that a far more likely reason is the absence of the letter "J" in the Latin alphabet). There are also two small muzzle-loading cannons on Trinity's bowling green pointing in the direction of John's, though this orientation may be coincidental. In similar fashion, the eagle on top of the entrance to St John's New Court is said to have been sculptured so that it shuns even the sight of its neighbouring rival. Generally, however, the colleges maintain a cordial relationship with one other; compatriotism led famously to the splitting of the atomic nucleus in 1932 by <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Walton" title="Ernest Walton">Ernest Walton</a> (Trinity) and <a href="/wiki/John_Cockcroft" title="John Cockcroft">John Cockcroft</a> (St John's).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shield_and_arms">Shield and arms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Shield and arms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>St John's College and <a href="/wiki/Christ%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="Christ&#39;s College, Cambridge">Christ's College, Cambridge</a> both bear the arms of the Lady Margaret Beaufort, Countess of Richmond and Derby, mother of Henry VII. These arms are recorded in the College of Arms as being borne by right, and are described as: <i>Quarterly: 1 and 4 azure three fleurs-de-lis gold (France, Modern); 2 and 3 gules three lions passant gardant or (England); all within a border compony silver and azure</i>. In addition, both foundations use the Beaufort crest, <i>an eagle displayed arising out of a coronet of roses and fleurs-de-lis all gold</i>, but their title to this is more doubtful. When displayed in their full achievement, the arms are flanked by mythical <a href="/wiki/Yale_(mythical_creature)" title="Yale (mythical creature)">yales</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Motto">Motto</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Motto">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The college motto is <i>souvent me souvient</i>, supplied by Lady Margaret Beaufort, and written in Mediaeval French. It is inscribed over gates, lintels and within <a href="/wiki/Tympanum_(architecture)" title="Tympanum (architecture)">tympana</a> throughout the college, functioning as a triple pun. It means 'often I remember', 'think of me often' and, when spoken (exploiting the homonym <i>souvent me sous vient</i>), 'I often pass beneath it' (referring to the inscriptions). The college shares its motto with <a href="/wiki/Christ%27s_College,_Cambridge" title="Christ&#39;s College, Cambridge">Christ's College, Cambridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lady_Margaret_Hall,_Oxford" title="Lady Margaret Hall, Oxford">Lady Margaret Hall, Oxford</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="College_Grace">College Grace</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: College Grace">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

The Grace is said shortly after the fellows enter the Hall, signalled by the sounding of a Gong, and accompanied by the ringing of the college's Grace Bell. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_life">Student life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Student life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The buildings of St John's College include the Chapel, the Hall, the old library, a more contemporary "new" library, a bar, and common rooms for fellows, graduates and undergraduates. There are also extensive gardens, lawns, a neighbouring sportsground, College School and boat-house. On-site accommodation is provided for all undergraduate and most graduate students. This is generally spacious, and many undergraduate rooms comprise 'sets' of living and sleeping rooms, where two students share a suite of two bedrooms, a kitchen and a bathroom. Members of the college can choose to dine either in the Hall, where silver service three-course meals are served six evenings per week, or in the buttery, where food can be purchased from a cafeteria-style buffet. College catering is organised by Bill Brogan, overseer of the intercollegiate Stewards' Cup.
</p><p>The college maintains an extensive library, which supplements the university libraries. Most undergraduate supervisions are carried out in the college, though for some specialist subjects undergraduates may be sent to tutors in other colleges.
</p><p>The college has two official combination rooms for junior members, which represent the interests of students in college and are responsible for social aspects of college life. Undergraduates are members of the Junior Combination Room (JCR). Graduate students have membership to the JCR, but also belong to the <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Butler_Room_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="Samuel Butler Room Society">Samuel Butler Room</a>, which is the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Combination_Room" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle Combination Room">Middle Combination Room</a> (MCR) of St John's College.
</p><p>The fleet of <a href="/wiki/Punt_(boat)" title="Punt (boat)">punts</a> is kept in a purpose-built punt pool behind the Cripps Building. Punt boats are available for use by all members of the college as well as alumni.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>St John's tends to be ranked near the middle of the <a href="/wiki/Tompkins_Table" title="Tompkins Table">Tompkins Table</a> of undergraduate degree results, with an average position of 12.8 since 1997.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Samuel_Butler_Room_Society">Samuel Butler Room Society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Samuel Butler Room Society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sbr_outside_2010.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ed/Sbr_outside_2010.JPG/220px-Sbr_outside_2010.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ed/Sbr_outside_2010.JPG/330px-Sbr_outside_2010.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ed/Sbr_outside_2010.JPG/440px-Sbr_outside_2010.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sbr_outside_2010.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Entrance to the SBR, I1 First Court</div></div></div>
<p>The Samuel Butler Room Society (SBR) is the Middle Combination Room (MCR) of St John's College. The Society traces its foundation to 1960, when graduate student members submitted an application to College Council for official separation from the <a href="/wiki/Junior_Combination_Room" class="mw-redirect" title="Junior Combination Room">Junior Combination Room</a> (JCR). The name of the Society refers to the physical rooms which are used by members of the Society. The rooms were named after the noted Johnian author and polymath <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Butler_(novelist)" title="Samuel Butler (novelist)">Samuel Butler</a>. The membership of the Society comprises all members of the College who are registered graduate students of the College and affiliated students of the College.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The college has a sporting history, enjoying success in most of the major sports on offer in Cambridge. The college has a cardio gym and a weights gym on site, and has pristine pitches right behind college.
</p><p>The Red Boys, St John's College Rugby Club, won the Division One League title for nine years in a row, before finally losing to Jesus in 2010‚Äì11, and the <a href="/wiki/Cuppers" title="Cuppers">cuppers</a> trophy for 6 years in a row from 2006 to 2011, making it one of the most successful collegiate sports teams in Cambridge's history. The Redboys occupy eighteen out of the twenty-four plates on the current Division One League Shield, and thirteen out of the seventeen names on the Cuppers trophy. Since 2014, the club has taken home two Cuppers trophies, three Division One League Shields, and the inaugural Sevens Shield in 2016/17. In the 2016/17 season, the Redboys went undefeated.
</p><p>St John‚Äôs regularly produces a strong contingent of university players, but also ensures new players develop and get game time swiftly. The rugby club has produced several notable alumni including former RFU executive <a href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Baron&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Francis Baron (page does not exist)">Francis Baron</a>, former Newcastle, England and Lions fly-half and former RFU Director of Elite Rugby <a href="/wiki/Rob_Andrew" title="Rob Andrew">Rob Andrew</a>, and Battlestar Galactica actor <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Bamber" title="Jamie Bamber">Jamie Bamber</a>.
</p><p>The college rowing club, the <a href="/wiki/Lady_Margaret_Boat_Club" title="Lady Margaret Boat Club">Lady Margaret Boat Club</a> (LMBC), is the oldest in the university, and was founded in 1825. Despite many gruesome rumours concerning the name of the club, it was merely the most successful of the many boat clubs established in the college in the 19th century. In a similar fashion the traditional rival of the LMBC, the Boat Club of <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College</a>, is known as '<a href="/wiki/First_and_Third" class="mw-redirect" title="First and Third">First and Third</a>' in a reference to its formation from two original clubs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scholarships_and_prizes">Scholarships and prizes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Scholarships and prizes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Every year the college awards scholarships to a handful of graduate students under the Benefactors' and Scholarships Scheme.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The most generous of all the early benefactors of St John's College was <a href="/wiki/Roger_Lupton" title="Roger Lupton">Dr Roger Lupton</a> (died 1540), <a href="/wiki/Provost_(education)" title="Provost (education)">Provost</a> of <a href="/wiki/Eton_College" title="Eton College">Eton</a> and chaplain to <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry VIII">Henry VIII</a>. Lupton had amassed immense wealth through a lifetime of royal service and ecclesiastical <a href="/wiki/Pluralities" class="mw-redirect" title="Pluralities">pluralism</a> and his scholarships exist today as  the Lupton and Hebblethwaite Exhibitions.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Other scholarships include the Craik Scholarship, the J.C. Hall Scholarship, the Luisa Aldobrandini Studentship Competition, the Paskin Scholarship and the Pelling Scholarship. Competition for these scholarships is very fierce as students from any country reading for any graduate degree‚Äînot only members of the college‚Äîcan apply.
</p>

<p>There is also the famous <a href="/wiki/Adams_Prize" title="Adams Prize">Adams Prize</a> in mathematics, named after the mathematician (and alumnus of St John's) <a href="/wiki/John_Couch_Adams" title="John Couch Adams">John Couch Adams</a> for his discovery of Neptune ‚Äì it is an annual competition and can be awarded to any mathematician resident in the UK, with an age limit of under 40. The college is also associated with the <a href="/wiki/Dr_Manmohan_Singh_Scholarship" title="Dr Manmohan Singh Scholarship">Dr Manmohan Singh Scholarship</a>, first awarded in 2008.
</p><p>Students at the college wishing to practice law can apply for a McMahon Law Studentship to cover the expense of further study or obtaining professional qualifications.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="May_Ball">May Ball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: May Ball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>St John's hosts a large and typically spectacular <a href="/wiki/May_Ball" class="mw-redirect" title="May Ball">May Ball</a>, which is traditionally held on the Tuesday of <a href="/wiki/May_Week" title="May Week">May Week</a>. In recent years, tickets have only been available to Johnians and their guests. Highlights include an extravagant fireworks display and a variety of musical acts which in recent years have included <a href="/wiki/Rudimental" title="Rudimental">Rudimental</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gorgon_City" title="Gorgon City">Gorgon City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Katy_B" title="Katy B">Katy B</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span id="St_John.27s_and_the_abolition_of_the_British_slave_trade"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="St_John's_and_the_abolition_of_the_British_slave_trade">St John's and the abolition of the British slave trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=St_John%27s_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: St John&#039;s and the abolition of the British slave trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Anti-Slavery_Society_Convention,_1840_by_Benjamin_Robert_Haydon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/The_Anti-Slavery_Society_Convention%2C_1840_by_Benjamin_Robert_Haydon.jpg/220px-The_Anti-Slavery_Society_Convention%2C_1840_by_Benjamin_Robert_Haydon.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/The_Anti-Slavery_Society_Convention%2C_1840_by_Benjamin_Robert_Haydon.jpg/330px-The_Anti-Slavery_Society_Convention%2C_1840_by_Benjamin_Robert_Haydon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/The_Anti-Slavery_Society_Convention%2C_1840_by_Benjamin_Robert_Haydon.jpg/440px-The_Anti-Slavery_Society_Convention%2C_1840_by_Benjamin_Robert_Haydon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3600" data-file-height="2798" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Anti-Slavery_Society_Convention,_1840_by_Benjamin_Robert_Haydon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>St John's alumnus <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Clarkson" title="Thomas Clarkson">Thomas Clarkson</a> addresses delegates at the 1840 convention of the <a href="/wiki/British_and_Foreign_Anti-Slavery_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="British and Foreign Anti-Slavery Society">British and Foreign Anti-Slavery Society</a></div></div></div>
<p>Several of St John's graduates were involved in the efforts to abolish the British Slave Trade that culminated in the <a href="/wiki/Slave_Trade_Act_1807" title="Slave Trade Act 1807">Act of 1807</a>. In particular, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Clarkson" title="Thomas Clarkson">Thomas Clarkson</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Wilberforce" title="William Wilberforce">William Wilberforce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gisborne" title="Thomas Gisborne">Thomas Gisborne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Babington" title="Thomas Babington">Thomas Babington</a> were active in the <a href="/wiki/Committee_for_the_Abolition_of_the_Slave_Trade" class="mw-redirect" title="Committee for the Abolition of the Slave Trade">Committee for the Abolition of the Slave Trade</a> and other abolitionist efforts.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As part of the commemoration of the bicentenary of the 1807 Act, and as a representative of one of the <a href="/wiki/Ivy_League" title="Ivy League">Ivy League</a> universities offering American historical perspective on the <a href="/wiki/Triangular_Trade" class="mw-redirect" title="Triangular Trade">Triangular Trade</a>, President <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Simmons" title="Ruth Simmons">Ruth J. Simmons</a> of <a href="/wiki/Brown_University" title="Brown University">Brown University</a> (herself a descendant of American slaves) gave a public lecture at St John's College entitled "Hidden in Plain Sight: Slavery and Justice in Rhode Island" on 16 February 2007. St John's College hosted some of the events relating to the commemoration,<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> including an academic conference and a Gospel Mass in the College Chapel with the London Adventist Chorale.
</p>
