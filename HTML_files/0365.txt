	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Professional degree</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>professional degree</b>, formerly known in the US as a <b>first professional degree</b>, is a degree that prepares someone to work in a particular <a href="/wiki/Profession" title="Profession">profession</a>, often, but not always, meeting the academic requirements for <a href="/wiki/Licensure" title="Licensure">licensure</a> or <a href="/wiki/Accreditation" title="Accreditation">accreditation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Professional degrees may be either graduate or undergraduate entry, depending on the profession concerned and the country, and may be classified as bachelor's, master's or doctoral degrees. For a variety of reasons, professional degrees may bear the name of a different level of qualification from their classification in qualifications frameworks, e.g. some UK professional degrees are named bachelor's but are at master's level, while some Australian and Canadian professional degrees  have the name "doctor" but are classified as master's or bachelor's degrees.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professional_degree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_professional_degrees_in_Europe">History of professional degrees in Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professional_degree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History of professional degrees in Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first doctorates were awarded in the mid twelfth century to recognise teachers (doctors) in mediaeval universities, either in civil law at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bologna" title="University of Bologna">University of Bologna</a><sup id="cite_ref-cathen_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cathen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> or in theology at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Paris" title="University of Paris">University of Paris</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> These were followed shortly afterwards by doctorates in canon law, and then in the thirteenth century by doctorates in medicine, grammar, logic and philosophy. These mediaeval doctorates remained, however, essentially teaching qualifications, with their major importance being the <i>ius ubique docendi</i> – the right to teach anywhere.<sup id="cite_ref-cathen_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cathen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_professional_degrees_in_the_United_Kingdom">History of professional degrees in the United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professional_degree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History of professional degrees in the United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first university medical school to be established in the United Kingdom was at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">University of Edinburgh</a> in 1726, followed in 1744 by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Glasgow" title="University of Glasgow">University of Glasgow</a>. In 1817 Glasgow became the first British university to offer a separate degree in surgery, the Master of Surgery. However, other Scottish universities – <a href="/wiki/University_of_St_Andrews" title="University of St Andrews">St Andrews</a> and the two universities in Aberdeen – also offered medical degrees, often <i>in absentia</i> and without examination, despite not having medical schools.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In England, the two universities (Oxford and Cambridge) were only sporadically interested in medical teaching, which was mainly carried out in the London hospitals.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It was not until the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a> in 1836, however, that students at the hospital medical schools could earn degrees. Following the passing of the <a href="/wiki/Medical_Act_1858" title="Medical Act 1858">Medical Act 1858</a> and the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/General_Medical_Council" title="General Medical Council">General Medical Council</a>, Scottish graduates gained the right to practice in England and degrees in both medicine and surgery became the norm. The Scottish practice of awarding the <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Medicine" title="Doctor of Medicine">Doctor of Medicine</a> (MD) as a first degree was abolished by the university commissioners appointed under the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_university_governance_in_Scotland#The_Universities_(Scotland)_Acts" title="Ancient university governance in Scotland">Universities (Scotland) Act 1858</a>, it being replaced by the Bachelor of Medicine and <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Surgery" title="Master of Surgery">Master of Surgery</a> (MS), with the MD becoming a higher degree as in England. The commissioners under the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_university_governance_in_Scotland#The_Universities_(Scotland)_Acts" title="Ancient university governance in Scotland">Universities (Scotland) Act 1889</a> completed the reform by replacing the MS with the Bachelor of Surgery, the MS joining the MD as a higher degree and the initial professional qualification taking on its modern form as the double <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Medicine,_Bachelor_of_Surgery" title="Bachelor of Medicine, Bachelor of Surgery">Bachelor of Medicine, Bachelor of Surgery</a> degree.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first university in England to offer training in theology for those intending to become priests in the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a> was the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Durham" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Durham">University of Durham</a> in 1833, following the lead of colleges such as <a href="/wiki/St_Bees_Theological_College" title="St Bees Theological College">St Bees Theological College</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_David%27s_College,_Lampeter" class="mw-redirect" title="St David&#39;s College, Lampeter">St David's College, Lampeter</a>. The Licence in Theology could be taken as either a one year graduate course, following on from a BA, or a three year undergraduate course.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Shortly after, in 1837, Durham also became the first British university to teach engineering (although the course closed after a few years), followed only a few months later by <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_London" title="King&#39;s College London">King's College London</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Anglican <a href="/wiki/Theological_college" class="mw-redirect" title="Theological college">theological colleges</a> partnered with local universities to offer professional degrees in theology and ministry during the twentieth century. Since 2014, however, the <a href="/wiki/Common_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Common Award">Common Award</a> degrees, validated by Durham, have offered a more unified training across the theological colleges.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  Some colleges continue to offer other degrees in addition to the Common Awards, such as the Cambridge Bachelor of Theology at the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Theological_Federation" title="Cambridge Theological Federation">Cambridge Theological Federation</a>
</p><p>Legal studies in England were mainly confined to the Inns of Court until the late nineteenth century. The only undergraduate course was at Cambridge and concentrated on Roman <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a> rather than English <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a>; in terms of employment that the bishops accepted it as equivalent to a BA for ordination was more useful than the legal training it provided, and it was generally seen as an easy option for those who couldn't cope with the mathematics on the BA course.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Cambridge reformed its course in 1858, and London established an undergraduate course a few years later. However, it has only been since the 1960s that law schools have taken on a leading role in training lawyers and truly established professional degrees.<sup id="cite_ref-Langbein1996_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Langbein1996-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the latter part of the twentieth century, many chartered bodies introduced educational requirements for their <a href="/wiki/Chartered_(professional)" title="Chartered (professional)">chartered professional</a> statuses, most notably the <a href="/wiki/Engineering_Council" title="Engineering Council">Engineering Council</a> requirements for <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Engineer_(UK)" title="Chartered Engineer (UK)">Chartered Engineer</a>. This led to the accreditation of degrees by the relevant professional bodies and, in the case of engineering, to the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Accord" title="Washington Accord">Washington Accord</a> – an international agreement between engineering regulatory bodies to recognise professional degrees accredited in each country – signed originally in 1989 by the UK, US, Australia, Canada, Ireland and New Zealand, and since expanded to include many other countries.<sup id="cite_ref-Washington_Accord_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Washington_Accord-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In the twenty-first century, the standard professional degree for many science and engineering fields was raised from bachelor's to master's level, including for qualification as a <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Physicist" title="Chartered Physicist">Chartered Physicist</a> (from 2001), <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Scientist" title="Chartered Scientist">Chartered Scientist</a> (from 2004) and Chartered Engineer (from 2012).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_professional_degrees_in_North_America">History of professional degrees in North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professional_degree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History of professional degrees in North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The M.B. or Bachelor of Medicine was the first medical degree to be granted in the United States and Canada. The first medical schools that granted the M.B. degree were at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pennsylvania_School_of_Medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Pennsylvania School of Medicine">University of Pennsylvania</a> and <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University_College_of_Physicians_and_Surgeons" title="Columbia University College of Physicians and Surgeons">Columbia University</a>. Columbia University was the first American university to grant the M.D. degree in 1770, although, as in England, this followed the M.B. (which was the qualifying degree) and required completion of a thesis.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  Professional societies started licensing doctors from the 1760s, and in the early nineteenth century started setting up their own medical schools, known as  proprietary medical colleges, the first being the medical college of the Medical Society of the County of New York, which opened March 12, 1807. These eliminated the general education and long lecture terms of the university schools, making them much more popular. Without effective regulation, abuses arose, and national conventions in 1846 and 1847 led to the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/American_Medical_Association" title="American Medical Association">American Medical Association</a>. This new body set the first nationwide standards for M.D. degrees, requiring that students had a liberal education in arts and sciences as part of their degree, that they had served an apprenticeship before starting the course, and that the course lasted three years.<sup id="cite_ref-Medline_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Medline-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The M.D. was thus the first entry-level professional degree to be awarded as a purely trade school 'doctor'-degree in the United States, before the first European-style doctorate, the Ph.D., was awarded by an American Institution in 1861,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> although the M.D. was not established as a post-baccalaureate degree until much later.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The President of <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Twining_Hadley" title="Arthur Twining Hadley">Arthur Twining Hadley</a>, stated in the early 20th century that: "However convenient it might be to insist on the possession of a bachelor's degree by all pupils in the schools of law or medicine, I feel that it would be a violation of our duty to these professions to hedge ourselves about by any such artificial limitations."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> This changed (for medicine) after <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Flexner" title="Abraham Flexner">Abraham Flexner</a>'s damning <a href="/wiki/Flexner_report" class="mw-redirect" title="Flexner report">report</a> into the state of medical education in 1910: by 1930 almost all medical schools required a previous liberal arts degree before starting the M.D. course.<sup id="cite_ref-Medline_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Medline-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Law degrees were introduced in the US by the <a href="/wiki/William_%26_Mary_Law_School" title="William &amp; Mary Law School">College of William &amp; Mary</a> in 1792, with its "Batchellor of Law" (sic) (L.B.) degree. This was followed by the "Graduate of Law" at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Virginia_School_of_Law" title="University of Virginia School of Law">University of Virginia</a> in 1829, which became the first American LL.B. in 1840. The J.D. was introduced by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago_Law_School" title="University of Chicago Law School">University of Chicago</a> in 1902, with the same curriculum as the LL.B. but requiring a previous B.A. or B.S. for entry. The J.D. spread, but encountered opposition, and <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_School" title="Harvard Law School">Harvard</a>, which imposed graduate entry as a requirement for its LL.B. course in 1909, and <a href="/wiki/Yale_Law_School" title="Yale Law School">Yale</a> used the name for their post-LL.B. degree, elsewhere called the LL.M. By the 1930s, when most law schools had shifted to graduate entry, the standard degree was once again the LL.B. The second shift to the J.D., again without a change of curriculum, came in the 1960s, with all <a href="/wiki/American_Bar_Association" title="American Bar Association">American Bar Association</a>-accredited professional degrees adopting the nomenclature by 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the late twentieth and early twenty-first century, other professions, particularly in clinical fields, transitioned their professional degrees to doctorates, following the example of the M.D. and J.D. The Master of Public Health (M.P.H.degree) and the Doctor of Public Health (Dr.P.H.) are multi-disciplinary professional degrees awarded for studies in areas related to public health. The MPH degree focuses on public health practice, as opposed to research or teaching. In the 1990s there was also some debate in the architectural community about renaming the professional degree in architecture a "doctorate".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The spread of professional doctorates raised concerns about the standards of the new degrees, particularly in cases such as physical therapy, where the standard set by the <a href="/wiki/American_Physical_Therapy_Association" title="American Physical Therapy Association">American Physical Therapy Association</a> for the doctorate is the same as that for the master's degree. Critics have claimed that these degrees should not be called doctorates, pointing out that a Ph.D. takes an average of twelve years from the start of college, compared to five and a half to eight years for professional doctorates, while defenders of the new professional doctorates have said the point of comparison should be the M.D. and J.D., not the Ph.D.<sup id="cite_ref-creep_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-creep-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_degrees_by_country">Professional degrees by country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professional_degree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Professional degrees by country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professional_degree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Among the professional degrees in the United States, one particular form was the graduate-entry first-professional degree, often denominated as a doctorate. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Education" title="United States Department of Education">U.S. Department of Education</a> defined these as: "A first-professional degree was an award that required completion of a program that met all of the following criteria: (1) completion of the academic requirements to begin practice in the profession; (2) at least 2 years of college work prior to entering the program; and (3) a total of at least 6 academic years of college work to complete the degree program, including prior required college work plus the length of the professional program itself."<sup id="cite_ref-NCES_Glossary_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NCES_Glossary-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The use of the term "first-professional" was discontinued by the Department of Education as of 2010-11, when new post-baccalaureate award categories were introduced.<sup id="cite_ref-IPEDS_reclassification_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPEDS_reclassification-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Prior to this, first-professional degrees (so defined) were awarded in the following ten fields:<sup id="cite_ref-NCES_Glossary_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NCES_Glossary-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Chiropractic (D.C., D.C.M.)</li>
<li>Dentistry (D.D.S., D.M.D.)</li>
<li>Law (LL.B., J.D.)</li>
<li>Medicine (M.D.)</li>
<li>Optometry (O.D.)</li>
<li>Osteopathic Medicine (D.O.)</li>
<li>Pharmacy (Pharm.D.)</li>
<li>Podiatry (D.P.M., D.P., Pod.D.)</li>
<li>Theology (M.Div., M.H.L., B.D., ordination)</li>
<li>Veterinary Medicine (D.V.M., V.M.D.)</li></ul>
<p>Since 2011, the classification "doctor's degree - professional practice" has been used for "[a] doctor's degree that is conferred upon completion of a program providing the knowledge and skills for the recognition, credential, or license required for professional practice." As with the "first professional degree", this classification also requires that the total time in higher education is at least six years, although the requirement for at least two years of college-level study prior to entering the program was removed.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The Department of Education does not define which fields professional doctorates may be awarded in, unlike with the "first professional degree". Besides professional doctorates, other professional degrees can exist that use the title of bachelor or master, e.g. B.Arch. and M.Arch. in architecture.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> In particular, first professional degrees in theology, which did not use the title of doctor, were reclassified as master's degrees in 2011 - including the B.D.<sup id="cite_ref-IPEDS_reclassification_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IPEDS_reclassification-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A distinction is drawn in the US between professional doctorates and "doctor's degree - research/scholarship", with the latter being "[a] Ph.D. or other doctor's degree that requires advanced work beyond the master's level, including the preparation and defense of a dissertation based on original research, or the planning and execution of an original project demonstrating substantial artistic or scholarly achievement."<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Internationally, US professional doctorates (which, unlike research doctorates, are not defined as requiring work beyond the master's level) are not generally considered to be doctoral level qualifications.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The classification of "Doctor's degree - other" also exists for doctorates that do not meet the definition of either professional doctorates or research doctorates.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/US_Census_Bureau" class="mw-redirect" title="US Census Bureau">US Census Bureau</a> uses the classification "professional degree behind bachelor's degree" as one of the possible answers to “What is the highest degree or level of school this person has completed?” in the <a href="/wiki/American_Community_Survey" title="American Community Survey">American Community Survey</a>, with examples of MD, DDS, DVM, LLB and JD. This sits between "master's degree" and "doctorate degree" (with the examples for a doctorate being PhD and EdD).<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some professional fields offer degrees beyond the professional doctorate or other degree required for qualification, sometimes termed post-professional degrees. Higher professional degrees may also be offered in fields that do not have specific academic requirements for entry, such as Fine Arts. These degrees may be at master's or doctorate levels.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professional_degree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Professional degrees in the UK are accredited by professional, statutory and regulatory bodies, which work with the <a href="/wiki/Quality_Assurance_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Quality Assurance Agency">Quality Assurance Agency</a> on defining benchmark statements for their subjects.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> Specific benchmark statements have also been produced for professional qualifications in Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many professional degrees span teaching at bachelor's and master's level, leading to a master's level award. This includes older degrees that retain the names of bachelor's degrees for historic reasons, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Medicine,_Bachelor_of_Surgery" title="Bachelor of Medicine, Bachelor of Surgery">Bachelor of Medicine, Bachelor of Surgery</a> (MBBS, MBChB, etc.), <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Dental_Surgery" class="mw-redirect" title="Bachelor of Dental Surgery">Bachelor of Dental Surgery</a> (BDS) and <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Veterinary_Science" title="Bachelor of Veterinary Science">Bachelor of Veterinary Science</a> (BVS), and newer integrated master's degrees such as the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Engineering" title="Master of Engineering">Master of Engineering</a> (MEng) or <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Pharmacy" title="Master of Pharmacy">Master of Pharmacy</a> (MPharm).<sup id="cite_ref-QAA_Master&#39;s_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QAA_Master&#39;s-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-targetjobs_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-targetjobs-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> In some subjects, qualification can be via separate bachelor's and master's degrees, e.g. a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Engineering" title="Bachelor of Engineering">Bachelor of Engineering</a> (BEng) followed by a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">Master of Science</a> (MSc) in Engineering,<sup id="cite_ref-targetjobs_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-targetjobs-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> or a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">Bachelor of Arts</a> (BA) or <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">Bachelor of Science</a> (BSc) in Architecture followed by a year of professional experience, then a two-year <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Architecture" title="Master of Architecture">Master of Architecture</a> (MArch).<sup id="cite_ref-RIBA_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIBA-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> In some subjects the normal professional degree is a bachelor's degree, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Laws" title="Bachelor of Laws">Bachelor of Laws</a> (LLB) or BA in Law (for both <a href="/wiki/Solicitors" class="mw-redirect" title="Solicitors">solicitors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barristers" class="mw-redirect" title="Barristers">barristers</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> or a BSc in Surveying.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Some professional bodies also offer different levels of professional recognition, e.g. a master's degree is needed for <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Engineer_(UK)" title="Chartered Engineer (UK)">Chartered Engineers</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Scientist" title="Chartered Scientist">Chartered Scientists</a> but a bachelor's degree for <a href="/wiki/Incorporated_Engineer" class="mw-redirect" title="Incorporated Engineer">Incorporated Engineers</a> and a bachelor's or <a href="/wiki/Foundation_degree" title="Foundation degree">foundation degree</a> for <a href="/wiki/Registered_Scientist" title="Registered Scientist">Registered Scientists</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-targetjobs_53-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-targetjobs-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is common for professional qualification in the UK to require professional experience in addition to academic qualification. For Architecture, the standard route has a year of experience between the bachelor's and master's stages and a further year after the master's before the final examination;<sup id="cite_ref-RIBA_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIBA-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> becoming a Chartered Engineer requires post-degree <a href="/wiki/Initial_Professional_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="Initial Professional Development">Initial Professional Development</a> that typically takes four to six years;<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> becoming a <a href="/wiki/General_Practitioner" class="mw-redirect" title="General Practitioner">General Practitioner</a> requires five years of study beyond the MBBS, while qualifying as a <a href="/wiki/Consultant_(medicine)" title="Consultant (medicine)">Consultant</a> takes seven to nine more years.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to initial professional degrees, some professional master's degrees and most professional doctorates, e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Business_Administration" title="Master of Business Administration">Master of Business Administration</a> (MBA), <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Education" title="Doctor of Education">Doctor of Education</a> (EdD) and <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Engineering" title="Doctor of Engineering">Doctor of Engineering</a> (EngD), are offered for those already established in professions. UK professional doctorates are research degrees at the same level as PhDs, normally including teaching at doctoral level but still assessed by a doctoral research thesis or equivalent.<sup id="cite_ref-QAA_Master&#39;s_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QAA_Master&#39;s-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some professional degrees are designed specifically for trainees or members within a particular organisation, rather than being available via general enrolment. Examples of these include the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Common_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Common Award">Common Awards</a> with <a href="/wiki/Durham_University" title="Durham University">Durham University</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Chartered_Certified_Accountants" title="Association of Chartered Certified Accountants">Association of Chartered Certified Accountants</a>' BSc in Applied Accounting with <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Brookes_University" title="Oxford Brookes University">Oxford Brookes University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_equivalence">International equivalence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professional_degree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: International equivalence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_medicine">In medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professional_degree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Medical_education" title="Medical education">Medical education</a></div>
<p>In medicine, individual countries specify rules for recognising foreign qualifications; in the US, for example, this is carried out by the <a href="/wiki/Educational_Commission_for_Foreign_Medical_Graduates" title="Educational Commission for Foreign Medical Graduates">Educational Commission for Foreign Medical Graduates</a> (ECFMG) and in the UK by the <a href="/wiki/General_Medical_Council" title="General Medical Council">General Medical Council</a> (GMC).<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Australian_Medical_Council" title="Australian Medical Council">Australian Medical Council</a>, US ECFMG, UK GMC, <a href="/wiki/Medical_Council_of_Canada" title="Medical Council of Canada">Medical Council of Canada</a>, Danish Health and Medicines Authority and Korean Institute of Medical Education and Evaluation jointly sponsor the <a href="/wiki/World_Directory_of_Medical_Schools" title="World Directory of Medical Schools">World Directory of Medical Schools</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> At least one state in the US, <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>, permits foreign graduates to use the title "MD" if licensed to practice in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_engineering">In engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Professional_degree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Regulation_and_licensure_in_engineering" title="Regulation and licensure in engineering">Regulation and licensure in engineering</a></div>
<p>In engineering, the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Accord" title="Washington Accord">Washington Accord</a> (1989) recognised that the academic training (i.e. professional degrees) for full professional status (<a href="/wiki/Professional_Engineer" class="mw-redirect" title="Professional Engineer">Professional Engineer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Engineer_(UK)" title="Chartered Engineer (UK)">Chartered Engineer</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_Engineer" title="European Engineer">European Engineer</a> etc.) is equivalent in the signatory countries.<sup id="cite_ref-Washington_Accord_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Washington_Accord-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Similarly the <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Accord" title="Sydney Accord">Sydney Accord</a> (2001) recognises similar academic training between signatories for <a href="/wiki/Engineering_Technologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Engineering Technologist">Engineering Technologists</a>, <a href="/wiki/Incorporated_Engineer" class="mw-redirect" title="Incorporated Engineer">Incorporated Engineers</a>, etc. and the <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Accord" title="Dublin Accord">Dublin Accord</a> (2002) for <a href="/wiki/Engineering_Technician" class="mw-redirect" title="Engineering Technician">Engineering Technicians</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> For computing and information technology, the <a href="/wiki/Seoul_Accord" title="Seoul Accord">Seoul Accord</a> (2008) recognises similar academic training on accredited courses for computing and information technology professionals in the signatory countries.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
