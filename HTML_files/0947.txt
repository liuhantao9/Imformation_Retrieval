<p><b>Czechoslovakia</b>, or <b>Czecho-Slovakia</b><sup id="cite_ref-covenant_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-covenant-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="/tʃ/: &#39;ch&#39; in &#39;China&#39;">tʃ</span><span title="/ɛ/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">ɛ</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/oʊ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;code&#39;">oʊ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/oʊ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;code&#39;">oʊ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;v&#39; in &#39;vie&#39;">v</span><span title="/æ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">æ</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/i/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;happy&#39;">i</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span></span>-, <wbr />-<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;v&#39; in &#39;vie&#39;">v</span><span title="/ɑː/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;father&#39;">ɑː</span></span>-/</a></span></span>;<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">Slovak</a>: <i lang="sk">Československo</i>, <i>Česko-Slovensko</i><sup id="cite_ref-KulturaSlova_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KulturaSlova-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>), was a <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign state</a> in <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central Europe</a> that existed from October 1918, when it declared its independence from the <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austro-Hungarian Empire</a>, until <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Dissolution of Czechoslovakia">its peaceful dissolution</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a> on 1 January 1993.
</p><p>From 1939 to 1945, following its forced division and partial incorporation into <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>, the state did not <i>de facto</i> exist but <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovak_government-in-exile" title="Czechoslovak government-in-exile">its government-in-exile</a> continued to operate.
</p><p>From 1948 to 1990, Czechoslovakia was part of the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Bloc" title="Eastern Bloc">Eastern Bloc</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Command_economy" class="mw-redirect" title="Command economy">command economy</a>. Its economic status was formalized in membership of <a href="/wiki/Comecon" title="Comecon">Comecon</a> from 1949 and its defense status in the <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact</a> of May 1955. A period of political liberalization in 1968, known as the <a href="/wiki/Prague_Spring" title="Prague Spring">Prague Spring</a>, was forcibly ended when the Soviet Union, assisted by several other Warsaw Pact countries, <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact_invasion_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Warsaw Pact invasion of Czechoslovakia">invaded</a> Czechoslovakia. In 1989, as <a href="/wiki/Marxist%E2%80%93Leninist" class="mw-redirect" title="Marxist–Leninist">Marxist–Leninist</a> governments and <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialism</a> were <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1989" title="Revolutions of 1989">ending</a> all over Europe, Czechoslovaks peacefully deposed their government in the <a href="/wiki/Velvet_Revolution" title="Velvet Revolution">Velvet Revolution</a>; state price controls were removed after a period of preparation. In 1993, Czechoslovakia split into the two sovereign states of the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl><dt>Form of state</dt></dl>
<ul><li>1918 – 1938: A <a href="/wiki/Democratic_republic" title="Democratic republic">democratic republic</a> championed by <a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1%C5%A1_Masaryk" class="mw-redirect" title="Tomáš Masaryk">Tomáš Masaryk</a>.</li>
<li>1938 – 1939: After annexation of <a href="/wiki/Sudetenland" title="Sudetenland">Sudetenland</a> by <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> in 1938, the region gradually turned into a state with loosened connections among the Czech, Slovak, and Ruthenian parts. A large strip of southern Slovakia and <a href="/wiki/Carpatho-Ukraine" title="Carpatho-Ukraine">Carpatho-Ukraine</a> was annexed by Hungary, and the <a href="/wiki/Teschen_conflict" class="mw-redirect" title="Teschen conflict">Zaolzie region</a> was annexed by Poland.</li>
<li>1939 – 1945: The region was split into the <b><a href="/wiki/Protectorate_of_Bohemia_and_Moravia" title="Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia">Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia</a></b> and the <b><a href="/wiki/Slovak_Republic_(1939%E2%80%931945)" title="Slovak Republic (1939–1945)">Slovak Republic</a></b>. A <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovak_Government-in-Exile" class="mw-redirect" title="Czechoslovak Government-in-Exile">government-in-exile</a> continued to exist in London, supported by the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and their <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allies</a>; after the <a href="/wiki/German_invasion_of_Russia" class="mw-redirect" title="German invasion of Russia">German invasion of Russia</a>, it was also recognized by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. Czechoslovakia adhered to the <a href="/wiki/Declaration_by_United_Nations" title="Declaration by United Nations">Declaration by United Nations</a> and was a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>.</li>
<li>1946 – 1948: The country was governed by a <a href="/wiki/National_Front_(Czechoslovakia)" title="National Front (Czechoslovakia)">coalition government</a> with <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Communist Party of Czechoslovakia">communist</a> ministers, including the prime minister and the minister of interior. <a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Ruthenia" title="Carpathian Ruthenia">Carpathian Ruthenia</a> was ceded to the Soviet Union.</li>
<li>1948 – 1989: The country became a <a href="/wiki/Marxist-Leninist_state" class="mw-redirect" title="Marxist-Leninist state">Marxist-Leninist state</a> under <a href="/wiki/Soviet_sphere_of_influence" class="mw-redirect" title="Soviet sphere of influence">Soviet domination</a> with a <a href="/wiki/Command_economy" class="mw-redirect" title="Command economy">command economy</a>. In 1960, the country officially became a socialist republic, the <b><a href="/wiki/Czechoslovak_Socialist_Republic" title="Czechoslovak Socialist Republic">Czechoslovak Socialist Republic</a></b>. It was a <a href="/wiki/Satellite_state" title="Satellite state">satellite state</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>.</li>
<li>1969 – 1990: The <a href="/wiki/Federal_republic" title="Federal republic">federal republic</a> consisted of the <b><a href="/wiki/Czech_Socialist_Republic" title="Czech Socialist Republic">Czech Socialist Republic</a></b> and the <b><a href="/wiki/Slovak_Socialist_Republic" title="Slovak Socialist Republic">Slovak Socialist Republic</a></b>.</li>
<li>1990 – 1992: Following the <a href="/wiki/Velvet_Revolution" title="Velvet Revolution">Velvet Revolution</a>, the state was renamed the <b><a href="/wiki/Czech_and_Slovak_Federal_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="Czech and Slovak Federal Republic">Czech and Slovak Federal Republic</a></b>, consisting of the Czech Republic and the Slovak Republic (Slovakia), and reverted to a democratic republic.</li></ul>
<dl><dt>Neighbours</dt></dl>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> 1918 – 1938, 1945 – 1992</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> (Both predecessors, <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/East_Germany" title="East Germany">East Germany</a>, were neighbors between 1949 and 1990.)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hungarian_People%27s_Republic" title="Hungarian People&#39;s Republic">Hungary</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_Poland" class="mw-redirect" title="People&#39;s Republic of Poland">Poland</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Romania" title="Kingdom of Romania">Romania</a> 1918 – 1938</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> 1945 – 1991</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a> 1991 – 1992 (<a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic">Soviet Union member</a> until 1991)</li></ul>
<dl><dt>Topography</dt></dl>
<p>The country was of generally irregular terrain. The western area was part of the north-central European uplands. The eastern region was composed of the northern reaches of the <a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Mountains" title="Carpathian Mountains">Carpathian Mountains</a> and lands of the <a href="/wiki/Danube_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Danube River">Danube River</a> basin.
</p>
<dl><dt>Climate</dt></dl>
<p>The weather is mild winters and mild summers. Influenced by the Atlantic Ocean from the west, Baltic Sea from the north, and Mediterranean Sea from the south. There is no continental weather.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Czechoslovakia" title="History of Czechoslovakia">History of Czechoslovakia</a>, <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Czech_lands" title="History of the Czech lands">History of the Czech lands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/History_of_Slovakia" title="History of Slovakia">History of Slovakia</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Origins_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Origins of Czechoslovakia">Origins of Czechoslovakia</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tom%C3%A1%C5%A1_Garrigue_Masaryk_1925.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Tom%C3%A1%C5%A1_Garrigue_Masaryk_1925.PNG/220px-Tom%C3%A1%C5%A1_Garrigue_Masaryk_1925.PNG" decoding="async" width="220" height="282" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Tom%C3%A1%C5%A1_Garrigue_Masaryk_1925.PNG/330px-Tom%C3%A1%C5%A1_Garrigue_Masaryk_1925.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Tom%C3%A1%C5%A1_Garrigue_Masaryk_1925.PNG/440px-Tom%C3%A1%C5%A1_Garrigue_Masaryk_1925.PNG 2x" data-file-width="705" data-file-height="905" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tom%C3%A1%C5%A1_Garrigue_Masaryk_1925.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1%C5%A1_Garrigue_Masaryk" title="Tomáš Garrigue Masaryk">Tomáš Garrigue Masaryk</a>, founder and first president</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Czech_Troops.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Czech_Troops.jpg/220px-Czech_Troops.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="111" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Czech_Troops.jpg/330px-Czech_Troops.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Czech_Troops.jpg/440px-Czech_Troops.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="353" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Czech_Troops.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Czechoslovak troops in Vladivostok (1918)</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:28._%C5%99%C3%ADjen_1918.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/28._%C5%99%C3%ADjen_1918.jpg/220px-28._%C5%99%C3%ADjen_1918.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="201" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/28._%C5%99%C3%ADjen_1918.jpg/330px-28._%C5%99%C3%ADjen_1918.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/28._%C5%99%C3%ADjen_1918.jpg/440px-28._%C5%99%C3%ADjen_1918.jpg 2x" data-file-width="876" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:28._%C5%99%C3%ADjen_1918.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Czechoslovak_declaration_of_independence" title="Czechoslovak declaration of independence">Czechoslovak declaration of independence</a> rally in Prague on Wenceslas Square, 28 October 1918</div></div></div>
<p>The area was long a part of the <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austro-Hungarian Empire</a> until the empire collapsed at the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. The new state was founded by <a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1%C5%A1_Garrigue_Masaryk" title="Tomáš Garrigue Masaryk">Tomáš Garrigue Masaryk</a><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> (1850–1937), who served as its first president from 14 November 1918 to 14 December 1935. He was succeeded by his close ally, <a href="/wiki/Edvard_Bene%C5%A1" title="Edvard Beneš">Edvard Beneš</a> (1884–1948).
</p><p>The roots of Czech nationalism go back to the 19th century, when philologists and educators, influenced by <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">Romanticism</a>, promoted the <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech language</a> and pride in the <a href="/wiki/Czech_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Czech people">Czech people</a>. Nationalism became a mass movement in the second half of the 19th century. Taking advantage of the limited opportunities for participation in political life under Austrian rule, Czech leaders such as historian <a href="/wiki/Franti%C5%A1ek_Palack%C3%BD" title="František Palacký">František Palacký</a> (1798–1876) founded many patriotic, self-help organizations which provided a chance for many of their compatriots to participate in communal life prior to independence. Palacký supported <a href="/wiki/Austro-Slavism" title="Austro-Slavism">Austro-Slavism</a> and worked for a reorganized and federal Austrian Empire, which would protect the Slavic speaking peoples of Central Europe against Russian and German threats.
</p><p>An advocate of democratic reform and Czech autonomy within Austria-Hungary, Masaryk was elected twice to the <i><a href="/wiki/Reichsrat_(Austria)" class="mw-redirect" title="Reichsrat (Austria)">Reichsrat</a></i> (Austrian Parliament), first from 1891 to 1893 for the <a href="/wiki/Young_Czech_Party" title="Young Czech Party">Young Czech Party</a>, and again from 1907 to 1914 for the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Realist_Party" title="Czech Realist Party">Czech Realist Party</a>, which he had founded in 1889 with <a href="/wiki/Karel_Kram%C3%A1%C5%99" title="Karel Kramář">Karel Kramář</a> and <a href="/wiki/Josef_Kaizl" title="Josef Kaizl">Josef Kaizl</a>.
</p><p>During World War I small numbers of Czechs and Slovaks, the <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovak_Legions" class="mw-redirect" title="Czechoslovak Legions">Czechoslovak Legions</a>, fought with the <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I" title="Allies of World War I">Allies</a> in France and Italy, while large numbers deserted to Russia in exchange for its support for the independence of Czechoslovakia from the Austrian Empire.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> With the outbreak of World War I, Masaryk began working for Czech independence in a union with Slovakia. With Edvard Beneš and <a href="/wiki/Milan_Rastislav_%C5%A0tef%C3%A1nik" title="Milan Rastislav Štefánik">Milan Rastislav Štefánik</a>, Masaryk visited several Western countries and won support from influential publicists.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a>, under Austrian rule, were Czech-speaking industrial centres, while Slovakia, which was part of the <a href="/wiki/Transleithania" class="mw-redirect" title="Transleithania">Kingdom of Hungary</a>, was an undeveloped agrarian region. Conditions were much better for the development of a mass national movement in the Czech lands than in Slovakia. Nevertheless, the two regions united and created a new nation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Czechoslovak_Republic">First Czechoslovak Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: First Czechoslovak Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/First_Czechoslovak_Republic" title="First Czechoslovak Republic">First Czechoslovak Republic</a></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Czechoslovakia01.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Czechoslovakia01.png/220px-Czechoslovakia01.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="100" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Czechoslovakia01.png/330px-Czechoslovakia01.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Czechoslovakia01.png/440px-Czechoslovakia01.png 2x" data-file-width="1128" data-file-height="513" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Czechoslovakia01.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Czechoslovakia in 1928</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bohemian_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Bohemian Kingdom">Bohemian Kingdom</a> ceased to exist in 1918 when it was incorporated into Czechoslovakia. Czechoslovakia was founded in October 1918, as one of the successor states of the Austro-Hungarian Empire at the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and as part of the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Saint-Germain-en-Laye_(1919)" title="Treaty of Saint-Germain-en-Laye (1919)">Treaty of Saint-Germain-en-Laye</a>. It consisted of the present day territories of <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a>, Slovakia and <a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Ruthenia" title="Carpathian Ruthenia">Carpathian Ruthenia</a>. Its territory included some of the most industrialized regions of the former Austria-Hungary.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethnicity">Ethnicity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ethnicity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_minorities_in_Czechoslovakia" title="Ethnic minorities in Czechoslovakia">Ethnic minorities in Czechoslovakia</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Czechoslovakia_1930_linguistic_map_-_created_2008-10-30.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Czechoslovakia_1930_linguistic_map_-_created_2008-10-30.svg/220px-Czechoslovakia_1930_linguistic_map_-_created_2008-10-30.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="134" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Czechoslovakia_1930_linguistic_map_-_created_2008-10-30.svg/330px-Czechoslovakia_1930_linguistic_map_-_created_2008-10-30.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Czechoslovakia_1930_linguistic_map_-_created_2008-10-30.svg/440px-Czechoslovakia_1930_linguistic_map_-_created_2008-10-30.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1281" data-file-height="778" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Czechoslovakia_1930_linguistic_map_-_created_2008-10-30.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Linguistic map of Czechoslovakia in 1930</div></div></div>
<p>The new country was a multi-ethnic state, with Czechs and Slovaks as <i>constituent peoples</i>. The population consisted of <a href="/wiki/Czechs" title="Czechs">Czechs</a> (51%), <a href="/wiki/Slovaks" title="Slovaks">Slovaks</a> (16%), <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">Germans</a> (22%), <a href="/wiki/Hungarians" title="Hungarians">Hungarians</a> (5%) and <a href="/wiki/Rusyns" title="Rusyns">Rusyns</a> (4%).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Many of the Germans, Hungarians, Ruthenians and Poles<sup id="cite_ref-pp_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pp-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and some Slovaks, felt oppressed because the political elite did not generally allow political autonomy for minority ethnic groups.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This policy led to unrest among the non-Czech population, particularly in German-speaking <a href="/wiki/Sudetenland" title="Sudetenland">Sudetenland</a>, which initially had proclaimed itself part of the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_German-Austria" title="Republic of German-Austria">Republic of German-Austria</a> in accordance with the self-determination principle.
</p><p>The state proclaimed the official ideology that there were no separate Czech and Slovak nations, but only one nation of Czechoslovaks (see <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakism" title="Czechoslovakism">Czechoslovakism</a>), to the disagreement of Slovaks and other ethnic groups. Once a unified Czechoslovakia was restored after World War II (after the country had been divided during the war), the conflict between the <a href="/wiki/Czechs" title="Czechs">Czechs</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Slovaks" title="Slovaks">Slovaks</a> surfaced again. The governments of Czechoslovakia and other eastern European nations deported ethnic Germans to the West, reducing the presence of minorities in the nation. Most of the Jews had been killed during the war by the Nazis and their allies.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Interwar_period">Interwar period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Interwar period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During the period between the two world wars, democracy thrived in Czechoslovakia. Of all the new states established in central Europe after 1918, only Czechoslovakia preserved a democratic government until the war broke out. Thus, despite regional disparities, its level of development was much higher than that of neighboring states.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The population was generally literate, and contained fewer alienated groups. The influence of these conditions was augmented by the political values of Czechoslovakia's leaders and the policies they adopted. Under <a href="/wiki/Tomas_Masaryk" class="mw-redirect" title="Tomas Masaryk">Tomas Masaryk</a>, Czech and Slovak politicians promoted progressive social and economic conditions that served to defuse discontent.
</p><p>Foreign minister Beneš became the prime architect of the Czechoslovak-Romanian-Yugoslav alliance (the "<a href="/wiki/Little_Entente" title="Little Entente">Little Entente</a>", 1921–38) directed against Hungarian attempts to reclaim lost areas. Beneš worked closely with France. Far more dangerous was the German element, which after 1933 became allied with the Nazis in Germany. The increasing feeling of inferiority among the Slovaks,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> who were hostile to the more numerous Czechs, weakened the country in the late 1930s. Many Slovaks supported an extreme nationalist movement and welcomed the puppet Slovak state set up under Hitler's control in 1939.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>After 1933, Czechoslovakia remained the only democracy in central and eastern Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Munich_Agreement.2C_and_Two-Step_German_Occupation"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Munich_Agreement,_and_Two-Step_German_Occupation">Munich Agreement, and Two-Step German Occupation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Munich Agreement, and Two-Step German Occupation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/German_occupation_of_Czechoslovakia" title="German occupation of Czechoslovakia">German occupation of Czechoslovakia</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Czechoslovakia_1939.SVG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Czechoslovakia_1939.SVG/220px-Czechoslovakia_1939.SVG.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="88" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Czechoslovakia_1939.SVG/330px-Czechoslovakia_1939.SVG.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Czechoslovakia_1939.SVG/440px-Czechoslovakia_1939.SVG.png 2x" data-file-width="1059" data-file-height="426" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Czechoslovakia_1939.SVG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The partition of Czechoslovakia after <a href="/wiki/Munich_Agreement" title="Munich Agreement">Munich Agreement</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-1972-039-44,_Heydrich-Attentat.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-1972-039-44%2C_Heydrich-Attentat.jpg/220px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-1972-039-44%2C_Heydrich-Attentat.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-1972-039-44%2C_Heydrich-Attentat.jpg/330px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-1972-039-44%2C_Heydrich-Attentat.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-1972-039-44%2C_Heydrich-Attentat.jpg/440px-Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-1972-039-44%2C_Heydrich-Attentat.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="510" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_Bild_146-1972-039-44,_Heydrich-Attentat.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The car in which <a href="/wiki/Reinhard_Heydrich" title="Reinhard Heydrich">Reinhard Heydrich</a> was killed</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Czechoslovak_Republic_(1939).svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Czechoslovak_Republic_%281939%29.svg/220px-Czechoslovak_Republic_%281939%29.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="223" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Czechoslovak_Republic_%281939%29.svg/330px-Czechoslovak_Republic_%281939%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Czechoslovak_Republic_%281939%29.svg/440px-Czechoslovak_Republic_%281939%29.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="450" data-file-height="456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Czechoslovak_Republic_(1939).svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Territory of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Czechoslovak_Republic" title="Second Czechoslovak Republic">Second Czechoslovak Republic</a> (1938–1939)</div></div></div>
<p>In September 1938, <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> demanded control of the <a href="/wiki/Sudetenland" title="Sudetenland">Sudetenland</a>. On 29 September 1938, Britain and France ceded control in the <a href="/wiki/Appeasement" title="Appeasement">Appeasement</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Munich_Conference" class="mw-redirect" title="Munich Conference">Munich Conference</a>; France ignored the military alliance it had with Czechoslovakia. During October 1938, <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a> occupied and annexed the Sudetenland border region, effectively crippling Czechoslovak defences.
</p><p>On 15 March 1939, the remainder ("rump") of Czechoslovakia was invaded and divided into the <a href="/wiki/Protectorate_of_Bohemia_and_Moravia" title="Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia">Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia</a> and the puppet <a href="/wiki/Slovak_State" class="mw-redirect" title="Slovak State">Slovak State</a>.
</p><p>Much of Slovakia and all of <a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Ruthenia" title="Carpathian Ruthenia">Carpathian Ruthenia</a> were annexed by Hungary. Poland <a href="/wiki/Teschen_conflict" class="mw-redirect" title="Teschen conflict">occupied</a> Zaolzie, an area whose population was majority Polish, in October 1938.
</p><p>The eventual goal of the German state under Nazi leadership was to eradicate Czech nationality through assimilation, deportation, and extermination of the Czech intelligentsia; the intellectual elites and middle class made up a considerable number of the 200,000 people who passed through concentration camps and the 250,000 who died during German occupation.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Under <a href="/wiki/Generalplan_Ost" title="Generalplan Ost">Generalplan Ost</a>, it was assumed that around 50% Czechs would be fit for <a href="/wiki/Germanization" class="mw-redirect" title="Germanization">Germanization</a>. The Czech intellectual elites were to be removed not only from Czech territories but from Europe completely. The authors of Generalplan Ost believed it would be best if they emigrated overseas, as even in <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">Siberia</a> they were considered a threat to German rule. Just like Jews, Poles, Serbs, and several other nations, Czechs were considered to be <a href="/wiki/Untermenschen" class="mw-redirect" title="Untermenschen">untermenschen</a> by the Nazi state.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In 1940, in a secret Nazi plan for the Germanization of the Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia it was declared that those considered to be of racially Mongoloid origin and the Czech intelligentsia were not to be Germanized.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The deportation of Jews to concentration camps was organized under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Reinhard_Heydrich" title="Reinhard Heydrich">Reinhard Heydrich</a>, and the fortress town of <a href="/wiki/Theresienstadt_concentration_camp" class="mw-redirect" title="Theresienstadt concentration camp">Terezín</a> was made into a ghetto way station for Jewish families. On 4 June 1942 Heydrich died after being wounded by an assassin in <a href="/wiki/Operation_Anthropoid" title="Operation Anthropoid">Operation Anthropoid</a>. Heydrich's successor, Colonel General <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Daluege" title="Kurt Daluege">Kurt Daluege</a>, ordered mass arrests and executions and the destruction of the villages of <a href="/wiki/Lidice" title="Lidice">Lidice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Le%C5%BE%C3%A1ky" title="Ležáky">Ležáky</a>. In 1943 the German war effort was accelerated. Under the authority of <a href="/wiki/Karl_Hermann_Frank" title="Karl Hermann Frank">Karl Hermann Frank</a>, German minister of state for Bohemia and Moravia, some 350,000 Czech laborers were dispatched to the Reich. Within the protectorate, all non-war-related industry was prohibited. Most of the Czech population obeyed quiescently up until the final months preceding the end of the war, while thousands were involved in the <a href="/wiki/Resistance_movement" title="Resistance movement">resistance movement</a>.
</p><p>For the Czechs of the Protectorate Bohemia and Moravia, <a href="/wiki/Occupation_of_Czechoslovakia" class="mw-redirect" title="Occupation of Czechoslovakia">German occupation</a> was a period of brutal oppression. Czech losses resulting from political persecution and deaths in concentration camps totaled between 36,000 and 55,000. The Jewish population of <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a> (118,000 according to the 1930 census) was virtually annihilated. Many Jews emigrated after 1939; more than 70,000 were killed; 8,000 survived at Terezín. Several thousand Jews managed to live in freedom or in hiding throughout the occupation.
</p><p>Despite the estimated 136,000 deaths at the hands of the Nazi regime, the population in the Reichsprotektorate saw a net increase during the war years of approximately 250,000 in line with an increased birth rate.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 6 May 1945, the third US Army of General Patton entered Pilsen from the south west. On 9 May 1945, Soviet Red Army troops entered Prague.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Socialist_Czechoslovakia">Socialist Czechoslovakia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Socialist Czechoslovakia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Czechoslovakia_(1948%E2%80%931989)" class="mw-redirect" title="History of Czechoslovakia (1948–1989)">History of Czechoslovakia (1948–1989)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovak_Socialist_Republic" title="Czechoslovak Socialist Republic">Czechoslovak Socialist Republic</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Coat_of_arms_of_Czechoslovakia_(1961-1989).svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Coat_of_arms_of_Czechoslovakia_%281961-1989%29.svg/170px-Coat_of_arms_of_Czechoslovakia_%281961-1989%29.svg.png" decoding="async" width="170" height="243" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Coat_of_arms_of_Czechoslovakia_%281961-1989%29.svg/255px-Coat_of_arms_of_Czechoslovakia_%281961-1989%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Coat_of_arms_of_Czechoslovakia_%281961-1989%29.svg/340px-Coat_of_arms_of_Czechoslovakia_%281961-1989%29.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="696" data-file-height="996" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Coat_of_arms_of_Czechoslovakia_(1961-1989).svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Socialist <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Coat of arms of Czechoslovakia">coat of arms</a> in 1960–1990</div></div></div>
<p>After World War II, pre-war Czechoslovakia was re-established, with the exception of Sub<a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Ruthenia" title="Carpathian Ruthenia">carpathian Ruthenia</a>, which was annexed by the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic">Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bene%C5%A1_decrees" title="Beneš decrees">Beneš decrees</a> were promulgated concerning ethnic Germans (see <a href="/wiki/Potsdam_Agreement" title="Potsdam Agreement">Potsdam Agreement</a>) and ethnic Hungarians. Under the decrees, <a href="/wiki/Citizenship" title="Citizenship">citizenship</a> was abrogated for people of German and Hungarian <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_origin" title="Ethnic origin">ethnic origin</a> who had accepted German or Hungarian citizenship during the occupations. In 1948, this provision was cancelled for the Hungarians, but only partially for the Germans. The government then confiscated the property of the Germans and <a href="/wiki/Expulsion_of_Germans_after_World_War_II" class="mw-redirect" title="Expulsion of Germans after World War II">expelled about 90% of the ethnic German population</a>, over 2 million people. Those who remained were <a href="/wiki/Collective_accountability" class="mw-redirect" title="Collective accountability">collectively accused</a> of supporting the Nazis after the <a href="/wiki/Munich_Agreement" title="Munich Agreement">Munich Agreement</a>, as 97.32% of Sudeten Germans had voted for the <a href="/wiki/NSDAP" class="mw-redirect" title="NSDAP">NSDAP</a> in the December 1938 elections. Almost every decree explicitly stated that the sanctions did not apply to antifascists. Some 250,000 Germans, many married to Czechs, some antifascists, and also those required for the post-war reconstruction of the country, remained in Czechoslovakia. The Beneš Decrees still cause controversy among nationalist groups in the Czech Republic, Germany, Austria and Hungary.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Spartaki%C3%A1da_-_1960.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Spartaki%C3%A1da_-_1960.JPG/220px-Spartaki%C3%A1da_-_1960.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="102" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Spartaki%C3%A1da_-_1960.JPG/330px-Spartaki%C3%A1da_-_1960.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Spartaki%C3%A1da_-_1960.JPG/440px-Spartaki%C3%A1da_-_1960.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1236" data-file-height="573" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Spartaki%C3%A1da_-_1960.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Spartakiad_(Czechoslovakia)" title="Spartakiad (Czechoslovakia)">Spartakiad</a></i> in 1960</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Carpathian_Ruthenia" title="Carpathian Ruthenia">Carpathian Ruthenia</a> (Podkarpatská Rus) was occupied by (and in June 1945 formally ceded to) the Soviet Union. In the 1946 parliamentary election, the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Communist Party of Czechoslovakia">Communist Party of Czechoslovakia</a> was the winner in the <a href="/wiki/Czech_lands" title="Czech lands">Czech lands</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(Slovakia,_1944)" title="Democratic Party (Slovakia, 1944)">Democratic Party</a> won in Slovakia. In February 1948 the Communists seized power. Although they would maintain the fiction of political pluralism through the existence of the <a href="/wiki/National_Front_(Czechoslovakia)" title="National Front (Czechoslovakia)">National Front</a>, except for a short period in the late 1960s (the <a href="/wiki/Prague_Spring" title="Prague Spring">Prague Spring</a>) the country had no <a href="/wiki/Liberal_democracy" title="Liberal democracy">liberal democracy</a>. Since citizens lacked significant electoral methods of registering protest against government policies, periodically there were street protests that became violent. For example, there were riots in the town of <a href="/wiki/Plze%C5%88_uprising_of_1953" title="Plzeň uprising of 1953">Plzeň in 1953</a>, reflecting economic discontent. Police and army units put down the rebellion, and hundreds were injured but no one was killed. While its economy remained more advanced than those of its neighbors in Eastern Europe, Czechoslovakia grew increasingly economically weak relative to Western Europe.
</p><p>The currency reform of 1953 caused dissatisfaction among Czechoslovakian laborers.  Prior to World War II, the Czech purchasing power surpassed that of the Soviet Union by 115-144%.  This disparity was noted after Czechoslovakia came under the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Bloc" class="mw-redirect" title="Soviet Bloc">Soviet Bloc</a>. To equalize the wage rate, Czechoslovakians had to turn in their old money for new at a decreased value. This lowered the real value of wages by about 11%.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The banks also confiscated savings and bank deposits to control the amount of money in circulation.  The economy continued to suffer as production achievements of bituminous coal was less than anticipated.  Bituminous coal powered 85% of Czechoslovakia's economy.  Because of low production, coal was utilized in industry only.  Pre-war years, consumers used both coal and lignite for fuel, however due to low production, coal was for industrial use only which meant the consumer was only able to utilize lignite.  In 1929, a typical family of four consumed approximately 2.34 tons of lignite but, by 1953, this same family unit was only allowed to use 1.6-1.8 tons per year.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Czechoslovakia.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Czechoslovakia.png/220px-Czechoslovakia.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="117" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Czechoslovakia.png/330px-Czechoslovakia.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Czechoslovakia.png/440px-Czechoslovakia.png 2x" data-file-width="1221" data-file-height="652" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Czechoslovakia.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Czechoslovakia after 1969</div></div></div>
<p>In 1968, when the reformer <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Dub%C4%8Dek" title="Alexander Dubček">Alexander Dubček</a> was appointed to the key post of First Secretary of the Czechoslovak Communist Party, there was a brief period of liberalization known as the <a href="/wiki/Prague_Spring" title="Prague Spring">Prague Spring</a>. In response, after failing to persuade the Czechoslovak leaders to change course, five other <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact_invasion_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Warsaw Pact invasion of Czechoslovakia">members of the Warsaw Pact invaded</a>. Soviet tanks rolled into Czechoslovakia on the night of 20–21 August 1968.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Communist_Party_General_Secretary" class="mw-redirect" title="Soviet Communist Party General Secretary">Soviet Communist Party General Secretary</a> <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Brezhnev" title="Leonid Brezhnev">Leonid Brezhnev</a> viewed this intervention as vital for the preservation of the Soviet, socialist system and vowed to intervene in any state that sought to replace <a href="/wiki/Marxism-Leninism" class="mw-redirect" title="Marxism-Leninism">Marxism-Leninism</a> with <a href="/wiki/Capitalism_(Marxism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Capitalism (Marxism)">capitalism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In the week after the invasion there was a spontaneous campaign of <a href="/wiki/Civil_resistance" title="Civil resistance">civil resistance</a> against the occupation. This resistance involved a wide range of acts of non-cooperation and defiance: this was followed by a period in which the Czechoslovak Communist Party leadership, having been forced in Moscow to make concessions to the Soviet Union, gradually put the brakes on their earlier liberal policies.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> In April 1969 Dubček was finally dismissed from the First Secretaryship of the Czechoslovak Communist Party. Meanwhile, one plank of the reform program had been carried out: in 1968-69, Czechoslovakia was turned into a federation of the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Socialist_Republic" title="Czech Socialist Republic">Czech Socialist Republic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slovak_Socialist_Republic" title="Slovak Socialist Republic">Slovak Socialist Republic</a>. The theory was that under the federation, social and economic inequities between the Czech and Slovak halves of the state would be largely eliminated. A number of ministries, such as education, now became two formally equal bodies in the two formally equal republics. However, the centralised political control by the Czechoslovak Communist Party severely limited the effects of federalization.
</p><p>The 1970s saw the rise of the dissident movement in Czechoslovakia, represented among others by <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A1clav_Havel" title="Václav Havel">Václav Havel</a>. The movement sought greater political participation and expression in the face of official disapproval, manifested in limitations on work activities, which went as far as a ban on professional employment, the refusal of higher education for the dissidents' children, police harassment and prison.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="After_1989">After 1989</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: After 1989">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Czechoslovakia_(1989%E2%80%931992)" class="mw-redirect" title="History of Czechoslovakia (1989–1992)">History of Czechoslovakia (1989–1992)</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Foundation_of_the_Visegr%C3%A1d_Group.tiff" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Foundation_of_the_Visegr%C3%A1d_Group.tiff/lossy-page1-220px-Foundation_of_the_Visegr%C3%A1d_Group.tiff.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="116" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Foundation_of_the_Visegr%C3%A1d_Group.tiff/lossy-page1-330px-Foundation_of_the_Visegr%C3%A1d_Group.tiff.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Foundation_of_the_Visegr%C3%A1d_Group.tiff/lossy-page1-440px-Foundation_of_the_Visegr%C3%A1d_Group.tiff.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1020" data-file-height="540" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Foundation_of_the_Visegr%C3%A1d_Group.tiff" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Visegr%C3%A1d_Group" title="Visegrád Group">Visegrád Group</a> signing ceremony in February 1991</div></div></div>
<p>In 1989, the <a href="/wiki/Velvet_Revolution" title="Velvet Revolution">Velvet Revolution</a> restored <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">democracy</a>. This occurred at around the same time as the fall of communism in Romania, Bulgaria, Hungary and Poland.
</p><p>The word "socialist" was removed from the country's full name on 29 March 1990 and replaced by "federal".
</p><p>In 1992, because of growing <a href="/wiki/Nationalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Nationalist">nationalist</a> tensions in the government, Czechoslovakia was <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Dissolution of Czechoslovakia">peacefully dissolved</a> by parliament. On 1 January 1993 it formally separated into two independent countries, the Czech Republic and the Slovak Republic.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_and_politics">Government and politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Government and politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Czechoslovakia_(1918%E2%80%931938)" title="History of Czechoslovakia (1918–1938)">History of Czechoslovakia (1918–1938)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Communist_Czechoslovakia" title="Politics of Communist Czechoslovakia">Politics of Communist Czechoslovakia</a></div>
<p>After World War II, a political monopoly was held by the <a href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Communist Party of Czechoslovakia">Communist Party of Czechoslovakia</a> (KSČ). <a href="/wiki/Gust%C3%A1v_Hus%C3%A1k" title="Gustáv Husák">Gustáv Husák</a> was elected first secretary of the KSČ in 1969 (changed to general secretary in 1971) and president of Czechoslovakia in 1975. Other parties and organizations existed but functioned in subordinate roles to the KSČ. All political parties, as well as numerous mass organizations, were grouped under umbrella of the <a href="/wiki/National_Front_(Czechoslovakia)" title="National Front (Czechoslovakia)">National Front</a>. Human rights activists and religious activists were severely repressed.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Constitutional_development">Constitutional development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Constitutional development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Court_of_Czechoslovakia" class="mw-redirect" title="Constitutional Court of Czechoslovakia">Constitutional Court of Czechoslovakia</a></div>

</p>
<ul><li>Temporary constitution of 14 November 1918 (democratic): see <a href="/wiki/History_of_Czechoslovakia_(1918%E2%80%931938)" title="History of Czechoslovakia (1918–1938)">History of Czechoslovakia (1918–1938)</a></li>

It was amended several more times during 1990–1992 (for example, 1990, name change to Czecho-Slovakia, 1991 incorporation of the human rights charter)</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_policy">Foreign policy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Foreign policy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="International_agreements_and_membership">International agreements and membership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: International agreements and membership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the 1930s, the nation formed a military alliance with France, which collapsed in the <a href="/wiki/Munich_Agreement" title="Munich Agreement">Munich Agreement</a> of 1938. After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, active participant in Council for Mutual Economic Assistance (<a href="/wiki/Comecon" title="Comecon">Comecon</a>), <a href="/wiki/Warsaw_Pact" title="Warsaw Pact">Warsaw Pact</a>, United Nations and its specialized agencies; signatory of <a href="/wiki/Conference_on_Security_and_Cooperation_in_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="Conference on Security and Cooperation in Europe">conference on Security and Cooperation in Europe</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Administrative_divisions">Administrative divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Administrative divisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Administrative_divisions_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Administrative divisions of Czechoslovakia">Administrative divisions of Czechoslovakia</a></div>
<ul><li>1918–1923: Different systems in former Austrian territory (<a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a>, a small part of <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesia</a>) compared to former Hungarian territory (Slovakia and <a href="/wiki/Ruthenia" title="Ruthenia">Ruthenia</a>): three lands (<i>země</i>) (also called district units (<i>kraje</i>)): Bohemia, Moravia, Silesia, plus 21 counties (<i>župy</i>) in today's Slovakia and three counties in today's Ruthenia; both lands and counties were divided into districts (<i><a href="/wiki/Okres" title="Okres">okresy</a></i>).</li>
<li>1923–1927: As above, except that the Slovak and Ruthenian counties were replaced by six (grand) counties (<i>(veľ)župy</i>) in Slovakia and one (grand) county in Ruthenia, and the numbers and boundaries of the <i>okresy</i> were changed in those two territories.</li>
<li>1928–1938: Four lands (Czech: <i>země</i>, Slovak: <i>krajiny</i>): Bohemia, Moravia-Silesia, Slovakia and Sub-Carpathian Ruthenia, divided into districts (<i>okresy</i>).</li>
<li>Late 1938 – March 1939: As above, but Slovakia and Ruthenia gained the status of "autonomous lands". Slovakia was called <i>Slovenský štát</i>, with its own currency and government.</li>
<li>1945–1948: As in 1928–1938, except that Ruthenia became part of the Soviet Union.</li>
<li>1949–1960: 19 regions (<i>kraje</i>) divided into 270 <i>okresy</i>.</li>
<li>1960–1992: 10 <i>kraje</i>, <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>, and (from 1970) <a href="/wiki/Bratislava" title="Bratislava">Bratislava</a> (capital of Slovakia); these were divided into 109–114 okresy; the kraje were abolished temporarily in Slovakia in 1969–1970 and for many purposes from 1991 in Czechoslovakia; in addition, the Czech Socialist Republic and the Slovak Socialist Republic were established in 1969 (without the word <i>Socialist</i> from 1990).</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population_and_ethnic_groups">Population and ethnic groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Population and ethnic groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Czechoslovakia" title="Demographics of Czechoslovakia">Demographics of Czechoslovakia</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Czechoslovakia" class="mw-redirect" title="Economy of Czechoslovakia">Economy of Czechoslovakia</a></div>
<p>Before World War II, the economy was about the fourth in all industrial states in Europe. The state was based on strong economy, manufacturing cars (<a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Auto" title="Škoda Auto">Škoda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tatra_(company)" title="Tatra (company)">Tatra</a>), trams, aircraft (<a href="/wiki/Aero_Vodochody" title="Aero Vodochody">Aero</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avia" title="Avia">Avia</a>), ships, ship engines (<a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Works" title="Škoda Works">Škoda</a>), canons, shoes (<a href="/wiki/Bata_Shoes" class="mw-redirect" title="Bata Shoes">Baťa</a>), turbines, guns (<a href="/wiki/Zbrojovka_Brno" title="Zbrojovka Brno">Zbrojovka Brno</a>). It was the industrial workshop for Austro-Hungarian empire. The Slovak lands were more in agriculture.
</p><p>After World War II, the economy was centrally planned, with command links controlled by the communist party, similarly to the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>. The large metallurgical industry was dependent on imports of iron and non-ferrous ores.
</p>
<ul><li>Industry: Extractive industry and manufacturing dominated the sector, including machinery, chemicals, food processing, metallurgy, and textiles. The sector was wasteful in its use of energy, materials, and labor and was slow to upgrade technology, but the country was a major supplier of high-quality machinery, instruments, electronics, aircraft, airplane engines and arms to other socialist countries.</li>
<li>Agriculture: Agriculture was a minor sector, but collectivized farms of large acreage and relatively efficient mode of production enabled the country to be relatively self-sufficient in food supply. The country depended on imports of grains (mainly for livestock feed) in years of adverse weather. Meat production was constrained by shortage of feed, but the country still recorded high per capita consumption of meat.</li>
<li>Foreign trade: Exports were estimated at US$17.8 billion in 1985. Exports were machinery (55%), fuel and materials (14%), and manufactured consumer goods (16%). Imports stood at estimated US$17.9 billion in 1985, including fuel and materials (41%), machinery (33%), and agricultural and forestry products (12%). In 1986, about 80% of foreign trade was with other socialist countries.</li>
<li>Exchange rate: Official, or commercial, rate was crowns (Kčs) 5.4 per US$1 in 1987. Tourist, or non-commercial, rate was Kčs 10.5 per US$1. Neither rate reflected purchasing power. The exchange rate on the <a href="/wiki/Black_market" title="Black market">black market</a> was around Kčs 30 per US$1, which became the official rate once the currency became convertible in the early 1990s.</li>
<li>Fiscal year: Calendar year.</li>
<li>Fiscal policy: The state was the exclusive owner of means of production in most cases. Revenue from state enterprises was the primary source of revenues followed by <a href="/wiki/Turnover_tax" title="Turnover tax">turnover tax</a>. The government spent heavily on social programs, subsidies, and investment. Budget was usually balanced or left small surplus.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Resource_base">Resource base</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Resource base">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Resource_base_of_Communist_Czechoslovakia" title="Resource base of Communist Czechoslovakia">Resource base of Communist Czechoslovakia</a></div>
<p>After World War II, the country was short of energy, relying on imported <a href="/wiki/Crude_oil" class="mw-redirect" title="Crude oil">crude oil</a> and natural gas from Soviet Union, domestic <a href="/wiki/Lignite" title="Lignite">brown coal</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectric_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroelectric energy">hydroelectric energy</a>. Energy constraints were a major factor in the 1980s.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport_and_communications">Transport and communications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Transport and communications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Transport_in_Czechoslovakia" title="Transport in Czechoslovakia">Transport in Czechoslovakia</a></div>
<table class="box-Expand_section plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg" class="image"><img alt="[icon]" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" decoding="async" width="20" height="14" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="44" data-file-height="31" /></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">September 2016</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Slightly after the foundation of Czechoslovakia in 1918, there was a lack of needful infrastructure in many areas – paved roads, railways, bridges etc. Massive improvement in the following years enabled Czechoslovakia to develop its industry. Prague's civil airport in Ruzyně became one of the most modern terminals in the world, when it was finished in 1937. <a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1%C5%A1_Ba%C5%A5a" title="Tomáš Baťa">Tomáš Baťa</a>, Czech entrepreneur and visionary outlined his ideas in the publication "Budujme stát pro 40 milionů lidí", where he described the future motorway system. Construction of the first motorways in Czechoslovakia begun in 1939, nevertheless, they were stopped after Nazi occupation during the World War II.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Society">Society</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Society">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Communist_Czechoslovakia" title="Society of Communist Czechoslovakia">Society of Communist Czechoslovakia</a></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Czechoslovakia" title="Education in Czechoslovakia">Education in Czechoslovakia</a></div>
<p>Education was free at all levels and compulsory from age 6 to 15. The vast majority of the population was literate. There was a highly developed system of apprenticeship training and vocational schools supplemented general secondary schools and institutions of higher education.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_Czechoslovakia" title="Religion in Czechoslovakia">Religion in Czechoslovakia</a></div>
<p>In 1991: <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholics" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholics">Roman Catholics</a> 46%, <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">Evangelical Lutheran</a> 5.3%, <a href="/wiki/Atheist" class="mw-redirect" title="Atheist">Atheist</a> 30%, n/a 17%, but there were huge differences in religious practices between the two constituent republics; see Czech Republic and Slovakia.
</p>
<h2><span id="Health.2C_social_welfare_and_housing"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Health,_social_welfare_and_housing">Health, social welfare and housing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Health, social welfare and housing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Health_and_social_welfare_in_Communist_Czechoslovakia" title="Health and social welfare in Communist Czechoslovakia">Health and social welfare in Communist Czechoslovakia</a></div>
<p>After World War II, <a href="/wiki/Universal_healthcare" class="mw-redirect" title="Universal healthcare">free health care</a> was available to all citizens. National health planning emphasised preventive medicine; factory and local health care centres supplemented hospitals and other inpatient institutions. There was substantial improvement in rural health care during the 1960s and 1970s.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mass_media">Mass media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Mass media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mass_media_in_Communist_Czechoslovakia" title="Mass media in Communist Czechoslovakia">Mass media in Communist Czechoslovakia</a></div>
<p>During the era between the World Wars, Czechoslovak democracy and liberalism facilitated conditions for free publication. The most significant daily newspapers in these times were Lidové noviny, Národní listy, Český deník and Československá republika.
</p><p>During Communist rule, the mass media in Czechoslovakia were controlled by the Communist Party. Private ownership of any publication or agency of the mass media was generally forbidden, although churches and other organizations published small periodicals and newspapers. Even with this information monopoly in the hands of organizations under KSČ control, all publications were <a href="/wiki/Censorship" title="Censorship">reviewed</a> by the government's Office for Press and Information.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Czechoslovakia&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia_at_the_Olympics" title="Czechoslovakia at the Olympics">Czechoslovakia at the Olympics</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovakia_national_football_team" title="Czechoslovakia national football team">Czechoslovakia national football team</a> was a consistent performer on the international scene, with eight appearances in the <a href="/wiki/FIFA_World_Cup" title="FIFA World Cup">FIFA World Cup</a> Finals, finishing in second place in <a href="/wiki/1934_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1934 FIFA World Cup">1934</a> and <a href="/wiki/1962_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1962 FIFA World Cup">1962</a>. The team also won the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_European_Football_Championship" class="mw-redirect" title="UEFA European Football Championship">European Football Championship</a> in <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Euro_1976" title="UEFA Euro 1976">1976</a>, came in third in <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Euro_1980" title="UEFA Euro 1980">1980</a> and won the <a href="/wiki/Football_at_the_Summer_Olympics" title="Football at the Summer Olympics">Olympic gold</a> in <a href="/wiki/Football_at_the_1980_Summer_Olympics" title="Football at the 1980 Summer Olympics">1980</a>.
</p><p>Well-known football players such as <a href="/wiki/Pavel_Nedv%C4%9Bd" title="Pavel Nedvěd">Pavel Nedvěd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anton%C3%ADn_Panenka" title="Antonín Panenka">Antonín Panenka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milan_Baro%C5%A1" title="Milan Baroš">Milan Baroš</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1%C5%A1_Rosick%C3%BD" title="Tomáš Rosický">Tomáš Rosický</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vladim%C3%ADr_%C5%A0micer" title="Vladimír Šmicer">Vladimír Šmicer</a> or <a href="/wiki/Petr_%C4%8Cech" title="Petr Čech">Petr Čech</a> were all born in Czechoslovakia.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> code for Czechoslovakia is TCH, which is still used in historical listings of results.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovak_national_ice_hockey_team" class="mw-redirect" title="Czechoslovak national ice hockey team">Czechoslovak national ice hockey team</a> won many medals from the world championships and Olympic Games. <a href="/wiki/Peter_%C5%A0%C5%A5astn%C3%BD" title="Peter Šťastný">Peter Šťastný</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jarom%C3%ADr_J%C3%A1gr" title="Jaromír Jágr">Jaromír Jágr</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dominik_Ha%C5%A1ek" title="Dominik Hašek">Dominik Hašek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Bondra" title="Peter Bondra">Peter Bondra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petr_Kl%C3%ADma" title="Petr Klíma">Petr Klíma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mari%C3%A1n_G%C3%A1bor%C3%ADk" title="Marián Gáborík">Marián Gáborík</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mari%C3%A1n_Hossa" title="Marián Hossa">Marián Hossa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miroslav_%C5%A0atan" title="Miroslav Šatan">Miroslav Šatan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pavol_Demitra" title="Pavol Demitra">Pavol Demitra</a> all come from Czechoslovakia.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Emil_Z%C3%A1topek" title="Emil Zátopek">Emil Zátopek</a>, winner of four Olympic gold medals in <a href="/wiki/Athletics_(sport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Athletics (sport)">athletics</a>, is considered one of the top athletes in Czechoslovakian history.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/V%C4%9Bra_%C4%8C%C3%A1slavsk%C3%A1" title="Věra Čáslavská">Věra Čáslavská</a> was an Olympic gold medallist in gymnastics, winning seven gold medals and four silver medals. She represented Czechoslovakia in three consecutive Olympics.
</p><p>Several accomplished professional <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">tennis</a> players including <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Lendl" title="Ivan Lendl">Ivan Lendl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jan_Kode%C5%A1" title="Jan Kodeš">Jan Kodeš</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miloslav_Me%C4%8D%C3%AD%C5%99" title="Miloslav Mečíř">Miloslav Mečíř</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hana_Mandl%C3%ADkov%C3%A1" title="Hana Mandlíková">Hana Mandlíková</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martina_Hingis" title="Martina Hingis">Martina Hingis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martina_Navratilova" title="Martina Navratilova">Martina Navratilova</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jana_Novotna" class="mw-redirect" title="Jana Novotna">Jana Novotna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petra_Kvitov%C3%A1" title="Petra Kvitová">Petra Kvitová</a> and <a href="/wiki/Daniela_Hantuchov%C3%A1" title="Daniela Hantuchová">Daniela Hantuchová</a> were born in Czechoslovakia.
</p>
