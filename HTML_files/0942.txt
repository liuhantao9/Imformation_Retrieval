<p><b>The Fens</b>, also known as the <span class="nowrap"><b>Fenlands</b></span>, are a coastal plain in eastern England. This natural marshy region supported a rich ecology and numerous species, as well as absorbing storms. Most of the fens were drained several centuries ago, resulting in a flat, dry, low-lying agricultural region supported by a system of drainage channels and man-made rivers (dykes and drains) and automated pumping stations. There have been unintended consequences to this reclamation, as the land level has continued to sink and the dykes must be built higher to protect it from flooding.
</p><p>A <i><a href="/wiki/Fen" title="Fen">fen</a></i> is the local term for an individual area of <a href="/wiki/Marsh" title="Marsh">marshland</a> or former marshland. It also designates the type of marsh typical of the area, which has <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">neutral</a> or <a href="/wiki/Alkali" title="Alkali">alkaline</a> water chemistry and relatively large quantities of dissolved <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">minerals</a>, but few other <a href="/wiki/Plant_nutrition" title="Plant nutrition">plant nutrients</a>.
</p><p>Fenland primarily lies around the coast of <a href="/wiki/The_Wash" title="The Wash">the Wash</a>, occupying an area of nearly 1,500&#160;sq&#160;mi (3,900&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) in <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most of the Fenland lies within a few metres of <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>. As with similar areas in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, much of the Fenland originally consisted of fresh- or salt-water wetlands. These have been artificially drained and continue to be protected from floods by drainage banks and pumps. With the support of this drainage system, the Fenland has become a major <a href="/wiki/Arable_land" title="Arable land">arable</a> agricultural region in Britain for grains and vegetables. The Fens are particularly fertile, containing around half of the <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_Land_Classification" title="Agricultural Land Classification">grade 1 agricultural land</a> in England.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Fens have been referred to as the "Holy Land of the English" because of the former monasteries, now churches and <a href="/wiki/Cathedral" title="Cathedral">cathedrals</a>, of  <a href="/wiki/Crowland" title="Crowland">Crowland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ely,_Cambridgeshire" title="Ely, Cambridgeshire">Ely</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peterborough" title="Peterborough">Peterborough</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Ramsey,_Cambridgeshire" title="Ramsey, Cambridgeshire">Ramsey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thorney,_Cambridgeshire" title="Thorney, Cambridgeshire">Thorney</a>. Other significant settlements in the Fens include <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Lincolnshire" title="Boston, Lincolnshire">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spalding,_Lincolnshire" title="Spalding, Lincolnshire">Spalding</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wisbech" title="Wisbech">Wisbech</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background:_historical_flooding_and_drainage">Background: historical flooding and drainage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background: historical flooding and drainage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wicken_Fen_Windpump.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Wicken_Fen_Windpump.jpg/220px-Wicken_Fen_Windpump.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="331" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Wicken_Fen_Windpump.jpg/330px-Wicken_Fen_Windpump.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Wicken_Fen_Windpump.jpg/440px-Wicken_Fen_Windpump.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="3008" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wicken_Fen_Windpump.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Windpump" title="Windpump">windpump</a> at <a href="/wiki/Wicken_Fen" title="Wicken Fen">Wicken Fen</a></div></div></div>
<p>The Fens are very low-lying compared with the <a href="/wiki/Chalk" title="Chalk">chalk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> uplands that surround them&#160;– in most places no more than 10 metres (33&#160;ft) above sea level. As a result of drainage and the subsequent shrinkage of the <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> fens, many parts of the Fens now lie below <a href="/wiki/Mean_sea_level" class="mw-redirect" title="Mean sea level">mean sea level</a>. Although one writer in the 17th century described the Fenland as entirely above sea level (in contrast to the Netherlands),<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> the area now includes the lowest land in the United Kingdom. <a href="/wiki/Holme,_Cambridgeshire" title="Holme, Cambridgeshire">Holme Fen</a> in Cambridgeshire, is around 2.75 metres (9&#160;ft 0&#160;in) metres below sea level.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Within the Fens are a few hills, which have historically been called "islands", as they remained dry when the low-lying fens around them were flooded. The largest of the fen-islands was the 23-square-mile (60 km2) Kimmeridge Clay island, on which the cathedral city of <a href="/wiki/Ely,_Cambridgeshire" title="Ely, Cambridgeshire">Ely</a> was built: its highest point is 39 metres (128&#160;ft) above mean sea level.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Without artificial drainage and flood protection, the Fens would be liable to periodic flooding, particularly in winter due to the heavy load of water flowing down from the uplands and overflowing the rivers. Some areas of the Fens were once permanently flooded, creating small lakes or <i><a href="/wiki/Mere_(lake)" title="Mere (lake)">meres</a></i>, while others were flooded only during periods of high water. In the pre-modern period, <a href="/wiki/Agronomy" title="Agronomy">arable farming</a> was limited to the higher areas of the surrounding uplands, the fen islands, and the so-called "Townlands", an arch of <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a> ground around <a href="/wiki/The_Wash" title="The Wash">the Wash</a>, where the towns had their arable fields. Though these lands were lower than the peat fens before the peat shrinkage began, the more stable <a href="/wiki/Silt" title="Silt">silt</a> soils were reclaimed by medieval farmers and <a href="/wiki/Embankment_dam" title="Embankment dam">embanked</a> against any floods coming down from the peat areas or from the sea. The rest of the Fenland was dedicated to <a href="/wiki/Pastoral_farming" title="Pastoral farming">pastoral farming</a>, fishing, <a href="/wiki/Fowling" title="Fowling">fowling</a>, and the harvesting of reeds or sedge for thatch. In this way, the medieval and early modern Fens stood in contrast to the rest of southern England, which was primarily an arable agricultural region.
</p><p>Since the advent of modern drainage in the 19th and 20th centuries, the Fens have been radically transformed. Today arable farming has almost entirely replaced pastoral. The economy of the Fens is heavily invested in the production of crops such as grains, vegetables, and some cash crops such as <a href="/wiki/Rapeseed" title="Rapeseed">rapeseed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canola" class="mw-redirect" title="Canola">canola</a>.
</p><p>Drainage in the Fenland consists of both <a href="/wiki/Drainage_basin" title="Drainage basin">river drainage</a> and internal drainage of the land between the rivers. The internal drainage was organised by <i>levels</i> or districts, each of which includes the fen parts of one or several <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">parishes</a>. The details of the organisation vary with the history of their development, but the areas include:
</p>
<ul><li>The Great Level of the Fens is the largest region of fen in eastern England: including the lower drainage basins of the <a href="/wiki/River_Nene" title="River Nene">River Nene</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Great_Ouse" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Ouse">Great Ouse</a>, it covers about 500&#160;sq&#160;mi (1,300&#160;km<sup>2</sup>). It is also known as the Bedford Level, after <a href="/wiki/Francis_Russell,_4th_Earl_of_Bedford" title="Francis Russell, 4th Earl of Bedford">Francis Russell, 4th Earl of Bedford</a>, who headed the so-called <a href="/wiki/Adventurers_(land_drainage)" title="Adventurers (land drainage)">adventurers</a> (investors) in the 17th-century drainage in this area; <a href="/wiki/William_Russell,_1st_Duke_of_Bedford" title="William Russell, 1st Duke of Bedford">his son</a> became the first governor of the <a href="/wiki/Bedford_Level_Corporation" title="Bedford Level Corporation">Bedford Level Corporation</a>. In the 17th century, the Great Level was divided into the North, Middle and South Levels for the purposes of administration and maintenance. In the 20th century, these levels have been given new boundaries; they include some fens that were never part of the jurisdiction of the Bedford Level Corporation.
<ul><li>The South Level lies to the southeast of the <a href="/wiki/Ouse_Washes" title="Ouse Washes">Ouse Washes</a> and surrounds Ely, as it did in the 17th century.</li>
<li>The Middle Level lies between the Ouse Washes and the Nene, but historically was defined as between the Ouse Washes and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Morton%27s_Leam&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Morton&#39;s Leam (page does not exist)">Morton's Leam</a>, a 15th-century canal that runs north of the town of <a href="/wiki/Whittlesey" title="Whittlesey">Whittlesey</a>.</li>
<li>The North Level now includes all of the fens in Cambridgeshire and <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a> between the Nene and the <a href="/wiki/River_Welland" title="River Welland">River Welland</a>. It originally included only a small part of these lands, including the ancient parishes of <a href="/wiki/Thorney,_Cambridgeshire" title="Thorney, Cambridgeshire">Thorney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Crowland" title="Crowland">Crowland</a>, but excluding most of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wisbech_Hundred&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wisbech Hundred (page does not exist)">Wisbech Hundred</a> and Lincolnshire, which were under their own local jurisdictions.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Deeping_Fen" title="Deeping Fen">Deeping Fen</a>, in the southern part of Lincolnshire, lies between the River Welland and the <a href="/wiki/River_Glen,_Lincolnshire" title="River Glen, Lincolnshire">River Glen</a> with its tributary the <a href="/wiki/Bourne_Eau" title="Bourne Eau">Bourne Eau</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Black_Sluice" title="Black Sluice">Black Sluice</a> District, much of which was known as the Lindsey Level when it was first drained in 1639, extends from the Glen and Bourne Eau to <a href="/wiki/Swineshead,_Lincolnshire" title="Swineshead, Lincolnshire">Swineshead</a>. Its water is carried through to <a href="/wiki/The_Haven,_Boston" title="The Haven, Boston">the Haven</a> at <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Lincolnshire" title="Boston, Lincolnshire">Boston</a>.</li></ul>
<p>The above were all redrained at one time or another after the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">Civil War</a> (1642-1649).
</p>
<ul><li>The Witham Commission Fens:
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Witham_First_District_IDB" title="Witham First District IDB">First District</a>: from <a href="/wiki/Washingborough" title="Washingborough">Washingborough</a> to <a href="/wiki/Billinghay" title="Billinghay">Billinghay</a> Dales</li>
<li>Second District: Blacksluice – <a href="/wiki/Holland_Fen" title="Holland Fen">Holland Fen</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Witham_First_District_IDB" title="Witham First District IDB">Third District</a>: north of the <a href="/wiki/River_Witham" title="River Witham">River Witham</a> above <a href="/wiki/Bardney" title="Bardney">Bardney</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Witham_Navigable_Drains" title="Witham Navigable Drains">Fourth District</a>: East, West and Wildmore Fens and the Townland from Boston to <a href="/wiki/Wainfleet,_Lincolnshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Wainfleet, Lincolnshire">Wainfleet</a></li>
<li>Fifth District: Kyme Eau to <a href="/wiki/Billinghay" title="Billinghay">Billinghay</a> Skirth</li>
<li>Sixth District: Blacksluice – Helpringham Eau to Kyme Eau</li></ul></li></ul>
<p>These were drained in the 18th and 19th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_geography">Formation and geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation and geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the end of the most recent <a href="/wiki/Ice_Age" class="mw-redirect" title="Ice Age">glacial period</a>, known in Britain as the <a href="/wiki/Devensian" class="mw-redirect" title="Devensian">Devensian</a>, ten thousand years ago, Britain and <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">continental Europe</a> were joined by the ridge between <a href="/wiki/Friesland" title="Friesland">Friesland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a>. The topography of the bed of the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a> indicates that the rivers of the southern part of eastern England flowed into the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>, hence through the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a>. From the Fens northward along the modern coast, the drainage flowed into the <a href="/wiki/Northern_North_Sea_basin" title="Northern North Sea basin">northern North Sea basin</a>. As the ice melted, the rising sea level drowned the lower lands, leading ultimately to the present coastline.<sup id="cite_ref-John_Coles_1994_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-John_Coles_1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>These rising sea levels flooded the previously inland woodland of the Fenland basin; over the next few thousand years both saltwater and freshwater wetlands developed as a result. Silt and <a href="/wiki/Clay" title="Clay">clay</a> soils were deposited by marine floods in the saltwater areas and along the beds of tidal rivers, while organic soils, or peats, developed in the <a href="/wiki/Freshwater" class="mw-redirect" title="Freshwater">freshwater</a> marshes. Fenland water levels peaked in the Iron Age; earlier Bronze and Neolithic settlements were covered by peat deposits, and have only recently been found after periods of extensive droughts revealed them.<sup id="cite_ref-John_Coles_1994_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-John_Coles_1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  During the Roman period, water levels fell once again. Settlements developed on the new silt soils deposited near the coast. Though water levels rose once again in the early medieval period, by this time artificial banks protected the coastal settlements and the interior from further deposits of marine silts. Peats continued to develop in the freshwater wetlands of the interior fens.<sup id="cite_ref-John_Coles_1994_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-John_Coles_1994-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The wetlands of the fens have historically included:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Arroyo_(creek)" title="Arroyo (creek)">Washes</a>: these are places such as tidal <a href="/wiki/Mud_flat" class="mw-redirect" title="Mud flat">mud flats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Braided_river" title="Braided river">braided rivers</a>, which are sometimes exposed and at other times covered with water.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Salt_marsh" title="Salt marsh">Salt marsh</a>: this is the higher part of a tidal wash, on which salt-adapted plants grew.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fen" title="Fen">Fen</a>: this is a broad expanse of nutrient-rich shallow water in which dead plants do not fully decay, resulting in a flora of emergent plants growing in saturated peat.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">Moor</a>: this developed where the peat grew above the reach of the land water which carried the nutrients to the fen. Its development was enabled where the fen was watered directly by rainfall. The slightly acidic rain washed the <a href="/wiki/Hydroxide_ion" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydroxide ion">hydroxide ions</a> out of the peat, making it more suitable for acid-loving plants, notably <i><a href="/wiki/Sphagnum" title="Sphagnum">Sphagnum</a></i> species. This is the same as <a href="/wiki/Bog" title="Bog">bog</a>, but the word <i>moor</i> was applied to this acid peatland occurring on hills. These moors disappeared in the 19th century. Researchers did not think that the Fenland had this kind of peat, until the discovery of archaeological and documentary evidence showing that it did until the early 19th century.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Waters: these have included:
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Creek_(tidal)" title="Creek (tidal)">tidal creeks</a>, which reached from the sea into the marsh, the Townlands and in some places, the fen. They were named only if big enough to be regarded as havens</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mere_(lake)" title="Mere (lake)">meres</a>, or shallow lakes, which were more or less static but aerated by wind action</li>
<li>many rivers, both natural and (from Roman times on) artificial</li></ul></li></ul>
<p>Major areas for settlement were:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Townlands</i>, a broad bank of silt (the remains of the huge creek <a href="/wiki/Levee" title="Levee">levees</a> that developed naturally during the Bronze and Iron Ages), on which the settlers built homes and grew vegetables for households</li>
<li><i>fen islands</i>: areas of higher land, which were never covered by the growing peat</li>
<li><i>fen edges</i>: uplands surrounding the fens</li></ul>
<p>In general, of the three principal soil types found in the Fenland today, the mineral-based silt resulted from the energetic marine environment of the creeks, the clay was deposited in tidal mud-flats and salt-marsh, while the peat grew in the fen and bog. The peat produces black soils, which are directly comparable to the American <a href="/wiki/Muck_(soil)" class="mw-redirect" title="Muck (soil)">muck soils</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Roddon" title="Roddon">roddon</a>, the dried raised bed of a watercourse, is more suitable for building than the less stable peat.
</p><p>Since the 19th century, all of the acid peat in the Fens has disappeared. Drying and wastage of peats has greatly reduced the depth of the alkaline peat soils and reduced the overall elevation of large areas of the peat fens.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-Roman_settlement">Pre-Roman settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pre-Roman settlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is evidence of human settlement near the Fens from the <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> on.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The evidence suggests that Mesolithic settlement in Cambridgeshire was particularly along the fen edges and on the low islands within the fens, to take advantage of the hunting and fishing opportunities of the wetlands.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Internationally important sites include <a href="/wiki/Flag_Fen" title="Flag Fen">Flag Fen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Must_Farm" class="mw-redirect" title="Must Farm">Must Farm</a> quarry Bronze Age settlement and <a href="/wiki/Stonea_Camp" title="Stonea Camp">Stonea Camp</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_farming_and_engineering">Roman farming and engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Roman farming and engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Romans constructed the <a href="/wiki/Fen_Causeway" title="Fen Causeway">Fen Causeway</a>, a road across the Fens to link what later became <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a> with what later became central England; it runs between <a href="/wiki/Denver,_Norfolk" title="Denver, Norfolk">Denver</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peterborough" title="Peterborough">Peterborough</a>. They also linked <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ely,_Cambridgeshire" title="Ely, Cambridgeshire">Ely</a>. Generally, their road system avoided the Fens, except for minor roads designed for exporting the products of the region, especially salt, beef and leather. Sheep were probably raised on the higher ground of the Townlands and fen islands, then as in the early 19th century. There may have been some drainage efforts during the Roman period, including the <a href="/wiki/Car_Dyke" title="Car Dyke">Car Dyke</a> along the western edge of the Fenland between Peterborough and Lincolnshire, but most canals were constructed for transportation.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>How far seaward the Roman settlement extended is unclear owing to the deposits laid down above them during later floods.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_post-Roman_settlements">Early post-Roman settlements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Early post-Roman settlements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The early post-Roman settlements were made on the <a href="/wiki/Townlands" class="mw-redirect" title="Townlands">Townlands</a>. It is clear that there was some prosperity there, particularly where rivers permitted access to the upland beyond the fen. Such places were <a href="/wiki/Wisbech" title="Wisbech">Wisbech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spalding,_Lincolnshire" title="Spalding, Lincolnshire">Spalding</a>, Swineshead and Boston. All the Townlands parishes were laid out as elongated strips, to provide access to the products of fen, marsh and sea. On the fen edge, parishes are similarly elongated to provide access to both upland and fen. The townships are therefore often nearer to each other than they are to the distant farms in their own parishes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Middle_Ages_and_Middle_Ages">Early Middle Ages and Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Early Middle Ages and Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the end of Roman Britain, there is a break in written records. It is thought that some <a href="/wiki/Iceni" title="Iceni">Iceni</a> may have moved west into the Fens to avoid the <a href="/wiki/Angles" title="Angles">Angles</a>, who were migrating across the North Sea from <a href="/wiki/Angeln" class="mw-redirect" title="Angeln">Angeln</a> (modern <a href="/wiki/Schleswig" class="mw-redirect" title="Schleswig">Schleswig</a>) and settling what would become <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a>. Surrounded by water and marshes, the Fens provided a safe area that was easily defended and not particularly desirable to invading <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxons</a>.
</p><p>The names of <a href="/wiki/West_Walton" title="West Walton">West Walton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Walsoken" title="Walsoken">Walsoken</a> and <a href="/wiki/Walpole,_Norfolk" title="Walpole, Norfolk">Walpole</a> are believed to suggest the native British population, as the <i>Wal-</i> coming from the Old English <i>walh</i>, meaning "foreigner".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> However, the villages are in close proximity to the old Roman sea wall, so the <i>wal-</i> element is more probably from <i>wal</i> or <i>weal</i>, meaning "wall". <i>Walton</i> is generally believed to mean "wall-town",<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <i>Walsoken</i> to mean "the district under particular jurisdiction by the wall",<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and <i>Walpole</i> to mean simply "wall-pole" (Old English <i>wal</i> and <i>pal</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> or perhaps "well pool" (Old English <i>welle</i> and <i>pol</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When written records resume in Anglo-Saxon England, the names of a number of peoples of the Fens are recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Tribal_Hidage" title="Tribal Hidage">Tribal Hidage</a> and Christian histories. They include North <a href="/wiki/Gyrwas" title="Gyrwas">Gyrwe</a> (Peterborough and Crowland), South Gyrwe (Ely), the Spalda (Spalding), and Bilmingas (part of south Lincolnshire).
</p><p>In the early Christian period of Anglo-Saxon England, a number of Christians sought the isolation that could be found in the wilderness of the Fens. Later classified as saints, often with close royal links, they include <a href="/wiki/Guthlac" class="mw-redirect" title="Guthlac">Guthlac</a>, <a href="/wiki/Etheldreda" class="mw-redirect" title="Etheldreda">Etheldreda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pega" title="Pega">Pega</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Wendreda" title="Wendreda">Wendreda</a>. Hermitages on the islands became centres of communities which later developed as monasteries with massive estates. In the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Life_of_Saint_Guthlac&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Life of Saint Guthlac (page does not exist)">Life of Saint Guthlac</a></i>, a biography of the East Anglian <a href="/wiki/Hermit" title="Hermit">hermit</a> who lived in the Fens during the early 8th century,  Saint Guthlac was described as attacked on several occasions by people he believed were <a href="/wiki/Britons" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons">Britons</a>, who were then living in the Fens. However, Bertram Colgrave, in the introduction to one edition, doubts this account, because of the lack of evidence of British survival in the region. British place names in the area are "very few".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Monastic life was disrupted by Danish (Anglo-Saxon) raids and centuries of settlement from the 6th century but was revived in the mid-10th-century monastic revival. In the 11th century, the whole area was incorporated into a united <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon England">Anglo-Saxon England</a>. The Fens remained a place of refuge and intrigue. It was here that <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Aetheling" title="Alfred Aetheling">Alfred Aetheling</a> was brought to be murdered and here where <a href="/wiki/Hereward_the_Wake" title="Hereward the Wake">Hereward the Wake</a> based his insurgency against <a href="/wiki/Norman_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman England">Norman England</a>.
</p><p>Fenland monastic houses include the so-called <i>Fen Five</i> (<a href="/wiki/Ely_Cathedral" title="Ely Cathedral">Ely Cathedral Priory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thorney_Abbey" title="Thorney Abbey">Thorney Abbey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Croyland_Abbey" class="mw-redirect" title="Croyland Abbey">Croyland Abbey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ramsey_Abbey" title="Ramsey Abbey">Ramsey Abbey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peterborough_Cathedral" title="Peterborough Cathedral">Peterborough Abbey</a>)<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> as well as <a href="/wiki/Spalding_Priory" title="Spalding Priory">Spalding Priory</a>. As major landowners, the monasteries played a significant part in the early efforts at drainage of the Fens.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_Forest">Royal Forest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Royal Forest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>During most of the 12th century and the early 13th century, the south Lincolnshire fens were <a href="/wiki/Royal_Forest" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Forest">afforested</a>. The area was enclosed by a line from Spalding, along the River Welland to <a href="/wiki/Market_Deeping" title="Market Deeping">Market Deeping</a>, then along the Car Dyke to <a href="/wiki/Dowsby" title="Dowsby">Dowsby</a> and across the fens to the Welland. It was deforested in the early 13th century. There is little agreement as to the exact dates of the establishment and demise of the forest, but it seems likely that the deforestation was connected with the <a href="/wiki/Magna_Carta" title="Magna Carta">Magna Carta</a> or one of its early 13th-century restatements, though it may have been as late as 1240. The forest would have affected the economies of the townships around it and it appears that the present <a href="/wiki/Bourne_Eau" title="Bourne Eau">Bourne Eau</a> was constructed at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, as <a href="/wiki/Bourne,_Lincolnshire" title="Bourne, Lincolnshire">the town</a> seems to have joined in the general prosperity by about 1280.
</p><p>Though the forest was about half in <a href="/wiki/Holland,_Lincolnshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Holland, Lincolnshire">Holland (Lincolnshire)</a> and half in <a href="/wiki/Kesteven" title="Kesteven">Kesteven</a>, it is known as Kesteven Forest.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_attempts_to_drain_the_Fens">Early modern attempts to drain the Fens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Early modern attempts to drain the Fens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Twenty,_Lincolnshire" title="Twenty, Lincolnshire">Twenty, Lincolnshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bedford_Level_Corporation" title="Bedford Level Corporation">Bedford Level Corporation</a></div>
<p>Though some signs of Roman hydraulics survive, and there were also some medieval drainage works, land drainage was begun in earnest during the 1630s by the various investors who had contracts with <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">King Charles I</a> to do so.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The leader of one of these syndicates was the Earl of Bedford, who employed <a href="/wiki/Cornelius_Vermuyden" title="Cornelius Vermuyden">Cornelius Vermuyden</a> as engineer. Contrary to popular belief, Vermuyden was not involved with the draining of the Great Fen in Cambridgeshire and Norfolk in the 1630s, but only became involved with the second phase of construction in the 1650s.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The scheme was imposed despite huge opposition from locals who were losing their livelihoods based on fishing and wildfowling. Fenmen known as the Fen Tigers tried to sabotage the drainage efforts.
</p><p>Two cuts were made in the Cambridgeshire Fens to join the <a href="/wiki/River_Great_Ouse" title="River Great Ouse">River Great Ouse</a> to the sea at King's Lynn – the <a href="/wiki/Old_Bedford_River" title="Old Bedford River">Old Bedford River</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_Bedford_River" title="New Bedford River">New Bedford River</a>, the latter being known also as the <i>Hundred Foot Drain</i>. Both cuts were named after the <a href="/wiki/Francis_Russell,_4th_Earl_of_Bedford" title="Francis Russell, 4th Earl of Bedford">Fourth Earl of Bedford</a> who, along with some <i>gentlemen adventurers</i> (<a href="/wiki/Venture_capitalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Venture capitalist">venture capitalists</a>), funded the construction and were rewarded with large grants of the resulting farmland. The work was directed by engineers from the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a>. Following this initial drainage, the Fens were still extremely susceptible to flooding, so <a href="/wiki/Windpump" title="Windpump">windpumps</a> were used to pump water away from affected areas. The Company of Adventurers were more formally incorporated in 1663 as the <a href="/wiki/Bedford_Level_Corporation" title="Bedford Level Corporation">Bedford Level Corporation</a>.
</p><p>However, their success was short-lived. Once drained of water, the peat shrank, and the fields lowered further. The more effectively they were drained, the worse the problem became, and soon the fields were lower than the surrounding rivers. By the end of the 17th century, the land was under water once again.
</p><p>Though the three Bedford Levels together formed the biggest scheme, they were not the only ones. <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bertie,_1st_Earl_of_Lindsey" title="Robert Bertie, 1st Earl of Lindsey">Lord Lindsey</a> and his partner <a href="/wiki/William_Killigrew_(1606%E2%80%931695)" title="William Killigrew (1606–1695)">Sir William Killigrew</a> had the Lindsey Level inhabited by farmers by 1638, but the onset of the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">Civil War</a> permitted the destruction of the works until the 1765 <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a> that led to the formation of the Black Sluice Commissioners.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Many original records of the Bedford Level Corporation, including maps of the Levels, are now held by <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire_Archives_and_Local_Studies" title="Cambridgeshire Archives and Local Studies">Cambridgeshire Archives and Local Studies</a> Service at the County Record Office in Cambridge.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_drainage">Modern drainage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Modern drainage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The major part of the draining of the Fens was effected in the late 18th and early 19th century, again involving fierce local rioting and sabotage of the works. The final success came in the 1820s when windpumps were replaced with powerful coal-powered <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engines</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Stretham_Old_Engine" title="Stretham Old Engine">Stretham Old Engine</a>, which were themselves replaced with diesel-powered pumps, such as those at <a href="/wiki/Prickwillow_Museum" title="Prickwillow Museum">Prickwillow Museum</a> and, following <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the small electric stations that are still used today.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Prickwillow_Drainage_Engine_Museum.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Prickwillow_Drainage_Engine_Museum.jpg/220px-Prickwillow_Drainage_Engine_Museum.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Prickwillow_Drainage_Engine_Museum.jpg/330px-Prickwillow_Drainage_Engine_Museum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Prickwillow_Drainage_Engine_Museum.jpg/440px-Prickwillow_Drainage_Engine_Museum.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="474" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Prickwillow_Drainage_Engine_Museum.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Prickwillow_Museum" title="Prickwillow Museum">Prickwillow Museum</a> shows the changing face of the Fens and the story of their drainage. It is housed in an old pumping station.</div></div></div>
<p>The dead vegetation of the peat remained undecayed because it was deprived of air (the peat being anaerobic). When it was drained, the oxygen of the air reached it, since then the peat has been slowly oxidizing.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> This, together with the shrinkage on its initial drying and the removal of soil by the wind, has meant that much of the Fens lies below <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">high tide level</a>. As the highest parts of the drained fen are now only a few metres above mean sea level, only sizeable <a href="/wiki/Dike_(construction)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dike (construction)">embankments</a> of the rivers, and general flood defences, stop the land from being inundated. Nonetheless, these works are now much more effective than they were.
</p><p>The Fens today are protected by 60 miles (97&#160;km) of embankments defending against the sea and 96 miles (154&#160;km) of river embankments. Eleven <a href="/wiki/Internal_drainage_board" title="Internal drainage board">internal drainage board</a> (IDB) groups maintain 286 pumping stations and 3,800 miles (6,100&#160;km) of watercourses, with the combined capacity to pump 16,500 <a href="/wiki/Olympic-size_swimming_pool" title="Olympic-size swimming pool">Olympic-size swimming pools</a> in a 24-hour period or to empty <a href="/wiki/Rutland_Water" title="Rutland Water">Rutland Water</a> in 3 days.<sup id="cite_ref-NFU_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NFU-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_farming_and_food_manufacturing_in_the_Fens">Modern farming and food manufacturing in the Fens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Modern farming and food manufacturing in the Fens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2008, there are estimated to be 4,000 farms in the Fens involved in agriculture and horticulture, including arable, livestock, poultry, dairy, orchards, vegetables and ornamental plants and flowers. They employ about 27,000 people in full-time and seasonal jobs. In turn, they support around 250 businesses involved in food and drink manufacturing and distribution, employing around 17,500 people.<sup id="cite_ref-NFU_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NFU-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Over 70% of the Fens is involved in environmental stewardship schemes, under which 270 miles (430&#160;km) of hedgerow and 1,780 miles (2,860&#160;km) of ditches are managed, providing large wildlife corridors and habitat for endangered animals such as the <a href="/wiki/European_water_vole" title="European water vole">water vole</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NFU_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NFU-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Restoration">Restoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Restoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2003, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Fen" title="Great Fen">Great Fen</a> Project was initiated to return parts of the Fens to their original pre-agricultural state. The periodic flooding by the North Sea, which renewed the character of the Fenlands, was characterised conventionally by the 1911 <i>Encyclopædia Britannica</i> as "ravaged by serious inundations of the sea". The modern approach is to allow a little farmland to be flooded again and turned into <a href="/wiki/Nature_reserve" title="Nature reserve">nature reserves</a>. By introducing fresh water, the organisers of the project hope to encourage species such as the <a href="/wiki/Common_snipe" title="Common snipe">snipe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_lapwing" title="Northern lapwing">lapwing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_bittern" title="Eurasian bittern">bittern</a>. Endangered species such as the <a href="/wiki/Fen_violet" class="mw-redirect" title="Fen violet">fen violet</a> will be seeded.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Fens_Waterways_Link" title="Fens Waterways Link">Fens Waterways Link</a> is a scheme to restore navigation to some of the drainage works. It is planned to bring the <a href="/wiki/South_Forty-Foot_Drain" title="South Forty-Foot Drain">South Forty-Foot Drain</a> and parts of the Car Dyke into use as part of a route between Boston and Cambridge.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Fens is the origin of <a href="/wiki/Fen_skating" title="Fen skating">English bandy and speed skating</a>. It is the base of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain_Bandy_Federation" title="Great Britain Bandy Federation">Great Britain Bandy Federation</a><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Littleport,_Cambridgeshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Littleport, Cambridgeshire">Littleport</a> there is a project in place aiming at building an indoor stadium for ice sports. If successful it will have the largest sheet of ice in the country with both a <a href="/wiki/Bandy_field" title="Bandy field">bandy pitch</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Speed_skating_rink" title="Speed skating rink">speed skating oval</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fens&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some authors have featured the Fens repeatedly in their work. For example:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/John_Gordon_(author)" title="John Gordon (author)">John Gordon</a>, adolescent fiction writer and author of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Giant_Under_The_Snow" class="mw-redirect" title="The Giant Under The Snow">The Giant Under The Snow</a></i>, drew inspiration for many of his supernatural fantasies from the Fens.
