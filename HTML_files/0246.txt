	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Akan names</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Akan_people" title="Akan people">Akan people</a></b> of  <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a> frequently name their children after the day of the week they were born and the order in which they were born. These "day names" have further meanings concerning the soul and character of the person. Middle names have considerably more variety and can refer to their birth order, twin status, or an ancestor's middle name.
</p><p>This naming tradition is shared throughout <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a> and the <a href="/wiki/African_diaspora" title="African diaspora">African diaspora</a>. During the 18th–19th centuries, slaves in the Caribbean from the region that is modern-day Ghana were referred to as <a href="/wiki/Coromantees" class="mw-redirect" title="Coromantees">Coromantees</a>. Many of the leaders of slave rebellions had "day names" including <a href="/wiki/Cuffy_(Guyanese_rebel)" title="Cuffy (Guyanese rebel)">Cuffy</a> or Kofi, <a href="/wiki/Cudjoe" title="Cudjoe">Cudjoe</a> or Kojo, and <a href="/wiki/Quamina" title="Quamina">Quamina</a> or Kwame/Kwamina.
</p><p>Most <a href="/wiki/Ghanaian_people" title="Ghanaian people">Ghanaians</a> have at least one name from this system, even if they also have an English or Christian name. Notable figures with day names include Ghana's first president <a href="/wiki/Kwame_Nkrumah" title="Kwame Nkrumah">Kwame Nkrumah</a> and former <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/UNSG" class="mw-redirect" title="UNSG">Secretary-General</a> <a href="/wiki/Kofi_Annan" title="Kofi Annan">Kofi Annan</a>.
</p><p>In the official orthography of the <a href="/wiki/Twi" title="Twi">Twi</a> language, the <a href="/wiki/Ashanti_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ashanti language">Ashanti</a> versions of these names as spoken in <a href="/wiki/Kumasi" title="Kumasi">Kumasi</a> are as follows. The diacritics on á a̍ à represent high, mid, and low tone (tone does not need to be marked on every vowel), while the diacritic on a̩ is used for vowel harmony and can be ignored. (Diacritics are frequently dropped in any case.) Variants of the names are used in other languages, or may represent different <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">transliteration</a> schemes. The variants mostly consist of different <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">affixes</a> (in Ashanti, <i>kwa-</i> or <i>ko-</i> for men and <i>a-</i> plus <i>-a</i> or <i>-wa</i> for women). For example, among the <a href="/wiki/Fante_people" title="Fante people">Fante</a>, the prefixes are <i>kwe-</i> and <i>e-</i>, respectively. Akan <i>d̩wo</i> is pronounced something like English <i>Joe</i>, but there do appear to be two sets of names for those born on Tuesday. 
</p>

href="#cite_note-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The family name (surname) are always given after close relatives and sometimes friends.<sup id="cite_ref-List_of_Ashanti_(Twi,_Asante)_Names_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-List_of_Ashanti_(Twi,_Asante)_Names-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Since Ashanti names are always given by the men if a couple receives a son as their first born-baby then the son is named after the father of the husband and if the baby is a girl then she will be named after her mother of the  husband.<sup id="cite_ref-List_of_Ashanti_(Twi,_Asante)_Names_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-List_of_Ashanti_(Twi,_Asante)_Names-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> As a result if the man is called <i>Osei Kofi</i> and his wife gives birth to a girl as their first born, the girl may be called <i>Yaa Dufie</i> even if she was not born on Friday.<sup id="cite_ref-List_of_Ashanti_(Twi,_Asante)_Names_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-List_of_Ashanti_(Twi,_Asante)_Names-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The reason is that the  mother of the  husband (<i>Osei Kofi</i>) is called <i>Yaa Dufie</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-List_of_Ashanti_(Twi,_Asante)_Names_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-List_of_Ashanti_(Twi,_Asante)_Names-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The Ashanti people usually give these names so that the names of close relatives be maintained in the families to show the love for their families.<sup id="cite_ref-List_of_Ashanti_(Twi,_Asante)_Names_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-List_of_Ashanti_(Twi,_Asante)_Names-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the olden days of <a href="/wiki/Ashanti_Region" title="Ashanti Region">Ashanti</a> it was a disgrace if an Ashanti man was not able to name any child after his father and/or  mother because that was the pride of every Ashanti household.<sup id="cite_ref-List_of_Ashanti_(Twi,_Asante)_Names_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-List_of_Ashanti_(Twi,_Asante)_Names-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Most of the ethnic-Ashanti family name (surname) given to boys could also be given to girls just by adding the letters "aa".<sup id="cite_ref-List_of_Ashanti_(Twi,_Asante)_Names_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-List_of_Ashanti_(Twi,_Asante)_Names-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Some Ashanti family names (surnames) can be given to both boys and girls without changing or adding anything.<sup id="cite_ref-List_of_Ashanti_(Twi,_Asante)_Names_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-List_of_Ashanti_(Twi,_Asante)_Names-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> However, there are other ethnic-Ashanti family name (surnames) that are exclusively male names, while others are exclusively female names.<sup id="cite_ref-List_of_Ashanti_(Twi,_Asante)_Names_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-List_of_Ashanti_(Twi,_Asante)_Names-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names_5-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Sociolinguistic_of_Ashanti_Personal_Names-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
