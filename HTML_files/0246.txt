<p><b>Ethnogenesis</b> (from <a href="/wiki/Greek_Language" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek Language">Greek</a> <i>ethnos</i> <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">ἔθνος</span>, "group of people, <a href="/wiki/Nation" title="Nation">nation</a>", and <i>genesis</i> <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">γένεσις</span>, "beginning, coming into being"; plural <b>ethnogeneses</b>) is "the formation and development of an ethnic group."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> This can originate through a process of self-identification as well as come about as the result of outside identification.
</p><p>The term is a mid-20th century neologism, and refers to the observable phenomenon of emergence of new social groups that are identified as having a cohesive identity, i.e. an "ethnic group" in anthropological terms. Relevant sciences do not only observe this phenomenon but search for explanation of its causes. The term <i>ethnogeny</i> is also used as a variant of <i>ethnogenesis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Passive_or_active_ethnogenesis">Passive or active ethnogenesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethnogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Passive or active ethnogenesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ethnogenesis can occur passively, in the accumulation of markers of group identity forged through interaction with the physical environment, cultural and religious divisions between sections of a society, migrations and other processes, for which ethnic subdivision is an unintended outcome. It can occur actively, as persons deliberately and directly 'engineer' separate identities to attempt to solve a political problem – the preservation or imposition of certain cultural values, power relations, etc.  Since the late eighteenth century, such attempts have often been related to <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a> revival or creation of a new language, in what eventually becomes a "<a href="/wiki/National_literature" class="mw-redirect" title="National literature">national literature</a>".
</p><p>In the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, societies challenged by the obsolescence of those <a href="/wiki/Narrative" title="Narrative">narratives</a> which previously afforded them coherence have fallen back on ethnic or racial narratives as a means of maintaining or reaffirming their collective identity, or <i>polis</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inclusive_or_exclusive_nationalism">Inclusive or exclusive nationalism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethnogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Inclusive or exclusive nationalism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Ethnogenesis can be promoted to include or exclude any ethnic minority living within a certain country. In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, the integrationalist policy of the <a href="/wiki/French_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="French Republic">French Republic</a> was inclusive; their laws stated all persons born or legally residing in France proper (including overseas departments and territories) were "Frenchmen".  The law did not make any ethnic distinctions nor racial categories in between the "French" people. All people in France were Frenchmen and became citizens of the French Republic as far the country's law was concerned.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language_revival">Language revival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethnogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Language revival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Language has been a critical asset for authenticating ethnic identities.  The process of reviving an antique ethnic identity often poses an immediate language challenge, as obsolescent languages  lack expressions for contemporary experiences.  In Europe in the 1990s, examples of proponents of ethnic revivals were from <a href="/wiki/Celts_(modern)" title="Celts (modern)">Celtic</a> fringes in <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> and nationalists in the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(greater_region)" title="Basque Country (greater region)">Basque Country</a>.  Activists' attempts since the 1970s to revive the <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">Occitan language</a> in <a href="/wiki/Southern_France" title="Southern France">Southern France</a> are a similar example.
</p><p>Similarly, in the 19th century, the <a href="/wiki/Fennoman" class="mw-redirect" title="Fennoman">Fennoman</a> <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Finland" title="Grand Duchy of Finland">Grand Duchy of Finland</a> aimed to raise the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish language</a> from peasant-status to the position of an official national language, which had been only <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a> for some time.  The Fennoman also founded the <a href="/wiki/Finnish_Party" title="Finnish Party">Finnish Party</a> to pursue their nationalist aims. The publication in 1835 of the Finnish <a href="/wiki/National_epic" title="National epic">national epic</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Kalevala" title="Kalevala">Kalevala</a></i>, was a founding stone of Finnish nationalism and ethnogenesis.  Finnish was recognized as the <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">official language</a> of Finland only in 1892.  Fennomans were opposed by the <a href="/wiki/Svecomans" class="mw-redirect" title="Svecomans">Svecomans</a>, headed by <a href="/wiki/Axel_Olof_Freudenthal" title="Axel Olof Freudenthal">Axel Olof Freudenthal</a> (1836–1911).  He supported continuing the use of <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a> as the official language; it had been a minority language used by the educated elite in government and administration. In line with contemporary <a href="/wiki/Scientific_racism" title="Scientific racism">scientific racism</a> theories, Freudenthal believed that Finland had two "<a href="/wiki/Race_(historical_definition)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (historical definition)">races</a>", one speaking Swedish and the other Finnish. The Svecomans claimed that the Swedish "Germanic race" was superior to the majority Finnish people. In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, revival of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish language</a> was part of the reclaiming of <a href="/wiki/Irish_people" title="Irish people">Irish</a> identity in the republic.
</p><p>Language has been an important and divisive political force in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> between the Dutch and Germanic <a href="/wiki/Flemings" class="mw-redirect" title="Flemings">Flemings</a> and Franco-Celtic <a href="/wiki/Walloons" title="Walloons">Walloons</a> since the kingdom was created in 1831. <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> is divided among Alemannic <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_German" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethnic German">German-speaking</a> or <i><a href="/wiki/Swiss_German" title="Swiss German">Deutschschweizer</a></i> against the <a href="/wiki/French_Swiss" class="mw-redirect" title="French Swiss">French-speaking</a> <i>Romands</i> or Arpitians, and the <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>/<a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">Lombard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romansch_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Romansch language">Romansh</a>-speaking minorities in the south and east.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, there were ethnological and linguistic differences between regional groups, from the <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">Lombardians</a> of the North to the <a href="/wiki/Sicily#Demographics" title="Sicily">Sicilians</a> of the south.  Mountainous terrain had allowed the development of relatively isolated communities and numerous dialects and languages before unification in the 19th century.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethnogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The set of cultural markers that accompanies each of the major <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religions</a> may become a component of distinct ethnic identities, but they almost never exist in isolation.  Ethnic definitions are subject to change over time, both within and outside groups. For example, 19th-century Europeans classified Jews and Arabs as one 'ethnic' bloc, the <a href="/wiki/Semitic_people" title="Semitic people">Semites</a> or <a href="/wiki/Hamitic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hamitic">Hamites</a>. Later, the term Hamites came to be associated with <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_African" class="mw-redirect" title="Sub-Saharan African">Sub-Saharan Africans</a> instead.
</p><p>Christian, Jewish, Hindu and Muslim followers have historically been aligned with ethnicities (and later nations) speaking different languages and having different cultures that arise on the basis of the languages that followers of each religion historically favoured:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit_Language" class="mw-redirect" title="Sanskrit Language">Sanskrit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>, respectively). The sources of religious differentiation are contested among <a href="/wiki/Sociology_of_religion" title="Sociology of religion">sociologists</a> and among <a href="/wiki/Anthropology_of_religion" title="Anthropology of religion">anthropologists</a>, as much as between the faith groups themselves.
</p><p>The line between a well-defined religious <a href="/wiki/Sect" title="Sect">sect</a> and a discrete ethnicity cannot always be sharply defined. Sects that most observers would accept as constituting a separate ethnicity usually have, as a minimum, a firm set of rules related to maintenance of <a href="/wiki/Endogamy" title="Endogamy">endogamy</a>, censuring those who 'marry out' or who fail to raise their children in the proper faith. Examples might include the <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amish" title="Amish">Amish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Druze" title="Druze">Druze</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mormons" title="Mormons">Mormons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sikh" class="mw-redirect" title="Sikh">Sikhs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yazidi" class="mw-redirect" title="Yazidi">Yazidi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zoroastrians" class="mw-redirect" title="Zoroastrians">Zoroastrians</a><sup id="cite_ref-Harrison_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harrison-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethnogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Geographical factors can lead to both cultural and <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetic</a> isolation from larger human societies.  Groups which settle remote habitats and intermarry over generations will acquire distinctive cultural and genetic traits, evolving from cultural continuity and through interaction with their unique environmental circumstances.  Ethnogenesis in these circumstances typically results in an identity that is less value-laden than one forged in contradistinction to competing populations.  Particularly in <a href="/wiki/Pastoral" title="Pastoral">pastoral</a> mountain peoples, social organization tends to hinge primarily on familial identification, not a wider <a href="/wiki/Collective_identity" title="Collective identity">collective identity</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Specific_cases">Specific cases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethnogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Specific cases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Greeks">Ancient Greeks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethnogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ancient Greeks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Anthony_D._Smith" title="Anthony D. Smith">Anthony D. Smith</a> notes that in general there is a lack of evidence which hampers the assessment of existence of nations or nationalisms in antiquity. The two cases where more evidence exists are those of ancient Greece and Israel. In Ancient Greece a cultural rather than political unity is observed. Yet, there were ethnic divisions within the wider Hellenic ethnic community, mainly the divisions between Ionians, Aeolians, Boeotians and Dorians. These groups were further divided into   city-states. Smith postulates that there is no more than a semblance of nationalism in ancient Greece.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Jonathan_M._Hall" title="Jonathan M. Hall">Jonathan M. Hall</a>'s work “Ethnic Identity in Greek Antiquity” (1997), was acclaimed as the first full-length modern study on Ancient Greek ethnicity. According to Hall, Ancient Greek ethnic identity was much based on kinship, descent and genealogy, which was reflected in elaborate genealogy myths. On his view, genealogy is the most fundamental way any population defines itself as an ethnic group. There was a change in the way Greeks constructed their ethnic identity in the Persian Wars period (first half of  5th c BC). Before that (archaic period) Greeks tended to attach themselves to one another by a process of genealogical assimilation. After the Persian invasion, they tended to define themselves against the enemy they perceived as the barbarian “other”. An indication of this disposition is the Athenians' speech to their allies in 480 BC, mentioning that all Hellenes are bound with the <i>homaimon</i> (“same blood”), <i>homoglosson</i> (same language) and common religious practices. Hall believes that Hellenic identity was clearly envisaged in the 6th c. BC as being ethnic in character, cultural forms of identification emerged in the 5th century, and there is evidence that by the 4th century this identity was conceived more in cultural terms.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Goths">Goths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethnogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Goths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Herwig Wolfram offers "a radically new explanation of the circumstances under which the <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a> were settled in <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">Spain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Since "they dissolved at their downfall into a myth accessible to everyone" at the head of a long history of attempts to lay claim to a "Gothic" tradition, the ethnogenesis by which disparate bands came to self-identify as "Goths" is of wide interest and application. The problem is in extracting a <a href="/wiki/Ethnohistory" title="Ethnohistory">historical ethnography</a> from sources that are resolutely Latin and Roman-oriented.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_Indian_North_American_Southwest">American Indian North American Southwest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethnogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: American Indian North American Southwest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the arrival of the Spanish in southwestern North America, the <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Jumano" class="mw-redirect" title="Jumano">Jumano</a> cultural sphere underwent social changes partly in reaction, which spurred their ethnogenesis, Clayton Anderson has observed.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Ethnogenesis in the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Texas_Plains&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Texas Plains (page does not exist)">Texas Plains</a> and along the coast took two forms: a disadvantaged group identified with a stronger group and became absorbed into it, on the one hand, and on the other hand, cultural institutions were modified and in a sense reinvented. The 17th-century Jumano disintegration, a collapse in part by the widespread deaths from introduced diseases, was followed by their reintegration as <a href="/wiki/Kiowa" title="Kiowa">Kiowa</a>, Nancy Hickerson has argued.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  Exterior stresses that produced ethnogenetic shifts preceded the arrival of the Spanish and their horse culture: recurring cycles of drought had previously forced non-kin to band together or to disband and mobilize. Intertribal hostilities forced weaker groups to associate with stronger ones.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Southeastern_North_American_Indians">Southeastern North American Indians</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethnogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Southeastern North American Indians">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1539 to 1543, a Spanish expedition led by <a href="/wiki/Hernando_de_Soto" title="Hernando de Soto">Hernando de Soto</a> departed <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> for <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Southeast" class="mw-redirect" title="American Southeast">American Southeast</a>.  Although asked to practice restraint, Soto led 600 men on a violent rampage through present-day Florida, Georgia, South Carolina, North Carolina, Tennessee, Alabama, Mississippi, Arkansas, and East Texas. Frustrated with not finding gold or silver in the areas suspected to contain such valuable materials, they destroyed villages and decimated native populations. Despite his death in 1542, Soto's men continued their expedition until 1543 when about half of their original force reached Mexico. Their actions introduced European diseases that further weakened native populations.  The population collapse forced natives to relocate from their cities into the countryside, where smaller villages and new political structures developed, replacing the older chiefdom models of tribal governance. By 1700, the major tribal settlements Soto and his men had encountered were no more.  Smaller tribes began to form loose confederations of smaller, more autonomous villages. From that blending of many tribes, ethnogenesis led to the emergence of new ethnic groups and identities for the consolidated natives who had managed to survive the invasion of European people, animals, and diseases.  After 1700, most North American Indian "tribes" were relatively new composite groups formed by these refugees who were trying to cope with massive epidemics and violence brought by the Europeans who were exploring the area.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_American_Aboriginals_in_the_Canadian_West">North American Aboriginals in the Canadian West</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethnogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: North American Aboriginals in the Canadian West">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The bringing of disease by the Europeans redistributed the indigenous communities and this caused a lot of war, disagreements (since they were going into each other's land and sacred areas), and a rise in mortality rates. Some Aboriginal groups were destroyed while some began to band together and create new Aboriginal groups within these areas of Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Creation_of_Moldovan_identity">Creation of Moldovan identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethnogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Creation of Moldovan identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The separate <a href="/wiki/Moldovans" title="Moldovans">Moldovan</a> ethnic identification was promoted under Soviet rule when the Soviet Union set up an autonomous <a href="/wiki/Moldavian_ASSR" class="mw-redirect" title="Moldavian ASSR">Moldavian Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic</a> in 1924. It was set apart from the <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_SSR" class="mw-redirect" title="Ukrainian SSR">Ukrainian SSR</a> on part of the territory between the <a href="/wiki/Dniester" title="Dniester">Dniester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_Bug" title="Southern Bug">Southern Bug</a> rivers (<a href="/wiki/Transnistria" title="Transnistria">Transnistria</a>). The scholar Charles King concluded<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> that this action was in part a prop to Soviet propaganda and help for a potential communist revolution in Romania. At first, a Moldovan ethnicity supported territorial claims to the then-Romanian territories of <a href="/wiki/Bessarabia" title="Bessarabia">Bessarabia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Bukovina" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Bukovina">Northern Bukovina</a>. The claims were based on the fact that the territory of eastern Bessarabia with Chisinau had belonged to the Russian Empire between 1812 and 1918. After having been for 500 years part of the Romanian Principality of Moldova, Russia was awarded the East of Moldova as a recompensation for its losses during the Napoleonic Wars: that was the beginning of the 100 years Russian history in East Moldova. After the <a href="/wiki/June_1940_Soviet_Ultimatum" class="mw-redirect" title="June 1940 Soviet Ultimatum">Soviet occupation of the two territories in 1940</a>, potential reunification claims were offset by the <a href="/wiki/Moldavian_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Moldavian Soviet Socialist Republic">Moldavian Soviet Socialist Republic</a>. At the establishment of the Moldavian ASSR, Chișinău was named its capital, a role which it continued to play after the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Moldavian_SSR" class="mw-redirect" title="Moldavian SSR">Moldavian SSR</a> in 1940.
</p><p>The recognition of <a href="/wiki/Moldovans" title="Moldovans">Moldovans</a> as a separate ethnicity, distinct from <a href="/wiki/Romanians" title="Romanians">Romanians</a>, remains today a controversial subject. On one side, the Moldovan Parliament adopted in 2003 "The Concept on National Policy of the Republic of Moldova", which states that Moldovans and Romanians are two distinct peoples and speak two different languages, Romanians form an ethnic minority in Moldova, and the Republic of Moldova is the legitimate successor to the Principality of Moldavia. However, Moldovans are recognized as a distinct ethnic group only by former Soviet states.
</p><p>Moreover, in Romania, people from Wallachia and Transylvania call the Romanians inhabiting western Moldavia, now part of Romania, as Moldovans. People in Romanian Moldova call themselves Moldovans, as subethnic denomination, and Romanians, as ethnic denomination (like Kentish and English for English people living in Kent). Romanians from Romania call the Romanians of the Republic of Moldova Bessarabians, as identification inside the subethnic group, Moldovans as subethnic group and Romanians as ethnic group. The subethnic groups referred to here are historically connected to independent Principalities. The Principality of Moldavia/Moldova founded in 1349 had various extensions between 1349 and 1859 and comprised Bucovina and Bessarabia as regional subdivisions. That way, Romanians of southern Bukovina (today part of Romania and formerly part of the historical Moldova) are called Bukovinans, Moldovans and Romanians.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/2004_Moldovan_Census" title="2004 Moldovan Census">2004 Moldovan Census</a>, of the 3,383,332 people living in Moldova, 16.5% (558,508) chose Romanian as their mother tongue, and 60% chose Moldovan. While 40% of all <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban</a> Romanian/Moldovan speakers indicated Romanian as their mother tongue, in the countryside, barely one out of seven Romanian/Moldovan speakers indicated Romanian as his mother tongue.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_scholarship">Historical scholarship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethnogenesis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Historical scholarship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Within the historical profession, the term "ethnogenesis" has been borrowed as a <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologism</a> to explain the origins and evolution of so-called <a href="/wiki/Barbarian" title="Barbarian">barbarian</a> ethnic cultures,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> stripped of its metaphoric connotations drawn from biology, of "natural" birth and growth. That view is closely associated with the Austrian historian <a href="/wiki/Herwig_Wolfram" title="Herwig Wolfram">Herwig Wolfram</a> and his followers, who argued that such ethnicity was not a matter of genuine genetic descent ("tribes").
</p><p>Rather, using Reinhard Wenskus' term <i>Traditionskerne</i> ("nuclei of tradition"),<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> ethnogenesis arose from small groups of aristocratic warriors carrying ethnic traditions from place to place and generation to generation. Followers would coalesce or disband around these nuclei of tradition; ethnicities were available to those who wanted to participate in them with no requirement of being born into a "tribe". Thus, questions of race and place of origin became secondary.
</p><p>Proponents of ethnogenesis may claim it is the only alternative to the sort of <a href="/wiki/Ethnocentric" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethnocentric">ethnocentric</a> and <a href="/wiki/Historiography_and_nationalism" class="mw-redirect" title="Historiography and nationalism">nationalist scholarship</a> that is commonly seen in disputes over the origins of many ancient peoples such as the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">Goths</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Huns" title="Huns">Huns</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  It has also been used as an alternative to the Near East's "race history" that had supported <a href="/wiki/Phoenicianism" title="Phoenicianism">Phoenicianism</a> and claims to the antiquity of the variously called <a href="/wiki/Assyrian/Chaldean/Syriac_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Assyrian/Chaldean/Syriac people">Assyrian/Chaldean/Syriac peoples</a>.
</p>
