<p><b>Placeholder names</b> are words that can refer to objects or people whose names do not exist, are temporarily forgotten, irrelevant, or unknown in the context in which they are being discussed.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_role">Linguistic role</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Placeholder_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Linguistic role">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>These <a href="/wiki/Free_variables_and_bound_variables" title="Free variables and bound variables">placeholders</a> typically function <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammatically</a> as <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">nouns</a> and can be used for people (e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/John_Doe" title="John Doe">John Doe, Jane Doe</a></i>), objects (e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Widget_(economics)" title="Widget (economics)">widget</a></i>), locations ("Main Street"), or places (e.g. <i>Anytown, USA</i>). They share a property with <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">pronouns</a>, because their <a href="/wiki/Reference" title="Reference">referents</a> must be supplied by context; but, unlike a pronoun, they may be used with no referent—the important part of the communication is not the thing nominally referred to by the placeholder, but the context in which the placeholder occurs.
</p><p>In their <i>Dictionary of American Slang</i> (1960), <a href="/wiki/Stuart_Berg_Flexner" title="Stuart Berg Flexner">Stuart Berg Flexner</a> and Harold Wentworth use the term <i>kadigan</i> for placeholder words. They define "kadigan" as a synonym for <i>thingamajig</i>. The term may have originated with <a href="/wiki/Willard_R._Espy" title="Willard R. Espy">Willard R. Espy</a>, though others, such as David Annis, also used it (or <i><b>cadigans</b></i>) in their writing. Its <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">etymology</a> is obscure—Flexner and Wentworth related it to the generic word <i>gin</i> for <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">engine</a> (as in the <i><a href="/wiki/Cotton_gin" title="Cotton gin">cotton gin</a></i>). It may also relate to the <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Irish</a> <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> <i>Cadigan</i>. <a href="/wiki/Hypernym" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypernym">Hypernyms</a> (words for generic categories; e.g., "flower" for <a href="/wiki/Tulip" title="Tulip">tulips</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rose" title="Rose">roses</a>) may also be used in this function of a placeholder, but they are not considered to be kadigans.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Placeholder_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Placeholder words exist in a highly informal <a href="/wiki/Register_(sociolinguistics)" title="Register (sociolinguistics)">register</a> of the English language. In formal speech and writing, words like <i>accessory</i>, <i>paraphernalia</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cultural_artifact" title="Cultural artifact">artifact</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Tool" title="Tool">instrument</a></i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Cookware_and_bakeware" title="Cookware and bakeware">utensil</a></i> are preferred; these words serve substantially the same function, but differ in connotation.
</p><p>Most of these words can be documented in at least the 19th century. <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a> wrote a short story entitled "The Literary Life of Thingum Bob, Esq"., showing that particular form to be in familiar use in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> in the 1840s. In <a href="/wiki/Gilbert_and_Sullivan" title="Gilbert and Sullivan">Gilbert and Sullivan</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mikado" title="The Mikado">The Mikado</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/W._S._Gilbert" title="W. S. Gilbert">W. S. Gilbert</a> makes the Lord High Executioner sing of a "little list" which includes:
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>... apologetic statesmen of a compromising kind,<br />
Such as: What d'ye call him: Thing'em-bob, and likewise: Never-mind,<br />
and 'St: 'st: 'st: and What's-his-name, and also You-know-who:<br />
The task of filling up the blanks I'd rather leave to you.
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Some fields have their own specific placeholder terminology. For example, "<a href="/wiki/Widget_(economics)" title="Widget (economics)">widget</a>" in economics, engineering and electronics, or "<a href="/wiki/Blackacre" title="Blackacre">Blackacre</a>" and "John Doe" or "Jane Doe" in law. "<a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">X-ray</a>" was originally a placeholder name for an unexplained phenomenon.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Companies_and_organizations">Companies and organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Placeholder_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Companies and organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>"Ace" and "Acme" were popular in company names as positioning words in alphabetical directories. They were generic, laudatory of whatever products they were used to promote and appeared at the beginning of most alpha-sorted lists. The <a href="/wiki/Acme_Corporation" title="Acme Corporation">Acme Corporation</a> of cartoon fame is one placeholder example. (<i>Acme</i> is a regular English word from the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a> <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">ἀκμή</span>, <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek language text">akme</i> meaning summit, highest point, extremity or peak, and thus sometimes used for "best".)</li>
<li>"Mom and Pop" (in the United States) are occasional placeholders for the individual owners of a generic, very small family business</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Main_Street" title="Main Street">Main Street</a> or <a href="/wiki/High_Street" title="High Street">High Street</a> for the business district of a small town or village, often contrasted as a commercial business entity against <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a> as the financial market of <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.</li>
<li>"Advent corporation" is a term used by lawyers to describe an as yet unnamed corporation, while legal incorporation documents are being prepared. In the case of <a href="/wiki/Advent_Corporation" title="Advent Corporation">Advent Corporation</a>, founder Henry Kloss decided to adopt this placeholder name as the formal legal name of his new company.</li>
<li>"NewCo" or "Newco" is used in a similar way in British English for an as-yet-unnamed company.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fictional_brand" title="Fictional brand">Fictional brands</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Morley_(cigarette)" title="Morley (cigarette)">Morley</a> are often used in television and cinema as placeholders to avoid unintended <a href="/wiki/Product_placement" title="Product placement">product placement</a>. "Brand X" has been used in television advertisements as a <a href="/wiki/Generic_brand" title="Generic brand">generic brand</a> representing any other brand than the one being advertised.</li>
<li>"XYZ Widget Company" has long been used in business and economics textbooks as a sample company. Also used as engraving text example on items such as plaques, trophy plates, etc. Occasionally appears on customizable promotional materials including stationery templates, business cards, advertising signage, cups, backpacks, and other "swag" samples.</li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/Contoso" title="Contoso">Contoso</a>" is used as a fictional business in <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>'s training materials.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Computing">Computing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Placeholder_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Computing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Metasyntactic_variable" title="Metasyntactic variable">Metasyntactic variable</a></div>
<p>Placeholder names are commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Computing" title="Computing">computing</a>:
</p>
<ul><li>Foo, Bar, Baz, and Qux (and combinations thereof) are commonly used as placeholders for <a href="/wiki/Computer_file" title="Computer file">file</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subroutine" title="Subroutine">function</a> and <a href="/wiki/Variable_(programming)" class="mw-redirect" title="Variable (programming)">variable</a> names. Foo and bar are derived from <a href="/wiki/Foobar" title="Foobar">foobar</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jargon_File" title="Jargon File">Hacker slang</a> includes a number of placeholders, such as <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/frob" class="extiw" title="wikt:frob">frob</a></i> which may stand for any small piece of equipment. <i>To frob</i>, likewise, means to do something to something. In practice it means to adjust (a device) in an aimless way.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alice_and_Bob" title="Alice and Bob">Alice and Bob</a>, alternatives for 'Person A'/'Person B' when describing processes in telecommunications; in cryptography Eve (the eavesdropper) is also added.</li>
<li><i>J. Random</i> X (e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/J._Random_Hacker" title="J. Random Hacker">J. Random Hacker</a></i>, <i>J. Random User</i>) is a term used in computer jargon for a randomly selected member of a set, such as the set of all users. Sometimes used as <i>J. Random Loser</i> for any not-very-computer-literate user.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Johnny/Jane Appleseed</i>, commonly used as a placeholder name by <a href="/wiki/Apple,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Apple, Inc.">Apple</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Contoso" title="Contoso">Contoso</a> is a fictional company name commonly used as an example in <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> documentation.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Domain_names">Domain names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Placeholder_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Domain names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Certain <a href="/wiki/Domain_name" title="Domain name">domain names</a> in the format <a href="/wiki/Example.com" title="Example.com">example</a><a href="/wiki/Top-level_domain" title="Top-level domain">.tld</a> (such as <i>example.com</i>, <i>example.net</i>, and <i>example.org</i>) are officially reserved as placeholders for the purpose of presentation.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Various example <a href="/wiki/Reserved_IP_addresses" title="Reserved IP addresses">reserved IP addresses</a> exist in <a href="/wiki/IPv4" title="IPv4">IPv4</a> and <a href="/wiki/IPv6" title="IPv6">IPv6</a>, such as <i><span class="ipaddr"><span style="padding-right: 1px;">192.0.2.0</span></span></i> in IPv4 documentation and <i><span class="ipaddr"><span style="padding-right: 1px;">2001:db8::</span></span></i> in IPv6 documentation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographical_locations">Geographical locations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Placeholder_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geographical locations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Placeholders such as <i>Main Street</i>, <i>Your County</i>, and <i>Anytown</i> are often used in sample mailing addresses. <i><a href="/wiki/Ruritania" title="Ruritania">Ruritania</a></i> is commonly used as a placeholder country.
</p><p><i>Something</i><a href="/wiki/-stan" title="-stan">-stan</a>, where <i>something</i> is often <a href="/wiki/Profanity" title="Profanity">profanity</a>, is commonly used as a placeholder for a <a href="/wiki/Middle_Eastern" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle Eastern">Middle Eastern</a> or <a href="/wiki/South_Asian" class="mw-redirect" title="South Asian">South Asian</a> country or for a politically disliked portion of one's own country.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Timbuktu" title="Timbuktu">Timbuktu</a></i>, which is also a real city, is often used to mean a place that is far away, in the middle of nowhere, and/or exotic.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Podunk" title="Podunk">Podunk</a></i> is used in <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a> for a hypothetical small town regarded as typically dull or insignificant, a place that you have likely never heard of, though still in the United States. Another example is <i>East Cupcake</i> to refer to a generic small town in the <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwestern United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_English" title="New Zealand English">New Zealand English</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Woop_Woop" title="Woop Woop">Woop Woops</a></i> (or, alternatively, <i>Wop-wops</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> is a (generally humorous) name for an out-of-the-way location, usually rural and sparsely populated. The similar <a href="/wiki/Australian_English" title="Australian English">Australian English</a> <i>Woop Woop</i>, (or, less frequently, <i>Woop Woops</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> can refer to any remote location, or outback town or district. Another New Zealand English term with a similar use is <a href="/wiki/Waikikamukau" title="Waikikamukau">Waikikamukau</a> ("Why kick a moo-cow"), a generic name for a small rural town.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bongo_Bongo_Land" title="Bongo Bongo Land">Bongo Bongo Land</a></i> (or <i>Bongo-bongo</i> Land) is a pejorative term used to refer to <a href="/wiki/Third_World" title="Third World">Third World</a> countries, particularly in Africa, or to a fictional such country.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal">Legal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Placeholder_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/John_Doe" title="John Doe">John Doe</a></i> and the variations <i>Jane Doe</i> (for females) and John Roe or Richard Roe (for a second party): used in legal action and cases when the true identity of a person is unknown or must be withheld for legal reasons. <i>Jane Roe</i> was used for the then-unidentified plaintiff (<a href="/wiki/Norma_McCorvey" title="Norma McCorvey">Norma Leah McCorvey</a>) in one of the most famous legal cases in United States history, <i><a href="/wiki/Roe_v._Wade" title="Roe v. Wade">Roe v. Wade</a></i>. These parties also appear in the <a href="/wiki/Legal_fiction" title="Legal fiction">legal fictions</a> of the action in <a href="/wiki/Ejectment" title="Ejectment">ejectment</a>, which was the usual proceeding to <a href="/wiki/Quiet_title" title="Quiet title">quiet title</a> to <a href="/wiki/Real_property" title="Real property">real property</a> under <a href="/wiki/Common_law_pleading" class="mw-redirect" title="Common law pleading">common law pleading</a>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Mopery" title="Mopery">Mopery</a></i>: used in informal legal discussions as a placeholder for some infraction, when the exact nature of the infraction is not important.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Blackacre" title="Blackacre">Blackacre</a></i> and its neighbors <i>Whiteacre</i>, <i>Greenacre</i>, <i>Brownacre</i>, <i>Greyacre</i>, <i>Pinkacre</i>, etc. are used as placeholders for parcels of real property, usually on Law School examinations and the several State Bar Exams. They are sometimes located in <i>Acre County</i> in the fictional <a href="/wiki/State_of_Franklin" title="State of Franklin">State of Franklin</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicine">Medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Placeholder_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>St. Elsewhere is often used as a placeholder name for any regional hospital or other care facility from which an admitted patient was referred. The medical slang is honored in the name of the 1980s television show of the <a href="/wiki/St._Elsewhere" title="St. Elsewhere">same name</a>.</li>
<li>GOMER (<i>g</i>et <i>o</i>ut of <i>m</i>y <i>e</i>mergency <i>r</i>oom) is a name in <a href="/wiki/Medical_slang" title="Medical slang">medical slang</a> for any patient who continually uses emergency room services for non-emergency conditions; its use is informal and <a href="/wiki/Pejorative" title="Pejorative">pejorative</a>. See <a href="/wiki/Gomer" title="Gomer">Gomer</a> for its possible origin.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Placeholder_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Often used in example names and addresses to indicate to the serviceman where to put his own details.
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Tommy_Atkins" title="Tommy Atkins">Tommy Atkins</a></i>, the generic name for a soldier of the British Army. Also, colloquially, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Oddie" title="Bill Oddie">Bill Oddie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rhyming_slang" title="Rhyming slang">rhyming slang</a> on the nickname 'squaddie'.</li>
<li>In the US Army and Air Force, Private (or Airman) Tentpeg and Snuffy are commonly used in examples (to explain various procedures) or cautionary tales. In the Marine Corps, Lance Corporal Schmuckatelli serves the same purpose.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In the US Coast Guard, a generic Coast Guardsman is referred to as Joe Coastie (or Jane).</li>
<li>In the Coast Guard, Navy, and Marines, a hypothetical member who has his act together is A.J. Squared-Away.</li>
<li>In the Canadian Armed Forces, the generic name for a soldier is Private/Corporal/rank Bloggins</li></ul>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a>, the fictional <a href="/wiki/Loamshire_Regiment" title="Loamshire Regiment">Loamshire Regiment</a> is used as a placeholder to provide examples for its procedures such as addressing mail or <a href="/wiki/Specimen_charges" title="Specimen charges">specimen charges</a> for violations of military law.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Numbers">Numbers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Placeholder_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Numbers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Indefinite_and_fictitious_numbers" title="Indefinite and fictitious numbers">indefinite and fictitious numbers</a></div>
<ul><li><i>Umpteen</i> is any annoyingly large number, as in the phrase "for the umpteenth time".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fictitious_telephone_number" title="Fictitious telephone number">Placeholder telephone numbers</a> are often allocated from ranges such as <a href="/wiki/555_(telephone_number)" title="555 (telephone number)">555</a> (where +1-areacode-555-1212 is reserved in North America for <a href="/wiki/Directory_assistance" title="Directory assistance">directory assistance</a> applications) to avoid generating <a href="/wiki/Wrong_number" class="mw-redirect" title="Wrong number">misdialled calls</a> to working numbers. In the United Kingdom, <a href="/wiki/Ofcom" title="Ofcom">Ofcom</a> has set aside a range of numbers in larger geographic area codes, as well as fictional area code 01632 (0632 having been the code for <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a> until replaced by 091 in the 1980s), for dramatic use.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Common placeholders for <a href="/wiki/Postcode" class="mw-redirect" title="Postcode">postcodes</a> in Canada include A1A 1A1 (a real postal code for Lower Battery Road, <a href="/wiki/St._John%27s,_Newfoundland" class="mw-redirect" title="St. John&#39;s, Newfoundland">St. John's, Newfoundland</a>) and K1A 0B2 (<a href="/wiki/Canada_Post" title="Canada Post">Canada Post</a> Place in <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a>). H0H 0H0 is reserved by Canada Post for fictional use (specifically for the mythical <a href="/wiki/Santa%27s_workshop" title="Santa&#39;s workshop">Santa's workshop</a>). In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/ZIP_Code" title="ZIP Code">ZIP Code</a> 90210 (from TV series <a href="/wiki/Beverly_Hills_90210" class="mw-redirect" title="Beverly Hills 90210">Beverly Hills 90210</a>) is frequently used. Numeric codes with repeated or sequential digits like 12345 (a <a href="/wiki/General_Electric" title="General Electric">General Electric</a> plant in <a href="/wiki/Schenectady,_New_York" title="Schenectady, New York">Schenectady, New York</a>), or 99999-9999 (unused but in a <a href="/wiki/List_of_ZIP_Code_prefixes" title="List of ZIP Code prefixes">prefix range</a> for the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Ketchikan,_Alaska" title="Ketchikan, Alaska">Ketchikan, Alaska</a>) may also appear. 00000, which lies in an unused prefix range, can be used without confusion.</li>
<li>In computing, some <a href="/wiki/Magic_number_(programming)" title="Magic number (programming)">magic numbers</a> (and other uses of hexadecimal numbers) apply <a href="/wiki/Hexspeak" title="Hexspeak">hexspeak</a> to create memorable hexadecimal values.</li></ul>
