<p><b>Representative democracy</b> (also <b>indirect democracy</b>, <b>representative government</b> or <b>psephocracy</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Types_of_democracy" title="Types of democracy">type of democracy</a> founded on the principle of elected officials representing a group of people, as opposed to <a href="/wiki/Direct_democracy" title="Direct democracy">direct democracy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Nearly all modern <a href="/wiki/Liberal_democracy" title="Liberal democracy">Western-style democracies</a> are types of representative democracies; for example, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Unitary_state" title="Unitary state">unitary</a>  <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_system" title="Parliamentary system">parliamentary</a> <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Unitary_state" title="Unitary state">unitary</a> <a href="/wiki/Semi-presidential_system" title="Semi-presidential system">semi-presidential</a> <a href="/wiki/Republic" title="Republic">republic</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Federation" title="Federation">federal</a> <a href="/wiki/Presidential_system" title="Presidential system">presidential republic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is an element of both the <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_system" title="Parliamentary system">parliamentary</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Presidential_system" title="Presidential system">presidential systems</a> of <a href="/wiki/Form_of_government" class="mw-redirect" title="Form of government">government</a> and is typically used in a <a href="/wiki/Lower_chamber" class="mw-redirect" title="Lower chamber">lower chamber</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons" title="House of Commons">House of Commons</a> of the United Kingdom, <a href="/wiki/Lok_Sabha" title="Lok Sabha">Lok Sabha</a> of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, and may be curtailed by constitutional constraints such as an <a href="/wiki/Upper_chamber" class="mw-redirect" title="Upper chamber">upper chamber</a>. It has been described by some political theorists including <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Dahl" title="Robert A. Dahl">Robert A. Dahl</a>, Gregory Houston and Ian Liebenberg as <a href="/wiki/Polyarchy" title="Polyarchy">polyarchy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In it the power is in the hands of the representatives who are elected by the people. Political parties are often central to this form of democracy because electoral systems require voters to vote for political parties as opposed to individual representatives.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Powers_of_representatives">Powers of representatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Representative_democracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Powers of representatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Representatives are elected by the public, as in national elections for the national legislature.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Elected representatives may hold the power to select other representatives, presidents, or other officers of the government or of the legislature, as the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister" class="mw-redirect" title="Prime Minister">Prime Minister</a> in the latter case. (indirect representation).
</p><p>The power of representatives is usually curtailed by a <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">constitution</a> (as in a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_democracy" class="mw-redirect" title="Constitutional democracy">constitutional democracy</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a>) or other measures to balance representative power:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<ul><li>An <a href="/wiki/Independent_judiciary" class="mw-redirect" title="Independent judiciary">independent judiciary</a>, which may have the power to declare legislative acts unconstitutional (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_court" title="Constitutional court">constitutional court</a>, <a href="/wiki/Supreme_court" title="Supreme court">supreme court</a>).</li>
<li>The constitution may also provide for some <a href="/wiki/Deliberative_democracy" title="Deliberative democracy">deliberative democracy</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Royal_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Commission">Royal Commissions</a>) or direct popular measures (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Initiative" title="Initiative">initiative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Referendum" title="Referendum">referendum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Recall_election" title="Recall election">recall elections</a>). However, these are not always binding and usually require some legislative action—legal power usually remains firmly with representatives.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Naming_conventions_(geographic_names)" title="Wikipedia:Naming conventions (geographic names)"><span title="The geographic scope near this tag is ambiguous. (July 2011)">where?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>
<li>In some cases, a <a href="/wiki/Bicameralism" title="Bicameralism">bicameral legislature</a> may have an "<a href="/wiki/Upper_house" title="Upper house">upper house</a>" that is not directly elected, such as the <a href="/wiki/Senate_of_Canada" title="Senate of Canada">Senate of Canada</a>, which was in turn modeled on the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Lords" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Lords">British House of Lords</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Theorists such as <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Burke#Democracy" title="Edmund Burke">Edmund Burke</a> believe that part of the duty of a representative was not simply to communicate the wishes of the electorate but also to use their own judgement in the exercise of their powers, even if their views are not reflective of those of a majority of voters:<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote><p>...it ought to be the happiness and glory of a representative to live in the strictest union, the closest correspondence, and the most unreserved communication with his constituents. Their wishes ought to have great weight with him; their opinion, high respect; their business, unremitted attention. It is his duty to sacrifice his repose, his pleasures, his satisfactions, to theirs; and above all, ever, and in all cases, to prefer their interest to his own. But his unbiassed opinion, his mature judgment, his enlightened conscience, he ought not to sacrifice to you, to any man, or to any set of men living. These he does not derive from your pleasure; no, nor from the law and the constitution. They are a trust from Providence, for the abuse of which he is deeply answerable. Your representative owes you, not his industry only, but his judgment; and he betrays, instead of serving you, if he sacrifices it to your opinion.</p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Representative_democracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Democratization" title="Democratization">Democratization</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">Roman Republic</a> was the first known government in the western world to have a representative government, despite taking the form of a direct government in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_assemblies" title="Roman assemblies">Roman assemblies</a>. The Roman model of governance inspired many political thinkers over the centuries,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and today's modern representative democracies imitate more the Roman than the Greek models because it was a state in which supreme power was held by the people and their elected representatives, and which had an elected or nominated leader.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Representative democracy is a form of democracy in which people vote for representatives who then vote on policy initiatives as opposed to a direct democracy, a form of democracy in which people vote on policy initiatives directly.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> A European medieval tradition of selecting representatives from the various <a href="/wiki/Estates_of_the_realm" title="Estates of the realm">estates</a> (<a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">classes</a>, but not as we know them today) to advise/control monarchs led to relatively wide familiarity with representative systems inspired by Roman systems.
</p><p>In Britain, <a href="/wiki/Simon_de_Montfort,_6th_Earl_of_Leicester" title="Simon de Montfort, 6th Earl of Leicester">Simon de Montfort</a> is remembered as one of the fathers of representative government for holding two famous parliaments.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Parliament_(1258)" title="Oxford Parliament (1258)">The first</a>, in 1258, stripped the King of unlimited authority and the second, in 1265, included <a href="/wiki/Simon_de_Montfort%27s_Parliament" title="Simon de Montfort&#39;s Parliament">ordinary citizens from the towns</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dnb_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dnb-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Later, in the 17th century, the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_England" title="Parliament of England">Parliament of England</a> pioneered some of the ideas and systems of <a href="/wiki/Liberal_democracy#Origins" title="Liberal democracy">liberal democracy</a> culminating in the <a href="/wiki/Glorious_Revolution" title="Glorious Revolution">Glorious Revolution</a> and passage of the <a href="/wiki/Bill_of_Rights_1689" title="Bill of Rights 1689">Bill of Rights 1689</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-refIIP_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-refIIP-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a> led to the creation of a new <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Constitution">Constitution</a> of the United States in 1787, with a national legislature based partly on direct elections of representatives every two years, and thus responsible to the electorate for continuance in office. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senators</a> were not directly elected by the people until the adoption of the <a href="/wiki/Seventeenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Seventeenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Seventeenth Amendment</a> in 1913.  Women, men who owned no property, and blacks, and others not originally given voting rights in most states eventually <a href="/wiki/Voting_rights_in_the_United_States#Background" title="Voting rights in the United States">gained the vote through changes in state and federal law</a> in the course of the 19th and 20th centuries. Until it was repealed by the <a href="/wiki/Fourteenth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Fourteenth Amendment</a> following the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Three-Fifths_Compromise" title="Three-Fifths Compromise">Three-Fifths Compromise</a> gave a disproportionate representation of slave states in the House of Representatives relative to the voters in free states.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1789, <a href="/wiki/Revolutionary_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Revolutionary France">Revolutionary France</a> adopted the <a href="/wiki/Declaration_of_the_Rights_of_Man_and_of_the_Citizen" title="Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen">Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen</a> and, although short-lived, the <a href="/wiki/National_Convention" title="National Convention">National Convention</a> was elected by all males in 1792.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Universal_male_suffrage" class="mw-redirect" title="Universal male suffrage">Universal male suffrage</a> was re-established in France in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution_of_1848" title="French Revolution of 1848">French Revolution of 1848</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Representative democracy came into particular general favour in post-<a href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial revolution">industrial revolution</a> <a href="/wiki/Nation_state" title="Nation state">nation states</a> where large numbers of <a href="/wiki/Citizenship" title="Citizenship">citizens</a> evinced interest in <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">politics</a>, but where technology and population figures remained unsuited to direct democracy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> As noted above, <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Burke" title="Edmund Burke">Edmund Burke</a> in his speech to the electors of <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a> classically analysed their operation in Britain and the rights and duties of an elected representative.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:USHouseStructure2012-2022_SeatsByState.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/USHouseStructure2012-2022_SeatsByState.png/220px-USHouseStructure2012-2022_SeatsByState.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="116" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/USHouseStructure2012-2022_SeatsByState.png/330px-USHouseStructure2012-2022_SeatsByState.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/USHouseStructure2012-2022_SeatsByState.png/440px-USHouseStructure2012-2022_SeatsByState.png 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="1053" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:USHouseStructure2012-2022_SeatsByState.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The U.S. House of Representatives, one example of representative democracy</div></div></div>
<p>Globally, a majority of the world's people live in representative democracies including constitutional monarchies and republics with strong representative branches.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research_on_representation_per_se">Research on representation <i>per se</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Representative_democracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Research on representation per se">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Representation_(politics)" title="Representation (politics)">Representation (politics)</a></div>
<p>Separate but related, and very large, bodies of research in political philosophy and social science investigate how and how well elected representatives, such as legislators, represent the interests or preferences of one or another constituency.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Representative_democracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his book <i><a href="/wiki/Political_Parties_(book)" class="mw-redirect" title="Political Parties (book)">Political Parties</a></i>, written in 1911, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Michels" title="Robert Michels">Robert Michels</a> argues that most representative systems deteriorate towards an <a href="/wiki/Oligarchy" title="Oligarchy">oligarchy</a> or <a href="/wiki/Particracy" title="Particracy">particracy</a>. This is known as the <a href="/wiki/Iron_law_of_oligarchy" title="Iron law of oligarchy">iron law of oligarchy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
Representative democracies which are stable have been analysed by <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Gasser" title="Adolf Gasser">Adolf Gasser</a> and compared to the unstable representative democracies in his book "Gemeindefreiheit als Rettung Europas" which was published in 1943 (first edition in German) and a second edition in 1947 (in German).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Adolf Gasser stated the following requirements for a representative democracy in order to remain stable, unaffected by the iron law of oligarchy: 
</p>
<ul><li>Society has to be built up from bottom to top. As a consequence, society is built up by people, who are free and have the power to defend themselves with weapons.</li>
<li>These free people join or form local communities. These local communities are independent, which includes financial independence, and they are free to determine their own rules.</li>
<li>Local communities join together into a higher unit e.g. a canton.</li>
<li>There is no hierarchical bureaucracy.</li>
<li>There is competition between these local communities e.g. on services delivered or on taxes.</li></ul>
<p>A drawback to this type of government is that elected officials are not required to fulfill promises made before their election and are able to promote their own self-interests once elected, providing an incohesive system of governance.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Legislators are also under scrutiny as the system of majority-won legislators voting for issues for the large group of people fosters inequality among the marginalized.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_solutions">Proposed solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Representative_democracy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Proposed solutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The system of <a href="/wiki/Stochocracy" class="mw-redirect" title="Stochocracy">stochocracy</a> has been proposed as an improved system compared to the system of representative democracy, where representatives are elected. Stochocracy aims to at least reduce this degradation by having all representatives appointed by lottery instead of by voting. Therefore, this system is also called lottocracy. The system was proposed by the writer Roger de Sizif in 1998 in his book <i>La Stochocratie</i>. Choosing officeholders by lot was also the standard practice in ancient <a href="/wiki/Athenian_democracy" title="Athenian democracy">Athenian democracy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The rationale behind this practice was to avoid lobbying and electioneering by economic oligarchs.
</p><p>The system of <a href="/wiki/Deliberative_democracy" title="Deliberative democracy">deliberative democracy</a> is a mix between a majority ruled system and a consensus-based system. It allows for representative democracies or direct democracies to coexist with its system of governance, providing an initial advantage.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
