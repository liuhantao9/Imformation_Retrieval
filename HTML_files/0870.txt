<p>The <b>Yorkshire Terrier</b> is a small <a href="/wiki/Dog_breed" title="Dog breed">dog breed</a> of <a href="/wiki/Terrier" title="Terrier">terrier</a> <a href="/wiki/Dog_type" title="Dog type">type</a>, developed during the 19th century in <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>, England.<sup id="cite_ref-Get_to_Know_the_Yorkshire_Terrier_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Get_to_Know_the_Yorkshire_Terrier-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Ideally its maximum size is 7 pounds (3.2&#160;kg).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> A popular <a href="/wiki/Companion_dog" title="Companion dog">companion dog</a>, the Yorkshire Terrier has also been part of the development of other breeds, such as the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Silky_Terrier" title="Australian Silky Terrier">Silky Terrier</a>. It has a grey, black and tan coat, and the breed's nickname is Yorkie.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Yorkshire_Terrier_from_1915.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Yorkshire_Terrier_from_1915.JPG/220px-Yorkshire_Terrier_from_1915.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="253" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Yorkshire_Terrier_from_1915.JPG/330px-Yorkshire_Terrier_from_1915.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Yorkshire_Terrier_from_1915.JPG/440px-Yorkshire_Terrier_from_1915.JPG 2x" data-file-width="920" data-file-height="1058" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Yorkshire_Terrier_from_1915.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Yorkshire Terrier from 1915</div></div></div>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ernest_Gustave_Girardot04.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Ernest_Gustave_Girardot04.jpg/220px-Ernest_Gustave_Girardot04.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="286" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Ernest_Gustave_Girardot04.jpg/330px-Ernest_Gustave_Girardot04.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Ernest_Gustave_Girardot04.jpg/440px-Ernest_Gustave_Girardot04.jpg 2x" data-file-width="528" data-file-height="686" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ernest_Gustave_Girardot04.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A painting from the 19th century depicting a Yorkshire-like terrier by <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Gustave_Girardot" title="Ernest Gustave Girardot">Ernest Gustave Girardot</a></div></div></div>
<p>The Yorkshire Terrier (also called the "Yorkie") originated in <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>, a county in northern England (and the adjoining <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Get_to_Know_the_Yorkshire_Terrier_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Get_to_Know_the_Yorkshire_Terrier-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Weston_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weston-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In the mid-19th century, workers from Scotland came to Yorkshire in search of work and brought with them several different varieties of small terriers. Breeding of the Yorkshire Terrier was "principally accomplished by the people&#8212;mostly operatives in cotton and woollen mills&#8212;in the counties of Yorkshire and Lancashire."<sup id="cite_ref-Shields_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shields-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Details are scarce. Mrs A. Foster is quoted as saying in 1886, "If we consider that the mill operatives who originated the breed...were nearly all ignorant men, unaccustomed to imparting information for public use, we may see some reason why reliable facts have not been easily attained."<sup id="cite_ref-Foster_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Foster-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The breed comes from three different dogs, a male named Old Crab, a female named Kitty, and another female whose name is not known.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Paisley_Terrier" title="Paisley Terrier">Paisley Terrier</a>, a smaller version of the <a href="/wiki/Skye_Terrier" title="Skye Terrier">Skye Terrier</a> that was bred for a beautiful long silky coat, also figured into the early dogs. Some authorities believed that the <a href="/wiki/Maltese_(dog)" class="mw-redirect" title="Maltese (dog)">Maltese</a> was used as well.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> "They were all originally bred from Scotch Terriers (note: meaning dogs from Scotland, not today's <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Terrier" title="Scottish Terrier">Scottish Terrier</a>) and shown as such...the name Yorkshire Terrier was given to them on account of their being improved so much in Yorkshire."<sup id="cite_ref-Foster_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Foster-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Yorkshire Terriers were shown in a dog show category (class) at the time called "Rough and Broken-coated, Broken-haired Scotch and Yorkshire Terriers". Hugh Dalziel, writing in 1878, says that "the classification of these dogs at shows and in the Kennel Club Stud Book is confusing and absurd" in lumping together these different types.<sup id="cite_ref-British_Islands_1878,_pg_213_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-British_Islands_1878,_pg_213-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the early days of the breed, "almost anything in the shape of a Terrier having a long coat with blue on the body and fawn or silver coloured head and legs, with tail docked and ears trimmed, was received and admired as a Yorkshire Terrier".<sup id="cite_ref-Shields_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shields-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> But in the late 1860s, a popular Paisley-type Yorkshire Terrier show dog named <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_Ben" title="Huddersfield Ben">Huddersfield Ben</a>, owned by a woman living in Yorkshire, Mary Ann Foster, was seen at dog shows throughout Great Britain, and defined the breed type for the Yorkshire Terrier.<sup id="cite_ref-Clark/Brace_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clark/Brace-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Huddersfield_Ben">Huddersfield Ben</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Huddersfield Ben">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_Ben" title="Huddersfield Ben">Huddersfield Ben</a></div>
<p>Huddersfield Ben was a famous dog. His portrait was painted by <a href="/wiki/George_Earl_(painter)" title="George Earl (painter)">George Earl</a><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and in 1891 an authority on the breed wrote, "Huddersfield Ben was the best stud dog of his breed during his lifetime, and one of the most remarkable dogs of any pet breed that ever lived; and most of the show specimens of the present day have one or more crosses of his blood in their pedigree."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> A show winner, Huddersfield Ben quickly became the type of dog everyone wanted, and through his puppies has defined the breed as we know it today. He is still referred to as "father of the breed."<sup id="cite_ref-Clark/Brace_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clark/Brace-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="In_North_America">In North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Yorkshire Terrier was introduced in North America in 1872<sup id="cite_ref-Weston_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Weston-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and the first Yorkshire Terrier was registered with the American Kennel Club (AKC) in 1885.<sup id="cite_ref-Get_to_Know_the_Yorkshire_Terrier_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Get_to_Know_the_Yorkshire_Terrier-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Coile5_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coile5-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian era</a>, the Yorkshire Terrier was a popular pet, and show dog in England, and as Americans embraced Victorian customs, so too did they embrace the Yorkshire Terrier.<sup id="cite_ref-Yorkshire_Terrier_-_Yahoo!_Pets_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yorkshire_Terrier_-_Yahoo!_Pets-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The breed's popularity dipped in the 1940s, when the percentage of small breed dogs registered fell to an all-time low of 18% of total registrations.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Smoky_(dog)" title="Smoky (dog)">Smoky</a>, a Yorkshire Terrier and famous war dog from World War II, is credited with beginning a renewal of interest in the breed.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/American_Kennel_Club" title="American Kennel Club">American Kennel Club</a> ranked the Yorkshire Terrier as the 6th most popular pure-breed in the United States of America in 2012 and 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-Get_to_Know_the_Yorkshire_Terrier_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Get_to_Know_the_Yorkshire_Terrier-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Coat">Coat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Coat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For adult Yorkshire Terriers, importance is placed on coat colour, quality and texture.<sup id="cite_ref-AKCstandard_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AKCstandard-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The hair must be glossy, fine, straight and silky. Traditionally the coat is grown out very long and is parted down the middle of the back, but "must never impede movement."<sup id="cite_ref-KCstandard_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KCstandard-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From the back of the neck to the base of the tail, the coat should be a dark grey to a black colour, and the hair on the tail should be a darker black. On the head, high chest and legs, the hair should be a bright, rich tan, darker at the roots than in the middle, that shades into a lighter tan at the tips, but not for all dogs. Also, in adult dogs there should be no black hairs intermingled with any of the tan-coloured fur. The fine, straight, silky coat is considered  <a href="/wiki/Hypoallergenic" title="Hypoallergenic">hypoallergenic</a>.
</p>

<p>Adult Yorkshire Terriers whose coat colours deviate from the standard, or that have woolly or extra fine coats, are still Yorkshire Terriers. The only difference is that atypical Yorkshire Terriers should not intentionally be bred.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In addition, care may be more difficult for "woolly" or "cottony" textured coats, or coats that are overly fine.<sup id="cite_ref-AKCstandard_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AKCstandard-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> One of the reasons given for not breeding "off-coloured" Yorkies is that the colour could be a potential indicator of a genetic defect that may affect the dog's health; a careful health screening can clarify if any health risks exist.<sup id="cite_ref-YTCAcolour_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YTCAcolour-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Coats may vary in colour. For example, a grown Yorkie may have a silver/blue with light brown, while another might have a black and creamy colour.
</p><p>The long coat on the Yorkshire Terrier means that the breed requires regular brushing.<sup id="cite_ref-Get_to_Know_the_Yorkshire_Terrier_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Get_to_Know_the_Yorkshire_Terrier-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hypoallergenic_coats">Hypoallergenic coats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hypoallergenic coats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hypoallergenic_dog_breed" title="Hypoallergenic dog breed">Hypoallergenic dog breed</a></div>
<p>The typical fine, straight and silky Yorkshire Terrier <a href="/wiki/Coat_(dog)" title="Coat (dog)">coat</a> has also been listed by many popular dog information websites as being <a href="/wiki/Hypoallergenic_dog_breed" title="Hypoallergenic dog breed">hypoallergenic</a>. In comparison with many other breeds, Yorkies do not shed to the same degree, only losing small amounts when bathed or brushed.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and it is the dog's dander and saliva that trigger most allergic reactions.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Allergists do recognise that at times a particular allergy patient will be able to tolerate a particular dog, but they agree that "the luck of the few with their pets cannot be stretched to fit all allergic people and entire breeds of dogs."<sup id="cite_ref-NYTallergy_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTallergy-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The Yorkshire Terrier coat is said to fall out only when brushed or broken, or just said to not shed.<sup id="cite_ref-Wash_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wash-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Although neither of those statements agree with what biologists, veterinarians and allergists know about dog fur, allergists "think there really are differences in protein production between dogs that may help one patient and not another".<sup id="cite_ref-NYTallergy_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTallergy-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_colours">Other colours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Other colours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Origin_of_Biewer_and_Parti-Color_Yorkshire_Terrier.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Origin_of_Biewer_and_Parti-Color_Yorkshire_Terrier.png/220px-Origin_of_Biewer_and_Parti-Color_Yorkshire_Terrier.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Origin_of_Biewer_and_Parti-Color_Yorkshire_Terrier.png/330px-Origin_of_Biewer_and_Parti-Color_Yorkshire_Terrier.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Origin_of_Biewer_and_Parti-Color_Yorkshire_Terrier.png/440px-Origin_of_Biewer_and_Parti-Color_Yorkshire_Terrier.png 2x" data-file-width="1616" data-file-height="1238" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Origin_of_Biewer_and_Parti-Color_Yorkshire_Terrier.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Common origin of Biewer and particolour Yorkshire Terrier from purebred Blue and Tan Yorkshire Terriers with ancesters, that were <a href="/wiki/Conductor_of_recessive_gene_(genetics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Conductor of recessive gene (genetics)">carriers</a> of the recessive <a href="/wiki/Piebald" title="Piebald">Piebald</a> gene.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The Yorkshire Terrier is a tan dog with a blue saddle. Particolours exist, although they are not correct for the breed standard. The particolour coat is white with black/blue and tan. The white is caused by the <a href="/wiki/Recessive" class="mw-redirect" title="Recessive">recessive</a> <a href="/wiki/Piebald" title="Piebald">piebald gene</a>. It is very rare to get a particolour, and if one is found, it tends to be very expensive.<sup id="cite_ref-YTCAparti_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YTCAparti-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Some Yorkshire Terriers are solid golden, they only produce <a href="/wiki/Melanin#Pheomelanin" title="Melanin">pheomelanin</a>, others are liver or chocolate, a brown colour; they only produce brown <a href="/wiki/Melanin#Eumelanin" title="Melanin">eumelanin</a> but are unable to produce black eumelanin.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The standard prescribes clearly defined fur-colors, and non-standard colours may indicate crossbreeding with other breeds or in rare cases even health problems.<sup id="cite_ref-YTCAcolour_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YTCAcolour-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The AKC registration form for Yorkshire Terriers allows for four choices: blue and tan, blue and gold, black and tan, black and gold. Colour alone will not affect whether or not a dog is a good companion and pet. Even though off-coloured Yorkshire Terriers are advertised at premium prices, being of an unusual or atypical colour is stated to be neither new, desirable, nor exotic.<sup id="cite_ref-YTCAparti_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YTCAparti-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Until recently, mismatched Yorkshire Terriers could be crossed with Biewer Terriers, a new breed that originated in Germany from particoloured Yorkshire Terriers.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Although the American Kennel Club will not deny registration of a Yorkshire Terrier on colour alone, meaning that particolours are now registerable with the AKC, the Yorkshire Terrier Club of America has a directive that "any solid colour or combination of colours other than black and tan" for adult dogs is a disqualification, and "dogs of solid colour, unusual combination of colours, and particolours should be disqualified."<sup id="cite_ref-YTCA_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YTCA-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Puppy_coats">Puppy coats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Puppy coats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Winston,_a_Yorkshire_Terrier_puppy.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Winston%2C_a_Yorkshire_Terrier_puppy.jpg/220px-Winston%2C_a_Yorkshire_Terrier_puppy.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Winston%2C_a_Yorkshire_Terrier_puppy.jpg/330px-Winston%2C_a_Yorkshire_Terrier_puppy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Winston%2C_a_Yorkshire_Terrier_puppy.jpg/440px-Winston%2C_a_Yorkshire_Terrier_puppy.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3763" data-file-height="2519" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Winston,_a_Yorkshire_Terrier_puppy.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Yorkshire Terrier puppy, displaying a black and tan coat</div></div></div>
<p>It may take three or more years for the coat to reach its final colour. The final colour is usually a black or greyish colour.<sup id="cite_ref-YTCA_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YTCA-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> P. H. Combs, writing in 1891, complained about show wins awarded to puppies, when the dog's coat does not fully come in until three or four years old, "and the honor of winning such a prize (for a puppy) can therefore be of but little practical benefit to the owner" since the adult dog's colour cannot be exactly predicted.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ytca-standard_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ytca-standard-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coat_care">Coat care</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Coat care">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Yorkie_or_Yorkshire_Terrier_(11406003593).png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Yorkie_or_Yorkshire_Terrier_%2811406003593%29.png/220px-Yorkie_or_Yorkshire_Terrier_%2811406003593%29.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="103" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Yorkie_or_Yorkshire_Terrier_%2811406003593%29.png/330px-Yorkie_or_Yorkshire_Terrier_%2811406003593%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Yorkie_or_Yorkshire_Terrier_%2811406003593%29.png/440px-Yorkie_or_Yorkshire_Terrier_%2811406003593%29.png 2x" data-file-width="1082" data-file-height="508" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Yorkie_or_Yorkshire_Terrier_(11406003593).png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Yorkshire Terrier after a day at the groomer</div></div></div>
<p>Owners may trim the hair short for easier care. For shows, the coat is left long, and may be trimmed to floor length to give ease of movement and a neater appearance. Hair on the feet and the tips of ears can also be trimmed.<sup id="cite_ref-AKCstandard_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AKCstandard-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-KCstandard_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KCstandard-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The traditional long coat is extremely high maintenance. The coat might get knotted if not brushed daily. In order to prevent breakage, the coat may be wrapped in rice paper, tissue paper or plastic, after a light oiling with a coat oil. The oil has to be washed out once a month and the wraps must be fixed periodically during the week to prevent them from sliding down and breaking the hair. Elaborate coat care dates from the earliest days of the breed. In 1878, John Walsh described similar preparations: the coat is "well greased" with coconut oil, the dog is bathed weekly, and the dog's feet are "carefully kept in stockings".<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Temperament">Temperament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Temperament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Henri_the_yorki_from_alabama_.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Henri_the_yorki_from_alabama_.jpg/220px-Henri_the_yorki_from_alabama_.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Henri_the_yorki_from_alabama_.jpg/330px-Henri_the_yorki_from_alabama_.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Henri_the_yorki_from_alabama_.jpg/440px-Henri_the_yorki_from_alabama_.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3024" data-file-height="4032" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Henri_the_yorki_from_alabama_.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Yorkshire Terrier character is described as "conveying an important air". According to the standard the dog's high head carriage and confident manner should give the appearance of vigor and self importance.</div></div></div>
<p>The ideal Yorkshire Terrier character or "personality" is described with a "carriage very upright feisty" and "conveying an important air".<sup id="cite_ref-KCstandard_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KCstandard-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Though small, the Yorkshire Terrier is active, very overprotective, curious and fond of attention.  Mentally sound and emotionally secure ones should normally not show the soft submissive temperament seen in <a href="/wiki/Lap_dog" title="Lap dog">lap dogs</a> although many exhibit this behaviour due to improper training.  Because of this, it is advised that a Yorkie would not be suitable for a home with typical young children. Instead, they make ideal companions for older families with many more reputable breeders routinely only homing to families with children older than about 10 years for the comfort of the dog, but more so for the benefit of the child.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Yorkshire Terriers are an easy dog breed to train. This results from their own nature to work without human assistance. They are naturally smart and quick to learn with many being food and or praise motivated.  Because they were developed as a working breed many need a lot of both physical and mental stimulation—with both long walks/runs but also indoor games and training to keep their mind busy. They are known for being yappy, but many have reported that a contented Yorkie is a quiet one—that will happily curl up on your knee in the evening. However, they are all individuals, with some being much more laid back than others and the breeder should ideally be able to advise on the needs and temperaments of their particular line. Yorkies are easily adaptable to all surroundings, travel well and make suitable pets for many homes. Due to their small size, they require limited exercise but need daily interaction with people. They thrive on attention and love. Many however are more timid around other dogs and prefer to stay close to their humans for comfort.<sup id="cite_ref-Get_to_Know_the_Yorkshire_Terrier_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Get_to_Know_the_Yorkshire_Terrier-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Yorkshire Terriers do tend to <a href="/wiki/Bark_(sound)" title="Bark (sound)">bark</a> a lot. This makes them excellent watchdogs, as they will sound the alarm when anyone gets close. However, this barking problem can be resolved with proper training and exercise.
</p><p>Yorkshire Terriers are ranked 34th in <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Coren" title="Stanley Coren">Stanley Coren</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Intelligence_of_Dogs" title="The Intelligence of Dogs">The Intelligence of Dogs</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Health issues often seen in the Yorkshire Terrier include <a href="/wiki/Bronchitis" title="Bronchitis">bronchitis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lymphangiectasia" title="Lymphangiectasia">lymphangiectasia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portosystemic_shunt" title="Portosystemic shunt">portosystemic shunt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cataract" title="Cataract">cataracts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Keratoconjunctivitis_sicca" class="mw-redirect" title="Keratoconjunctivitis sicca">keratitis sicca</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lane_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lane-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, Yorkies often have a delicate <a href="/wiki/Digestive_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Digestive system">digestive system</a>, with vomiting or diarrhoea resulting from consumption of foods outside of a regular diet.<sup id="cite_ref-Shultz_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shultz-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The relatively small size of the Yorkshire Terrier means that it usually has a poor tolerance for <a href="/wiki/Anaesthesia" class="mw-redirect" title="Anaesthesia">anaesthesia</a>. Additionally, a toy dog such as the Yorkie is more likely to be injured by falls, other dogs and owner clumsiness.<sup id="cite_ref-Shultz_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shultz-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Injection reactions (inflammation or hair loss at the site of an injection) can occur. In addition allergies can cause skin to be dry, itchy and/or red.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Life_expectancy" title="Life expectancy">life span</a> of a Yorkie is 13–20 years.<sup id="cite_ref-Shultz_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shultz-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-doi10.1016.2Fj.tvjl.2013.09.020_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-doi10.1016.2Fj.tvjl.2013.09.020-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Undersized Yorkies (under 3&#160;lb&#160;[1.4&#160;kg]) generally have a shorter life span, as they are especially prone to health problems such as chronic <a href="/wiki/Diarrhoea" class="mw-redirect" title="Diarrhoea">diarrhoea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vomiting" title="Vomiting">vomiting</a>; are more sensitive to anaesthesia; and are more easily injured.<sup id="cite_ref-Shultz_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shultz-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Teeth">Teeth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Teeth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Morkie_puppy_retained_teeth.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Morkie_puppy_retained_teeth.jpg/220px-Morkie_puppy_retained_teeth.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Morkie_puppy_retained_teeth.jpg/330px-Morkie_puppy_retained_teeth.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Morkie_puppy_retained_teeth.jpg/440px-Morkie_puppy_retained_teeth.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="1333" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Morkie_puppy_retained_teeth.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Morkie (half Maltese, half Yorkshire Terrier) puppy with his deciduous teeth and adult teeth growing in</div></div></div>
<p>As with all other dogs, Yorkies have two sets of teeth in their life. The first set of teeth is the 28-piece deciduous teeth (often referred to as "milk teeth", "baby teeth" or "puppy teeth"). The second set is the 42-piece permanent or adult teeth. Sometimes the number of permanent or adult teeth may vary, which is fine as long as they do not cause bad bite. When puppies are born, they have no teeth because milk is the only food they need. The deciduous teeth will grow from the age of 3 to 8 weeks old, in the order of incisors, canine/ fangs and premolars. Yorkie puppies have no molar teeth. Yorkie puppies will start to lose their deciduous or baby teeth when the permanent or adult teeth come in. The permanent or adult grow when the Yorkie puppies are 4 to 8 months old. By around 8 months old, those teeth should fully develop. The permanent or adult teeth will grow in the order of incisors, canine/fangs, premolars and molars. Molar teeth will develop at around 6 to 8 months old.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Yorkies and other small dog breeds may have problems if the deciduous or baby teeth do not fall out as the permanent or adult teeth grow. This is caused by the new teeth not growing right underneath the deciduous teeth. (Usually, a puppy’s body will absorb the roots of puppy teeth.) If the puppy tooth does not yield to the incoming tooth, it should be removed because it can cause a malocclusion or bad bite.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>  Retained teeth can cause tooth decay because food can be easily caught in between the deciduous and permanent teeth. Sometimes the new teeth are forced to grow into an abnormal position and further cause a bad bite. The retained teeth may stay or fall weeks after the new teeth have developed. When necessary, the retained deciduous or baby teeth need to be removed surgically.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Yorkie%27s_Retained_Deciduous_or_Baby_Fangs.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Yorkie%27s_Retained_Deciduous_or_Baby_Fangs.jpg/220px-Yorkie%27s_Retained_Deciduous_or_Baby_Fangs.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="190" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Yorkie%27s_Retained_Deciduous_or_Baby_Fangs.jpg/330px-Yorkie%27s_Retained_Deciduous_or_Baby_Fangs.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Yorkie%27s_Retained_Deciduous_or_Baby_Fangs.jpg/440px-Yorkie%27s_Retained_Deciduous_or_Baby_Fangs.jpg 2x" data-file-width="916" data-file-height="792" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Yorkie%27s_Retained_Deciduous_or_Baby_Fangs.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Yorkshire Terrier's retained deciduous or baby fangs</div></div></div>
<p>Like other small breeds, Yorkies are also prone to severe dental disease. Because they have a small jaw, their teeth can become crowded and may not fall out naturally. This can cause food and plaque to build up, and bacteria can eventually develop on the surface of the teeth, leading to periodontal disease. In addition, the bacteria can spread to other parts of the body and cause heart and kidney problems. The best prevention is regular brushing of the teeth with a toothpaste formulated specifically for dogs. Human toothpaste is not recommended, because it foams more easily and may be swallowed. Professional teeth cleaning by a veterinarian may also be required to prevent the development of dental problems.<sup id="cite_ref-Teeth_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Teeth-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetic_defects">Genetic defects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Genetic defects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Certain <a href="/wiki/Genetic_disorder" title="Genetic disorder">genetic disorders</a> can be found in Yorkshire Terriers, including <a href="/wiki/Distichia" title="Distichia">distichiasis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrocephalus" title="Hydrocephalus">hydrocephalus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hypoplasia_of_dens" title="Hypoplasia of dens">hypoplasia of dens</a>, <a href="/wiki/Legg%E2%80%93Calv%C3%A9%E2%80%93Perthes_syndrome" class="mw-redirect" title="Legg–Calvé–Perthes syndrome">Legg–Calvé–Perthes syndrome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luxating_patella" title="Luxating patella">luxating patella</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portosystemic_shunt" title="Portosystemic shunt">portosystemic shunt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Retinal_dysplasia" title="Retinal dysplasia">retinal dysplasia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tracheal_collapse" title="Tracheal collapse">tracheal collapse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bladder_stone_(animal)" title="Bladder stone (animal)">bladder stones</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-KSU_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KSU-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>  The following are among the most common <a href="/wiki/Congenital_disorder" class="mw-redirect" title="Congenital disorder">congenital</a> <a href="/wiki/Genetic_disorder" title="Genetic disorder">defects</a> that affect Yorkie's.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Distichia" title="Distichia">Distichiae</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eyelash" title="Eyelash">eyelashes</a> arising from an abnormal spot (usually the duct of the <a href="/wiki/Meibomian_gland" title="Meibomian gland">meibomian gland</a> at the edge of the eyelid), are often found in Yorkies.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>  Distichiae can irritate the eye and cause tearing, squinting, inflammation,  <a href="/wiki/Corneal_abrasion" title="Corneal abrasion">corneal abrasions</a> or <a href="/wiki/Corneal_ulcers_in_animals" title="Corneal ulcers in animals">corneal ulcers</a>, and scarring. Treatment options may include manual removal, <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> or <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgery</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hypoplasia_of_dens" title="Hypoplasia of dens">Hypoplasia of dens</a> is a non-formation of the pivot point of the second <a href="/wiki/Cervical_vertebrae" title="Cervical vertebrae">cervical vertebra</a>, which leads to <a href="/wiki/Spinal_cord" title="Spinal cord">spinal cord</a> damage.<sup id="cite_ref-KSU_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KSU-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>  Onset of the condition may occur at any age, producing signs ranging from neck pain to quadriplegia.<sup id="cite_ref-Bob_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bob-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Legg%E2%80%93Calv%C3%A9%E2%80%93Perthes_syndrome" class="mw-redirect" title="Legg–Calvé–Perthes syndrome">Legg–Calvé–Perthes syndrome</a>, which causes the top of the <a href="/wiki/Femur" title="Femur">femur</a> (thigh bone) to <a href="/wiki/Degeneration_(medical)" title="Degeneration (medical)">degenerate</a>, occurs in Yorkies in certain lines.<sup id="cite_ref-Legg_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Legg-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>  The condition appears to result from insufficient <a href="/wiki/Circulatory_system" title="Circulatory system">circulation</a> to the area around the <a href="/wiki/Hip#Articulation" title="Hip">hip joint</a>. As the blood supply is reduced, the bone in the head of the femur collapses and dies and the <a href="/wiki/Cartilage" title="Cartilage">cartilage</a> coating around it becomes cracked and deformed.<sup id="cite_ref-Legg_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Legg-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>  Usually the disease appears when the Yorkie is young (between five and eight months of age); signs are pain, limping or lameness.<sup id="cite_ref-Voit_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voit-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>  The standard treatment is surgery to remove the affected part of the bone.<sup id="cite_ref-Voit_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voit-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>  Following surgery, muscles hold the femur in place and fibrous tissue forms in the area of removal to prevent bone rubbing on bone.<sup id="cite_ref-FHO_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FHO-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>  Although the affected leg will be slightly shorter than prior to surgery, the Yorkie may regain almost normal use.<sup id="cite_ref-Voit_50-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Voit-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Luxating_patella" title="Luxating patella">Luxating patellas</a> (slipping <a href="/wiki/Patella" title="Patella">kneecaps</a>) are another common defect considered to be genetic in Yorkies, although it may also be caused by an accidental fall. Weak <a href="/wiki/Ligament" title="Ligament">ligaments</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tendon" title="Tendon">tendons</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Knee" title="Knee">knee</a> or malformed (too shallow) patellar grooves, allow the patella to slip out of its groove sideways.<sup id="cite_ref-Luxating_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luxating-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>This causes the leg to 'lock up' with the foot held off the ground.<sup id="cite_ref-Luxating_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luxating-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>A dog with this problem may experience frequent pain and lameness, or may be bothered by it only on occasion. Over time, the patellar ridges can become worn down, making the groove even more shallow and causing the dog to become increasingly lame.<sup id="cite_ref-Luxating_52-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luxating-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>  Surgery is the main treatment option available for luxating patellas, although it is not necessary for every dog with the condition. The severity of luxating patellas are on a scale of 1 to 4, with 4 being the most severe. Many dogs will not develop past a stage 1 or 2.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Portosystemic_shunt" title="Portosystemic shunt">Portosystemic shunt</a>, a congenital malformation of the <a href="/wiki/Hepatic_portal_vein" class="mw-redirect" title="Hepatic portal vein">portal vein</a> (which brings blood to the <a href="/wiki/Liver" title="Liver">liver</a> for cleansing), is also common in Yorkies. In this condition some of the dog's blood bypasses the liver and the "dirty" blood goes on to poison the heart, brain, lungs and other <a href="/wiki/Organ_(anatomy)" title="Organ (anatomy)">organs</a> with toxins. A Yorkie with this condition might exhibit a wide variety of symptoms, such as small stature, poor appetite, weak muscle development, decreased ability to learn, inferior coordination, occasional vomiting and diarrhoea, <a href="/wiki/Abnormality_(behavior)" title="Abnormality (behavior)">behavioral abnormalities</a>, seizures (especially after a meal) and <a href="/wiki/Blindness" class="mw-redirect" title="Blindness">blindness</a>, which could lead to a coma and death. Often, the shunt can be treated with surgery.<sup id="cite_ref-UFAW_website1_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UFAW_website1-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Trachealkollaps.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Trachealkollaps.png/220px-Trachealkollaps.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="110" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Trachealkollaps.png/330px-Trachealkollaps.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Trachealkollaps.png/440px-Trachealkollaps.png 2x" data-file-width="787" data-file-height="393" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Trachealkollaps.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tracheal collapse</div></div></div>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Tracheal_collapse" title="Tracheal collapse">Tracheal collapse</a>, caused by a progressive weakening of the walls of the <a href="/wiki/Vertebrate_trachea" class="mw-redirect" title="Vertebrate trachea">trachea</a>, occurs in many toy breeds, especially very tiny Yorkies.<sup id="cite_ref-Degner_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Degner-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>  As a result of genetics, the walls of the trachea can be flaccid, a condition that becomes more severe with age. <a href="/wiki/Cushing%27s_syndrome" title="Cushing&#39;s syndrome">Cushing's syndrome</a>, a disorder that causes production of excess steroid hormone by the adrenal glands, can also weaken cartilage and lead to tracheal collapse.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>  There is a possibility that physical strain on the neck might cause or contribute to trachea collapse. Since this is usually caused by an energetic Yorkie pulling against his collar, many veterinarians recommend use of a harness for leashed walks.<sup id="cite_ref-Degner_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Degner-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>  An occasional "goose honking" cough, especially on exertion or excitement, is usually the first sign of this condition.<sup id="cite_ref-Degner_54-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Degner-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>  Over time, the cough may become almost constant in the Yorkie's later life. Breathing through the obstruction of a collapsed (or partially collapsed) trachea for many years can result in complications, including chronic lung disease. The coughing can be countered with <a href="/wiki/Cough_medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="Cough medicine">cough suppressants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bronchodilator" title="Bronchodilator">bronchodilators</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Degner_54-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Degner-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>  If the collapse is advanced and unresponsive to medication, sometimes surgery can repair the trachea.<sup id="cite_ref-Degner_54-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Degner-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hypoglycaemia">Hypoglycaemia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Hypoglycaemia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Blood_sugar#Low_blood_sugar" class="mw-redirect" title="Blood sugar">Low blood sugar</a> in puppies, or <a href="/wiki/Hypoglycemia" title="Hypoglycemia">transient juvenile hypoglycaemia</a>, is caused by fasting (too much time between meals).<sup id="cite_ref-Hypoglycaemia_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hypoglycaemia-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>  In rare cases hypoglycaemia may continue to be a problem in mature, usually very small, Yorkies. It is often seen in Yorkie puppies at 5 to 16 weeks of age.<sup id="cite_ref-Hypoglycaemia_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hypoglycaemia-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>  Very tiny Yorkie puppies are especially predisposed to hypoglycaemia because a lack of muscle mass makes it difficult to store <a href="/wiki/Glucose" title="Glucose">glucose</a> and regulate blood sugar.<sup id="cite_ref-Hypoglycaemia_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hypoglycaemia-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>Factors such as stress, fatigue, a cold environment, poor nutrition, and a change in diet or feeding schedule may bring on hypoglycaemia.<sup id="cite_ref-Chihuahua_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chihuahua-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>Low blood sugar can also be the result of a <a href="/wiki/Pathogenic_bacteria" title="Pathogenic bacteria">bacterial infection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Parasitism" title="Parasitism">parasite</a> or portosystemic liver shunt.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>Hypoglycaemia causes the puppy to become drowsy, listless (glassy-eyed), shaky, uncoordinated, since the <a href="/wiki/Brain" title="Brain">brain</a> relies on sugar to function. During a hypoglycaemic attack, the puppy usually has very pale or grey gums.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>The puppy also may not eat unless force-fed.<sup id="cite_ref-Hypoglycaemia_56-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hypoglycaemia-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>Hypoglycaemia and dehydration seem to go hand-in-hand, and force-feeding or injecting fluids may also be necessary. Additionally, a hypoglycaemic Yorkie may have a lower than <a href="/wiki/Normal_human_body_temperature" class="mw-redirect" title="Normal human body temperature">normal body temperature</a> and, in extreme cases, may have a <a href="/wiki/Epileptic_seizure" title="Epileptic seizure">seizure</a> or go into a <a href="/wiki/Coma" title="Coma">coma</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lee_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>A dog showing <a href="/wiki/Symptom" title="Symptom">symptoms</a> should be given sugar in the form of corn syrup or Nutri-Cal and be treated by a <a href="/wiki/Veterinarian" title="Veterinarian">veterinarian</a> immediately, as prolonged or recurring attacks of hypoglycaemia can permanently damage the dog's brain.<sup id="cite_ref-Lee_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lee-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>In severe cases, it can be fatal.<sup id="cite_ref-Hypoglycaemia_56-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hypoglycaemia-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Docking">Docking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Docking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Docking_(dog)" title="Docking (dog)">Docking (dog)</a></div>
<p>Traditionally, the Yorkshire Terrier's tail is <a href="/wiki/Docking_(dog)" title="Docking (dog)">docked</a> to a medium length.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>   Opposition to this practice began very early in the history of the breed; Hugh Dalziel, writing about Yorkshire Terriers in 1878, declared that "There is no reason for mutilating pet dogs, and perfect ears and tails should be bred, not clipped into shape with scissors."<sup id="cite_ref-British_Islands_1878,_pg_213_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-British_Islands_1878,_pg_213-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> American Kennel Club and Canadian Kennel club still require the Yorkie’s tail be docked in order to compete at its events. The majority of the rest of the world has adopted a "no docking/no cropping" rule. Often, a Yorkshire Terrier's <a href="/wiki/Dewclaw" title="Dewclaw">dewclaws</a>, if any, are removed in the first few days of life,<sup id="cite_ref-AKCstandard_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AKCstandard-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> another controversial practice.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_breeds_and_crosses">Similar breeds and crosses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Similar breeds and crosses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Yorkshire Terrier breed descends from larger but similar Scottish breeds, such as the <a href="/wiki/Skye_Terrier" title="Skye Terrier">Skye Terrier</a> and the now-extinct <a href="/wiki/Paisley_Terrier" title="Paisley Terrier">Paisley Terrier</a>. In its turn, other breeds have been created from the Yorkshire Terrier, such as the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Silky_Terrier" title="Australian Silky Terrier">Silky Terrier</a>. Demand for unusual pets has resulted in high prices being paid for Yorkshire Terriers crossed with various other breeds, which are described with a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau word</a> made up of <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">syllables</a> (or sounds) from Yorkshire Terrier and the breed name of the other parent. Some of these such portmanteau-named <a href="/wiki/Crossbreed" title="Crossbreed">crosses</a> can be found on the page <a href="/wiki/List_of_dog_crossbreeds" title="List of dog crossbreeds">List of dog crossbreeds</a>.
</p><p>Two other breeds that look similar to the Yorkshire Terrier and have shorter hair are the prick-eared <a href="/wiki/Norwich_Terrier" title="Norwich Terrier">Norwich Terrier</a>, along with its drop-eared relative the <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_Terrier" title="Norfolk Terrier">Norfolk Terrier</a>. Another is the Biewer Terrier, which derives from the Yorkshire Terrier. The Biewer Terrier, bred from blue, white and gold puppies named Schneeflocke and Schneeflöckchen von Friedheck,<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> owned by Mr. and Mrs. Biewer in Germany,<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> was once considered a variation of the Yorkshire Terrier but has since been recognised as a separate breed by many kennel clubs, including the American Kennel Club (AKC).<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id=".22Teacup_Yorkies.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Teacup_Yorkies&quot;">"Teacup Yorkies"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: &quot;Teacup Yorkies&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Teacupyorkie.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Teacupyorkie.jpg/220px-Teacupyorkie.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Teacupyorkie.jpg/330px-Teacupyorkie.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Teacupyorkie.jpg/440px-Teacupyorkie.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3024" data-file-height="1998" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Teacupyorkie.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A teacup Yorkshire Terrier</div></div></div>
<p>"Teacup" Yorkshire terriers are very small Yorkshire terriers. The American Kennel Club and other <a href="/wiki/Kennel_club" title="Kennel club">kennel clubs</a> do not acknowledge the Teacup as a variation of the breed or recognise it as a separate variety.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
Usually a teacup is any dog weighing less than 4&#160;lb (1.8&#160;kg) when fully grown, when the actual breed standard is given at 7&#160;lb (3.2&#160;kg) maximum. Breeding for "teacup" size is a controversial practice that is not encouraged by responsible breeders.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>They are bred to appeal with their puppy-like features, rather than bred to avoid health issues. There is great risk to a dam (mother) during pregnancy who is too small; most of these litters are born as a result of <a href="/wiki/Caesarean_section" title="Caesarean section">Caesarean sections</a> and have a high mortality rate.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are many health issues associated with teacup dogs, such as luxating patellae, heart disease, <a href="/wiki/Hydrocephalus" title="Hydrocephalus">hydrocephalus</a>, hypoglycaemia, <a href="/wiki/Chronic_prostatitis/chronic_pelvic_pain_syndrome" title="Chronic prostatitis/chronic pelvic pain syndrome">chronic pelvic pain syndrome</a>, open <a href="/wiki/Fontanelle" title="Fontanelle">fontanels</a> and <a href="/wiki/Epileptic_seizure" title="Epileptic seizure">seizures</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Small_dogs">Small dogs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Small dogs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Sylvia, a matchbox-sized Yorkshire Terrier owned by Arthur Marples of <a href="/wiki/Blackburn" title="Blackburn">Blackburn</a>, England, was the smallest dog in recorded history. The dog died in 1945 when she was two years old, at which point she stood <span class="frac nowrap">2<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span>&#160;in (6.4&#160;cm) tall at the shoulder, measured <span class="frac nowrap">3<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span>&#160;in (8.9&#160;cm) from nose tip to tail, and weighed 4&#160;oz (110&#160;g).<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="War_dogs">War dogs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: War dogs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Smoky_(dog)" title="Smoky (dog)">Smoky</a>, a war dog and hero of World War II, was owned by <a href="/wiki/Bill_Wynne" title="Bill Wynne">William Wynne</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cleveland" title="Cleveland">Cleveland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>. Wynne adopted Smoky while he was serving with the 5th Air Force in the Pacific.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="White_House_dogs">White House dogs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yorkshire_Terrier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: White House dogs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Pasha, <a href="/wiki/Tricia_Nixon_Cox" title="Tricia Nixon Cox">Tricia Nixon Cox</a>'s pet Yorkie, lived in the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> during <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>'s presidency.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
