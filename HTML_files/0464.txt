	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Kirkpatrick Sale</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table class="infobox biography vcard" style="width:22em"><tbody><tr><th colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-size:125%;font-weight:bold"><div class="fn" style="display:inline">Kirkpatrick Sale</div></th></tr><tr><th scope="row">Born</th><td><span style="display:none"> (<span class="bday">1937-06-27</span>) </span>June 27, 1937<span class="noprint ForceAgeToShow"> (age&#160;82)</span><br /><div style="display:inline" class="birthplace"><a href="/wiki/Ithaca,_New_York" title="Ithaca, New York">Ithaca, New York</a></div></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Nationality</th><td class="category"><a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Alma&#160;mater</th><td><a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a> (B.A., History, 1958)</td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Occupation</th><td class="role">Author</td></tr><tr><th scope="row"><span class="nowrap">Spouse(s)</span></th><td>Faith Apfelbaum (1958–1999; her death)</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><b>Kirkpatrick Sale</b> (born June 27, 1937) is an <a href="/wiki/Author" title="Author">author</a> who has written prolifically about political decentralism, <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Luddism" class="mw-redirect" title="Luddism">luddism</a> and technology. He has been described as having a "philosophy unified by <a href="/wiki/Decentralism" class="mw-redirect" title="Decentralism">decentralism</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and as being "a leader of the <a href="/wiki/Neo-Luddite" class="mw-redirect" title="Neo-Luddite">Neo-Luddites</a>,"<sup id="cite_ref-Kelly_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> an "<a href="/wiki/Anti-globalization" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-globalization">anti-globalization</a> <a href="/wiki/Left-wing_politics" title="Left-wing politics">leftist</a>,"<sup id="cite_ref-Untied_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Untied-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and "the <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/theoretician" class="extiw" title="wikt:theoretician">theoretician</a> for a new <a href="/wiki/Secession" title="Secession">secessionist</a> movement."<sup id="cite_ref-Applebome_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Applebome-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_work">Life and work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirkpatrick_Sale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sale grew up in <a href="/wiki/Cayuga_Heights" class="mw-redirect" title="Cayuga Heights">Cayuga Heights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ithaca,_New_York" title="Ithaca, New York">Ithaca, New York</a>, and would later say of the village that he "spent most of my first twenty years there, and that has made an imprint on me—on my philosophy, social attitudes, certainly on my politics—that has lasted powerfully for the rest of my life."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> He graduated from <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>, majoring in English and history, in 1958.<sup id="cite_ref-Farina_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Farina-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thinkquest_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thinkquest-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> He served as associate editor and editor-in-chief of the student-owned and managed newspaper, the <i><a href="/wiki/Cornell_Daily_Sun" class="mw-redirect" title="Cornell Daily Sun">Cornell Daily Sun</a></i>. Sale was one of the leaders of the May 23, 1958, protest against university policies forbidding male and female students fraternizing and its <i><a href="/wiki/In_loco_parentis" title="In loco parentis">in loco parentis</a></i> policy. Sale and his friend and roommate <a href="/wiki/Richard_Fari%C3%B1a" title="Richard Fariña">Richard Fariña</a>, and three others, were charged by Cornell. The protest was described in Fariña's 1966 novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Been_Down_So_Long_It_Looks_Like_Up_to_Me" title="Been Down So Long It Looks Like Up to Me">Been Down So Long It Looks Like Up to Me</a>.</i><sup id="cite_ref-Applebome_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Applebome-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In 1958 he collaborated with <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Pynchon" title="Thomas Pynchon">Thomas Pynchon</a> on an unproduced futuristic musical called <i>Minstrel Island</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Harry_Ransom_Center_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Harry_Ransom_Center-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Upon graduating in 1958, Sale married Faith Apfelbaum, who later worked as an editor with Thomas Pynchon, <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Vonnegut" title="Kurt Vonnegut">Kurt Vonnegut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Heller" title="Joseph Heller">Joseph Heller</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amy_Tan" title="Amy Tan">Amy Tan</a>. Faith died in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sale worked initially in <a href="/wiki/Journalism" title="Journalism">journalism</a> for the leftist journal <i><a href="/wiki/New_Leader" class="mw-redirect" title="New Leader">New Leader</a></i>, "a magazine founded in 1924 in part by <a href="/wiki/Socialism" title="Socialism">socialists</a> <a href="/wiki/Norman_Thomas" title="Norman Thomas">Norman Thomas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Debs" class="mw-redirect" title="Eugene Debs">Eugene Debs</a>,"<sup id="cite_ref-Radical_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Radical-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Magazine" title="The New York Times Magazine">The New York Times Magazine</a></i>, before becoming a <a href="/wiki/Freelance" class="mw-redirect" title="Freelance">freelance journalist</a>. He spent time in <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a> and wrote his first book about it. His second book, <i>SDS</i>, was about the radical 1960s group <a href="/wiki/Students_for_a_Democratic_Society_(1960_organization)" class="mw-redirect" title="Students for a Democratic Society (1960 organization)">Students for a Democratic Society</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Untied_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Untied-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The book "is still considered one of the best sources on the youth activist organization that helped define <a href="/wiki/New_Left#1960s_in_the_United_States" title="New Left">1960s radicalism</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-Radical_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Radical-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In 1968, he signed the "<a href="/wiki/Writers_and_Editors_War_Tax_Protest" class="mw-redirect" title="Writers and Editors War Tax Protest">Writers and Editors War Tax Protest</a>" pledge, vowing to refuse tax payments in protest against the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Subsequent books explored radical <a href="/wiki/Decentralist" class="mw-redirect" title="Decentralist">decentralism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bioregionalism" title="Bioregionalism">bioregionalism</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalism</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Luddites" class="mw-redirect" title="Luddites">Luddites</a> and similar themes.<sup id="cite_ref-thinkquest_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thinkquest-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> He "has been a regular contributor to <a href="/wiki/Progressivism_in_the_United_States#Contemporary_progressivism" title="Progressivism in the United States">progressive</a> magazines like <i><a href="/wiki/Mother_Jones_(magazine)" title="Mother Jones (magazine)">Mother Jones</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Nation" title="The Nation">The Nation</a></i> for the better part of his writing career"<sup id="cite_ref-Radical_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Radical-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and has continued to write for those publications,<sup id="cite_ref-Untied_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Untied-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> as well as for <i><a href="/wiki/The_American_Conservative" title="The American Conservative">The American Conservative</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/CounterPunch" title="CounterPunch">CounterPunch</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Review_of_Books" title="The New York Review of Books">The New York Review of Books</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Utne_Reader" title="Utne Reader">Utne Reader</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Sale presented public affairs programming for <a href="/wiki/WBAI" title="WBAI">WBAI</a> in the early 1980s<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and has made appearances on alternative radio over the years.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
Sale has donated 16 boxes of materials—typescripts, galley proofs, correspondence, etc.—for each one of his books to the archives at Cornell University, where they are available for public inspection.<sup id="cite_ref-papers_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-papers-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Views">Views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirkpatrick_Sale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirkpatrick_Sale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In his 1990 book, <i>The Conquest of Paradise: Christopher Columbus and the Columbian Legacy</i>, Sale argued that <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> was an <a href="/wiki/Imperialist" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperialist">imperialist</a> bent on conquest from his first voyage. In a <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">New York Times</a></i> book review, historian and member of the Christopher Columbus Quincentenary Jubilee Committee <a href="/wiki/William_Hardy_McNeill" class="mw-redirect" title="William Hardy McNeill">William Hardy McNeill</a> wrote about Sale: "he has set out to destroy the heroic image that earlier writers have transmitted to us. Mr. Sale makes Columbus out to be cruel, greedy and incompetent (even as a sailor), and a man who was perversely intent on abusing the natural paradise on which he intruded." However, McNeill also declares Sale's work to be "unhistorical, in the sense that [it] selects from the often cloudy record of Columbus's actual motives and deeds what suits the researcher's 20th-century purposes." In McNeill's opinion, Columbus' advocates and detractors  present a "sort of history [that] caricatures the complexity of human reality by turning Columbus into either a bloody ogre or a plaster saint, as the case may be."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirkpatrick_Sale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sale "has written extensively and skeptically about technology," and has said he is "a great admirer" of <a href="/wiki/Anarchoprimitivism" class="mw-redirect" title="Anarchoprimitivism">anarchoprimitivist</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Zerzan" title="John Zerzan">John Zerzan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> He has described personal computers as "the devil's work"<sup id="cite_ref-Applebome_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Applebome-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and in the past opened personal appearances by smashing one.<sup id="cite_ref-Kelly_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> During promotion of his 1995 book <i>Rebels Against the Future: The Luddites and Their War on the Industrial Revolution</i>, Sale debated with <i><a href="/wiki/Newsweek" title="Newsweek">Newsweek</a></i> magazine senior editor and technology columnist <a href="/wiki/Steven_Levy" title="Steven Levy">Steven Levy</a> "about the relative merits of the communications age".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sale has said that he does not "care much for <a href="/wiki/Popular_music" title="Popular music">popular music</a> outside some of the <a href="/wiki/Tin_Pan_Alley" title="Tin Pan Alley">Tin Pan Alley</a> era tunes of the early 20th century."<sup id="cite_ref-Radical_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Radical-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> For example, "he once heard a 'racket' in a nightclub during his left activist days in the 1960s from some 'young man' everyone told him was a 'big deal.' That 'young man' turned out to be <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a>." Kirk recalls that "he'd never heard anything so awful in his life."<sup id="cite_ref-Radical_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Radical-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1995, Sale agreed to a public bet with <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Kelly_(editor)" title="Kevin Kelly (editor)">Kevin Kelly</a> that by the year 2020, there would be a convergence of three disasters: global <a href="/wiki/Currency" title="Currency">currency</a> collapse, significant warfare between rich and poor, and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_disaster" title="Environmental disaster">environmental disasters</a> of some significant size.  The bet was turned into a claim on the FX <a href="/wiki/Prediction_market" title="Prediction market">prediction market</a>, where the probability has hovered around 25%.<sup id="cite_ref-Kelly_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kelly-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secession">Secession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirkpatrick_Sale&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Secession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sale has been described as "one of the intellectual godfathers of the secessionist movement."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  He argues that the major theme of contemporary history, from the dissolution of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> to the expansion of <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> membership from 51 in 1945 to 193 nations today, is the breakup of great empires. Some on both left and right call for smaller, less powerful government.<sup id="cite_ref-Applebome_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Applebome-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2004, Sale and members of the <a href="/wiki/Second_Vermont_Republic" title="Second Vermont Republic">Second Vermont Republic</a> formed the Middlebury Institute which is dedicated to the study of <a href="/wiki/Separatism" title="Separatism">separatism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Secession" title="Secession">secession</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Self-determination" title="Self-determination">self-determination</a>. Sale is director of the institute. In 2006, Middlebury sponsored the First North American Secessionist Convention, which attracted 40 participants from 16 secessionist organizations and was described as the first gathering of secessionists since the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>. Delegates issued a statement of principles of secession which they presented as the Burlington Declaration.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In October 2007, <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> interviewed Sale about the Second North American Secessionist Convention, co-hosted by the Middlebury Institute. Sale told the interviewer, "The virtue of small government is that the mistakes are small as well." He went on to say, "If you want to leave a nation you think is corrupt, inefficient, militaristic, oppressive, repressive, but you don't want to move to Canada or France, what do you do? Well, the way is through secession, where you could stay home and be where you want to be."<sup id="cite_ref-Applebome_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Applebome-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  The convention received worldwide media attention.<sup id="cite_ref-Poovey_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Poovey-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Doyle_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Doyle-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>News stories about the Second North American Secessionist Convention in 2007 mentioned the controversial <a href="/wiki/Southern_Poverty_Law_Center" title="Southern Poverty Law Center">Southern Poverty Law Center</a>'s allegations that the other co-sponsor, <a href="/wiki/The_League_of_the_South" class="mw-redirect" title="The League of the South">The League of the South</a>, was a "<a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racist</a> hate group." Sale responded, "They call everybody racists. There are, no doubt, racists in the League of the South, and there are, no doubt, racists everywhere."<sup id="cite_ref-Poovey_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Poovey-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Doyle_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Doyle-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The Southern Poverty Law Center later criticized <i>The New York Times</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span> October 2007 Peter Applebombe interview of Sale for not covering its allegations.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sale wrote the foreword to <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Naylor" title="Thomas Naylor">Thomas Naylor</a>'s 2008 book <i>Secession: How Vermont and all the Other States Can Save Themselves from the Empire.</i><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Sale, Thomas Naylor and four others issued "The Montpelier Manifesto" in September, 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
