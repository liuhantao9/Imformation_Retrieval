<p>A <i><b>gurdwara</b></i> (<i lang="sa-Latn" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit transliteration"><b>gurdwārā</b></i>; meaning "door to the <a href="/wiki/Sikh_gurus" title="Sikh gurus">guru</a>") is a place of assembly and <a href="/wiki/Place_of_worship" title="Place of worship">worship</a> for <a href="/wiki/Sikh" class="mw-redirect" title="Sikh">Sikhs</a>.
People from all faiths, and those who do not profess any faith, are welcomed in Sikh <i>gurdwaras</i>. 
Each <i>gurdwara</i> has a <i><a href="/wiki/Darbar_Sahib_Hall" title="Darbar Sahib Hall">Darbar Sahib</a></i> where the current and everlasting guru of the Sikhs, the scripture <a href="/wiki/Guru_Granth_Sahib" title="Guru Granth Sahib">Guru Granth Sahib</a>, is placed on a <i>takhat</i> (an elevated throne) in a prominent central position. The <i>raagis</i> (who sing <a href="/wiki/Raga" title="Raga">Ragas</a>) recite, sing and explain, the verses from the Guru Granth Sahib, in the presence of the congregation.
</p><p>All <i>gurdwaras</i> have a <i><a href="/wiki/Langar_(Sikhism)" title="Langar (Sikhism)">langar</a></i> hall, where people can eat free vegetarian food served by volunteers at the <i>gurdwara</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> They may also have a medical facility room, library, nursery, classroom, meeting rooms, playground, sports ground, a gift shop, and finally a repair shop.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> A <i>gurdwara</i> can be identified from a distance by tall flagpoles bearing the <i><a href="/wiki/Nishan_Sahib" title="Nishan Sahib">Nishan Sahib</a></i>, the Sikh flag.
</p><p>The most well-known <i>gurdwaras</i> are in the Darbar Sahib complex in <a href="/wiki/Amritsar" title="Amritsar">Amritsar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Punjab_(India)" class="mw-redirect" title="Punjab (India)">Punjab</a> <sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Editors_of_Encyclopaedia_Britannica2014_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Editors_of_Encyclopaedia_Britannica2014-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Golden_Temple" title="Golden Temple">Darbar Sahib</a>, the spiritual center of the Sikhs and <a href="/wiki/Akal_Takht" title="Akal Takht">Akal Takht</a>, the political center of the Sikhs.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Editors_of_Encyclopaedia_Britannica2014_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Editors_of_Encyclopaedia_Britannica2014-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Gurudwara_Bangla_Sahib" title="Gurudwara Bangla Sahib">Gurudwara Bangla Sahib</a> is one of the most prominent <a href="/wiki/Sikhism" title="Sikhism">Sikh</a> gurdwara in <a href="/wiki/Delhi,_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Delhi, India">Delhi, India</a> and known for its association with the eighth <a href="/wiki/Sikh_Guru" class="mw-redirect" title="Sikh Guru">Sikh Guru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guru_Har_Krishan" title="Guru Har Krishan">Guru Har Krishan</a>, as well as the pool inside its complex, known as the "Sarovar."
</p>

<p>The first <i>gurdwara</i> was built in <a href="/wiki/Kartarpur,_Pakistan" title="Kartarpur, Pakistan">Kartarpur</a>, on the banks of <a href="/wiki/Ravi_River" title="Ravi River">Ravi River</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Punjab_region" class="mw-redirect" title="Punjab region">Punjab</a> region by the first Sikh guru, <a href="/wiki/Guru_Nanak_Dev" class="mw-redirect" title="Guru Nanak Dev">Guru Nanak Dev</a> in the year 1521. It now lies in the <a href="/wiki/Narowal_District" title="Narowal District">Narowal District</a> of west Punjab (Pakistan).
</p><p>The worship centres were built as a place where Sikhs could gather to hear the guru give spiritual discourse and sing religious <a href="/wiki/Hymns" class="mw-redirect" title="Hymns">hymns</a> in the praise of <i><a href="/wiki/Waheguru" title="Waheguru">Waheguru</a></i>. As the Sikh population continued to grow, <a href="/wiki/Guru_Hargobind" title="Guru Hargobind">Guru Hargobind</a>, the sixth Sikh guru, introduced the word <i>gurdwara</i>.
</p><p>The etymology of the term 'gurdwara' is from the words 'gur (ਗੁਰ)' (a reference to the Sikh gurus) and 'dwara (ਦੁਆਰਾ)' (gateway in <a href="/wiki/Gurmukhi" title="Gurmukhi">Gurmukhi</a>), together meaning 'the gateway through which the guru could be reached'.<sup id="cite_ref-SGPC_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SGPC-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
Thereafter, all Sikh places of worship came to be known as <i>gurdwaras</i>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nishan_sahib_golden_temple.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Nishan Sahib flags on tall poles over Harmandir Sahib in India" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Nishan_sahib_golden_temple.jpg/200px-Nishan_sahib_golden_temple.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Nishan_sahib_golden_temple.jpg/300px-Nishan_sahib_golden_temple.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Nishan_sahib_golden_temple.jpg/400px-Nishan_sahib_golden_temple.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="2048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nishan_sahib_golden_temple.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Nishan Sahib flags on poles at Harmandir Sahib, Amritsar.</div></div></div>
<p>Some of the prominent Sikh shrines established by the Sikh gurus are:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Gurdwara_Janam_Asthan" title="Gurdwara Janam Asthan">Nankana Sahib</a>, established in the 1490s by first Sikh Guru, Guru Nanak Dev, Punjab, Pakistan.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sultanpur_Lodhi#Historical_Gurudwaras" title="Sultanpur Lodhi">Sultanpur Lodhi</a>, established in 1499 became the Sikh centre during Guru Nanak Dev time Kapurthala District, Punjab (India).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gurdwara_Darbar_Sahib_Kartarpur" title="Gurdwara Darbar Sahib Kartarpur">Kartarpur Sahib</a>, established in 1521 by the first Sikh Guru, Guru Nanak Dev, near River Ravi, Narowal, Punjab, Pakistan.</li>
<li>Khadur Sahib, established in 1539 by the second Sikh Guru, Guru Angad Dev ji, near River Beas, Amritsar District, Punjab, India.</li>
<li>Goindwal Sahib, established in 1552 by the third Sikh Guru, Guru Amar Das ji, near River Beas, Amritsar District Punjab, India.</li>
<li>Sri Amritsar, established in 1577 By the fourth Sikh Guru, Guru Ram Das ji, District Amritsar, Punjab India.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tarn_Taran_Sahib" title="Tarn Taran Sahib">Tarn Taran Sahib</a>, established in 1590 by the fifth Sikh Guru, [Guru Arjan Dev ji], District Tarn Taran Sahib, Punjab India.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kartarpur,_India" title="Kartarpur, India">Kartarpur</a> Sahib, established in 1594 by the fifth Sikh Guru, Guru Arjan Dev, near river Beas, Jalandhar District, Punjab India.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sri_Hargobindpur" title="Sri Hargobindpur">Sri Hargobindpur</a>, established by the fifth Sikh Guru, Guru Arjan Dev, near river Beas, Gurdaspur District, Punjab India.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Kiratpur_Sahib" title="Kiratpur Sahib">Kiratpur Sahib</a>, established in 1627 by the sixth Sikh Guru, Guru Hargobind, near river Sutlej, Ropar District, Punjab, India.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Anandpur_Sahib" title="Anandpur Sahib">Anandpur Sahib</a>, established in 1665 by the ninth Sikh Guru, <a href="/wiki/Guru_Tegh_Bahadur" title="Guru Tegh Bahadur">Guru Tegh Bahadur</a>, near river Sutlej, Punjab, India.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Gurudwara_Paonta_Sahib" title="Gurudwara Paonta Sahib">Paonta Sahib</a>, established in 1685 by the tenth Sikh Guru, <a href="/wiki/Guru_Gobind_Singh" title="Guru Gobind Singh">Guru Gobind Singh</a>, near river Yamuna, Himachal Pradesh India.</li></ul>
<p>By the early 20th century, a number of Sikh <i>gurdwaras</i> in British India were under the control of the <a href="/wiki/Udasi" title="Udasi">Udasi</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Mahant" title="Mahant">mahant</a></i>s (clergymen).<sup id="cite_ref-HSSingha2000_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HSSingha2000-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Gurdwara_Reform_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Gurdwara Reform Movement">Gurdwara Reform Movement</a> of the 1920s resulted in <a href="/wiki/Shiromani_Gurdwara_Parbandhak_Committee" title="Shiromani Gurdwara Parbandhak Committee">Shiromani Gurdwara Parbandhak Committee</a> taking control of these <i>gurdwaras</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rajit2003_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rajit2003-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Panj_Takht">Panj Takht</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gurdwara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Panj Takht">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Panj_Takht" title="Panj Takht">Panj Takht</a></i> which literally means five seats or thrones of authority, are five <i>gurdwaras</i> which have a very special significance for the Sikh community.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> They are result of the historical growth of the religion of <a href="/wiki/Sikhism" title="Sikhism">Sikhism</a> and represent the centers of power of the religion.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Akal_Takht" title="Akal Takht">Akal Takht Sahib</a>, (the Throne of the Timeless One) established by <a href="/wiki/Guru_Hargobind" title="Guru Hargobind">Guru Hargobind</a> in 1609 is situated in the complex of <a href="/wiki/The_Golden_Temple" class="mw-redirect" title="The Golden Temple">The Golden Temple</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amritsar" title="Amritsar">Amritsar</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Editors_of_Encyclopaedia_Britannica2014_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Editors_of_Encyclopaedia_Britannica2014-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Takht_Sri_Kesgarh_Sahib" class="mw-redirect" title="Takht Sri Kesgarh Sahib">Takht Sri Kesgarh Sahib</a>, located in Anandpur Sahib, <a href="/wiki/Punjab_(India)" class="mw-redirect" title="Punjab (India)">Punjab</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a><sup id="cite_ref-_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note--8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Takht_Sri_Damdama_Sahib" title="Takht Sri Damdama Sahib">Takht Sri Damdama Sahib</a>, located in <a href="/wiki/Bathinda" title="Bathinda">Bathinda</a>, Punjab, India<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Takht_Sri_Patna_Sahib" title="Takht Sri Patna Sahib">Takhat Sri Harimandir Patna Sahib</a>, in the neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Patna_Sahib" class="mw-redirect" title="Patna Sahib">Patna Sahib</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patna" title="Patna">Patna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bihar" title="Bihar">Bihar</a>, India<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hazur_Sahib_Nanded" class="mw-redirect" title="Hazur Sahib Nanded">Takht Sri Hazur Sahib</a>, located on banks of the <a href="/wiki/River_Godavari" class="mw-redirect" title="River Godavari">River Godavari</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nanded" title="Nanded">Nanded</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">Maharashtra</a>, India.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gurdwara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <i>gurdwara</i> has a main hall called a <a href="/wiki/Darbar_Sahib_Hall" title="Darbar Sahib Hall">darbar</a>, a community kitchen called a <i>langar</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-SGPC_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SGPC-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and other facilities. The essential features of a <i>gurdwara</i> are these public spaces, the presence of the holy book and eternal Sikh guru the <i>Granth Sahib</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> the pursuit of the <a href="/wiki/Sikh_Rehat_Maryada" title="Sikh Rehat Maryada">Sikh Rehat Maryada</a> (the Sikh code of conduct and convention), and the provision of daily services:
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Shabad_(hymn)" class="mw-redirect" title="Shabad (hymn)">Shabad</a></i> <i><a href="/wiki/Kirtan" title="Kirtan">Kirtan</a></i>:  singing hymns from the <i>Granth Sahib</i>. Strictly speaking only Shabads from <i>Guru Granth Sahib</i>, <a href="/wiki/Dasam_Granth" title="Dasam Granth">Dasam Granth</a>, and the compositions of <a href="/wiki/Bhai_Gurdas" title="Bhai Gurdas">Bhai Gurdas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bhai_Nand_Lal" title="Bhai Nand Lal">Bhai Nand Lal</a>, can be performed within a <i>gurdwara</i>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Paath" title="Paath">Paath</a></i>: religious discourse and reading of <a href="/wiki/Gurbani" title="Gurbani">Gurbani</a> from the Guru Granth Sahib, with its explanations. There are two types of discourse: <i><a href="/wiki/Akhand_Paath" class="mw-redirect" title="Akhand Paath">Akhand Paath</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Sadharan_Paath" title="Sadharan Paath">Sadharan Paath</a></i>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Sangat_(term)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sangat (term)">Sangat</a></i> and <i>Pangat</i>: providing a free community kitchen called a langar<sup id="cite_ref-SGPC_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SGPC-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> for all visitors, regardless of religious, regional, cultural, racial, caste, or class affiliations.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Typical.darbar.sahib.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/ac/Typical.darbar.sahib.png/200px-Typical.darbar.sahib.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="196" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/ac/Typical.darbar.sahib.png/300px-Typical.darbar.sahib.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/ac/Typical.darbar.sahib.png/400px-Typical.darbar.sahib.png 2x" data-file-width="509" data-file-height="500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Typical.darbar.sahib.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A typical <a href="/wiki/Darbar_Sahib_Hall" title="Darbar Sahib Hall">Darbar Sahib</a> layout. Men and women usually sit on separate sides.</div></div></div>
<p>Other ceremonies performed there include the Sikh marriage ceremony, <i><a href="/wiki/Anand_Karaj" title="Anand Karaj">Anand Karaj</a></i>; some of the rites of the death ceremony, <i><a href="/wiki/Antam_Sanskar" title="Antam Sanskar">Antam Sanskar</a></i>; and most of the important <a href="/wiki/Sikh_Festivals" class="mw-redirect" title="Sikh Festivals">Sikh Festivals</a>. The <i><a href="/wiki/Nagar_Kirtan" title="Nagar Kirtan">Nagar Kirtan</a></i>, a Sikh processional singing of holy hymns throughout a community, begin and conclude at a <i>gurdwara</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Gurdwaras</i> around the world may also serve the Sikh community in other ways, including acting as libraries of Sikh literature and schools to teach children Gurmukhi, housing the <a href="/wiki/Sikh_scriptures" title="Sikh scriptures">Sikh scriptures</a>, and organizing charitable work in the wider community on behalf of Sikhs. Many historical <i>gurdwaras</i> associated with the lives of the Sikh Gurus have a sarovar (eco-friendly pool) attached for bathing.
</p><p><i>Gurdwaras</i> have no <a href="/wiki/Cult_image" title="Cult image">idols</a>, statues, or religious pictures.
</p>

<p>Many <i>gurdwaras</i> are designed to seat men on one side and women on the other, although designs vary, and the divided seating is far from mandatory. They do not generally sit together but on separate sides of the room, both at an equal distance from the Guru Granth Sahib, as a sign of equality. Worshippers are offered <i><a href="/wiki/Karah_Parshad" title="Karah Parshad">Karah Parshad</a></i> (sweet flour and <i><a href="/wiki/Ghee" title="Ghee">ghee</a></i>-based food offered as <i><a href="/wiki/Prashad" class="mw-redirect" title="Prashad">prashad</a></i>) in the hall, which is usually given into cupped hands by a <i><a href="/wiki/Sewadar" title="Sewadar">sewadar</a></i> (<i>gurdwara</i> volunteer).
</p><p>In the <i><a href="/wiki/Langar_(Sikhism)" title="Langar (Sikhism)">langar</a></i> room, food is cooked and served by the volunteers in the community. Only <a href="/wiki/Vegetarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Vegetarian">vegetarian</a> food is served in the langar hall, to suit the visitors from different backgrounds so that no person may be offended. All people belonging to different faiths sit together to share a common meal, regardless of any dietary restrictions. The main philosophy behind the <i>langar</i> is two-fold: to provide training to engage in <i>seva</i> and an opportunity to serve people from all walks of life, and to help banish all distinctions between high and low or rich and poor.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gurdwara_architecture">Gurdwara architecture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gurdwara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gurdwara architecture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Guru_Nanak_Gurdwara_Smethwick.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Guru_Nanak_Gurdwara_Smethwick.jpg/220px-Guru_Nanak_Gurdwara_Smethwick.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="122" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Guru_Nanak_Gurdwara_Smethwick.jpg/330px-Guru_Nanak_Gurdwara_Smethwick.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Guru_Nanak_Gurdwara_Smethwick.jpg/440px-Guru_Nanak_Gurdwara_Smethwick.jpg 2x" data-file-width="896" data-file-height="498" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Guru_Nanak_Gurdwara_Smethwick.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Guru_Nanak_Gurdwara_Smethwick" title="Guru Nanak Gurdwara Smethwick">Guru Nanak Gurdwara Smethwick</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sandwell" title="Sandwell">Sandwell</a>, one of the first and largest gurdwaras in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><i>Gurdwara</i> buildings do not have to conform to any set architectural design. The only established requirements are: the installation of the Granth Sahib under a canopy or in a canopied seat, usually on a platform higher than the specific floor on which the devotees sit, and a tall Sikh pennant flag atop the building.
</p><p>In the 21st century, more and more <i>gurdwaras</i> (especially within India) have been following the <a href="/wiki/Harimandir_Sahib" class="mw-redirect" title="Harimandir Sahib">Harimandir Sahib</a> pattern, a synthesis of Indo-Islamic and Sikh architecture. Most of them have square halls, stand on a higher plinth, have entrances on all four sides, and have square or octagonal domed sanctums usually in the middle. During recent decades, to meet the requirements of larger gatherings, bigger and better ventilated assembly halls, with the sanctum at one end, have become accepted style. The location of the sanctum, more often than not, is such as to allow space for circumambulation. Sometimes, to augment the space, <a href="/wiki/Verandah" class="mw-redirect" title="Verandah">verandahs</a> are built to skirt the hall. A popular model for the dome is the ribbed lotus, topped by an ornamental pinnacle. Arched copings, <a href="/wiki/Kiosk" title="Kiosk">kiosks</a> and solid domelets are used for exterior decorations.
</p>

<p>It is the duty of all Sikhs to engage in personal and communal meditation, <a href="/wiki/Kirtan" title="Kirtan">Kirtan</a> and the study of the holy Scriptures. Meditating and understanding the meaning of texts from the <i> Granth Sahib</i> is important for the proper moral and spiritual development of a Sikh. One must study Gurmukhi script and be able to read <a href="/wiki/Gurbani" title="Gurbani">Gurbani</a> to understand the meaning of the text. A Sikh has to revert to the <i> Granth Sahib</i> for the all spiritual guidance in one's life.
</p>
<h3><span id="Holy_congregation_.28Sadh_Sangat.29_and_reflecting_on_Gurbani"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Holy_congregation_(Sadh_Sangat)_and_reflecting_on_Gurbani">Holy congregation (<a href="/wiki/Sadh_Sangat" class="mw-redirect" title="Sadh Sangat">Sadh Sangat</a>) and reflecting on Gurbani</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gurdwara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Holy congregation (Sadh Sangat) and reflecting on Gurbani">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:InsideSikhGurdwara.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/InsideSikhGurdwara.jpg/220px-InsideSikhGurdwara.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/InsideSikhGurdwara.jpg/330px-InsideSikhGurdwara.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/InsideSikhGurdwara.jpg/440px-InsideSikhGurdwara.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:InsideSikhGurdwara.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Darbar_Sahib_Hall" title="Darbar Sahib Hall">Darbar Sahib</a> of a <i>gurdwara</i>.</div></div></div>
<p>It is believed that a Sikh is more easily and deeply engrossed by Gurbani when engaged in congregation gatherings. For this reason, it is necessary for a Sikh to visit <i>gurdwara</i>. On joining the holy congregation, Sikhs should take part and obtain the benefit from the combined study of the holy scriptures. No one is to be barred from entering a <i>gurdwara</i> regardless of their religious or regional background and are welcomed in.
</p>
<h3><span id="Voluntary_service_.28Seva.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Voluntary_service_(Seva)">Voluntary service (Seva)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gurdwara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Voluntary service (Seva)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Communal_lunch_in_Keshgarh_Sahib.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Communal_lunch_in_Keshgarh_Sahib.jpg/220px-Communal_lunch_in_Keshgarh_Sahib.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Communal_lunch_in_Keshgarh_Sahib.jpg/330px-Communal_lunch_in_Keshgarh_Sahib.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Communal_lunch_in_Keshgarh_Sahib.jpg/440px-Communal_lunch_in_Keshgarh_Sahib.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="533" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Communal_lunch_in_Keshgarh_Sahib.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Khalsa principles of <a href="/wiki/Deg_Tegh_Fateh" title="Deg Tegh Fateh">Deg</a> to cook food (<a href="/wiki/Langar_(Sikhism)" title="Langar (Sikhism)">langar</a>) in huge amount</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Selfless_service" title="Selfless service">Seva</a> is an important and prominent part of the Sikh religion. <a href="/wiki/Dasvand" title="Dasvand">Dasvand</a> forms a central part of Sikh belief (of <a href="/wiki/Vand_Chhako" title="Vand Chhako">Vand Chhako</a>) and literally means donating ten percent of one's harvest, both financial and in the form of time and service such as seva to the <i>gurdwara</i> and anywhere where help is needed. All Sikhs therefore get involved in this communal service whenever an opportunity arises.  This in its simple forms can be: sweeping and washing the floors of the <i>gurdwara</i>, serving water and food (<a href="/wiki/Langar_(Sikhism)" title="Langar (Sikhism)">Langar</a>) to or fanning the congregation, offering provisions or preparing food and doing other 'house keeping' duties.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Community_life_and_other_matters">Community life and other matters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gurdwara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Community life and other matters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gurdwara_Sri_Guru_Singh_Sabha,_Birmingham_-_2019-04-28_-_Andy_Mabbett_-_02.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Gurdwara_Sri_Guru_Singh_Sabha%2C_Birmingham_-_2019-04-28_-_Andy_Mabbett_-_02.jpg/220px-Gurdwara_Sri_Guru_Singh_Sabha%2C_Birmingham_-_2019-04-28_-_Andy_Mabbett_-_02.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Gurdwara_Sri_Guru_Singh_Sabha%2C_Birmingham_-_2019-04-28_-_Andy_Mabbett_-_02.jpg/330px-Gurdwara_Sri_Guru_Singh_Sabha%2C_Birmingham_-_2019-04-28_-_Andy_Mabbett_-_02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Gurdwara_Sri_Guru_Singh_Sabha%2C_Birmingham_-_2019-04-28_-_Andy_Mabbett_-_02.jpg/440px-Gurdwara_Sri_Guru_Singh_Sabha%2C_Birmingham_-_2019-04-28_-_Andy_Mabbett_-_02.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3872" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gurdwara_Sri_Guru_Singh_Sabha,_Birmingham_-_2019-04-28_-_Andy_Mabbett_-_02.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An office block repurposed as a Gurdwara, opened in Birmingham, England in April 2019</div></div></div>
<p>Sikhism offers strong support for a healthy communal life, and a Sikh must undertake to support all worthy projects which would benefit the larger community and promote Sikh principles. Importance is given to <a href="/wiki/Inter-faith_dialogue" class="mw-redirect" title="Inter-faith dialogue">Inter-faith dialogue</a>, support for the poor and weak; better community understanding and co-operation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Learning_and_other_facilities">Learning and other facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gurdwara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Learning and other facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many <i>gurdwaras</i> also have other facilities for Sikhs to learn more about their religion, such as libraries, complexes for courses in Gurmukhi, <a href="/wiki/Sikhism" title="Sikhism">Sikhism</a> and Sikh scriptures, meeting rooms, and room-and-board accommodation for those who need it. <i>Gurdwaras</i> are open to all people - regardless of gender, age, sexuality or religion - and are generally open all hours of a day. Some <i>gurdwaras</i> also provide temporary accommodations (<i>serais</i>) for visitors or devotees. The <i>gurdwara</i> also serves as a community centre and a guest house for travellers, occasionally a clinic, and a base for local charitable activities. Apart from morning and evening services, the <i>gurdwaras</i> hold special congregations to mark important anniversaries on the Sikh calendar. They become scenes of much éclat and festivity during celebrations in honour of the birth and death (<i>Joythi Joyth Samaey</i>) anniversaries of the Gurus and <a href="/wiki/Vaisakhi" title="Vaisakhi">Vaisakhi</a>.
</p>
