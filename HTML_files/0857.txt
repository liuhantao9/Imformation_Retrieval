Kennedy Presidential Library and Museum</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Presidential_library" title="Presidential library">presidential library</a> and <a href="/wiki/Museum" title="Museum">museum</a> of <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John Fitzgerald Kennedy</a> (1917–1963), the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Presidents_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Presidents of the United States">35th</a> <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> (1961–1963). It is located on <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Point,_Boston" title="Columbia Point, Boston">Columbia Point</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Dorchester,_Boston" title="Dorchester, Boston">Dorchester</a> neighborhood of <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, next to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Massachusetts_Boston" title="University of Massachusetts Boston">University of Massachusetts at Boston</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Edward_M._Kennedy_Institute_for_the_United_States_Senate" title="Edward M. Kennedy Institute for the United States Senate">Edward M. Kennedy Institute for the United States Senate</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Archives" title="Massachusetts Archives">Massachusetts Archives and Commonwealth Museum</a>. Designed by the architect <a href="/wiki/I._M._Pei" title="I. M. Pei">I. M. Pei</a>, the building is the official repository for original papers and correspondence of the Kennedy Administration, as well as special bodies of published and unpublished materials, such as books and papers by and about <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">Ernest Hemingway</a>.
</p><p>The library and museum is part of the Presidential Library System, which is administered by the Office of Presidential Libraries, a part of the <a href="/wiki/National_Archives_and_Records_Administration" title="National Archives and Records Administration">National Archives and Records Administration</a> (NARA).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The library and Museum were dedicated in 1979 by 39th President <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a> and members of the <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_family" title="Kennedy family">Kennedy family</a>. It can be reached from nearby <a href="/wiki/Interstate_93" title="Interstate 93">Interstate 93</a> or via <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">shuttle bus</a> or walk from the <a href="/wiki/JFK/UMass_(MBTA_station)" class="mw-redirect" title="JFK/UMass (MBTA station)">JFK/UMass</a> stop on the <a href="/wiki/Red_Line_(MBTA)" title="Red Line (MBTA)">Boston Subway's Red Line</a>.
</p>

<h2><span id="Location.2C_design_and_dedication"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Location,_design_and_dedication">Location, design and dedication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Presidential_Library_and_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location, design and dedication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Original_site_and_name">Original site and name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Presidential_Library_and_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Original site and name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During a weekend visit to <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> on October 19, 1963, President Kennedy, along with <a href="/wiki/John_Carl_Warnecke" title="John Carl Warnecke">John Carl Warnecke</a> — the architect who would design the President's  <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_Eternal_Flame" title="John F. Kennedy Eternal Flame">tomb in Arlington</a><sup id="cite_ref-Franklin_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Franklin-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ArlingtonCemetary_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ArlingtonCemetary-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> — viewed several locations offered by <a href="/wiki/Harvard" class="mw-redirect" title="Harvard">Harvard</a> as a site for the library and museum. At the time there were only four other <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Library" class="mw-redirect" title="Presidential Library">Presidential Libraries</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Hoover_Presidential_Library" class="mw-redirect" title="Hoover Presidential Library">Hoover Presidential Library</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt_Presidential_Library" class="mw-redirect" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt Presidential Library">Franklin D. Roosevelt Library</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Truman_Library" class="mw-redirect" title="Truman Library">Truman Library</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower_Library" class="mw-redirect" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower Library">Dwight D. Eisenhower Library</a>. They were all scattered around the country in small towns from New York to Iowa. Kennedy had not decided on any design concept yet, but he felt that the existing presidential libraries were placed too "far away from scholarly resources."<sup id="cite_ref-Franklin_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Franklin-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kennedy chose a plot of land next to the <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School" title="Harvard Business School">Harvard Graduate School of Business Administration</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Franklin_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Franklin-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The building would face the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River" title="Charles River">Charles River</a> which was a few feet away, and on the other side of which, the dormitories that included <a href="/wiki/Winthrop_House" title="Winthrop House">Winthrop House</a> where Kennedy spent his upperclassman days.<sup id="cite_ref-Franklin_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Franklin-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since Kennedy encouraged his <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Kennedy administration">administration</a> to save effects of both personal and official nature, the complex would not just be a collection of the President's papers, but "a complete record of a Presidential era." Therefore, the building would have the word "museum" appended to its name as the John F. Kennedy Presidential Library and Museum.<sup id="cite_ref-Franklin_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Franklin-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Initial_progress">Initial progress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Presidential_Library_and_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Initial progress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_assassination" class="mw-redirect" title="John F. Kennedy assassination">President Kennedy was assassinated</a> in November 1963, his <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_family" title="Kennedy family">family</a> and friends discussed how to construct a library that would serve as a fitting memorial. A committee was formed to advise Kennedy's widow <a href="/wiki/Jacqueline_Kennedy_Onassis" title="Jacqueline Kennedy Onassis">Jacqueline</a>, who would make the final decision. The group deliberated for months, and visited with architects from around the world including <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Belluschi" title="Pietro Belluschi">Pietro Belluschi</a> and others from the United States, Brazil's <a href="/wiki/Lucio_Costa" class="mw-redirect" title="Lucio Costa">Lucio Costa</a>, and Italy's <a href="/wiki/Franco_Albini" title="Franco Albini">Franco Albini</a>. Mrs. Kennedy and others met with the candidates together at the <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Compound" title="Kennedy Compound">Kennedy Compound</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hyannis,_Massachusetts" title="Hyannis, Massachusetts">Hyannis, Massachusetts</a>, and visited several in their offices. The committee also conducted a secretive process whereby the architects voted anonymously for the most capable of their colleagues.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Progress on the building began shortly after his death. On January 13, 1964, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Attorney_General" title="United States Attorney General">Attorney General</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a> announced that a taped <a href="/wiki/Oral_history" title="Oral history">oral-history</a> project was to be undertaken for inclusion in the library. The project would feature administration staff, friends, family, and politicians from home and abroad. The Attorney General also announced that <a href="/wiki/Eugene_R._Black,_Sr." class="mw-redirect" title="Eugene R. Black, Sr.">Eugene R. Black, Sr.</a> agreed to serve as chairman of the board of trustees and that $1 million of Black's $10 million goal had been given to the trust by the <a href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Kennedy,_Jr._Foundation" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph P. Kennedy, Jr. Foundation">Joseph P. Kennedy, Jr. Foundation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-LewisJanuary_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LewisJanuary-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The death of the President was still fresh in the hearts and minds of the American public and by March of that year $4.3 million had been pledged, including 18,727 unsolicited donations from the public.<sup id="cite_ref-LewisMarch_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LewisMarch-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Large donations came from the Hispanic world with <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> pledging $100,000 and <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Governor of Puerto Rico">Governor of Puerto Rico</a> <a href="/wiki/Luis_Mu%C3%B1oz_Mar%C3%ADn" title="Luis Muñoz Marín">Luis Muñoz Marín</a> offering the same. The oral-history project also began recording, starting with Jacqueline Kennedy and Robert Kennedy. Originally projected to consist of interviews with 150 people, 178 had agreed to participate and the total number of expected participants doubled to 300, with just one person (a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secret_Service" title="United States Secret Service">Secret Service</a> agent) declining to take part.<sup id="cite_ref-LewisMarch_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LewisMarch-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Also by this time fourteen architects were named to serve on a design advisory committee:<sup id="cite_ref-LewisMarch_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LewisMarch-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Over the following months pledges continued to funnel in for the building still being conceptualized by the various architects. Some notable donations include $900,000 handed over to <a href="/wiki/United_States_Postmaster_General" title="United States Postmaster General">Postmaster General</a> <a href="/wiki/John_A._Gronouski" title="John A. Gronouski">John A. Gronouski</a> on July 9, 1964. It was the sum of a campaign encompassing 102 Federal agencies. Gronouski said many of the Federal employee contributions were in the form of a $5 withholding each payday for a period of three years.<sup id="cite_ref-EmployeeContributions_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EmployeeContributions-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The next day the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ambassador_to_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian Ambassador to the United States">Indian ambassador to the United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Braj_Kumar_Nehru" title="Braj Kumar Nehru">Braj Kumar Nehru</a>. presented Black with a check for $100,000 during a ceremony at the River Club. Nehru said that the Indian people were hit by a "sad blow" when the President died, and that they held him "in the highest regard, esteem and affection." He desired for <a href="/wiki/Indian_students_abroad" title="Indian students abroad">Indian students abroad</a> in the United States to use the library, then still planned for construction at Harvard along the banks of the Charles River.<sup id="cite_ref-IndiaGives_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IndiaGives-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pei_selected_as_architect">Pei selected as architect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Presidential_Library_and_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pei selected as architect">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JFK_Library_Boston_09222016.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/af/JFK_Library_Boston_09222016.jpg/220px-JFK_Library_Boston_09222016.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/af/JFK_Library_Boston_09222016.jpg/330px-JFK_Library_Boston_09222016.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/af/JFK_Library_Boston_09222016.jpg/440px-JFK_Library_Boston_09222016.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JFK_Library_Boston_09222016.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The John F, Kennedy Presidential Library and Museum as seen from the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Harborwalk" title="Boston Harborwalk">Boston Harborwalk</a> on the Columbia Point segment</div></div></div>
<p>On December 13, 1964, the Kennedy family announced that <a href="/wiki/I._M._Pei" title="I. M. Pei">I. M. Pei</a> was unanimously chosen by a subcommittee as the architect of the library.<sup id="cite_ref-HuxtableDec12-64_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HuxtableDec12-64-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Even though Pei was relatively unknown amongst the list of candidates, Mrs. Kennedy, who viewed him as filled with promise and imagination and after spending several months inspecting the many architects' offices and creations, selected him to create the vision she held for the project.<sup id="cite_ref-jfkl.org-Pei_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfkl.org-Pei-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Pei did not have a design yet, but the idea as described by Robert Kennedy was to "stimulate interest in politics." Meanwhile, the suggestion that Harvard may not be a suitable site for the library had begun cropping up. When asked if Pei may have had to start from scratch, he said this was the case. With an "encouraging grin" Robert Kennedy simply wished Mr. Pei "Good luck."<sup id="cite_ref-HuxtableDec12-64_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HuxtableDec12-64-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mrs. Kennedy chose Pei to design the library, based on two considerations. First, she appreciated the variety of ideas he had used for earlier projects. "He didn't seem to have just one way to solve a problem," she said. "He seemed to approach each commission thinking only of it and then develop a way to make something beautiful."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Ultimately, however, Kennedy made her choice based on her personal connection with Pei. Calling it "really an emotional decision", she explained: "He was so full of promise, like Jack; they were born in the same year. I decided it would be fun to take a great leap with him."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Not long before Pei was selected, the $10 million goal set by Black had been reached.<sup id="cite_ref-HuxtableDec12-64_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HuxtableDec12-64-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> By 1965, fundraising was suspended when the contributions reached $20 million.<sup id="cite_ref-Raymont_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raymont-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Years_of_setbacks">Years of setbacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Presidential_Library_and_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Years of setbacks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 1966, when <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Massachusetts" title="Governor of Massachusetts">Massachusetts Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/John_A._Volpe" class="mw-redirect" title="John A. Volpe">John A. Volpe</a> signed a bill allowing the state to purchase the land for the site — an old train yard belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Bay_Transit_Authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Massachusetts Bay Transit Authority">Massachusetts Bay Transit Authority</a> (MBTA) — it was expected that the project would be complete by 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-Samuelson_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Samuelson-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> The original design was a large complex comprising the John F. Kennedy Library and Museum, the <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_School_of_Government" title="John F. Kennedy School of Government">John F. Kennedy School of Government</a>, and an <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Institute_of_Politics" title="Harvard Institute of Politics">Institute of Politics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Huxtable1979_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huxtable1979-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> However the project faced many delays. The MBTA would not agree to remove the heavy machinery from the land until 1970. By that time construction costs had risen to over $20 million.<sup id="cite_ref-Raymont-RisingCosts_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raymont-RisingCosts-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Only now could Pei prepare a six-month study of the site's soil, and he said the "money we had six years ago, today will barely pay for 60 percent of the original plans."<sup id="cite_ref-Raymont-RisingCosts_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raymont-RisingCosts-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Robert Kennedy, by then a senator from New York, had been serving as president of the John F. Kennedy Library Corporation until he was <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Assassination of Robert F. Kennedy">assassinated in 1968</a>. Weeks before, <a href="/wiki/William_Manchester" title="William Manchester">William Manchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harper_%26_Row" class="mw-redirect" title="Harper &amp; Row">Harper &amp; Row</a> donated $750,000 to the library.<sup id="cite_ref-Raymont-Manchester_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raymont-Manchester-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The first in a series of installments expected to total $5 million, came from the profits of the book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Death_of_a_President" title="The Death of a President">The Death of a President</a></i> which caused a bitter feud between the Kennedys and Manchester. Mrs. Kennedy remarked "I think it is so beautiful what Mr. Manchester did. I am glad that Senator Kennedy knew about it before he died."<sup id="cite_ref-Raymont-Manchester_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raymont-Manchester-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The youngest of the <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_family#Third_generation" title="Kennedy family">Kennedy brothers</a>, Senator <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Edward M. Kennedy</a>, would step down as vice president of the corporation to fill the newly vacant position.
</p><p>By 1971, construction had still not begun; researchers and scholars were forced to work out of the Federal Records Center which was temporarily housing some of the 15 million documents and manuscripts. Pei said there was finally "a clear way ahead"; however, he was asked to save on construction expenses by using inexpensive materials.<sup id="cite_ref-Raymont_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Raymont-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This would translate into Pei working with concrete instead of his preferred stone.<sup id="cite_ref-WGBH_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WGBH-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On May 22, 1971, President <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a>, who succeeded Kennedy as president, saw the dedication of the <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_Baines_Johnson_Library_and_Museum" title="Lyndon Baines Johnson Library and Museum">Lyndon Baines Johnson Library and Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin, Texas</a>. On the campus of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Texas_at_Austin" title="University of Texas at Austin">University of Texas at Austin</a>, and next to the <a href="/wiki/LBJ_School_of_Public_Affairs" class="mw-redirect" title="LBJ School of Public Affairs">LBJ School of Public Affairs</a>, he would beat the Kennedy team to building the first Presidential Library that also served as a place of scholarly research. He would not live to see work begin on his predecessor's.
</p><p>Meanwhile, the Cambridge community was in fierce opposition to having the library being built in Cambridge at all. Although originally welcomed in 1965, the library was now seen as a great attractor of over a million annual tourists who would change the neighborhood with "hordes of tourists, automobiles, fast-food franchises and souvenir shops,"<sup id="cite_ref-KifnerBlockedby_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KifnerBlockedby-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jfkl.org-history_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfkl.org-history-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> as well as cause a negative environmental impact.<sup id="cite_ref-Kifner_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kifner-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> One neighborhood group filed a lawsuit in federal court demanding that the <a href="/wiki/General_Services_Administration" title="General Services Administration">General Services Administration</a> study, which found that the great number of visitors would have "no adverse effect on the area," be reexamined.<sup id="cite_ref-Kifner_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kifner-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Stephen E. Smith, a Kennedy in-law who heads the John F. Kennedy Library Corporation decided that "we want the Kennedy Library to be a happy place. It would not be in keeping with the nature of this memorial for it to open in an atmosphere of discord and controversy."<sup id="cite_ref-Kifner_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kifner-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> And in February 1975 the plans for having the library where President Kennedy would have wanted it, were dropped.<sup id="cite_ref-jfkl.org-history_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfkl.org-history-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kifner_22-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kifner-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="New_location.2C_new_plan"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="New_location,_new_plan">New location, new plan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Presidential_Library_and_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: New location, new plan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:137px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JFK_Library_Pavillion_%26_flag.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/JFK_Library_Pavillion_%26_flag.jpg/135px-JFK_Library_Pavillion_%26_flag.jpg" decoding="async" width="135" height="206" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/46/JFK_Library_Pavillion_%26_flag.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="200" data-file-height="305" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JFK_Library_Pavillion_%26_flag.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>From the pavilion (pictured), designer I. M. Pei says there is a restricted access area that offers the best view in the complex.<sup id="cite_ref-WGBH_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WGBH-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The new location of the site was <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Point_(Boston)" class="mw-redirect" title="Columbia Point (Boston)">Columbia Point</a> in the Dorchester neighborhood of Boston, near the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Massachusetts_Boston" title="University of Massachusetts Boston">University of Massachusetts Boston</a>, described as a group of "massive, blocky structures...in sharp contrast to the mellow and urbane atmosphere of the library's original site near Harvard Square."<sup id="cite_ref-Goldberger_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldberger-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The site was originally a <a href="/wiki/Garbage_dump" class="mw-redirect" title="Garbage dump">garbage dump</a>, Pei recalls finding old refrigerators and appliances under the soil. In all seriousness, he asserted that one could toss a lit match on the earth and watch the ground ignite as the soil emitted <a href="/wiki/Methane_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Methane gas">methane gas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WGBH_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WGBH-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> One thing the site did have going for it was that the community was not opposed to the area being <a href="/wiki/Landscaped" class="mw-redirect" title="Landscaped">landscaped</a> to house the library.
</p><p>June 12, 1977 marked the official <a href="/wiki/Groundbreaking_ceremony" class="mw-redirect" title="Groundbreaking ceremony">groundbreaking</a> for the library with construction following in August. Although the site was a landfill it did overlook Boston, <a href="/wiki/Dorchester_Bay_(Boston_Harbor)" title="Dorchester Bay (Boston Harbor)">Dorchester Bay</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_ocean" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlantic ocean">ocean</a>. The area was covered in 15 feet (4.6&#160;m) of earth and <a href="/wiki/Topsoil" title="Topsoil">topsoil</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-jfkl.org-Pei_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfkl.org-Pei-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Pei was particularly proud of the landscaping results.<sup id="cite_ref-WGBH_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WGBH-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The design would be a simple geometric structure with a large glass pavilion. The concrete tower stands 125 feet (38&#160;m) tall and houses offices and archives. A circular section contains two theaters and is connected to the tower by the 115 foot (35&#160;m) grey-glass pavilion. The <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">concrete</a> finish of the building directly reflects the budget.<sup id="cite_ref-Huxtable1979_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huxtable1979-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> With more money Pei would have made the building with stone which he believes offers a nicer finish with more detail.<sup id="cite_ref-WGBH_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WGBH-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The materials chosen kept the costs within budget, in total costing $20.8 million.<sup id="cite_ref-Huxtable1979_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Huxtable1979-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Over 30 million people contributed to the cost of construction,<sup id="cite_ref-jfkl.org-LibraryDedication_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfkl.org-LibraryDedication-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> which more than 225 construction workers labored to complete before the end of 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-jfkl.org-Pei_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfkl.org-Pei-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dedication">Dedication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Presidential_Library_and_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dedication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The official dedication was held on October 20, 1979. Outside the building on <a href="/wiki/Lawn" title="Lawn">the green</a>, on a blue-carpeted stage with a bank of yellow <a href="/wiki/Chrysanthemum" title="Chrysanthemum">chrysanthemums</a> sat the Kennedy family and those close to them. Among many others, President <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a> was in their company. The ceremony began with President Kennedy's daughter, <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Kennedy" title="Caroline Kennedy">Caroline Kennedy</a>, introducing her brother, <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="John F. Kennedy, Jr.">John F. Kennedy, Jr.</a>, who read from the <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Spender" title="Stephen Spender">Stephen Spender</a> poem, <i>I Think Continually of Those Who Were Truly Great</i>.
</p><p>President Carter said of John F. Kennedy that he embodied "the ideals of a generation as few public figures have ever done in the history of the earth."<sup id="cite_ref-TerranceSmith_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TerranceSmith-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-jfkl.org-CarterDedication_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfkl.org-CarterDedication-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> He spoke of openly weeping upon hearing about the death of Kennedy, something that he had not done since his own father died, ten years before. Afterwards, he accepted the library "on behalf of the American people"<sup id="cite_ref-TerranceSmith_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TerranceSmith-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/National_Archives_and_Records_Administration" title="National Archives and Records Administration">National Archives and Records Administration</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-jfkl.org-LibraryDedication_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfkl.org-LibraryDedication-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Senator <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Edward M. Kennedy</a>, said of his brother's life, that it "was a voyage of discovery, a quest for excellence that inspired universal trust and faith. In that brief unfinished journey, he made us believe once more in the great historic purpose of this land. He filled America with pride and made the nation young again."<sup id="cite_ref-jfkl.org-Tedication_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfkl.org-Tedication-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Critics generally liked the finished building, but the architect himself was unsatisfied. The years of conflict and compromise had changed the nature of the design, and Pei felt that the final result lacked its original passion. "I wanted to give something very special to the memory of President Kennedy," he said in 2000. "It could and should have been a great project."<sup id="cite_ref-vb56_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vb56-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Perhaps the most important consequence of the Kennedy project for Pei was his elevation in the public's consciousness as an architect of note.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Pei considered the John F. Kennedy Library "the most important commission in my life."<sup id="cite_ref-vb56_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vb56-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exhibits_and_collection">Exhibits and collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Presidential_Library_and_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Exhibits and collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The library's first floor features a museum containing video monitors, family photographs, political memorabilia. Visitors to the museum begin their visit by watching a film narrated by President Kennedy in one of two cinemas that show an orientation film, and a third shows a documentary on the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Missile_Crisis" title="Cuban Missile Crisis">Cuban Missile Crisis</a>.
</p><p>There are seven permanent exhibits:<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Campaign Trail – Exhibit on the <a href="/wiki/1960_United_States_presidential_election" title="1960 United States presidential election">presidential campaign of 1960</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Frontier" title="New Frontier">New Frontier</a>, featuring <a href="/wiki/1960_Democratic_National_Convention" title="1960 Democratic National Convention">1960 Democratic National Convention</a> memorabilia, and a replica of a Kennedy campaign office.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The Briefing Room – Exhibit on Kennedy's speeches and <a href="/wiki/Press_conference" class="mw-redirect" title="Press conference">press conferences</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The Space Race – Exhibit on the <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">Space Race</a> and the <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">U.S. space program</a> during <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Project Mercury</a>; features the <a href="/wiki/Mercury-Redstone_3" title="Mercury-Redstone 3">Mercury-Redstone 3</a> (<i>Freedom 7</i>) <a href="/wiki/Space_capsule" title="Space capsule">space capsule</a> in which astronaut <a href="/wiki/Alan_B._Shepard" class="mw-redirect" title="Alan B. Shepard">Alan B. Shepard</a> became the first American in space.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The capsule, which was displayed at the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Academy" title="United States Naval Academy">United States Naval Academy's</a> Armel-Leftwich Visitor Center from 1998 to 2012, came to the JFK Library in 2012, and will return to the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian Institution's</a> <a href="/wiki/National_Air_and_Space_Museum" title="National Air and Space Museum">National Air and Space Museum</a> in Washington, D.C.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Attorney General's Office – Exhibit on <a href="/wiki/United_States_Attorney_General" title="United States Attorney General">Attorney General</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a>, President Kennedy's brother and closest political advisor. Features information on RFK's role in fighting organized crime as chief counsel for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Select_Committee_on_Improper_Activities_in_Labor_and_Management" title="United States Senate Select Committee on Improper Activities in Labor and Management">Senate McClellan Committee</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Justice" title="United States Department of Justice">Department of Justice's</a> role in the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_Rights_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="American Civil Rights Movement">American Civil Rights Movement</a> during RFK's time as attorney general. The centerpiece of the exhibit are items that RFK had in his office at the <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy_Department_of_Justice_Building" title="Robert F. Kennedy Department of Justice Building">Department of Justice Building</a>. These include documents, personal items, and a <a href="/wiki/Bust_(sculpture)" title="Bust (sculpture)">bust</a> of Winston Churchill by <a href="/wiki/Leo_Cherne" title="Leo Cherne">Leo Cherne</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The Oval Office – Exhibit features information on the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_Rights_Movement" class="mw-redirect" title="American Civil Rights Movement">American Civil Rights Movement</a> during the Kennedy presidency, items that Kennedy kept in the <a href="/wiki/Oval_Office" title="Oval Office">Oval Office</a>, and a replica of the <a href="/wiki/Resolute_desk" title="Resolute desk"><i>Resolute</i> desk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>First Lady Jacqueline Bouvier Kennedy – Exhibit on the life of <a href="/wiki/First_Lady_of_the_United_States" title="First Lady of the United States">First Lady</a> <a href="/wiki/Jacqueline_Kennedy_Onassis" title="Jacqueline Kennedy Onassis">Jacqueline Bouvier Kennedy</a>; features footage of the First Lady and artifacts from her life, include several pieces of clothing.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The Kennedy Family – This exhibit on the famous <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_family" title="Kennedy family">Kennedy family</a> features a number of artifacts, including <a href="/wiki/Kathleen_Cavendish,_Marchioness_of_Hartington" title="Kathleen Cavendish, Marchioness of Hartington">Kathleen Kennedy's</a> <a href="/wiki/American_Red_Cross" title="American Red Cross">Red Cross</a> uniform jacket, a commemorative cup, a <a href="/wiki/Blackthorn" class="mw-redirect" title="Blackthorn">blackthorn</a> <a href="/wiki/Walking_stick" title="Walking stick">walking stick</a>, and a replica of the Great Mace of <a href="/wiki/Galway" title="Galway">Galway</a>, Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_F_Kennedy_sailboat_Victura_at_Kennedy_LibraryIMG_20160510_141846009_HDRc.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/John_F_Kennedy_sailboat_Victura_at_Kennedy_LibraryIMG_20160510_141846009_HDRc.jpg/160px-John_F_Kennedy_sailboat_Victura_at_Kennedy_LibraryIMG_20160510_141846009_HDRc.jpg" decoding="async" width="160" height="84" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/John_F_Kennedy_sailboat_Victura_at_Kennedy_LibraryIMG_20160510_141846009_HDRc.jpg/240px-John_F_Kennedy_sailboat_Victura_at_Kennedy_LibraryIMG_20160510_141846009_HDRc.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/John_F_Kennedy_sailboat_Victura_at_Kennedy_LibraryIMG_20160510_141846009_HDRc.jpg/320px-John_F_Kennedy_sailboat_Victura_at_Kennedy_LibraryIMG_20160510_141846009_HDRc.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5341" data-file-height="2804" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_F_Kennedy_sailboat_Victura_at_Kennedy_LibraryIMG_20160510_141846009_HDRc.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The sailboat <i>Victura</i> and the exterior of the Library</div></div></div>
<p>Among the Library's art collection is a 1962 portrait of Robert F. Kennedy by <a href="/wiki/Lajos_Markos" title="Lajos Markos">Lajos Markos</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> a watercolor sketch of John F. Kennedy by <a href="/wiki/Jamie_Wyeth" title="Jamie Wyeth">Jamie Wyeth</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> a watercolor painting of the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> painted by Jacqueline Kennedy and given as a gift to her husband, who had it hung in the Oval Office,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Fingerpaint" title="Fingerpaint">fingerpainting</a> by <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Kennedy" title="Caroline Kennedy">Caroline Kennedy</a> as a child,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> and a bust of John F. Kennedy sculpted by <a href="/wiki/Felix_de_Weldon" title="Felix de Weldon">Felix de Weldon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kennedy's 25-foot <a href="/wiki/Wianno_Senior" title="Wianno Senior">Wianno Senior</a> <a href="/wiki/Sailboat" title="Sailboat">sailboat</a> <i>Victura</i> is on display on the grounds of the Library from May to October.  Acquired by the family when Kennedy was 15, it played an important role in forging sibling bonds and, after the president's death, continued being sailed by other members of the family, especially race-enthusiast brother <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Ted</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Library has a variety of temporary and special exhibits.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Audiovisual">Audiovisual</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Presidential_Library_and_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Audiovisual">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The audiovisual archives contain over 400,000 still photographs taken from 1863–1984, over 7.5 million feet (2.29×<a href="/wiki/One_million" class="mw-redirect" title="One million">10<sup>6</sup></a> m) of film shot between 1910–83, and 11,000 reels of audio recordings from 1910–85.<sup id="cite_ref-jfkl.org-AV_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfkl.org-AV-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oral-history_project">Oral-history project</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Presidential_Library_and_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Oral-history project">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Begun in 1964, the oral-history project was a unique undertaking to document and preserve interviews with those associated with Kennedy. Initially expected to have about 150 participants,<sup id="cite_ref-LewisMarch_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LewisMarch-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> today it contains over 1,100 interviews and continues to this day.<sup id="cite_ref-jfkl.org-oral-history_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfkl.org-oral-history-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> It is modelled after a program by the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University_Oral_History_Research_Office" title="Columbia University Oral History Research Office">Columbia University Oral History Research Office</a>, the worlds oldest, which began in 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-LewisJanuary_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LewisJanuary-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> At its conception, while serving as Attorney General, Robert Kennedy speculated that some of the interviews, such as ones relating to the Cuban Missile Crisis, might have to be sealed for a while due to containing "highly classified material." He said that although the emphasis would be on releasing everything as soon as possible, some items might remain closed for 10 to 25 years.<sup id="cite_ref-LewisJanuary_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LewisJanuary-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Because the interviewees are allowed to review their transcripts before the interviews are released for use by scholars, the audio may differ from the written record; so that the interviewing may disambiguate any misunderstanding in their speech and make it clear in a written form.<sup id="cite_ref-jfkl.org-oral-history_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfkl.org-oral-history-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Artifacts">Artifacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Presidential_Library_and_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Artifacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The library keeps a wide range of artifacts, many of which can be found in their respective exhibits. One is the original coconut on which a rescue message was inscribed by Kennedy to rescue the crew of the <i><a href="/wiki/Motor_Torpedo_Boat_PT-109" class="mw-redirect" title="Motor Torpedo Boat PT-109">PT-109</a></i>, and which was delivered by <a href="/wiki/Biuku_Gasa_and_Eroni_Kumana" title="Biuku Gasa and Eroni Kumana">Biuku Gasa and Eroni Kumana</a>, natives of the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-jfkl.org-coconut_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jfkl.org-coconut-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Ernest_Hemingway_Collection">The Ernest Hemingway Collection</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Presidential_Library_and_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: The Ernest Hemingway Collection">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The library is also home to a collection of documents and belongings from <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">Ernest Hemingway</a>.  The collection was established in 1968 following an exchange of letters between <a href="/wiki/Mary_Welsh_Hemingway" title="Mary Welsh Hemingway">Hemingway's widow Mary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacqueline_Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacqueline Kennedy">Jacqueline Kennedy</a> that confirmed that Hemingway's papers would be archived there.<sup id="cite_ref-ehchist_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ehchist-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> In 1961, despite a U.S. travel ban to Cuba, President Kennedy had arranged to allow Mary Hemingway to go there to claim her recently deceased husband's documents and belongings.<sup id="cite_ref-ehchist_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ehchist-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>  A room for the collection was dedicated on July 18, 1980 by <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Hemingway" title="Patrick Hemingway">Patrick Hemingway</a> and Jacqueline Kennedy Onassis.<sup id="cite_ref-ehchist_51-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ehchist-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Ernest Hemingway Collection spans Hemingway's career, and includes "ninety percent of existing Hemingway manuscript materials, making the Kennedy Library the world's principal center for research" on his life and work.<sup id="cite_ref-ehccoll_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ehccoll-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>  It includes:<sup id="cite_ref-ehccoll_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ehccoll-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Over 1000 manuscripts of varying lengths, including hand-written drafts of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sun_Also_Rises" title="The Sun Also Rises">The Sun Also Rises</a></i> and dozens of hand-drafted alternate endings to <i><a href="/wiki/A_Farewell_to_Arms" title="A Farewell to Arms">A Farewell to Arms</a></i></li>
<li>Research material on bullfighting, used as background for <i><a href="/wiki/Death_in_the_Afternoon" title="Death in the Afternoon">Death in the Afternoon</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Dangerous_Summer" title="The Dangerous Summer">The Dangerous Summer</a></i></li>
<li>Thousands of letters written by or to Hemingway; this included correspondence with fellow writers such as <a href="/wiki/Sherwood_Anderson" title="Sherwood Anderson">Sherwood Anderson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Baker" title="Carlos Baker">Carlos Baker</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Dos_Passos" title="John Dos Passos">John Dos Passos</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_Faulkner" title="William Faulkner">William Faulkner</a>, <a href="/wiki/F._Scott_Fitzgerald" title="F. Scott Fitzgerald">F. Scott Fitzgerald</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Frost" title="Robert Frost">Robert Frost</a>, <a href="/wiki/Martha_Gellhorn" title="Martha Gellhorn">Martha Gellhorn</a>, <a href="/wiki/A._E._Hotchner" title="A. E. Hotchner">A. E. Hotchner</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Joyce" title="James Joyce">James Joyce</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archibald_MacLeish" title="Archibald MacLeish">Archibald MacLeish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ezra_Pound" title="Ezra Pound">Ezra Pound</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gertrude_Stein" title="Gertrude Stein">Gertrude Stein</a>, as well as with actress <a href="/wiki/Marlene_Dietrich" title="Marlene Dietrich">Marlene Dietrich</a>, restaurateur <a href="/wiki/Toots_Shor" title="Toots Shor">Toots Shor</a>, Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Francis_Spellman" title="Francis Spellman">Francis Spellman</a>, publisher <a href="/wiki/Charles_Scribner_II" title="Charles Scribner II">Charles Scribner</a>, his editor <a href="/wiki/Maxwell_Perkins" title="Maxwell Perkins">Maxwell Perkins</a>, and his lawyer Alfred Rice</li>
<li>More than 10,000 photographs, as well as press clippings and other <a href="/wiki/Ephemera" title="Ephemera">ephemera</a></li>
<li>Books from his private library, many with <a href="/wiki/Marginalia" title="Marginalia">marginalia</a>, and including a rare copy of <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Goya" title="Francisco Goya">Francisco Goya</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Los_disparates" title="Los disparates">Los Proverbios</a></i></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="2013_fire">2013 fire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Presidential_Library_and_Museum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: 2013 fire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On April 15, 2013, a fire occurred in the library. Initial reports stated the fire appears to have started in a mechanical room.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> An official communication from the library stated that the fire is being investigated. The fire was unrelated to the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Marathon_bombings" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Marathon bombings">Boston Marathon bombings</a>, which occurred simultaneously.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
