<p><b>Brookline</b>  <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;b&#39; in &#39;buy&#39;">b</span><span title="&#39;r&#39; in &#39;rye&#39;">r</span><span title="/ʊ/: &#39;u&#39; in &#39;push&#39;">ʊ</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a town in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk_County,_Massachusetts" title="Norfolk County, Massachusetts">Norfolk County, Massachusetts</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, and is a part of <a href="/wiki/Greater_Boston" title="Greater Boston">Greater Boston</a>. Brookline borders six of <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>'s neighborhoods: <a href="/wiki/Brighton,_Boston" title="Brighton, Boston">Brighton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Allston" title="Allston">Allston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fenway%E2%80%93Kenmore" title="Fenway–Kenmore">Fenway–Kenmore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mission_Hill,_Boston" title="Mission Hill, Boston">Mission Hill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica_Plain" title="Jamaica Plain">Jamaica Plain</a>, and <a href="/wiki/West_Roxbury" title="West Roxbury">West Roxbury</a>. The city of <a href="/wiki/Newton,_Massachusetts" title="Newton, Massachusetts">Newton</a> lies to the west of Brookline.
</p><p>At the 2010 census, the population of the town was 58,732. It is the most populous municipality in Massachusetts to have a <a href="/wiki/New_England_town" title="New England town">town</a> (rather than city) form of government.
</p><p>Brookline was first settled in 1638 as a <a href="/wiki/Hamlet_(place)" title="Hamlet (place)">hamlet</a> in Boston, but was incorporated as a separate town in 1705.
</p><p>Brookline was the birthplace and hometown of <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>, 35th <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a>.
</p>

<p>Once part of <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_peoples" title="Algonquian peoples">Algonquian</a> territory, Brookline was first settled by <a href="/wiki/White_people" title="White people">European</a> colonists in the early 17th century. The area was an outlying part of the colonial settlement of Boston and known as the hamlet of Muddy River. In 1705, it was incorporated as the independent town of Brookline. The northern and southern borders of the town were marked by two small rivers or brooks, hence the name. The northern border with Brighton (which was itself part of <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge</a> until 1807) was Smelt Brook. (That name appears on maps starting at least as early as 1852, but sometime between 1888 and 1925 the brook was covered over.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>) The southern boundary, abutting Boston, was the <a href="/wiki/Muddy_River_(Massachusetts)" title="Muddy River (Massachusetts)">Muddy River</a>.
</p><p>The Town of <a href="/wiki/Brighton,_Boston" title="Brighton, Boston">Brighton</a> was merged with Boston in 1874, and the Boston-Brookline border was redrawn to connect the new Back Bay neighborhood with Allston-Brighton. This merger created a narrow strip of land along the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River" title="Charles River">Charles River</a> belonging to Boston, cutting Brookline off from the shoreline. It also put certain lands north of the Muddy River on the Boston side, including what are now <a href="/wiki/Kenmore_Square" title="Kenmore Square">Kenmore Square</a> and <a href="/wiki/Packard%27s_Corner" title="Packard&#39;s Corner">Packard's Corner</a>. The current northern border follows Commonwealth Avenue, and on the northeast, St. Mary's Street. When <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Law_Olmsted" title="Frederick Law Olmsted">Frederick Law Olmsted</a> designed the <a href="/wiki/Emerald_Necklace" title="Emerald Necklace">Emerald Necklace</a> of parks and parkways for Boston in the 1890s, the Muddy River was integrated into the <a href="/wiki/Riverway" title="Riverway">Riverway</a> and <a href="/wiki/Olmsted_Park" title="Olmsted Park">Olmsted Park</a>, creating parkland accessible by both Boston and Brookline residents.
</p><p>Throughout its history, Brookline has resisted being annexed by Boston, in particular during the <a href="/wiki/Boston%E2%80%93Brookline_annexation_debate_of_1873" title="Boston–Brookline annexation debate of 1873">Boston–Brookline annexation debate of 1873</a>. The neighboring towns of <a href="/wiki/West_Roxbury" title="West Roxbury">West Roxbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hyde_Park,_Boston" title="Hyde Park, Boston">Hyde Park</a> connected Brookline to the rest of Norfolk County until they were annexed by Boston in 1874 and 1912, respectively, putting them in <a href="/wiki/Suffolk_County,_Massachusetts" title="Suffolk County, Massachusetts">Suffolk County</a>. Brookline is now separated from the remainder of Norfolk County.
</p><p>Brookline has long been regarded as a pleasant and verdant environment. In the 1841 edition of the <i>Theory and Practice of Landscape Gardening</i>, Andrew Jackson Downing described the area this way:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The whole of this neighborhood of Brookline is a kind of landscape garden, and there is nothing in America of the sort, so inexpressibly charming as the lanes which lead from one cottage, or villa, to another. No animals are allowed to run at large, and the open gates, with tempting vistas and glimpses under the pendent boughs, give it quite an Arcadian air of rural freedom and enjoyment. These lanes are clothed with a profusion of trees and wild shrubbery, often almost to the carriage tracks, and curve and wind about, in a manner quite bewildering to the stranger who attempts to thread them alone; and there are more hints here for the lover of the picturesque in lanes than we ever saw assembled together in so small a compass.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Brookline residents were among the first in the country to propose extending the vote to women. Benjamin F. Butler, in his 1882 campaign for Governor, advocated the idea.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation_history">Transportation history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookline,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Transportation history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Two branches of upper <a href="/wiki/Boston_Post_Road" title="Boston Post Road">Boston Post Road</a>, established in the 1670s, passed through Brookline. Brookline Village was the original center of retail activity.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 1810, the Boston and Worcester Turnpike, now <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Route_9" title="Massachusetts Route 9">Massachusetts Route 9</a>, was laid out, starting on <a href="/wiki/Huntington_Avenue" title="Huntington Avenue">Huntington Avenue</a> in Boston and passing through the village center on its way west.
</p><p>Steam railroads came to Brookline in the middle of the 19th century. The <a href="/wiki/Boston_and_Worcester_Railroad" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston and Worcester Railroad">Boston and Worcester Railroad</a> was constructed in the early 1830s, and passed through Brookline near the Charles River. The rail line is still in active use, now paralleled by the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Turnpike" title="Massachusetts Turnpike">Massachusetts Turnpike</a>. The Highland Branch of the <a href="/wiki/Boston_and_Albany_Railroad" title="Boston and Albany Railroad">Boston and Albany Railroad</a> was built from Kenmore Square to Brookline Village in 1847, and was extended into Newton in 1852. In the late 1950s, this would become the <a href="/wiki/Green_Line_%22D%22_Branch" title="Green Line &quot;D&quot; Branch">Green Line "D" Branch</a>.
</p><p>The portion of Beacon Street west of Kenmore Square was laid out in 1850.  <a href="/wiki/Streetcar" class="mw-redirect" title="Streetcar">Streetcar</a> tracks were laid above ground on Beacon Street in 1888, from <a href="/wiki/Coolidge_Corner" title="Coolidge Corner">Coolidge Corner</a> to <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Avenue_(metropolitan_Boston)" title="Massachusetts Avenue (metropolitan Boston)">Massachusetts Avenue</a> in Boston, via Kenmore Square.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  In 1889, they were electrified and extended over the Brighton border at <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Circle" title="Cleveland Circle">Cleveland Circle</a>. They would eventually become the <a href="/wiki/Green_Line_%22C%22_Branch" title="Green Line &quot;C&quot; Branch">Green Line "C" Branch</a>.
</p><p>Thanks to the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Elevated_Railway" title="Boston Elevated Railway">Boston Elevated Railway</a> system, this upgrade from <a href="/wiki/Horse-drawn_carriage" class="mw-redirect" title="Horse-drawn carriage">horse-drawn carriage</a> to electric trolleys occurred on many major streets all over the region, and made transportation into downtown Boston faster and cheaper. Much of Brookline was developed into a <a href="/wiki/Streetcar_suburb" title="Streetcar suburb">streetcar suburb</a>, with large brick apartment buildings sprouting up along the new streetcar lines.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookline,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Brookline was known as the hamlet of <b>Muddy River</b> and was considered part of Boston until the Town of Brookline was independently incorporated in 1705.  (The <a href="/wiki/Muddy_River_(Massachusetts)" title="Muddy River (Massachusetts)">Muddy River</a> was used as the Brookline–Boston border at incorporation.) It is said that the name derives from a farm therein once owned by Judge <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Sewall" title="Samuel Sewall">Samuel Sewall</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookline,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, Brookline has a total area of 6.8&#160;sq&#160;mi (17.7&#160;km<sup>2</sup>), all but 0.039&#160;sq&#160;mi (0.1&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) (0.44%) of which is land.
</p><p>The northern part of Brookline, roughly north of the D-line tracks, is urban in character, as highly walkable and transit rich. The population density of this part of town is nearly 20,000 inhabitants per square mile (8,000/km<sup>2</sup>), on a par with the densest neighborhoods in nearby <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somerville,_Massachusetts" title="Somerville, Massachusetts">Somerville</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chelsea,_Massachusetts" title="Chelsea, Massachusetts">Chelsea</a>, Massachusetts (the densest cities in New England), and just below that of central Boston's residential districts (Back Bay, South End, Fenway, etc.).  The overall density of Brookline, which also includes suburban districts and grand estates south of the D-line, is still higher than that of many of the largest cities in the United States, especially in the South and West.  Brookline borders <a href="/wiki/Newton,_Massachusetts" title="Newton, Massachusetts">Newton</a> (part of <a href="/wiki/Middlesex_County,_Massachusetts" title="Middlesex County, Massachusetts">Middlesex County</a>) to the west and <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> (part of <a href="/wiki/Suffolk_County,_Massachusetts" title="Suffolk County, Massachusetts">Suffolk County</a>) in all other directions; it is therefore non-contiguous with any other part of Norfolk County.  Brookline became an <a href="/wiki/Enclave_and_exclave" title="Enclave and exclave">exclave</a> in 1873, when the neighboring town of <a href="/wiki/West_Roxbury" title="West Roxbury">West Roxbury</a> was annexed by Boston (and left Norfolk County to join <a href="/wiki/Suffolk_County,_Massachusetts" title="Suffolk County, Massachusetts">Suffolk County</a>), and Brookline refused to be annexed by Boston after the <a href="/wiki/Boston%E2%80%93Brookline_annexation_debate_of_1873" title="Boston–Brookline annexation debate of 1873">Boston–Brookline annexation debate of 1873</a>.
</p><p>Brookline separates the bulk of the city of Boston (except for a narrow neck or corridor near the <a href="/wiki/Charles_River" title="Charles River">Charles River</a>) from its westernmost neighborhoods of <a href="/wiki/Allston%E2%80%93Brighton" title="Allston–Brighton">Allston–Brighton</a>, which had been the separate town of Brighton until annexed by Boston in 1873.
</p>

<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup id="cite_ref-GR2_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> of 2010, there were 58,732 people, 24,891 households, and 12,233 families residing in the town. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 8,701.0 people per square mile (3,247.3/km²). There were 26,448 housing units at an average density of 3,889.6 per square mile (1,501.9/km²). The racial makeup of the town was 73.3% <a href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 3.4% <a href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="African American (U.S. Census)">Black</a> or <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">African American</a>, 0.12% <a href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 15.6% <a href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a> (6.7% <a href="/wiki/Chinese_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese American">Chinese</a>, 2.6% <a href="/wiki/Indian_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian American">Indian</a>, 2.3% <a href="/wiki/Korean_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Korean American">Korean</a>, 1.8% <a href="/wiki/Japanese_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese American">Japanese</a>), 0.03% <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 1.01% from <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 3.0% from two or more races.  <a href="/wiki/Race_and_ethnicity_in_the_United_States_Census" title="Race and ethnicity in the United States Census">Hispanic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 5.0% of the population (0.9% <a href="/wiki/Mexican_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Mexican American">Mexican</a>, 0.8% <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Ricans_in_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Puerto Ricans in the United States">Puerto Rican</a>). (Source: 2010 Census Quickfacts)
</p><p>There were 25,594 households out of which 21.9% had children under the age of 18, living with them, 38.4% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 7.1% had a female householder with no husband present, and 52.2% were non-families. 36.7% of all households were made up of individuals and 10.1% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.18 and the average family size was 2.86.
</p><p>In the town, the population was spread out with 16.6% under the age of 18, 11.7%, from 18 to 24, 37.3% from 25 to 44, 21.9% from 45 to 64, and 12.4% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 34 years. For every 100 females, there were 82.6 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 79.1 males.
</p><p>The median income for a household in the town was $66,711, and the median income for a family was $92,993. Males had a median income of $56,861 versus $43,436 for females. The per capita income for the town was $44,327.  About 4.5% of families and 9.3% of the population were below the <a href="/wiki/Poverty_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 5.3% of those under the age of 18 and 7.5% of those ages 65 and older.
</p><p>Serving as a residential zone for nearby academic and medical institutes such as <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Medical_School" title="Harvard Medical School">Harvard Medical School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boston_University" title="Boston University">Boston University</a>, the town of Brookline was reported as the city with the most doctoral degree holders (14.0% of the total population) in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arts_and_culture">Arts and culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookline,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Arts and culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Brookline, along with the nearby Boston neighborhood of Brighton and the city of Newton, is a cultural hub for the Jewish community of Greater Boston.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Archdiocese_of_America" title="Greek Orthodox Archdiocese of America">Greek Orthodox Archdiocese of America</a> Metropolis of Boston is headquartered in Brookline.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<ul><li>Brookline Village is home to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Puppet_Showplace_Theater&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Puppet Showplace Theater (page does not exist)">Puppet Showplace Theater</a>, New England’s only dedicated puppet theater and center for puppetry arts. The theater is located in the historic 32 Station Street building directly across from the Brookline Village MBTA Green Line stop.</li></ul>
<ul><li>There have been three <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.brooklinearts.org/brookline-poet-laureate.html">Poet Laureates of Brookline</a>: Judith Steinbergh, <a href="/wiki/Jan_Schreiber" title="Jan Schreiber">Jan Schreiber</a>, and, currently, Zvi Sesling.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Points_of_interest">Points of interest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookline,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Points of interest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Chestnut_Hill,_Massachusetts" title="Chestnut Hill, Massachusetts">Chestnut Hill Points of Interest</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Overlooking_Leverett_Pond_in_Olmsted_Park_from_the_Brookline,_MA_side.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Overlooking_Leverett_Pond_in_Olmsted_Park_from_the_Brookline%2C_MA_side.JPG/220px-Overlooking_Leverett_Pond_in_Olmsted_Park_from_the_Brookline%2C_MA_side.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Overlooking_Leverett_Pond_in_Olmsted_Park_from_the_Brookline%2C_MA_side.JPG/330px-Overlooking_Leverett_Pond_in_Olmsted_Park_from_the_Brookline%2C_MA_side.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Overlooking_Leverett_Pond_in_Olmsted_Park_from_the_Brookline%2C_MA_side.JPG/440px-Overlooking_Leverett_Pond_in_Olmsted_Park_from_the_Brookline%2C_MA_side.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1936" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Overlooking_Leverett_Pond_in_Olmsted_Park_from_the_Brookline,_MA_side.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Overlooking Leverett Pond in Olmsted Park from the Brookline side</div></div></div>
<p>The following historic buildings are open to the public:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/John_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_National_Historic_Site" title="John Fitzgerald Kennedy National Historic Site">birthplace of John F. Kennedy</a> stands in Brookline and is listed in the <a href="/wiki/National_Register_of_Historic_Places" title="National Register of Historic Places">National Register of Historic Places</a>. It is maintained by the <a href="/wiki/National_Park_Service" title="National Park Service">National Park Service</a> and is open to the public from May through September.</li>
<li>"Fairsted", the 100-year-old business headquarters and design office for renowned landscape architect <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Law_Olmsted" title="Frederick Law Olmsted">Frederick Law Olmsted</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Olmsted_Brothers" title="Olmsted Brothers">Olmsted Brothers</a> firm, has been carefully preserved as the <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Law_Olmsted_National_Historic_Site" title="Frederick Law Olmsted National Historic Site">Frederick Law Olmsted National Historic Site</a>, on 7 acres (2.8&#160;ha) of landscaped grounds at 99 Warren Street.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/John_Goddard_House" title="John Goddard House">John Goddard House</a>, an historic house at 235 Goddard Avenue, was built in 1767 and added to the National Register of Historic Places in 1985.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Larz_Anderson_Park" title="Larz Anderson Park">Larz Anderson Park</a> is in Brookline on the 64-acre (26&#160;ha) estate once owned by <a href="/wiki/Larz_Anderson" title="Larz Anderson">Larz Anderson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isabel_Weld_Perkins" title="Isabel Weld Perkins">Isabel Weld Perkins</a>. The park contains the <a href="/wiki/Larz_Anderson_Auto_Museum" title="Larz Anderson Auto Museum">Larz Anderson Auto Museum</a>, the oldest automobile collection in the country, as well as <a href="/wiki/Putterham_School" title="Putterham School">Putterham School</a>, a <a href="/wiki/One-room_schoolhouse" class="mw-redirect" title="One-room schoolhouse">one-room schoolhouse</a> from <a href="/wiki/Colonial_America" class="mw-redirect" title="Colonial America">colonial times</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Other historic and cultural sites include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/St._Aidan%27s_Church_(Brookline,_Massachusetts)" title="St. Aidan&#39;s Church (Brookline, Massachusetts)">St. Aidan's Church</a> was where John F. Kennedy was baptized and where the Kennedy family and other prominent Irish-Americans were parishioners. The church was designed by architect Charles Maginnis, who was awarded the <a href="/wiki/American_Institute_of_Architects" title="American Institute of Architects">American Institute of Architects</a>' gold medal. Although it is listed on the National Register of Historic Places, St. Aidan's Church has been closed and converted into housing.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Dutch_House" title="The Dutch House">The Dutch House</a>, one of only five surviving buildings from the <a href="/wiki/World%27s_Columbian_Exposition" title="World&#39;s Columbian Exposition">World's Columbian Exposition</a> of 1893 was relocated to Brookline.</li>
<li>There were two stops on the <a href="/wiki/Underground_Railroad" title="Underground Railroad">Underground Railroad</a> in Brookline: <a href="/wiki/William_Ingersoll_Bowditch_House" title="William Ingersoll Bowditch House">9 Toxteth Street</a> and 182 Walnut Street.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Country_Club" title="The Country Club">The Country Club</a>, an exclusive sporting club in the town, was the first private club in the United States formed exclusively for outdoor activities. It is most famous as a golf club; it was one of the five clubs that formed what is now the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Golf_Association" title="United States Golf Association">United States Golf Association</a> and has hosted the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Open_(golf)" title="U.S. Open (golf)">U.S. Open</a> three times and the <a href="/wiki/Ryder_Cup" title="Ryder Cup">Ryder Cup Matches</a> once.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Coolidge_Corner" title="Coolidge Corner">Coolidge Corner</a>, which is located at the crossing of <a href="/wiki/Beacon_Street" title="Beacon Street">Beacon Street</a> and Harvard Street, is one of Brookline's two primary retail districts (the other being <a href="/wiki/Washington_Square_(Brookline)" title="Washington Square (Brookline)">Washington Square</a>). It includes a number of historically significant sites, including the <a href="/wiki/S.S._Pierce" title="S.S. Pierce">S.S. Pierce</a> Building, and the Coolidge Corner Theatre.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.coolidge.org/">[1]</a></li>
<li>Brookline is home to part of <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Law_Olmsted" title="Frederick Law Olmsted">Frederick Law Olmsted</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Emerald_Necklace" title="Emerald Necklace">Emerald Necklace</a> of park systems, including <a href="/wiki/Olmsted_Park" title="Olmsted Park">Olmsted Park</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Puppet_Showplace" title="Puppet Showplace">Puppet Showplace Theatre</a>, one of the four oldest puppet theatres in the United States, is located in Brookline Village.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government">Government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookline,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Brookline is governed by a <a href="/wiki/Representative_town_meeting" title="Representative town meeting">representative (elected) town meeting</a>, which is the legislative body of the town, and a five-person <a href="/w/index.php?title=Select_Board&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Select Board (page does not exist)">Select Board</a> that serves as the executive branch of the town. <sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2017, a Brookline Town Meeting voted to recognize Indigenous People's Day instead of Columbus Day. <sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_schools">Public schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookline,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Public schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The town is served by the Public Schools of Brookline.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.brookline.k12.ma.us/">[2]</a> The student body at <a href="/wiki/Brookline_High_School" title="Brookline High School">Brookline High School</a> includes students from more than 76 countries. Many students attend Brookline High from surrounding neighborhoods in Boston such as Mission Hill and Mattapan through the Metropolitan Council for Educational Opportunity (<a href="/wiki/METCO" title="METCO">METCO</a>) system.
</p><p>There are eight elementary schools in the Brookline Public School system: <a href="/wiki/Edith_C._Baker_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Edith C. Baker School">Baker School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Devotion_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward Devotion School">Coolidge Corner School</a>, Driscoll, <a href="/wiki/Heath_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Heath School">Heath</a>, Lawrence, <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_School_(Brookline,_Massachusetts)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lincoln School (Brookline, Massachusetts)">Lincoln</a>, Pierce, and <a href="/wiki/John_D._Runkle" class="mw-redirect" title="John D. Runkle">Runkle</a>. As of December 2006, there were 6,089 K-12 students enrolled in the Brookline public schools. The system includes one early learning center, eight grades K-8 schools, and one comprehensive high school. The Old Lincoln School is a surplus building used by the town to temporarily teach students in when another school building is being renovated. It was rented in 2009 as the venue for the play <a href="/wiki/Sleep_No_More_(2009_play)" title="Sleep No More (2009 play)">Sleep No More</a>.
</p><p>The student body is 57.4% White, 18.1% Asian, 6.4% Black, 9.9% Hispanic, and 8.2% Multi-race. Approximately 30% of students come from homes where English is not the first language. (Data from Massachusetts department of education 2012–2013 Year)
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_department">Fire department</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Brookline,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Fire department">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The town of Brookline is protected full-time by the 158 paid, professional <a href="/wiki/Firefighter" title="Firefighter">firefighters</a> of the Brookline Fire Department (BFD). It currently operates out of five fire stations located throughout the town, under the command of a Deputy Chief per shift. The BFD also operates a fire apparatus fleet of four engines, two ladders, one quint, one cross-staffed rescue (special operations), two squads, one special operations unit, one decontamination trailer, two maintenance units, as well as numerous other special, support, and reserve units. The Brookline Fire Department responds to approximately 6,500 emergency calls annually. The current Chief of Department is John F. Sullivan.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
