<p><b>Toponymy</b> is the study of place names (<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/toponym" class="extiw" title="wikt:toponym">toponyms</a>), their origins, meanings, use and <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/typology" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:typology">typology</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toponymy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "toponymy" is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> words <i>tópos</i> (τόπος) "place" and <i>ónoma</i> (ὄνομα) "name". Toponymy is itself a branch of <a href="/wiki/Onomastics" title="Onomastics">onomastics</a>, the study of names of all kinds.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meaning_and_history">Meaning and history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toponymy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Meaning and history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Toponym</i> is the general name for any place or geographical entity.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Related, more specific types of toponym include <i><a href="/wiki/Hydronymy" class="mw-redirect" title="Hydronymy">hydronym</a></i> for a body of water and <i>oronym</i> for a mountain or hill. A <i>toponymist</i> is one who studies toponymy.
</p><p>According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i>, the word "toponymy" first appeared in English in 1876; since then, toponym has come to replace "place-name" in professional discourse among <a href="/wiki/Geographer" title="Geographer">geographers</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It can be argued that the first toponymists were the storytellers and poets who explained the origin of specific place names as part of their tales; sometimes place-names served as the basis for the <a href="/wiki/Etiology" title="Etiology">etiological</a> legends. The process of <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">folk etymology</a> usually took over, whereby a false meaning was extracted from a name based on its structure or sounds. Thus, the toponym of <a href="/wiki/Hellespont" class="mw-redirect" title="Hellespont">Hellespont</a> was explained by Greek poets as being named after <a href="/wiki/Helle_(mythology)" title="Helle (mythology)">Helle</a>, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Athamas" title="Athamas">Athamas</a>, who drowned there as she crossed it with her brother <a href="/wiki/Phrixus" title="Phrixus">Phrixus</a> on a flying golden ram. The name, however, is probably derived from an older language, such as <a href="/wiki/Pelasgian" class="mw-redirect" title="Pelasgian">Pelasgian</a>, which was unknown to those who explained its origin. <a href="/wiki/George_R._Stewart" title="George R. Stewart">George R. Stewart</a> theorized, in his book <i>Names on the Globe</i>, that <i>Hellespont</i> originally meant something like "narrow Pontus" or "entrance to Pontus", "Pontus" being an ancient name for the region around the <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">Black Sea</a>, and by extension, for the sea itself.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Place names provide the most useful geographical reference system in the world. Consistency and accuracy are essential in referring to a place to prevent confusion in everyday business and recreation. A toponymist, through well-established local principles and procedures developed in cooperation and consultation with the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Group_of_Experts_on_Geographical_Names" title="United Nations Group of Experts on Geographical Names">United Nations Group of Experts on Geographical Names</a> (UNGEGN), applies the science of toponymy to establish officially recognized geographical names. A toponymist relies not only on maps and local histories, but interviews with local residents to determine names with established local usage. The exact application of a toponym, its specific language, its pronunciation, and its origins and meaning are all important facts to be recorded during name surveys.
</p><p>Scholars have found that toponyms provide valuable insight into the historical geography of a particular region. In 1954 <a href="/wiki/F._M._Powicke" title="F. M. Powicke">F. M. Powicke</a> said of place-name study that it "uses, enriches and tests the discoveries of archaeology and history and the rules of the <a href="/wiki/Philology" title="Philology">philologists</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Toponyms not only illustrate ethnic settlement patterns, but they can also help identify discrete periods of immigration.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Toponymists are responsible for the active preservation of their region's culture through its toponymy. They typically ensure the ongoing development of a geographical names data base and associated publications, for recording and disseminating authoritative hard-copy and digital toponymic data. This data may be disseminated in a wide variety of formats, including hard-copy topographic maps as well as digital formats such as <a href="/wiki/Geographic_information_system" title="Geographic information system">geographic information systems</a> and <a href="/wiki/Google_Maps" title="Google Maps">Google Maps</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Issues">Issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Toponymy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2002, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_the_Standardization_of_Geographical_Names" class="mw-redirect" title="United Nations Conference on the Standardization of Geographical Names">United Nations Conference on the Standardization of Geographical Names</a> acknowledged that while common, the practice of naming geographical places after living persons could be problematic. As such, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Group_of_Experts_on_Geographical_Names" title="United Nations Group of Experts on Geographical Names">United Nations Group of Experts on Geographical Names</a> recommends that it be avoided and that national authorities should set their own guidelines as to the time required after a person's death for the use of a commemorative name.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the same vein, authors Pinchevski and Torgovnik considers the naming of streets as a political act in which holders of the legitimate monopoly to name aspire to engrave their ideological views in the social space.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, the <a href="/wiki/Historical_revisionism" title="Historical revisionism">revisionist</a> practice of
<a href="/wiki/Street_or_road_name#Street_renaming" title="Street or road name">renaming streets</a>, as both the celebration of triumph and the repudiation of the old regime is another issue of toponymy.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Also, in the context of <a href="/wiki/Russian_nationalism" title="Russian nationalism">Slavic nationalism</a>, the name of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a> was changed to the more Slavic sounding <i>Petrograd</i> from 1914 to 1924,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> then to <i>Leningrad</i> following the death of <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Vladimir Lenin</a> and back to <i>Saint-Peterburg</i> in 1991 after the fall of the Soviet Union. After 1830, in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_War_of_Independence" title="Greek War of Independence">Greek War of Independence</a> and the establishment of an independent Greek state, Turkish, Slavic and Italian place names were Hellenized, as an effort of "toponymic cleansing". This nationalization of place names can also manifest itself in a <a href="/wiki/Postcolonialism" title="Postcolonialism">postcolonial</a> context.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Frictions sometimes arise between countries because of toponymy, as illustrated by the <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_naming_dispute" title="Macedonia naming dispute">Macedonia naming dispute</a> in which <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> has claimed the name <i>Macedonia</i>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf_naming_dispute" title="Persian Gulf naming dispute">Persian Gulf naming dispute</a>. Over the years, it has also been noted that a map producer used the name <a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf" title="Persian Gulf">Persian Gulf</a> in a 1977 map of <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a> while retaining "<i>Arabian Gulf</i>" in another 1977 map focusing on the <a href="/wiki/Arab_states_of_the_Persian_Gulf" title="Arab states of the Persian Gulf">Arab states of the Persian Gulf</a>, underlying the occasional spilling of place names issues into the economic sphere.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
