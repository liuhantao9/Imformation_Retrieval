<p><b>Sranan Tongo</b> (also <b>Sranantongo</b> "Surinamese tongue", <b>Sranan</b>, <b>Surinaams</b>, <b>Surinamese</b>, <b>Surinamese Creole</b>, <b>Taki Taki</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/English-based_creole_language" title="English-based creole language">English-based creole language</a> that is spoken as a <i><a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a></i> by approximately 500,000 people in <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-e18_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-e18-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Because the language is shared by communities speaking <a href="/wiki/Surinamese_Dutch" title="Surinamese Dutch">Dutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">Indigenous languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Javanese_language" title="Javanese language">Javanese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Hindustani#Sarnami_Hindustani" title="Caribbean Hindustani">Sarnami Hindustani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saramaccan_language" title="Saramaccan language">Saramaccan</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Chinese" title="Varieties of Chinese">varieties of Chinese</a>, most Surinamese speak it as a <i>lingua franca</i> both in Suriname, a former Dutch <a href="/wiki/Colony" title="Colony">colony</a>, and by immigrants of Surinamese origin in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sranan_Tongo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Surinamscina_Dezela_kozolcev.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Surinamscina_Dezela_kozolcev.jpg/220px-Surinamscina_Dezela_kozolcev.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Surinamscina_Dezela_kozolcev.jpg/330px-Surinamscina_Dezela_kozolcev.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/Surinamscina_Dezela_kozolcev.jpg/440px-Surinamscina_Dezela_kozolcev.jpg 2x" data-file-width="960" data-file-height="720" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Surinamscina_Dezela_kozolcev.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Message written in Sranan Tongo in the guestbook in the <a href="/wiki/Land_of_Hayracks" class="mw-redirect" title="Land of Hayracks">Land of Hayracks</a>, an open-air museum in <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">Slovenia</a> (April 2016)</div></div></div>
<p>The Sranan Tongo words for "to know" and "small children" are <i>sabi</i> and <i>pikin</i> (respectively from Portuguese <i><i lang="pt"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/saber#Portuguese" class="extiw" title="wikt:saber">saber</a></i></i> and <i><i lang="pt"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pequeno#Portuguese" class="extiw" title="wikt:pequeno">pequeno</a></i></i>), because the Portuguese were the first explorers of the West African coast, where they developed a pidgin language from which a few words became common in interactions with Africans by later explorers, including the English. However, research has established from its <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">lexicon</a> that Sranan Tongo is mostly an English-based creole language, with a substantial overlay of words from Dutch because of the Dutch takeover of Suriname in 1667.
</p><p>Sranan Tongo's lexicon is thus a fusion of mostly English<sup id="cite_ref-sherriah_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sherriah-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
and Dutch (+/-85%), plus some Portuguese and <a href="/wiki/West_African_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="West African languages">West African languages</a>. It began as a pidgin spoken primarily by African slaves in Suriname who often did not have a common African language. Sranan Tongo also became the language of communication between the slaves and the slaveowners, as the slaves were not permitted to learn or speak Dutch. As other ethnic groups were brought to Suriname as contract workers, Sranan Tongo became a <i>lingua franca</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_use">Modern use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sranan_Tongo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><div class="mediaContainer" style="width:220px"><audio id="mwe_player_0" controls="" preload="none" style="width:220px" class="kskin" data-durationhint="170.57378684807" data-startoffset="0" data-mwtitle="Sranan_Tongo_(Wikitongues).ogg" data-mwprovider="wikimediacommons"><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/91/Sranan_Tongo_%28Wikitongues%29.ogg" type="audio/ogg; codecs=&quot;vorbis&quot;" data-title="Original Ogg file (138 kbps)" data-shorttitle="Ogg source" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="137629" /><source src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/9/91/Sranan_Tongo_%28Wikitongues%29.ogg/Sranan_Tongo_%28Wikitongues%29.ogg.mp3" type="audio/mpeg" data-title="MP3" data-shorttitle="MP3" data-transcodekey="mp3" data-width="0" data-height="0" data-bandwidth="141360" /></audio></div>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sranan_Tongo_(Wikitongues).ogg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Spoken Sranan Tongo<br />(note: not a native speaker)</div></div></div>
<p>Although the formal Dutch-based educational system repressed Sranan Tongo, it gradually became more accepted by the establishment, especially during the 1980s when it was popularized by the dictator <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9si_Bouterse" title="Dési Bouterse">Dési Bouterse</a>, who often delivered national speeches in Sranan Tongo.
</p><p>Sranan Tongo remains widely used in Suriname and in large Dutch urban areas populated by immigrants from Suriname, especially in casual conversation, which often freely mixes it with Dutch. Written <a href="/wiki/Code-switching" title="Code-switching">code-switching</a> between Sranan Tongo and Dutch is also common in <a href="/wiki/Computer-mediated_communication" title="Computer-mediated communication">computer-mediated communication</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> 
People often greet each other in Sranan Tongo by saying, for example, <i>fa waka</i> (how are you), instead of the more formal Dutch <i>hoe gaat het</i> (how are you).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sranan_Tongo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a written language, Sranan Tongo has existed since the late 19th century. An official orthography was adopted by the government of Suriname on July 15, 1986 in Resolution 4501. A few writers have used Sranan in their work, most notably the poet <a href="/wiki/Henri_Frans_de_Ziel" title="Henri Frans de Ziel">Henri Frans de Ziel</a> ("Trefossa"), who also wrote <i><a href="/wiki/God_zij_met_ons_Suriname" title="God zij met ons Suriname">God zij met ons Suriname</a></i>, Suriname's <a href="/wiki/National_anthem" title="National anthem">national anthem</a>, whose second verse is sung in Sranan Tongo.
</p>
