<p><b>Thomas Phillip</b> "<b>Tip</b>" <b>O'Neill Jr. </b>(December 9, 1912&#160; – January 5, 1994) was an American politician who served as the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Speakers_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="List of Speakers of the United States House of Representatives">47th Speaker of the United States House of Representatives</a> from 1977 to 1987, representing northern <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston, Massachusetts</a>, as a <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrat</a> from 1953 to 1987. The only Speaker to serve for five complete consecutive Congresses, he is the third longest-serving Speaker in <a href="/wiki/United_States_History" class="mw-redirect" title="United States History">American history</a> after <a href="/wiki/Sam_Rayburn" title="Sam Rayburn">Sam Rayburn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clay" title="Henry Clay">Henry Clay</a> in terms of total tenure, and longest-serving in terms of continuous tenure (Rayburn and Clay having served multiple terms in the Speakership).
</p><p>Born in <a href="/wiki/North_Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="North Cambridge, Massachusetts">North Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>, O'Neill began campaigning at a young age, volunteering for <a href="/wiki/Al_Smith" title="Al Smith">Al Smith</a>'s campaign in the <a href="/wiki/1928_United_States_presidential_election" title="1928 United States presidential election">1928 presidential election</a>. After graduating from <a href="/wiki/Boston_College" title="Boston College">Boston College</a>, O'Neill won election to the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_House_of_Representatives" title="Massachusetts House of Representatives">Massachusetts House of Representatives</a>, where he became a strong advocate of <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>'s <a href="/wiki/New_Deal" title="New Deal">New Deal</a> policies. He became Speaker of the Massachusetts House of Representatives in 1949 and won election to the United States House of Representatives in 1952 to the seat vacated by <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>.
</p><p>In the House, O'Neill became a protege of fellow Massachusetts Representative <a href="/wiki/John_William_McCormack" class="mw-redirect" title="John William McCormack">John William McCormack</a>. O'Neill broke with President <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> in 1967, and called for <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>'s resignation in light of the <a href="/wiki/Watergate_scandal" title="Watergate scandal">Watergate scandal</a>. He quickly moved up the leadership ranks in the 1970s, becoming House Majority Whip in 1971, House Majority Leader in 1973, and Speaker of the House in 1977. With the election of President <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a>, O'Neill hoped to establish a <a href="/wiki/Universal_health_care" title="Universal health care">universal health care system</a> and a guaranteed jobs program. However, relations between Carter and Congress collapsed and Democrats lost control of the presidency in the <a href="/wiki/1980_United_States_presidential_election" title="1980 United States presidential election">1980 presidential election</a>. O'Neill became a leading opponent of <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> President <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a>'s conservative domestic policies. O'Neill and Reagan found more common ground in foreign policy, fostering the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Agreement" title="Anglo-Irish Agreement">Anglo-Irish Agreement</a> and implementing the <a href="/wiki/Reagan_Doctrine" title="Reagan Doctrine">Reagan Doctrine</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet%E2%80%93Afghan_War" title="Soviet–Afghan War">Soviet–Afghan War</a>.
</p><p>O'Neill retired from Congress in 1987, but remained active in public life. He published a best-selling autobiography and appeared in several commercials and other media. He died of cardiac arrest in 1994.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tip_O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>O'Neill was the third of three children born to Thomas Phillip O'Neill Sr. and Rose Ann (née Tolan) O'Neill in the <a href="/wiki/Irish-Americans" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish-Americans">Irish</a> middle-class area of <a href="/wiki/North_Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="North Cambridge, Massachusetts">North Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>, known at the time as "Old Dublin." His mother died when he was nine months old, and he was raised largely by a French-Canadian housekeeper until his father remarried when he was eight. O'Neill Sr. started out as a bricklayer, but later won a seat on the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_City_Council,_Massachusetts#Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Cambridge City Council, Massachusetts">Cambridge City Council</a> and was appointed Superintendent of Sewers. During his childhood, O'Neill received the nickname "Tip" after the <a href="/wiki/Canadian" class="mw-redirect" title="Canadian">Canadian</a> baseball player <a href="/wiki/Tip_O%27Neill_(baseball)" title="Tip O&#39;Neill (baseball)">James "Tip" O'Neill</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He was educated in <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> schools, graduating in 1931 from the now defunct St. John High School in Cambridge, where he was captain of the basketball team; he was a lifelong parishioner at the school's affiliated parish church <a href="/wiki/St._John_the_Evangelist_Church_(Cambridge,_Massachusetts)" title="St. John the Evangelist Church (Cambridge, Massachusetts)">St. John the Evangelist Church</a>. From there he went to <a href="/wiki/Boston_College" title="Boston College">Boston College</a>, from which he graduated in 1936.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Entry_into_politics">Entry into politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tip_O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Entry into politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>O'Neill first became active in politics at 15, campaigning for <a href="/wiki/Al_Smith" title="Al Smith">Al Smith</a> in his <a href="/wiki/U.S._presidential_election,_1928" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. presidential election, 1928">1928 presidential campaign</a>. Four years later, he helped campaign for <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Delano_Roosevelt" class="mw-redirect" title="Franklin Delano Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>. As a senior at <a href="/wiki/Boston_College" title="Boston College">Boston College</a>, O'Neill ran for a seat on the Cambridge City Council, but lost; his first race and only electoral defeat. The campaign taught him the lesson that became his best-known quote: "<a href="/wiki/All_politics_is_local" title="All politics is local">All politics is local</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After graduating in 1936, O'Neill was elected at the age of 24 to the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_House_of_Representatives" title="Massachusetts House of Representatives">Massachusetts House of Representatives</a>, aided by tough economic times among his constituents; the experience made him a strong advocate of the <a href="/wiki/New_Deal" title="New Deal">New Deal</a> policies of Roosevelt, which were just then coming to an end. His biographer <a href="/wiki/John_Aloysius_Farrell" class="mw-redirect" title="John Aloysius Farrell">John Aloysius Farrell</a> said his background in <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression_in_the_United_States" title="Great Depression in the United States">Depression-era</a> working-class Boston, and his interpretation of his Catholic faith, led O'Neill to view the role of government as intervening to cure social ailments. O'Neill was "an absolute, unrepentant, unreconstructed New Deal Democrat," Farrell wrote.<sup id="cite_ref-last_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-last-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1949, he became the first Democratic <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_Massachusetts_House_of_Representatives" class="mw-redirect" title="Speaker of the Massachusetts House of Representatives">Speaker of the Massachusetts House of Representatives</a> in its history. He remained in that post until 1952, when he ran for the United States House of Representatives from his home district.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._House_of_Representatives">U.S. House of Representatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tip_O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: U.S. House of Representatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Calvert_Woodley.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/The_Calvert_Woodley.JPG/220px-The_Calvert_Woodley.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/The_Calvert_Woodley.JPG/330px-The_Calvert_Woodley.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/The_Calvert_Woodley.JPG/440px-The_Calvert_Woodley.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2382" data-file-height="1703" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Calvert_Woodley.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>O'Neill's Washington, D.C. residence from 1964 to 1978</div></div></div>
<p>O'Neill was elected to the congressional seat vacated by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senator" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Senator">Senator-elect</a> <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> in 1952. He would be reelected 16 more times, never facing serious opposition.  His district, centered around the northern half of Boston, was originally numbered as the 11th District, but became the 8th District in 1963.
</p><p>During his second term in the House, O'Neill was selected to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_Committee_on_Rules" title="United States House Committee on Rules">House Rules Committee</a> where he proved a crucial asset for the Democratic leadership, particularly his mentor, fellow Boston congressman and later Speaker, <a href="/wiki/John_William_McCormack" class="mw-redirect" title="John William McCormack">John William McCormack</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After wrestling with the issues surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, in 1967 O'Neill broke with President <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a> and came out in opposition to America's involvement.<sup id="cite_ref-last_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-last-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> O'Neill wrote in his autobiography that he also became convinced that the conflict in Vietnam was a civil war and that US involvement was morally wrong. While the decision cost O'Neill some support among older voters in his home district, he benefited from new support among students and faculty members at the many colleges and universities there. In the House of Representatives itself, O'Neill picked up the trust and support of younger House members who shared his antiwar views, and they became important friends who contributed to O'Neill's rise through the ranks in the House.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1971, O'Neill was appointed <a href="/wiki/Party_whips_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives" class="mw-redirect" title="Party whips of the United States House of Representatives">Majority Whip</a> in the House, the number three position for the Democratic Party in the House. Two years later, in 1973, he was elected <a href="/wiki/Party_leaders_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="Party leaders of the United States House of Representatives">House Majority Leader</a>, following the disappearance of a small plane carrying Majority Leader <a href="/wiki/Hale_Boggs" title="Hale Boggs">Hale Boggs</a> and Congressman <a href="/wiki/Nick_Begich" title="Nick Begich">Nick Begich</a> in Alaska. As Majority Leader, O'Neill was the most prominent Democrat in the House to call for the <a href="/wiki/Impeachment" title="Impeachment">impeachment</a> of President <a href="/wiki/Richard_M._Nixon" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard M. Nixon">Richard M. Nixon</a> in light of the <a href="/wiki/Watergate_scandal" title="Watergate scandal">Watergate scandal</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Speaker_of_the_House">Speaker of the House</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tip_O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Speaker of the House">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gerald_Ford_and_Tip_O%27Neill_(cropped)_-_USNWR.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Gerald_Ford_and_Tip_O%27Neill_%28cropped%29_-_USNWR.jpg/220px-Gerald_Ford_and_Tip_O%27Neill_%28cropped%29_-_USNWR.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="204" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Gerald_Ford_and_Tip_O%27Neill_%28cropped%29_-_USNWR.jpg/330px-Gerald_Ford_and_Tip_O%27Neill_%28cropped%29_-_USNWR.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Gerald_Ford_and_Tip_O%27Neill_%28cropped%29_-_USNWR.jpg/440px-Gerald_Ford_and_Tip_O%27Neill_%28cropped%29_-_USNWR.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2709" data-file-height="2513" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gerald_Ford_and_Tip_O%27Neill_(cropped)_-_USNWR.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>O'Neill with President <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Gerald Ford</a>, 1976</div></div></div>
<p>As a result of the <a href="/wiki/Tongsun_Park" title="Tongsun Park">Tongsun Park</a> influence-peddling scandal, <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="Speaker of the United States House of Representatives">House Speaker</a> <a href="/wiki/Carl_Albert" title="Carl Albert">Carl Albert</a> retired from Congress and O'Neill was elected Speaker in 1977, the same year <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">Jimmy Carter</a> became <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carter_administration">Carter administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tip_O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Carter administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With substantial majorities in both houses of Congress and control of the White House, O'Neill hoped that Democrats would be able to implement Democratic-favored legislation, including <a href="/wiki/Universal_health_care" title="Universal health care">universal health care</a> and guaranteed jobs programs. The Democrats, however, lacked <a href="/wiki/Party_discipline" title="Party discipline">party discipline</a>, and while the Carter administration and O'Neill started out strong with the passage of ethics and energy packages in 1977, there were major stumbles. Troubles began with Carter's threats to <a href="/wiki/Presidential_veto" class="mw-redirect" title="Presidential veto">veto</a> a water projects bill, a pet project of many members of Congress. O'Neill and other Democratic leaders were also upset by Carter's appointments of a number of his fellow Georgians, whom O'Neill considered arrogant and parochial, to federal offices and White House staff.
</p><p>O'Neill was also put off by Carter's frugal behavior in cutting executive staff and reducing the scale of White House entertaining. Carter, a Southern Baptist, even ended the practice of serving <a href="/wiki/Alcohol_(drug)" title="Alcohol (drug)">alcohol</a> other than beer and wine at the White House. As Carter's term began in early 1977, Democratic leaders on <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Hill" title="Capitol Hill">Capitol Hill</a> were invited to the White House for a breakfast with the new President, where Carter served them sugar cookies and coffee. O'Neill, a man of expansive appetite, expected the until-then-traditional eggs and sausage. He looked across the table at Carter and said, "Mr. President... you know, we won the election." Carter was a reform-minded executive who often clashed with O'Neill on legislation. The Speaker wanted to reward loyal Democrats with rewarding projects at a time when Carter wanted to reduce government spending. A continuing weak economy and the <a href="/wiki/Iran_hostage_crisis" title="Iran hostage crisis">Iran hostage crisis</a> made prospects bleak for Carter and the Democrats in the 1980 <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives_elections,_1980" class="mw-redirect" title="United States House of Representatives elections, 1980">congressional</a> and <a href="/wiki/1980_United_States_presidential_election" title="1980 United States presidential election">presidential</a> elections.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reagan_administration">Reagan administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tip_O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reagan administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>O'Neill was a leading opponent of the <a href="/wiki/Reagan_administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Reagan administration">Reagan administration</a>'s domestic and defense policies. Following the 1980 election, with the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Senate" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Senate">U.S. Senate</a> controlled by Republicans, O'Neill became the leader of the congressional opposition. O'Neill even went as far as calling <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Reagan" title="Ronald Reagan">Ronald Reagan</a> "the most ignorant man who had ever occupied the White House".<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> O'Neill also said that Reagan was "<a href="/wiki/Herbert_Hoover" title="Herbert Hoover">Herbert Hoover</a> with a smile" and "a cheerleader for selfishness." He also said that Reagan's policies meant that his presidency was "one big Christmas party for the rich." Privately, O'Neill and Reagan were always on cordial terms, or as Reagan himself put it in his memoirs, they were friends "after 6PM". O'Neill in that same memoir when questioned by Reagan regarding a personal attack against the President that made the paper, explained that "before 6PM it's all politics".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Reagan once compared O'Neill to the classic arcade game <i><a href="/wiki/Pac-Man" title="Pac-Man">Pac-Man</a></i> in a speech, saying that he was "a round thing that gobbles up money". He also once joked he had received a <a href="/wiki/Valentine%27s_Day" title="Valentine&#39;s Day">valentine card</a> from O'Neill: "I knew it was from Tip, because the <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bleeding-heart" class="extiw" title="wikt:bleeding-heart">heart was bleeding</a>."
</p><p>O'Neill, however, gave tacit approval to Democratic Congressman <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Nesbitt_Wilson" class="mw-redirect" title="Charlie Nesbitt Wilson">Charlie Nesbitt Wilson</a> to implement the <a href="/wiki/Reagan_Doctrine" title="Reagan Doctrine">Reagan Doctrine</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Soviet-Afghan_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Soviet-Afghan war">Soviet-Afghan war</a>. Wilson's position on the appropriations committees, and his close relations with CIA officer <a href="/wiki/Gust_Avrakotos" title="Gust Avrakotos">Gust Avrakotos</a>, allowed him to steer billions of dollars to the <a href="/wiki/Mujahideen" title="Mujahideen">Mujahideen</a> through the CIA and <a href="/wiki/Zia_ul-Haq" class="mw-redirect" title="Zia ul-Haq">Zia ul-Haq</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Inter_Services_Intelligence" class="mw-redirect" title="Inter Services Intelligence">ISI</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-crile_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crile-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There was some contention about <a href="/wiki/Amendment_XXV_of_the_United_States_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="Amendment XXV of the United States Constitution">constitutional order of succession</a>, which involved O'Neill, when <a href="/wiki/Attempted_assassination_of_Ronald_Reagan" title="Attempted assassination of Ronald Reagan">Reagan was shot</a> in March 1981. Then-Secretary of State <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Haig" title="Alexander Haig">Alexander Haig</a> famously said he was "in control here," in response to a question as to who was in charge (with the president under anesthesia and Vice President <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">George Bush</a> traveling), but it was later pointed out that O'Neill was next in line after Bush. Reagan was only under for a few hours, and no formal invocation of the line of succession took place.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland">Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tip_O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of O'Neill's greatest accomplishments as Speaker involved <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. O'Neill worked with fellow Irish-American politicians <a href="/wiki/New_York_Governor" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Governor">New York Governor</a> <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Carey" title="Hugh Carey">Hugh Carey</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/Edward_Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward Kennedy">Edward&#160;Kennedy</a>, and Senator <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Patrick_Moynihan" title="Daniel Patrick Moynihan">Daniel Patrick Moynihan</a> to craft a peace accord between the warring factions. Beginning with the "<a href="/wiki/Saint_Patrick%27s_Day" title="Saint Patrick&#39;s Day">Saint Patrick's Day</a> declaration" in 1977, denouncing violence in Northern Ireland and culminating with the Irish aid package upon the signing of the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Agreement" title="Anglo-Irish Agreement">Anglo-Irish Agreement</a> in 1985, the "Four Horsemen" as they were called, convinced both Carter and Reagan to press the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> government on the subject.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In 1981, O'Neill also created the <a href="/wiki/Friends_of_Ireland_(U.S._Congress)" title="Friends of Ireland (U.S. Congress)">Friends of Ireland</a> with Kennedy and Moynihan, an organization to promote peace in Northern Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>O'Neill was made an <a href="/wiki/Irish_nationality_law#By_grant_of_honorary_citizenship" title="Irish nationality law">honorary Irish citizen</a> (an honor granting legally full citizenship) by the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-dail_debate_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dail_debate-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-speakership">Post-speakership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tip_O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Post-speakership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:227px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pelosi_O%27Neill.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Pelosi_O%27Neill.jpg/225px-Pelosi_O%27Neill.jpg" decoding="async" width="225" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Pelosi_O%27Neill.jpg/338px-Pelosi_O%27Neill.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Pelosi_O%27Neill.jpg/450px-Pelosi_O%27Neill.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1259" data-file-height="968" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pelosi_O%27Neill.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>O'Neill with Congresswoman (and future two-time Speaker) <a href="/wiki/Nancy_Pelosi" title="Nancy Pelosi">Nancy Pelosi</a></div></div></div>
<p>After retiring from Congress in 1987, O'Neill published his autobiography, <i>Man of the House</i>. It was well received by critics, and became a national best-seller. The book also helped turn the former Speaker into a national icon, and O'Neill starred in a number of commercials, including ones for <a href="/wiki/Trump_Shuttle" title="Trump Shuttle">Trump Shuttle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Commodore_International" title="Commodore International">Commodore Computers</a>, Quality International Budget Hotels, and one for <a href="/wiki/Miller_Lite" title="Miller Lite">Miller Lite</a>, starring in one with <a href="/wiki/Bob_Uecker" title="Bob Uecker">Bob Uecker</a>.
</p><p>In 1987 he received the <a href="/wiki/Four_Freedoms_Award#Freedom_Medal" title="Four Freedoms Award">Freedom Medal</a>.
</p><p>On November 18, 1991, O'Neill was presented with the <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Medal_of_Freedom" title="Presidential Medal of Freedom">Presidential Medal of Freedom</a> by President <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">George H. W. Bush</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Later on in retirement, O'Neill, who suffered from <a href="/wiki/Colon_cancer" class="mw-redirect" title="Colon cancer">colon cancer</a>, made public service advertisements about cancer in which he joined athletes and movie stars in talking candidly about having the disease.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tip_O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>O'Neill's emergence as a cultural figure was not restricted to commercials. Four years before his retirement, he had a cameo role in the February 17, 1983, episode of <i><a href="/wiki/Cheers" title="Cheers">Cheers</a></i> entitled "No Contest", which featured him ducking into the bar to escape Diane Chambers who pestered him on the street about his political ideals.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The show, which was ranked 60th in the <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_ratings" title="Nielsen ratings">Nielsen ratings</a> at that time, jumped 20 places the following week. He appeared in an  episode of the <a href="/wiki/NBC" title="NBC">NBC</a> sitcom <i><a href="/wiki/Silver_Spoons" title="Silver Spoons">Silver Spoons</a></i>, which featured him delivering a mock press conference praising recurring character Freddy Lippincottleman's efforts on behalf of the homeless.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> O'Neill also made a brief appearance in the 1993 film <i><a href="/wiki/Dave_(film)" title="Dave (film)">Dave</a></i> as himself, assessing the work of the fictional American President in the movie. He also did narration for a segment of the <a href="/wiki/Ken_Burns" title="Ken Burns">Ken Burns</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/Baseball_(TV_series)" title="Baseball (TV series)">Baseball</a></i> in which O'Neill, a lifelong <a href="/wiki/Red_Sox" class="mw-redirect" title="Red Sox">Red Sox</a> fan, read <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i> from the day the Red Sox won the 1918 <a href="/wiki/World_Series" title="World Series">World Series</a>.
</p><p>In the comic strip <i><a href="/wiki/Shoe_(comic_strip)" title="Shoe (comic strip)">Shoe</a></i>, the character Sen. Batson D. Belfry is modelled after O'Neill.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tip_O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>O'Neill resided on 26 Russell Street in North Cambridge, although he had lived as a child around the corner on Orchard St.. He had a vacation home on Woodland Rd. near Bank Street Beach in <a href="/wiki/Harwich_Port,_Massachusetts" title="Harwich Port, Massachusetts">Harwich Port, Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> His wife was Mildred "Millie" Anne Miller (1914–2003). They had five children. His oldest son and namesake, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_P._O%27Neill_III" title="Thomas P. O&#39;Neill III">Thomas P. O'Neill III</a>, a former <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Governor_of_Massachusetts" title="Lieutenant Governor of Massachusetts">Lieutenant Governor of Massachusetts</a>, works in public relations in Boston. Another son, Christopher, is a <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington</a> lawyer. His third son, Michael, is deceased; daughter Susan has a business in Washington DC, and  the other daughter, Rosemary, is a political officer for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State" title="United States Department of State">State Department</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_and_legacy">Death and legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tip_O%27Neill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Death and legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Philip_O%27Neill_Jr%27s_grave.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Thomas_Philip_O%27Neill_Jr%27s_grave.jpg/220px-Thomas_Philip_O%27Neill_Jr%27s_grave.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Thomas_Philip_O%27Neill_Jr%27s_grave.jpg/330px-Thomas_Philip_O%27Neill_Jr%27s_grave.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Thomas_Philip_O%27Neill_Jr%27s_grave.jpg/440px-Thomas_Philip_O%27Neill_Jr%27s_grave.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5184" data-file-height="3456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Thomas_Philip_O%27Neill_Jr%27s_grave.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>O'Neill's <a href="/wiki/Cenotaph" title="Cenotaph">cenotaph</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Cemetery" title="Congressional Cemetery">Congressional Cemetery</a>, Washington D.C.</div></div></div>
<p>O'Neill died of <a href="/wiki/Cardiac_arrest" title="Cardiac arrest">cardiac arrest</a> on January 5, 1994, survived by his wife and their children. At his passing, President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> said: "Tip O'Neill was the nation's most prominent, powerful and loyal champion of working people... He loved politics and government because he saw that politics and government could make a difference in people's lives. And he loved people most of all." Millie, who never remarried after his death, died in 2003 and is buried near her husband, at Mount Pleasant Cemetery in <a href="/wiki/Harwich_Port,_Massachusetts" title="Harwich Port, Massachusetts">Harwich Port, Massachusetts</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Thomas_P._O%27Neill_Jr._Tunnel" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas P. O&#39;Neill Jr. Tunnel">Thomas P. O'Neill Jr. Tunnel</a>, built through downtown <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston</a> as part of the <a href="/wiki/Big_Dig_(Boston,_Massachusetts)" class="mw-redirect" title="Big Dig (Boston, Massachusetts)">Big Dig</a> to carry <a href="/wiki/Interstate_93" title="Interstate 93">Interstate 93</a> under Boston, was named after him. Other structures named after him include a <a href="/wiki/O%27Neill_House_Office_Building" title="O&#39;Neill House Office Building">House Office Building</a> (now demolished), the <a href="/w/index.php?title=O%27Neill_Branch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="O&#39;Neill Branch (page does not exist)">O'Neill Branch</a> of the Cambridge Public Library (including an outdoor mural<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>), the <a href="/wiki/Thomas_P._O%27Neill_Jr._Federal_Building" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas P. O&#39;Neill Jr. Federal Building">Thomas P. O'Neill Jr. Federal Building</a> in Boston, a <a href="/wiki/Thomas_P._O%27Neill_Jr._Federal_Building_(Washington,_D.C.)" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas P. O&#39;Neill Jr. Federal Building (Washington, D.C.)">federal office building</a> in Washington DC,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> a golf course in Cambridge, and the main library (and the plaza in front of it) at his alma mater, <a href="/wiki/Boston_College" title="Boston College">Boston College</a>.
</p><p>On June 22, 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Play_(theatre)" title="Play (theatre)">play</a> <i>According to Tip</i> debuted in <a href="/wiki/Watertown,_Massachusetts" title="Watertown, Massachusetts">Watertown, Massachusetts</a>, produced by the <a href="/wiki/New_Repertory_Theatre" title="New Repertory Theatre">New Repertory Theatre</a>. The one-man <a href="/wiki/Biography" title="Biography">biographical</a> play, written by longtime Boston sportswriter <a href="/wiki/Dick_Flavin_(poet)" title="Dick Flavin (poet)">Dick Flavin</a>, features O'Neill telling stories of his life, from his childhood to after his retirement in politics. <a href="/wiki/Tony_Award" title="Tony Award">Tony Award</a> winner <a href="/wiki/Ken_Howard" title="Ken Howard">Ken Howard</a> played the title role in the premiere production.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2012, the <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_Presidential_Library_and_Museum" title="John F. Kennedy Presidential Library and Museum">John F. Kennedy Presidential Library and Museum</a> hosted a forum to celebrate the <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/centennial" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:centennial">centennial</a> of O'Neill's birth.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> O'Neill himself contributed several oral history interviews to its archives chronicling his work for the Democratic Party and friendship with President Kennedy.
</p>
