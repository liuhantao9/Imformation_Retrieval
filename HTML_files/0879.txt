<p><i><b>Encyclopedia Americana</b></i> is one of the largest general <a href="/wiki/Encyclopedia" title="Encyclopedia">encyclopedias</a> in the English language. Following the acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Grolier" title="Grolier">Grolier</a> in 2000, the encyclopedia has been produced by <a href="/wiki/Scholastic_Corporation" title="Scholastic Corporation">Scholastic</a>.
</p><p>The encyclopedia has more than 45,000 articles, most of them more than 500 words and many running to considerable length (the "United States" article is over 300,000 words). The work's coverage of American and Canadian geography and history has been a traditional strength. Written by 6,500 contributors, the <i>Encyclopedia Americana</i> includes over 9,000 bibliographies, 150,000 cross-references, 1,000+ tables, 1,200 maps, and almost 4,500 black-and-white line art and color images. It also has 680 factboxes. Most articles are signed by their contributors.
</p><p>Long available as a 30-volume print set, the <i>Encyclopedia Americana</i> is now marketed as an online encyclopedia requiring a subscription. In March 2008, <a href="/wiki/Scholastic_Corporation" title="Scholastic Corporation">Scholastic</a> said that print sales remained good but that the company was still deciding on the future of the print edition.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The company did not produce an edition in 2007, a change from its previous approach of releasing a revised print edition each year. The most recent print edition of the <i>Encyclopedia Americana</i> was published in 2006.
</p><p>The online version of the <i>Encyclopedia Americana</i>, first introduced in 1997, continues to be updated and sold. This work, like the print set from which it is derived, is designed for high school and first-year college students along with public library users. It is available to libraries as one of the options in the Grolier Online reference service, which also includes the <i><a href="/wiki/Grolier_Multimedia_Encyclopedia" class="mw-redirect" title="Grolier Multimedia Encyclopedia">Grolier Multimedia Encyclopedia</a></i>, intended for middle and high school students, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Book_of_Knowledge" title="The New Book of Knowledge">The New Book of Knowledge</a></i>, an encyclopedia for elementary and middle school students. Grolier Online is not available to individual subscribers.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_predecessors">History and predecessors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Encyclopedia_Americana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and predecessors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1921EncycAmericanAd.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/1921EncycAmericanAd.jpg/200px-1921EncycAmericanAd.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="291" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/1921EncycAmericanAd.jpg/300px-1921EncycAmericanAd.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/1921EncycAmericanAd.jpg/400px-1921EncycAmericanAd.jpg 2x" data-file-width="894" data-file-height="1302" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1921EncycAmericanAd.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This 1921 advertisement for the <i>Encyclopedia Americana</i> suggests that other encyclopedias are as out-of-date as the locomotives of 90 years earlier.</div></div></div>
<p>There have been three separate works using the title <i>Encyclopedia Americana</i>.
</p><p>The first began publishing in the 1820s by the German exile <a href="/wiki/Francis_Lieber" title="Francis Lieber">Francis Lieber</a>. The 13 volumes of the first edition were completed in 1833, and other editions and printings followed in 1835, 1836, 1847-1848, 1849 and 1858. Lieber's work was based upon and was in no small part a translation of the 7th edition of the well established <a href="/wiki/Brockhaus_Enzyklop%C3%A4die" title="Brockhaus Enzyklopädie"><i>Konversations-Lexikon</i></a> of <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Arnold_Brockhaus" title="Friedrich Arnold Brockhaus">Brockhaus</a>. Some material from this set was carried over into the modern version, as well as the Brockhaus short article method.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A separate <i>Encyclopedia Americana</i> was published by <a href="/wiki/Stoddart" title="Stoddart">J.M. Stoddart</a> between 1883 and 1889, as a supplement to American reprintings of the 9th edition of the <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica" title="Encyclopædia Britannica">Encyclopædia Britannica</a></i>. It was four <a href="/wiki/Quarto" title="Quarto">quarto</a> volumes meant to "extend and complete the articles in <i>Britannica</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Stoddart's work, however, is not connected to the earlier work by Lieber.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1902 a new version in 16 volumes was published under the title <i>Encyclopedia Americana,</i> under the editorial supervision of <i><a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">Scientific American</a></i> magazine. The magazine's editor, <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Converse_Beach" title="Frederick Converse Beach">Frederick Converse Beach</a>, was editor-in-chief, and was said to be assisted by hundreds of eminent scholars and authorities who served as consulting editors or authors. The first publisher was <a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_S._Peale&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Richard S. Peale (page does not exist)">R.S. Peale &amp; Co</a>; between 1903 and 1906 further editions were issued by the Americana Corp. and the Scientific American Compiling Department, with <a href="/wiki/George_Edwin_Rines" title="George Edwin Rines">George Edwin Rines</a> appointed managing editor in 1903.<sup id="cite_ref-americana_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-americana-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The relationship with <i>Scientific American</i> was terminated in 1911.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> From 1907 to 1912, the work was published as <i>The Americana.</i>
</p><p>A major new edition appeared in 1918–20 in 30 volumes, with George Edwin Rines as editor-in-chief.<sup id="cite_ref-americana_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-americana-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> An <i>Annual</i> or <i>Yearbook</i> was also published each year beginning in 1923 and continuing until 2000.
</p><p>The encyclopedia was purchased by <a href="/wiki/Grolier" title="Grolier">Grolier</a> in 1945. By the 1960s, sales of the <i>Americana</i> and its sister publications under <a href="/wiki/Grolier" title="Grolier">Grolier</a>—<i><a href="/wiki/The_Book_of_Knowledge" class="mw-redirect" title="The Book of Knowledge">The Book of Knowledge</a></i>, the <i>Book of Popular Science,</i> and <i>Lands and Peoples</i>—were strong enough to support the company's occupancy of a large building (variously named the Americana Building and the Grolier Building) in Midtown <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>, at 575 <a href="/wiki/Lexington_Avenue" title="Lexington Avenue">Lexington Avenue</a>. Sales during this period were accomplished primarily through <a href="/wiki/Mail-order" class="mw-redirect" title="Mail-order">mail-order</a> and <a href="/wiki/Door-to-door" title="Door-to-door">door-to-door</a> operations. <a href="/wiki/Telemarketing" title="Telemarketing">Telemarketing</a> and third-party distribution through their Lexicon division added to sales volumes in the 1970s. By the late 1970s, Grolier had moved its operations to <a href="/wiki/Danbury,_Connecticut" title="Danbury, Connecticut">Danbury, Connecticut</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_developments">Later developments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Encyclopedia_Americana&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Later developments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1988 <a href="/wiki/Grolier" title="Grolier">Grolier</a> was purchased by the French media company <a href="/wiki/Hachette_(publishing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hachette (publishing)">Hachette</a>, which owned a well-known French-language encyclopedia, the <i>Hachette Encyclopedia</i>. Hachette was later absorbed by the French conglomerate the <a href="/wiki/Lagard%C3%A8re_Group" title="Lagardère Group">Lagardère Group</a>.
</p><p>A CD-ROM version of the encyclopedia was published in 1995. Although the text and images were stored on separate disks, it was in keeping with standards current at the time. More importantly, the work had been digitized, allowing for release of an online version in 1997. Over the next few years the product was augmented with additional features, functions, supplementary references, Internet links, and current events journal. A redesigned interface and partly reengineered product, featuring enhanced search capabilities and a first-ever <a href="/wiki/Americans_with_Disabilities_Act_of_1990" title="Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990">ADA</a>-compliant, text-only version for users with disabilities, was presented in 2002.
</p><p>The acquisition of <a href="/wiki/Grolier" title="Grolier">Grolier</a> by <a href="/wiki/Scholastic_Corporation" title="Scholastic Corporation">Scholastic</a> for US$400 million, took place in 2000. The new owners projected a 30% increase in operating income, although historically <a href="/wiki/Grolier" title="Grolier">Grolier</a> had experienced earnings of 7% to 8% on income.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Staff reductions as a means of controlling costs followed soon thereafter, even while an effort was made to augment the sales force. Cuts occurred every year between 2000 and 2007, leaving a much-depleted work force to carry out the duties of maintaining a large encyclopedia database.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Today, <i>Encyclopedia Americana</i> lives on as an integral database within the Grolier Online product.
</p>
