	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Operation Northwoods</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">1962 proposed US false flag operation against the Cuban government</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:187px;"><a href="/wiki/File:NorthwoodsMemorandum.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/NorthwoodsMemorandum.jpg/185px-NorthwoodsMemorandum.jpg" decoding="async" width="185" height="251" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/NorthwoodsMemorandum.jpg/278px-NorthwoodsMemorandum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/NorthwoodsMemorandum.jpg/370px-NorthwoodsMemorandum.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1206" data-file-height="1636" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:NorthwoodsMemorandum.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Operation Northwoods memorandum (13 March 1962)<sup id="cite_ref-northwoods1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-northwoods1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:187px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lyman_L._Lemnitzer.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Lyman_L._Lemnitzer.jpg/185px-Lyman_L._Lemnitzer.jpg" decoding="async" width="185" height="242" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Lyman_L._Lemnitzer.jpg/278px-Lyman_L._Lemnitzer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Lyman_L._Lemnitzer.jpg/370px-Lyman_L._Lemnitzer.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1448" data-file-height="1896" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lyman_L._Lemnitzer.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lyman L. Lemnitzer, who was in charge as the Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff</div></div></div>
<p><b>Operation Northwoods</b> was a proposed <a href="/wiki/False_flag" title="False flag">false flag</a> operation against the Cuban government that originated within the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Defense" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Department of Defense">U.S. Department of Defense</a> (DoD) and the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Chiefs_of_Staff" title="Joint Chiefs of Staff">Joint Chiefs of Staff</a> (JCS) of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_government" class="mw-redirect" title="United States government">United States government</a> in 1962.  The proposals called for the <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">Central Intelligence Agency</a> (CIA) or other <a href="/wiki/Federal_Government_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Government of the United States">U.S. government</a> operatives to commit acts of terrorism against American civilians and military targets, blaming them on the Cuban government, and using it to justify a war against Cuba. The possibilities detailed in the document included the possible assassination of Cuban <a href="/wiki/%C3%89migr%C3%A9" title="Émigré">émigrés</a>, sinking boats of Cuban refugees on the high seas, hijacking planes, blowing up a U.S. ship, and orchestrating violent terrorism in U.S. cities.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The proposals were rejected by <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-feinsilber1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-feinsilber1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Communists led by <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a> had taken power in Cuba in 1959, which aroused the concern of the U.S. military due to the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>.  The operation proposed creating public support for a war against Cuba by blaming it for terrorist acts that would actually be perpetrated by the U.S. Government.<sup id="cite_ref-northwoods1_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-northwoods1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  To this end, Operation Northwoods proposals recommended hijackings and <a href="/wiki/Bomb" title="Bomb">bombings</a> followed by the introduction of phony evidence that would implicate the Cuban government. It stated:
</p>
<blockquote><p>The desired resultant from the execution of this plan would be to place the United States in the apparent position of suffering defensible grievances from a rash and irresponsible government of Cuba and to develop an international image of a Cuban threat to peace in the Western Hemisphere.</p></blockquote>
<p>Several other proposals were included within Operation Northwoods, including real or simulated actions against various <a href="/wiki/U.S._military" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. military">U.S. military</a> and civilian targets. The operation recommended developing a "Communist Cuban terror campaign in the Miami area, in other Florida cities and even in Washington".
</p><p>The plan was drafted by the Joint Chiefs of Staff, signed by Chairman <a href="/wiki/Lyman_Lemnitzer" title="Lyman Lemnitzer">Lyman Lemnitzer</a> and sent to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">Secretary of Defense</a>. Although part of the U.S. government's anti-communist <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Project" title="Cuban Project">Cuban Project</a>, Operation Northwoods was never officially accepted; it was authorized by the Joint Chiefs of Staff, but then rejected by President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>. According to currently released documentation, none of the operations became active under the auspices of the Operation Northwoods proposals.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_public_release">Origins and public release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Northwoods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins and public release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main proposal was presented in a document titled "Justification for <a href="/wiki/U.S._military_intervention" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. military intervention">U.S.&#160;Military Intervention</a> in Cuba (TS)," a top secret collection of draft <a href="/wiki/Memoranda" class="mw-redirect" title="Memoranda">memoranda</a> written by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a> (DoD) and the Joint Chiefs of Staff (JCS).<sup id="cite_ref-northwoods1_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-northwoods1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  The document was presented by the Joint Chiefs of Staff to Secretary of Defense <a href="/wiki/Robert_McNamara" title="Robert McNamara">Robert McNamara</a> on 13 March 1962 as a preliminary submission for planning purposes. The Joint Chiefs of Staff recommended that both the covert and overt aspects of any such operation be assigned to them.
</p><p>The previously <a href="/wiki/Secret_Documents" class="mw-redirect" title="Secret Documents">secret document</a> was originally made public on 18 November 1997, by the <a href="/wiki/Assassination_Records_Review_Board" class="mw-redirect" title="Assassination Records Review Board">John F. Kennedy Assassination Records Review Board</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> a U.S. federal agency overseeing the release of government records related to <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_assassination" class="mw-redirect" title="John F. Kennedy assassination">John F. Kennedy's assassination</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-weiner_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weiner-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> A total of 1,521 pages of once-secret military records covering 1962 to 1964 were concomitantly <a href="/wiki/Declassification" title="Declassification">declassified</a> by said Review Board.
</p><p>"Appendix to Enclosure A" and "Annex to Appendix to Enclosure A" of the Northwoods document were first published online by the <a href="/wiki/National_Security_Archive" title="National Security Archive">National Security Archive</a> on 6 November 1998 in a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a> as part of its 1998 <i>Cold War</i> television documentary series<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>—specifically, as a documentation supplement to "Episode 10: Cuba," which aired on 29 November 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> "Annex to Appendix to Enclosure A" is the section of the document which contains the proposals to stage <a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">terrorist attacks</a>.
</p><p>The Northwoods document was published online in a more complete form, including cover <a href="/wiki/Memorandum" title="Memorandum">memoranda</a>, by the National Security Archive on 30 April 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Content">Content</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Northwoods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Content">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In response to a request for pretexts for military intervention by the Chief of Operations of the Cuba Project, Brig. Gen. <a href="/wiki/Edward_Lansdale" title="Edward Lansdale">Edward Lansdale</a>, the document listed methods, and outlined plans, that the authors believed would garner public and international support for U.S. military intervention in Cuba. According to the documents, the plan called for the following:
</p>
<div><ol><li>Since it would seem desirable to use legitimate provocation as the basis for U.S. military intervention in Cuba, a cover and deception plan, to include requisite preliminary actions such as had been developed in response to Task 33  c, could be executed as an initial effort to provoke Cuban reactions. Harassment plus deceptive actions to convince the Cubans of imminent invasion would be emphasized. Our military posture throughout execution of the plan will allow a rapid change from exercise to intervention if Cuban response justifies.</li><li>A series of well coordinated incidents will be planned to take place in and around Guantanamo to give genuine appearance of being done by hostile Cuban forces.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li><li>United States would respond by executing offensive operations to secure water and power supplies, destroying artillery and mortar emplacements which threaten the base.</li><li>Commence large scale United States military operations.</li></ol></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Related_Operation_Mongoose_proposals">Related Operation Mongoose proposals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Northwoods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Related Operation Mongoose proposals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Operation_Mongoose" class="mw-redirect" title="Operation Mongoose">Operation Mongoose</a></div>
<p>In addition to Operation Northwoods, under the Operation Mongoose program the U.S. Department of Defense had a number of similar proposals to be taken against the Cuban regime of <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Major-general-lansdale.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Major-general-lansdale.jpg/220px-Major-general-lansdale.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="286" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Major-general-lansdale.jpg/330px-Major-general-lansdale.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Major-general-lansdale.jpg/440px-Major-general-lansdale.jpg 2x" data-file-width="530" data-file-height="689" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Major-general-lansdale.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Edward Lansdale</div></div></div>
<p>Twelve of these proposals come from a 2 February 1962 memorandum entitled "Possible Actions to Provoke, Harass or Disrupt Cuba," written by Brig. Gen. William H. Craig and submitted to Brig. Gen. <a href="/wiki/Edward_Lansdale" title="Edward Lansdale">Edward Lansdale</a>, the commander of the Operation Mongoose project.<sup id="cite_ref-feinsilber1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-feinsilber1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-weiner_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weiner-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The memorandum outlines Operation Bingo, a plan to "create an incident which has the appearance of an attack on <a href="/wiki/Guantanamo_Bay_Naval_Base" title="Guantanamo Bay Naval Base">U.S. facilities (GMO) in Cuba</a>, thus providing an excuse for use of U.S. military might to overthrow the current government of Cuba."
</p><p>It also includes Operation Dirty Trick, a plot to blame Castro if the 1962 <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Mercury</a> manned space flight carrying <a href="/wiki/John_Glenn" title="John Glenn">John Glenn</a> crashed, saying: "The objective is to provide irrevocable proof that, should the MERCURY manned orbit flight fail, the fault lies with the Communists et al. Cuba &#32;&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>&#93;." It continues, "This to be accomplished by manufacturing various pieces of evidence which would prove electronic interference on the part of the Cubans."
</p><p>Even after General Lemnitzer lost his job as the <a href="/wiki/Chairman_of_the_Joint_Chiefs_of_Staff" title="Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff">Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff</a>, the Joint Chiefs of Staff still planned false-flag pretext operations at least into 1963. A different U.S. Department of Defense policy paper created in 1963 discussed a plan to make it appear that Cuba had attacked a member of the <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_American_States" title="Organization of American States">Organization of American States</a> (OAS) so that the United States could retaliate. The U.S. Department of Defense document says of one of the scenarios, "A contrived 'Cuban' attack on an OAS member could be set up, and the attacked state could be urged to take measures of self-defense and request assistance from the U.S. and OAS."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The plan expressed confidence that by this action, "the U.S. could almost certainly obtain the necessary two-thirds support among OAS members for collective action against Cuba."<sup id="cite_ref-bamford_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bamford-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Included in the nations the Joint Chiefs suggested as targets for covert attacks were <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Trinidad_and_Tobago" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad-Tobago</a>. Since both were members of the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">British Commonwealth</a>, the Joint Chiefs hoped that by secretly attacking them and then falsely blaming Cuba, the United States could incite the people of the United Kingdom into supporting a war against Castro.<sup id="cite_ref-bamford_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bamford-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> As the U.S. Department of Defense report noted:
</p>
<blockquote><p>Any of the contrived situations described above are inherently, extremely risky in our democratic system in which security can be maintained, after the fact, with very great difficulty. If the decision should be made to set up a contrived situation it should be one in which participation by U.S. personnel is limited only to the most highly trusted covert personnel. This suggests the infeasibility of the use of military units for any aspect of the contrived situation."<sup id="cite_ref-bamford_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bamford-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>The U.S. Department of Defense report even suggested covertly paying a person in the Castro government to attack the United States: "The only area remaining for consideration then would be to bribe one of Castro's subordinate commanders to initiate an attack on [the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">U.S. Navy</a> base at] Guantanamo."<sup id="cite_ref-bamford_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bamford-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction">Reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Northwoods&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_F._Kennedy_speaks_at_Rice_University.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/John_F._Kennedy_speaks_at_Rice_University.jpg/220px-John_F._Kennedy_speaks_at_Rice_University.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="320" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/John_F._Kennedy_speaks_at_Rice_University.jpg/330px-John_F._Kennedy_speaks_at_Rice_University.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/John_F._Kennedy_speaks_at_Rice_University.jpg/440px-John_F._Kennedy_speaks_at_Rice_University.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2034" data-file-height="2954" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_F._Kennedy_speaks_at_Rice_University.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>President Kennedy speaks at Rice University</div></div></div>
<p>Kennedy personally rejected the Northwoods proposal. A JCS/Pentagon document (Ed Lansdale memo) titled MEETING WITH THE PRESIDENT, 16 MARCH 1962 reads: "General Lemnitzer commented that the military had contingency plans for U.S. intervention. Also it had plans for creating plausible pretexts to use force, with the pretext either attacks on U.S. aircraft or a Cuban action in Latin America for which we could retaliate. The President said bluntly that we were not discussing the use of military force, that General Lemnitzer might find the U.S. so engaged in Berlin or elsewhere that he couldn't use the contemplated 4 divisions in Cuba."<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The proposal was sent for approval to the Secretary of Defense, <a href="/wiki/Robert_McNamara" title="Robert McNamara">Robert McNamara</a>, but was not implemented.
</p><p>Following presentation of the Northwoods plan, Kennedy removed Lemnitzer as Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, although he became <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Allied_Commander" title="Supreme Allied Commander">Supreme Allied Commander</a> of <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">NATO</a> in January 1963. U.S. military leaders began to perceive Kennedy as going soft on Cuba, and the President became increasingly unpopular with the military, a rift that came to a head during Kennedy's disagreements with the service chiefs over the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Missile_Crisis" title="Cuban Missile Crisis">Cuban Missile Crisis</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>On 3 August 2001, the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_People%27s_Power_of_Cuba" class="mw-redirect" title="National Assembly of People&#39;s Power of Cuba">National Assembly of People's Power of Cuba</a> (the main legislative body of the Republic of Cuba) issued a statement referring to Operation Northwoods and Operation Mongoose wherein it condemned such U.S. government plans.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
