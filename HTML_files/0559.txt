<p>In sociology, a <b>minority group</b> refers to a <a href="/wiki/Categorization" title="Categorization">category</a> of people who experience relative disadvantage as compared to members of a dominant social group.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Minority group membership is typically based on differences in observable characteristics or practices, such as: <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnicity</a> (<b>ethnic minority</b>), <a href="/wiki/Race_(human_categorization)" title="Race (human categorization)">race</a> (<b>racial minority</b>), religion (<a href="/wiki/Minority_religion" title="Minority religion">religious minority</a>), sexual orientation (<a href="/wiki/Sexual_minority" title="Sexual minority">sexual minority</a>), <a href="/wiki/Disability" title="Disability">disability</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Gender_identity" title="Gender identity">gender identity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Utilizing the framework of <a href="/wiki/Intersectionality" title="Intersectionality">intersectionality</a>, it is important to recognize that an individual may simultaneously hold membership in multiple minority groups (e.g. both a racial and religious minority).<sup id="cite_ref-:4_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Likewise, individuals may also be part of a minority group in regard to some characteristics, but part of a dominant group in regard to others.<sup id="cite_ref-:4_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "minority group" often occurs within the <a href="/wiki/Discourse" title="Discourse">discourse</a> of <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil rights">civil rights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Collective_rights" class="mw-redirect" title="Collective rights">collective rights</a>, as members of minority groups are prone to differential treatment in the countries and societies in which they live.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Minority group members often face <a href="/wiki/Discrimination" title="Discrimination">discrimination</a> in multiple areas of social life, including housing, employment, healthcare, and education, among others.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  While discrimination may be committed by individuals, it may also occur through <a href="/wiki/Structural_inequality" title="Structural inequality">structural inequalities</a>, in which rights and opportunities are not equally accessible to all.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The language of <a href="/wiki/Minority_rights" title="Minority rights">minority rights</a> is often used to discuss laws designed to protect minority groups from discrimination and afford them equal social status to the dominant group.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minority_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sociological">Sociological</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minority_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sociological">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Louis_Wirth" title="Louis Wirth">Louis Wirth</a> defined a minority group as "a group of people who, because of their physical or cultural characteristics, are singled out from the others in the <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a> in which they live for differential and unequal treatment, and who therefore regard themselves as objects of collective discrimination".<sup id="cite_ref-:3_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The definition includes both objective and subjective criteria: membership of a minority group is objectively <a href="/wiki/Ascribed_status" title="Ascribed status">ascribed</a> by society, based on an individual's physical or behavioral characteristics; it is also subjectively applied by its members, who may use their status as the basis of group identity or <a href="/wiki/Social_solidarity" class="mw-redirect" title="Social solidarity">solidarity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Thus, minority group status is categorical in nature: an individual who exhibits the physical or behavioral characteristics of a given minority group is accorded the status of that group and is subject to the same treatment as other members of that group.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Joe_Feagin" title="Joe Feagin">Joe Feagin</a>, states that a minority group has five characteristics: (1) suffering discrimination and subordination, (2) physical and/or cultural traits that set them apart, and which are disapproved by the dominant group, (3) a shared sense of collective identity and common burdens, (4) socially shared rules about who belongs and who does not determine minority status, and (5) tendency to marry within the group.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minority_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There is a controversy with the use of the word minority, as it has a generic and an academic usage.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Common usage of the term indicates a statistical minority; however, academics refer to power differences among groups rather than differences in population size among groups.<sup id="cite_ref-Barzilai_20102_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barzilai_20102-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The above criticism is based on the idea that a group can be considered minority even if it includes such a large number of people that it is numerically not a minority in society.
</p><p>Some sociologists have criticised the concept of "minority/majority", arguing this language excludes or neglects changing or unstable cultural identities, as well as cultural affiliations across national boundaries.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> As such, the term historically excluded groups (HEGs) is often similarly used to highlight the role of historical oppression and domination, and how this results in the underrepresentation of particular groups in various areas of social life.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political">Political</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minority_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Political">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The term national minority is often used to discuss minority groups in international and national politics.<sup id="cite_ref-:5_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:5-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> All countries contain some degree of racial, ethnic, or linguistic diversity.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.pewresearch.org/fact-tank/2013/07/18/the-most-and-least-culturally-diverse-countries-in-the-world/">[1]</a> In addition, minorities may also be immigrant, indigenous or landless nomadic communities.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> This often results in variations in language, culture, beliefs, practices, that set some groups apart from the dominant group. As these differences are usually perceived negatively by, this results in loss of social and political power for members of minority groups.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is no legal definition of national minorities in international law, though protection of minority groups is outlined by the United Nations <a href="/wiki/Declaration_on_the_Rights_of_Persons_Belonging_to_National_or_Ethnic,_Religious_and_Linguistic_Minorities" title="Declaration on the Rights of Persons Belonging to National or Ethnic, Religious and Linguistic Minorities">Declaration on the Rights of Persons Belonging to National or Ethnic, Religious and Linguistic Minorities</a>. <a href="/wiki/International_criminal_law" title="International criminal law">International criminal law</a> can protect the rights of racial or ethnic minorities in a number of ways.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The right to <a href="/wiki/Self-determination" title="Self-determination">self-determination</a> is a key issue. The Council of Europe regulates minority rights in the <a href="/wiki/European_Charter_for_Regional_or_Minority_Languages" title="European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages">European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Framework_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_National_Minorities" title="Framework Convention for the Protection of National Minorities">Framework Convention for the Protection of National Minorities</a>.
</p><p>In some places, subordinate <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic groups</a> may constitute a numerical majority, such as Blacks in South Africa under <a href="/wiki/South_Africa_under_apartheid" class="mw-redirect" title="South Africa under apartheid">apartheid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, for example, non-Hispanic Whites constitute the majority (63.4%) and all other racial and ethnic groups (Hispanic or <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_and_Latino_Americans" title="Hispanic and Latino Americans">Latino</a>, <a href="/wiki/African_Americans" title="African Americans">African Americans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asian_Americans" title="Asian Americans">Asian Americans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">American Indian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Native_Hawaiians" title="Native Hawaiians">Native Hawaiians</a>) are classified as "minorities".<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> If the non-Hispanic White population falls below 50% the group will only be the <i>plurality</i>, not the majority.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_minority_groups">Examples of minority groups</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minority_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Examples of minority groups">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Age_minorities">Age minorities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minority_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Age minorities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The elderly, while traditionally influential or even (in a <a href="/wiki/Gerontocracy" title="Gerontocracy">gerontocracy</a>) dominant in the past, are now usually reduced to the minority role of economically 'non-active' groups.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Children can also be understood as a minority group in these terms, and the discrimination faced by the young is known as <a href="/wiki/Adultism" title="Adultism">adultism</a>. Discrimination against the elderly is known as <a href="/wiki/Ageism" title="Ageism">ageism</a>.
</p><p>Various local and <a href="/wiki/International_human_rights_instruments" title="International human rights instruments">international statutes</a> are in place to mitigate the exploitation of children, such as the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Rights_of_the_Child" title="Convention on the Rights of the Child">Convention on the Rights of the Child</a>, as well as a number of organizations that make up the <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_rights_movement" title="Children&#39;s rights movement">children's rights movement</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Youth_rights" title="Youth rights">youth rights</a> movement campaigns for social empowerment for young people, and against the legal and social restrictions placed on <a href="/wiki/Minor_(law)" title="Minor (law)">legal minors</a>. Groups that advocate the interests of <a href="/wiki/Senior_citizen" class="mw-redirect" title="Senior citizen">senior citizens</a> range from the charitable (<a href="/wiki/Help_the_Aged" title="Help the Aged">Help the Aged</a>) to grass-roots activism (<a href="/wiki/Gray_Panthers" title="Gray Panthers">Gray Panthers</a>), and often overlap with disability rights issues.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Educational_minorities">Educational minorities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minority_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Educational minorities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Involuntary_minorities">Involuntary minorities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minority_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Involuntary minorities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Also known as "castelike minorities," involuntary minorities are a term for people who were originally brought into any society against their will. In the United States, for instance, it includes but is not limited to Native Americans, Puerto Ricans, African Americans,<sup id="cite_ref-:02_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:02-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and native-born Mexican Americans.<sup id="cite_ref-:12_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> For reasons of cultural differences, involuntary minorities may experience difficulties in school more than members of other (voluntary) minority groups. Social capital helps children engage with different age groups that share a common goal.<sup id="cite_ref-:22_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:22-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voluntary_minorities">Voluntary minorities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minority_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Voluntary minorities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Immigrants take on minority status in their new country, usually in hopes of a better future economically, educationally, and politically than in their homeland. Because of their focus on success, voluntary minorities are more likely to do better in school than other migrating minorities.<sup id="cite_ref-:02_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:02-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Adapting to a very different culture and language make difficulties in the early stages of life in the new country. Voluntary immigrants do not experience a sense of divided identity as much as involuntary minorities, and are often rich in social capital because of their educational ambitions.<sup id="cite_ref-:22_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:22-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Major immigrant groups in the United States include Mexicans, Central and South Americans, Cubans, Africans, and Indians.<sup id="cite_ref-:12_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:12-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_and_sexuality_minorities">Gender and sexuality minorities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minority_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Gender and sexuality minorities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sexual_minority" title="Sexual minority">Sexual minority</a></div>
<p>The term sexual minority is frequently used by public health researchers to recognize a wide variety of individuals who engage in same-sex sexual behavior, including those who do not identify under the LGBTQ umbrella. For example, men who have sex with men (MSM), but do not identify as gay. In addition, the term gender minorities can include many types of <a href="/wiki/Gender_variance" title="Gender variance">gender variant</a> people, such as <a href="/wiki/Intersex" title="Intersex">intersex</a> people, <a href="/wiki/Transsexuality" class="mw-redirect" title="Transsexuality">transgender people</a>, or gender non-conforming individuals. However, the terms sexual and gender minority are often not preferred by LGBTQ people, as they represent clinical categories rather than individual identity.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Though <a href="/wiki/Lesbian" title="Lesbian">lesbian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gay" title="Gay">gay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bisexuality" title="Bisexuality">bisexual</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transgender" title="Transgender">transgender</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Queer" title="Queer">queer</a> (LGBTQ) people have existed throughout human history, LGBT rights movements across many western countries led to the recognition of LGBTQ people as members of a minority group.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> LGBTQ people represent a numerical and social minority. They experience numerous social inequalities stemming from their group membership as LGBTQ people. These inequalities include social discrimination and isolation, unequal access to healthcare, employment, and housing, and experience negative mental and physical health outcomes due to these experiences.<sup id="cite_ref-:6_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:6-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="People_with_disabilities">People with disabilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minority_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: People with disabilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3><p>
The <a href="/wiki/Disability_rights_movement" title="Disability rights movement">disability rights movement</a> has contributed to an understanding of people with disabilities (including not to be called 'disabled') as a minority or a coalition of minorities who are disadvantaged by society, not just as people who are disadvantaged by their impairments. Advocates of disability rights emphasize difference in physical or psychological functioning, rather than inferiority. For example, some people with <a href="/wiki/Autism" title="Autism">autism</a> argue for acceptance of <a href="/wiki/Neurodiversity" title="Neurodiversity">neurodiversity</a>, much as opponents of <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a> argue for acceptance of ethnic diversity. The <a href="/wiki/Deaf_community" class="mw-redirect" title="Deaf community">deaf community</a> is often regarded as a linguistic and cultural minority rather than a group with disabilities, and some <a href="/wiki/Deaf" class="mw-redirect" title="Deaf">deaf</a> people do not see themselves as having a disability at all. Rather, they are disadvantaged by technologies and social institutions that are designed to cater for the dominant group. (<i>See the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Rights_of_Persons_with_Disabilities" title="Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities">Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities</a></i>.)</p><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Julius_and_Ethel_Rosenberg_NYWTS.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Julius_and_Ethel_Rosenberg_NYWTS.jpg/220px-Julius_and_Ethel_Rosenberg_NYWTS.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Julius_and_Ethel_Rosenberg_NYWTS.jpg/330px-Julius_and_Ethel_Rosenberg_NYWTS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Julius_and_Ethel_Rosenberg_NYWTS.jpg/440px-Julius_and_Ethel_Rosenberg_NYWTS.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2139" data-file-height="1947" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Julius_and_Ethel_Rosenberg_NYWTS.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Photo of the Rosenbergs in jail</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_minorities">Political minorities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minority_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Political minorities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the most controversial minorities in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and other countries has been communists. Along with the <a href="/wiki/Red_Scare" title="Red Scare">Red Scare</a> and execution of <a href="/wiki/Julius_and_Ethel_Rosenberg" title="Julius and Ethel Rosenberg">Julius and Ethel Rosenberg</a>, the United States ran open campaigns to fight, contain and promote fear of communism in the country. Some were persecuted as communist even when they were not actually so: for example, many activists for civil rights were portrayed as inspired by a communist agenda. Communists in the United States, as in many European countries, are often afraid to proclaim their politics, fearing abuse and discrimination from the political majority.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_minorities">Religious minorities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minority_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Religious minorities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Minority_religion" title="Minority religion">Minority religion</a></div>
<p>People belonging to religious minorities have a faith which is different from that held by the majority. Most countries of the world have religious minorities. It is now widely accepted in the west that people should have the freedom to choose their own religion, including not having any religion (<a href="/wiki/Atheism" title="Atheism">atheism</a> and/or <a href="/wiki/Agnosticism" title="Agnosticism">agnosticism</a>), and including the right to convert from one religion to another. However, in many countries this freedom is constricted. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a>, a new system of identity cards<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> requires all citizens to state their religion—and the only choices are <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a> (See <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_identification_card_controversy" title="Egyptian identification card controversy">Egyptian identification card controversy</a>). Another example is the case of decreasing population of <a href="/wiki/Minorities_in_Pakistan" title="Minorities in Pakistan">minorities in Pakistan</a>, where they are being forcefully converted or killed. <sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> <sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_minorities">Regional minorities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minority_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Regional minorities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Authors have pointed out that many coal workers would be unwilling to move for work or were not likely to be able to be retrained as Appalachians are an "ethnic minority".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Women_as_minorities">Women as minorities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minority_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Women as minorities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While in most societies, numbers of men and women are roughly equal, the status of women as a subordinate group has led to many social scientists to study them as a minority group.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Though women's legal rights and status vary widely across countries, women experience social inequalities relative to men in most societies.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Women are often denied access to education, subject to violence, and lack access to the same economic opportunities as men.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Law_and_government">Law and government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minority_group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Law and government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the politics of some countries, a "minority" is an <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">ethnic group</a> recognized by law, and having specified rights. Speakers of a legally recognized <a href="/wiki/Minority_language" title="Minority language">minority language</a>, for instance, might have the right to education or communication with the government in their mother tongue. Countries with special provisions<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (May 2016)">which?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> for minorities include <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The various minority groups in a country are often not given equal treatment. Some groups are too small or indistinct to obtain minority protections. For example, a member of a particularly small ethnic group might be forced to check "Other" on a checklist of different backgrounds and so might receive fewer privileges than a member of a more defined group.
</p><p>Many contemporary governments prefer to assume the people they rule all belong to the same nationality rather than separate ones based on ethnicity. The United States asks for <a href="/wiki/Race_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (U.S. Census)">race and ethnicity</a> on its official census forms, which thus breaks up and organizes its population into sub-groups, primarily racial rather than national. <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> does not divide its nationals by ethnic group, although it does maintain an official notion of minority languages.
</p><p>Some especially significant or powerful minorities receive comprehensive protection and political representation. For example, the former Yugoslav republic of <a href="/wiki/Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Bosnia and Herzegovina">Bosnia and Herzegovina</a> recognizes the three constitutive nations, none of which constitutes a numerical majority (see <a href="/wiki/Nations_of_Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" class="mw-redirect" title="Nations of Bosnia and Herzegovina">nations of Bosnia and Herzegovina</a>). However, other minorities such as <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romani</a><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a>, are officially labelled "foreign" and are excluded from many of these protections. For example, they may be excluded from political positions, including the presidency.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is debate over recognizing minority groups and their privileges. One view<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> is that the application of special rights to minority groups may harm some countries, such as new states in Africa or Latin America not founded on the European <a href="/wiki/Nation-state" class="mw-redirect" title="Nation-state">nation-state</a> model, since minority recognition may interfere with establishing a national identity. It may hamper the integration of the minority into mainstream society, perhaps leading to <a href="/wiki/Separatism" title="Separatism">separatism</a> or <a href="/wiki/Supremacist" class="mw-redirect" title="Supremacist">supremacism</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2012)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> feel that the failure of the dominant <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>-speaking majority to integrate <a href="/wiki/French_Canadians" title="French Canadians">French Canadians</a> has provoked <a href="/wiki/Quebec_separatism" class="mw-redirect" title="Quebec separatism">Quebec separatism</a>.
</p><p>Others assert that minorities require specific protections to ensure that they are not marginalised: for example, <a href="/wiki/Bilingual_education" title="Bilingual education">bilingual education</a> may be needed to allow linguistic minorities to fully integrate into the school system and compete equally in society. In this view, rights for minorities strengthen the nation-building project, as members of minorities see their interests well served, and willingly accept the legitimacy of the nation and their integration (not assimilation) within it.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
