	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Mongolian name</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Mongolian names</b> have undergone a number of changes in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Mongolia" title="History of Mongolia">history of Mongolia</a>, both with regard to their meaning and their source languages. In modern day, <a href="/wiki/Inner_Mongolia" title="Inner Mongolia">Inner Mongolian</a> naming customs are similar to <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a>, but do display some differences.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_evolution">Historical evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongolian</a> names traditionally have an important symbolic character&#8212;a name with <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/auspicious" class="extiw" title="wikt:auspicious">auspicious</a> connotations being thought to bring good fortune to its bearer. The naming of children was usually done by the parents or a respected elder of the family or religious figures. For example, it is said that in the 13th century, the prominent shaman, Teb-tengeri, saw in the stars a great future for <a href="/wiki/Tolui" title="Tolui">Tolui</a>'s eldest son and bestowed on the child the name <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6ngke_Khan" title="Möngke Khan">Möngke</a> (meaning "eternal" in the Mongolian language).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Nowadays most parents give <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_language" title="Mongolian language">Mongolian</a> names to their children, often in the form of <a href="/wiki/Compound_(linguistics)" title="Compound (linguistics)">compounds</a> consisting of two <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">nouns</a> or <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjectives</a>, representing qualities such as solidity and strength for boys or beauty in the case of girls.
</p><p>Generally, the Mongols (unlike their southern neighbours, the Chinese) have only one personal name, which remains the same throughout their lives. While clan organization remained important among the Mongols into the 17th century, clan names were not linked with the personal name in a family name system.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Clan name is still important among the Buryats and to a lesser extent among Kalmyks today. While the personal name of the living ruler was not originally tabooed, as in China, the names of deceased rulers were tabooed for several generations. In the past this prohibition was even stronger. In Mongolia and Inner Mongolia, the Mongols also use clan names.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most common category of Mongol names were those of auspicious or (for boys) manly things, such as gold (<a href="/wiki/Altan_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Altan (disambiguation)">altan</a>), eternity (Möngke), surplus (<a href="/wiki/Hulagu_Khan" title="Hulagu Khan">hulagu</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> blue (köke), white (chagha’an), good health (<a href="/wiki/Esen_taishi" class="mw-redirect" title="Esen taishi">esen</a>), uncle (<a href="/wiki/Abaqa_Khan" title="Abaqa Khan">abaqa</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> firmness (<a href="/wiki/Batu_(given_name)" title="Batu (given name)">batu</a>), stability (<a href="/wiki/Toqto%27a_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Toqto&#39;a (disambiguation)">toqto'a</a>), bulls (<a href="/wiki/Buga_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Buga (disambiguation)">buqa</a>, for men), iron (<a href="/wiki/Timur_(name)" title="Timur (name)">temür</a>), steel (<a href="/wiki/Bolad_(given_name)" title="Bolad (given name)">bolad</a>), black (qara), hardness (<a href="/wiki/Berke" title="Berke">berke</a>) or nine (yisü).
</p><p>Such names were often combined with suffixes used only for personal names, such as -<i>dai</i>, -<i>ge</i>/<i>gei</i>, and -<i>der</i> for boys and -<i>jin</i>, -<i>tani</i>, and -<i>lun</i> for girls. However, <a href="/wiki/Genghis_Khan" title="Genghis Khan">Temüjin</a>'s -<i>jin</i> is a form of the occupational <a href="/wiki/Derivation_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Derivation (linguistics)">derivational</a> suffix -<i>cin</i>, but not a feminine suffix: <i>temür</i> 'iron' + -<i>cin</i> = <i>temüjin</i> 'smith'. 
</p><p>Other names were based on either conquests or clan names. For example, <a href="/wiki/Sartaq" class="mw-redirect" title="Sartaq">Sartaq</a> (merchants of western Asian or western Central Asian origin), <i>Hasi</i> (Mongolian form of <a href="/wiki/Tangut_people" title="Tangut people">Tangut</a> <a href="/wiki/Xia_dynasty" title="Xia dynasty">Xia dynasty</a>), Orus (<a href="/wiki/Rus%27_people" title="Rus&#39; people">Rus</a>), Asudai (<a href="/wiki/Alani" class="mw-redirect" title="Alani">Alani</a>) and so on. Clan-based personal names did not relate to the person’s own clan or tribe. For example, <a href="/wiki/Eljigidey" title="Eljigidey">Eljigidey</a> was not from Iljigin (Eljigin) tribe and the Mongol general Mangghudai was a Tatar (not to be confused with the modern Turkic people of the <a href="/wiki/Tatars" title="Tatars">Tatars</a> in Europe), but not a <a href="/wiki/Manghud" title="Manghud">Manghud</a>.
</p><p>One finds a number of degrading or inauspicious names during the 13–14th centuries such as <a href="/wiki/Sorqaqtani" class="mw-redirect" title="Sorqaqtani">Sorqaqtani</a>, “Pox girl,” or <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nohai_(disambiguation)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nohai (disambiguation) (page does not exist)">Nohai</a> (~Nokai) "dog", obviously in an attempt to fool bad spirits or disease into thinking it had already afflicted them.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This tradition is still preserved in <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">Mongolia</a> in modern times. Symbolic names that express frustration can be found such as the not uncommon girls’ name <a href="/wiki/Oghul_Qaimish" title="Oghul Qaimish">Oghul-qaimish</a> (<a href="/wiki/Old_Turkic_language" title="Old Turkic language">Middle Turkic</a> "next time a boy"), while the name <i><a href="/wiki/Jochi" title="Jochi">Jochi</a></i> “Guest” indicated doubts about the child’s paternity.
</p><p>Turkic names were common among Mongols (such as Oghul-qaimish, Abishqa, Qutlugh and so on). However, names of other foreign origin were introduced when the Mongol Empire expanded all over Eurasia, increasing international trade and cultural connections and also partly due to religious dignity. <a href="/wiki/Ghazan" title="Ghazan">Ghazan</a>, Kharbanda, or Toghus (peacock) are not Mongol words. <a href="/wiki/Christian_name" title="Christian name">Christian names</a> also existed among the Turkic and Mongol peoples (especially Onguts and Keraits) on the <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_Plateau" title="Mongolian Plateau">Mongolian Plateau</a>. The Ilkhan <a href="/wiki/%C3%96ljeit%C3%BC" class="mw-redirect" title="Öljeitü">Öljeitü</a>'s name at birth was <i>Nicholas</i>.
</p><p>Some Mongols had <a href="/wiki/Chinese_name" title="Chinese name">Chinese names</a>. For example, the Buddhist monk, Haiyun, bestowed the name <a href="/wiki/Zhenjin" title="Zhenjin">Zhenjin</a> (True-gold) to <a href="/wiki/Khubilai" class="mw-redirect" title="Khubilai">Khubilai</a> and Chabi's eldest son. In the late 13th century, many newborn children in <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> were named after Mongol rulers, including <i>Hülegü</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>From the mid-13th century <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Middle_Turkic_languages" title="Middle Turkic languages">Uyghur</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Classical_Tibetan" title="Classical Tibetan">Tibetan</a> <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">Buddhist</a> names (<a href="/wiki/Ananda" class="mw-redirect" title="Ananda">Ananda</a> (the Yuan prince), <a href="/wiki/Vajra" title="Vajra">Dorji</a> (Khubilai's son) or <i>Wachir</i> (a Yuan official), <i>Gammala</i> (Khubilai's son), <i>Irinchin</i> (the Ilkhanid general), etc.) granted by Tibetan teachers became common in the royal family and the aristocratic clans. In the west some Mongols took Islamic names such as <i>Bū Sa'īd</i><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> (misspelled as <a href="/wiki/Abu_Sa%27id_(Ilkhanid_dynasty)" class="mw-redirect" title="Abu Sa&#39;id (Ilkhanid dynasty)">Abu Sa'id</a>) or <i>Khwaja</i> after they converted, although many kept their Mongolian names.
</p><p>After the expulsion of the Mongol regime from China, the Sanskrit names in the imperial family soon disappeared. Christian names appeared occasionally before disappearing (<a href="/wiki/Mark%C3%B6rgis_Khan" class="mw-redirect" title="Markörgis Khan">Markörgis Khan</a>). Muslim and Turkic names also declined (<a href="/wiki/Akbarjin" class="mw-redirect" title="Akbarjin">Akbarjin</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ismayil&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ismayil (page does not exist)">Ismayil</a> or <a href="/wiki/Arghun" title="Arghun">Arghun</a>), leaving primarily the auspicious Mongolian names similar to those in the early empire. For example, some of the later Mongolian Emperors' names include <a href="/wiki/Batum%C3%B6ngke_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Batumöngke (disambiguation)">Batumöngke</a>, Buyan, Esen, Toγtoγa Buqa and Manduul. Mongol name customs also affected the nations under Mongol rule. The <a href="/wiki/Jurchens" class="mw-redirect" title="Jurchens">Jurchens</a> (ancestors of <a href="/wiki/Manchus" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchus">Manchus</a>) in Ming China often used Mongolian names.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sayyid_Ajjal_Shams_al-Din_Omar" class="mw-redirect" title="Sayyid Ajjal Shams al-Din Omar">Sayyid Ajjal Shams al-Din Omar</a>'s great-grandson was given the name <i>Bayan</i> "rich".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_Middle_Ages_and_early_modern_period">Late Middle Ages and early modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Late Middle Ages and early modern period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the beginning of the new wave of Buddhism in 1575, however, Buddhist and Tibetan names were reintroduced into Mongolia. By 1700 the vast majority of Mongols had Buddhist names, usually Tibetan, but also sometimes Sanskrit or from Mongolian Buddhist terminology. A number of Mongolian-language names survived, particularly with more pacific elements designating peace
(<i>Engke</i>, <i>Amur</i>), happiness (<i>Jirgal</i>), long life (<i>Nasu</i>), and blessing (<i>Öljei</i>, <i>Kesig</i>).
</p><p>Buddhist names were granted according to several different principles. The most common for laymen are
based on the Tibetan or Sanskrit names of powerful deities: <i>Damdin/Damrin</i> (<a href="/wiki/Hayagriva" title="Hayagriva">Hayagriva</a>), <i>Dulma/Dari</i> (<a href="/wiki/Tara_(Buddhism)" title="Tara (Buddhism)">Tara</a>), <i>Gombo</i> (<a href="/wiki/Mah%C4%81k%C4%81la" class="mw-redirect" title="Mahākāla">Mahākāla</a>), Cagdur/Shagdur (<a href="/wiki/Vajrapani" title="Vajrapani">Vajrapani</a>), Jamsrang (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Begtser&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Begtser (page does not exist)">Begtser</a>), Jamyang (<a href="/wiki/Manjusri" class="mw-redirect" title="Manjusri">Manjusri</a>), etc. Another type of Buddhist name derives from the Tibetan days of the week, themselves named after the Sun, Moon, and five
visible planets (Nima, Dawa, Migmar, Lhagba, Pürbü, Basang, Bimba). Another astrological scheme divides the days of the month into five classes, each under an element: <i>Dorji</i> (<a href="/wiki/Vajra" title="Vajra">power bolt</a>), <i>Badma</i> (<a href="/wiki/Padma_(attribute)" title="Padma (attribute)">lotus</a>), and <i>Sangjai</i> (<a href="/wiki/Buddha" class="mw-redirect" title="Buddha">Buddha</a>). The suffixes -<i>jab</i> (Tibetan <i>skyabs</i> “protecting”) and -<i>sürüng</i> (Tibetan -<i>srung</i> “guarding”) were commonly added to these Buddhist names. Finally, some names, particularly for monks, were based on Tibetan words for desired qualities or aspects of the religion: <i>Lubsang</i> “good intellect”, <i>Agwang</i> “powerful in speech”, <i>Danzin</i> “instruction keeper”, <i>Dashi/Rashi</i>, “blessed.” A number of Buddhist terms exist in multiple forms transmitted from Old Uyghur, Tibetan, and Sanskrit: thus, <i>Wachir/Ochir</i>, <i>Dorji</i>, and <i>Bazar</i> all mean “power bolt,” while <i>Erdeni</i>, <i>Rinchin</i>, and <i>Radna</i> all mean “jewel”.
</p><p>A distinctive type of Mongolian name that flourished in this period and is still common in the countryside is the avoidance name, designed to avert misfortune from the child: <i>Nergüi</i> “No Name”, <i>Enebish</i> “Not This”, <i>Terbish</i> "Not That".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern">Modern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Mongolia">Mongolia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mongolia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the 20th century, when Mongolia had close ties to the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, Mongols were sometimes given <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> names like <i>Alexander</i> or <i>Sasha</i>, or mixed ones like <i>Ivaanjav</i> consisting of the Russian <i>Ivan</i> and the Tibetan <i>-jav</i>. Politically active parents may have chosen <i>Oktyabr</i> (October), <i>Seseer</i> (USSR), <i>Mart</i> (March) and even <i>Molotov</i> as names for their offspring. One such example is <i>Melschoi</i>, composed of the first letters of <a href="/wiki/Karl_Marx" title="Karl Marx">Marx</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Engels" title="Friedrich Engels">Engels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Lenin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stalin" title="Joseph Stalin">Stalin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Khorloogiin_Choibalsan" title="Khorloogiin Choibalsan">Choibalsan</a>.
</p><p>Today male names still include the names of old Mongolian elements such as 'iron' or 'steel', or other words denoting strength, such as 'hero', 'strong', or 'ax': some examples are <i>Gansükh</i> 'steel-ax', <i>Batsaikhan</i> 'strong-nice', or <i>Tömörbaatar</i> 'iron-hero', <i>Chuluunbold</i> 'stone-steel'. Temujin, Borte, Yisu and other old names are commonly given to newborn children after 1990.
</p><p>Women's names commonly refer to fine colours or flowers, the sun and moon, or may be made up of any other word with positive connotations using the feminine <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffix</a> <i>-maa</i> (Tib. 'mother'): some common examples are <i>Altantsetseg</i> 'golden-flower', <i>Narantuyaa</i> 'sun-beam', <i>Uranchimeg</i> 'artistic-decoration', <i>Sarangerel</i> 'moon-light', <i>Erdenetungalag</i> 'jewel-clear', and <i>Tsetsegmaa</i> 'flower'.
</p><p>Many gender-neutral name components refer to auspicious qualities such as eternity or happiness: some examples are <i>Mönkh</i> 'eternal', <i>Erdene</i> 'jewel', <i>Oyuun</i> 'mind', <i>Altan</i> 'golden', <i>Saikhan</i> 'fine' and <i>Enkh</i> 'peace'. Many names include the names of places, including mountains, rivers etc., e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Altai_Mountains" title="Altai Mountains">Altai</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Tuul_River" title="Tuul River">Tuul</a></i>.
</p><p>Mongolians do not use <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surnames</a> in the way that most Westerners, Chinese or Japanese do. Since the <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_People%27s_Republic" title="Mongolian People&#39;s Republic">socialist period</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymics</a> &#8212; at that time called <i>ovog</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> now known as <i>etsgiin ner</i> &#8212; are used instead of a surname. If the father's name is not legally established (i.e. by marriage) or altogether unknown, a <a href="/wiki/Matronymic" title="Matronymic">matronymic</a> is used. The patro- or matronymic is written before the given name. 
</p><p>Therefore, if a man with given name Tsakhia has a son, and gives the son the name <i>Elbegdorj</i>, the son's full name, as it appears in passports and the like, is <i>Tsakhia Elbegdorj</i>. Very frequently, as in texts and speech, the patronymic is given in <a href="/wiki/Genitive" class="mw-redirect" title="Genitive">genitive</a> case, i.e. <i>Tsakhiagiin Elbegdorj</i>, with (in this case) -giin being the genitive suffix. However, the patronymic is rather insignificant in everyday use and usually just abbreviated to an initial - <i>Ts. Elbegdorj</i>. People are normally just referred to and addressed by their given name (<i>Elbegdorj guai</i> - 'Mr. Elbegdorj'), and the patronymic is only used to distinguish two people with a common given name. Even then, they are usually just distinguished by their initials, not by the full patronymic. There are cases in which a matronymic has been legally bestowed for one or the other reason, while a patronymic is known. If the patronymic is to be conveyed anyway, this can take a form like <i>Altan Choi ovogt Dumaagiin Sodnom</i> with the patronymic preceding the word ovog that takes the suffix <i>-t</i> 'having'.
</p><p>The basic differences between Mongolian and European names, in connection with trying to fit Mongolian names into foreign schemata, frequently lead to confusion. For example, <a href="/wiki/Otryadyn_G%C3%BCndegmaa" title="Otryadyn Gündegmaa">O. Gündegmaa</a>, a Mongolian shooter, is often incorrectly referred to as Otryad, i.e. by the (given) name of her father.
</p><p>Since 2000, Mongolians have been officially using <a href="/wiki/Clan" title="Clan">clan names</a> &#8212; <i>ovog</i>, the same word that was used for the patronymics during the socialist period &#8212; on their ID cards (the clan name is also referred to as <i>urgiin ovog</i>, meaning lineage name, among general use, to distinguish from the patro- or matronymic). Many people chose the names of the ancient clans and tribes such <a href="/wiki/Borjigin" title="Borjigin">Borjigin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Behsud_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Behsud (disambiguation)">Besud</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jalair" class="mw-redirect" title="Jalair">Jalair</a>, etc. Others chose the names of the native places of their ancestors, or the names of their most ancient known ancestor. Some just decided to pass their own given names (or modifications of their given names) to their descendants as clan names. A few chose other attributes of their lives as surnames; Mongolia's first cosmonaut <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BCgderdemidiin_G%C3%BCrragchaa" title="Jügderdemidiin Gürragchaa">Gürragchaa</a> chose 'Sansar' (Outer space). Clan names precede the patronymics and given names, as in <i>Besud Tsakhiagiin Elbegdorj</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
In practice, these clan names seem to have had no significant effect &#8212; nor are they included in Mongolian passports.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Inner_Mongolia">Inner Mongolia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Inner Mongolia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Inner Mongolia, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_name" title="Chinese name">Chinese names</a> are quite common, as are Mongolian names. Mongolian names of Tibetan origin are usually restricted to people in their 50s and older. Because China does not recognize Mongolian clan names, <a href="/wiki/Mongols_in_China" title="Mongols in China">Mongols in China</a> who have acquired their passports since 2001 have "XXX" printed in place of their surnames, while before not only "XXX" but also a syllable from an individual's given name or the first syllable of the patronymic was used in place of the Chinese surname. Also, for some individuals, "XXX" is printed in the slot for the given name, while the given name is printed in the slot for the surname. 
</p><p>Some Mongolians in China do have surnames because their parents are <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hui_Chinese" class="mw-redirect" title="Hui Chinese">Hui</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchu_people" title="Manchu people">Manchu</a> etc. Some others use an abbreviation (like <i>Bao</i> 'Borjigin') of their clan name.
</p><p>Officially, Mongolian name in China is transcribed with the <a href="/wiki/SASM/GNC/SRC_transcriptions" class="mw-redirect" title="SASM/GNC/SRC transcriptions">SASM/GNC/SRC transcriptions</a> (e.g. Ulanhu), but this system is rarely performed. Generally, the names in Chinese passports are given as the <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">Pinyin</a> form of the Chinese character transcription of the original Mongolian. For example, <i>Mengkebateer</i> (from <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">孟克巴特尔</span>) would be used instead of <i>Möngkebaghatur</i> (<a href="/wiki/Mongolian_script" title="Mongolian script">Mongolian script</a>), <i>Mөnghebagatur</i> (Mongolian pinyin) or <i>Munkhbaatar</i> (approximate English pronunciation).
</p><p>Sometimes in such <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a> transcriptions of Mongolian names, the first syllable is sometimes reanalyzed as the surname, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Ulanhu" title="Ulanhu">Ulanhu</a> becomes Wu Lanfu. <a href="/wiki/Syllable_onset" class="mw-redirect" title="Syllable onset">Onset</a> (if available) and <a href="/wiki/Syllable_nucleus" class="mw-redirect" title="Syllable nucleus">nucleus</a> of the first <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">syllable</a> of the father's name can be used for disambiguation, but have no official status, e.g. <i>Na. Gereltü</i>. Rarely, the onset is used on its own, e.g. <i>L. Toγtambayar</i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Siblings">Siblings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Siblings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Siblings are sometimes given names containing similar <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">morphemes</a>, like <i>Gan-Ochir</i>, <i>Gantömör</i> etc., or names related to the same theme, like <i>Naran</i> ('Sun'), <i>Saran</i> ('Moon'), <i>Tsolmon</i> ('Morning star').
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Taboo_names">Taboo names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Taboo names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There is also a tradition of giving names with unpleasant qualities to children born to a couple whose previous children have died, in the belief that the unpleasant name will mislead evil spirits seeking to steal the child. <i>Muunokhoi</i> 'Vicious Dog' may seem a strange name, but Mongolians have traditionally been given such taboo names to avoid misfortune and confuse evil spirits. Other examples include <i>Nekhii</i> 'Sheepskin', <i>Nergüi</i> 'No Name', <i>Medekhgüi</i>, 'I Don't Know', <i>Khünbish</i> 'Not A Human Being', <i>Khenbish</i> 'Nobody', <i>Ogtbish</i> 'Not At All', <i>Enebish</i> 'Not This One', <i>Terbish</i> 'Not That One'.
</p><p>Couples whose previous boys have died would give female names to their son to mislead the evil spirits, and vice versa. Synchronically, taboo meaning may be stronger or obliterated: <i>Nergüi</i>, for example, is very common and does not immediately raise any association, while <i>Khünbish</i> might semantically be perceived as <i>khün bish</i> (cf. the same phenomenon in German with the unremarkable <i>Burkhart</i> (lit. 'castle-strong')<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> versus the unusual <i>Fürchtegott</i> ('fear-God')).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nicknames">Nicknames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Nicknames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When addressing a familiar person, names are shortened, most commonly by choosing one of the parts of the name and adding a vowel, melting it into one or adding the suffix <i>-ka</i>. E.g., a woman named <i>Delgerzayaa</i> might be called <i>Delgree</i>, <i>Zayaa</i> or <i>Deegii</i>, a man named <i>Arslandorj</i> might become <i>Askaa</i>, or his sister <i>Idertuyaa</i> could become <i>Idree</i>, and so might her boyfriend <i>Iderbayar</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_structure">Name structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Mongolian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Name structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the Middle Ages, there were no patronymics, but clan names were sometimes used in a particular morphological form.
</p>
