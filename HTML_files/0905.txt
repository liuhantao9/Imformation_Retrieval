<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponymy">Toponymy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Toponymy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The name <i>Ossett</i> derives from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> and is either "the fold of a man named Osla" or " a fold frequented by blackbirds".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
Ossett is sometimes misspelled as "Osset". In Ellis' <i>On Early English Pronunciation</i>, one of the founding works of British <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistics</a>, the incorrect spelling is used.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/British_Library" title="British Library">British Library</a> has an online dialect study that uses the spelling.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> One new alternative theory is that it is the place where King Osbehrt died after receiving fatal wounds when fighting the Great Heathen Army of the Vikings at York on 21 March 867. An exceedingly rare clustering of high status Anglian graves, one bearing the Anglian royal symbol of the dragon and the name Osbehrt, was found in the churchyard at Thornhill Parish Church directly across the valley from – and within sight of – Ossett.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ossett appears in the 1086 <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> as "Osleset", which was in the Manor of Wakefield. The <i>Domesday Book</i> was compiled for <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a> in 1086. "Osleset" was recorded as three and a half <a href="/wiki/Carucate" title="Carucate">carucates</a> which is the land needed to be ploughed by three teams of eight oxen. Woodland pasture measured "half a league long as much broad" (roughly six furlongs by six furlongs). Four villans and three bordars lived in Osleset, a villan was an upper status villager, a bordar was a lower status villager.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_Revolution">Industrial Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Industrial Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Coal-mining was, up to the late 1960s, Ossett's second industry in terms of people employed and the first in terms of males employed. Coal has been mined since the 14th century and there were a large number of pits in Ossett during the 19th century. The pits included Old Roundwood, opened in 1851 mining the Gawthorpe seam. The Haigh Moor seam opened in 1860 and the Silkstone seam opened in 1893. Pildacre pit shut due to flooding in 1875 but remained as a source of water for Ossett. Westfield shut in the early 1900s. The Chidswell riot in 1893 was caused by striking miners trying to reach Westfield to stop other miners working. Another pit down Healey Road was also the scene of tension between police and striking miners. Low Laithes pit shut in 1926, however the seams later flooded and were responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Lofthouse_Colliery_disaster" title="Lofthouse Colliery disaster">Lofthouse Colliery disaster</a> in 1973. Greatfield shut in the 1950s, Old Roundwood shut in 1966 and Shaw Cross, on the Ossett/<a href="/wiki/Dewsbury" title="Dewsbury">Dewsbury</a> border near the current <a href="/wiki/Crown_Flatt" title="Crown Flatt">Dewsbury Rugby League stadium</a>, closed in 1968.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Steam_Engine,_Runtlings_Mill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_673828.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Steam_Engine%2C_Runtlings_Mill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_673828.jpg/220px-Steam_Engine%2C_Runtlings_Mill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_673828.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Steam_Engine%2C_Runtlings_Mill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_673828.jpg/330px-Steam_Engine%2C_Runtlings_Mill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_673828.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Steam_Engine%2C_Runtlings_Mill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_673828.jpg/440px-Steam_Engine%2C_Runtlings_Mill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_673828.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="427" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Steam_Engine,_Runtlings_Mill_-_geograph.org.uk_-_673828.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The steam engine at Runtlings Mill in 1987.</div></div></div>
<p>Author and local resident <a href="/wiki/Stan_Barstow" title="Stan Barstow">Stan Barstow</a> said that Ossett and <a href="/wiki/Horbury" title="Horbury">Horbury</a> were the "border country" where the north-west of the coalfield merged with the south-east of the wool towns. Local historian John Goodchild said, "The place was essentially one of small mines and small mills". The town was once a thriving centre of the "shoddy" industry; recycling woollen garments. Whilst some mill towns employed mostly females in its textile sector, Ossett's mills always had roughly equal numbers of men and women. The town's mills were generally small, but they had a reputation as high-quality producers. Whitehead's Mill used to have a <a href="/wiki/Float_(parade)" title="Float (parade)">carnival float</a> that said "We Export to the World" at the Gawthorpe May Pole parade.
</p><p>During the 1970s, Woodhead Manufacturing employed 1,500 people on this site in its two premises fronting Church Street and Kingsway. The shock absorber business was the last part of the site operations to close in the early 1990s. The site is now a housing estate and Woodhead's exists in name only and is run from an industrial estate in Leeds. 
There is however, a large old 'mill type' building situated on Church Street, which, prior to its refurbishment, had shown Woodhead signage in large blue lettering on the buildings' facade. 
The building was for many years left in a very derelict and dangerous state, largely due to vandalism. Arson in particular inflicted significant damage, leaving the buildings' roof black and charred. 
Building work was attempted many times until the building was eventually renovated and converted into flats in 2016.
The yard and building has a large stone wall and locked iron gates to the front, which edges right up to the pavement on Church Street, and high metal fencing to the rear, which edges up to a grassed area next to the large housing estate. 
The housing estate situated at the rear, is fairly large and has a selection of mixed style and sized modern houses and four-storey flats, occupied by singletons, couples and families.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_World_War">Second World War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Second World War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, Ossett was accidentally <a href="/wiki/Strategic_bombing_during_World_War_II" title="Strategic bombing during World War II">bombed</a> on 16 September 1940. Ten <a href="/wiki/Explosive_material" class="mw-redirect" title="Explosive material">High Explosive</a> bombs were dropped. No one was killed, save for a number of chickens and several properties were damaged. Later in the war a <a href="/wiki/V-1_flying_bomb" title="V-1 flying bomb">V-1's</a> engine was reportedly heard to cut out, and came down at Grange Moor, to the west of the town.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spa">Spa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Spa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ossett was, for a brief period in the 19th century, a <a href="/wiki/Spa_town" title="Spa town">spa town</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Having been founded by a local stonemason who was inspired by <a href="/wiki/Harrogate" title="Harrogate">Harrogate</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham" title="Cheltenham">Cheltenham</a>, the waters were popular with those seeking relief from certain skin diseases in the early 19th century, but it remained a small spa during this period. In the 1870s, a plan to transform Ossett into a "second Harrogate" ended in failure, and the spa closed as a result.  The south-east of the town is still known as "Ossett Spa".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ossett_coatofarms2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5c/Ossett_coatofarms2.jpg/170px-Ossett_coatofarms2.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="182" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/5c/Ossett_coatofarms2.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="200" data-file-height="214" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ossett_coatofarms2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Although not granted by the <a href="/wiki/College_of_Arms" title="College of Arms">College of Arms</a>, this icon was adopted for the former <a href="/wiki/Municipal_Borough_of_Ossett" class="mw-redirect" title="Municipal Borough of Ossett">Municipal Borough of Ossett</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Ossett cum Gawthorpe was a township in the ancient parish of <a href="/wiki/Dewsbury" title="Dewsbury">Dewsbury</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> it became a <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> in 1866, and was incorporated as the <a href="/wiki/Municipal_borough" title="Municipal borough">Municipal Borough of Ossett</a> in 1890.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Under the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a>, it became an <a href="/wiki/Unparished_area" title="Unparished area">unparished area</a> in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Wakefield" title="City of Wakefield">City of Wakefield</a>. In an earlier draft of the Act, Ossett was to be part of the <a href="/wiki/Kirklees" title="Kirklees">Kirklees</a> district on the grounds that the area was originally part of Dewsbury; after an appeal by the Ossett Labour Party, it was decided Ossett would be part of the Wakefield district.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ossett has changed its parliamentary constituency several times. In 1983, the town transferred from the Dewsbury seat to the <a href="/wiki/Normanton_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Normanton (UK Parliament constituency)">Normanton</a> constituency. The seat has been continually represented by <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> since 1885: longer than any other British <a href="/wiki/Constituency" class="mw-redirect" title="Constituency">constituency</a>.
</p><p>Since the 2010 election, Ossett has been part of the <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Wakefield (UK Parliament constituency)">Wakefield seat</a>, held by <a href="/wiki/Mary_Creagh" title="Mary Creagh">Mary Creagh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> When Ossett was part of the Dewsbury constituency, the MP was <a href="/wiki/David_Ginsburg" title="David Ginsburg">David Ginsburg</a>, one of the Labour MPs who defected to the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_(UK)" title="Social Democratic Party (UK)">Social Democratic Party</a>. On transferring to the Normanton constituency, the MP for many years was Bill O'Brien until he entered the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> and was succeeded by Ed Balls.
</p><p>Most of the town is in the <i>Ossett</i> ward on the local council, but the south-eastern part of the town is in the <i>Horbury and South Ossett</i> ward.  The <i>Ossett</i> ward is extremely marginal, and has been won over the last ten years by Labour, Liberal Democrat, Conservative and UKIP candidates.  As of 24 May 2014, the ward is represented by one councillor each from Labour, Conservative and UKIP.  <i>Horbury and South Ossett</i> is less of a marginal seat and is represented by three Labour concillors as of 24 May 2014, although it has elected Conservatives in some past elections.
</p><p>There is a small area of Ossett (defined within the pre-1974 boundaries) in the <i>Wakefield West</i> ward, but this is mostly a business area with few residents (an industrial park was built on the site of the Old Roundwood Colliery).  There is also a very small area around the <i>Waggon and Horses</i> pub on Wakefield Road that has a WF5 Ossett address but is part of the <a href="/wiki/Kirklees" title="Kirklees">Kirklees</a> district and the <i>Dewsbury East</i> ward.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ossett experiences an <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">oceanic climate</a> (<a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6ppen_climate_classification" title="Köppen climate classification">Köppen climate classification</a> <i>Cfb</i>) similar to almost all of the United Kingdom.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demography">Demography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Demography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ossett_TownHall_Yorkshire.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Ossett_TownHall_Yorkshire.JPG/220px-Ossett_TownHall_Yorkshire.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Ossett_TownHall_Yorkshire.JPG/330px-Ossett_TownHall_Yorkshire.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Ossett_TownHall_Yorkshire.JPG/440px-Ossett_TownHall_Yorkshire.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ossett_TownHall_Yorkshire.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ossett town centre, showing the town hall building</div></div></div>
<p>At the 2001 census, the town's population was 20,988 residents plus an extra 88 in communal establishments, making a total of 21,076.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> As of 2007, West Yorkshire Police estimate the population at 21,284.<sup id="cite_ref-beatcrime.info_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beatcrime.info-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Ossett's convenient proximity to the M1 motorway has led the old industrial town to become more affluent in recent years, attracting both industry and resident commuters to <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, following on from a period of economic decline that lasted almost three decades. This however does leave Ossett with higher priced housing compared to nearby areas.
</p><p>In contrast to the above, <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Council" title="Wakefield Council">Wakefield Council</a> cites the population of Ossett in mid-2014 as standing at 16,414 made up of 8,012 males and 8,402 females with a 92.8% White British population.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ossett has some of the lowest crime rates in West Yorkshire.<sup id="cite_ref-beatcrime.info_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beatcrime.info-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are four operational textile mills in the town: Ings Mill, on Dale Street, deals in recycled textiles; Burmatex Ltd, based at Victoria Mills on the Green produce carpet tiles; Edward Clay &amp; Son Ltd, Wesley Street manufactures felts for the mattress making and horticultural industries and Wilson Briggs &amp; Son by the River Calder off Healey Road deals with textile mill waste and remnant processing. Other have been converted into units, some of the most prominent being Royd's Mill on the Leeds Road roundabout and the large congregation of mills in the <a href="/wiki/Healey,_Ossett" title="Healey, Ossett">Healey area</a>. Some mills remain derelict.
</p><p>Ossett is home to two <a href="/wiki/Real_ale" class="mw-redirect" title="Real ale">real ale</a> breweries. Ossett Brewery, located in <a href="/wiki/Healey,_Wakefield" class="mw-redirect" title="Healey, Wakefield">Healey</a><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and Bob's Brewing Company, formerly the Red Lion Brewery.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Landmarks">Landmarks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Landmarks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ossett_Town_Hall_2008.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ee/Ossett_Town_Hall_2008.jpg/220px-Ossett_Town_Hall_2008.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ee/Ossett_Town_Hall_2008.jpg/330px-Ossett_Town_Hall_2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/ee/Ossett_Town_Hall_2008.jpg/440px-Ossett_Town_Hall_2008.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ossett_Town_Hall_2008.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ossett Town Hall Square</div></div></div>
<p>Trinity Church was consecrated in 1865 and its spire which rises to 226 feet is a landmark that can be seen for miles around.
A red phone booth in Ossett town centre, opposite the Kingsway roundabout, is a grade II listed building.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
Ossett Town Hall celebrated its centenary in June 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gawthorpe,_Wakefield" title="Gawthorpe, Wakefield">Gawthorpe</a>, an area of north Ossett, is known for its landmark <a href="/wiki/Water_tower" title="Water tower">water tower</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Romans</a> constructed a road from <a href="/wiki/Halifax,_West_Yorkshire" title="Halifax, West Yorkshire">Halifax</a> to Wakefield, this road became a turnpike road in 1741, its route is roughly similar to the modern day Dewsbury Road. Streetside Post Office is a reminder of the Roman origins of the road. The <a href="/wiki/M1_motorway" title="M1 motorway">M1 motorway</a> between Junctions 40 and 42 to the east of Ossett was opened in April 1967. The stretch from junction 38 to 40 was opened in October 1968. The <a href="/wiki/Highways_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Highways Agency">Highways Agency</a> have plans to widen the M1 to 4 lanes between <a href="/wiki/Chesterfield" title="Chesterfield">Chesterfield</a> and Leeds. In 2004 a bus station was opened in the town built by the <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire_Passenger_Transport_Executive" class="mw-redirect" title="West Yorkshire Passenger Transport Executive">West Yorkshire Passenger Transport Executive</a> replacing an earlier bus station constructed in the 1970s.
</p><p>The railways arrived in Ossett in 1862 when the Bradford, Wakefield &amp; Leeds Railway company opened a branch line to Flushdyke. The line was extended to Ossett in 1864 and then onto Dewsbury and Batley.
Ossett railway station, located roughly where Southdale Gardens now is, was opened in 1889 by the Great Northern Railway. The line ran underneath Station Road and the bump in the road today is the only reminder of the bridge that used to exist there until its removal in the 1980s. The railway station closed in 1964. The town was close to four other railway stations: Chickenley Heath closed in 1911, Earlsheaton in 1953, Flushdyke closed in 1941 and <a href="/wiki/Horbury_and_Ossett_railway_station" title="Horbury and Ossett railway station">Horbury &amp; Ossett</a> in 1970. It is now the largest town in Yorkshire and one of the largest towns in Britain without a railway station. Railway sidings and yards are still to be found at the old Horbury &amp; Ossett railway station site and <a href="/wiki/Healey_Mills_Yard" class="mw-redirect" title="Healey Mills Yard">Healey Mills Marshalling Yard</a> where Queen Elizabeth II spent a night aboard the royal train during her 1977 Silver Jubilee tour.
</p><p>In June 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Train_Operating_Companies" class="mw-redirect" title="Association of Train Operating Companies">Association of Train Operating Companies</a> proposed Ossett, as one of seven English towns with a strong <a href="/wiki/Business_case" title="Business case">business case</a> for the location of a new railway station. It is likely that an unmanned station would be erected at Healey Mills.<sup id="cite_ref-ATOCConnectingCommunitiesReportS109_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATOCConnectingCommunitiesReportS109-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ossett has nine primary schools; Gawthorpe Community Academy, Ossett Flushdyke School, Towngate Primary Academy;<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Ossett Holy Trinity C of E Primary School, St Ignatius Catholic Primary School, Ossett South Parade Primary, South Ossett Infant Academy, Ossett Southdale C of E Junior School and Dimplewell Infants School and Nursery. Ossett has one mainstream secondary school, <a href="/wiki/Ossett_Academy" title="Ossett Academy">Ossett Academy &amp; Sixth Form College</a>, and then has The Grange School, which is an independent special school and Highfield School which caters for children aged 11 to 16 who have learning difficulties, using the buildings of the old North Ossett High School which closed in 1997.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ossett_Church_from_Dale_St.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Ossett_Church_from_Dale_St.jpg/220px-Ossett_Church_from_Dale_St.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Ossett_Church_from_Dale_St.jpg/330px-Ossett_Church_from_Dale_St.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Ossett_Church_from_Dale_St.jpg/440px-Ossett_Church_from_Dale_St.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ossett_Church_from_Dale_St.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Holy Trinity Church, Ossett, viewed from Dale Street</div></div></div>
<p>There are seven churches in the town, each with their own particular identities and initiatives. Many of the leaders of these churches meet regularly to collaborate and support each other. In the 18th and 19th centuries, the town had a reputation as a centre of religious <a href="/wiki/Nonconformism" class="mw-redirect" title="Nonconformism">Nonconformism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Although Nonconformist churches were common all over West Yorkshire, Ossett was a particular hotbed. In 1890, seventeen different churches were recorded in Ossett, excluding "spiritualist churches". Trinity Church is one of the two <a href="/wiki/Anglican" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> churches in the town.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The other is Christ Church, South Ossett. St Mary's Church on Dewsbury Road closed in 2002, and its parish was divided between Dewsbury (Chickenley) and Ossett and Gawthorpe (Gawthorpe).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ignatius_of_Loyola" title="Ignatius of Loyola">St. Ignatius</a> <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> was built in 1878. The <a href="/wiki/Salvation_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="Salvation Army">Salvation Army</a> is the only church in Gawthorpe. The Salvation Army building also acts as a community centre providing dinners for senior citizens &amp; two parent &amp; toddler groups. There is also a <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_Hall" title="Kingdom Hall">Kingdom Hall</a> of <a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah&#39;s Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a> on Ventnor Way, and a <a href="/wiki/Spiritualism" title="Spiritualism">spiritualist</a> church in the town centre.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ossett Rugby<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> are based at Ossett Cricket and Athletic Club and play at Southdale playing fields with two men’s and one senior ladies team.
</p><p>Ossett Trinity, the local rugby league club, resigned from the <a href="/wiki/Rugby_League_Conference" title="Rugby League Conference">Rugby League Conference</a> in 2006. Ossett Cricket Club also play at Dimplewells. The <a href="/wiki/Heavy_Woollen_District" title="Heavy Woollen District">Heavy Woollen District</a> has its own cricket association and its own cricket team. Residents of Ossett are eligible to play for the Heavy Woollen District team.
</p><p>Ossett hosts two semi-professional football teams, both currently playing in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Premier_League_Division_One_North" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Premier League Division One North">Northern Premier League Division One North</a>: <a href="/wiki/Ossett_Town_F.C." class="mw-redirect" title="Ossett Town F.C.">Ossett Town</a> play at Ingfield, and neighbours <a href="/wiki/Ossett_Albion_F.C." class="mw-redirect" title="Ossett Albion F.C.">Ossett Albion</a> play at Queen's Terrace, more commonly known as Dimplewells. In February 2018, the two clubs announced an agreement to merge under the name <a href="/wiki/Ossett_United_F.C." title="Ossett United F.C.">Ossett United</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>  For the remainder of the 2017-18 season, a season ticket at either club is valid for matches at either Ossett Albion or Ossett Town.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There was an Ossett Football Club in the 1890s, they played in the original West Yorkshire League, but the oldest current club in Ossett is Ossett Common Rovers, formed in 1910 and currently playing in the modern West Yorkshire League. Other clubs in Ossett include Ossett Wanderers, Ossett United and Ossett Panthers. Little Bull F.C., Ossett Two Brewers and AFC Two Brewers play in the Wakefield &amp; District League.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_and_the_Humber" title="Yorkshire and the Humber">Yorkshire and the Humber</a> branch of the Disability Sports Federation has its headquarters on the Longlands Industrial Estate in the town.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture_and_media">Culture and media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Culture and media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Express" title="Wakefield Express">Wakefield Express</a></i> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Dewsbury_Reporter" title="Dewsbury Reporter">Dewsbury Reporter</a></i>  report local news. The <i><a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Express" title="Wakefield Express">Wakefield Express</a></i> publishes an <i>Ossett Edition</i>, and also contains an Ossett and district section. Ossett has a free magazine <i>The Ossett Review</i><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> established in July 2005. <i>The Ossett Civic Trust</i> produce a quarterly newsletter <i>Ossett Times</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Gawthorpe hosts the annual World Coal-Carrying Championships<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> (Easter Monday) and an annual Maypole parade in May. Ossett Beercart takes place on the first weekend of June. Ossett Gala takes place in July. The turning on of the Christmas lights is another focal point for the community, along with the fire station's bonfire on the Friday evening nearest to 5 November. The Ossett Beer Festival takes place annually at the <i>Brewers' Pride</i> pub, Healey Road, Ossett over the August bank holiday weekend.
</p>
<ul><li>The town is mentioned in the song <i>It's Grim Up North</i> by <a href="/wiki/The_KLF" title="The KLF">The KLF</a>.</li>
<li>Ossett was defined as "wheeare the' black-leead t'tram lines" in both <i>A Yorkshireman's Dictionary</i> by Peter Wright and <i>The Yorkshire Dictionary</i> by Arnold Kellett, although neither book gives any explanation for this. One interpretation is that it was mocking the town's heavy pollution when it was industrialised. Another is that Ossett people were seen as fussy and pedantic.</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_people">Notable people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Notable people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Alumni plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png" decoding="async" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/60px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/80px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="48" data-file-height="48" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article's list of residents <b>may not follow Wikipedia's <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verifiability</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Notability_(people)" title="Wikipedia:Notability (people)">notability</a> policies</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by removing names that do not have independent <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Reliable sources">reliable sources</a> showing they are notable AND residents, or by incorporating the relevant publications into the body of the article through appropriate <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">citations</a>.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">May 2017</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<ul><li>The astronomer <a href="/wiki/Cyril_Jackson_(astronomer)" title="Cyril Jackson (astronomer)">Cyril Jackson</a> (1903–1988), who moved to South Africa, was born in Ossett, honoured the town when he named asteroid <a href="/wiki/1244_Deira" title="1244 Deira">1244 Deira</a>; the citation he submitted to the <a href="/wiki/IAU" class="mw-redirect" title="IAU">IAU</a> boils down to "Ancient name of Ossett, Yorkshire".<sup id="cite_ref-springer_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-springer-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> That is something of an exaggeration: the ancient Celtic <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Deira" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Deira">Kingdom of Deira</a> actually encompassed (at its height) most of <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Ingham" title="Benjamin Ingham">Benjamin Ingham</a> (1712–1772) founder of the Inghamite <a href="/wiki/Methodists" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodists">Methodists</a> was born in Ossett. He was educated at <a href="/wiki/Batley_Grammar_School" title="Batley Grammar School">Batley Grammar School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_College,_Oxford" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen&#39;s College, Oxford">Queen's College, Oxford</a>. He was ordained in 1735 and accompanied <a href="/wiki/John_Wesley" title="John Wesley">John</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wesley" title="Charles Wesley">Charles Wesley</a> as a missionary to the colony of Georgia in the USA. In 1737, after his return to Ossett, Ingham started to establish the Inghamite Methodists after being banned in 1739 from preaching in churches. By 1755 there were over eighty Inghamite congregations, mainly in <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>. A vestige of Ingham's Church still survives in the Lancashire/Yorkshire border area.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Eli_Marsden_Wilson" title="Eli Marsden Wilson">Eli Marsden Wilson</a>, A.R.E., A.R.C.E. (1877–1965) was a successful Ossett-born artist who had seventeen pictures exhibited at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Academy">Royal Academy</a>. After studying at Wakefield College of Art, he moved to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Art" title="Royal College of Art">Royal College of Art</a> in London where he became a pupil of Sir Frank Short. The first picture Wilson exhibited at the R.A. in 1905 was an etching of "Ossett Market" as it was in Victorian times. There is a copy of "Ossett Market" by E. M. Wilson on display in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Wakefield_Art_Gallery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Wakefield Art Gallery (page does not exist)">Wakefield Art Gallery</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Thomas_Tomlinson_Cussons" title="Thomas Tomlinson Cussons">Thomas Cussons</a> (chemist) first established the 'Cussons' personal care brand in Ossett. The initials of Thomas' eldest son John W. Cussons (1867–1922) can still be found on the wall of the original building, now the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Bank" title="Yorkshire Bank">Yorkshire Bank</a> on Station Road. Thomas's youngest son <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Tom_Cussons" title="Alexander Tom Cussons">Alex T. Cussons</a> (1875–1951) who was apprenticed in Ossett, went on to manufacture the famous Cussons <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Leather" title="Imperial Leather">Imperial Leather</a> soap.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Michael_Taylor_(Ossett)" class="mw-redirect" title="Michael Taylor (Ossett)">Michael Taylor</a>, born circa 1944 became notable in 1974 as a result of Ossett murder case.</li>
<li>Edward Clay, the borough's first mayor was a rag and <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_textile_manufacturing#M" title="Glossary of textile manufacturing">mungo</a> manufacturer. (The business still remains in Wesley Street).<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Sir <a href="/wiki/Edward_Luckhoo" title="Edward Luckhoo">Edward Luckhoo</a>, Governor General of Guyana, lived in Ossett in retirement, and is buried in the town.</li>
<li>The Mitchells of South Ossett were Farmers and Clothiers and in the late 18th and the 19th Century they had significant land ownerships on Ossett Low Common (the present day Teall Street/ South Parade area) and Ossett Lights (to the east of Teall Street and south of Manor Road).</li>
<li>Benjamin Scott born in 1719 was a cloth/ feltmakers. His mother was Sarah, née Ingham the daughter of Gervase Ingham a member of a well known land-owning Ossett family</li>
<li>Vincent Norman Wilson (1885–1974) ran a successful small business -V.N.Wilson and Co. Bedding &amp; Mattress manufacturer, at Prospect Works, then Intake Lane Ossett – employing 25+ people until the General Strike 1926 and the following recession sent him and many others out of business.</li>
<li>Ted Pickles, a local clown, gained some attention during the <a href="/wiki/UK_miners%27_strike_(1984-85)" class="mw-redirect" title="UK miners&#39; strike (1984-85)">miners' strike of 1984–5</a> for entertaining miners' children during the dispute.<sup id="cite_ref-Pickles_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickles-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> His work as a clown was a part-time job in addition to his work as a miner at Denby Grange Colliery in <a href="/wiki/Netherton,_Wakefield" title="Netherton, Wakefield">Netherton</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Pickles_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pickles-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> but he later appeared in some cinema and television.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> His house on the Green was renamed Ossett Clown Hall and exhibited a clock running backwards.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writers">Writers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ossett&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Writers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Novelist <a href="/wiki/Stan_Barstow" title="Stan Barstow">Stan Barstow</a>, 1928–2011, the author of <i><a href="/wiki/A_Kind_of_Loving" title="A Kind of Loving">A Kind of Loving</a></i>, was born in Horbury, yet lived almost all of his life in Ossett and attended Ossett Grammar School.</li>
<li>The crime novelist <a href="/wiki/David_Peace" title="David Peace">David Peace</a> b. 1967, originates from Ossett and set the first six of his books in the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding</a>. In <i>Nineteen Seventy Four</i>, Ed Dunford, the main character, lived at 10, Wesley Street in the town. There is also an insider joke where Ed tries to make up a fake name of a solicitors' firm to a policeman, and says "Edward Clay &amp; Son Ltd." <a href="/wiki/Ossett#Industry" title="Ossett">see above</a>, which the policeman immediately deduces as false.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Elaine_Storkey" title="Elaine Storkey">Elaine Storkey</a>, (née Lively): b.1944, broadcaster, writer and academic, was brought up in Ossett and wrote for the <i>Ossett Observer</i> as a child. She was Head Girl of Ossett Grammar School in 1962.</li>
<li>Matt Abbott, who is the frontman and lyricist in alternative pop act <a href="/wiki/Skint_%26_Demoralised" title="Skint &amp; Demoralised">Skint &amp; Demoralised</a>.</li>
<li>Portia da Costa (Wendy Wootton), erotic novelist grew up and currently lives in Ossett.</li></ul>

<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Black_Lace_(band)" class="mw-redirect" title="Black Lace (band)">Black Lace</a>, legendary British pop-music group, notable for their 1984 single "Agadoo".</li>

Bradley performed with his band at many prestigious occasions including on the <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_2" title="Queen Elizabeth 2">QE2</a>, <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> broadcasts and in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Voice_(film)" title="Little Voice (film)">Little Voice</a></i> leading the George Bradley Band and George Bradley Swing Band</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jill_Summers" title="Jill Summers">Jill Summers</a> (actress, known for playing<a href="/wiki/List_of_Coronation_Street_characters_(1982)#Phyllis_Pearce" title="List of Coronation Street characters (1982)">Phyllis Pearce</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Coronation_Street" title="Coronation Street">Coronation Street</a></i>) lived in the town</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Helen_Worth" title="Helen Worth">Helen Worth</a> (actress, known for playing <a href="/wiki/Gail_Platt" title="Gail Platt">Gail Platt</a> in <i>Coronation Street</i>) was born and brought up in Ossett</li></ul>
