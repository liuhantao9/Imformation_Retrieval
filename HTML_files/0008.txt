	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">CD single</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>CD single</b> (sometimes abbreviated to <b>CDS</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Single_(music)" title="Single (music)">music single</a> in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Compact_disc" title="Compact disc">compact disc</a>. The standard in the <a href="/wiki/Rainbow_Books" title="Rainbow Books">Red Book</a> for the term <i>CD single</i> is an 8cm (3 inch) CD (or <b>Mini CD</b>).<sup id="cite_ref-e-words.jp_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-e-words.jp-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It now refers to any single recorded onto a CD of any size, particularly the CD5, or 5-inch CD single. The format was introduced in the mid-1980s but did not gain its place in the market until the early 1990s. With the rise in digital downloads in the early 2010s, sales of CD singles have decreased.
</p><p>Commercially released CD singles can vary in length from two songs (an A side and B side, in the tradition of 7" 45rpm records) up to six songs like an <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">EP</a>. Some contain multiple mixes of one or more songs (known as <a href="/wiki/Remix" title="Remix">remixes</a>), in the tradition of <a href="/wiki/12-inch_single" class="mw-redirect" title="12-inch single">12" vinyl singles</a>, and in some cases, they may also contain a <a href="/wiki/Music_video" title="Music video">music video</a> for the single itself as well as a collectible <a href="/wiki/Poster" title="Poster">poster</a>. Depending on the nation, there may be limits on the number of songs and total length for sales to count in singles charts.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CD_single&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dire_Straits" title="Dire Straits">Dire Straits</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Brothers_in_Arms_(song)" title="Brothers in Arms (song)">Brothers in Arms</a>" (1985) is reported to have been the world's first CD single,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> issued in the UK in two separate singles as a promotional item, one distinguished with a logo for the tour, Live in '85, and a second to commemorate the Australian leg of the tour marked Live in '86. Containing four tracks, it had a very limited print run. The first commercially released CD Single was Angeline by <a href="/wiki/John_Martyn" title="John Martyn">John Martyn</a> released on 1 February 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> CD singles were first made eligible for the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a> in 1987, and the first number 1 available on the format in that country was "<a href="/wiki/I_Wanna_Dance_with_Somebody_(Who_Loves_Me)" title="I Wanna Dance with Somebody (Who Loves Me)">I Wanna Dance with Somebody (Who Loves Me)</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Whitney_Houston" title="Whitney Houston">Whitney Houston</a> in May 1987.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Mini_CD_single" class="mw-redirect" title="Mini CD single">Mini CD single</a> CD3 format was originally created for use for singles in the late 1980s, but met with limited success, particularly in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The smaller CDs were more successful in Japan<sup id="cite_ref-RSCDNews1989_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSCDNews1989-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and had a resurgence in Europe early this century, marketed as "Pock it" CDs, being small enough to fit in a shirt pocket. By 1989, the CD3 was in decline in the US (replaced by the 5-inch CD single, called CD-5).<sup id="cite_ref-RSCDNews1989_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSCDNews1989-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was common in the 1990s for US record companies to release both a two-track CD and a multi-track (usually "remix") maxi CD. In the UK, record companies would also release two CDs but, usually, these consisted of three tracks or more each.
</p><p>During the 1990s, CD single releases became less common in certain countries and were often released in smaller editions, as the major record labels feared they were cannibalizing the sales of higher-profit-margin CD albums. Pressure from record labels made singles charts in some countries become song charts, allowing album cuts to chart based only on airplay, without a single ever being released. In the US, the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100"><i>Billboard</i> Hot 100</a> made this change in December 1998, after which very few songs were released in the CD single format in the US, but they remained extremely popular in the UK and other countries, where charts were still based solely on single sales and not radio airplay. At the end of the 1990s, the CD was the biggest-selling single format in the UK, but in the US, the dominant single format was airplay. With the advent of digital music sales, the CD single has largely been replaced as a distribution format in most countries, and most charts now include digital download counts as well as physical single sales.
</p><p>In Australia, the <i><a href="/wiki/Herald_Sun" title="Herald Sun">Herald Sun</a></i> reported the CD single is "set to become extinct". In early July 2009, leading music store <a href="/wiki/JB_Hi-Fi" title="JB Hi-Fi">JB Hi-Fi</a> ceased stocking CD singles because of declining sales, with copies of the week's No. 1 single often selling as few as only 350 copies across all their stores nationwide.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> While CD singles no longer maintain their own section of the store, copies are still distributed but placed with the artist's albums. That is predominantly the case for popular Australian artists such as <a href="/wiki/Jessica_Mauboy" title="Jessica Mauboy">Jessica Mauboy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kylie_Minogue" title="Kylie Minogue">Kylie Minogue</a> and, most recently, <a href="/wiki/Delta_Goodrem" title="Delta Goodrem">Delta Goodrem</a>, whose then-recent singles ("<a href="/wiki/What_Happened_to_Us" title="What Happened to Us">What Happened to Us</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Put_Your_Hands_Up_(If_You_Feel_Love)" title="Put Your Hands Up (If You Feel Love)">Put Your Hands Up (If You Feel Love)</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Sitting_on_Top_of_the_World_(Delta_Goodrem_song)" title="Sitting on Top of the World (Delta Goodrem song)">Sitting on Top of the World</a>" respectively) were released on CD in limited quantities. The <a href="/wiki/ARIA_Charts" title="ARIA Charts">ARIA Singles Chart</a> is now "predominantly compiled from legal downloads", and <a href="/wiki/Australian_Recording_Industry_Association" title="Australian Recording Industry Association">ARIA</a> also stopped compiling their physical singles sales chart. "<a href="/wiki/On_a_Mission_(song)" title="On a Mission (song)">On a Mission</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Gabriella_Cilmi" title="Gabriella Cilmi">Gabriella Cilmi</a> was the last CD single to be stocked in <a href="/wiki/Kmart" title="Kmart">Kmart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Target_Australia" title="Target Australia">Target</a> and <a href="/wiki/Big_W" title="Big W">Big W</a>, who then concluded stocking newly released singles. <a href="/wiki/Sanity_(music_store)" title="Sanity (music store)">Sanity Entertainment</a>, having resisted the decline for longer than the other major outlets, has also ceased selling CD singles.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, CD single releases have been rare ever since the format was introduced, due of the amount of infringement and illegal file sharing over the internet, and most of the time singles have generally been album cuts chart based only on airplay, but with the advent of digital music the charts have also occasionally included digital download counts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, the term "CD single" is used to describe an <a href="/wiki/Extended_play" title="Extended play">extended play</a> (EP) in which there may be anywhere from three to six different tracks. These releases charted on the <a href="/wiki/IFPI_Greece" title="IFPI Greece">Greek Singles Chart</a> (before it abandoned tracking altogether) with songs released as <a href="/wiki/Single_(music)" title="Single (music)">singles</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Mini_CD">Mini CD</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CD_single&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mini CD">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="infobox" style="width:22em"><caption>Mini CD single</caption><tbody><tr><td colspan="2" style="text-align:center"><a href="/wiki/File:8cm_CD_single_jacket.jpg" class="image"><img alt="8cm CD single jacket.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/8cm_CD_single_jacket.jpg/220px-8cm_CD_single_jacket.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/8cm_CD_single_jacket.jpg/330px-8cm_CD_single_jacket.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/8cm_CD_single_jacket.jpg/440px-8cm_CD_single_jacket.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="533" /></a><div>Mini CD single</div></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Media type</th><td><a href="/wiki/Optical_disc" title="Optical disc">Optical disc</a></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Capacity</th><td>80 mm holds up to 24 minutes of music, or 210 MiB (210 × 2<sup>20</sup> bytes) of data.</td></tr><tr><th scope="row"><a href="/wiki/International_standard" title="International standard">Standard</a></th><td><a href="/wiki/Compact_Disc_Digital_Audio" title="Compact Disc Digital Audio">Red Book</a></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Dimensions</th><td>8 <a href="/wiki/Centimeter" class="mw-redirect" title="Centimeter">Centimeters</a> (3.1<a href="/wiki/Inch" title="Inch">Inch</a>)</td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Usage</th><td>Audio storage</td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Released</th><td>1987<sup id="cite_ref-auto_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The original <i>CD single</i> (sometimes <b>mini CD single</b> or <b>3-inch CD</b> or <b>CD3</b> in the US) is a <a href="/wiki/Music_single" class="mw-redirect" title="Music single">music single</a> released on a <a href="/wiki/Mini_CD" title="Mini CD">mini Compact Disc</a> that measures 8&#160;cm (3.1&#160;in) in diameter, rather than the standard 12&#160;cm (4.7&#160;in). They are manufactured using the same methods as standard full-size CDs, and can be played in most standard audio <a href="/wiki/CD_player" title="CD player">CD players</a> and <a href="/wiki/CD-ROM" title="CD-ROM">CD-ROM</a> <a href="/wiki/Disk_storage" title="Disk storage">disc drives</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The format was first released in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> in 1987 as the replacement for the 7-inch single.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> While mini CDs have fallen out of popularity among most major record labels (partly due to their incompatibility with many slot-loading CD players), they remain a popular, low cost way for independent musicians and groups to release music.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> 
Capable of holding up to 20 minutes of music, most mini CD singles contain at least two tracks, often consisting of a single edit and an <a href="/wiki/Instrumental" title="Instrumental">instrumental</a> version in the same way as 7-inch <a href="/wiki/Phonograph_record" title="Phonograph record">vinyl</a> singles.<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CD_single&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These were released in both a 5" size slim <a href="/wiki/Jewel_case" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewel case">jewel case</a> and long flip-out sleeve snap-packs or (<a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>: tanzaku) as they are known in Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> These sleeves could be 'snapped' and folded into a small 3" (8cm) square, rather than the original 6" by 3" inch (15×8&#160;cm) length when originally sold. 
</p><p>Beginning in 1999 some labels began to package mini CDs in 12cm slimline cases. As the "tanzaku" sleeves slowly morphed into the use of slimline jewel cases, the mini CD single was haulted from production in the early 2000s. 
</p><p>As popularity diminished, one of the last Japanese 8cm CD single released was a reissue and repackaging of "<a href="/wiki/I_Was_Born_to_Love_You_(song)" title="I Was Born to Love You (song)">I Was Born to Love You</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Queen_(band)" title="Queen (band)">Queen</a> in 2004. Many of the artists who released 3" CDs are from that era, including <a href="/wiki/Wink_(Japanese_band)" title="Wink (Japanese band)">Wink</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phil_Collins" title="Phil Collins">Phil Collins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Queen_(band)" title="Queen (band)">Queen</a>, <a href="/wiki/U2" title="U2">U2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metallica" title="Metallica">Metallica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bros" title="Bros">Bros</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huey_Lewis_and_the_News" title="Huey Lewis and the News">Huey Lewis and the News</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bon_Jovi" title="Bon Jovi">Bon Jovi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kylie_Minogue" title="Kylie Minogue">Kylie Minogue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Falco_(musician)" title="Falco (musician)">Falco</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Michael" title="George Michael">George Michael</a>. Some singles packaged in 5" single jewel cases contained a 5" CD adapter. 
</p><p>Most were sold at around <a href="/wiki/Yen" class="mw-redirect" title="Yen">¥</a>1,000 (<a href="/wiki/UK%C2%A3" class="mw-redirect" title="UK£">£</a>6 or <a href="/wiki/US$" class="mw-redirect" title="US$">$</a>9) at the time of release during the late 1980s, early 2000s to the present day. 
</p><p>However, there has been a small revival in the mini CD single with Japanese artists such as ベッド・イン（Bed In) releasing a 2017 single in long sleeve "tanzaku" form, by the record company <a href="/wiki/King_Records_(Japan)" title="King Records (Japan)">King Records</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CD_single&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>US versions were often packaged in cardboard slipcases, either 3" square or 6" by 3" gatefold. Others were released in 5" slimline single cases, which allowed an adapter to be included with the CD. At the time of first release in 1987, their retail price was between $4 and $6 at least $3 less than even the least-expensive 5-inch discs. <a href="/wiki/Delos_Productions" title="Delos Productions">Delos Records</a>, a small, independent label, issued the first commercially available 3-inch CDs in 1987 with 20 classical and jazz titles. The Massachusetts-based <a href="/wiki/Rykodisc" title="Rykodisc">Rykodisc</a> issued Frank Zappa’s “<a href="/wiki/Peaches_en_Regalia" title="Peaches en Regalia">Peaches en Regalia</a>” the first pop 3-inch CD. Initially when released, 300,000 of the discs were shipped to retail outlets.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CD_single&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>UK 3" CDs were made mostly in a small square case/cover form only, some including a 5" CD adapter to use in normal compact disc players. Although the format was not widely available in the United Kingdom, several artists have released singles in the format.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technology">Technology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CD_single&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Technology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From a technical standpoint, a 3-inch CD follows the <a href="/wiki/Red_Book_(audio_CD_standard)" class="mw-redirect" title="Red Book (audio CD standard)">Red Book</a> standard for CD digital audio. The major difference is that the smaller physical size of the disc allows for fewer data sectors, meaning the disc can store less audio. The majority of audio CD players and CD-ROM drives have a smaller circular indentation in the CD tray for holding these discs. Most slot-loading drives, such as those found in some car CD players, are unable to manipulate the smaller discs or their adapters. Laptop drives generally only require the centre hole to hold the disc so the smaller diameter is irrelevant. In fact, software and drivers for some hardware are provided on the 3" discs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Longevity">Longevity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CD_single&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Longevity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, the format barely lasted into the early 1990s, partly due to inconvenience of needing to attach an adapter on every disc (very few packages were issued with one) before playing. <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony</a> remained in support of the 3-inch CD having had plans to launch a 3-inch CD player for the Japanese market in 1988.<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> 
It was largely replaced by simply putting less music on a regular full-size CD, at least among major labels.  The full-size discs are a more standard manufacturing process and so may end up being cheaper to press. The CD single format continued until 2000 for <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> releases. Despite the unpopularity it survived with publishers adopting the 3-inch CD as an inexpensive way of presenting bonus material with books.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_specifications">Technical specifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=CD_single&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Technical specifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Data: 185-210MiB (versus 650-703MiB on a 12&#160;cm CD)</li>
<li>Audio: 21–24 minutes (versus 74–80 minutes on a 12&#160;cm CD)<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The slim <a href="/wiki/CD_and_DVD_packaging" class="mw-redirect" title="CD and DVD packaging">jewel boxes</a> used for 3" CDs are nearly the same size as 3.5" <a href="/wiki/Floppy_disk" title="Floppy disk">floppy disks</a>, making storage boxes for 3.5" floppies usable for 3" CDs as well.
</p>

<p>In September 2003, there was talk of ringtones for mobile phones outstripping CD singles sales for the year 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Woolworths_Group" title="Woolworths Group">Woolworths Group</a>, which previously accounted for one third of all CD sales in the country, stopped selling CD singles in August 2008, citing the "terminal decline" of the format as customers moved to digital downloads as their preferred method of purchasing single tracks<sup id="cite_ref-Wallop_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wallop-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> (the Woolworths chain itself would collapse the following November).
</p><p>In July 2009, <i>The Guardian</i> reported that <a href="/wiki/Florence_%2B_The_Machine" class="mw-redirect" title="Florence + The Machine">Florence + The Machine</a>'s single '<a href="/wiki/Rabbit_Heart_(Raise_It_Up)" title="Rabbit Heart (Raise It Up)">Rabbit Heart (Raise It Up)</a>' sold a CD and 7" vinyl combined total of 64 copies, where it reached No. 16 in the Mid-Week Chart.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In March 2011, <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Records" title="Mercury Records">Mercury Records</a> announced that they were to stop manufacturing CD singles for lack of demand and loss of money on the format in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 2012, selected <a href="/wiki/HMV" title="HMV">HMV</a> stores sold a small selection of CD singles, mostly charity releases and acts from <i><a href="/wiki/The_X_Factor_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="The X Factor (UK)">The X Factor</a></i>.
</p>
