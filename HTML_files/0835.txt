<p>The <b>inauguration of John F. Kennedy</b> as the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Presidents_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Presidents of the United States">35th</a> <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> was held on Friday, January 20, 1961 at the eastern portico of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">United States Capitol</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>. The <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_inauguration" title="United States presidential inauguration">inauguration</a> marked the commencement of <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>'s only term as President and of <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a>'s only term as <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President</a>. Kennedy was <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="Assassination of John F. Kennedy">assassinated</a> 2&#160;years, 306&#160;days into this term, and Johnson succeeded to the presidency.
</p><p>Kennedy took office following the November 1960 <a href="/wiki/1960_United_States_presidential_election" title="1960 United States presidential election">presidential election</a>, in which he narrowly defeated <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>, the then–<a href="/wiki/Incumbent" title="Incumbent">incumbent</a> Vice President. He was the first <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> to become President, and became the youngest person elected to the office.
</p><p>His inaugural address encompassed the major themes of his <a href="/wiki/Elections_in_the_United_States" title="Elections in the United States">campaign</a> and would define his <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_the_presidency_of_John_F._Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Timeline of the presidency of John F. Kennedy">presidency</a> during a time of economic prosperity, emerging social changes, and diplomatic challenges.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> This inauguration was the first in which a poet, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Frost" title="Robert Frost">Robert Frost</a>, participated in the program.
</p><p>Presidential inaugurations are organized by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress_Joint_Committee_on_Inaugural_Ceremonies" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Congress Joint Committee on Inaugural Ceremonies">Joint Committee on Inaugural Ceremonies</a>. For John F. Kennedy's inauguration, this <a href="/wiki/United_States_congressional_committee" title="United States congressional committee">committee</a> was chaired by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Sparkman" title="John Sparkman">John Sparkman</a>, and included Senators <a href="/wiki/Carl_Hayden" title="Carl Hayden">Carl Hayden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Styles_Bridges" title="Styles Bridges">Styles Bridges</a>, and <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">Representatives</a> <a href="/wiki/Sam_Rayburn" title="Sam Rayburn">Sam Rayburn</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_William_McCormack" class="mw-redirect" title="John William McCormack">John William McCormack</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Charles_A._Halleck" title="Charles A. Halleck">Charles A. Halleck</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sinatra_inaugural_ball">Sinatra inaugural ball</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inauguration_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sinatra inaugural ball">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:John_F._Kennedy_Inaugural_Ball,_20_January_1961.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/John_F._Kennedy_Inaugural_Ball%2C_20_January_1961.jpg/250px-John_F._Kennedy_Inaugural_Ball%2C_20_January_1961.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="197" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/John_F._Kennedy_Inaugural_Ball%2C_20_January_1961.jpg/375px-John_F._Kennedy_Inaugural_Ball%2C_20_January_1961.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/John_F._Kennedy_Inaugural_Ball%2C_20_January_1961.jpg/500px-John_F._Kennedy_Inaugural_Ball%2C_20_January_1961.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2962" data-file-height="2337" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:John_F._Kennedy_Inaugural_Ball,_20_January_1961.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> and First Lady <a href="/wiki/Jacqueline_Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacqueline Kennedy">Jacqueline Kennedy</a>, wearing a gown designed by Ethel Franken of <a href="/wiki/Bergdorf_Goodman" title="Bergdorf Goodman">Bergdorf Goodman</a>, arrive at Sinatra's inaugural ball on the evening of Inauguration Day.</div></div></div>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:22em;&#32;;"><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r887775652">.mw-parser-output .quotebox{background-color:#F9F9F9;border:1px solid #aaa;box-sizing:border-box;padding:10px;font-size:88%;max-width:100%}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft{margin:0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright{margin:0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.centered{margin:0.5em auto 0.8em auto}.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatleft p,.mw-parser-output .quotebox.floatright p{font-style:inherit}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-title{background-color:#F9F9F9;text-align:center;font-size:larger;font-weight:bold}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:before{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" “ ";vertical-align:-45%;line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox-quote.quoted:after{font-family:"Times New Roman",serif;font-weight:bold;font-size:large;color:gray;content:" ” ";line-height:0}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .left-aligned{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .right-aligned{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .quotebox .center-aligned{text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .quotebox cite{display:block;font-style:normal}@media screen and (max-width:360px){.mw-parser-output .quotebox{min-width:100%;margin:0 0 0.8em!important;float:none!important}}</style>
<div class="quotebox-quote left-aligned" style="">[Sinatra's ball] may have marked the moment when popular entertainment became an indispensable part of modern politics.</div>
<p><cite class="left-aligned" style="">--<a href="/wiki/Todd_Purdum" class="mw-redirect" title="Todd Purdum">Todd S. Purdum</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Vanity_Fair_(magazine)" title="Vanity Fair (magazine)">Vanity Fair</a></i>, Feb. 2011<sup id="cite_ref-purdum201102_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-purdum201102-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PopHistory_Gala_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PopHistory_Gala-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Frank_Sinatra" title="Frank Sinatra">Frank Sinatra</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peter_Lawford" title="Peter Lawford">Peter Lawford</a> organized and hosted a <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_inaugural_balls" title="United States presidential inaugural balls">pre-inaugural ball</a> at the <a href="/wiki/D.C._Armory" title="D.C. Armory">D.C. Armory</a> on the eve of Inauguration day, January 19, 1961, considered as one of the biggest parties ever held in Washington, D.C.<sup id="cite_ref-purdum201102_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-purdum201102-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PopHistory_Gala_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PopHistory_Gala-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Sinatra recruited many Hollywood stars who performed and attended, and went as far as convincing <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway theatres</a> to suspend their shows for the night to accommodate some of their actors attending the gala.<sup id="cite_ref-PopHistory_Gala_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PopHistory_Gala-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> With tickets ranging from $100 per person to $10,000 per group, Sinatra hoped to raise $1.7 million ($14.3&#160;million in today's dollars) for the Democratic Party to eliminate its debt brought on by a hard-fought campaign.<sup id="cite_ref-purdum201102_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-purdum201102-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PopHistory_Gala_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PopHistory_Gala-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Many Hollywood stars gave brief speeches or performed acts, rehearsed by <a href="/wiki/Kay_Thompson" title="Kay Thompson">Kay Thompson</a> and directed by <a href="/wiki/Roger_Edens" title="Roger Edens">Roger Edens</a>, and stayed at the <a href="/wiki/Capital_Hilton" title="Capital Hilton">Statler-Hilton Hotel</a> where preparations and rehearsals were photographed by <a href="/wiki/Phil_Stern" title="Phil Stern">Phil Stern</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PopHistory_Gala_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PopHistory_Gala-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Performances and speeches included <a href="/wiki/Fredric_March" title="Fredric March">Fredric March</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Poitier" title="Sidney Poitier">Sidney Poitier</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nat_King_Cole" title="Nat King Cole">Nat King Cole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ella_Fitzgerald" title="Ella Fitzgerald">Ella Fitzgerald</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gene_Kelly" title="Gene Kelly">Gene Kelly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tony_Curtis" title="Tony Curtis">Tony Curtis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Janet_Leigh" title="Janet Leigh">Janet Leigh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Dana_(comedian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bill Dana (comedian)">Bill Dana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milton_Berle" title="Milton Berle">Milton Berle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Durante" title="Jimmy Durante">Jimmy Durante</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harry_Belafonte" title="Harry Belafonte">Harry Belafonte</a>, and Sinatra himself.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sammy_Davis,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Sammy Davis, Jr.">Sammy Davis, Jr.</a>, a long-time friend of Sinatra, supporter of the Democratic Party, and member of the <a href="/wiki/Rat_Pack" title="Rat Pack">Rat Pack</a>, was asked by John F. Kennedy not to attend the gala at the behest of his father <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph Kennedy">Joseph</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-purdum201102_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-purdum201102-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> fearing that his <a href="/wiki/Interracial_marriage" title="Interracial marriage">interracial marriage</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> actress <a href="/wiki/May_Britt" title="May Britt">May Britt</a> was too controversial for the time and occasion, much to Sammy's and Sinatra's dismay.<sup id="cite_ref-purdum201102_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-purdum201102-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PopHistory_Gala_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PopHistory_Gala-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Davis had already postponed his wedding to Britt until after the election, also at the request of the Kennedy campaign via Sinatra.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Davis eventually switched his support to the Republican Party and Richard Nixon in the early 1970s. Harry Belafonte expressed sadness at the controversy, stating "It was the ambassador, [but] we didn't know that until after. Sammy not being there was a loss."<sup id="cite_ref-purdum201102_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-purdum201102-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the end of the ball, Kennedy spoke to thank Sinatra on the festivities and his support of the Democratic Party throughout his life and the 1960 campaign, adding "The happy relationship between the arts and politics which has characterized our long history I think reached culmination tonight."<sup id="cite_ref-PopHistory_Gala_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PopHistory_Gala-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Jacqueline retired to the White House before the ball ended at 1:30am (ET), and John went to a second pre-inaugural ball hosted by his father <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph Kennedy">Joseph Kennedy</a>, and would finally return to the White House at around 3:30am.<sup id="cite_ref-PopHistory_Gala_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PopHistory_Gala-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="The_inaugural_nor.27easter"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_inaugural_nor'easter">The inaugural nor'easter</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inauguration_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The inaugural nor&#039;easter">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/January_1961_nor%27easter" title="January 1961 nor&#39;easter">January 1961 nor'easter</a></div>
<p>A strong <a href="/wiki/January_1961_nor%27easter" title="January 1961 nor&#39;easter">nor'easter</a> fell the day before the inauguration, with temperatures at 20&#160;°F (−7&#160;°C) and snowfall at 1–2 inches (2.5–5.1&#160;cm) per hour<sup id="cite_ref-Inauguration_Weather_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Inauguration_Weather-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and a total of 8 inches (20&#160;cm) during the night,<sup id="cite_ref-NOAA_NWS_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_NWS-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> causing transportation and logistical problems in Washington and serious concern for the inauguration.<sup id="cite_ref-Inauguration_Weather_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Inauguration_Weather-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NOAA_NWS_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_NWS-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kocin_Uccellini_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kocin_Uccellini-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On inauguration day, January 20, 1961, the skies began to clear but the snow created chaos in Washington, almost canceling the inaugural parade.<sup id="cite_ref-Inauguration_Weather_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Inauguration_Weather-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers was put in charge of clearing the streets during the evening and morning before the inauguration, and were assisted by more than 1,000 District of Columbia employees and 1,700 <a href="/wiki/Boy_Scouts_of_America" title="Boy Scouts of America">Boy Scouts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Inauguration_Weather_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Inauguration_Weather-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> This task force employed hundreds of dump trucks, front-end loaders, sanders, plows, rotaries, and flamethrowers to clear the route.<sup id="cite_ref-Inauguration_Weather_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Inauguration_Weather-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Over 1,400 cars which had been stranded due to the conditions and lack of fuel had to be removed from the parade route along <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Avenue_(Washington,_D.C.)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pennsylvania Avenue (Washington, D.C.)">Pennsylvania Avenue</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Inauguration_Weather_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Inauguration_Weather-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The snowstorm dropped visibility at <a href="/wiki/Washington_National_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington National Airport">Washington National Airport</a> to less than half a mile,<sup id="cite_ref-Inauguration_Weather_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Inauguration_Weather-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> preventing former President <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Hoover" title="Herbert Hoover">Herbert Hoover</a> from flying into Washington and attending the inauguration.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Inauguration_proceedings">Inauguration proceedings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inauguration_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Inauguration proceedings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;"><a href="/wiki/File:President_Kennedy_inaugural_address_(color).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/President_Kennedy_inaugural_address_%28color%29.jpg/350px-President_Kennedy_inaugural_address_%28color%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="350" height="277" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/President_Kennedy_inaugural_address_%28color%29.jpg/525px-President_Kennedy_inaugural_address_%28color%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/President_Kennedy_inaugural_address_%28color%29.jpg/700px-President_Kennedy_inaugural_address_%28color%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1263" data-file-height="1000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:President_Kennedy_inaugural_address_(color).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View of the extended East Front of the Capitol where the inauguration was held. President Kennedy is in the center delivering his inaugural address, with Vice-President Johnson and official and invited guests sitting behind him.</div></div></div>
<p>Before the proceeding to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Capitol" title="United States Capitol">Capitol</a> in company with outgoing President <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a>, Kennedy went to a morning <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">Mass</a> at <a href="/wiki/Holy_Trinity_Catholic_Church_(Washington,_D.C.)" title="Holy Trinity Catholic Church (Washington, D.C.)">Holy Trinity Catholic Church</a> in <a href="/wiki/Georgetown,_Washington,_D.C." class="mw-redirect" title="Georgetown, Washington, D.C.">Georgetown</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-purdum201102_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-purdum201102-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Cardinal <a href="/wiki/Richard_Cushing" title="Richard Cushing">Richard Cushing</a> gave the <a href="/wiki/Prayers_at_United_States_presidential_inaugurations" title="Prayers at United States presidential inaugurations">invocation at the inaugural</a> which lasted for 12 minutes,<sup id="cite_ref-Newdow_v_Bush_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newdow_v_Bush-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> with additional prayers recited by <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Archdiocese_of_America" title="Greek Orthodox Archdiocese of America">Archbishop</a> <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_Iakovos_of_America" title="Archbishop Iakovos of America">Iakovos</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Church" title="Greek Orthodox Church">Greek Orthodox Church</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Church_(Disciples_of_Christ)" title="Christian Church (Disciples of Christ)">Reverend</a> Dr. John Barclay of the <a href="/wiki/Central_Christian_Church_(Austin,_Texas)" title="Central Christian Church (Austin, Texas)">Central Christian Church</a> of <a href="/wiki/Austin,_Texas" title="Austin, Texas">Austin, Texas</a>, and a <a href="/wiki/Berakhah" title="Berakhah">blessing</a> offered by <a href="/wiki/Rabbi" title="Rabbi">Rabbi</a> <a href="/wiki/Nelson_Glueck" title="Nelson Glueck">Nelson Glueck</a>. The invocation and prayers lasted a total of 28 minutes.<sup id="cite_ref-Newdow_v_Bush_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newdow_v_Bush-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Marian_Anderson" title="Marian Anderson">Marian Anderson</a> sang "<a href="/wiki/The_Star-Spangled_Banner" title="The Star-Spangled Banner">The Star-Spangled Banner</a>", and a composition by musical <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Bernstein" title="Leonard Bernstein">Leonard Bernstein</a> titled "Fanfare for the Inauguration of John F. Kennedy" was played.
</p><p>The oath of office for Vice President was administered by the <a href="/wiki/Speaker_of_the_United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="Speaker of the United States House of Representatives">Speaker of the House of Representatives</a> <a href="/wiki/Sam_Rayburn" title="Sam Rayburn">Sam Rayburn</a> to Lyndon Johnson.<sup id="cite_ref-Congress_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Congress-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> This marked the first time a Speaker administered the oath, which had been given in previous inaugurations by either the <a href="/wiki/President_pro_tempore_of_the_United_States_Senate" title="President pro tempore of the United States Senate">President pro tempore of the Senate</a>, the ex-Vice President, or a United States Senator.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Frost" title="Robert Frost">Robert Frost</a>, then 86 years old,<sup id="cite_ref-Smithsonian_Inaugural_Firsts_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smithsonian_Inaugural_Firsts-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Boston_50_anniversary_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boston_50_anniversary-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  recited his <a href="/wiki/Poem" class="mw-redirect" title="Poem">poem</a> "<a href="/wiki/The_Gift_Outright" title="The Gift Outright">The Gift Outright</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-tuten_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tuten-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Frost_1963_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Frost_1963-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy requested Frost to read a poem at the inauguration, suggesting "The Gift Outright",<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Frost_1963_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Frost_1963-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Frost_1961_poem_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Frost_1961_poem-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Poets.org_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Poets.org-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> considered an act of gratitude towards Frost for his help during the campaign.<sup id="cite_ref-Poets.org_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Poets.org-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy would later state that he admired the "courage, the towering skill and daring" of Frost, and adding that "I've never taken the view the world of politics and the world of poetry are so far apart. I think politicians and poets share at least one thing, and that is their greatness depends upon the courage with which they face the challenges of life."<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Frost_1963_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Frost_1963-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> American poet <a href="/wiki/William_Morris_Meredith,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="William Morris Meredith, Jr.">William Meredith</a> would say that the request "focused attention on Kennedy as a man of culture, as a man interested in culture."<sup id="cite_ref-Poets.org_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Poets.org-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="quotebox pullquote floatright" style="width:25em; border-width: 2px; background-color: Cornsilk;"><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r887775652"/>
<div class="quotebox-title" style="background-color: Cornsilk;"><u>For John F. Kennedy His Inauguration</u></div>
<div class="quotebox-quote center-aligned" style=""><div class="poem">
<p>The glory of a next Augustan age<br />
Of a power leading from its strength and pride,<br />
Of young ambition eager to be tried,<br />
Firm in our free beliefs without dismay,<br />
In any game the nations want to play.<br />
A golden age of poetry and power<br />
Of which this noonday's the beginning hour.
</p>
</div></div>
<p><cite class="right-aligned" style="">—Closing seven lines from Robert Frost's poem<br />"For John F. Kennedy His Inauguration",<br />the expanded version of "Dedication".<sup id="cite_ref-USA_Today_Frost_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USA_Today_Frost-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></cite>
</p>
</div>
<p>Frost composed a new poem titled <i>Dedication</i> specifically for the ceremony as a <a href="/wiki/Preface" title="Preface">preface</a> to the poem Kennedy suggested,<sup id="cite_ref-Boston_50_anniversary_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boston_50_anniversary-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Poets.org_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Poets.org-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> to the surprise of Kennedy's friends.<sup id="cite_ref-LOC_Udall_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LOC_Udall-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> On the morning of the inauguration, Frost asked <a href="/wiki/Stewart_Udall" title="Stewart Udall">Stewart Udall</a>, Kennedy's future <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_the_Interior" title="United States Secretary of the Interior">Secretary of the Interior</a>, to have his handwritten draft <a href="/wiki/Typewriter" title="Typewriter">type scripted</a> for easier reading, to which Udall obliged.<sup id="cite_ref-LOC_Udall_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LOC_Udall-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Once at the presidential podium, however, the glare of the sun and snow prevented him from reading his papers.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Frost_1963_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Frost_1963-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LOC_treasures_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LOC_treasures-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> When Frost started reading, he stumbled on the first three lines, squinting at his papers in view of the crowd and cameras.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Frost_1963_17-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Frost_1963-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Vice-President Johnson tried to assist by using his top hat as a shade, however Frost waved the offer aside, took the hat and jokingly said "I'll help you with that", sparking laughter and applause from the crowd and President Kennedy. Understanding the immediacy of the situation, Frost stated to the microphones that "this [the poem] was to have been a preface to a poem which I do not have to read",<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Frost_1961_poem_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Frost_1961_poem-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and began to recite "The Gift Outright" from memory.<sup id="cite_ref-Boston_50_anniversary_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boston_50_anniversary-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Frost_1963_17-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Frost_1963-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-LOC_treasures_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LOC_treasures-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> This marks the first time a poem was read at a Presidential inauguration, <a href="/wiki/Poems_at_United_States_presidential_inaugurations" class="mw-redirect" title="Poems at United States presidential inaugurations">a feature repeated</a> by future Presidents <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> at their respective ceremonies.<sup id="cite_ref-Smithsonian_Inaugural_Firsts_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smithsonian_Inaugural_Firsts-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Poems_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Poems-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Frost gave the type scripted version of the undelivered "Dedication" poem to Udall after the ceremony, who eventually donated the document to the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a> where it is stored today.<sup id="cite_ref-LOC_Udall_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LOC_Udall-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The original manuscript version, personally dedicated by Frost, was provided to the President and currently held by the <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_Presidential_Library" class="mw-redirect" title="John F. Kennedy Presidential Library">John F. Kennedy Presidential Library</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-USA_Today_Frost_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USA_Today_Frost-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-JFKlibrary_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JFKlibrary-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>  Kennedy's wife <a href="/wiki/Jacqueline_Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacqueline Kennedy">Jacqueline</a> <a href="/wiki/Picture_frame" title="Picture frame">framed</a> this manuscript version, writing on the back of the frame: <i>For Jack.  First thing I had framed to be put in your <a href="/wiki/Oval_office" class="mw-redirect" title="Oval office">office</a>. First thing to be hung there.</i><sup id="cite_ref-USA_Today_Frost_20-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USA_Today_Frost-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-JFKlibrary_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JFKlibrary-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Frost officially presented the poem, retitled to <i>For John F. Kennedy His Inauguration</i> and expanded from 42 to 77 lines, to Kennedy in March 1962.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_Frost_1963_17-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_Frost_1963-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The unread poem (published in 1962 as part of Frost's <i><a href="/wiki/In_the_Clearing" title="In the Clearing">In the Clearing</a></i> poetry collection) was finally recited at the U.S. Capitol by Chaplain Daniel P. Coughlin during the 50th anniversary celebrations of Kennedy's inauguration.<sup id="cite_ref-Boston_50_anniversary_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boston_50_anniversary-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oath_of_office">Oath of office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inauguration_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oath of office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Oath_of_office_of_the_President_of_the_United_States" title="Oath of office of the President of the United States">oath of office for the President</a> was administered by <a href="/wiki/Chief_Justice_of_the_United_States" title="Chief Justice of the United States">Chief Justice of the United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Earl_Warren" title="Earl Warren">Earl Warren</a> to Kennedy using a closed family <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> at 12:51 (<a href="/wiki/Eastern_Time_Zone_(North_America)" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Time Zone (North America)">ET</a>) although he officially became president at the stroke of noon.<sup id="cite_ref-Congress_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Congress-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-one_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-one-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy did not wear an overcoat when taking the oath of office and delivering the inaugural address, despite the cold conditions of 22&#160;°F (−6&#160;°C) with <a href="/wiki/Windchill" class="mw-redirect" title="Windchill">windchill</a> at 7&#160;°F (−14&#160;°C) at noon.<sup id="cite_ref-Inauguration_Weather_6-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Inauguration_Weather-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NOAA_NWS_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NOAA_NWS-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kennedy_Obama_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy_Obama-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Immediately after reciting the oath of office, President Kennedy turned to address the crowd gathered at the Capitol. His 1366-word<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> inaugural address, the first delivered to a televised audience in color,<sup id="cite_ref-Smithsonian_Inaugural_Firsts_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Smithsonian_Inaugural_Firsts-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> is considered among the best presidential inaugural speeches in American history.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Two_authors_ask_not_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Two_authors_ask_not-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>

</p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drafting">Drafting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inauguration_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Drafting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JFKInauguralInscriptionGravesite.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/JFKInauguralInscriptionGravesite.jpg/220px-JFKInauguralInscriptionGravesite.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/JFKInauguralInscriptionGravesite.jpg/330px-JFKInauguralInscriptionGravesite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/JFKInauguralInscriptionGravesite.jpg/440px-JFKInauguralInscriptionGravesite.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JFKInauguralInscriptionGravesite.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The most famous passage from the inaugural address is etched in stone at <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_Eternal_Flame" title="John F. Kennedy Eternal Flame">Kennedy's gravesite</a> in <a href="/wiki/Arlington_National_Cemetery" title="Arlington National Cemetery">Arlington National Cemetery</a>, with the <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Memorial" title="Lincoln Memorial">Lincoln Memorial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington_Monument" title="Washington Monument">Washington Monument</a> in the background.</div></div></div><p> The speech was crafted by Kennedy and his speech writer <a href="/wiki/Ted_Sorensen" title="Ted Sorensen">Ted Sorensen</a>. Kennedy had Sorensen study President <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Gettysburg_Address" title="Gettysburg Address">Gettysburg Address</a> as well as other inaugural speeches.<sup id="cite_ref-JFK_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JFK-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy began collecting thoughts and ideas for his inauguration speech in late November 1960. He took suggestions from various friends, aides and counselors, including suggestions from clergymen for biblical quotations. Kennedy then made several drafts using his own thoughts and some of those suggestions.<sup id="cite_ref-NARA_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NARA-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy included in his speech several suggestions made by Harvard economist <a href="/wiki/John_Kenneth_Galbraith" title="John Kenneth Galbraith">John Kenneth Galbraith</a> and by the former Democratic presidential candidate <a href="/wiki/Adlai_Stevenson_II" title="Adlai Stevenson II">Adlai Stevenson II</a>. Kennedy's line "Let us never negotiate out of fear. But let us never fear to negotiate." is nearly identical to Galbraith's suggestion "We shall never negotiate out of fear. But we shall never fear to negotiate." Stevenson's suggestion "if the free way of life doesn't help the many poor of this world it will never save the few rich." was the basis for Kennedy's line "If a free society cannot help the many who are poor, it cannot save the few who are rich."<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_ideas_of_the_speech">Main ideas of the speech</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inauguration_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Main ideas of the speech">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a president coming into power at the height of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, President Kennedy's duty of maintaining peaceful international relations with representing the United States as a force to be reckoned was daunting, at the very least. It is this overarching goal of his presidential term that dominates his inaugural address.  Kennedy highlights the newly discovered dangers of nuclear power coupled with the accelerating <a href="/wiki/Arms_race" title="Arms race">arms race</a>, and essentially makes the main point that this focus on pure firepower should be replaced with a focus on maintenance of international relations and helping the impoverished in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhetorical_elements">Rhetorical elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inauguration_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rhetorical elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The main focus of the speech can crudely be boiled down to one theme – the relationship between duty and power.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>  This is emphasized by Kennedy's strong use of juxtaposition in the first part of the speech. For example, he states in the second passage, "... Man holds in his mortal hands the power to abolish all forms of human poverty and all forms of human life," a clear calling-out of not only America, but also other nations of power for skewed Cold War priorities. He again employs the strategy in the fifth passage when he says, "United there is little we cannot do in a host of cooperative ventures. Divided there is little we can do," again appealing to the idea of refocusing of international values.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Again, after exhorting "both sides" to action, he calls on all of "us" "to bear the burden of a long twilight struggle ... against the common enemies of man: tyranny, poverty, disease, and war itself,"<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> though the phrase "long twilight struggle" came to be associated with the <a href="/wiki/Cold_war" class="mw-redirect" title="Cold war">cold war</a> struggle against communism.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the main components of classical <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetoric</a>, to prepon (the appropriate), is also extremely prevalent in this address.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Recognizing the fear and anxiety prevalent in the American people since the start of the Cold War, Kennedy geared his speech to have an optimistic and even idealistic tone as a means of providing comfort. He does this by quickly moving the time of the speech into the future, and invokes repetition of the phrase "Let both sides ..." to allude to how he plans to deal with strained relations while also appealing to the end goal of international unity. He also phrases negative ideas in a manner so as to present them as opportunities – a challenge, appealing to innately American ideals.  A great line to emphasize this is in the fourth from last passage, where he states, "In the long history of the world, only a few generations have been granted the role of defending freedom in its hour of maximum danger," a simple twist of words that challenges the American public rather than frightening them.
</p><p>It was also in his inaugural address that John F. Kennedy spoke his famous words, "ask not what your country can do for you, ask what you can do for your country." This use of <a href="/wiki/Chiasmus" title="Chiasmus">chiasmus</a> can be seen even as a thesis statement of his speech – a call to action for the public to do what is right for the greater good.  (This appears to be an elegant rephrasing of <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>'s acceptance speech at the <a href="/wiki/1936_Democratic_National_Convention" title="1936 Democratic National Convention">1936 Democratic National Convention</a>: <i>"To some generations much is given. Of other generations much is expected. This generation of Americans has a rendezvous with destiny."</i>) <sup id="cite_ref-fdrDNC1936_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fdrDNC1936-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Invited_guests">Invited guests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inauguration_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Invited guests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Along with official presidential guests and honorees, including former presidents, vice-presidents, cabinet members, and other Washington officials, the Kennedys invited famous men and women of the arts, including <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sandburg" title="Carl Sandburg">Carl Sandburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Steinbeck" title="John Steinbeck">John Steinbeck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hemingway" title="Ernest Hemingway">Ernest Hemingway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brendan_Behan" title="Brendan Behan">Brendan Behan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Rothko" title="Mark Rothko">Mark Rothko</a>, and fashion icon and future <i><a href="/wiki/Vogue_(magazine)" title="Vogue (magazine)">Vogue</a></i> editor <a href="/wiki/Diana_Vreeland" title="Diana Vreeland">Diana Vreeland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-purdum201102_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-purdum201102-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Congressman <a href="/wiki/Tip_O%27Neill" title="Tip O&#39;Neill">Tip O'Neill</a> sat next to wealthy Boston businessman George Kara:<sup id="cite_ref-purdum201102_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-purdum201102-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>O'Neill recalled that Kara had nudged him and said, "Years from now, historians will wonder what was on the young man's mind as he strode to take his oath of office. I bet he's asking himself how George Kara got such a good seat." That night, O'Neill and his wife danced over to the president's box at the ball in the Mayflower Hotel to congratulate him, and sure enough, Kennedy asked, "Was that George Kara sitting beside you?" O'Neill told Kennedy what Kara had said, and J.F.K replied, "Tip, you'll never believe it. I had my left hand on the Bible and my right hand in the air, and I was about to take the oath of office, and I said to myself, 'How the hell did Kara get that seat?'"
</p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Presidents_and_First_ladies">Presidents and First ladies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inauguration_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Presidents and First ladies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Five first Ladies, <a href="/wiki/Edith_Wilson" title="Edith Wilson">Edith Wilson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Roosevelt" title="Eleanor Roosevelt">Eleanor Roosevelt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bess_Truman" title="Bess Truman">Bess Truman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mamie_Eisenhower" title="Mamie Eisenhower">Mamie Eisenhower</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Jackie Kennedy">Jackie Kennedy</a> attended the event, as did future First Ladies <a href="/wiki/Lady_Bird_Johnson" title="Lady Bird Johnson">Lady Bird Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pat_Nixon" title="Pat Nixon">Pat Nixon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Betty_Ford" title="Betty Ford">Betty Ford</a>.
</p><p>Former President <a href="/wiki/Harry_S_Truman" class="mw-redirect" title="Harry S Truman">Harry S Truman</a> joined Presidents <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Eisenhower</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">Kennedy</a> on the platform, as did future Presidents <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_M._Nixon" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard M. Nixon">Richard M. Nixon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Ford" title="Gerald Ford">Gerald Ford</a>, making this, retroactively, the largest conclave of the "presidential fraternity" prior to the opening of the Reagan Library in the 1990s.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parade_to_the_White_House">Parade to the White House</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inauguration_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Parade to the White House">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A vast parade along <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania_Avenue" title="Pennsylvania Avenue">Pennsylvania Avenue</a> followed the inauguration ceremony, bearing the new President from Capitol Plaza to the White House. Upon his arrival, Kennedy mounted a reviewing stand shared with honored guests such as former President <a href="/wiki/Harry_Truman" class="mw-redirect" title="Harry Truman">Harry Truman</a> and former First Ladies <a href="/wiki/Edith_Wilson" title="Edith Wilson">Edith Wilson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Roosevelt" title="Eleanor Roosevelt">Eleanor Roosevelt</a>. Throngs of onlookers and millions of television viewers also watched the procession; it took three hours to pass by. Sixteen thousand members of the US armed forces marched with displays of modern weaponry like the <a href="/wiki/Minuteman_missile" class="mw-redirect" title="Minuteman missile">Minuteman missile</a> and the supersonic <a href="/wiki/B-70_bomber" class="mw-redirect" title="B-70 bomber">B-70 bomber</a>. A further sixteen thousand marchers were civilians ranging from federal and state officials to high school bands and <a href="/wiki/Boy_Scouts_of_America" title="Boy Scouts of America">Boy Scouts</a>, accompanied by forty <a href="/wiki/Float_(parade)" title="Float (parade)">floats</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inauguration_of_John_F._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kennedy's inauguration marked many firsts for the United States. Kennedy was the first, and to this date, the only Catholic inaugurated as commander-in-chief.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> At the inauguration, Kennedy, then 43, was the youngest elected president and was replacing the oldest president in American history at that time, Eisenhower.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> The age difference and visual impact of the turnover from Eisenhower's presence to Kennedy's was noticeable at the inauguration.<sup id="cite_ref-Kennedy_Obama_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kennedy_Obama-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ike_Phase_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ike_Phase-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>  In addition, Kennedy was the first person born in the 20th century to have been inaugurated as President.<sup id="cite_ref-New_Yorker_Frost_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Yorker_Frost-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The claim that Kennedy did not wear a hat to his inauguration, and so single-handedly killed the men's hat industry,<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-snopes_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snopes-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> is false.<sup id="cite_ref-snopes_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snopes-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dude_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dude-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy wore a <a href="/wiki/Top_hat" title="Top hat">top hat</a> to the inauguration and to the balls in the evening, removing it only to be sworn in and give his address. He in fact restored the tradition, after Eisenhower broke with it by wearing a <a href="/wiki/Homburg_(hat)" class="mw-redirect" title="Homburg (hat)">homburg</a> instead of a top hat to both of his inaugurations.<sup id="cite_ref-snopes_55-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snopes-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Johnson, at his inauguration in 1965, was the first President to go completely hatless.<sup id="cite_ref-snopes_55-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-snopes-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dude_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dude-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
