<p><b>Fireworks</b> are a class of <a href="/wiki/Low_explosive" class="mw-redirect" title="Low explosive">low explosive</a> <a href="/wiki/Pyrotechnics" title="Pyrotechnics">pyrotechnic</a> devices used for aesthetic and entertainment purposes. The most common use of a firework is as part of a fireworks display (also called a fireworks show or pyrotechnics), a display of the effects produced by firework devices. 
</p><p>Fireworks take many forms to produce the four primary effects: noise, light, smoke, and floating materials (<a href="/wiki/Confetti" title="Confetti">confetti</a> for example). They may be designed to burn with colored flames and sparks including red, orange, yellow, green, blue, purple, and silver. Displays are common throughout the world and are the focal point of many cultural and religious <a href="/wiki/Celebration_(party)" class="mw-redirect" title="Celebration (party)">celebrations</a>.
</p><p>Fireworks are generally classified as to where they perform, either as a ground or aerial firework. In the latter case they may provide their own <a href="/wiki/Air_propulsion" class="mw-redirect" title="Air propulsion">propulsion</a> (<a href="/wiki/Skyrocket" title="Skyrocket">skyrocket</a>) or be shot into the air by a <a href="/wiki/Mortar_(weapon)" title="Mortar (weapon)">mortar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Aerial_shell" class="mw-redirect" title="Aerial shell">aerial shell</a>).
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fuochi_d%27artificio.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Fuochi_d%27artificio.gif/220px-Fuochi_d%27artificio.gif" decoding="async" width="220" height="180" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Fuochi_d%27artificio.gif/330px-Fuochi_d%27artificio.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/93/Fuochi_d%27artificio.gif 2x" data-file-width="352" data-file-height="288" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fuochi_d%27artificio.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>New Year fireworks</div></div></div>
<p>The most common feature of fireworks is a <a href="/wiki/Paper" title="Paper">paper</a> or <a href="/wiki/Card_stock" title="Card stock">pasteboard</a> tube or casing filled with the <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustible</a> material, often <a href="/wiki/Pyrotechnic_stars" class="mw-redirect" title="Pyrotechnic stars">pyrotechnic stars</a>. A number of these tubes or cases are often combined so as to make when kindled, a great variety of sparkling shapes, often variously colored. A skyrocket is a common form of firework, although the first skyrockets were used in <a href="/wiki/Warfare" class="mw-redirect" title="Warfare">warfare</a>. The aerial shell, however, is the backbone of today's commercial aerial display, and a smaller version for consumer use is known as the festival ball in the United States. Such <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">rocket</a> technology has also been used for the <a href="/wiki/Rocket_mail" title="Rocket mail">delivery of mail by rocket</a> and is used as propulsion for most <a href="/wiki/Model_rocket" title="Model rocket">model rockets</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Fireworks were originally <a href="/wiki/List_of_Chinese_inventions" title="List of Chinese inventions">invented in China</a>. One of the cultural practices for fireworks was to scare away <a href="/wiki/Evil_spirit" class="mw-redirect" title="Evil spirit">evil spirits</a>. Cultural events and festivities such as the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_New_Year" title="Chinese New Year">Chinese New Year</a> and the Mid-Autumn <a href="/wiki/Moon_Festival" class="mw-redirect" title="Moon Festival">Moon Festival</a> were and still are times when fireworks are guaranteed sights. China is the largest manufacturer and exporter of fireworks in the world.
</p>

<p>The earliest fireworks came from China during the <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a> (960–1279).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Fireworks were used to accompany many festivities.<sup id="cite_ref-temple-r_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-temple-r-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The art and science of firework making has developed into an independent profession. In China, pyrotechnicians were respected for their knowledge of complex techniques in mounting firework displays.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Chinese people originally believed that the fireworks could expel evil <a href="/wiki/Spirit" title="Spirit">spirits</a> and bring about luck and happiness.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a> (202 BC – 220 AD), people threw bamboo stems into a fire to produce an explosion with a loud sound.<sup id="cite_ref-frcrk_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-frcrk-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In later times, gunpowder packed into small containers was used to mimic the sounds of burning bamboo.<sup id="cite_ref-frcrk_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-frcrk-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> These exploding bamboo stems and gunpowder <a href="/wiki/Firecrackers" class="mw-redirect" title="Firecrackers">firecrackers</a> were interchangeable known as <i>baozhu</i> (爆竹) or <i>baogan</i> (爆竿).<sup id="cite_ref-frcrk_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-frcrk-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> By the 12th and possibly the 11th century, the term <i>baozhang</i> (爆仗) was used to specifically refer to gunpowder firecrackers.<sup id="cite_ref-frcrk_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-frcrk-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song dynasty</a> (960–1279), many of the common people could purchase various kinds of fireworks from market vendors.<sup id="cite_ref-gernet1962_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gernet1962-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Grand displays of fireworks were also known to be held. In 1110, a large fireworks display in a martial demonstration was held to entertain <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Huizong_of_Song" title="Emperor Huizong of Song">Emperor Huizong of Song</a> (r. 1100–1125) and his court.<sup id="cite_ref-kelly2004_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kelly2004-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> A record from 1264 states that a rocket-propelled firework went off near the <a href="/wiki/Empress_Dowager" class="mw-redirect" title="Empress Dowager">Empress Dowager</a> Gong Sheng and startled her during a feast held in her honor by her son <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Lizong_of_Song" class="mw-redirect" title="Emperor Lizong of Song">Emperor Lizong of Song</a> (r. 1224–1264).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Rocket propulsion was common in warfare, as evidenced by the <i><a href="/wiki/Huolongjing" title="Huolongjing">Huolongjing</a></i> compiled by <a href="/wiki/Liu_Bowen" title="Liu Bowen">Liu Bowen</a> (1311–1375) and <a href="/wiki/Jiao_Yu" title="Jiao Yu">Jiao Yu</a> (fl. c. 1350–1412).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In 1240 the Arabs acquired knowledge of gunpowder and its uses from China. A Syrian named Hasan al-Rammah wrote of rockets, fireworks, and other incendiaries, using terms that suggested he derived his knowledge from Chinese sources, such as his references to fireworks as "Chinese flowers".<sup id="cite_ref-temple-r_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-temple-r-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-kellyj_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kellyj-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In regards to colored fireworks, this was derived and developed from earlier (possibly <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han</a> or soon thereafter) Chinese application of chemical substances to create colored smoke and fire.<sup id="cite_ref-needh-v7-color_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-needh-v7-color-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Such application appears in the <i><a href="/wiki/Huolongjing" title="Huolongjing">Huolongjing</a></i> (14th century) and <i><a href="/wiki/Wubeizhi" class="mw-redirect" title="Wubeizhi">Wubeizhi</a></i> (preface of 1621, printed 1628), which describes recipes, several of which used low-nitrate gunpowder, to create military signal smokes with various colors.<sup id="cite_ref-needh-v7-color_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-needh-v7-color-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In the <i>Wubei Huolongjing</i> (武備火龍經; <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming</a>, completed after 1628), two formulas appears for firework-like signals, the <i>sanzhangju</i> (三丈菊) and <i>baizhanglian</i> (百丈蓮), that produces silver sparkles in the smoke.<sup id="cite_ref-needh-v7-color_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-needh-v7-color-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In the <i>Huoxilüe</i> (火戲略; 1753) by Zhao Xuemin (趙學敏), there are several recipes with low-nitrate gunpowder and other chemical substances to tint flames and smoke.<sup id="cite_ref-needh-v7-color_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-needh-v7-color-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> These included, for instance, arsenical sulphide for yellow, copper acetate (verdigris) for green, lead carbonate for lilac-white, and mercurous chloride (calomel) for white.<sup id="cite_ref-needh-v7-color_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-needh-v7-color-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The Chinese pyrotechnics have been written about by foreign authors such as <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Caillot" title="Antoine Caillot">Antoine Caillot</a> (1818) who wrote "It is certain that the variety of colours which the Chinese have the secret of giving to flame is the greatest mystery of their fireworks." or <a href="/wiki/Sir_John_Barrow" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir John Barrow">Sir John Barrow</a> (ca. 1797) who wrote "The diversity of colours indeed with which the Chinese have the secret of cloathing fire seems to be the chief merit of their pyrotechny."<sup id="cite_ref-needh-v7-color_13-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-needh-v7-color-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Fireworks were produced in Europe by the 14th century, becoming popular by the 17th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ssec_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ssec-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-werret-181_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-werret-181-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Lev Izmailov, ambassador of <a href="/wiki/Peter_the_Great" title="Peter the Great">Peter the Great</a>, once reported from China: "They make such fireworks that no one in Europe has ever seen."<sup id="cite_ref-werret-181_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-werret-181-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> In 1758, the Jesuit missionary <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Nicolas_d%27Incarville" title="Pierre Nicolas d&#39;Incarville">Pierre Nicolas le Chéron d'Incarville</a>, living in <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>, wrote about the methods and composition on how to make many types of Chinese fireworks to the <a href="/wiki/Paris_Academy_of_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Paris Academy of Sciences">Paris Academy of Sciences</a>, which revealed and published the account five years later.<sup id="cite_ref-werret-183_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-werret-183-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-werret-183_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-werret-183-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Am%C3%A9d%C3%A9e-Fran%C3%A7ois_Fr%C3%A9zier" title="Amédée-François Frézier">Amédée-François Frézier</a> published his revised work <i>Traité des feux d'artice pour le spectacle</i> (Treatise on Fireworks) in 1747 (originally 1706),<sup id="cite_ref-werret-144_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-werret-144-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> covering the recreational and ceremonial uses of fireworks, rather than their military uses. <i><a href="/wiki/Music_for_the_Royal_Fireworks" title="Music for the Royal Fireworks">Music for the Royal Fireworks</a></i> was composed by <a href="/wiki/George_Frideric_Handel" title="George Frideric Handel">George Frideric Handel</a> in 1749 to celebrate the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Aix-la-Chapelle_(1748)" title="Treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle (1748)">Peace treaty of Aix-la-Chapelle</a>, which had been declared the previous year.
</p><p>"Prior to the nineteenth century and the advent of modern chemistry they [fireworks]] must have been relatively dull and unexciting."<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  Bertholet in 1786 discovered that oxidations with potassium chlorate resulted in a violet emission.  Subsequent developments revealed that oxidations with the chlorates of barium, strontium, copper, and sodium result in intense emission of bright colors. The isolation of metallic magnesium and aluminium marked another breakthrough as these metals burn with an intense silvery light.<sup id="cite_ref-Ullmann_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span></h2>
<p>Improper use of fireworks may be dangerous, both to the person operating them (risks of <a href="/wiki/Burn_(injury)" class="mw-redirect" title="Burn (injury)">burns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wound" title="Wound">wounds</a>) and to bystanders; in addition, they may start fires after landing on <a href="/wiki/Flammable" class="mw-redirect" title="Flammable">flammable</a> material. For this reason, the use of fireworks is generally legally restricted.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Naming_conventions_(geographic_names)" title="Wikipedia:Naming conventions (geographic names)"><span title="The geographic scope near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2017)">where?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Display fireworks are restricted by law<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Naming_conventions_(geographic_names)" title="Wikipedia:Naming conventions (geographic names)"><span title="The geographic scope near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2017)">where?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> for use by <a href="/wiki/Professionals" class="mw-redirect" title="Professionals">professionals</a>; consumer items, available to the public, are smaller versions containing limited amounts of explosive material to reduce potential danger.
</p><p>Fireworks are also a problem for animals, both domestic and wild, which can be frightened by their noise, leading to them running away, often into danger, or hurting themselves on fences or in other ways in an attempt to escape.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Competitions">Competitions</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fireworks_competitions" title="Fireworks competitions">Fireworks competitions</a></div>
<p>Pyrotechnical competitions involving fireworks are held in many countries. The most prestigious fireworks competition is the <a href="/wiki/L%27International_des_Feux_Loto-Qu%C3%A9bec" title="L&#39;International des Feux Loto-Québec">Montreal Fireworks Festival</a>, an annual competition held in Montreal, Quebec, Canada. Another magnificent competition is Le Festival d'Art Pyrotechnique held in the summer annually at the Bay of Cannes in <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te_d%27Azur" class="mw-redirect" title="Côte d&#39;Azur">Côte d'Azur</a>, France. The <a href="/wiki/World_Pyro_Olympics" class="mw-redirect" title="World Pyro Olympics">World Pyro Olympics</a> is an annual competition amongst the top fireworks companies in the world. It is held in Manila, Philippines. The event is one of the largest and most intense international fireworks competitions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Clubs">Clubs</span></h2>
<p>Enthusiasts in the United States have formed clubs which unite hobbyists and professionals. The groups provide safety instruction and organize meetings and private "shoots" at remote premises where members shoot commercial fireworks as well as fire pieces of their own manufacture. Clubs secure permission to fire items otherwise banned by state or local ordinances. Competition among members and between clubs, demonstrating everything from single shells to elaborate displays choreographed to music, are held.
One of the oldest clubs is Crackerjacks, Inc.,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> organized in 1976 in the Eastern Seaboard region of the U.S.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="PGI_annual_convention">PGI annual convention</span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Pyrotechnics_Guild_International" title="Pyrotechnics Guild International">Pyrotechnics Guild International</a>, Inc. or PGI,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> founded in 1969, is an independent worldwide nonprofit organization of amateur and professional fireworks enthusiasts. It is notable for its large number of members, around 3,500 in total. The PGI exists solely to further the safe usage and enjoyment of both professional grade and consumer grade fireworks while both advancing the art and craft of pyrotechnics and preserving its historical aspects. Each August the PGI conducts its annual week-long convention, where some the world's biggest and best fireworks displays occur. Vendors, competitors, and club members come from around the US and from various parts of the globe to enjoy the show and to help out at this all-volunteer event. Aside from the nightly firework shows, the competition is a highlight of the convention. This is a completely unique event where individual classes of hand-built fireworks are competitively judged, ranging from simple fireworks rockets to extremely
large and complex aerial shells. Some of the biggest, best, most intricate fireworks displays in the United States take place during the convention week.
</p><p>Amateur and professional members can come to the convention to purchase fireworks, paper goods, novelty items, non-explosive chemical components and much more at the PGI trade show. Before the nightly fireworks displays and competitions, club members have a chance to enjoy open shooting of any and all legal consumer or professional grade fireworks, as well as testing and display of hand-built fireworks. The week ends with the Grand Public Display on Friday night, which gives the chosen display company a chance to strut their stuff in front of some of the world's biggest fireworks aficionados. The stakes are high and much planning is put into the show. In 1994 a shell of 36 inches (910&#160;mm) in diameter was fired during the convention, more
than twice as large as the largest shell usually seen in the US, and shells as large as 24 inches (610&#160;mm) are frequently fired.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Halloween">Halloween</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Feu_d%27artifice_-_292.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Feu d&#39;artifice - 292.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Feu_d%27artifice_-_292.jpg/220px-Feu_d%27artifice_-_292.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Feu_d%27artifice_-_292.jpg/330px-Feu_d%27artifice_-_292.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Feu_d%27artifice_-_292.jpg/440px-Feu_d%27artifice_-_292.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2085" data-file-height="2085" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Feu_d%27artifice_-_292.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>
<dl><dt>Canada</dt></dl>
<p>Both fireworks and firecrackers are a popular tradition during Halloween in <a href="/wiki/Vancouver" title="Vancouver">Vancouver</a>, although apparently this is not the custom elsewhere in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Ireland</dt></dl>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> there are many fireworks displays, during the <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a> season. The largest are in the cities of <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derry" title="Derry">Derry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>. The 2010 Derry Halloween fireworks attracted an audience of over 20,000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The sale of fireworks is strongly restricted in the Republic of Ireland, though many illegal fireworks are sold throughout October or smuggled from Northern Ireland. In the Republic the maximum punishment for possessing fireworks without a licence, or lighting fireworks in a public place, is a €10,000 fine  and a five-year prison sentence.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>United States</dt></dl>
<p>Two firework displays on All Hallows' Eve in the United States are the annual "<a href="/wiki/HalloWishes" title="HalloWishes">Happy Hallowishes</a>" show at <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_World" title="Walt Disney World">Walt Disney World</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Magic_Kingdom" title="Magic Kingdom">Magic Kingdom</a> "<a href="/wiki/Mickey%27s_Not-So-Scary_Halloween_Party" title="Mickey&#39;s Not-So-Scary Halloween Party">Mickey's Not-So-Scary Halloween Party</a>" event, which began in 2005, and the "<a href="/wiki/Halloween_Screams" title="Halloween Screams">Halloween Screams</a>" at <a href="/wiki/Disneyland" title="Disneyland">Disneyland Park</a>, which began in 2009.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fireworks_celebrations_throughout_the_world">Fireworks celebrations throughout the world</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, fireworks are traditionally displayed on the eve of Bastille day (14 July) to commemorate the French revolution and the <a href="/wiki/Storming_of_the_Bastille" title="Storming of the Bastille">storming of the Bastille</a> on that same day in 1789. Every city in France lights up the sky for the occasion with a special mention to Paris that offers a spectacle around the <a href="/wiki/Eiffel_Tower" title="Eiffel Tower">Eiffel Tower</a>.
</p>

<p>During the summer in Japan, fireworks festivals<span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">花火大会</span><span style="display:none">,</span>&#32;<i>hanabi taikai</i>)</span> are held nearly every day someplace in the country, in total numbering more than 200 during August. The festivals consist of large fireworks shows, the largest of which use between 100,000 and 120,000 rounds (<a href="/wiki/Tondabayashi,_Osaka" title="Tondabayashi, Osaka">Tondabayashi, Osaka</a>), and can attract more than 800,000 spectators. Street vendors set up stalls to sell various drinks and staple Japanese food (such as <a href="/wiki/Yakisoba" title="Yakisoba">Yakisoba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Okonomiyaki" title="Okonomiyaki">Okonomiyaki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Takoyaki" title="Takoyaki">Takoyaki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kakig%C5%8Dri" title="Kakigōri">Kakigōri</a> (shaved ice), and traditionally held festival games, such as Kingyo-sukui, or <a href="/wiki/Goldfish_scooping" title="Goldfish scooping">Goldfish scooping</a>.
</p><p>Even today, men and women attend these events wearing the traditional <a href="/wiki/Yukata" title="Yukata">Yukata</a>, summer <a href="/wiki/Kimono" title="Kimono">Kimono</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Jinbei" title="Jinbei">Jinbei</a> (men only), collecting in large social circles of family or friends to sit picnic-like, eating and drinking, while watching the show.
</p><p>The first fireworks festival in Japan was held in 1733.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sumidagawa_Fireworks_Festival" title="Sumidagawa Fireworks Festival">Sumidagawa Fireworks Festival</a> is one of the many being celebrated annually throughout Japan in summer.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Malta">Malta</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Malta_-_St._Paul%27s_Bay_-_Malta_International_Fireworks_Festival_09_ies.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Malta_-_St._Paul%27s_Bay_-_Malta_International_Fireworks_Festival_09_ies.jpg/220px-Malta_-_St._Paul%27s_Bay_-_Malta_International_Fireworks_Festival_09_ies.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Malta_-_St._Paul%27s_Bay_-_Malta_International_Fireworks_Festival_09_ies.jpg/330px-Malta_-_St._Paul%27s_Bay_-_Malta_International_Fireworks_Festival_09_ies.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Malta_-_St._Paul%27s_Bay_-_Malta_International_Fireworks_Festival_09_ies.jpg/440px-Malta_-_St._Paul%27s_Bay_-_Malta_International_Fireworks_Festival_09_ies.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5616" data-file-height="3744" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Malta_-_St._Paul%27s_Bay_-_Malta_International_Fireworks_Festival_09_ies.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fireworks at a Maltese festival in 2014</div></div></div>
<p>Fireworks have been used in Malta for hundreds of years. When the islands were <a href="/wiki/History_of_Malta_under_the_Order_of_Saint_John" title="History of Malta under the Order of Saint John">ruled by the Order of St John</a>, fireworks were used on special occasions such as the election of a new <a href="/wiki/List_of_Grand_Masters_of_the_Knights_Hospitaller" title="List of Grand Masters of the Knights Hospitaller">Grand Master</a>, the appointment of a new <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a> or the birth of a prince.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Nowadays, fireworks are used in village feasts throughout the summer. The Malta International Fireworks Festival is also held annually.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monte-Carlo_International_Fireworks_Festival">Monte-Carlo International Fireworks Festival</span></h3>
<p>Pyrotechnics experts from around the world have competed in <a href="/wiki/Monte_Carlo" title="Monte Carlo">Monte Carlo</a>, Monaco since 1966.  The festival runs from July to August every year, and the winner returns in 18 November for the fireworks display on the night before the <a href="/wiki/National_Day_of_Monaco" title="National Day of Monaco">National Day of Monaco</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The event is held in <a href="/wiki/Port_Hercules" title="Port Hercules">Port Hercule</a>, beginning at around 9:30pm every night, depending on the sunset.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Singapore_Fireworks_Celebrations" title="Singapore Fireworks Celebrations">Singapore Fireworks Celebrations</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Singapore_Fireworks_Celebrations" title="Singapore Fireworks Celebrations">Singapore Fireworks Celebrations</a> (previously the Singapore Fireworks Festival) is an annual event held in <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> as part of its <a href="/wiki/National_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="National Day">National Day</a> celebrations. The festival features local and foreign teams which launch displays on different nights. While currently non-competitive in nature, the organizer has plans to introduce a competitive element in the future.
</p><p>The annual festival has grown in magnitude, from 4,000 rounds used in 2004, to 6,000 in 2005, to over 9,100 in 2006.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. </p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span></h3>
<p>America's earliest settlers brought their enthusiasm for fireworks to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. Fireworks and black ash were used to celebrate important events long before the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The very first celebration of <a href="/wiki/Independence_Day_(United_States)" title="Independence Day (United States)">Independence Day</a> was in 1777, six years before Americans knew whether or not the new nation would survive the war; fireworks were a part of all festivities.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In 1789, <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>'s inauguration was accompanied by a fireworks display.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This early fascination with fireworks' noise and color continues today.
</p><p>In 2004, <a href="/wiki/Disneyland_Park_(Anaheim)" class="mw-redirect" title="Disneyland Park (Anaheim)">Disneyland</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Anaheim,_California" title="Anaheim, California">Anaheim, California</a>, pioneered the commercial use of aerial fireworks launched with <a href="/wiki/Compressed_air" title="Compressed air">compressed air</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Gunpowder" title="Gunpowder">gunpowder</a>. The display shell explodes in the air using an electronic timer. The advantages of compressed air launch are a reduction in fumes, and much greater accuracy in height and timing.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Walt Disney Company">Walt Disney Company</a> is now the largest consumer of fireworks in the United States.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_other_than_public_displays">Uses other than public displays</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Consumer_fireworks" title="Consumer fireworks">Consumer fireworks</a></div>
<p>In addition to large public displays, people often buy small amounts of fireworks for their own celebrations. Fireworks on general sale are usually less powerful than professional fireworks. Types include firecrackers, rockets, cakes (multishot aerial fireworks) and smoke balls.
</p><p>Fireworks can also be used in an agricultural capacity as <a href="/wiki/Bird_scarer" title="Bird scarer">bird scarers</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pyrotechnic_compounds">Pyrotechnic compounds</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flametest--Cu.swn.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Flametest--Cu.swn.jpg/220px-Flametest--Cu.swn.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="367" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Flametest--Cu.swn.jpg/330px-Flametest--Cu.swn.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Flametest--Cu.swn.jpg/440px-Flametest--Cu.swn.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="2500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flametest--Cu.swn.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">Copper</a> compounds glow green or blue-green in a flame.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pyrotechnic_composition" title="Pyrotechnic composition">Pyrotechnic composition</a></div>
<p>Colors in fireworks are usually generated by <i><b><a href="/wiki/Pyrotechnic_star" title="Pyrotechnic star">pyrotechnic stars</a></b></i>—usually just called <i><b>stars</b></i>—which produce intense light when ignited. Stars contain five basic types of ingredients.
</p>
<ul><li>A <b>fuel</b></li>
<li>An <b>oxidizer</b>—a compound that combines with the fuel to produce intense heat</li>
<li><b>Color</b>-producing salts (when the fuel itself is not the colorant)</li>
<li>A <b>binder</b> which holds the pellet together.</li></ul>
<p>Some of the more common <a href="/wiki/Pyrotechnic_colorant" title="Pyrotechnic colorant">color-producing compounds</a> are tabulated here. The color of a compound in a firework will be the same as its color in a <a href="/wiki/Flame_test" title="Flame test">flame test</a> (shown at right). Not all compounds that produce a colored flame are appropriate for coloring fireworks, however. Ideal colorants will produce a pure, intense color when present in moderate concentration. 
</p><p>The color of <a href="/wiki/Spark_(fire)" title="Spark (fire)">sparks</a> is limited to red/orange, yellow/gold and white/silver. This is explained by light emission from an incandescent solid particle in contrast to the element-specific emission from the vapor phase of a flame.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Light emitted from a solid particle is defined by <a href="/wiki/Black-body_radiation" title="Black-body radiation">black-body radiation</a>. Low boiling metals can form  sparks with an intensively colored glowing shell surrounding the basic particle.<sup id="cite_ref-spark_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spark-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> This is caused by vapor phase combustion of the metal. 
</p>

</p><p>Many of the chemicals used in the manufacture of fireworks are non-toxic, while many more have some degree of toxicity, can cause skin sensitivity, or exist in dust form and are thereby inhalation hazards. Still others are poisons if directly ingested or inhaled.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_effects">Types of effects</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cake">Cake</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cake_(firework)" title="Cake (firework)">Cake (firework)</a></div>
<p>A cake is a cluster of individual tubes linked by <a href="/wiki/Fuse_(explosives)" title="Fuse (explosives)">fuse</a> that fires a series of aerial effects. Tube diameters can range in size from <span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span>–4 inches (6.4–101.6&#160;mm), and a single cake can have over 1,000 shots. The variety of effects within individual cakes is often such that they defy descriptive titles and are instead given cryptic names such as "Bermuda Triangle", "Pyro Glyphics", "Waco Wakeup", and "Poisonous Spider", to name a few. Others are simply quantities of 2.5–4&#160;in (64–102&#160;mm) shells fused together in single-shot tubes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Crossette">Crossette</span></h3>
<p>A shell containing several large stars that travel a short distance before breaking apart into smaller stars, creating a crisscrossing grid-like effect. Strictly speaking, a crossette star should split into 4 pieces which fly off symmetrically, making a cross. Once limited to silver or gold effects, colored crossettes such as red, green, or white are now very common.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chrysanthemum">Chrysanthemum</span></h3>
<p>A spherical break of colored stars, similar to a peony, but with stars that leave a visible trail of sparks.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dahlia">Dahlia</span></h3>
<p>Essentially the same as a peony shell, but with fewer and larger stars. These stars travel a longer-than-usual distance from the shell break before burning out. For instance, if a 3&#160;in (76&#160;mm) peony shell is made with a star size designed for a 6&#160;in (150&#160;mm)  shell, it is then considered a dahlia. Some dahlia shells are cylindrical rather than spherical to allow for larger stars.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diadem">Diadem</span></h3>
<p>A type of <b>Chrysanthemum</b> or <b>Peony</b>,  with a center cluster of non-moving stars, normally of a contrasting color or effect.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fish">Fish</span></h3>
<p>Inserts that propel themselves rapidly away from the shell burst, often resembling fish swimming away.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Horsetail">Horsetail</span></h3>
<p>Named for the shape of its break, this shell features heavy long-burning tailed stars that only travel a short distance from the shell burst before free-falling to the ground. Also known as a waterfall shell. Sometimes there is a glittering through the "waterfall".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kamuro">Kamuro</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Artilleryshells1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Artilleryshells1.jpg/220px-Artilleryshells1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Artilleryshells1.jpg/330px-Artilleryshells1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Artilleryshells1.jpg/440px-Artilleryshells1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2272" data-file-height="1704" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Artilleryshells1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A typical kamuro effect</div></div></div>
<p>Kamuro is a Japanese word meaning "boys haircut", which is what this shell resembles when fully exploded in the air. It is a dense burst of glittering silver or gold stars which leave a heavy glitter trail and shine bright in the night's sky.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mine">Mine</span></h3>
<p>A mine (aka. pot à feu) is a ground firework that expels stars and/or other garnitures into the sky. Shot from a mortar like a shell, a mine consists of a canister with the lift charge on the bottom with the effects placed on top. Mines can project small reports, serpents, small shells, as well as just stars. Although mines up to 12 inches (300&#160;mm) diameter appear on occasion, they are usually between 3–5 inches (76–127&#160;mm), in diameter.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Multi-break_shells">Multi-break shells</span></h3>
<p>A large shell containing several smaller shells of various sizes and types. The initial burst scatters the shells across the sky before they explode. Also called a bouquet shell. When a shell contains smaller shells of the same size and type, the effect is usually referred to as "Thousands". Very large bouquet shells (up to 48 inches (1,200&#160;mm)) are frequently used in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Noise-related_effects">Noise-related effects</span></h3>
<ul><li><b>Bang</b><br />The bang is the most common effect in fireworks and sounds like a gunshot; technically a "report".</li>
<li><b>Crackle</b><br />The firework produces a crackling sound.</li>
<li><b>Hummer</b><br />Tiny tube fireworks that are ejected into the air spinning with such force that they shred their outer coating, in doing so they whizz and hum.</li>
<li><b>Whistle</b><br />High pitched often very loud screaming and screeching created by the resonance of gas. This is caused by a very fast strobing (on/off burning stage) of the fuel. The rapid bursts of gas from the fuel vibrate the air many hundreds of times per second causing the familiar whistling sound. It is not, as is commonly thought, made in the conventional way that musical instruments are using specific tube shapes or apertures. Common whistle fuels contain Benzoate or Salicylate compounds and a suitable oxidizer such as Potassium Perchlorate.</li>
<li><b>Zilch</b><br />Zilches <i>epitomize</i> <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">onomatopoeia</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Palm">Palm</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tybee_island_georgia_july_4_fireworks.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Tybee_island_georgia_july_4_fireworks.jpg/220px-Tybee_island_georgia_july_4_fireworks.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="245" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Tybee_island_georgia_july_4_fireworks.jpg/330px-Tybee_island_georgia_july_4_fireworks.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Tybee_island_georgia_july_4_fireworks.jpg/440px-Tybee_island_georgia_july_4_fireworks.jpg 2x" data-file-width="522" data-file-height="581" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tybee_island_georgia_july_4_fireworks.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A collection of palm-shell fireworks illuminating the beach of <a href="/wiki/Tybee_Island,_Georgia" title="Tybee Island, Georgia">Tybee Island, Georgia</a></div></div></div>
<p>A shell containing a relatively few large <a href="/wiki/Comet_(pyrotechnics)" title="Comet (pyrotechnics)">comet</a> stars arranged in such a way as to burst with large arms or tendrils, producing a palm tree-like effect. Proper palm shells feature a thick rising tail that displays as the shell ascends, thereby simulating the tree trunk to further enhance the "palm tree" effect. One might also see a burst of color inside the palm burst (given by a small insert shell) to simulate coconuts.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Peony">Peony</span></h3>
<p>A spherical break of colored stars that burn without a tail effect. The peony is the most commonly seen shell type.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ring">Ring</span></h3>
<p>A shell with stars specially arranged so as to create a ring. Variations include smiley faces, hearts, and clovers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_candle">Roman candle</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Roman_candle_(firework)" title="Roman candle (firework)">Roman candle (firework)</a></div>
<p>A Roman candle is a long tube containing several large stars which fire at a regular interval. These are commonly arranged in fan shapes or crisscrossing shapes, at a closer proximity to the audience. Some larger Roman candles contain small shells (bombettes) rather than stars.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Salute">Salute</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Salute_(pyrotechnics)" title="Salute (pyrotechnics)">Salute (pyrotechnics)</a></div>
<p>A shell intended to produce a loud report rather than a visual effect. Salute shells usually contain <a href="/wiki/Flash_powder" title="Flash powder">flash powder</a>, producing a quick flash followed by a very loud report. Titanium may be added to the flash powder mix to produce a cloud of bright sparks around the flash. Salutes are commonly used in large quantities during finales to create intense noise and brightness. They are often cylindrical in shape to allow for a larger payload of flash powder, but ball shapes are common and cheaper as well. Salutes are also called <a href="/wiki/Maroon_(rocket)" title="Maroon (rocket)">Maroons</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spider">Spider</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Spider-Firework-Omiya-Japan.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Spider-Firework-Omiya-Japan.jpg/220px-Spider-Firework-Omiya-Japan.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c8/Spider-Firework-Omiya-Japan.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="240" data-file-height="320" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Spider-Firework-Omiya-Japan.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A typical spider effect</div></div></div>
<p>A shell containing a fast burning tailed or charcoal star that is burst very hard so that the stars travel in a straight and flat trajectory before slightly falling and burning out. This appears in the sky as a series of radial lines much like the legs of a spider.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Time_Rain">Time Rain</span></h3>
<p>An effect created by large, slow-burning stars within a shell that leave a trail of large glittering sparks behind and make a sizzling noise. The "time" refers to the fact that these stars burn away gradually, as opposed to the standard brocade "rain" effect where a large amount of glitter material is released at once.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Willow">Willow</span></h3>
<p>Similar to a <b>chrysanthemum</b>, but with long-burning silver or gold stars that produce a soft, dome-shaped weeping willow-like effect.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hazards_and_regulation">Hazards and regulation</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Launching_firework_rocket.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Launching_firework_rocket.jpg/220px-Launching_firework_rocket.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Launching_firework_rocket.jpg/330px-Launching_firework_rocket.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Launching_firework_rocket.jpg/440px-Launching_firework_rocket.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4608" data-file-height="3456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Launching_firework_rocket.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A firework rocket preparing its launch on the <a href="/wiki/Independence_Day_(United_States)" title="Independence Day (United States)">American Independence Day</a>.</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_2">Safety</span></h3>
<p>Fireworks pose risks of injury to people, and of damage, largely as a fire hazard.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pollution">Pollution</span></h3>
<p>Fireworks produce smoke and dust that may contain residues of <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metals" title="Heavy metals">heavy metals</a>, sulfur-coal compounds and some low concentration toxic chemicals. These by-products of fireworks combustion will vary depending on the mix of ingredients of a particular firework. (The color green, for instance, may be produced by adding the various compounds and salts of <a href="/wiki/Barium" title="Barium">barium</a>, some of which are toxic, and some of which are not.) Some fishers have noticed and reported to environmental authorities that firework residues can hurt fish and other water-life because some may contain toxic compounds such as <a href="/wiki/Antimony_sulfide" title="Antimony sulfide">antimony sulfide</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This is a subject of much debate due to the fact that large-scale pollution from other sources makes it difficult to measure the amount of pollution that comes specifically from fireworks. The possible toxicity of any fallout may also be affected by the amount of <a href="/wiki/Black_powder" class="mw-redirect" title="Black powder">black powder</a> used, type of oxidizer, colors produced and launch method.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Perchlorate" title="Perchlorate">Perchlorate</a> <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">salts</a>, when in solid form, dissolve and move rapidly in groundwater and surface water. Even in low concentrations in drinking water supplies, perchlorate ions are known to inhibit the uptake of iodine by the thyroid gland. As of 2010, there are no federal drinking water standards for perchlorates in the United States, but the
<a href="/wiki/US_Environmental_Protection_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="US Environmental Protection Agency">US Environmental Protection Agency</a> has studied the impacts of perchlorates on the environment as well as drinking water.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several US states have enacted drinking water standard for perchlorates, including Massachusetts in 2006.  California's legislature enacted AB 826, the Perchlorate Contamination Prevention Act of 2003, requiring California's Department of Toxic Substance Control (DTSC) to adopt regulations specifying best management practices for perchlorate-containing substances. The Perchlorate Best Management Practices were adopted on 31 December 2005 and became operative on 1 July 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>  California issued drinking water standards in 2007.  Several other states, including Arizona, Maryland, Nevada, New Mexico, New York, and Texas have established non-enforceable, advisory levels for perchlorates.
</p><p>The courts have also taken action with regard to perchlorate contamination.  For example, in 2003, a federal district court in California found that Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation and Liability Act (CERCLA) applied because perchlorate is ignitable and therefore a "characteristic" hazardous waste.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pollutants from fireworks raise concerns because of potential health risks associated with hazardous by-products. For most people the effects of exposure to low levels of toxins from many sources over long periods are unknown. For persons with <a href="/wiki/Asthma" title="Asthma">asthma</a> or multiple chemical sensitivity the smoke from fireworks may aggravate existing health problems.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Environmental pollution is also a concern because heavy metals and other chemicals from fireworks may contaminate water supplies and because fireworks combustion gases might contribute to such things as acid rain which can cause vegetation and even property damage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> However, gunpowder smoke and the solid residues are basic, and as such the net effect of fireworks on acid rain is debatable.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The carbon used in fireworks is produced from wood and does not lead to more carbon dioxide in the air.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> What is not disputed is that most consumer fireworks leave behind a considerable amount of solid debris, including both readily biodegradable components as well as nondegradable plastic items. Concerns over pollution, consumer safety, and debris have restricted the sale and use of consumer fireworks in many countries. Professional displays, on the other hand, remain popular around the world.
</p><p>Others argue that alleged concern over pollution from fireworks constitutes a red herring, since the amount of contamination from fireworks is minuscule in comparison to emissions from sources such as the burning of fossil fuels. In the US, some states and local governments restrict the use of fireworks in accordance with the <a href="/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States)" title="Clean Air Act (United States)">Clean Air Act</a> which allows laws relating to the prevention and control of outdoor air pollution to be enacted. Few governmental entities, by contrast, effectively limit pollution from burning fossil fuels such as diesel fuel or coal. Coal-fueled electricity generation alone is a much greater source of heavy metal contamination in the environment than fireworks.
</p><p>Some companies within the U.S. fireworks industry claim they are working with Chinese manufacturers to reduce and ultimately hope to eliminate of the pollutant perchlorate.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_regulations_around_the_world">Government regulations around the world</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, Type 1 fireworks are permitted to be sold to the public. For anything that has a large explosion or gets airborne, users need to register for a Type 2 Licence. On 24 August 2009 the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Capital_Territory" title="Australian Capital Territory">ACT</a> Government announced a complete ban on backyard fireworks.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">Northern Territory</a> allows fireworks to be sold to residents 18 years or older in the days leading up to Northern Territory Day (1 July) for personal purposes. The types of fireworks allowed for sale is restricted to quieter fireworks, which can only be used at the address provided to the seller.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Canada_Day_2016_Fireworks_(27929885402).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Canada_Day_2016_Fireworks_%2827929885402%29.jpg/220px-Canada_Day_2016_Fireworks_%2827929885402%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="330" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Canada_Day_2016_Fireworks_%2827929885402%29.jpg/330px-Canada_Day_2016_Fireworks_%2827929885402%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Canada_Day_2016_Fireworks_%2827929885402%29.jpg/440px-Canada_Day_2016_Fireworks_%2827929885402%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1424" data-file-height="2136" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Canada_Day_2016_Fireworks_(27929885402).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Canada_Day" title="Canada Day">Canada Day</a> 2016 firework show.</div></div></div>
<p>The use, storage and sale of commercial-grade fireworks in Canada is licensed by <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Canada" title="Natural Resources Canada">Natural Resources Canada</a>'s Explosive Regulatory Division (ERD). Unlike their consumer counterpart, commercial-grade fireworks function differently, and come in a wide range of sizes from 50&#160;mm (2&#160;inches) up to 300&#160;mm (12&#160;inches) or more in diameter. Commercial grade fireworks require a Fireworks Operator Certificate (FOC), obtained from the ERD by completing a one-day safety course. There are two different categories of FOC: one for pyrotechnics (those used on stage and in movies) and another for display fireworks (those used in dedicated fireworks shows). Each requires completion of its own course, though there are special categories of FOC which allow visiting operators to run their shows with the assistance of a Canadian supervisor.
</p><p>The display fireworks FOC has 2 levels: assistant (which allows you to work under a qualified supervisor until you are familiar with the basics), and fully licensed. A fully licensed display fireworks operator can also be further endorsed for marine launch, flying saucers, and other more technically demanding fireworks displays.
</p><p>The pyrotechnician FOC has 3 levels: pyrotechnician (which allows work under a supervisor), supervising pyrotechnician, and special effects pyrotechnician (which allows the fabrication of certain types of pyrotechnic devices). Additionally, a special effects pyrotechnician can be endorsed for the use of detonating cord.
</p><p>Since commercial-grade fireworks are shells which are loaded into separate mortars by hand, there is danger in every stage of the setup.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>  Setup of these fireworks involves the placement and securing of mortars on wooden or wire racks; loading of the shells; and if electronically firing, wiring and testing. The mortars are generally made of FRE (fiber-reinforced <a href="/wiki/Epoxy" title="Epoxy">epoxy</a>) or <a href="/wiki/HDPE" class="mw-redirect" title="HDPE">HDPE</a>
(high-density polyethelene). Older mortars made of sheet steel have been banned by most countries due to the problem of shrapnel produced during a misfire.
</p><p>Setup of mortars in Canada for an oblong firing site require that a mortar be configured at an angle of 10 to 15&#160;degrees down-range with a safety distance of at least 200&#160;meters down-range and 100&#160;meters surrounding the mortars, plus distance adjustments for wind speed and direction. In June 2007, the ERD approved circular firing sites for use with vertically fired mortars with a safety distance of at least 175&#160;meter radius, plus distance adjustments for wind speed and direction.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Loading of shells is a delicate process, and must be done with caution, and a loader must ensure not only the mortar is clean, but also make sure that no part of their body is directly over the mortar in case of a premature fire. Wiring the shells is a painstaking process; whether the shells are being fired manually or electronically, any "chain fusing" or wiring of electrical ignitors, care must be taken to prevent the fuse (an electrical match, often incorrectly called a <a href="/wiki/Squib_(explosive)" title="Squib (explosive)">squib</a>) from igniting. If the setup is wired electrically, the electrical matches are usually plugged into a "firing rail" or "breakout box" which runs back to the main firing board; from there, the Firing Board is simply hooked up to a car battery, and can proceed with firing the show when ready.
</p><p>Since commercial-grade fireworks are so much larger and more powerful, setup and firing crews are always under great pressure to ensure they safely set up, fire, and clean up after a show.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chile">Chile</span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>, the manufacture, importation, possession and use of fireworks is prohibited to unauthorized individuals; only certified firework companies can legally use fireworks. As they are considered a type of explosive, offenders can in principle be tried before military courts, though this is unusual in practice.
</p>

</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Belgium">Belgium</span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fireworks_policy_in_Belgium" title="Fireworks policy in Belgium">Fireworks policy in Belgium</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, each municipality can decide how to regulate fireworks. During <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Eve" title="New Year&#39;s Eve">New Year's Eve</a>, lighting fireworks without a licence is allowed in 35% of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_municipalities_of_the_Flemish_Region" title="List of municipalities of the Flemish Region">308 Flemish municipalities</a>, in around 50% a permit from the <a href="/wiki/Burgemeester" class="mw-redirect" title="Burgemeester">burgemeester</a> (<a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">mayor</a>) is required, and around 14% of municipalities have banned consumer fireworks altogether.<sup id="cite_ref-OVV_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OVV-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Finland">Finland</span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> those under 18 years old haven't been allowed to buy any fireworks since 2009. Safety goggles are required. The use of fireworks is generally allowed on the evening and night of New Year's Eve, 31 December. In some municipalities of Western Finland it is allowed to use fireworks without a fire station's permission on the last weekend of August. With the fire station's permission, fireworks can be used year-round.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, amateurs over 18 years old are allowed to buy and ignite fireworks of Category F2 for several hours on 31 December and 1 January; each German municipality is authorised to limit the number of hours this may last locally.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> The sale of Category F3 and F4 fireworks to consumers is prohibited.<sup id="cite_ref-OVV_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OVV-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Lighting fireworks is forbidden near churches, hospitals, retirement homes and wooden or thatch-roofed buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-OVV_46-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OVV-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> All major German cities organise professional fireworks shows.<sup id="cite_ref-OVV_46-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OVV-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fireworks_policy_in_the_Netherlands" title="Fireworks policy in the Netherlands">Fireworks policy in the Netherlands</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, fireworks cannot be sold to anyone under the age of 16. It may only be sold during a period of three days before a new year. If one of these days is a Sunday, that day is excluded from sale and sale may commence one day earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Republic_of_Ireland">Republic of Ireland</span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fireworks_policy_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Fireworks policy in the Republic of Ireland">Fireworks policy in the Republic of Ireland</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, fireworks are illegal and possession is punishable by huge fines and/or prison. However, around <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a> a large amount of fireworks are set off, due to the ease of being able to purchase from <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span></h4>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, fireworks can only be purchased and used by people 18 or older. <a href="/wiki/Fire_cracker" class="mw-redirect" title="Fire cracker">Fire crackers</a> used to be banned, but are now allowed under <a href="/wiki/Fireworks_policy_in_the_European_Union" title="Fireworks policy in the European Union">European Union fireworks policy</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom_2">United Kingdom</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jodrell_Bank_Live_2013_22.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Jodrell_Bank_Live_2013_22.jpg/220px-Jodrell_Bank_Live_2013_22.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Jodrell_Bank_Live_2013_22.jpg/330px-Jodrell_Bank_Live_2013_22.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/Jodrell_Bank_Live_2013_22.jpg/440px-Jodrell_Bank_Live_2013_22.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5184" data-file-height="3456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jodrell_Bank_Live_2013_22.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Firework display at the <a href="/wiki/Jodrell_Bank_Observatory" title="Jodrell Bank Observatory">Jodrell Bank Observatory</a> 2013.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fireworks_law_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Fireworks law in the United Kingdom">Fireworks law in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>Fireworks in the UK have become more strictly regulated since 1997. Since 2005, the law has been gradually harmonised in accordance with other EU member states' laws.
</p><p>Fireworks are mostly used in England, Scotland and Wales around <a href="/wiki/Diwali" title="Diwali">Diwali</a>, in late October or early November, and <a href="/wiki/Bonfire_Night" title="Bonfire Night">Guy Fawkes Night</a>, 5 November. In the UK, responsibility for the safety of firework displays is shared between the <a href="/wiki/Health_and_Safety_Executive" title="Health and Safety Executive">Health and Safety Executive</a>, fire brigades and local authorities. Currently, there is no national system of licensing for fireworks operators, but in order to purchase display fireworks, operators must have licensed explosives storage and public liability insurance.
</p><p>Fireworks cannot be sold to people under the age of 18 and are not permitted to be set off between 11pm and 7am with exceptions only for:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Guy_Fawkes_Night" title="Guy Fawkes Night">Bonfire Night</a> (5 November) (permitted until midnight)<sup id="cite_ref-Stat_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stat-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Chinese_New_Year" title="Chinese New Year">Chinese New Year</a> (permitted until 1am)<sup id="cite_ref-Stat_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stat-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Diwali" title="Diwali">Diwali</a> (permitted until 1am)<sup id="cite_ref-Stat_49-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stat-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/New_Year" title="New Year">New Year</a> (permitted until midnight <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Eve" title="New Year&#39;s Eve">New Year's Eve</a>, and continuing to be permitted until 1am)<sup id="cite_ref-Stat_49-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stat-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The maximum legal NEC (net explosive content) of a UK firework available to the public is two kilograms. Jumping jacks, strings of firecrackers, shell firing tubes, bangers and mini-rockets were all banned during the late 1990s. In 2004, single-shot air bombs and bottle rockets were banned, and rocket sizes were limited. From March 2008 any firework with over 5% flashpowder per tube has been classified 1.3G.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The aim of these measures was to eliminate "pocket money" fireworks, and to limit the disruptive effects of loud bangs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iceland">Iceland</span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, the Icelandic law states that anyone may purchase and use fireworks during a certain period around New Year's Eve. Most places that sell fireworks in Iceland make their own rules about age of buyers, usually it is around 16. The people of <a href="/wiki/Reykjav%C3%ADk" title="Reykjavík">Reykjavík</a> spend enormous sums of money on fireworks, most of which are fired as midnight approaches on 31 December. As a result, every New Year's Eve the city is lit up with fireworks displays.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span></h3>
<p>Fireworks in <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> are available from 2 to 5 November, around Guy Fawkes Day, and may be purchased only by those 18 years of age and older (up from 14 years pre-2007). Despite the restriction on when fireworks may be sold, there is no restriction regarding when fireworks may be used. The types of fireworks available to the public are multi-shot "cakes", Roman candles, single shot shooters, ground and wall spinners, fountains, cones, sparklers, and various novelties, such as smoke bombs and Pharaoh's serpents. Consumer fireworks are also not allowed to be louder than 90 decibels.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norway">Norway</span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, fireworks can only be purchased and used by people 18 or older. Sale is restricted to a few days before New Year's Eve. Rockets are not allowed.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_2">United States</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fireworks_policy_in_the_United_States" title="Fireworks policy in the United States">Fireworks policy in the United States</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the laws governing fireworks vary widely from state to state, or from county to county. Federal, state, and local authorities govern the use of display fireworks in the United States. At the federal level, the Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC) regulates consumer fireworks through the Federal Hazardous Substances Act (FHSA). The <a href="/wiki/National_Fire_Protection_Association" title="National Fire Protection Association">National Fire Protection Association</a> (NFPA) sets forth a set of codes which give the minimum standards of display fireworks use and safety in the US. Both state and local jurisdictions can further add restrictions on the use and safety requirements of display fireworks. There are currently 45 states in the United States in which fireworks are legal <sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>.
</p>
