<p><b>Dutch orthography</b> uses the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a> and has evolved to suit the needs of the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch language</a>. The spelling system is issued by government decree and is compulsory for all government documentation and educational establishments.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alphabet">Alphabet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_orthography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Alphabet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dutch_alphabet_(1560).gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Dutch_alphabet_%281560%29.gif/220px-Dutch_alphabet_%281560%29.gif" decoding="async" width="220" height="183" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Dutch_alphabet_%281560%29.gif/330px-Dutch_alphabet_%281560%29.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Dutch_alphabet_%281560%29.gif/440px-Dutch_alphabet_%281560%29.gif 2x" data-file-width="1008" data-file-height="838" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dutch_alphabet_(1560).gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Dutch alphabet in 1560, still including the <a href="/wiki/Long_s" title="Long s">long s</a></div></div></div>
<p>The modern Dutch alphabet consists of the 26 letters of the <a href="/wiki/ISO_basic_Latin_alphabet" title="ISO basic Latin alphabet">ISO basic Latin alphabet</a> and is used for the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch language</a>. Five (or six) letters are <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">vowels</a> and 21 (or 20) letters are <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">consonants</a>. The letter <i>E</i> is the most frequently used letter in the Dutch alphabet. The least frequently used letters are <i>Q</i> and <i>X</i>. In some aspects, the digraph <i><a href="/wiki/IJ_(digraph)" title="IJ (digraph)">IJ</a></i> behaves as a single letter.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Loanwords">Loanwords</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_orthography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Loanwords">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Loanwords often keep their original spellings: <i>cadeau</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/kaːˈdoː/</span> 'gift' (from French). The Latin letters <i>c</i>, <i>qu</i>, <i>x</i> and <i>y</i> (from Greek υ) are sometimes adapted to <i>k</i>, <i>kw</i>, <i>ks</i> and <i>i</i>. Greek letters φ and ῥ become <i>f</i> and <i>r</i>, not <i>ph</i> or <i>rh</i>, but θ mostly becomes <i>th</i> (except before a consonant, after <i>f</i> or <i>ch</i> and at the end of words). Combinations <i>-eon-, -ion-, -yon-</i> in loanwords from French are written with a single <i>n</i> (<i>mayonaise</i>) except when a schwa follows (<i>stationnement</i>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vowel_length">Vowel length</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_orthography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vowel length">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Vowel length is always indicated but in different ways by using an intricate system of single and double letters.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_overview">Historical overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_orthography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Historical overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Old_Dutch" title="Old Dutch">Old Dutch</a> possessed phonemic consonant length in addition to phonemic vowel length, with no correspondence between them. Thus, long vowels could appear in closed syllables, and short vowels could occur in open syllables. In the transition to early <a href="/wiki/Middle_Dutch" title="Middle Dutch">Middle Dutch</a>, short vowels were lengthened when they stood in open syllables. Short vowels could now occur only in closed syllables. Consonants could still be long in pronunciation and acted to close the preceding syllable. Therefore, any short vowel that was followed by a long consonant remained short.
</p><p>The spelling system used by early Middle Dutch scribes accounted for that by indicating the vowel length only when it was necessary (sometimes by writing a double vowel but also in other ways). As the length was implicit in open syllables, it was not indicated there, and only a single vowel was written. Long consonants were indicated usually by writing the consonant letter double, which meant that a short vowel was always followed by at least two consonant letters or by just one consonant at the end of a word.
</p><p>Later in Middle Dutch, the distinction between short and long consonants started to disappear. That made it possible for short vowels to appear in open syllables once again. Because there was no longer a phonetic distinction between single and double consonants (they were both pronounced short now), Dutch writers started to use double consonants to indicate that the preceding vowel was short even when the consonant had not been long in the past. That eventually led to the modern Dutch spelling system.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Checked_and_free_vowels">Checked and free vowels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_orthography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Checked and free vowels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Modern Dutch spelling still retains many of the details of the late Middle Dutch system. The distinction between <a href="/wiki/Checked_and_free_vowels" title="Checked and free vowels">checked and free vowels</a> is important in Dutch spelling. A checked vowel is one that is followed by a consonant in the same syllable (the syllable is closed) while a free vowel ends the syllable (the syllable is open). This distinction can apply to pronunciation or spelling independently, but a syllable that is checked in pronunciation will always be checked in spelling as well (except in some unassimilated loanwords).
</p>
<ul><li>Checked in neither: <i><b>la</b>-ten</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈlaː.tə(n)/</span> ("to leave", "to let")</li>
<li>Checked in spelling only: <i><b>lat</b>-ten</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈlɑ.tə(n)/</span> ("laths")</li>
<li>Checked in both: <i>lat</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/lɑt/</span> ("lath"), <i><b>lat</b>-je</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈlɑt.jə/</span> ("little lath")</li></ul>
<p>A single vowel that is checked in <b>neither</b> is always long/tense. A vowel that is checked in <b>both</b> is always short/lax. <p>Free &#x27e8;i&#x27e9; is fairly rare and is mostly confined to loanwords and names. As tense <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/y/</span> is rare except before <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/r/</span>, free &#x27e8;u&#x27e9; is likewise rare except before &#x27e8;r&#x27e9;.
</p><p>The same rule applies to word-final vowels, which are always long because they are not followed by any consonant (but see below on &#x27e8;e&#x27e9;). Short vowels, not followed by any consonant, do not normally exist in Dutch, and there is no normal way to indicate them in the spelling.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Double_vowels_and_consonants">Double vowels and consonants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_orthography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Double vowels and consonants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When a vowel is short/lax but is free in pronunciation, the spelling is made checked by writing the following consonant doubled, so that the vowel is kept short according to the default rules. That has no effect on pronunciation, as modern Dutch does not have long consonants:
</p>
<ul><li><i>ram-men</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈrɑ.mə(n)/</span> ("rams, to ram")</li>
<li><i>tel-len</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈtɛ.lə(n)/</span> ("to count")</li>
<li><i>tin-nen</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈtɪ.nə(n)/</span> ("made of tin")</li>
<li><i>kop-pen</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈkɔ.pə(n)/</span> ("cups, heads, to head [a ball]")</li>
<li><i>luk-ken</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈlʏ.kə(n)/</span> ("to succeed")</li></ul>
<p>When a vowel is long/tense but still checked in pronunciation, it is necessarily checked in spelling as well. A change is thus needed to indicate the length, which is done by writing the vowel doubled. Doubled &#x27e8;i&#x27e9; does not occur.
</p>
<ul><li><i>raam</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/raːm/</span> ("window"), <i>raam-de</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈraːm.də/</span> ("estimated")</li>
<li><i>teel</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/teːl/</span> ("cultivate"), <i>teel-de</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈteːl.də/</span> ("cultivated")</li>
<li><i>koop</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/koːp/</span> ("buy, sale"), <i>koop-sel</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈkoːp.səl/</span> ("something bought")</li>
<li><i>Luuk</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/lyk/</span> (a name)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_letter_e">The letter e</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_orthography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The letter e">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A single &#x27e8;e&#x27e9; indicates short and long e but is also used to indicate the neutral schwa sound <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ə/</span> in unstressed syllables. Because the schwa is always short, &#x27e8;e&#x27e9; is never followed by a double consonant when it represents <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ə/</span>.
</p>
<ul><li><i>ap-pe-len</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈɑ.pə.lə(n)/</span> ("apples")</li>
<li><i>ge-ko-men</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɣə.ˈkoː.mə(n)/</span> ("(has) come")</li>
<li><i>kin-de-ren</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈkɪn.də.rə(n)/</span> ("children")</li></ul>
<p>A word-final long <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/eː/</span> is written &#x27e8;ee&#x27e9; (or &#x27e8;é&#x27e9; in some loanwords), as an exception to the normal rules. That means that a word-final single &#x27e8;e&#x27e9; will always represent a schwa.
</p>
<ul><li><i>jee</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/jeː/</span> (expression of woe), <i>je</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/jə/</span> ("you")</li>
<li><i>mee</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/meː/</span> ("along, with"), <i>me</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/mə/</span> ("me")</li>
<li><i>wee</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʋeː/</span> ("contraction of the womb"), <i>we</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʋə/</span> ("we")</li></ul>
<p>Because the position of the stress in a polysyllabic word is not indicated in the spelling, that can lead to ambiguity. Some pairs of words are spelled the same, but &#x27e8;e&#x27e9; represents either stressed <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɛ/</span> or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/eː/</span> or unstressed <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ə/</span>, depending on how the stress is placed.
</p>
<ul><li><i>be-de-len</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈbeː.də.lə(n)/</span> ("to beg") or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/bə.ˈdeː.lə(n)/</span> ("to impart with, to grant")</li>
<li><i>ver-gaan-de</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈvɛr.ˌɣaːn.də/</span> ("far-going, far-reaching") or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/vər.ˈɣaːn.də/</span> ("perishing")</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphological_alternations">Morphological alternations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_orthography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Morphological alternations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The length of a vowel generally does not change in the pronunciation of different forms of a word. However, in different forms of a word, a syllable may alternate between checked and free depending on the syllable that follows. The spelling rules nonetheless follow the simplest representation, writing double letters only when necessary. Consequently, some forms of the same word may be written with single letters while others are written with double letters. That commonly occurs between the singular and plural of a noun or between the infinitive and the conjugated forms of verbs. Examples of alternations are shown below. Note that there are no examples with <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/i/</span> because free &#x27e8;i&#x27e9; does not occur in native words:

<p>There are some irregular nouns that change their vowel from short/lax in the singular to long/tense in the plural. Their spelling does not alternate between single and double letters. However, the sound <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɪ/</span> becomes <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/eː/</span> in the plural in such nouns, not <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/iː/</span> That is reflected in the spelling.
</p>
<ul><li><i>dag</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/dɑx/</span> ("day"), <i>da-gen</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈdaː.ɣə(n)/</span> ("days")</li>
<li><i>weg</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʋɛx/</span> ("road, way"), <i>we-gen</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈʋeː.ɣə(n)/</span> ("roads, ways")</li>
<li><i>schip</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/sxɪp/</span> ("ship"), <i>sche-pen</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈsxeː.pə(n)/</span> ("ships")</li>
<li><i>lot</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/lɔt/</span> ("lottery ticket"), <i>lo-ten</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈloː.tə(n)/</span> ("lottery tickets")</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Exceptions">Exceptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_orthography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Exceptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a rule, the simplest representation is always chosen. A double vowel is never written in an open syllable, and a double consonant is never written at the end of a word or when next to another consonant. A double vowel is rarely followed by a double consonant, as it could be simplified by writing them both single.
</p><p>The past tense of verbs may have a double vowel, followed by a double consonant, to distinguish those forms from the present tense.
</p>
<ul><li><i>ha-ten</i> ("hate"), <i>haat-ten</i> ("hated"), both <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈɦaː.tə(n)/</span></li>
<li><i>ra-den</i> ("guess"), <i>raad-den</i> ("guessed"), both <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈraː.də(n)/</span></li></ul>
<p>Compounds should be read as if each word were spelled separately, and they may therefore appear to violate the normal rules. That may sometimes cause confusion if the word is not known to be a compound.
</p>
<ul><li><i>dag-ar-bei-der</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈdɑx.ˌɑr.bɛi.dər/</span> or more fluently <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈdɑ.ˌɣɑr.bɛi.dər/</span> ("day labourer"), a compound of <i>dag</i> ("day") + <i>arbeider</i> ("labourer") so it is not divided as <i>*da-gar-bei-der</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">*/ˈdaː.ˌɣɑr.bɛi.dər/</span>. If it were not a compound, it would be written <i>*dag-gar-bei-der</i> to keep the first "a" short.</li>
<li><i>een-en-twin-tig</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈeː.nən.ˌtʋɪn.təx/</span> ("twenty-one"), a compound of <i>een</i> ("one") + <i>en</i> ("and") + <i>twintig</i> ("twenty"). If it were not a compound, it would be written <i>*e-nen-twin-tig</i> to avoid having a double vowel at the end of a syllable.</li>
<li><i>mee-doen</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈmeː.dun/</span> ("to participate"), a compound of <i>mee</i> ("along (with)") + <i>doen</i> ("do"). If it were not a compound, it would be written <i>*me-doen</i> to avoid having a double vowel at the end of a syllable. The word <i>mee</i> itself has a double vowel because of the exception with final <i>-e</i>, as noted above.</li></ul>
<h2><span id="Final_devoicing_and_the_.27t_kofschip_rule"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_devoicing_and_the_'t_kofschip_rule">Final devoicing and the '<i>t kofschip</i> rule</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_orthography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Final devoicing and the &#039;t kofschip rule">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Final_obstruent_devoicing" class="mw-redirect" title="Final obstruent devoicing">Final obstruent devoicing</a> and <a href="/wiki/%27t_kofschip" title="&#39;t kofschip">'t kofschip</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/T-rules" title="T-rules">T-rules</a></div>
<p>Final devoicing is not indicated in Dutch spelling; words are usually spelled according to the historically original consonant. Therefore, a word may be written with a letter for a voiced consonant at the end of a word but still be pronounced with a voiceless consonant:
</p>
<ul><li><i>heb</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɦɛp/</span> "(I) have" but <i>hebben</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈɦɛbə(n)/</span> "to have"</li>
<li><i>paard</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/paːrt/</span> "horse" but <i>paarden</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈpaːrdə(n)/</span> "horses"</li>
<li><i>leg</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/lɛx/</span> "(I) lay" but <i>leggen</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈlɛɣə(n)/</span> "to lay"</li></ul>
<p>Weak verbs form their past tense and past participle by addition of a dental, &#x27e8;d&#x27e9; or &#x27e8;t&#x27e9; depending on the <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">voicing</a> of the preceding consonant(s) (see <a href="/wiki/Assimilation_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Assimilation (linguistics)">Assimilation (linguistics)</a>). However, because final consonants are always devoiced, there is no difference in pronunciation between these in the participle. Nonetheless, in accordance with the above rules, the orthography operates as if the consonant were still voiced. It is written in the past participle the same as the other past tense forms in which it is not word-final. To help memorise when to write &#x27e8;d&#x27e9; and when &#x27e8;t&#x27e9;, Dutch students are taught the rule "'t kofschip is met thee beladen" ("the merchant ship is loaded with tea"). If the verb stem in the <b>infinitive</b> ends with one of the consonants of "'t kofschip" (-t, -k, -f, -s, -ch or -p), the past tense dental is a -t-; otherwise, it is a -d-. However, the rule also applies to loanwords ending in -c, -q or -x, as these are also voiceless.
</p>

<h3><span id=".E2.9F.A8v.E2.9F.A9_and_.E2.9F.A8z.E2.9F.A9"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="⟨v⟩_and_⟨z⟩">&#x27e8;v&#x27e9; and &#x27e8;z&#x27e9;</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_orthography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: ⟨v⟩ and ⟨z⟩">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The letters &#x27e8;v&#x27e9; and &#x27e8;z&#x27e9; are somewhat special:
</p>
<ul><li>They are permitted only at the start of a syllable in native words, not at the end.</li>
<li>For historic reasons, they are never preceded by a short/lax vowel and so never occur doubled.</li>
<li>When the sounds <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/v/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/z/</span> occur at the end of a syllable, they are written &#x27e8;f&#x27e9; and &#x27e8;s&#x27e9; respectively.</li></ul>
<p>Then, therefore, final devoicing is reflected in the spelling:
</p>
<ul><li><i>blijven</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈblɛivə(n)/</span> ("to stay") → <i>blijf</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/blɛif/</span> "(I) stay"</li>
<li><i>huizen</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈɦœyzə(n)/</span> "houses" → <i>huis</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ɦœys/</span> "house"</li></ul>
<p>However, &#x27e8;f&#x27e9; and &#x27e8;s&#x27e9; are also written at the end of a syllable that is not final. The pronunciation remains voiced even if the spelling shows a voiceless consonant. This is most common in the past tense forms of weak verbs:
</p>
<ul><li><i>leven</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈleːvə(n)/</span> ("to live") → <i>lee<b>fd</b>e</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈleːvdə/</span> "(I) lived"</li>
<li><i>blozen</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈbloːzə(n)/</span> ("to blush") → <i>bloo<b>sd</b>e</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈbloːzdə/</span> "(I) blushed"</li></ul>
<p>Compare this to verbs in which the final consonant is underlyingly voiceless. Here, the dental assimilation rule calls for the ending <i>-te</i>, which gives away the voicelessness of the previous sound even if the spelling of that sound itself does not:
</p>
<ul><li><i>blaffen</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈblɑfə(n)/</span> ("to bark") → <i>bla<b>ft</b>e</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈblɑftə/</span> "(I) barked"</li>
<li><i>ruisen</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈrœysə(n)/</span> ("to rustle, to hiss") → <i>rui<b>st</b>e</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ˈrœystə/</span> "(I) rustled"</li></ul>
<p>Some modern loanwords and new coinages do not follow these rules. However, these words tend to not follow the other spelling rules as well: <i>buzzen</i> ("to page (call on a pager)") → <i>buzz</i> ("(I) page"), <i>buzzde</i> ("(I) paged").
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Diacritics">Diacritics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_orthography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Diacritics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Dutch uses the <a href="/wiki/Acute_accent" title="Acute accent">acute accent</a> to mark stress and the <a href="/wiki/Diaeresis_(diacritic)" title="Diaeresis (diacritic)">diaeresis</a> (trema) to disambiguate diphthongs/triphthongs. Occasionally, other diacritics are used in loanwords. Accents are not necessarily placed on capital letters (for example, the word <i>Eén</i> at the beginning of a sentence) unless the whole word is written in capitals.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acute_accent">Acute accent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_orthography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Acute accent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:VRT_E%C3%A9n_logo.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/VRT_E%C3%A9n_logo.svg/220px-VRT_E%C3%A9n_logo.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="104" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/VRT_E%C3%A9n_logo.svg/330px-VRT_E%C3%A9n_logo.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/VRT_E%C3%A9n_logo.svg/440px-VRT_E%C3%A9n_logo.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="243" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:VRT_E%C3%A9n_logo.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Logo for <i><a href="/wiki/E%C3%A9n" title="Eén">Eén</a></i> (<i>One</i>), a Flemish TV station in Belgium</div></div></div>
<p>Acute accents may be used to emphasise a word in a phrase, on the vowel in the stressed syllable. If the vowel is written as a digraph, an acute accent is put on both parts of the digraph. Although that rule includes <i><a href="/wiki/IJ_(digraph)" title="IJ (digraph)">ij</a></i>, the acute accent on the j is frequently omitted in typing (resulting in íj instead of íj́), as putting an acute accent on a j is still problematic in most word processing software.<sup id="cite_ref-klemtoon_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-klemtoon-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> If the vowel is written as more than two letters, the accent is put on the first two vowel letters – except when the first letter is a capital one. According to the <a href="/wiki/Taalunie" class="mw-redirect" title="Taalunie">Taalunie</a>, accents on capital letters are used only in <a href="/wiki/All_caps" title="All caps">all caps</a> and in loanwords.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> So, it is correct to write <i>één</i>, <i>Eén</i>, and <i>ÉÉN</i>, but not to write <i>Één</i>. The <a href="/wiki/Genootschap_Onze_Taal" title="Genootschap Onze Taal">Genootschap Onze Taal</a> states that accents can be put on capital letters whenever the need arises, but makes an exception for <i>Eén</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Stress on a short vowel, written with only one letter, is occasionally marked with a <a href="/wiki/Grave_accent" title="Grave accent">grave accent</a>: <i>Kàn jij dat?</i> (equivalent to the example below), <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/wel" class="extiw" title="wikt:wel">wèl</a></i>. However, it is technically incorrect to do so.<sup id="cite_ref-klemtoon_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-klemtoon-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Additionally, the acute accent may also be used to mark different meanings of various words, including <i>een/één</i> (<a href="/wiki/Indefinite_article" class="mw-redirect" title="Indefinite article">a(n)</a>/<a href="/wiki/1_(number)" class="mw-redirect" title="1 (number)">one</a>), <i>voor/vóór</i> (for/before), <i>vóórkomen/voorkómen</i> (to occur/to prevent), and <i>vérstrekkend/verstrékkend</i> (far-reaching/issuing), as shown in the examples below.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diaeresis">Diaeresis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_orthography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Diaeresis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Diaeresis_(diacritic)" title="Diaeresis (diacritic)">diaeresis</a> is used to mark a <a href="/wiki/Hiatus_(linguistics)" title="Hiatus (linguistics)">hiatus</a>, if the combination of vowel letters may be either mistaken for a digraph or interpreted in more than one way: "geïnd" (collected), "geüpload" (uploaded), "egoïstisch" (egoistic), "sympathieën" (sympathies, preferences), coördinaat (coordinate), "reëel" (realistic), "zeeën" (seas). On a line break that separates the vowels but keeps parts of a digraph together, the diaeresis becomes redundant and so is not written: ego-/istisch, sympathie-/en, re-/eel, zee-/en.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diacritics_in_loanwords">Diacritics in loanwords</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dutch_orthography&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Diacritics in loanwords">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Grave_accent" title="Grave accent">grave accent</a> is used in some French loanwords and generally when pronunciation would be wrong without it, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Apr%C3%A8s-ski" class="mw-redirect" title="Après-ski">après-ski</a></i>, <i>barrière</i> (barrier), <i>bèta</i>, <i>caissière</i> (female cashier), <i>carrière</i> (career) and <i>hè?</i> ("What?"), <i>blèren</i> (to yell). Officially, <i>appel</i> is always written without an accent, but sometimes an accent is used to distinguish between <i>appel</i> ("apple") and <i>appèl</i> ("appeal", "roll call", and others).<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Besides being used to mark stress, <a href="/wiki/Acute_accent" title="Acute accent">acute accents</a> are also used in many loanwords (mainly from French) such as <i>logé</i> (overnight guest), <i>coupé</i> (train compartment), <i>oké</i> (okay) and <i>café</i>.
</p><p>Similarly, a <a href="/wiki/Circumflex_accent" class="mw-redirect" title="Circumflex accent">circumflex accent</a> is also used in some French loanwords, including <i>enquête</i> (survey), and <i>fêteren</i> (to treat). For <i>gênant</i> (embarrassing) it is indecisive, some sources state it should be without the accent since it makes no difference to the pronunciation, others prefer to use the accent.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The circumflex accent is also used in <a href="/wiki/West_Frisian_Dutch" title="West Frisian Dutch">West Frisian Dutch</a> and so in general Dutch as well if there is no translation. <i>Skûtsjesilen</i> is the most common example, where <i>silen</i> is West Frisian Dutch for <i>zeilen</i> (to sail) and a <i>skûtsje</i> is a specific type of sailboat.
</p>

<li id="cite_note-ij-y-28"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-ij-y_28-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-ij-y_28-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-ij-y_28-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-ij-y_28-3"><sup><i><b>d</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-ij-y_28-4"><sup><i><b>e</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-ij-y_28-5"><sup><i><b>f</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">In diphthongs, &#x27e8;ij&#x27e9; and &#x27e8;y&#x27e9; are obsolete spellings for &#x27e8;i&#x27e9;. They are found mostly in names.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-ae-29"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-ae_29-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-ae_29-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">&#x27e8;ae&#x27e9; is an obsolete spelling for &#x27e8;aa&#x27e9;. In Latin words, it is treated identical to &#x27e8;ee&#x27e9;.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-au-30"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-au_30-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In words of French origin.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-eu-31"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-eu_31-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"> &#x27e8;eu&#x27e9; is pronounced as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/œy̯/</span> by some speakers in words of Greek origin.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-oi-32"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-oi_32-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">&#x27e8;oi&#x27e9; is pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʋɑ/</span> or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʋaː/</span> in French loanwords. In archaic Dutch spellings, mostly proper nouns, it may also be <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/oː/</span> (for example, <a href="/wiki/Oisterwijk" title="Oisterwijk">Oisterwijk</a> or <a href="/wiki/Helvoirt" title="Helvoirt">Helvoirt</a>).</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-33"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-33">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">&#x27e8;oo&#x27e9; is generally <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/u(ː)/</span> in English loanwords.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-34"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-34">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">&#x27e8;ou&#x27e9; is generally <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/u(ː)/</span> in loanwords from French or Greek.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-35"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-35">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">&#x27e8;ui&#x27e9; is pronounced <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ʋi(ː)/</span> in loanwords from French. In English loanwords, such as <i>cruise</i>, it is <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/uː/</span>.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-y-36"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-y_36-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">&#x27e8;y&#x27e9; is usually pronounced as &#x27e8;i&#x27e9; or &#x27e8;ie&#x27e9;. It is also an obsolete spelling variant of &#x27e8;ij&#x27e9;.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-37"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-37">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text"><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.onzetaal.nl/taaladvies/advies/accenten-op-hoofdletters">Onze Taal: Accenten op hoofdletters (in Dutch)</a></span>
</li>

</li>
