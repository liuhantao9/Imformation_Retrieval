	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Warao people</h1>

<p>The <b>Warao</b> are an <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">indigenous Amerindian people</a> inhabiting northeastern <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>. Alternate common spellings of Warao are Waroa, Guarauno, Guarao, and Warrau. The term <i>Warao</i> translates as "the boat people," after the Warao's lifelong and intimate connection to the water. Most of the approximately 20,000 Warao inhabit Venezuela's <a href="/wiki/Orinoco_Delta" title="Orinoco Delta">Orinoco Delta</a> region, with smaller numbers in neighboring <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>.
They speak an <a href="/wiki/Agglutinative" class="mw-redirect" title="Agglutinative">agglutinative</a> language, <a href="/wiki/Warao_language" title="Warao language">Warao</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lifestyle">Lifestyle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warao_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lifestyle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dwellings">Dwellings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warao_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dwellings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the wide Orinoco River and its fertile delta composed of islands and marshes, Warao people inhabit wall-less thatched-roof huts built upon stilts for protection against <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">floods</a>. These houses are usually built on the highest ground to avoid the annual floods. Sometimes a group of houses is built upon a single large platform of trees. The huts each possess a clay cooking pit or oven located in the center, with sleeping hammocks encircling it. Besides the hammocks, the only other furniture sometimes present are wooden stools, sometimes carved in the shapes of animals.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warao_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Warao use canoes as their main form of transportation. Other modes, such as walking, are hampered by the hundreds of streams, rivulets, marshes, and high waters created by the Orinoco. Warao babies, toddlers, and small children are famed for their ability to hold tight to their mothers' necks, as well as to paddle. They often learn to swim before they learn to walk.
</p><p>The Warao use two types of canoes. <i>Bongos</i>, which carry up to 5 people, are built in an arduous process that starts with the search for large trees. When an old <i>bongo</i> is no longer usable, a consensus is reached by the male leaders of each household on which tree is best. At the start of the dry season, they find the tree and kill it. At the end of the dry season, they return to cut it down. It is then hollowed out and flattened with stone tools traded from the mountains (or local <a href="/wiki/Seashell" title="Seashell">shell</a> tools) along with fire.
</p><p>The other type of canoe is a small, seating only three people, and is used for daily travel to and from food sources.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diet">Diet</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warao_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diet">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Warao diet is varied with an emphasis on the products of the delta, mostly fish. By 1500 they had acquired basic <a href="/wiki/Horticulture" title="Horticulture">horticulture</a>, although many of their daily fruits and vegetables come from the wild orchards of the delta. In July and August, Warao feast on crabs when they come to the delta from the beach. Hunting is generally avoided due to cultural taboos. They occasionally also eat grubs found in the  moriche palm tree.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warao_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Warao are, according to their own reports, descended from an adventurous heavenly figure &#8212; the primordial hunter. This man originally dwelt in a sky world which had men, but was completely devoid of all animals except birds. Hunting these heavenly birds, the founding man used his bow and arrow to strike a bird in mid-air. The bird fell from the sky and eventually hit the heavenly floor. The birds burst through the floor and proceeded through the clouds and towards terrestrial land (<a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>) below. The hunter went to the hole in the floor made by the bird and looked through. He saw lush and fertile land (Earth) and resolved to descend to it to partake of its pleasures: beauty, abundant game, fruit, et cetera. The hunter took a long rope of heavenly cotton, tied it to a tree, and threw it through the hole and lowered himself through the clouds to what is now Earth, forsaking his sky world.
</p><p>The Warao have shamans, who perform music such as rain dances and songs.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_contact_with_Europeans">First contact with Europeans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warao_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: First contact with Europeans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Warao of eastern Venezuela's Orinoco first had contact with Europeans when, soon after <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a> reached the Orinoco river delta, <a href="/wiki/Alonso_de_Ojeda" title="Alonso de Ojeda">Alonso de Ojeda</a> decided to navigate the river upstream. There, in the delta, Ojeda saw the distinctively stilted Warao huts, balanced over the water. Similar architecture in <a href="/wiki/Sinamaica" title="Sinamaica">Sinamaica</a> far to the west had been likened to <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a>, with its famous canals below and buildings above; this new encounter propagated the name of <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> ("little Venice") for the whole land.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_issues">Contemporary issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warao_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Contemporary issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The inaccessibility of the Warao's lands makes access to health care difficult. <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">Tuberculosis</a> is common.<sup id="cite_ref-warao.org_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-warao.org-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a> was first detected in 2007 in the Waroa communities of the lower Orinoco Delta region of Venezuela.  Sex between men is a common practice among young Warao, especially before they are married, and, is thought to be a major factor in the rapid spread of the disease. In some communities already 35% test positive for the <a href="/wiki/HIV_virus" class="mw-redirect" title="HIV virus">HIV virus</a>. With Venezuela's failing health care system, there is an absence of prevention programs; this, together, with severe language barriers — many Warao are illiterate and do not speak fluent Spanish — have allowed ignorance about the disease to flourish. The culture is felt to be severely threatened.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>.
</p><p>In the summer of 2008, indigenous leaders and researchers from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a> issued a report detailing the deaths of 38 Warao in the <a href="/wiki/Delta_Amacuro" title="Delta Amacuro">Delta Amacuro</a> state from a mysterious illness. The disease, which causes "partial <a href="/wiki/Paralysis" title="Paralysis">paralysis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Convulsion" title="Convulsion">convulsions</a> and an extreme fear of water"<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> is believed to be a form of <a href="/wiki/Rabies" title="Rabies">rabies</a> transmitted by <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bats</a>. Upon reaching <a href="/wiki/Caracas" title="Caracas">Caracas</a> to inform the government of the outbreak and request assistance, the leaders and researchers "met with disrespect on every level, as if the deaths of indigenous people are not even worth anything."<sup id="cite_ref-NYTimes_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYTimes-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film_treatment">Film treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warao_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Film treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In April 2017, The Museum of Modern Art in New York presented a film by Cuban-Venezuelan director Mario Crespo with a cast of nonprofessional actors. The film presents the difficulty of choosing to stay with the Warao traditions and life or to leave to gain an education among the "Creoles". The film includes a detailed look at Warao culture and life along the river. The film is: "Dauna. Lo que lleva el río (Gone with the River). 2015, Venezuela, in the Spanish and Warao languages.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tourism">Tourism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warao_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tourism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tourism has come to the Warao which brings relative wealth for some.  Several individuals have set up rough and ready tourist accommodation in their own homes.  They offer canoe trips (hand and power) to see the wild life of the delta, also a chance to experience their traditional life and culture. These visits can be arranged in the state capital town of <a href="/wiki/Tucupita" title="Tucupita">Tucupita</a> through one of many travel agents.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Crime">Crime</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warao_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Crime">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is little local crime in the area.  However drugs are smuggled down the Orinoco river into the delta for transshipment to the USA. The Venezuelan Navy patrols the main river branches and the river police have stations at key points.  The traffic movements of everyone are monitored and recorded. However they are tourist friendly.
</p>
