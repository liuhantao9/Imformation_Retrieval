<p>The <b>Inter-American Treaty of Reciprocal Assistance</b> (commonly known as the <b>Rio Treaty</b>, the <b>Rio Pact</b>, the <b>Treaty of Reciprocal Assistance</b>, or by the <a href="/wiki/Spanish-language" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish-language">Spanish-language</a> acronym <b>TIAR</b> from <i><b>Tratado Interamericano de Asistencia Recíproca</b></i>) was an agreement signed in 1947 in <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> among many countries of the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The central principle contained in its articles is that an attack against one is to be considered an attack against them all; this was known as the "hemispheric defense" doctrine. Despite this, several members have breached the treaty on multiple occasions. The treaty was initially created in 1947 and came into force in 1948, in accordance with Article 22 of the treaty. <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">The Bahamas</a> was the most recent country to sign and ratify it in 1982.<sup id="cite_ref-sign_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sign-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_history">Background and history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Inter-American_Treaty_of_Reciprocal_Assistance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background and history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> maintained a hemispheric defense policy relative to European influence under the <a href="/wiki/Monroe_Doctrine" title="Monroe Doctrine">Monroe Doctrine</a> since 1823, which became increasingly interventionist with the <a href="/wiki/Roosevelt_Corollary" title="Roosevelt Corollary">Roosevelt Corollary</a> in 1904. During the 1930s the US had been alarmed by <a href="/wiki/Axis_Powers" class="mw-redirect" title="Axis Powers">Axis</a> overtures toward military cooperation with Latin American governments; apparent strategic threats against the <a href="/wiki/Panama_Canal" title="Panama Canal">Panama Canal</a> were of particular concern. These were discussed in a series of meetings of the <a href="/wiki/International_Conference_of_American_States" class="mw-redirect" title="International Conference of American States">International Conference of American States</a> and the 1936 Inter-American Conference for the Maintenance of Peace.<sup id="cite_ref-ChapAct_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ChapAct-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> During the war Washington had been able to secure <a href="/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_II" title="Allies of World War II">Allied</a> support from all individual governments except <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>, which remained neutral, and <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, whose government was not recognized by the Allied powers.<sup id="cite_ref-ChapAct_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ChapAct-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Some countries had signed the <a href="/wiki/Declaration_by_United_Nations" title="Declaration by United Nations">Declaration by United Nations</a> in early 1942 and more had signed by the end of 1945. At the Inter-American Conference on the Problems of War and Peace, in <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a> during February and March 1945, discussions of the post-war world order were held and produced the Act of Chapultepec.<sup id="cite_ref-ChapAct_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ChapAct-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In light of the developing <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> and following the statement of the <a href="/wiki/Truman_Doctrine" title="Truman Doctrine">Truman Doctrine</a>, the US wished to make those new anti-communist commitments permanent. The Inter-American Treaty of Reciprocal Assistance was the first of many so-called 'mutual security agreements',<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and the formalization of the Act of Chapultepec. The treaty was adopted by the original signatories on 2 September 1947 in <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">Rio de Janeiro</a> (hence the colloquial name "Rio Treaty"). It came into force on 3 December 1948 and was registered with the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> on 20 December 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-sign_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sign-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The treaty was invoked numerous times during the 1950s and 1960s, in particular supporting the United States' naval blockade unanimously during the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Missile_Crisis" title="Cuban Missile Crisis">Cuban Missile Crisis</a>. With the exceptions of <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a> (1967) and <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">The Bahamas</a> (1982), no countries that became independent after 1947 have joined the treaty; <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> is yet to become a member, though it already has <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Treaty_Organization" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic Treaty Organization">separate defense commitments</a> with the US. During the <a href="/wiki/Falklands_War" title="Falklands War">Falklands War</a> (1982), the United States favored the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> because Argentina had been the aggressor, and because Argentina had not been attacked, as did Chile and Colombia. This was seen by most Latin American countries as the final failure of the treaty.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In 2001, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> invoked the Rio Treaty after the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">September 11 attacks</a>.  In September 2002, citing the Falklands example<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and anticipating the <a href="/wiki/2003_invasion_of_Iraq" title="2003 invasion of Iraq">Iraq War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> formally withdrew from the treaty; after the requisite two years, Mexico ceased to be a signatory in September 2004.
</p><p>In 2008, the <a href="/wiki/Union_of_South_American_Nations" title="Union of South American Nations">Union of South American Nations</a> (UNASUR) created a new regional security council to take care of their own defence issues.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 5 June 2012, <a href="/wiki/Bolivarian_Alliance_for_the_Americas" class="mw-redirect" title="Bolivarian Alliance for the Americas">ALBA</a> countries <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">Bolivia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">Nicaragua</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, under the leadership of leftist governments, initiated the retirement from the TIAR,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> a decision which the <a href="/wiki/Obama_Administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Obama Administration">Obama Administration</a> deplored as "unfortunate" but respected.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The treaty has been denounced by Nicaragua on 20 September 2012, Bolivia on 17 October 2012, Venezuela on 14 May 2013, and Ecuador on 19 February 2014.
</p><p>In 2019 during the <a href="/wiki/2019_Venezuelan_presidential_crisis" title="2019 Venezuelan presidential crisis">presidential crisis</a>, the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_(Venezuela)" title="National Assembly (Venezuela)">National Assembly of Venezuela</a>, presided by opposition leader <a href="/wiki/Juan_Guaid%C3%B3" title="Juan Guaidó">Juan Guaidó</a>, opened talks on rejoining TIAR.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> On 11 May, Guaidó sent a letter to <a href="/wiki/Organization_of_American_States" title="Organization of American States">Organization of American States</a> (OAS) secretary <a href="/wiki/Luis_Almagro" title="Luis Almagro">Luis Almagro</a> requesting that Venezuela be reinstated.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> On 29 May 2019, the National Assembly approved its return to the Treaty in a preliminary discussion.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The National Assembly reiterated its approval to return to the treaty in July 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
