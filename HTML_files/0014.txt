<p><b>Willingham</b> is a village in <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a>, England. It is located in the <a href="/wiki/South_Cambridgeshire" title="South Cambridgeshire">South Cambridgeshire</a> district and sits just outside the border of <a href="/wiki/The_Fens" title="The Fens">the Fens</a>, just south of the <a href="/wiki/River_Great_Ouse" title="River Great Ouse">River Great Ouse</a>.
</p><p>Located approximately 12 miles (19&#160;km) northwest of <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, on the B1050 road, Willingham Parish occupies 4,641 acres (1,878&#160;ha), and had a population in 2007 of 3,900 people, increasing to 4,015 at the Census 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>   Although the highest point in the village is only approximately 23 feet (7&#160;m) above sea level, Willingham is not generally considered to be at risk from flooding.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Willingham,_Cambridgeshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name Willingham probably originated from being the homestead of the family or followers of a man called "Wifel" and was called Vuivlingeham c. 1050 and Wivelingham around 1086. The name Wivelingham was also used to refer to the village until the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-victoria_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-victoria-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The area at the edge of the fens to the north of the present village was already occupied by the 2nd century, though these were at some point abandoned. The <a href="/wiki/Aldreth" title="Aldreth">Aldreth</a> causeway, which formed the main route between Cambridge and <a href="/wiki/Ely,_Cambridgeshire" title="Ely, Cambridgeshire">Ely</a> in medieval times and perhaps dating from the <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a>, runs through the east of the parish past <a href="/wiki/Belsar%27s_Hill" title="Belsar&#39;s Hill">Belsar's Hill</a>, and until the opening of the Cambridge-Ely <a href="/wiki/Turnpike_trusts" title="Turnpike trusts">turnpike</a> in 1768, carriage traffic would have run through Willingham.<sup id="cite_ref-victoria_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-victoria-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the oldest houses in the village dates from the 15th century.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Willingham's history is closely associated to its position on the edge of the Fens and it was only with the major efforts to drain the Fens during the 17th century that the parish took its modern structure. During the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, the majority of the low-lying land in the parish was inundated annually, and the village had two permanent meres, with the larger of the two growing to 380 acres (150&#160;ha) at times of highest water level. It was only with the construction of the sluice at <a href="/wiki/Earith" title="Earith">Earith</a> in 1650 which diverted the flow of the Ouse from the Old West into the <a href="/wiki/New_Bedford_River" title="New Bedford River">New Bedford River</a> that the parish was able to remain largely unflooded. Additional areas were drained by <a href="/wiki/Windmill" title="Windmill">windmills</a> until replaced by steam pumps in the mid-19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-victoria_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-victoria-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Willingham's registered population grew from 23 at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> in 1086 to 79 in 1251, and in 1377 the poll tax was paid by 287 adults. By 1801 the population was almost 800 inhabitants growing to more than 1,600 in 1851 despite a wave of emigration to America in the 1830s. Numbers then remained generally constant until the 1960s when it grew rapidly again, and passing 2,500 by 1981 and 3,436 in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-victoria_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-victoria-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A local man named Jabez Few, who died in the 1920s, was regarded by the townspeople of Willingham as a witch. They claimed that the white mice he kept were his <a href="/wiki/Familiar_spirit" title="Familiar spirit">familiar spirits</a>, and that they could not be got rid of after his death until they were held over running water.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In 1940 a German spy parachuted into Willingham, was eventually captured and turned into a double agent.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Church">Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Willingham,_Cambridgeshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cmglee_Willingham_All_Saints_Church.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Cmglee_Willingham_All_Saints_Church.jpg/220px-Cmglee_Willingham_All_Saints_Church.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Cmglee_Willingham_All_Saints_Church.jpg/330px-Cmglee_Willingham_All_Saints_Church.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Cmglee_Willingham_All_Saints_Church.jpg/440px-Cmglee_Willingham_All_Saints_Church.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cmglee_Willingham_All_Saints_Church.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Willingham All Saints Church in July 2014</div></div></div>
<p>Evidence of Christian Anglo-Saxon activity in Willingham has been found, and there has probably been a church in the village since at least the 12th century. The present church of St Mary and All Saints was built on the foundations of the 12th century building. Mainly constructed in the 14th century, it consists of a chancel with north vestry, nave, a south porch and a west tower with an unusual spire. It has notable 14th and 15th century figure paintings including a rare picture of a pregnant Virgin Mary.<sup id="cite_ref-victoria_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-victoria-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Village_life">Village life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Willingham,_Cambridgeshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Village life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cmglee_Willingham_library_village_sign.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Cmglee_Willingham_library_village_sign.jpg/220px-Cmglee_Willingham_library_village_sign.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Cmglee_Willingham_library_village_sign.jpg/330px-Cmglee_Willingham_library_village_sign.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Cmglee_Willingham_library_village_sign.jpg/440px-Cmglee_Willingham_library_village_sign.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cmglee_Willingham_library_village_sign.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Willingham village sign, green and library in July 2014</div></div></div>
<p>Willingham has a primary school, library, surgery, post office and a number of shops and restaurants. There are currently three pubs in the village — the Duke of Wellington, The Bank and The Porterhouse Pub. The first recorded alehouses were the George in 1665 and the Five Bells in 1671 though by the late 19th century the number had risen to 16 with the George and the Vine the most notable. The White Hart opened in 1910; there were still 7 pubs in 1933 and 5 in 1982, of which the oldest was the Duke of Wellington, known as the Warriors in the 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-victoria_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-victoria-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The village has a number of amenities for young people provided by the Willingham Youth Trust and a well established Scout Group.
</p><p>Willingham also has a football club named Willingham Wolves. It's not known why they took the name of Wolves. Today the club offers grass roots football to almost 200 players from as young as 5 all the way to men's.
</p><p>Transport links nearby include the <a href="/wiki/A14_road_(England)" title="A14 road (England)">A14 trunk road</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire_Guided_Busway" title="Cambridgeshire Guided Busway">Cambridgeshire Guided Busway</a>, with the <a href="/wiki/River_Great_Ouse" title="River Great Ouse">River Great Ouse</a>, also known as the Old West River, less than 2 miles (3&#160;km) away. A bus service links Willingham and surrounding villages with Cambridge and St Ives.
</p><p>Villages adjacent to Willingham include <a href="/wiki/Earith" title="Earith">Earith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Over,_Cambridgeshire" title="Over, Cambridgeshire">Over</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rampton,_Cambridgeshire" title="Rampton, Cambridgeshire">Rampton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Longstanton" title="Longstanton">Longstanton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bar_Hill" title="Bar Hill">Bar Hill</a>. The village of Willingham is a direct neighbour of new town <a href="/wiki/Northstowe" title="Northstowe">Northstowe</a> which is currently in Phase 2 of construction.
</p>
