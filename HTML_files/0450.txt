<p><b>Table tennis</b>, also known as <b>ping-pong</b>, is a sport in which two or four players hit a lightweight ball back and forth across a table using small rackets. The game takes place on a hard table divided by a net. Except for the initial serve, the rules are generally as follows: players must allow a ball played toward them to bounce one time on their side of the table, and must return it so that it bounces on the opposite side at least once. A point is scored when a player fails to return the ball within the rules. Play is fast and demands quick reactions. Spinning the ball alters its trajectory and limits an opponent's options, giving the hitter a great advantage.
</p><p>Table tennis is governed by the worldwide organization <a href="/wiki/International_Table_Tennis_Federation" title="International Table Tennis Federation">International Table Tennis Federation</a> (ITTF), founded in 1926. ITTF currently includes 226 member associations.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The table tennis official rules are specified in the ITTF handbook.<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF2_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Table tennis has been an <a href="/wiki/Table_tennis_at_the_Summer_Olympics" title="Table tennis at the Summer Olympics">Olympic sport</a> since <a href="/wiki/Table_tennis_at_the_1988_Summer_Olympics" title="Table tennis at the 1988 Summer Olympics">1988</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Hurt_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hurt-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> with several event categories. From 1988 until 2004, these were men's singles, women's singles, men's doubles and women's doubles. Since <a href="/wiki/Table_tennis_at_the_2008_Summer_Olympics" title="Table tennis at the 2008 Summer Olympics">2008</a>, a team event has been played instead of the doubles.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ping-Pong_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ping-Pong_2.jpg/220px-Ping-Pong_2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="161" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ping-Pong_2.jpg/330px-Ping-Pong_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/Ping-Pong_2.jpg/440px-Ping-Pong_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3790" data-file-height="2767" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ping-Pong_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Parker Brothers Ping-Pong game</div></div></div>
<p>The sport originated in Victorian England, where it was played among the upper-class as an after-dinner <a href="/wiki/Parlour_game" title="Parlour game">parlour game</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-H2_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H2-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Letts_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Letts-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It has been suggested that makeshift versions of the game were developed by British military officers in <a href="/wiki/British_India" class="mw-redirect" title="British India">India</a> in around 1860s or 1870s, who brought it back with them.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> A row of books stood up along the center of the table as a net, two more books served as rackets and were used to continuously hit a golf-ball.<sup id="cite_ref-ITTFhistm_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTFhistm-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FH_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FH-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The name "ping-pong" was in wide use before British manufacturer <a href="/wiki/Jaques_of_London" title="Jaques of London">J. Jaques &amp; Son Ltd</a> <a href="/wiki/Trademark" title="Trademark">trademarked</a> it in 1901. The name "ping-pong" then came to describe the game played using the rather expensive Jaques's equipment, with other manufacturers calling it table tennis. A similar situation arose in the United States, where Jaques sold the rights to the "ping-pong" name to <a href="/wiki/Parker_Brothers" title="Parker Brothers">Parker Brothers</a>. Parker Brothers then enforced its trademark for the term in the 1920s making the various associations change their names to "table tennis" instead of the more common, but trademarked, term.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The next major innovation was by James W. Gibb, a British enthusiast of table tennis, who discovered novelty <a href="/wiki/Celluloid" title="Celluloid">celluloid</a> balls on a trip to the US in 1901 and found them to be ideal for the game. This was followed by E.C. Goode who, in 1901, invented the modern version of the racket by fixing a sheet of pimpled, or stippled, <a href="/wiki/Natural_rubber" title="Natural rubber">rubber</a> to the wooden blade. Table tennis was growing in popularity by 1901 to the extent that tournaments were being organized, books being written on the subject,<sup id="cite_ref-ITTFhistm_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTFhistm-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and an unofficial world championship was held in 1902.
</p><p>In 1921, the <a href="/wiki/Table_Tennis_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="Table Tennis Association">Table Tennis Association</a> was founded, and in 1926 renamed the English Table Tennis Association.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/International_Table_Tennis_Federation" title="International Table Tennis Federation">International Table Tennis Federation</a> (ITTF) followed in 1926.<sup id="cite_ref-H2_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H2-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> London hosted the first official <a href="/wiki/World_Table_Tennis_Championships" title="World Table Tennis Championships">World Championships</a> in 1926. In 1933, the <a href="/wiki/USATT" class="mw-redirect" title="USATT">United States Table Tennis Association</a>, now called USA Table Tennis, was formed.<sup id="cite_ref-H2_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H2-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-USATTabout_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USATTabout-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1930s, <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Snow" title="Edgar Snow">Edgar Snow</a> commented in <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Star_Over_China" title="Red Star Over China">Red Star Over China</a></i> that the Communist forces in the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Civil_War" title="Chinese Civil War">Chinese Civil War</a> had a "passion for the English game of table tennis" which he found "bizarre".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> On the other hand, the popularity of the sport waned in 1930s Soviet Union, partly because of the promotion of team and military sports, and partly because of a theory that the game had adverse health effects.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1950s, paddles that used a rubber sheet combined with an underlying sponge layer changed the game dramatically,<sup id="cite_ref-H2_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H2-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> introducing greater spin and speed.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> These were introduced to Britain by sports goods manufacturer S.W. Hancock Ltd. The use of <a href="/wiki/Speed_glue" title="Speed glue">speed glue</a> increased the spin and speed even further, resulting in changes to the equipment to "slow the game down". Table tennis was introduced as an Olympic sport at the <a href="/wiki/1988_Summer_Olympics" title="1988 Summer Olympics">Olympics in 1988</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rule_changes">Rule changes</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Assortment_of_40_mm_table_tennis_balls.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Assortment_of_40_mm_table_tennis_balls.jpg/220px-Assortment_of_40_mm_table_tennis_balls.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="139" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Assortment_of_40_mm_table_tennis_balls.jpg/330px-Assortment_of_40_mm_table_tennis_balls.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Assortment_of_40_mm_table_tennis_balls.jpg/440px-Assortment_of_40_mm_table_tennis_balls.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1148" data-file-height="726" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Assortment_of_40_mm_table_tennis_balls.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Assortment of 40 mm table tennis balls</div></div></div>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Table_tennis_at_the_2000_Summer_Olympics" title="Table tennis at the 2000 Summer Olympics">2000 Olympics</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a>, the ITTF instituted several rule changes that were aimed at making table tennis more viable as a televised spectator sport.<sup id="cite_ref-Clemett_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clemett-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bbaol_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbaol-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> First, the older 38&#160;mm (1.50&#160;in) balls were officially replaced by 40&#160;mm (1.57&#160;in) balls in October 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-ITTFhistm_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTFhistm-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-USATTBall_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USATTBall-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> This increased the ball's air resistance and effectively slowed down the game. By that time, players had begun increasing the thickness of the fast <a href="/wiki/Sponge_(material)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sponge (material)">sponge</a> layer on their paddles, which made the game excessively fast and difficult to watch on television. A few months later, the ITTF changed from a 21-point to an 11-point scoring system (and the serve rotation was reduced from five points to two), effective in September 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-ITTFhistm_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTFhistm-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This was intended to make games more fast-paced and exciting. The ITTF also changed the rules on service to prevent a player from hiding the ball during service, in order to increase the average length of rallies and to reduce the server's advantage, effective in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-stpwnr_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stpwnr-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
For the opponent to have time to realize a serve is taking place, the ball must be tossed a minimum of 16 centimetres (6.3&#160;in) in the air. The ITTF states that all events after July 2014 are played with a new poly material ball.<sup id="cite_ref-Board_of_Directors_Meeting_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Board_of_Directors_Meeting-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Equipment">Equipment</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ball">Ball</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Table_Tennis_Plastic_Ball_40%2B_mm.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Table_Tennis_Plastic_Ball_40%2B_mm.jpg/220px-Table_Tennis_Plastic_Ball_40%2B_mm.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Table_Tennis_Plastic_Ball_40%2B_mm.jpg/330px-Table_Tennis_Plastic_Ball_40%2B_mm.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Table_Tennis_Plastic_Ball_40%2B_mm.jpg/440px-Table_Tennis_Plastic_Ball_40%2B_mm.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1923" data-file-height="1281" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Table_Tennis_Plastic_Ball_40%2B_mm.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Table Tennis Plastic Ball 40+ mm</div></div></div>
<p>The international rules specify that the game is played with a sphere having a mass of 2.7 grams (0.095&#160;oz) and a diameter of 40 millimetres (1.57&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The rules say that the ball shall bounce up 24–26&#160;cm (9.4–10.2&#160;in) when dropped from a height of 30.5&#160;cm (12.0&#160;in) onto a standard steel block thereby having a <a href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_restitution" title="Coefficient of restitution">coefficient of restitution</a> of 0.89 to 0.92. Balls are now made of a polymer instead of <a href="/wiki/Celluloid" title="Celluloid">celluloid</a> as of 2015, colored white or orange, with a <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/matte" class="extiw" title="wikt:matte">matte</a> finish. The choice of ball color is made according to the table color and its surroundings. For example, a white ball is easier to see on a green or blue table than it is on a grey table. Manufacturers often indicate the quality of the ball with a star rating system, usually from one to three, three being the highest grade. As this system is not standard across manufacturers, the only way a ball may be used in official competition is upon ITTF approval<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> (the ITTF approval can be seen printed on the ball).
</p><p>The 40&#160;mm ball was introduced after the end of the <a href="/wiki/Table_tennis_at_the_2000_Summer_Olympics" title="Table tennis at the 2000 Summer Olympics">2000 Summer Olympics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-USATTBall_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USATTBall-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> This created some controversies. Then World No 1 table tennis professional <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Samsonov" title="Vladimir Samsonov">Vladimir Samsonov</a> threatened to pull out of the World Cup, which was scheduled to debut the new regulation ball on October 12, 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Table">Table</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Table_Tennis_Table_Blue.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Table_Tennis_Table_Blue.svg/450px-Table_Tennis_Table_Blue.svg.png" decoding="async" width="450" height="245" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Table_Tennis_Table_Blue.svg/675px-Table_Tennis_Table_Blue.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Table_Tennis_Table_Blue.svg/900px-Table_Tennis_Table_Blue.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="690" data-file-height="375" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Table_Tennis_Table_Blue.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diagram of a table tennis table showing the official dimensions</div></div></div>
<p>The table is 2.74&#160;m (9.0&#160;ft) long, 1.525&#160;m (5.0&#160;ft) wide, and 76&#160;cm (2.5&#160;ft) high with any continuous material so long as the table yields a uniform bounce of about 23&#160;cm (9.1&#160;in) when a standard ball is dropped onto it from a height of 30&#160;cm (11.8&#160;in), or about <span data-sort-value="7001770000000000000♠">77</span>%.<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF2.1_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF2.1-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The table or playing surface is uniformly dark coloured and matte, divided into two halves by a net at 15.25&#160;cm (6.0&#160;in) in height. The <a href="/wiki/International_Table_Tennis_Federation" title="International Table Tennis Federation">ITTF</a> approves only wooden tables or their derivates. Concrete tables with a steel net or a solid concrete partition are sometimes available in outside public spaces, such as parks.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Racket.2Fpaddle"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Racket/paddle">Racket/paddle</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Table_tennis_racket" title="Table tennis racket">Table tennis racket</a></div>
<p>Players are equipped with a laminated wooden racket covered with rubber on one or two sides depending on the grip of the player. The ITTF uses the term "racket",<sup id="cite_ref-brandingguidelines_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brandingguidelines-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> though "bat" is common in Britain, and "paddle" in the U.S. and Canada.
</p><p>The wooden portion of the racket, often referred to as the "blade", commonly features anywhere between one and seven plies of wood, though cork, glass fiber, carbon fiber, aluminum fiber, and Kevlar are sometimes used. According to the ITTF regulations, at least 85% of the blade by thickness shall be of natural wood.<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF2.4_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF2.4-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Common wood types include <a href="/wiki/Balsa" class="mw-redirect" title="Balsa">balsa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terminalia_superba" title="Terminalia superba">limba</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cypress" title="Cypress">cypress</a> or "<a href="/wiki/Hinoki" class="mw-redirect" title="Hinoki">hinoki</a>", which is popular in Japan. The average size of the blade is about 17 centimetres (6.7&#160;in) long and 15 centimetres (5.9&#160;in) wide, although the official restrictions only focus on the flatness and rigidity of the blade itself, these dimensions are optimal for most play styles.
</p><p>Table tennis regulations allow different surfaces on each side of the racket.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Various types of surfaces provide various levels of spin or speed, and in some cases they nullify spin. For example, a player may have a rubber that provides much spin on one side of their racket, and one that provides no spin on the other. By flipping the racket in play, different types of returns are possible. To help a player distinguish between the rubber used by his opposing player, international rules specify that one side must be red while the other side must be black.<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF2.4_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF2.4-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The player has the right to inspect their opponent's racket before a match to see the type of rubber used and what colour it is. Despite high speed play and rapid exchanges, a player can see clearly what side of the racket was used to hit the ball. Current rules state that, unless damaged in play, the racket cannot be exchanged for another racket at any time during a match.<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF3.4.2.4_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF3.4.2.4-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gameplay">Gameplay</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Competitive_table_tennis.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Competitive_table_tennis.jpg/220px-Competitive_table_tennis.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Competitive_table_tennis.jpg/330px-Competitive_table_tennis.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Competitive_table_tennis.jpg/440px-Competitive_table_tennis.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="410" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Competitive_table_tennis.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Competitive table tennis</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Starting_a_game">Starting a game</span></h3>
<p>According to ITTF rule 2.13.1, the first service is decided by lot,<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF2.13.1_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF2.13.1-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> normally a <a href="/wiki/Coin_toss" class="mw-redirect" title="Coin toss">coin toss</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-match_officials_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-match_officials-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> It is also common for one player (or the umpire/scorer) to hide the ball in one or the other hand, usually hidden under the table, allowing the other player to guess which hand the ball is in. The correct or incorrect guess gives the "winner" the option to choose to serve, receive, or to choose which side of the table to use. (A common but non-sanctioned method is for the players to play the ball back and forth three times and then play out the point. This is commonly referred to as "serve to play", "rally to serve", "play for serve", or "volley for serve".)
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Service_and_return">Service and return</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Alexander_Shibayev.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Alexander_Shibayev.jpg/220px-Alexander_Shibayev.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="337" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Alexander_Shibayev.jpg/330px-Alexander_Shibayev.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Alexander_Shibayev.jpg/440px-Alexander_Shibayev.jpg 2x" data-file-width="751" data-file-height="1150" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Alexander_Shibayev.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Service by professional <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russian</a> player <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Shibaev_(table_tennis)" title="Alexander Shibaev (table tennis)">Alexander Shibaev</a></div></div></div>
<p>In game play, the player serving the ball commences a play.<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF2.5_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF2.5-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The server first stands with the ball held on the open palm of the hand not carrying the paddle, called the freehand, and tosses the ball directly upward without spin, at least 16&#160;cm (6.3&#160;in) high.<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF2.6_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF2.6-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The server strikes the ball with the racket on the ball's descent so that it touches first his court and then touches directly the receiver's court without touching the net assembly. In casual games, many players do not toss the ball upward; however, this is technically illegal and can give the serving player an unfair advantage.
</p><p>The ball must remain behind the endline and above the upper surface of the table, known as the playing surface, at all times during the service. The server cannot use his/her body or clothing to obstruct sight of the ball; the opponent and the umpire must have a clear view of the ball at all times. If the umpire is doubtful of the legality of a service they may first interrupt play and give a warning to the server. If the serve is a clear failure or is doubted again by the umpire after the warning, the receiver scores a point.
</p><p>If the service is "good", then the receiver must make a "good" return by hitting the ball back before it bounces a second time on receiver's side of the table so that the ball passes the net and touches the opponent's court, either directly or after touching the net assembly.<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF2.7_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF2.7-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Thereafter, the server and receiver must alternately make a return until the rally is over. Returning the serve is one of the most difficult parts of the game, as the server's first move is often the least predictable and thus most advantageous shot due to the numerous spin and speed choices at his or her disposal.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Let">Let</span></h3>
<p>A Let is a rally of which the result is not scored, and is called in the following circumstances:<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF2.5.3_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF2.5.3-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The ball touches the net in service (service), provided the service is otherwise correct or the ball is obstructed by the player on the receiving side. Obstruction means a player touches the ball when it is above or traveling towards the playing surface, not having touched the player's court since last being struck by the player.</li>
<li>When the player on the receiving side is not ready and the service is delivered.</li>
<li>Player's failure to make a service or a return or to comply with the Laws is due to a disturbance outside the control of the player.</li>
<li>Play is interrupted by the umpire or assistant umpire.</li></ul>
<p>A let is also called foul service, if the ball hits the server's side of the table, if the ball does not pass further than the edge and if the ball hits the table edge and hits the net.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scoring">Scoring</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Table_tennis_umpire.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Table_tennis_umpire.jpg/220px-Table_tennis_umpire.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Table_tennis_umpire.jpg/330px-Table_tennis_umpire.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Table_tennis_umpire.jpg/440px-Table_tennis_umpire.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="683" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Table_tennis_umpire.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Table tennis umpire</div></div></div>
<p>A point is scored by the player for any of several results of the rally:<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF2.5.4_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF2.5.4-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>The opponent fails to make a correct service or return.</li>
<li>After making a service or a return, the ball touches anything other than the net assembly before being struck by the opponent.</li>
<li>The ball passes over the player's court or beyond their end line without touching their court, after being struck by the opponent.</li>
<li>The opponent obstructs the ball.</li>
<li>The opponent strikes the ball twice successively. Note that the hand that is holding the racket counts as part of the racket and that making a good return off one's hand or fingers is allowed. It is not a fault if the ball accidentally hits one's hand or fingers and then subsequently hits the racket.</li>
<li>The opponent strikes the ball with a side of the racket blade whose surface is not covered with rubber.</li>
<li>The opponent moves the playing surface or touches the net assembly.</li>
<li>The opponent's free hand touches the playing surface.</li>
<li>As a receiver under the expedite system, completing 13 returns in a rally.<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF2.15_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF2.15-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The opponent that has been warned by the umpire commits a second offense in the same individual match or team match. If the third offence happens, 2 points will be given to the player.<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF3.5.2_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF3.5.2-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> If the individual match or the team match has not ended, any unused penalty points can be transferred to the next game of that match.<sup id="cite_ref-match_officials_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-match_officials-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>A game shall be won by the player first scoring 11 points unless both players score 10 points, when the game shall be won by the first player subsequently gaining a lead of 2 points. A match shall consist of the best of any odd number of games.<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF2.11_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF2.11-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> In competition play, matches are typically best of five or seven games.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternation_of_services_and_ends">Alternation of services and ends</span></h3>
<p>Service alternates between opponents every two points (regardless of winner of the rally) until the end of the game, unless both players score ten points or the expedite system is operated, when the sequences of serving and receiving stay the same but each player serves for only one point in turn (Deuce).<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF2.13_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF2.13-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The player serving first in a game receives first in the next game of the match.
</p><p>After each game, players switch sides of the table. In the last possible game of a match, for example the seventh game in a best of seven matches, players change ends when the first player scores five points, regardless of whose turn it is to serve. If the sequence of serving and receiving is out of turn or the ends are not changed, points scored in the wrong situation are still calculated and the game shall be resumed with the order at the score that has been reached.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Doubles_game">Doubles game</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Service_en_double_(tennis_de_table).svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Service_en_double_%28tennis_de_table%29.svg/220px-Service_en_double_%28tennis_de_table%29.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Service_en_double_%28tennis_de_table%29.svg/330px-Service_en_double_%28tennis_de_table%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Service_en_double_%28tennis_de_table%29.svg/440px-Service_en_double_%28tennis_de_table%29.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="274" data-file-height="183" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Service_en_double_(tennis_de_table).svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Service zone in doubles game</div></div></div>
<p>In addition to games between individual players, pairs may also play table tennis. Singles and doubles are both played in international competition, including the Olympic Games since 1988 and the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Games" title="Commonwealth Games">Commonwealth Games</a> since 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> In 2005, the <a href="/wiki/International_Table_Tennis_Federation" title="International Table Tennis Federation">ITTF</a> announced that doubles table tennis only was featured as a part of team events in the 2008 Olympics.
</p><p>In doubles, all the rules of single play are applied except for the following.
</p><p><b>Service</b>
</p>
<dl><dd>A line painted along the long axis of the table to create doubles courts bisects the table. This line's only purpose is to facilitate the doubles service rule, which is that service must originate from the right hand "box" in such a way that the first bounce of the serve bounces once in said right hand box and then must bounce at least once in the opponent side's right hand box (far left box for server), or the receiving pair score a point.<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF2.6_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF2.6-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p><b>Order of play, serving and receiving</b>
</p>
<ol><li>Players must hit the ball in turn. For example, if A is paired with B, X is paired with Y, A is the server and X is the receiver. The order of play shall be A→X→B→Y. The rally proceeds this way until one side fails to make a legal return and the other side scores.<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF2.8_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF2.8-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>At each change of service, the previous receiver shall become the server and the partner of the previous server shall become the receiver. For example, if the previous order of play is A→X→B→Y, the order becomes X→B→Y→A after the change of service.<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF2.13_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF2.13-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In the second or the latter games of a match, the game begins in reverse order of play. For example, if the order of play is A→X→B→Y at beginning of the first game, the order begins with X→A→Y→B or Y→B→X→A in the second game depending on either X or Y being chosen as the first server of the game. That means the first receiver of the game is the player who served to the first server of the game in the preceding game. In each game of a doubles match, the pair having the right to serve first shall choose which of them will do so. The receiving pair, however, can only choose in the first game of the match.</li>
<li>When a pair reaches 5 points in the final game, the pairs must switch ends of the table and change the receiver to reverse the order of play. For example, when the last order of play before a pair score 5 points in the final game is A→X→B→Y, the order after change shall be A→Y→B→X if A still has the second serve. Otherwise, X is the next server and the order becomes X→A→Y→B.</li></ol>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expedite_system">Expedite system</span></h3>
<p>If a game is unfinished after 10 minutes' play and fewer than 18 points have been scored, the expedite system is initiated.<sup id="cite_ref-ITTF2.15_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ITTF2.15-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The umpire interrupts the game, and the game resumes with players serving for one point in turn. If the expedite system is introduced while the ball is not in play, the previous receiver shall serve first. Under the expedite system, the server must win the point before the opponent makes 13 consecutive returns or the point goes to the opponent. The system can also be initiated at any time at the request of both players or pairs. Once introduced, the expedite system remains in force until the end of the match. A rule to shorten the time of a match, it is mainly seen in defensive players' games.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grips">Grips</span></h2>
<p>Though table tennis players grip their rackets in various ways, their grips can be classified into two major families of styles, <i>penhold</i> and <i>shakehand</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>  The rules of table tennis do not prescribe the manner in which one must grip the racket, and numerous grips are employed.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Penhold">Penhold</span></h3>
<p>The penhold grip is so-named because one grips the racket similarly to the way one holds a <a href="/wiki/Writing_instrument" class="mw-redirect" title="Writing instrument">writing instrument</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-H13_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H13-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The style of play among penhold players can vary greatly from player to player. The most popular style, usually referred to as the Chinese penhold style, involves curling the middle, ring, and fourth finger on the back of the blade with the three fingers always touching one another.<sup id="cite_ref-H13_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H13-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Chinese penholders favour a round racket head, for a more over-the-table style of play. In contrast, another style, sometimes referred to as the Japanese/Korean penhold grip, involves splaying those three fingers out across the back of the racket, usually with all three fingers touching the back of the racket, rather than stacked upon one another.<sup id="cite_ref-H13_46-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H13-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Sometimes a combination of the two styles occurs, wherein the middle, ring and fourth fingers are straight, but still stacked, or where all fingers may be touching the back of the racket, but are also in contact with one another. Japanese and Korean penholders will often use a square-headed racket for an away-from-the-table style of play. Traditionally these square-headed rackets feature a block of cork on top of the handle, as well as a thin layer of cork on the back of the racket, for increased grip and comfort. Penhold styles are popular among players originating from <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asian</a> countries such as <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>.
</p><p>Traditionally, penhold players use only one side of the racket to hit the ball during normal play, and the side which is in contact with the last three fingers is generally not used. This configuration is sometimes referred to as "traditional penhold" and is more commonly found in square-headed racket styles. However, the <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Chinese</a> developed a technique in the 1990s in which a penholder uses both sides of the racket to hit the ball, where the player produces a backhand stroke (most often topspin) known as a reverse penhold backhand by turning the traditional side of the racket to face one's self, and striking the ball with the opposite side of the racket. This stroke has greatly improved and strengthened the penhold style both physically and psychologically, as it eliminates the strategic weakness of the traditional penhold backhand.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shakehand">Shakehand</span></h3>
<p>The shakehand grip is so-named because the racket is grasped as if one is performing a handshake.<sup id="cite_ref-H10_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H10-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Though it is sometimes referred to as the "tennis" or "Western" grip, it bears no relation to the Western <a href="/wiki/Tennis_grip" class="mw-redirect" title="Tennis grip">tennis grip</a>, which was popularized on the <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">West Coast of the United States</a> in which the racket is rotated 90°, and played with the wrist turned so that on impact the knuckles face the target.  In table tennis, "Western" refers to <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western</a> nations, for this is the grip that players native to Europe and the Americas have almost exclusively employed.
</p><p>The shakehand grip's simplicity and versatility, coupled with the acceptance among top-level Chinese trainers that the European style of play should be emulated and trained against, has established it as a common grip even in China.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Many world class European and East Asian players currently use the shakehand grip, and it is generally accepted that shakehands is easier to learn than penholder, allowing a broader range of playing styles both offensive and defensive.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seemiller">Seemiller</span></h3>
<p>The Seemiller grip is named after the American table tennis champion <a href="/wiki/Danny_Seemiller" title="Danny Seemiller">Danny Seemiller</a>, who used it. It is achieved by placing the thumb and index finger on either side of the bottom of the racquet head and holding the handle with the rest of the fingers. Since only one side of the racquet is used to hit the ball, two contrasting rubber types can be applied to the blade, offering the advantage of "twiddling" the racket to fool the opponent. Seemiller paired inverted rubber with anti-spin rubber. Many players today combine inverted and long-pipped rubber. The grip is considered exceptional for blocking, especially on the backhand side, and for forehand loops of backspin balls.<sup id="cite_ref-blog.paddlepalace.com_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blog.paddlepalace.com-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
The Seemiller grip's popularity reached its apex in 1985 when four (Danny Seemiller, Ricky Seemiller, Eric Boggan and Brian Masters) of the United States' five participants in the World Championships used it.<sup id="cite_ref-blog.paddlepalace.com_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blog.paddlepalace.com-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_strokes">Types of strokes</span></h2>
<p>Table tennis strokes generally break down into offensive and defensive categories.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Offensive_strokes">Offensive strokes</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Hit">Hit</span></h4>
<p>Also known as speed drive, a direct hit on the ball propelling it forward back to the opponent. This stroke differs from speed drives in other racket sports like tennis because the racket is primarily <i><a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">perpendicular</a></i> to the direction of the stroke and most of the energy applied to the ball results in <i>speed</i> rather than <i>spin</i>, creating a shot that does not arc much, but is fast enough that it can be difficult to return. A speed drive is used mostly for keeping the ball in play, applying pressure on the opponent, and potentially opening up an opportunity for a more powerful attack.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Loop">Loop</span></h4>
<p>Perfected during the 1960s,<sup id="cite_ref-H2_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H2-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hudetz_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hudetz-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> the loop is essentially the reverse of the chop. The racket is <i><a href="/wiki/Parallel_(geometry)" title="Parallel (geometry)">parallel</a></i> to the direction of the stroke ("closed") and the racket thus <i>grazes</i> the ball, resulting in a large amount of topspin. A good loop drive will arc quite a bit, and once striking the opponent's side of the table will jump forward, much like a kick serve in tennis. Most professional players nowadays, such as <a href="/wiki/Ding_Ning" title="Ding Ning">Ding Ning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Timo_Boll" title="Timo Boll">Timo Boll</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Jike" title="Zhang Jike">Zhang Jike</a>, primarily use loop for offense. 
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Counter-hit">Counter-hit</span></h4>
<p>The counter-hit is usually a counterattack against drives, normally high loop drives. The racket is held closed and near to the ball, which is hit with a short movement "off the bounce" (immediately after hitting the table) so that the ball travels faster to the other side. <a href="/wiki/Kenta_Matsudaira" title="Kenta Matsudaira">Kenta Matsudaira</a> is known for primarily using counter-hit for offense. 
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Flip">Flip</span></h4>
<p>When a player tries to attack a ball that has not bounced beyond the edge of the table, the player does not have the room to wind up in a <i>backswing</i>. The ball <i>may still be attacked</i>, however, and the resulting shot is called a flip because the backswing is compressed into a quick wrist action. A flip is not a single stroke and can resemble either a loop drive or a loop in its characteristics. What identifies the stroke is that the backswing is compressed into a short wrist flick.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Smash">Smash</span></h4>
<p>A player will typically execute a smash when the opponent has returned a ball that bounces too high or too close to the net. It is nearly always done with a forehand stroke. <i>Smashing</i> use rapid acceleration to impart as much speed on the ball as possible so that the opponent cannot react in time. The racket is generally perpendicular to the direction of the stroke. Because the speed is the main aim of this shot, the spin on the ball is often minimal, although it can be applied as well. An offensive table tennis player will think of a rally as a build-up to a winning smash. Smash is used more often with penhold grip. 
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defensive_strokes">Defensive strokes</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Push">Push</span></h4>
<p>The push (or "slice" in Asia) is usually used for keeping the point alive and creating offensive opportunities. A push resembles a tennis slice: the racket cuts underneath the ball, imparting backspin and causing the ball to float slowly to the other side of the table. A push can be difficult to attack because the backspin on the ball causes it to drop toward the table upon striking the opponent's racket. In order to attack a push, a player must usually loop (if the push is long) or flip (if the push is short) the ball back over the net. Often, the best option for beginners is to simply push the ball back again, resulting in pushing rallies. Against good players, it may be the worst option because the opponent will counter with a loop, putting the first player in a defensive position. Pushing can have advantages in some circumstances, such as when the opponent makes easy mistakes.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Chop">Chop</span></h4>
<p>A chop is the defensive, backspin counterpart to the offensive loop drive.<sup id="cite_ref-H89_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H89-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> A chop is essentially a bigger, heavier push, taken well back from the table. The racket face points primarily horizontally, perhaps a little bit upward, and the direction of the stroke is straight down. The object of a defensive chop is to match the topspin of the opponent's shot with backspin. A good chop will float nearly horizontally back to the table, in some cases having so much backspin that the ball actually <i>rises</i>. Such a chop can be <i>extremely</i> difficult to return due to its enormous amount of backspin. Some defensive players can also impart no-spin or sidespin variations of the chop. Some famous choppers include <a href="/wiki/Joo_Sae-hyuk" title="Joo Sae-hyuk">Joo Sae-hyuk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wu_Yang" title="Wu Yang">Wu Yang</a>. 
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Block">Block</span></h4>
<p>A block is executed by simply placing the racket in front of the ball right after the ball bounces; thus, the ball rebounds back toward the opponent with nearly as much energy as it came in with. This requires precision, since the ball's spin, speed, and location all influence the correct angle of a block. It is very possible for an opponent to execute a perfect loop, drive, or smash, only to have the blocked shot come back just as fast. Due to the power involved in offensive strokes, often an opponent simply cannot recover quickly enough to return the blocked shot, especially if the block is aimed at an unexpected side of the table. Blocks almost always produce the same spin as was received, many times topspin. 
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Lob">Lob</span></h4>
<p>The defensive lob propels the ball about five metres in height, only to land on the opponent's side of the table with great amounts of spin.<sup id="cite_ref-H96_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H96-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The stroke itself consists of lifting the ball to an enormous height before it falls back to the opponent's side of the table. A lob can have nearly any kind of spin. Though the opponent may <i>smash</i> the ball hard and fast, a good defensive lob could be more difficult to return due to the unpredictability and heavy amounts of the spin on the ball.<sup id="cite_ref-H96_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H96-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Thus, though backed off the table by tens of feet and running to reach the ball, a good defensive player can still win the point using good lobs. Lob is used less frequently by professional players. A notable exception is <a href="/wiki/Michael_Maze" title="Michael Maze">Michael Maze</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_of_spin">Effects of spin</span></h2>
<p>Adding spin onto the ball causes major changes in table tennis gameplay. Although nearly every stroke or serve creates some kind of spin, understanding the individual types of spin allows players to defend against and use different spins effectively.<sup id="cite_ref-H20_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H20-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:416px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wikipedia-TableTennis-BackspinCurve-4Phases.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d2/Wikipedia-TableTennis-BackspinCurve-4Phases.PNG" decoding="async" width="414" height="182" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="414" data-file-height="182" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption">4 phases in a backspin curve</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Backspin">Backspin</span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Backspin" title="Backspin">Backspin</a> is where the bottom half of the ball is rotating away from the player, and is imparted by striking the base of the ball with a downward movement.<sup id="cite_ref-H20_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H20-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> At the professional level, backspin is usually used defensively in order to keep the ball low.<sup id="cite_ref-H21_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H21-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Backspin is commonly employed in service because it is harder to produce an offensive return, though at the professional level most people serve sidespin with either backspin or topspin.  Due to the initial lift of the ball, there is a limit on how much speed with which one can hit the ball without missing the opponent's side of the table. However, backspin also makes it harder for the opponent to return the ball with great speed because of the required angular precision of the return. Alterations are frequently made to regulations regarding equipment in an effort to maintain a balance between defensive and offensive spin choices.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It is actually possible to smash with backspin offensively, but only on high balls that are close to the net.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:416px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wikipedia-TableTennis-TopspinCurve-4Phases.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6c/Wikipedia-TableTennis-TopspinCurve-4Phases.PNG" decoding="async" width="414" height="182" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="414" data-file-height="182" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption">4 phases in a topspin curve</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Topspin">Topspin</span></h3>
<p>The topspin stroke has a smaller influence on the first part of the ball-curve. Like the backspin stroke, however, the axis of spin remains roughly perpendicular to the trajectory of the ball thus allowing for the <a href="/wiki/Magnus_effect" title="Magnus effect">Magnus effect</a> to dictate the subsequent curvature. After the apex of the curve, the ball dips downwards as it approaches the opposing side, before bouncing. On the bounce, the topspin will accelerate the ball, much in the same way that a wheel which is already spinning would accelerate upon making contact with the ground. When the opponent attempts to return the ball, the topspin causes the ball to jump upwards and the opponent is forced to compensate for the topspin by adjusting the angle of his or her racket. This is known as "closing the racket".
</p><p>The speed limitation of the topspin stroke is minor compared to the backspin stroke. This stroke is the predominant technique used in professional competition because it gives the opponent less time to respond. In table tennis topspin is regarded as an offensive technique due to increased ball speed, lower bio-mechanical efficiency and the pressure that it puts on the opponent by reducing reaction time. (It is possible to play defensive topspin-lobs from far behind the table, but only highly skilled players use this stroke with any tactical efficiency.) Topspin is the least common type of spin to be found in service at the professional level, simply because it is much easier to attack a top-spin ball that is not moving at high speed.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sidespin">Sidespin</span></h3>
<p>This type of spin is predominantly employed during service, wherein the contact angle of the racket can be more easily varied. Unlike the two aforementioned techniques, sidespin causes the ball to spin on an axis which is vertical, rather than horizontal. The axis of rotation is still roughly perpendicular to the trajectory of the ball. In this circumstance, the <a href="/wiki/Magnus_effect" title="Magnus effect">Magnus effect</a> will still dictate the curvature of the ball to some degree. Another difference is that unlike backspin and topspin, sidespin will have relatively very little effect on the bounce of the ball, much in the same way that a spinning top would not travel left or right if its axis of rotation were exactly vertical. This makes sidespin a useful weapon in service, because it is less easily recognized when bouncing, and the ball "loses" less spin on the bounce. Sidespin can also be employed in offensive rally strokes, often from a greater distance, as an adjunct to topspin or backspin. This stroke is sometimes referred to as a "hook". The hook can even be used in some extreme cases to circumvent the net when away from the table.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Corkspin">Corkspin</span></h3>
<p>Players employ this type of spin almost exclusively when serving, but at the professional level, it is also used from time to time in the lob. Unlike any of the techniques mentioned above, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corkspin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Corkspin (page does not exist)">corkspin</a> (or "<a href="/w/index.php?title=Drill-spin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Drill-spin (page does not exist)">drill-spin</a>") has the axis of spin relatively parallel to the ball's trajectory, so that the <a href="/wiki/Magnus_effect" title="Magnus effect">Magnus effect</a> has little or no effect on the trajectory of a cork-spun ball: upon bouncing, the ball will dart right or left (according to the direction of the spin), severely complicating the return. In theory this type of spin produces the most obnoxious effects, but it is less strategically practical than sidespin or backspin, because of the limitations that it imposes upon the opponent during their return. Aside from the initial direction change when bouncing, unless it goes out of reach, the opponent can counter with <i>either</i> topspin or backspin. A backspin stroke is similar in the fact that the corkspin stroke has a lower maximum velocity, simply due to the contact angle of the racket when producing the stroke. To impart a spin on the ball which is parallel to its trajectory, the racket must be swung more or less perpendicular to the trajectory of the ball, greatly limiting the forward momentum that the racket transfers to the ball. Corkspin is almost always mixed with another variety of spin, since alone, it is not only less effective but also harder to produce.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Competition">Competition</span></h2>
<p>Competitive table tennis is popular in <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, and has been<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (September 2017)">vague</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> gaining attention in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Somaiya_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Somaiya-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> The most important international competitions are the <a href="/wiki/World_Table_Tennis_Championships" title="World Table Tennis Championships">World Table Tennis Championships</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Table_Tennis_World_Cup" title="Table Tennis World Cup">Table Tennis World Cup</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Table_tennis_at_the_Summer_Olympics" title="Table tennis at the Summer Olympics">Olympics</a> and the <a href="/wiki/ITTF_World_Tour" title="ITTF World Tour">ITTF World Tour</a>. Continental competitions include the following: 
</p>

<li><a href="/wiki/Europe_Top-16" title="Europe Top-16">Europe Top-16</a></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Asian_Table_Tennis_Championships" title="Asian Table Tennis Championships">Asian Championships</a></li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Asian_Games" title="Asian Games">Asian Games</a></li></ul>
<p>Chinese players have won 60% of the men's World Championships since 1959;<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> in the women's competition for the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corbillin_Cup&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Corbillin Cup (page does not exist)">Corbillin Cup</a>, Chinese players have won all but three of the World Championships since 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> Other strong teams come from East Asia and Europe, including countries such as <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">South Korea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are also professional competitions at the clubs level; the respective leagues of <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> (specifically, the <a href="/wiki/China_Table_Tennis_Super_League" title="China Table Tennis Super League">China Table Tennis Super League</a>), <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> are examples of the highest level. There are also some important international club teams competitions such as the <a href="/wiki/European_Champions_League_(table_tennis)" title="European Champions League (table tennis)">European Champions League</a> and its former competitor,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness"><span title="This information is too vague. (September 2017)">vague</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the <a href="/wiki/European_Club_Cup_of_Champions" title="European Club Cup of Champions">European Club Cup</a>, where the top club teams from European countries compete.
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Jean-Philippe_Gatien" title="Jean-Philippe Gatien">Jean-Philippe Gatien</a> (France) and <a href="/wiki/Wang_Hao_(table_tennis,_born_1983)" title="Wang Hao (table tennis, born 1983)">Wang Hao</a> (China) won both the World Championships and the World Cup, but lost in the gold medal matches at the Olympics. <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6rgen_Persson" title="Jörgen Persson">Jörgen Persson</a> (Sweden) also won the titles except the Olympic Games. Persson is one of the three table tennis players to have <a href="/wiki/List_of_athletes_with_the_most_appearances_at_Olympic_Games" title="List of athletes with the most appearances at Olympic Games">competed at seven Olympic Games</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ma_Lin_(table_tennis)" title="Ma Lin (table tennis)">Ma Lin</a> (China) won both the Olympic gold and the World Cup, but lost (three times, in 1999, 2005, and 2007) in the finals of the World Championships.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main category: <a href="/wiki/Category:Table_tennis_organizations" title="Category:Table tennis organizations">Table tennis organizations</a></div>
<p>Founded in 1926, the <a href="/wiki/International_Table_Tennis_Federation" title="International Table Tennis Federation">International Table Tennis Federation</a> (ITTF) is the worldwide <a href="/wiki/Sport_governing_body" class="mw-redirect" title="Sport governing body">governing</a> body for table tennis, which maintains an international ranking system in addition to organizing events like the <a href="/wiki/World_Table_Tennis_Championships" title="World Table Tennis Championships">World Table Tennis Championships</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-USATTabout_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USATTabout-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  In 2007, the governance for <a href="/wiki/Para_table_tennis" title="Para table tennis">table tennis for persons with a disability</a> was transferred from the <a href="/wiki/International_Paralympic_Committee" title="International Paralympic Committee">International Paralympic Committee</a> to the ITTF.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On many continents, there is a governing body responsible for table tennis on that continent. For example, the <a href="/wiki/European_Table_Tennis_Union" title="European Table Tennis Union">European Table Tennis Union</a> (ETTU) is the governing body responsible for table tennis in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> There are also national bodies and other local authorities responsible for the sport, such as <a href="/wiki/USA_Table_Tennis" title="USA Table Tennis">USA Table Tennis</a> (USATT), which is the national governing body for table tennis in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-USATTabout_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USATTabout-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
