<p><b>Surinam</b> (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i lang="nl">Suriname</i>) was a <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> <a href="/wiki/Plantation_economy" title="Plantation economy">plantation colony</a> in <a href="/wiki/The_Guianas" title="The Guianas">the Guianas</a>, neighboured by the equally Dutch colony of <a href="/wiki/Berbice" title="Berbice">Berbice</a> to the west, and the French colony of <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">Cayenne</a> to the east. Surinam was a Dutch colony from 26 February 1667, when Dutch forces captured <a href="/wiki/Francis_Willoughby,_5th_Baron_Willoughby_of_Parham" title="Francis Willoughby, 5th Baron Willoughby of Parham">Francis Willoughby</a>'s <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> colony during the <a href="/wiki/Second_Anglo-Dutch_War" title="Second Anglo-Dutch War">Second Anglo-Dutch War</a>, until 15 December 1954, when Surinam became a <a href="/wiki/Suriname_(Kingdom_of_the_Netherlands)" title="Suriname (Kingdom of the Netherlands)">constituent country of the Kingdom of the Netherlands</a>. The status quo of Dutch sovereignty over Surinam, and English sovereignty over <a href="/wiki/New_Netherland" title="New Netherland">New Netherland</a>, which it had conquered in 1664, was kept in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Breda_(1667)" title="Treaty of Breda (1667)">Treaty of Breda</a> of 31 July 1667, and again confirmed in the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Westminster_(1674)" title="Treaty of Westminster (1674)">Treaty of Westminster</a> of 1674.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOostindie20057-8_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOostindie20057-8-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_colonisation_of_the_Guianas" title="Dutch colonisation of the Guianas">other Dutch colonies in the Guianas</a>, i.e., <a href="/wiki/Berbice" title="Berbice">Berbice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Essequibo_(colony)" title="Essequibo (colony)">Essequibo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demerara" title="Demerara">Demerara</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pomeroon_(colony)" title="Pomeroon (colony)">Pomeroon</a>, were lost to the British in 1814, the remaining colony of Surinam was often referred to as <b>Dutch Guiana</b>, especially after 1831, when the British merged Berbice, Essequibo, and Demerara into <a href="/wiki/British_Guiana" title="British Guiana">British Guiana</a>. As the term Dutch Guiana was used in the 17th and 18th to refer to all Dutch colonies in the Guianas, this use of the term can be confusing (<a href="/wiki/Surinam_(Dutch_colony)#Dutch_Guiana" title="Surinam (Dutch colony)">see below</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surinam_(Dutch_colony)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Suriname" title="History of Suriname">History of Suriname</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:A_Surinam_Planter_in_his_Morning_Dress.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/A_Surinam_Planter_in_his_Morning_Dress.JPG/220px-A_Surinam_Planter_in_his_Morning_Dress.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="301" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/A_Surinam_Planter_in_his_Morning_Dress.JPG/330px-A_Surinam_Planter_in_his_Morning_Dress.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/A_Surinam_Planter_in_his_Morning_Dress.JPG/440px-A_Surinam_Planter_in_his_Morning_Dress.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1584" data-file-height="2167" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:A_Surinam_Planter_in_his_Morning_Dress.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An illustration of a Dutch plantation owner and slave from <a href="/wiki/William_Blake" title="William Blake">William Blake</a>'s illustrations of the work of <a href="/wiki/John_Gabriel_Stedman" title="John Gabriel Stedman">John Gabriel Stedman</a>, first published in 1792–1794.</div></div></div>
<p>The colonization of Surinam is marked by slavery. Plantations relied on slave labour, mostly supplied by the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_West_India_Company" title="Dutch West India Company">Dutch West India Company</a> from its trading posts in West Africa, to produce their crops. <a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">Sugar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indigo" title="Indigo">indigo</a> were the main goods exported from the colony to the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> until the early 18th century, when <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">coffee</a> became the single most important export product of Surinam. Planters' treatment of the slaves was notoriously bad<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>—historian <a href="/wiki/C.R._Boxer" class="mw-redirect" title="C.R. Boxer">C.R. Boxer</a> wrote that "man's inhumanity to man just about reached its limits in Surinam"<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>—and many slaves escaped the plantations. The <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Stock_Exchange" class="mw-redirect" title="Amsterdam Stock Exchange">Amsterdam stock market</a> crash of 1773 dealt a severe blow to the plantation economy, which was further exacerbated by the British abolition of slave trade in 1807.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBuddingh&#39;199957_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuddingh&#39;199957-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This abolition was adopted by <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_the_Netherlands" title="William I of the Netherlands">William I of the Netherlands</a>, who signed a royal decree in this regard in June 1814, and who concluded the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Dutch_Slave_Trade_Treaty" title="Anglo-Dutch Slave Trade Treaty">Anglo-Dutch Slave Trade Treaty</a> in May 1818. Many plantations went bankrupt as a consequence of the abolition of slave trade. Without supply of slaves, many plantations were merged to increase efficiency.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Abolition_of_slavery">Abolition of slavery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surinam_(Dutch_colony)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Abolition of slavery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Slavery was eventually abolished in 1863, although slaves were only released after a ten-year transitory period in 1873.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBakker199376_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBakker199376-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This spurred the immigration of <a href="/wiki/Indenture" title="Indenture">indentured</a> labourers from <a href="/wiki/British_India" class="mw-redirect" title="British India">British India</a>, after <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Dutch_Treaties_of_1870%E2%80%931871" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Dutch Treaties of 1870–1871">a treaty to that effect</a> had been signed between the Netherlands and the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> in 1870. Apart from immigration from British India, <a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Javanese</a> workers from the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Dutch East Indies</a> were also contracted to work on plantations in Surinam.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBakker1993105_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBakker1993105-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> At the same time, a largely unsuccessful attempt to colonize Surinam with <a href="/wiki/Boeroes" class="mw-redirect" title="Boeroes">impoverished farmers from the Netherlands</a> was started as well.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surinam_(Dutch_colony)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 20th century, the natural resources of Surinam, which include <a href="/wiki/Rubber" class="mw-redirect" title="Rubber">rubber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bauxite" title="Bauxite">bauxite</a>, were exploited. The <a href="/wiki/Gold_rush" title="Gold rush">gold rush</a> that followed the discovery of gold on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Lawa_River_(South_America)" title="Lawa River (South America)">Lawa River</a> spurred the construction of the Lawa Railway in 1902, although construction was halted after gold production proved disappointing. In 1916, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S.</a> <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> company <a href="/wiki/Alcoa" title="Alcoa">Alcoa</a> began mining bauxite in on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Cottica_River" title="Cottica River">Cottica River</a>, near the village of <a href="/wiki/Moengo" title="Moengo">Moengo</a>. In 1938, the company built an aluminium <a href="/wiki/Smelter" class="mw-redirect" title="Smelter">smelter</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paranam" title="Paranam">Paranam</a>.
</p><p>Partly due to the importance of Surinamese aluminium for the allied war effort, United States troops occupied Surinam under an agreement with the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_government_in_exile" class="mw-redirect" title="Dutch government in exile">Dutch government in exile</a> on 23 November 1941. Under the provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Charter" title="Atlantic Charter">Atlantic Charter</a> of August 1941, the Dutch government in exile promised to end the colonial relations between the Netherlands and its overseas possessions, promising them far-reaching autonomy and self-rule. This was eventually accomplished by the proclamation of the <a href="/wiki/Charter_for_the_Kingdom_of_the_Netherlands" title="Charter for the Kingdom of the Netherlands">Charter for the Kingdom of the Netherlands</a> on 15 December 1954, which constituted a Kingdom in which the Netherlands, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Antilles" title="Netherlands Antilles">Netherlands Antilles</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Suriname_(Kingdom_of_the_Netherlands)" title="Suriname (Kingdom of the Netherlands)">Suriname</a> participated in on a basis of equality. In 1975, Suriname left the Kingdom of the Netherlands to become the independent country of <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Administration">Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surinam_(Dutch_colony)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From 1683, the colony was governed by the <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Suriname" title="Society of Suriname">Society of Suriname</a>, a company composed of three equal shareholders, being the city of <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>, the family <a href="/wiki/Cornelis_van_Aerssen_van_Sommelsdijck" title="Cornelis van Aerssen van Sommelsdijck">Van Aerssen van Sommelsdijck</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_West_India_Company" title="Dutch West India Company">Dutch West India Company</a>. Although the organization and administration was of the colony was limited to these three shareholders, all citizens of the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Republic" title="Dutch Republic">Dutch Republic</a> were free to trade with Suriname.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Also, the planters were consulted in a <a href="/w/index.php?title=Council_of_Police&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Council of Police (page does not exist)">Council of Police</a>, which was a unique feature among the colonies of Guiana.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 1795, the Society was nationalized by the <a href="/wiki/Batavian_Republic" title="Batavian Republic">Batavian Republic</a>. From then on until 1954, the Batavian Republic and its legal successors (the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Holland" title="Kingdom of Holland">Kingdom of Holland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Netherlands" title="Kingdom of the Netherlands">Kingdom of the Netherlands</a>) governed the territory as a national colony, barring a period of British occupation between 1799 and 1802, and between 1804 and 1816.
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Batavian_Republic" title="Batavian Republic">Batavian Republic</a> took over in 1795, the Dutch government issued various <a href="/wiki/Government_regulation_for_Suriname" class="mw-redirect" title="Government regulation for Suriname">government regulations for Suriname</a> (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i>Regeringsreglement voor Suriname</i>), establishing the government of the colony. In 1865, a new government regulation replaced the previous regulation of 1832, which theoretically gave Suriname some limited self-rule.<sup id="cite_ref-Paula_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Paula-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The colonial elite was given the right to elect a Colonial Council (Dutch: <i>Koloniale Raad</i>) which would co-govern the colony together with the Governor-General appointed by the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_Netherlands" title="Monarchy of the Netherlands">Dutch crown</a>. Among others, the Colonial Council was allowed to decide over the colony's budget, which was subject to approval by the Dutch crown, but which did not see any involvement of Dutch parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-Paula_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Paula-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_60038537_Waarnemend_gouverneur_Rietberg_en_gou.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_60038537_Waarnemend_gouverneur_Rietberg_en_gou.jpg/220px-Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_60038537_Waarnemend_gouverneur_Rietberg_en_gou.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_60038537_Waarnemend_gouverneur_Rietberg_en_gou.jpg/330px-Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_60038537_Waarnemend_gouverneur_Rietberg_en_gou.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_60038537_Waarnemend_gouverneur_Rietberg_en_gou.jpg/440px-Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_60038537_Waarnemend_gouverneur_Rietberg_en_gou.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="493" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_60038537_Waarnemend_gouverneur_Rietberg_en_gou.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Baron <a href="/wiki/Aarnoud_van_Heemstra" title="Aarnoud van Heemstra">Aarnoud van Heemstra</a>, the governor of Suriname, 1923</div></div></div>
<p>In the wake of the 1922 Dutch constitutional revision, in which the term "colony" was replaced by "overseas territory", the 1865 government regulation was replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Basic_Law_of_Suriname" class="mw-redirect" title="Basic Law of Suriname">Basic Law of Suriname</a> (Dutch: <i>Staatsregeling van Suriname</i>) on 1 April 1937. This Basic Law renamed the Colonial Council to Estates of Suriname (Dutch: <i>Staten van Suriname</i>) and increased the membership from 13 to 15.
</p><p>After the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, during which the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_government_in_exile" class="mw-redirect" title="Dutch government in exile">Dutch government in exile</a> had pledged to review the relationship between the Netherlands and its colonies, the Basic Law was heavily revised. In March 1948, revisions to the Basic Law were adopted by Dutch parliament, which introduced <a href="/wiki/Universal_suffrage" title="Universal suffrage">universal suffrage</a> for both men and women, which increased the membership of the Estates from 15 to 21, and which introduced a College of General Government (Dutch: <i>College van Algemeen Bestuur</i>) which was to assist the Governor in the everyday government of the colony, and which was the precursor to the Cabinet of Ministers.<sup id="cite_ref-Suriname.nu_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Suriname.nu-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The new constitution took effect in July 1948.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Military">Military</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surinam_(Dutch_colony)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Military">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1868 the Dutch government created the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Troepenmacht_in_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Troepenmacht in Suriname (page does not exist)">Troepenmacht in Suriname</a> (TRIS) which served as the Dutch colonial army in Suriname.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> This meant that like the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Netherlands_East_Indies_Army" title="Royal Netherlands East Indies Army">Royal Netherlands East Indies Army</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Dutch East Indies</a>, TRIS fell under the responsibility of the Dutch Ministry of Colonies, instead of the Dutch Ministry of Defense. The size of the TRIS army was, however, small, compared to the KNIL army, it consisted of 
two infantry and two artillery companies. In total 636 soldiers served in the TRIS army. These soldiers were tasked with patrolling and policing duties within the Dutch colony of Surinam.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dutch_Guiana">Dutch Guiana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Surinam_(Dutch_colony)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dutch Guiana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although the colony has always been officially known as Surinam or Suriname, in both Dutch<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and English,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> the colony was often unofficially and semi-officially referred to as Dutch Guiana (Dutch: <i>Nederlands Guiana</i>) in the 19th and 20th century, in an analogy to <a href="/wiki/British_Guiana" title="British Guiana">British Guiana</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>. Using this term for Suriname is problematic, however, as historically Suriname was only one of many Dutch colonies in <a href="/wiki/The_Guianas" title="The Guianas">the Guianas</a>, others being <a href="/wiki/Berbice" title="Berbice">Berbice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Essequibo_(colony)" title="Essequibo (colony)">Essequibo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demerara" title="Demerara">Demerara</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pomeroon_(colony)" title="Pomeroon (colony)">Pomeroon</a>, which after being taken over by the United Kingdom in 1814, were united into British Guiana in 1831. Before 1814, the term Dutch Guiana did not describe a distinct political entity, but rather all colonies under Dutch sovereignty taken together.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> While referring to post-1814 governors of Suriname as governors of Dutch Guiana seems harmless, it is problematic doing the same for the pre-1814 governors, as that would imply that they had jurisdiction over the other Dutch colonies in the Guianas, which they had not.
</p>
