<p><b>Manchu names</b> are the names of the <a href="/wiki/Manchu_people" title="Manchu people">Manchu people</a> in their <a href="/wiki/Manchu_language" title="Manchu language">own language</a>. In addition to such names, most modern Manchus live in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> and possess <a href="/wiki/Chinese_name" title="Chinese name">Chinese names</a>.
</p><p>Traditionally, Manchus were called only by their <a href="/wiki/Manchu_given_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchu given name">given names</a> in daily life although each belonged to a <a href="/wiki/Manchu_clan" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchu clan">clan</a> with its own <a href="/wiki/Manchu_family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchu family name">clan name</a> (<a href="/wiki/Manchu_language" title="Manchu language">Manchu</a>: <i>hala</i>). Each clan would be divided into several sub-clans (<i>mukūn</i>), but these did not have separate names.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Given_names">Given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Manchu_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Manchus given names are distinctive. Generally, there are several forms, such as bearing suffixes "-ngga", "-ngge" or "-nggo", meaning "having the quality of";<sup id="cite_ref-杂识979_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-杂识979-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> bearing the suffixes "-tai" or "-tu", meaning "having";<sup id="cite_ref-Elliott243_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott243-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-杂识978_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-杂识978-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> bearing the suffix, "-ju", "-boo";<sup id="cite_ref-Elliott243_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott243-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> numerals <sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Elliott243_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott243-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-杂识978_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-杂识978-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> or animal names<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;note 2&#93;</a></sup>.<sup id="cite_ref-杂识979_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-杂识979-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Elliott243_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott243-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Manchu given names were used solely or with titles but not with clan names. For example, Fiyanggū, who was from the Donggo clan, belonged to the Manchu <a href="/wiki/Plain_White_Banner" title="Plain White Banner">Plain White Banner</a> and distinguished himself in the campaigns against the <a href="/wiki/Dzungars" class="mw-redirect" title="Dzungars">Dzungars</a>, was usually called "Fiyanggū be" (Lord Fiyanggū) since Fiyanggū (youngest) was a relatively major given name. Unlike Chinese and Europeans, he was not to be called by combination of family name and given name, i.e. Donggo Fiyanggū or Fiyanggū Donggo. Although we can find <a href="/wiki/Aisin-Gioro_Ulhicun" title="Aisin-Gioro Ulhicun">Aisin-Gioro Ulhicun</a> and other figures, but it is a very modern practice. To specify the clan name, Manchus would have said something like "Donggo hala-i Fiyanggū" (Fiyanggū of the Donggo clan).
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Manchu" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchu">Manchus</a> had an immense variety of <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given names</a>. For most of them, it is difficult to find the meanings. Some scholars try to categorize them. <a href="/wiki/Erich_Haenisch" title="Erich Haenisch">Erich Haenisch</a> classified them into sixteen categories including animals (Eje: bull, Yelu: boar), plants (Fodo: willow, Maca: garlic), qualities (Ayan: big, Bayan: rich, Niowanggiyan: green), etc. Ch'en Chieh-hsien classified Manchu personal names into seven main categories. But there are many names that are not included in either categorization.
</p><p>Some Manchu names seem nothing more than partial phonetic alternation of other ones. For example, the names of brothers of a clan were Ulušun, Hūlušun, Ilušun, Delušun, Fulušun and Jalušun in order of age, where only the initial syllables are changed. Another example is <a href="/wiki/Nurhaci" title="Nurhaci">Nurhaci</a>. His brothers were <a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%A0urgaci&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Šurgaci (page does not exist)">Šurgaci</a> and Murhaci.
</p><p>Like other non-Chinese terms, Manchu names are often transcribed into Chinese in a chaotic pattern since they were taken from Chinese sources. It is difficult to reconstruct original Manchu spellings from their Chinese transcription. Sometimes the first syllable of a Manchu given name is misinterpreted as a Chinese surname. For instance, the Manchu official <a href="/wiki/Tuli%C5%A1en" title="Tulišen">Tulišen</a>, who wrote <a href="/wiki/Narrative_of_the_Chinese_Embassy_to_the_Khan_of_the_Tourgouth_Tartars,_in_the_years_1712,_13,_14,_and_15" class="mw-redirect" title="Narrative of the Chinese Embassy to the Khan of the Tourgouth Tartars, in the years 1712, 13, 14, and 15">a famous travel record</a>, is mistaken for a Chinese man named "Tu Lishen."
</p><p>Nikan (Han Chinese) was a common first name for Manchus.<sup id="cite_ref-Elliott2001_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elliott2001-6">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nikan_Wailan" title="Nikan Wailan">Nikan Wailan</a> was a Jurchen leader who was an enemy of Nurhaci.<sup id="cite_ref-Crossley2000_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crossley2000-7">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-JR.1985_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JR.1985-8">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wakeman1977_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wakeman1977-9">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Nikan was the name of one the <a href="/wiki/Aisin_Gioro" title="Aisin Gioro">Aisin Gioro</a> princes and grandsons of Nurhaci who supported Prince <a href="/wiki/Dorgon" title="Dorgon">Dorgon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rawski1998_2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rawski1998_2-10">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-JR.1985_2_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JR.1985_2-11">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lui1989_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lui1989-12">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Nurhaci's first son was <a href="/wiki/Cuyen" title="Cuyen">Cuyen</a>, one of whose sons was Nikan.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Qing_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Qing Dynasty">Qing Dynasty</a>, the Manchus gradually adopted two-character Chinese given names (but not family names), and used Manchu transcription of them. We can find a tendency to leave a space between two syllables of the name of an exalted personage in the <a href="/wiki/Manchu_script" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchu script">Manchu script</a> and to stick them together for common people. For example, the real name of the <a href="/wiki/Qianlong_Emperor" title="Qianlong Emperor">Qianlong Emperor</a> was <i>Hung li</i>, which was derived from the Chinese name Hongli (弘曆). Certain combinations of Chinese sounds that never appeared at native Manchu terms make it difficult to determine syllable boundaries. The Manchus introduced what is called "Mongolian Sibe Syllable Boundary Marker" in Unicode. As the name says, it is formalized in the <a href="/wiki/Xibe_script" class="mw-redirect" title="Xibe script">Sibe script</a> but can also be found in Manchu literature. The marker represented as the grapheme of the middle form of letter A is put on a syllable boundary so that we can distinguish Guangying (Guwang'ing) from Guanjing (Guwanging), etc.
</p>

<p>Like the <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongols</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Manchu" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchu">Manchus</a> were simply called by given name but they had their own <a href="/wiki/Clan" title="Clan">clan</a> names (<i>hala</i> in <a href="/wiki/Manchu_language" title="Manchu language">Manchu</a>). <i>Hala</i> consisted of several <i>mukūn</i>, the unit of <a href="/wiki/Exogamy" title="Exogamy">exogamy</a>. Unlike <i>hala</i>, <i>mukūn</i> did not have corresponding names.
</p><p><i>The Comprehensive Book of the Eight Manchurian <a href="/wiki/Banner_(Manchu)" class="mw-redirect" title="Banner (Manchu)">Banners</a>' Surname-Clans</i> (八旗滿洲氏族通譜 <i>Baqi Manzhou Zhizu Tongpu</i>), compiled in the middle 18th century, records many Manchu clan names. Among more than a thousand names, about 600 names are the Manchus'.
</p><p>Gioro, a major clan name, is the one of the few <i>hala</i> that has various variants such as Irgen, Silin and Šušu, possibly to distinguish from the imperial family name Aisin-Gioro.  Since the mid-to-late Qing Dynasty, Manchus have increasingly adopted Han Chinese surnames, and today, very few Manchus bear traditional Manchu family names.
</p>
