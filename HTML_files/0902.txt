<p><b>Joseph Patrick Kennedy Jr.</b> (July 25, 1915 – August 12, 1944) was a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_(navy)" title="Lieutenant (navy)">lieutenant</a>. He was killed in action during World War II while serving as a land-based patrol bomber pilot, and was posthumously awarded the <a href="/wiki/Navy_Cross" title="Navy Cross">Navy Cross</a>. He was the eldest of nine children born to <a href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Kennedy_Sr." title="Joseph P. Kennedy Sr.">Joseph P. Kennedy Sr.</a> (1888–1969) and <a href="/wiki/Rose_Fitzgerald_Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Rose Fitzgerald Kennedy">Rose Fitzgerald Kennedy</a> (1890–1995). He was the only Kennedy son who never sought political office, though he had planned to. His three younger brothers attained distinguished political positions: <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> (1917–1963) served as a U.S. Senator from Massachusetts and as <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">U.S. President</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a> (1925–1968) served as <a href="/wiki/Attorney-General_of_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Attorney-General of the United States">U.S. Attorney General</a> and as a U.S. Senator from New York, and Senator <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Ted Kennedy</a> (1932–2009) also served as a U.S. Senator from Massachusetts.
</p><p>Joe Sr. had aspirations for Joe Jr. to become president.<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (August 2015)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  However, Joe Jr. was killed while participating in a <a href="/wiki/Operation_Aphrodite" title="Operation Aphrodite">top-secret mission</a> in 1944, and the high expectations of the father then fell upon Joe Jr.'s younger brother John, who was later elected president.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_P._Kennedy_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kennedy was born on July 25, 1915, in <a href="/wiki/Hull,_Massachusetts" title="Hull, Massachusetts">Hull, Massachusetts</a>. He first attended the <a href="/wiki/Dexter_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Dexter School">Dexter School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brookline,_Massachusetts" title="Brookline, Massachusetts">Brookline, Massachusetts</a>, with his brother, John. In 1933, he graduated from the <a href="/wiki/Choate_School" class="mw-redirect" title="Choate School">Choate School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wallingford,_Connecticut" title="Wallingford, Connecticut">Wallingford, Connecticut</a>. He then entered <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College" title="Harvard College">Harvard College</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge, Massachusetts</a>, graduating in 1938. Kennedy participated in <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">football</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rugby_football" title="Rugby football">rugby</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sport_rowing" class="mw-redirect" title="Sport rowing">crew</a>, and he served on the student council. Kennedy then spent a year studying under the tutelage of <a href="/wiki/Harold_Laski" title="Harold Laski">Harold Laski</a> at the <a href="/wiki/London_School_of_Economics" title="London School of Economics">London School of Economics</a> before enrolling in <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Law_School" title="Harvard Law School">Harvard Law School</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_ambitions_and_views">Political ambitions and views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_P._Kennedy_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political ambitions and views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From a very young age, Kennedy was groomed by his father and predicted to be the first Roman Catholic president of the United States. When he was born, his grandfather <a href="/wiki/John_F._Fitzgerald" title="John F. Fitzgerald">John F. Fitzgerald</a>, then Mayor of Boston, told the news, "This child is the future president of the nation."<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He often boasted that he would be president even without help from his father. He was a delegate to the <a href="/wiki/1940_Democratic_National_Convention" title="1940 Democratic National Convention">Democratic National Convention</a> in 1940. Kennedy planned to run for the U.S. House from <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts%27s_11th_congressional_district" title="Massachusetts&#39;s 11th congressional district">Massachusetts's 11th congressional district</a> in 1946.
</p><p>Kennedy expressed approval of <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a> before his conquest of Europe began. His father sent him to visit Nazi Germany in 1934. He wrote to his father, praising Hitler's sterilization policy as "a great thing" that "will do away with many of the disgusting specimens of men."<sup id="cite_ref-nyt.gordon_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt.gordon-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He explained that "Hitler is building a spirit in his men that could be envied in any country."<sup id="cite_ref-jpost_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jpost-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-vf_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vf-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="U.S._Navy_.281941.E2.80.931944.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="U.S._Navy_(1941–1944)">U.S. Navy (1941–1944)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_P._Kennedy_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: U.S. Navy (1941–1944)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kennedy left before his final year of law school at Harvard to enlist in the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Naval_Reserve" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Naval Reserve">U.S. Naval Reserve</a> on June 24, 1941.<sup id="cite_ref-navsource.org_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-navsource.org-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He entered flight training to be a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Aviator" title="United States Naval Aviator">Naval Aviator</a>, and after training, he received his wings and was commissioned an <a href="/wiki/Ensign_(rank)" title="Ensign (rank)">ensign</a> on May 5, 1942.<sup id="cite_ref-navsource.org_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-navsource.org-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He was assigned to Patrol Squadron 203 and then Bombing Squadron 110.<sup id="cite_ref-navsource.org_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-navsource.org-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In September 1943, he was sent to Britain and became a member of Bomber Squadron 110, Special Air Unit ONE, in 1944. He piloted land-based <a href="/wiki/B-24_Liberator" class="mw-redirect" title="B-24 Liberator">PB4Y Liberator</a> patrol bombers on <a href="/wiki/Anti-submarine_warfare" title="Anti-submarine warfare">anti-submarine</a> details during two tours of duty in the winter of 1943–1944. Kennedy had completed 25 combat missions and was eligible to return home. He instead volunteered for an <a href="/wiki/Operation_Aphrodite#Mission_theory" title="Operation Aphrodite">Operation Aphrodite mission</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sorensen_1966_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sorensen_1966-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operations_Anvil_and_Aphrodite">Operations Anvil and Aphrodite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_P._Kennedy_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Operations Anvil and Aphrodite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Operation_Aphrodite" title="Operation Aphrodite">Operation Aphrodite</a> (U.S. Army Air Corps) and Operation Anvil (U.S. Navy) made use of unmanned, explosive-laden Army Air Corps <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-17_Flying_Fortress" title="Boeing B-17 Flying Fortress">Boeing B-17 Flying Fortress</a> and Navy <a href="/wiki/Consolidated_B-24_Liberator" title="Consolidated B-24 Liberator">Consolidated PB4Y-1 Liberator</a> bombers that were deliberately crashed into their targets under <a href="/wiki/Radio_control" title="Radio control">radio control</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Sorensen_1966_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sorensen_1966-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> These aircraft could not take off safely on their own, so a crew of two would take off and fly to 2,000 feet (610&#160;m) before activating the remote control system, arming the detonators, and parachuting from the aircraft.
</p><p>Kennedy was appointed a <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_(navy)" title="Lieutenant (navy)">Lieutenant</a> on July 1, 1944.<sup id="cite_ref-navsource.org_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-navsource.org-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> After the U.S. Army Air Corps operation missions were drawn up on July 23, 1944, Lieutenants Wilford John Willy<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and Kennedy were designated as the first Navy flight crew. Willy, who was the executive officer of Special Air Unit ONE, had also volunteered for the mission and "pulled rank" over <a href="/wiki/Ensign_(rank)" title="Ensign (rank)">Ensign</a> James Simpson, who was Kennedy's regular co-pilot. Kennedy and Willy (co-pilot) flew a BQ-8 "robot" aircraft (drone; a converted B-24 Liberator) for the U.S. Navy's first Aphrodite mission. Two <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Ventura" title="Lockheed Ventura">Lockheed Ventura</a> mother planes and a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-17" class="mw-redirect" title="Boeing B-17">Boeing B-17</a> navigation plane took off from <a href="/wiki/RAF_Fersfield" title="RAF Fersfield">RAF Fersfield</a> at 1800 on Saturday, August 12, 1944. Then the BQ-8 aircraft, loaded with 21,170&#160;lb (9,600&#160;kg) of <a href="/wiki/Torpex" title="Torpex">Torpex</a>, took off. It was to be used against the U-boat pens at <a href="/wiki/Heligoland" title="Heligoland">Heligoland</a> in the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Freeman_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Freeman-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Baugher_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baugher-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Following them in a <a href="/wiki/USAAF" class="mw-redirect" title="USAAF">USAAF</a> <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Mosquito" title="De Havilland Mosquito">F-8 Mosquito</a> to film the mission were pilot Lt. Robert A. Tunnel and combat camera man Lt. David J. McCarthy, who filmed the event from the <a href="/wiki/Perspex" class="mw-redirect" title="Perspex">perspex</a> nose of the aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-Hansen_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hansen-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  As planned, Kennedy and Willy remained aboard as the BQ-8 completed its first remote-controlled turn at 2,000&#160;ft (610&#160;m) near the North Sea coast. Kennedy and Willy removed the safety pin, arming the explosive package, and Kennedy radioed the agreed code <i>Spade Flush</i>, his last known words.  Two minutes later (and well before the planned crew bailout, near <a href="/wiki/RAF_Manston" title="RAF Manston">RAF Manston</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Torpex" title="Torpex">Torpex</a> explosive detonated prematurely and destroyed the Liberator, killing Kennedy and Willy instantly. Wreckage landed near the village of <a href="/wiki/Blythburgh" title="Blythburgh">Blythburgh</a> in <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a>, England, causing widespread damage and small fires, but there were no injuries on the ground. According to one report, a total of 59 buildings were damaged in a nearby coastal town.
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Attempted first Aphrodite attack Twelve August with robot taking off from Fersfield at One Eight Zero Five Hours. Robot exploded in the air at approximately two thousand feet eight miles southeast of Halesworth at One Eight Two Zero hours. Wilford J. Willy Sr Grade Lieutenant and Joseph P. Kennedy Sr Grade Lieutenant, both USNR, were killed. Commander  Smith, in command of this unit, is making full report TO US Naval Operations. A more detailed report will be forwarded to you when interrogation is completed</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite>Top Secret <a href="/wiki/Telegram" class="mw-redirect" title="Telegram">telegram</a> to General <a href="/wiki/Carl_Andrew_Spaatz" class="mw-redirect" title="Carl Andrew Spaatz">Carl Andrew Spaatz</a> from General <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Doolittle" title="Jimmy Doolittle">Jimmy Doolittle</a>, August 1944<sup id="cite_ref-Renehan_2002_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Renehan_2002-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>According to USAAF records, the trailing Mosquito "was flying 300 feet above and about 300 yards to the rear of the robot. Engineer photographer on this ship was injured, and the ship was damaged slightly by the explosion."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The Mosquito, which made an immediate emergency landing at <a href="/wiki/RAF_Halesworth" title="RAF Halesworth">RAF Halesworth</a>, belonged to the <a href="/wiki/325th_Reconnaissance_Wing" class="mw-redirect" title="325th Reconnaissance Wing">325th Reconnaissance Wing</a>, a unit under the command of the son of President <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>, then Colonel <a href="/wiki/Elliott_Roosevelt" title="Elliott Roosevelt">Elliott Roosevelt</a>. Years later, Roosevelt claimed to have been aboard this trailing aircraft, and his version of the event has gained wide currency.<sup id="cite_ref-Searls_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Searls-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> However, Air Force records cannot substantiate this. Instead, an after-action account by the 8th Combat Camera Unit (CCU) noted that:
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JosephKennedy.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/JosephKennedy.jpg/170px-JosephKennedy.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/JosephKennedy.jpg/255px-JosephKennedy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/JosephKennedy.jpg/340px-JosephKennedy.jpg 2x" data-file-width="768" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JosephKennedy.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Memorial for Joseph Kennedy Jr. inside the <a href="/wiki/Fortress_of_Mimoyecques" title="Fortress of Mimoyecques">fortress of Mimoyecques</a> (France)</div></div></div>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>... the Baby just exploded in mid-air as we neared it and I was knocked halfway back to the cockpit. A few pieces of the Baby came through the <a href="/wiki/Plexiglass" class="mw-redirect" title="Plexiglass">plexiglass</a> nose and I got hit in the head and caught a lot of fragments in my right arm. I crawled back to the cockpit and lowered the wheels so that Bob could make a quick emergency landing, ...</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite>Lt. McCarthy reported from his hospital bed.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The 8th CCU film of the event, has, so far as is known, not been found.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 20th Fighter Group out of King's Cliffe was tasked with providing escort to the Aphrodite mission.  Escort consisted of P-51s out of the 55th and 79th Fighter Squadrons, each squadron provided two aircraft. VIII FC, Field Order 509 stated "20 GP (P-51's, 4 A/C) will proceed to Fersfield and land coordinating with operations where to provide close escort support to one B-34 special Operation."
</p><p>Lt John E. Klink reported in this mission summary report  "Took off to excort BXXX, 1 B24, 1 B17, 2 B34s, and 3 photo Recons (2 Mosq. -1 P38). When specially loaded B24 was at approx.. 2000 ft. NE of Ipswich it exploded and crashed near small lake. No one got out of the plane. Rest of ships OK in spite of terrific concussion from explosion.  All returned to base." [Sic]
</p><p>The Navy's informal board of review, discussing a number of theories, discounted the possibility of the crew making a mistake or that suspected jamming or a stray signal could have armed and detonated the explosives. An electronics officer, Earl Olsen, who believed the wiring harness had a design defect, had warned Kennedy of this possibility the day before the mission, but he was ignored.<sup id="cite_ref-Renehan_2002_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Renehan_2002-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy and Willy were both <a href="/wiki/Posthumous_recognition" class="mw-redirect" title="Posthumous recognition">posthumously</a> awarded the <a href="/wiki/Navy_Cross_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Navy Cross (United States)">Navy Cross</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Air_Medal" title="Air Medal">Air Medal</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Purple_Heart_Medal" class="mw-redirect" title="Purple Heart Medal">Purple Heart Medal</a>.
</p><p>The names of both men are listed on the Tablets of the Missing at the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_American_Cemetery_and_Memorial" title="Cambridge American Cemetery and Memorial">Cambridge American Cemetery and Memorial</a>, a cemetery and chapel near the village of <a href="/wiki/Madingley" title="Madingley">Madingley</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a>, Britain, that commemorates American servicemen who died in World War II.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
Later reports that Kennedy's final mission were kept top secret until many years later<sup id="cite_ref-Olsen_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Olsen-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> are negated by a detailed public account of the operation and Kennedy's death released in 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Navy_Cross_citation">Navy Cross citation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_P._Kennedy_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Navy Cross citation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The President of the United States of America takes pride in presenting the Navy Cross (Posthumously) to Lieutenant Joseph Patrick Kennedy, United States Navy, for extraordinary heroism in operations against the enemy while serving as Commander of a Navy Liberator Patrol Plane in Bombing Squadron ONE HUNDRED TEN (VB-110), Special Air Unit ONE (Europe), during a special air mission directed at Mimoyecques, France, on August 12, 1944. Well knowing the extreme dangers involved and totally unconcerned for his own safety, Lieutenant Kennedy unhesitatingly volunteered to conduct an exceptionally hazardous and special operational mission. Intrepid and daring in his tactics and with unwavering confidence in the vital importance of his task, he willingly risked his life in the supreme measure of service, and, by his great personal valor and fortitude in carrying out a perilous undertaking, sustained and enhanced the finest traditions of the United States Naval Service.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Joseph_P._Kennedy_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1946, the Navy named a <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyer</a> for Kennedy, the <a href="/wiki/USS_Joseph_P._Kennedy_Jr._(DD-850)" class="mw-redirect" title="USS Joseph P. Kennedy Jr. (DD-850)">USS&#160;<i>Joseph P. Kennedy Jr.</i></a>, aboard which his younger brother, (future <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">U.S. Senator</a>) <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a>, briefly served. Among the highlights of its service are the blockade of <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Missile_Crisis" title="Cuban Missile Crisis">Cuban Missile Crisis</a> in 1962 and the afloat recovery teams for <i><a href="/wiki/Gemini_6" class="mw-redirect" title="Gemini 6">Gemini 6</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Gemini_7" title="Gemini 7">Gemini 7</a></i>, both 1965 <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">manned spaceflights</a> in <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Gemini_program" class="mw-redirect" title="Gemini program">Gemini program</a>. It is now a floating museum in <a href="/wiki/Battleship_Cove" title="Battleship Cove">Battleship Cove</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fall_River,_Massachusetts" title="Fall River, Massachusetts">Fall River, Massachusetts</a>.
</p><p>In 1947, the Kennedys established the <a href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Kennedy_Jr._Foundation" title="Joseph P. Kennedy Jr. Foundation">Joseph P. Kennedy Jr. Foundation</a> and funded the construction of the Joseph P. Kennedy Jr. Memorial Hall at <a href="/wiki/Boston_College" title="Boston College">Boston College</a>, now a part of <a href="/wiki/Lynch_School_of_Education" title="Lynch School of Education">Campion Hall</a> and home to the college's <a href="/wiki/Lynch_School_of_Education" title="Lynch School of Education">Lynch School of Education</a>. The foundation was led by his youngest brother, U.S. Senator <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Ted Kennedy</a>, until his death in August 2009. In 1957, the <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Joseph_Patrick_Kennedy_Junior_Memorial_Skating_Rink" class="mw-redirect" title="Lieutenant Joseph Patrick Kennedy Junior Memorial Skating Rink">Lieutenant Joseph Patrick Kennedy Junior Memorial Skating Rink</a> was opened in <a href="/wiki/Hyannis,_Massachusetts" title="Hyannis, Massachusetts">Hyannis, Massachusetts</a>, with funds from the Joseph P. Kennedy Jr. Foundation.
</p><p>In 1969, <a href="/wiki/Hank_Searls" title="Hank Searls">Hank Searls</a> wrote a biography of Joe Jr., entitled <i>The Lost Prince: Young Joe, the Forgotten Kennedy</i>. <a href="/wiki/Young_Joe,_the_Forgotten_Kennedy" title="Young Joe, the Forgotten Kennedy">A television movie</a> based on Searls' book won a <a href="/wiki/Primetime_Emmy" class="mw-redirect" title="Primetime Emmy">primetime Emmy</a> in 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
