	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Revenue Act of 1964</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <b>Revenue Act of 1964</b> (<a href="/wiki/Act_of_Congress" title="Act of Congress">Pub.L.</a>&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://legislink.org/us/pl-88-272">88–272</a>), also known as the <b>Tax Reduction Act</b>, was a tax cut act proposed by President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>, passed by the <a href="/wiki/88th_United_States_Congress" title="88th United States Congress">88th United States Congress</a>, and signed into law by President <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a>. The act became law on February 26, 1964. 
</p><p>Kennedy proposed the bill on the advice of <a href="/wiki/Keynesian_economics" title="Keynesian economics">Keynesian</a> economist <a href="/wiki/Walter_Heller" title="Walter Heller">Walter Heller</a>, who believed that temporary deficit spending would boost economic growth. The act was initially blocked by conservatives like Senator <a href="/wiki/Harry_F._Byrd" title="Harry F. Byrd">Harry F. Byrd</a>, but Lyndon Johnson was able to guide it through Congress after the <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="Assassination of John F. Kennedy">assassination of Kennedy</a> in November 1963. The act cut federal <a href="/wiki/Income_taxes_in_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Income taxes in the United States">income taxes</a> by approximately twenty percent across the board, and the top federal income tax rate fell from 91 percent to 70 percent. The act also reduced the <a href="/wiki/Corporate_tax" title="Corporate tax">corporate tax</a> from 52 percent to 48 percent and created a minimum standard deduction.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Summary_of_provisions">Summary of provisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revenue_Act_of_1964&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Summary of provisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Office of Tax Analysis of the United States Department of the Treasury summarized the tax changes as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote>
<ul><li>reduced top marginal rate (on income over $100,000, roughly $770,000 in 2015 dollars, for individuals; and over $180,000; roughly $1,380,000 in 2015 dollars, for heads of households) from 91% to 70%</li>
<li>reduced corporate tax rate from 52% to 48%</li>
<li>phased-in acceleration of corporate estimated tax payments (through 1970)</li>
<li>created minimum standard deduction of $300 + $100/exemption (total $1,000 max)</li></ul>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Passage">Passage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revenue_Act_of_1964&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Passage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On the advice of <a href="/wiki/Walter_Heller" title="Walter Heller">Walter Heller</a>, the Chairman of the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Economic_Advisers" title="Council of Economic Advisers">Council of Economic Advisers</a>, President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> proposed a tax cut designed to help spur economic growth.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPatterson1996464–465_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPatterson1996464–465-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy believed that the tax cut would stimulate consumer demand, which in turn would lead to higher economic growth, lower unemployment, and increased federal revenues.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGiglio2006125_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGiglio2006125-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy's support for a tax cut reflected his conversion to <a href="/wiki/Keynesian_economics" title="Keynesian economics">Keynesian economics</a>, which favored temporary deficit spending in order to boost economic growth.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGiglio2006136–137_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGiglio2006136–137-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In January 1963, Kennedy presented Congress with a tax proposal that would reduce the top marginal tax rate from 91 percent to 65 percent, and lower the corporate tax rate from 52 percent to 47 percent; in total, the cut was projected to decrease income taxes by about $10 billion and corporate taxes by about $3.5 billion. The plan also included reforms designed to reduce the impact of <a href="/wiki/Itemized_deduction" title="Itemized deduction">itemized deductions</a>, as well as provisions to help the elderly and handicapped.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGiglio2006139–141_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGiglio2006139–141-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Conservatives revolted at giving Kennedy a key legislative victory before the election of 1964 and blocked the bill in Congress.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a> succeeded Kennedy as president after the latter was <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="Assassination of John F. Kennedy">assassinated</a> in November 1963. After agreed to decrease the total federal budget to under $100 billion, powerful conservative Senator <a href="/wiki/Harry_F._Byrd" title="Harry F. Byrd">Harry F. Byrd</a> dropped his opposition to a tax cut, clearing the way for its passage as the Revenue Act of 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Johnson signed the bill into law on February 26, 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Passage of the long-stalled tax cut facilitated efforts to move ahead on the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Rights_Act_of_1964" title="Civil Rights Act of 1964">Civil Rights Act of 1964</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Impact">Impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Revenue_Act_of_1964&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The stated goals of the tax cuts were to raise personal incomes, increase consumption, and increase capital investments. Evidence shows that these goals were exceeded by large degree with the combination of tax cuts and domestic spending programs President Johnson advocated, such as Medicare.<sup id="cite_ref-Policy1_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Policy1-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Unemployment fell from 5.2% in 1964 to 4.5% in 1965, and fell to 3.8% in 1966.<sup id="cite_ref-Policy1_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Policy1-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Initial estimates predicted a loss of revenue as a result of the tax cuts, however, tax revenue increased in 1964 and 1965.<sup id="cite_ref-Policy1_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Policy1-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
