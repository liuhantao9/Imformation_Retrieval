<p><b>Hebrew names</b> are <a href="/wiki/Name" title="Name">names</a> that have a <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew language</a> origin, classically from the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> They are mostly used by <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christians" title="Christians">Christians</a>, but many are also adapted to the <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> world, particularly if a Hebrew name is mentioned in the <a href="/wiki/Qur%27an" class="mw-redirect" title="Qur&#39;an">Qur'an</a> (example: <i>Ibrahim</i> is a common Arabic name from the Hebrew <i>Avraham</i>).  A typical Hebrew name can have many different forms, having been adapted to the <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonologies</a> of many different <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">languages</a>. A common Jewish practice worldwide is to give a Hebrew name to a child that is used religiously throughout its lifetime.
</p><p>Not all Hebrew names are strictly Hebrew in origin; some names may have been borrowed from other ancient languages,  including from <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_language" title="Egyptian language">Egyptian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Aramaic language">Aramaic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Phoenician_language" title="Phoenician language">Phoenician</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Canaanite_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Canaanite language">Canaanite</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_of_Hebrew_origin">Names of Hebrew origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebrew_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Names of Hebrew origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hebrew names used by <a href="/wiki/Jew" class="mw-redirect" title="Jew">Jews</a> (along with many Hebrew names used in <a href="/wiki/Christendom" title="Christendom">Christendom</a>) often come from the <a href="/wiki/Tanakh" class="mw-redirect" title="Tanakh">Tanakh</a>, also known as the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a> or <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a>.
</p><p>Many of these names are thought to have been adapted from Hebrew phrases and expressions, bestowing special meaning or the unique circumstances of birth to the one who receives that name. 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Theophoric_name" title="Theophoric name">Theophoric names</a> are those which include a form of a divine name, such by adding the suffix אל <i>-el</i>, meaning "God", forming names such as מיכאל <i><a href="/wiki/Michael" title="Michael">Michael</a></i> ("who is like God?") and גבריאל <i><a href="/wiki/Gabriel" title="Gabriel">Gabriel</a></i> ("man of God"). Another common form of theophory is the use of the <a href="/wiki/Tetragrammaton" title="Tetragrammaton">Tetragrammaton</a> as the basis for a suffix; the most common abbreviations used by Jews are יה <i>-yāh</i>/<i>-iyyāh</i> and יהו <i>-yāhû</i>/<i>-iyyāhû</i>/<i>-ayhû</i>, forming names such as ישׁעיהו <i><a href="/wiki/Isaiah" title="Isaiah">Yəšaʻªyāhû</a></i> (Isaiah), צדקיהו <i><a href="/wiki/Zedekiah" title="Zedekiah">Ṣiḏqiyyāhû</a></i> (Zedekiah) and שׂריה <i><a href="/wiki/Seraiah" title="Seraiah">Śərāyāh</a></i> (Seraiah). Most Christian usage is of the shorter suffix preferred in <a href="/wiki/Translation" title="Translation">translations</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Bible</a> to European languages: Greek -ιας <i>-ias</i> and English <a href="/wiki/Jah" title="Jah">-iah</a>, producing names such as Τωβιας <i><a href="/wiki/Tobias" title="Tobias">Tōbias</a></i> (Tobias, Toby) instead of <i>Tobiyyahu</i> and Ιερεμίας <i><a href="/wiki/Jeremiah" title="Jeremiah">Ieremias</a></i> (Jeremiah, Jeremy) instead of <i>Yirmeyahu</i>.
</p><p>In addition to devotion to Elohim and YHWH, names could also be sentences of praise in their own right. The name טוביהו 
<i><a href="/wiki/Tobiah" class="mw-redirect" title="Tobiah">Ṭôḇiyyāhû</a></i> means "Good of/is the LORD".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_of_Aramaic_origin">Names of Aramaic origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebrew_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Names of Aramaic origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Judæo-Aramaic was the vernacular language at the time of <a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>, and was also the language used to write parts of the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Daniel" title="Book of Daniel">Book of Daniel</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Ezra" title="Book of Ezra">Book of Ezra</a>, and the entire Jewish Babylonian <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">Talmud</a>.  Aramaic remained the <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a> of the Middle East until the time of Islam.
</p><p>Judæo-Aramaic names include עבד־נגו <i><a href="/wiki/Shadrach,_Meshach,_and_Abednego#Fiery_furnace" title="Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego">ʻĂḇēḏ-nəḡô</a></i>, בר־תלמי <i><a href="/wiki/Bartholomew" class="mw-redirect" title="Bartholomew">Bar-Talmay</a></i> and תום <i><a href="/wiki/Thomas_(name)" title="Thomas (name)">Tôm</a></i>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Bar_Kochba" class="mw-redirect" title="Bar Kochba">Bar Kochba</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hebrew-Greek_names">Hebrew-Greek names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebrew_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hebrew-Greek names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Due to the <a href="/wiki/Hellenization" title="Hellenization">Hellenisation</a> of the Eastern Mediterranean and the movement of Jews around the area, many Hebrew names were adapted to Greek, reinforced by the translation of the Tanakh in the <a href="/wiki/Septuagint" title="Septuagint">Septuagint</a> with many Hellenized names.    
</p><p>Many of the names in the <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">New Testament</a> are of Hebrew and Aramaic origin, but were adapted to the Greek by Hellenistic Christian writers such as <a href="/wiki/Paul_of_Tarsus" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul of Tarsus">Paul of Tarsus</a>. 
</p><p>Such Hebræo-Greek names include Ἰησοῦς <i><a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Iēsous</a></i> (originally from ישׁוע <i>Yēšûªʻ</i>), Νῶε <i><a href="/wiki/Noah" title="Noah">Nōē</a></i> (originally from נח <i>Nōªḥ</i>), Ἰσαΐας <i><a href="/wiki/Isaiah" title="Isaiah">Isaias</a></i> (originally from ישׁעיהו <i>Yəšaʻªyāhû</i>), Ἰσραήλ <i><a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israēl</a></i> (originally from ישראל <i>Yiśrā’ēl</i>). 
</p><p>Also, some Jews of the time had Greek <a href="/wiki/Gentile" title="Gentile">Gentile</a> names themselves, such as the Christian <a href="/wiki/Luke_(name)" title="Luke (name)">Luke</a> (Greek Λουκᾶς <i>Loukas</i>).  Though used by some Jews at the time, these names are generally not associated with Jews today, and are considered characteristically Greek and largely confined to use by Christians. Hebrew forms of the names exist, but they are extremely rare.
</p>
<h2><span id="Hebr.C3.A6o-Latin_names"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hebræo-Latin_names">Hebræo-Latin names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebrew_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hebræo-Latin names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many Hebrew names were adapted into Latin, some via Greek.  Such names include Jesus (from Greek Ιησους <i>Iēsous</i>) and <a href="/wiki/Maria_(given_name)" title="Maria (given name)">Maria</a> (from Greek Μαριαμ <i>Mariam</i>, originally from Hebrew מרים <i><a href="/wiki/Miriam" title="Miriam">Miryām</a></i>).
</p><p>Also, some Jews during <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman</a> times also had Latin names for themselves, such as the Christian evangelist <a href="/wiki/Mark_the_Evangelist" title="Mark the Evangelist">Mark</a> (Latin <a href="/wiki/Marcus_(name)" title="Marcus (name)">Marcus</a>). As was the case with contemporary Jewish names of Greek origin, most of these Latin names are generally not associated with Jews today, and today retain a Roman and Christian character.
</p>
<h2><span id="Hebr.C3.A6o-Arabic_names"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hebræo-Arabic_names">Hebræo-Arabic names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebrew_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hebræo-Arabic names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With the rise of Islam and the establishment of an Arab <a href="/wiki/Caliphate" title="Caliphate">Caliphate</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic language</a> became the lingua franca of the Middle East and some parts of <a href="/wiki/Berber_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Berber people">Berber</a> <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a>. Islamic scripture such as the Qurʼan, however, contains many names of Hebrew origin (often via Aramaic), and there were Jewish and Christian <a href="/wiki/Minority_group" title="Minority group">minorities</a> living under Arab Islamic rule. As such, many Hebrew names had been adapted to Arabic, and could be found in the Arab world. Jews and Christians generally used the Arabic adaptions of these names, just as in the present English-speaking Jews (and sometimes Muslims) often use Anglicized versions (Joshua rather than Yəhôšúªʼ, for instance.)
</p><p>While most such names are common to traditional Arabic translations of the Bible, a few differ; for instance, Arabic-speaking Christians use <i>Yasūʻ</i> instead of <i><a href="/wiki/Jesus_in_Islam" title="Jesus in Islam">ʻĪsā</a></i> for "<a href="/wiki/Jesus" title="Jesus">Jesus</a>".
</p><p>Such Hebræo-Arabic names include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Job_in_Islam" title="Job in Islam">ʼAyyūb</a> أيّوب (from Hebrew איוב <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Job" title="Book of Job">ʼIyyôḇ</a></i>) (Job)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Joseph_in_Islam" title="Joseph in Islam">Yūsuf</a> يوسف (from Hebrew יוסף <i><a href="/wiki/Joseph_(given_name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph (given name)">Yôsēp̄</a></i>) (Joseph)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/David_in_Islam" title="David in Islam">Dāʼūd</a> داۇد (from Hebrew דוד <i><a href="/wiki/David" title="David">Dāwiḏ</a></i>) (David)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ishmael_in_Islam" title="Ishmael in Islam">ʼIsmāʻīl</a> اسماعيل (from Hebrew ישׁמעאל <i><a href="/wiki/Ishmael" title="Ishmael">Yišmāʻêl</a></i>) (Ishmael)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Isaac_in_Islam" title="Isaac in Islam">ʼIsḥāq</a> إسحاق (from Hebrew יצחק <i><a href="/wiki/Isaac" title="Isaac">Yiṣḥāq</a></i>) (Isaac)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Jacob_in_Islam" title="Jacob in Islam">Yaʻqūb</a> يعقوب (from Hebrew יעקב <i><a href="/wiki/Jacob" title="Jacob">Yaʻªqōḇ</a></i>) (Jacob)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Adam_in_Islam" title="Adam in Islam">ʼĀdam</a> آدم (from Hebrew אדם <i><a href="/wiki/Adam_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Adam (name)">ʼĀḏām</a></i>) (Adam)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hawwa" class="mw-redirect" title="Hawwa">Ḥawwāʼ</a> حواء (from Hebrew חוה <i><a href="/wiki/Adam_and_Eve" title="Adam and Eve">Ḥawwāh</a></i>) (Eve)</li></ul>
<p>The influence of Aramaic is observable in several names, notably ʼIsḥāq (Isaac), where the <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">Syriac</a> form is simply <i>Îsḥāq</i>, contrasting with more Hebraic forms such as Yaʻqūb (Jacob).
</p><p>Some of these Arabic names preserve original Hebrew pronunciations that were later changed by regular sound shifts; <i>migdal</i>, recorded in the New Testament as <i>Magdal</i>ene and in Palestinian Arabic as <i>Majdala</i>, which turned <i>a</i> in unstressed closed syllables into <i>i</i>.
</p><p>Typically, Hebrew אל <i>-ʼēl</i> was adapted as ـايل <i>-īl</i>, and Hebrew יה <i>-yāh</i> as ـيا <i>-yāʼ</i>.
</p>
<h2><span id="Hebr.C3.A6o-English_names"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hebræo-English_names">Hebræo-English names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hebrew_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hebræo-English names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">James I of England</a> commissioned a <a href="/wiki/Translation" title="Translation">translation</a> of the Christian Bible from the original languages, including a translation of the Tanakh or Old Testament from Hebrew into English. This became known as the  <a href="/wiki/King_James_Version_of_the_Bible" class="mw-redirect" title="King James Version of the Bible">King James Version of the Bible</a>, often referred to today by the abbreviation "KJV".
</p><p>Even so, many KJV Old Testament names were not entirely without New Testament Greek influence. This influence mostly reflected the vowels of names, leaving most of the consonants largely intact, only modestly filtered to consonants of contemporary English phonology. However, all KJV names followed the Greek convention of not distinguishing between soft and <i>dāḡeš</i> forms of ב <i>bêṯ</i>.  These habits resulted in multilingually fused Hebræo-Helleno-English names, such as <a href="/wiki/Judah_(son_of_Jacob)" title="Judah (son of Jacob)">Judah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isaiah" title="Isaiah">Isaiah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jeremiah" title="Jeremiah">Jeremiah</a>. Additionally, a handful of names were adapted directly from Greek without even partial translations from Hebrew, including names such as <a href="/wiki/Isaac" title="Isaac">Isaac</a>, <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">Moses</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jesse" title="Jesse">Jesse</a>.
</p><p>Along with names from the KJV edition of the New Testament, these names constitute the large part of Hebrew names as they exist in the English-speaking world.
</p>
