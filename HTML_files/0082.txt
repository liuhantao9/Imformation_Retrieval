	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">George Pitt-Rivers</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">&#160;&#160;(Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Henry_Lane-Fox_Pitt-Rivers&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="George Henry Lane-Fox Pitt-Rivers">George Henry Lane-Fox Pitt-Rivers</a>)</span></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>

<b>George Henry Lane-Fox Pitt-Rivers</b> (22 May 1890 – 17 June 1966) was a British <a href="/wiki/Anthropologist" title="Anthropologist">anthropologist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eugenics" title="Eugenics">eugenicist</a> who was one of the wealthiest men in England in the <a href="/wiki/Interwar_period" title="Interwar period">interwar period</a>. He embraced <a href="/wiki/Anti-communism" title="Anti-communism">anti-Bolshevism</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anti-Semitism" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-Semitism">anti-Semitism</a>, and was interned by the British government for two years during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life">Life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Pitt-Rivers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pitt-Rivers was born in London,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> his birth registered under the surname Fox in <a href="/wiki/Chesterfield" title="Chesterfield">Chesterfield</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He was a son of Alexander Edward Lane Fox-Pitt-Rivers (2 November 1855 – 19 August 1927) and of his wife Alice Ruth Hermione, daughter of <a href="/wiki/Lord_Henry_Thynne" title="Lord Henry Thynne">Lord Henry Thynne</a>. His father was the eldest son of <a href="/wiki/Augustus_Pitt_Rivers" title="Augustus Pitt Rivers">Augustus Pitt Rivers</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> ethnologist and anthropologist and founder of the <a href="/wiki/Pitt_Rivers_Museum" title="Pitt Rivers Museum">Pitt Rivers Museum</a>, upon whose death in 1900 Alexander inherited the Pitt-Rivers estate.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  After Alexander died in 1927, the estate was inherited by George and it was so large that "it was said, albeit with exaggeration, that he could ride from coast to coast without leaving his own land".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He was a Captain in the <a href="/wiki/5th_Dragoon_Guards" title="5th Dragoon Guards">5th Dragoon Guards</a> and took part in World War I. He was wounded in the <a href="/wiki/First_Battle_of_Ypres" title="First Battle of Ypres">First Battle of Ypres</a> and subsequently sent to England for surgery and recuperation. After the war he published a book <i>The World Significance of the Russian Revolution</i>, the first of his anti-Bolshevik and anti-Semitic public activities. During 1922–25, Pitt-Rivers held the position of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Principal_Secretary_(Australia)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Principal Secretary (Australia) (page does not exist)">Principal Secretary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aide-de-Camp" class="mw-redirect" title="Aide-de-Camp">Aide-de-Camp</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Governor-General_of_Australia" title="Governor-General of Australia">Governor-General of Australia</a>, his father-in-law.  His experience with the <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Maori</a> led to his lasting interest in anthropology, which he studied at Oxford under <a href="/wiki/Bronis%C5%82aw_Malinowski" title="Bronisław Malinowski">Bronisław Malinowski</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1927 he attended the <a href="/wiki/World_Population_Conference" title="World Population Conference">World Population Conference</a> and published a book <i>Clash of Cultures and the Contact of Races</i>. Two years later, Pitt-Rivers was elected a fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Anthropological_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Anthropological Institute">Royal Anthropological Institute</a>; he also represented the <a href="/wiki/Galton_Institute" title="Galton Institute">Eugenics Society</a> at the <a href="/wiki/International_Federation_of_Eugenics_Organizations" title="International Federation of Eugenics Organizations">International Federation of Eugenics Organizations</a>. From 1931 to 1937, Pitt-Rivers held the positions of the Secretary General and Treasurer of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Union_for_the_Scientific_Investigation_of_Population_Problems&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="International Union for the Scientific Investigation of Population Problems (page does not exist)">International Union for the Scientific Investigation of Population Problems</a>, where he came to contact with German eugenicists <a href="/wiki/Eugen_Fischer" title="Eugen Fischer">Eugen Fischer</a> and his assistant Lothar Loeffler.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> During this time, he also became increasingly embroiled in politics, praising the ideas of <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">Benito Mussolini</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>.
</p><p>Pitt-Rivers was locked up in <a href="/wiki/HM_Prison_Brixton" title="HM Prison Brixton">Brixton Prison</a> and Ascot internment centre (1940–1942) during the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Oswald_Mosley" title="Oswald Mosley">Mosleyite</a> <a href="/wiki/Nazi" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi">Nazi</a> sympathiser under the <a href="/wiki/Defence_Regulation_18B" title="Defence Regulation 18B">Defence Regulation 18B</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  After the War, he met Stella Lonsdale, who had herself been incarcerated in Paris by the Germans, suspected of being an English spy;when she eventually managed to make her way to England, she was imprisoned suspected of being a German spy.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  She became George's mistress, and took his name, though they never married.
</p><p>Stella inherited from George when he died in 1966.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In his Will he left Stella instructions that any property to be sold must be sold individually, rather than as an estate, in order that tenants might buy the properties they leased from the Pitt-Rivers Estate.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  Much of the village of <a href="/wiki/Okeford_Fitzpaine" title="Okeford Fitzpaine">Okeford Fitzpaine</a> was thus released.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  Stella also sold off a large proportion of the artefacts held in the Pitt-Rivers Museum at Farnham that she also inherited from George. The Pitt-Rivers Museum in Oxford has been trying ever since to recover those items – which were uncatalogued.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Pitt-Rivers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>George Henry Lane-Fox Pitt-Rivers was twice married; firstly to the Hon. Rachel Forster (daughter of the <a href="/wiki/Henry_Forster,_1st_Baron_Forster" title="Henry Forster, 1st Baron Forster">1st Baron Forster</a>) on 22 December 1915, the marriage was dissolved in 1930.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> They had two sons:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Michael_Pitt-Rivers" title="Michael Pitt-Rivers">Michael Pitt-Rivers</a> (1917–1999), a <a href="/wiki/West_Country" title="West Country">West Country</a> landowner who gained national notoriety in the 1950s when he was put on trial charged with <a href="/wiki/Buggery" class="mw-redirect" title="Buggery">buggery</a>. This trial was instrumental in bringing public attention—and opposition—to the laws against homosexuality as they then stood;</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Julian_Pitt-Rivers" title="Julian Pitt-Rivers">Julian Pitt-Rivers</a> (1919–2001), a social anthropologist, an ethnographer, and a university professor.</li></ul>
<p>George Pitt-Rivers married secondly, on 14 October 1931, <a href="/wiki/Rosalind_Pitt-Rivers" title="Rosalind Pitt-Rivers">Rosalind Venetia Henley</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> (4 March 1907 – 14 January 1990), a biochemist, whose parents were the Hon. Anthony Morton Henley (son of the <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Henley,_3rd_Baron_Henley" title="Anthony Henley, 3rd Baron Henley">3rd Baron Henley</a>) and the Hon. Sylvia Laura Stanley (daughter of the <a href="/wiki/Edward_Stanley,_4th_Baron_Stanley_of_Alderley" title="Edward Stanley, 4th Baron Stanley of Alderley">4th Baron Stanley of Alderley</a>), by whom he had a third son:
</p>
<ul><li>Anthony (b. 1932).<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
