	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Gulf Stream</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Gulf Stream (disambiguation)">Gulf Stream (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">A warm, swift Atlantic current that originates in the Gulf of Mexico flows around the tip of Florida, along the east coast of the United States before crossing the Atlantic Ocean</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Golfstrom.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Golfstrom.jpg/300px-Golfstrom.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="300" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/19/Golfstrom.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="363" data-file-height="363" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Golfstrom.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sea_surface_temperature" title="Sea surface temperature">Surface temperatures</a> in the western North Atlantic. The North American landmass is black and dark blue (cold), while the Gulf Stream is red (warm). Source: <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Gulf Stream</b>, together with its northern extension the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Current" title="North Atlantic Current">North Atlantic Drift</a>, is a warm and swift <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic</a> <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">ocean current</a> that originates in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> and stretches to the tip of <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>, and follows the eastern coastlines of the United States and <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland</a> before crossing the Atlantic Ocean. The process of <a href="/wiki/Boundary_current" title="Boundary current">western intensification</a> causes the Gulf Stream to be a northward accelerating current off the east coast of North America. At about <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion"><a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Gulf_Stream&amp;params=40_0_N_30_0_W_"><span class="geo-default"><span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location"><span class="latitude">40°0′N</span> <span class="longitude">30°0′W</span></span></span><span class="geo-multi-punct">&#xfeff; / &#xfeff;</span><span class="geo-nondefault"><span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">40.000°N 30.000°W</span><span style="display:none">&#xfeff; / <span class="geo">40.000; -30.000</span></span></span></a></span>, it splits in two, with the northern stream, the North Atlantic Drift, crossing to Northern Europe and the southern stream, the <a href="/wiki/Canary_Current" title="Canary Current">Canary Current</a>, recirculating off West Africa.
</p><p>The Gulf Stream influences the climate of the east coast of North America from <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> to <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland" class="mw-redirect" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a>, and the west coast of Europe.  Although there has been recent debate, there is consensus that the climate of Western Europe and Northern Europe is warmer than it would otherwise be due to the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_drift" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic drift">North Atlantic drift</a> which is the northeastern section of the Gulf Stream. It is part of the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Gyre" title="North Atlantic Gyre">North Atlantic Gyre</a>. Its presence has led to the development of strong <a href="/wiki/Cyclone" title="Cyclone">cyclones</a> of all types, both within the <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">atmosphere</a> and within the ocean. The Gulf Stream is also a significant potential source of <a href="/wiki/Renewable_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Renewable power">renewable power</a> generation.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The Gulf Stream may be <a href="/wiki/Shutdown_of_thermohaline_circulation" title="Shutdown of thermohaline circulation">slowing down as a result of climate change</a>.
</p><p>The Gulf Stream is typically 100 kilometres (62&#160;mi) wide and 800 metres (2,600&#160;ft) to 1,200 metres (3,900&#160;ft) deep. The current velocity is fastest near the surface, with the maximum speed typically about 2.5 metres per second (9.0&#160;km/h; 5.6&#160;mph).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_Stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Franklin-Folger_Gulf_Stream_chart,_London,_1769_version,_LOC.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Franklin-Folger_Gulf_Stream_chart%2C_London%2C_1769_version%2C_LOC.jpg/300px-Franklin-Folger_Gulf_Stream_chart%2C_London%2C_1769_version%2C_LOC.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="223" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Franklin-Folger_Gulf_Stream_chart%2C_London%2C_1769_version%2C_LOC.jpg/450px-Franklin-Folger_Gulf_Stream_chart%2C_London%2C_1769_version%2C_LOC.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Franklin-Folger_Gulf_Stream_chart%2C_London%2C_1769_version%2C_LOC.jpg/600px-Franklin-Folger_Gulf_Stream_chart%2C_London%2C_1769_version%2C_LOC.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="2234" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Franklin-Folger_Gulf_Stream_chart,_London,_1769_version,_LOC.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a>'s chart of the Gulf Stream printed in London in 1769</div></div></div>
<p>European discovery of the Gulf Stream dates to the 1512 expedition of <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ponce_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Juan Ponce de León">Juan Ponce de León</a>, after which it became widely used by Spanish ships sailing from the Caribbean to Spain.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> A summary of Ponce de León's voyage log, on April 22, 1513, noted, "A current such that, although they had great wind, they could not proceed forward, but backward and it seems that they were proceeding well; at the end it was known that the current was more powerful than the wind."<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Its existence was also known to <a href="/wiki/Peter_Martyr_d%27Anghiera" title="Peter Martyr d&#39;Anghiera">Peter Martyr d'Anghiera</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a> became interested in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">North Atlantic Ocean</a> circulation patterns. In 1768, while in England, Franklin heard a curious complaint from the Colonial Board of Customs: Why did it take British packets several weeks longer to reach New York from England than it took an average American merchant ship to reach <a href="/wiki/Newport,_Rhode_Island" title="Newport, Rhode Island">Newport, Rhode Island</a>, despite the merchant ships leaving from London and having to sail down the <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">River Thames</a> and then the length of the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> before they sailed across the Atlantic, while the packets left from <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall" title="Falmouth, Cornwall">Falmouth</a> in Cornwall.<sup id="cite_ref-tuchman_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tuchman-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Franklin asked Timothy Folger, his cousin twice removed (Nantucket Historical Society), a <a href="/wiki/Nantucket_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Nantucket Island">Nantucket Island</a> whaling captain, for an answer. Folger explained that merchant ships routinely crossed the then-unnamed Gulf Stream—identifying it by whale behavior, measurement of the water's temperature, and changes in the water's color—while the mail packet captains ran against it.<sup id="cite_ref-tuchman_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tuchman-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Franklin had Folger sketch the path of the Gulf Stream on an old chart of the Atlantic and add written notes on how to avoid the Stream when sailing from England to America. Franklin then forwarded the chart to Anthony Todd, secretary of the British Post Office.<sup id="cite_ref-tuchman_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tuchman-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Franklin's Gulf Stream chart was printed in 1769 in London, but it was mostly ignored by British sea captains.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> A copy of the chart was printed in Paris circa 1770–1773, and a third version was published by Franklin in Philadelphia in 1786.<sup id="cite_ref-Ocean_explorer_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ocean_explorer-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">Properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_Stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Gulf Stream proper is a <a href="/wiki/Boundary_current#Western_intensification" title="Boundary current">western-intensified</a> current, driven largely by wind stress.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_current" class="mw-redirect" title="North Atlantic current">North Atlantic Drift</a>, in contrast, is largely <a href="/wiki/Thermohaline_circulation" title="Thermohaline circulation">thermohaline circulation</a>–driven. In 1958 the oceanographer <a href="/wiki/Henry_Stommel" title="Henry Stommel">Henry Stommel</a> noted that "very little water from the Gulf of Mexico is actually in the Stream".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> By carrying warm water northeast across the Atlantic, it makes <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western</a> and especially <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern Europe</a> warmer than it otherwise would be.<sup id="cite_ref-DRIFT_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DRIFT-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_and_behavior">Formation and behavior</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_Stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Formation and behavior">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Golfstream.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Golfstream.jpg/280px-Golfstream.jpg" decoding="async" width="280" height="204" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Golfstream.jpg/420px-Golfstream.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Golfstream.jpg/560px-Golfstream.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="745" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Golfstream.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Evolution of the Gulf Stream to the west of Ireland continuing as the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Current" title="North Atlantic Current">North Atlantic Current</a></div></div></div>
<p>A river of sea water, called the <a href="/wiki/North_Equatorial_Current" title="North Equatorial Current">Atlantic North Equatorial Current</a>, flows westward off the coast of Central Africa. When this current interacts with the northeastern coast of South America, the current forks into two branches. One passes into the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">Caribbean Sea</a>, while a second, the <a href="/wiki/Antilles_Current" title="Antilles Current">Antilles Current</a>, flows north and east of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">West Indies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> These two branches rejoin north of the <a href="/wiki/Straits_of_Florida" title="Straits of Florida">Straits of Florida</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Trade_winds" title="Trade winds">trade winds</a> blow westward in the tropics,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">westerlies</a> blow eastward at mid-latitudes.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  This wind pattern applies a <a href="/wiki/Stress_(physics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stress (physics)">stress</a> to the subtropical ocean surface with negative <a href="/wiki/Curl_(mathematics)" title="Curl (mathematics)">curl</a> across the north Atlantic Ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  The resulting <a href="/wiki/Sverdrup_transport" class="mw-redirect" title="Sverdrup transport">Sverdrup transport</a> is equatorward.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Because of conservation of <a href="/wiki/Potential_vorticity" title="Potential vorticity">potential vorticity</a> caused by the northward-moving winds on the <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_ridge" class="mw-redirect" title="Subtropical ridge">subtropical ridge</a>'s western periphery and the increased relative vorticity of northward moving water, transport is balanced by a narrow, accelerating poleward current. This flows along the western boundary of the ocean basin, outweighing the effects of friction with the western boundary current, and is known as the <a href="/wiki/Labrador_current" class="mw-redirect" title="Labrador current">Labrador current</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  The conservation of potential vorticity also causes bends along the Gulf Stream, which occasionally break off due to a shift in the Gulf Stream's position, forming separate warm and cold eddies.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  This overall process, known as western intensification, causes currents on the western boundary of an ocean basin, such as the Gulf Stream, to be stronger than those on the eastern boundary.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As a consequence, the resulting Gulf Stream is a strong ocean current. It transports water at a rate of 30 million cubic meters per second (30 <a href="/wiki/Sverdrup" title="Sverdrup">sverdrups</a>) through the Florida Straits.  As it passes south of <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_(island)" title="Newfoundland (island)">Newfoundland</a>, this rate increases to 150 million cubic metres per second.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The volume of the Gulf Stream dwarfs all rivers that empty into the Atlantic combined, which barely total 0.6 million cubic metres per second. It is weaker, however, than the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circumpolar_Current" title="Antarctic Circumpolar Current">Antarctic Circumpolar Current</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>  Given the strength and proximity of the Gulf Stream, beaches along the East Coast of the United States may be more vulnerable to large sea-level anomalies, which significantly impact rates of <a href="/wiki/Coastal_erosion" title="Coastal erosion">coastal erosion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Gulf Stream is typically 100 kilometres (62&#160;mi) wide and 800 metres (2,600&#160;ft) to 1,200 metres (3,900&#160;ft) deep.  The current velocity is fastest near the surface, with the maximum speed typically about 2.5 metres per second (5.6&#160;mph).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>  As it travels north, the warm water transported by the Gulf Stream undergoes evaporative cooling. The cooling is wind-driven: Wind moving over the water causes <a href="/wiki/Evaporation" title="Evaporation">evaporation</a>, cooling the water and increasing its <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">salinity</a> and density.  When sea ice forms, salts are left out of the ice, a process known as brine exclusion.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  These two processes produce water that is denser and colder (or, more precisely, water that is still liquid at a lower temperature). In the North Atlantic Ocean, the water becomes so dense that it begins to sink down through less salty and less dense water. (The <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">convective</a> action is not unlike that of a <a href="/wiki/Lava_lamp" title="Lava lamp">lava lamp</a>.) This downdraft of cold, dense water becomes a part of the <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Deep_Water" title="North Atlantic Deep Water">North Atlantic Deep Water</a>, a southgoing stream.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  Very little <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a> lies within the current, although seaweed lies in clusters to its east.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2018 two studies published in <i>Nature</i> <sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> found the Gulf Stream to be at its weakest for at least 1,600 years.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Localized_effects">Localized effects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_Stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Localized effects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_Florida" title="Climate of Florida">Climate of Florida</a></div>
<p>The Gulf Stream is influential on the climate of the Florida peninsula. The portion off the Florida coast, referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Florida_Current" title="Florida Current">Florida current</a>, maintains an average water temperature at or above 24&#160;°C (75&#160;°F) during the winter.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>  East winds moving over this warm water move warm air from over the Gulf Stream inland,<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> helping to keep temperatures milder across the state than elsewhere across the Southeast during the winter. Also, the Gulf Stream's proximity to <a href="/wiki/Nantucket" title="Nantucket">Nantucket</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> adds to its <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biodiversity</a>, as it is the northern limit for southern varieties of plant life, and the southern limit for northern plant species, Nantucket being warmer during winter than the mainland.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Current" title="North Atlantic Current">North Atlantic Current</a> of the Gulf Stream, along with similar warm air currents, helps keep Ireland and the western coast of Great Britain a couple of degrees warmer than the east.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>  However, the difference is most dramatic in the western coastal islands of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> A noticeable effect of the Gulf Stream and the strong westerly winds (driven by the warm water of the Gulf Stream) on Europe occurs along the Norwegian coast.<sup id="cite_ref-DRIFT_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DRIFT-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Northern parts of <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> lie close to the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a> zone, most of which is covered with ice and snow in winter. However, almost all of Norway's coast remains free of ice and snow throughout the year.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Weather systems warmed by the Gulf Stream drift into Northern Europe, also warming the climate behind the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_mountains" class="mw-redirect" title="Scandinavian mountains">Scandinavian mountains</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_cyclone_formation">Effect on cyclone formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_Stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Effect on cyclone formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sandy_Oct_28_2012_1600Z.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Sandy_Oct_28_2012_1600Z.jpg/220px-Sandy_Oct_28_2012_1600Z.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="287" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Sandy_Oct_28_2012_1600Z.jpg/330px-Sandy_Oct_28_2012_1600Z.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Sandy_Oct_28_2012_1600Z.jpg/440px-Sandy_Oct_28_2012_1600Z.jpg 2x" data-file-width="7200" data-file-height="9400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sandy_Oct_28_2012_1600Z.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Hurricane Sandy</a> intensifying along the axis of the Gulf Stream in 2012.</div></div></div>
<p>The warm water and temperature contrast along the edge of the Gulf Stream often increase the intensity of cyclones, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone" title="Tropical cyclone">tropical</a> or otherwise.  <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclogenesis" title="Tropical cyclogenesis">Tropical cyclone generation</a> normally requires water temperatures in excess of 26.5&#160;°C (79.7&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-AOML_FAQ_A15_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AOML_FAQ_A15-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Tropical cyclone formation is common over the Gulf Stream, especially in the month of July. Storms travel westward through the Caribbean and then either move in a northward direction and curve toward the eastern coast of the United States or stay on a north-westward track and enter the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HURDAT_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HURDAT-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Such storms have the potential to create strong winds and extensive damage to the <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">United States' Southeast Coastal Areas</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Sandy" title="Hurricane Sandy">Hurricane Sandy</a> in 2012 was a recent example of a hurricane passing over the Gulf Stream and gaining strength.<sup id="cite_ref-superstorm_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-superstorm-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Strong <a href="/wiki/Extratropical_cyclone" title="Extratropical cyclone">extratropical cyclones</a> have been shown to deepen significantly along a shallow <a href="/wiki/Weather_front" title="Weather front">frontal zone</a>, forced by the Gulf Stream itself, during the cold season.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Subtropical_cyclone" title="Subtropical cyclone">Subtropical cyclones</a> also tend to generate near the Gulf Stream. 75&#160;percent of such systems documented between 1951 and 2000 formed near this warm water current, with two annual peaks of activity occurring during the months of May and October.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>  Cyclones within the ocean form under the Gulf Stream, extending as deep as 3,500 metres (11,500&#160;ft) beneath the ocean's surface.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_renewable_power_source">Possible renewable power source</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_Stream&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Possible renewable power source">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The theoretical maximum energy dissipation from the Gulf Stream by turbines is in the range of 20–60&#160;GW.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One suggestion, which could theoretically supply power comparable to several nuclear power plants, would deploy a field of underwater turbines placed 300 meters (980&#160;ft) under the center of the core of the Gulf Stream.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ocean_thermal_energy_conversion" title="Ocean thermal energy conversion">Ocean thermal energy</a> could also be harnessed to produce electricity using the temperature difference between cold deep water and warm surface water.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
