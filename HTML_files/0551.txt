	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Ecotourism</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">&#160;&#160;(Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eco-tourism&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Eco-tourism">Eco-tourism</a>)</span></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Llano_del_Muerto_in_Perquin_El_Salvador.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Llano_del_Muerto_in_Perquin_El_Salvador.jpg/220px-Llano_del_Muerto_in_Perquin_El_Salvador.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="286" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Llano_del_Muerto_in_Perquin_El_Salvador.jpg/330px-Llano_del_Muerto_in_Perquin_El_Salvador.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Llano_del_Muerto_in_Perquin_El_Salvador.jpg/440px-Llano_del_Muerto_in_Perquin_El_Salvador.jpg 2x" data-file-width="443" data-file-height="575" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Llano_del_Muerto_in_Perquin_El_Salvador.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Llano del Muerto waterfall in <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">El Salvador</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>Ecotourism</b> is a form of <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a> involving visiting fragile, pristine, and relatively undisturbed natural areas, intended as a low-impact and often small scale alternative to standard commercial <a href="/wiki/Mass_tourism" class="mw-redirect" title="Mass tourism">mass tourism</a>. It means responsible travel to natural areas, conserving the environment, and improving the well-being of the local people.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Its purpose may be to educate the traveler, to provide funds for <a href="/wiki/Ecological_conservation" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological conservation">ecological conservation</a>, to directly benefit the <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">economic development</a> and political empowerment of local communities, or to foster respect for different cultures and for <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">human rights</a>. Since the 1980s, ecotourism has been considered a critical endeavor by environmentalists, so that future generations may experience destinations relatively untouched by human intervention.<sup id="cite_ref-Honey_EandSD_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Honey_EandSD-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>33</span></sup> Several university programs use this description as the working definition of ecotourism.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Generally, ecotourism deals with interaction with <a href="/wiki/Biotic_components" class="mw-redirect" title="Biotic components">biotic components</a> of the natural environments.<sup id="cite_ref-revues1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revues1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Ecotourism focuses on socially responsible travel, personal growth, and environmental sustainability. Ecotourism typically involves travel to destinations where <a href="/wiki/Flora" title="Flora">flora</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fauna" title="Fauna">fauna</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cultural_heritage" title="Cultural heritage">cultural heritage</a> are the primary attractions. Ecotourism is intended to offer tourists an insight into the impact of human beings on the environment and to foster a greater appreciation of our natural habitats.
</p><p>Responsible ecotourism programs include those that minimize the negative aspects of conventional tourism on the environment and enhance the cultural integrity of local people. Therefore, in addition to evaluating environmental and cultural factors, an integral part of ecotourism is the promotion of <a href="/wiki/Recycling" title="Recycling">recycling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Energy_conservation" title="Energy conservation">energy efficiency</a>, <a href="/wiki/Water_conservation" title="Water conservation">water conservation</a>, and creation of economic opportunities for local communities.<sup id="cite_ref-Randall_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Randall-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> For these reasons, ecotourism often appeals to advocates of environmental and social responsibility.
</p><p>Many consider the term "ecotourism", like "<a href="/wiki/Sustainable_tourism" title="Sustainable tourism">sustainable tourism</a>", an oxymoron. Like most forms of tourism, ecotourism generally depends on air transportation, which contributes to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global climate change</a>.  Additionally, "the overall effect of sustainable tourism is negative where like ecotourism philanthropic aspirations mask hard-nosed immediate self-interest."<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Attribution_needed" title="Wikipedia:Attribution needed"><span title="This sentence may contain perspectives and opinions without stating whose they are. (January 2019)">attribution needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criteria">Criteria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Criteria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:H%C3%BClgevaatlus,_postipaat_Helge.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/H%C3%BClgevaatlus%2C_postipaat_Helge.JPG/220px-H%C3%BClgevaatlus%2C_postipaat_Helge.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/H%C3%BClgevaatlus%2C_postipaat_Helge.JPG/330px-H%C3%BClgevaatlus%2C_postipaat_Helge.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/H%C3%BClgevaatlus%2C_postipaat_Helge.JPG/440px-H%C3%BClgevaatlus%2C_postipaat_Helge.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4512" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:H%C3%BClgevaatlus,_postipaat_Helge.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Halichoerus_grypus" class="mw-redirect" title="Halichoerus grypus">Seal</a> watching near Malusi Islands in <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Ecotourism is tourism which is conducted responsibly to conserve the environment and sustain the well-being of local people.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> It...
</p>
<ul><li>Builds environmental awareness</li>
<li>Provides direct financial benefits for conservation</li>
<li>Provides financial benefits and empowerment for local people</li>
<li>Respects local culture</li>
<li>Supports human rights and democratic movements<sup id="cite_ref-Honey_EandSD_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Honey_EandSD-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>29–31</span></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wight_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wight-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> such as:
<ul><li>conservation of <a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">biological diversity</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cultural_diversity" title="Cultural diversity">cultural diversity</a> through <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a> protection</li>
<li>promotion of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainable use</a> of biodiversity, by providing jobs to local populations</li>
<li>sharing of all socio-economic benefits with local communities and <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">indigenous peoples</a> by having their <a href="/wiki/Informed_consent" title="Informed consent">informed consent</a> and participation in the management of ecotourism enterprises</li>
<li>tourism to unspoiled natural resources, with minimal impact on the environment being a primary concern.</li>
<li>minimization of tourism's own environmental impact</li>
<li>affordability and lack of waste in the form of luxury</li>
<li>local culture, flora, and fauna being the main attractions</li>
<li>local people, who benefit from this form of tourism economically, and often more than mass tourism</li></ul></li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_International_Ecotourism_Society" title="The International Ecotourism Society">The International Ecotourism Society</a> defines ecotourism as "responsible travel to natural areas that conserves the environment, sustains the well-being of local people, and involves interpretation and education".
</p><p>For many countries, ecotourism is not simply a marginal activity to finance protection of the <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">environment</a>, but a major industry of the national <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economy</a>. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">Ecuador</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, Kenya, <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a> and territories such as <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>, ecotourism represents a significant portion of the <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> and <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economic activity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Eadington_and_Smith_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Eadington_and_Smith-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ecotourism is often misinterpreted as any form of tourism that involves nature (see <a href="/wiki/Jungle_tourism" title="Jungle tourism">Jungle tourism</a>). Self-proclaimed practitioners and hosts of ecotourism experiences assume it is achieved by simply creating destinations in natural areas. According to critics of this commonplace and assumptive practice, true ecotourism must, above all, sensitize people to the beauty and the fragility of nature. These critics condemn some operators as <a href="/wiki/Greenwash" class="mw-redirect" title="Greenwash">greenwashing</a> their operations: using the labels of "green" and "eco-friendly”, while behaving in environmentally irresponsible ways.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although academics disagree about who can be classified as an ecotourist and there is little statistical data, some estimate that more than five million ecotourists—the majority of the ecotourist population—come from the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, with many others from Western Europe, Canada and Australia.<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Currently, there are various moves to create national and international ecotourism accreditation programs, although the process is also controversial.<sup id="cite_ref-Crinion_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crinion-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> National ecotourism certification programs have been put in place in countries such as Costa Rica, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, Kenya, Estonia, and <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology_and_history">Terminology and history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Terminology and history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HANGING_BRIDGE_THENMALA.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/HANGING_BRIDGE_THENMALA.JPG/220px-HANGING_BRIDGE_THENMALA.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/HANGING_BRIDGE_THENMALA.JPG/330px-HANGING_BRIDGE_THENMALA.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/HANGING_BRIDGE_THENMALA.JPG/440px-HANGING_BRIDGE_THENMALA.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HANGING_BRIDGE_THENMALA.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A hanging bridge in ecotourism area of <a href="/wiki/Thenmala" title="Thenmala">Thenmala</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">Kerala</a> in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> - India's first planned ecotourism destination</div></div></div>
<p><b>Ecotourism</b> is a late 20th-century <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologism</a> compounded from <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/eco-" class="extiw" title="wikt:eco-">eco-</a> and <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tourism" class="extiw" title="wikt:tourism">tourism</a>. According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i>, <i>ecotour</i> was first recorded in 1973 and <i>ecotourism</i>, "probably after <i>ecotour</i>", in 1982.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>ecotour</b>, <i>n</i>. ... A tour of or visit to an area of ecological interest, usually with an educational element; (in later use also) a similar tour or visit designed to have as little detrimental effect on the ecology as possible or undertaken with the specific aim of helping conservation efforts.</li>
<li><b>ecotourism</b>, <i>n</i>. ... Tourism to areas of ecological interest (typically exotic and often threatened natural environments), esp. to support conservation efforts and observe wildlife; spec. access to an endangered environment controlled so as to have the least possible adverse effect.</li></ul>
<p>One source claims the terms were used earlier. Claus-Dieter (Nick) Hetzer, an academic and adventurer from Forum International in Berkeley, CA, supposedly coined <i>ecotourism</i> in 1965 and ran the first <i>ecotours</i> in the Yucatán during the early 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Improving_sustainability">Improving sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Improving sustainability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Principles">Principles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Principles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ecotourism in both terrestrial and marine ecosystems can benefit conservation, provided the complexities of history, culture, and ecology in the affected regions are successfully navigated.<sup id="cite_ref-Macdonald_2017_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macdonald_2017-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Catherine Macdonald and colleagues identify the factors which determine conservation outcome, namely whether: animals and their habits are sufficiently protected; conflict between people and wildlife is avoided or at least suitably mitigated; there is good outreach and education of the local population into the benefits of ecotourism; there is effective collaboration with stakeholders in the area; and there is proper use of the money generated by ecotourism to conserve the local ecology.<sup id="cite_ref-Macdonald_2017_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macdonald_2017-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> They conclude that ecotourism works best to conserve predators when the tourism industry is supported both politically and by the public, and when it is monitored and controlled at local, national, and international levels.<sup id="cite_ref-Macdonald_2017_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Macdonald_2017-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation_and_accreditation">Regulation and accreditation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Regulation and accreditation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because the regulation of ecotourism may be poorly implemented, ecologically destructive greenwashed operations like underwater hotels, helicopter tours, and wildlife theme parks can be categorized as ecotourism along with canoeing, camping, photography, and wildlife observation. The failure to acknowledge responsible, low-impact ecotourism puts legitimate ecotourism companies at a competitive disadvantage.
</p><p>Many environmentalists have argued for a global standard of accreditation, differentiating ecotourism companies based on their level of environmental commitment, creating a standard to follow. A national or international regulatory board would enforce accreditation procedures, with representation from various groups including governments, hotels, tour operators, travel agents, guides, airlines, local authorities, conservation organizations, and non-governmental organizations.<sup id="cite_ref-Elper-Wood_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elper-Wood-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The decisions of the board would be sanctioned by governments, so that non-compliant companies would be legally required to disassociate themselves from the use of the ecotourism brand.
</p><p>Crinion suggests a Green Stars System, based on criteria including a management plan, benefit for the local community, small group interaction, education value and staff training.<sup id="cite_ref-Crinion_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crinion-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Ecotourists who consider their choices would be confident of a genuine ecotourism experience when they see the higher star rating.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_assessment" title="Environmental impact assessment">Environmental impact assessments</a> could also be used as a form of accreditation. Feasibility is evaluated from a scientific basis, and recommendations could be made to optimally plan infrastructure, set tourist capacity, and manage the ecology. This form of accreditation is more sensitive to site specific conditions.
</p><p>Some countries have their own certification programs for ecotourism. Costa Rica, for example, runs the Certification of Sustainable Tourism (CST) program, which is intended to balance the effect that business has on the local environment. The CST program focuses on a company's interaction with natural and cultural resources, the improvement of quality of life within local communities, and the economic contribution to other programs of national development. CST uses a rating system that categorizes a company based upon how sustainable its operations are. CST evaluates the interaction between the company and the surrounding habitat; the management policies and operation systems within the company; how the company encourages its clients to become an active contributor towards sustainable policies; and the interaction between the company and local communities/the overall population. Based upon these criteria, the company is evaluated for the strength of its sustainability. The measurement index goes from 0 to 5, with 0 being the worst and 5 being the best.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Guidelines_and_education">Guidelines and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Guidelines and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2017_Sarasota_Adventure_Kayak_Guided_Tour_Cormorant_among_the_Fleet_04_FRD_9405.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/2017_Sarasota_Adventure_Kayak_Guided_Tour_Cormorant_among_the_Fleet_04_FRD_9405.jpg/220px-2017_Sarasota_Adventure_Kayak_Guided_Tour_Cormorant_among_the_Fleet_04_FRD_9405.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="118" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/2017_Sarasota_Adventure_Kayak_Guided_Tour_Cormorant_among_the_Fleet_04_FRD_9405.jpg/330px-2017_Sarasota_Adventure_Kayak_Guided_Tour_Cormorant_among_the_Fleet_04_FRD_9405.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/2017_Sarasota_Adventure_Kayak_Guided_Tour_Cormorant_among_the_Fleet_04_FRD_9405.jpg/440px-2017_Sarasota_Adventure_Kayak_Guided_Tour_Cormorant_among_the_Fleet_04_FRD_9405.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3853" data-file-height="2065" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2017_Sarasota_Adventure_Kayak_Guided_Tour_Cormorant_among_the_Fleet_04_FRD_9405.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Ecotour guide stands on a <a href="/wiki/Kayaking" title="Kayaking">kayak</a> spotting <a href="/wiki/Dolphins" class="mw-redirect" title="Dolphins">dolphins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manatees" class="mw-redirect" title="Manatees">manatees</a>, around <a href="/wiki/Lido_Key" title="Lido Key">Lido Key</a></div></div></div>
<p>An environmental protection strategy must address the issue of ecotourists removed from the cause-and-effect of their actions on the environment. More initiatives should be carried out to improve their awareness, sensitize them to environmental issues, and care about the places they visit.<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Tour guides are an obvious and direct medium to communicate awareness. With the confidence of ecotourists and intimate knowledge of the environment, tour guides can actively discuss conservation issues. Informing ecotourists about how their actions on the trip can negatively impact their environment and the local people. A tour guide training program in Costa Rica's <a href="/wiki/Tortuguero_National_Park" title="Tortuguero National Park">Tortuguero National Park</a> has helped mitigate negative environmental impacts by providing information and regulating tourists on the parks' beaches used by nesting <a href="/wiki/Endangered_sea_turtles" title="Endangered sea turtles">endangered sea turtles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jacobson_and_Robles_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobson_and_Robles-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-weaver_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weaver-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Small_scale.2C_slow_growth_and_local_control"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Small_scale,_slow_growth_and_local_control">Small scale, slow growth and local control</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Small scale, slow growth and local control">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Underdevelopment" title="Underdevelopment">underdevelopment</a> theory of tourism describes a new form of imperialism by multinational corporations that control ecotourism resources. These corporations finance and profit from the development of large scale ecotourism that causes excessive environmental degradation, loss of traditional culture and way of life, and exploitation of local labor. In <a href="/wiki/Zimbabwe" title="Zimbabwe">Zimbabwe</a> and Nepal's Annapurna region, where underdevelopment is taking place, more than 90 percent of ecotourism revenues are expatriated to the parent countries, and less than 5 percent go into local communities.<sup id="cite_ref-Ziffer_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ziffer-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The lack of sustainability highlights the need for small scale, slow growth, and locally based ecotourism. Local peoples have a vested interest in the well-being of their community, and are therefore more accountable to environmental protection than multinational corporations, though they receive very little of the profits. The lack of control, westernization, adverse impacts to the environment, loss of culture and traditions outweigh the benefits of establishing large scale ecotourism. Additionally, culture loss can be attributed to cultural commodification, in which local cultures are commodified in order to make a profit.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The increased contributions of communities to locally managed ecotourism create viable economic opportunities, including high-level management positions, and reduce environmental issues associated with poverty and unemployment. Because the ecotourism experience is marketed to a different lifestyle from large scale ecotourism, the development of facilities and infrastructure does not need to conform to corporate Western tourism standards, and can be much simpler and less expensive.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> There is a greater <a href="/wiki/Fractional-reserve_banking" title="Fractional-reserve banking">multiplier effect</a> on the economy, because local products, materials, and labor are used. Profits accrue locally and <a href="/wiki/Import" title="Import">import</a> leakages are reduced.<sup id="cite_ref-Cater_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cater-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The Great Barrier Reef Park in Australia reported over half of a billion dollars of indirect income in the area and added thousands of indirect jobs between 2004 and 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-weaver_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weaver-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> However, even this form of tourism may require foreign investment for promotion or start up. When such investments are required, it is crucial for communities to find a company or non-governmental organization that reflects the philosophy of ecotourism; sensitive to their concerns and willing to cooperate at the expense of profit. The basic assumption of the multiplier effect is that the economy starts off with unused resources, for example, that many workers are cyclically unemployed and much of industrial capacity is sitting idle or incompletely utilized. By increasing demand in the economy, it is then possible to boost production. If the economy was already at full employment, with only structural, frictional, or other supply-side types of unemployment, any attempt to boost demand would only lead to inflation. For various laissez-faire schools of economics which embrace Say's Law and deny the possibility of Keynesian inefficiency and under-employment of resources, therefore, the multiplier concept is irrelevant or wrong-headed.
</p><p>As an example, consider the government increasing its expenditure on roads by $1 million, without a corresponding increase in taxation. This sum would go to the road builders, who would hire more workers and distribute the money as wages and profits. The households receiving these incomes will save part of the money and spend the rest on consumer goods. These expenditures, in turn, will generate more jobs, wages, and profits, and so on with the income and spending circulating around the economy.
</p><p>The multiplier effect arises because of the induced increases in consumer spending which occur due to the increased incomes — and because of the feedback into increasing business revenues, jobs, and income again. This process does not lead to an economic explosion not only because of the supply-side barriers at potential output (full employment) but because at each "round", the increase in consumer spending is less than the increase in consumer incomes. That is, the <a href="/wiki/Marginal_propensity_to_consume" title="Marginal propensity to consume">marginal propensity to consume</a> (MPC) is less than one, so that each round some extra income goes into saving, leaking out of the cumulative process. Each increase in spending is thus smaller than that of the previous round, preventing an explosion.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Efforts_to_preserve_ecosystems_at_risk">Efforts to preserve ecosystems at risk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Efforts to preserve ecosystems at risk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some of the world's most exceptional biodiversity is located in the Galapagos Islands.  These islands were designated a UNESCO World Heritage site in 1979, then added to UNESCO’s List of World Heritage in Danger in 2007.  IGTOA is a non-profit dedicated to preserving this unique living laboratory against the challenges of invasive species, human impact, and tourism.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>  For travelers who want to be mindful of the environment and the impact of tourism, it is recommended to utilize an operator that is endorsed by a reputable ecotourism organization.  In the case of the Galapagos, IGTOA has a list<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> of the world’s premiere Galapagos Islands tour companies dedicated to the lasting protection and preservation of the destination.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_resource_management">Natural resource management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Natural resource management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Natural resource management can be utilized as a specialized tool for the development of ecotourism. There are several places throughout the world where a number of natural resources are abundant. But, with human encroachment and habitats, these resources are depleting. Without the sustainable use of certain resources, they are destroyed, and floral and faunal species are becoming extinct. Ecotourism programs can be introduced for the conservation of these resources. Several plans and proper management programs can be introduced so that these resources remain untouched. Several organizations, NGO's, and scientists are working on this field.
</p><p>Natural resources of hill areas like Kurseong in West Bengal are plenty in number with various flora and fauna, but tourism for business purpose poised the situation. Researchers' from Jadavpur University are presently working in this area for the development of ecotourism to be used as a tool for natural resource management.
</p><p>In Southeast Asia government and nongovernmental organizations are working together with academics and industry operators to spread the economic benefits of tourism into the kampungs and villages of the region. A recently formed alliance, the South-East Asian Tourism Organisation (SEATO), is bringing together these diverse players to discuss resource management concerns.
</p><p>A 2002 summit held in Quebec led to the 2008 Global Sustainable Tourism Criteria, a collaborative effort between the UN Foundation and other advocacy groups. The criteria, which are voluntary, involve the following standards: "effective sustainability planning, maximum social and economic benefits for local communities, minimum negative impacts on cultural heritage, and minimum negative impacts on the environment."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed (May 2016)">full citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>There is no enforcing agency or system of punishments.for summit.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the continuum of tourism activities that stretch from conventional tourism to ecotourism, there has been a lot of contention to the limit at which biodiversity preservation, local social-economic benefits, and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental impact">environmental impact</a> can be considered "ecotourism". For this reason, environmentalists, special interest groups, and governments define ecotourism differently. Environmental organizations have generally insisted that ecotourism is nature-based, sustainably managed, conservation supporting, and environmentally educated.<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Buckley_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Buckley-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Tourist_industry" class="mw-redirect" title="Tourist industry">tourist industry</a> and governments, however, focus more on the product aspect, treating ecotourism as equivalent to any sort of tourism based in nature.<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> As a further complication, many terms are used under the rubric of ecotourism.<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Nature tourism, low impact tourism, green tourism, bio-tourism, ecologically responsible tourism, and others have been used in literature and <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">marketing</a>, although they are not necessarily synonymous with ecotourism.<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The problems associated with defining ecotourism have often led to confusion among tourists and academics. Many problems are also subject of considerable public controversy and concern because of <a href="/wiki/Green_wash" class="mw-redirect" title="Green wash">green washing</a>, a trend towards the commercialization of tourism schemes disguised as sustainable, nature based, and <a href="/wiki/Environmentally_friendly" title="Environmentally friendly">environmentally friendly</a> ecotourism.<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> According to McLaren,<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> these schemes are environmentally destructive, economically exploitative, and culturally insensitive at its worst. They are also morally disconcerting because they mislead tourists and manipulate their concerns for the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-Barkin_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barkin-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The development and success of such large scale, energy intensive, and ecologically unsustainable schemes are a testament to the tremendous profits associated with being labeled as ecotourism.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Negative_impact">Negative impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Negative impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ecotourism has become one of the fastest-growing sectors of the tourism industry, growing annually by 10–15% worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-Miller_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miller-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed (May 2016)">full citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> One definition of ecotourism is "the practice of low-impact, educational, ecologically and culturally sensitive travel that benefits local communities and host countries".<sup id="cite_ref-Honey_EandSD_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Honey_EandSD-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>71</span></sup> Many of the ecotourism projects are not meeting these standards. Even if some of the guidelines are being executed, the local communities are still facing many of the negative impacts. South Africa is one of the countries that is reaping significant economic benefits from ecotourism, but the negative effects far outweigh the positive—including forcing people to leave their homes, gross violations of fundamental rights, and <a href="/wiki/Environmental_hazard" title="Environmental hazard">environmental hazards</a>—far outweigh the medium-term economic benefits.<sup id="cite_ref-Miller_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Miller-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed (September 2018)">full citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> A tremendous amount of money and human resources continue to be used for ecotourism despite unsuccessful outcomes, and even more, money is put into public relation campaigns to dilute the effects of criticism. Ecotourism channels resources away from other projects that could contribute more sustainable and realistic solutions to pressing social and environmental problems. "The money tourism can generate often ties parks and managements to ecotourism".<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> But there is a tension in this relationship because ecotourism often causes conflict and changes in land-use rights, fails to deliver promises of community-level benefits, damages environments, and has many other social impacts. Indeed, many argue repeatedly that ecotourism is neither ecologically nor socially beneficial, yet it persists as a strategy for conservation and development,<sup id="cite_ref-West2006_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-West2006-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> due to the large profits. While several studies are being done on ways to improve the ecotourism structure, some argue that these examples provide a rationale for stopping it altogether. However, there are some positive examples, among them the <a href="/wiki/Kavango-Zambezi_Transfrontier_Conservation_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Kavango-Zambezi Transfrontier Conservation Area">Kavango-Zambezi Transfrontier Conservation Area</a> (KAZA) and the <a href="/wiki/Virunga_National_Park" title="Virunga National Park">Virunga National Park</a>, as judged by WWF.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The ecotourism system exercises tremendous financial and political influence. The evidence above shows that a strong case exists for restraining such activities in certain locations. Funding could be used for field studies aimed at finding alternative solutions to tourism and the diverse problems Africa faces in result of urbanization, industrialization, and the <a href="/wiki/Overexploitation" title="Overexploitation">overexploitation</a> of agriculture.<sup id="cite_ref-Kamuaro2007_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kamuaro2007-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> At the local level, ecotourism has become a source of conflict over control of land, resources, and tourism profits. In this case, ecotourism has harmed the environment and local people and has led to conflicts over profit distribution. In a perfect world, more efforts would be made towards educating tourists of the environmental and social effects of their travels. Very few regulations or laws stand in place as boundaries for the investors in ecotourism. These should be implemented to prohibit the promotion of unsustainable ecotourism projects and materials which project false images of destinations, demeaning local and indigenous culture.
</p><p>Though conservation efforts in East Africa are indisputably serving the interests of tourism in the region it is important to make the distinction between conservation acts and the tourism industry.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>   Eastern African communities are not the only of developing regions to experience economic and social harms from conservation efforts.  Conservation in the Northwest <a href="/wiki/Yunnan" title="Yunnan">Yunnan</a> Region of China has similarly brought drastic changes to traditional land use in the region.  Prior to logging restrictions imposed by the Chinese Government the industry made up 80 percent of the regions revenue.  Following a complete ban on commercial logging the indigenous people of the Yunnan region now see little opportunity for economic development.<sup id="cite_ref-Yunnan_expedition_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yunnan_expedition-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>  Ecotourism may provide solutions to the economic hardships suffered from the loss of industry to conservation in the Yunnan in the same way that it may serve to remedy the difficulties faced by the Maasai.  As stated, the ecotourism structure must be improved to direct more money into host communities by reducing leakages for the industry to be successful in alleviating poverty in developing regions, but it provides a promising opportunity.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Direct_environmental_impacts">Direct environmental impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Direct environmental impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ecotourism operations occasionally fail to live up to conservation ideals. It is sometimes overlooked that ecotourism is a highly consumer-centered activity, and that <a href="/wiki/Environmental_conservation" class="mw-redirect" title="Environmental conservation">environmental conservation</a> is a means to further <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Kamauro_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kamauro-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although ecotourism is intended for small groups, even a modest increase in population, however temporary, puts extra pressure on the local environment and necessitates the development of additional infrastructure and amenities. The construction of <a href="/wiki/Water_treatment_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Water treatment plant">water treatment plants</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sanitation" title="Sanitation">sanitation</a> facilities, and lodges come with the exploitation of <a href="/wiki/Non-renewable_energy" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-renewable energy">non-renewable energy</a> sources and the utilization of already <a href="/wiki/Scarcity" title="Scarcity">limited</a> local resources.<sup id="cite_ref-Vivanco_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vivanco-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The conversion of natural land to such tourist infrastructure is implicated in deforestation and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Habitat_deterioration&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Habitat deterioration (page does not exist)">habitat deterioration</a> of butterflies in <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Squirrel_monkeys" class="mw-redirect" title="Squirrel monkeys">squirrel monkeys</a> in <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">Costa Rica</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Isaacs_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Isaacs-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> In other cases, the environment suffers because local communities are unable to meet the infrastructure demands of ecotourism. The lack of adequate sanitation facilities in many East African parks results in the disposal of campsite sewage in rivers, contaminating the wildlife, livestock, and people who draw drinking water from it.<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Aside from <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a> with tourist infrastructure, population pressures from ecotourism also leaves behind garbage and pollution associated with the Western lifestyle.<sup id="cite_ref-McLaren_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McLaren-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Although ecotourists claim to be educationally sophisticated and environmentally concerned, they rarely understand the ecological consequences of their visits and how their day-to-day activities append physical impacts on the environment. As one scientist observes, they "rarely acknowledge how the meals they eat, the toilets they flush, the water they drink, and so on, are all part of broader regional economic and ecological systems they are helping to reconfigure with their very activities."<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Nor do ecotourists recognize the great consumption of non-renewable energy required to arrive at their destination, which is typically more remote than conventional tourism destinations. For instance, an exotic journey to a place 10,000 kilometers away consumes about 700 liters of fuel per person.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ecotourism activities are, in and of themselves, issues in environmental impact because they may disturb fauna and flora. Ecotourists believe that because they are only taking pictures and leaving footprints, they keep ecotourism sites pristine, but even harmless-sounding activities such as nature hikes can be ecologically destructive. In the <a href="/wiki/Annapurna" class="mw-redirect" title="Annapurna">Annapurna</a> Circuit in Nepal, ecotourists have worn down the marked trails and created alternate routes, contributing to <a href="/w/index.php?title=Soil_impaction&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Soil impaction (page does not exist)">soil impaction</a>, erosion, and plant damage.<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Where the ecotourism activity involves wildlife viewing, it can scare away animals, disrupt their feeding and nesting sites,<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> or acclimate them to the presence of people.<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In Kenya, wildlife-observer disruption drives cheetahs off their reserves, increasing the risk of inbreeding and further endangering the species.<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_hazards">Environmental hazards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Environmental hazards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Industrialization" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrialization">industrialization</a>, <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agricultural</a> practices of human society are having a serious impact on the environment. Ecotourism is now also considered to be playing a role in environmental depletion including <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforestation</a>, disruption of ecological life systems and various forms of <a href="/wiki/Pollution" title="Pollution">pollution</a>, all of which contribute to <a href="/wiki/Environmental_degradation" title="Environmental degradation">environmental degradation</a>. For example, the number of motor vehicles crossing a park increases as tour drivers search for rare species. The number of roads disrupts the grass cover, which has serious consequences on plant and animal species. These areas also have a higher rate of <a href="/wiki/Ecological_disturbance" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecological disturbance">disturbances</a> and <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> due to increasing <a href="/wiki/Environmental_impact_of_transport" title="Environmental impact of transport">traffic</a> off of the beaten path into new, undiscovered areas.<sup id="cite_ref-Kamuaro2007_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kamuaro2007-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Ecotourism also has an effect on species through the value placed on them. "Certain species have gone from being little known or valued by local people to being highly valued commodities. The <a href="/wiki/Commodification_of_nature" title="Commodification of nature">commodification of plants</a> may erase their social value and lead to <a href="/wiki/Overproduction" title="Overproduction">overproduction</a> within protected areas. Local people and their images can also be turned into commodities".<sup id="cite_ref-West2006_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-West2006-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Kamuaro points out the relatively obvious contradiction that any commercial venture into unspoiled, pristine land inevitably means a higher pressure on the environment.<sup id="cite_ref-Kamuaro2007_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kamuaro2007-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_people">Local people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Local people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most forms of ecotourism are owned by foreign investors and corporations that provide few benefits to the local people. An overwhelming majority of profits are put into the pockets of investors instead of reinvestment into the local economy or environmental protection leading to further environmental degradation. The limited numbers of local people who are employed in the economy enter at its lowest level and are unable to live in tourist areas because of meager wages and a two-market system.<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In some cases, the resentment by local people results in environmental degradation. As a highly publicized case, the <a href="/wiki/Maasai_people" title="Maasai people">Maasai</a> nomads in Kenya killed wildlife in national parks but are now helping the national park to save the wildlife to show aversion to unfair compensation terms and displacement from traditional lands.<sup id="cite_ref-Cater_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cater-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The lack of economic opportunities for local people also constrains them to degrade the environment as a means of sustenance.<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The presence of affluent ecotourists encourage the development of destructive markets in wildlife souvenirs, such as the sale of coral trinkets on tropical islands and animal products in Asia, contributing to illegal harvesting and <a href="/wiki/Poaching" title="Poaching">poaching</a> from the environment. In <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>, sea turtle reserves use a very large portion of their budget to guard against these destructive activities.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Displacement_of_people">Displacement of people</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Displacement of people">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the worst examples of communities being moved in order to create a park is the story of the <a href="/wiki/Maasai_people" title="Maasai people">Maasai</a>. About 70% of national parks and game reserves in East Africa are on Maasai land.<sup id="cite_ref-Kamuaro2007_36-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kamuaro2007-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> The first negative impact of tourism was the land lost from the Maasai culture. Local and national governments took advantage of the Maasai’s ignorance on the situation and robbed them of huge chunks of grazing land, putting to risk their only socio-economic livelihood. In Kenya, the Maasai also have not gained any economic benefits. Despite the loss of their land, employment favors better-educated workers. Furthermore, the investors in this area are not local and have not put any profits back into the local economy. In some cases, <a href="/wiki/Game_reserve" title="Game reserve">game reserves</a> can be created without informing or consulting the local people. They only find out when an <a href="/wiki/Eviction" title="Eviction">eviction</a> notice is delivered.<sup id="cite_ref-Kamuaro2007_36-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kamuaro2007-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Another source of resentment is the manipulation of the local people by their government. "Eco-tourism works to create simplistic images of local people and their uses and understandings of their surroundings. Through the lens of these simplified images, officials direct policies and projects towards the local people and the local people are blamed if the projects fail" (West, 2006). Clearly, tourism as a trade is not empowering the local people who make it rich and satisfying. Instead, ecotourism exploits and depletes, particularly in African Maasai tribes. It has to be reoriented if it is to be useful to local communities and to become sustainable.<sup id="cite_ref-Kamuaro2007_36-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kamuaro2007-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Threats_to_indigenous_cultures">Threats to indigenous cultures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Threats to indigenous cultures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ecotourism often claims that it preserves and "enhances" local cultures. Evidence shows that with the establishment of protected areas local people have illegally lost their homes, and mostly with no compensation.<sup id="cite_ref-Kamuaro2007_36-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kamuaro2007-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Pushing people onto marginal lands with harsh climates, poor soils, lack of water, and infested with livestock and disease does little to enhance livelihoods even when a proportion of ecotourism profits are directed back into the community. The establishment of parks can create harsh survival realities and deprive the people of their traditional use of land and natural resources. Ethnic groups are increasingly being seen as a "backdrop" to the scenery and wildlife. The local people struggle for cultural survival and freedom of cultural expression while being "observed" by tourists. Local indigenous people also have a strong resentment towards the change, "Tourism has been allowed to develop with virtually no controls. Too many lodges have been built, too much firewood is being used and no limits are being placed on tourism vehicles. They regularly drive off-track and harass the wildlife. Their vehicle tracks criss-cross the entire Masai Mara. Inevitably <a href="/wiki/The_bush" title="The bush">the bush</a> is becoming <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">eroded</a> and <a href="/wiki/Land_degradation" title="Land degradation">degraded</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Kamuaro2007_36-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kamuaro2007-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mismanagement">Mismanagement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Mismanagement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While governments are typically entrusted with the administration and enforcement of environmental protection, they often lack the commitment or capability to manage ecotourism sites. The regulations for environmental protection may be vaguely defined, costly to implement, hard to enforce, and uncertain in effectiveness.<sup id="cite_ref-Baumol_and_Oates_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baumol_and_Oates-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Government regulatory agencies, are susceptible to making decisions that spend on politically beneficial but environmentally unproductive projects. Because of prestige and conspicuousness, the construction of an attractive visitor's center at an ecotourism site may take precedence over more pressing environmental concerns like acquiring habitat, protecting endemic species, and removing invasive ones.<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Finally, influential groups can <a href="/wiki/Lobbying" title="Lobbying">pressure, and sway</a> the interests of the government to their favor. The government and its regulators can become vested in the benefits of the ecotourism industry which they are supposed to regulate, causing restrictive environmental regulations and enforcement to become more lenient.
</p><p>Management of ecotourism sites by private ecotourism companies offers an alternative to the cost of regulation and deficiency of government agencies. It is believed that these companies have a self-interest in limited environmental degradation because tourists will pay more for pristine environments, which translates to higher profit. However, theory indicates that this practice is not economically feasible and will fail to manage the environment.
</p><p>The model of <a href="/wiki/Monopolistic_competition" title="Monopolistic competition">monopolistic competition</a> states that distinctiveness will entail profits, but profits will promote imitation. A company that protects its ecotourism sites is able to charge a premium for the novel experience and pristine environment. But when other companies view the success of this approach, they also enter the market with similar practices, increasing competition and reducing demand. Eventually, the demand will be reduced until the economic profit is zero. A cost-benefit analysis shows that the company bears the cost of environmental protection without receiving the gains. Without economic incentive, the whole premise of self-interest through environmental protection is quashed; instead, ecotourism companies will minimize environment related expenses and maximize tourism demand.<sup id="cite_ref-Tuohino_and_Hynonen_8-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuohino_and_Hynonen-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Tragedy_of_the_commons" title="Tragedy of the commons">tragedy of the commons</a> offers another model for economic unsustainability from environmental protection, in ecotourism sites utilized by many companies.<sup id="cite_ref-Hardin_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hardin-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Although there is a communal incentive to protect the environment, maximizing the benefits in the long run, a company will conclude that it is in their best interest to utilize the ecotourism site beyond its sustainable level. By increasing the number of ecotourists, for instance, a company gains all the economic benefit while paying only a part of the environmental cost. In the same way, a company recognizes that there is no incentive to actively protect the environment; they bear all the costs, while the benefits are shared by all other companies. The result, again, is mismanagement.
</p><p>Taken together, the mobility of foreign investment and lack of economic incentive for environmental protection means that ecotourism companies are disposed to establishing themselves in new sites once their existing one is sufficiently degraded.
</p><p>In addition, the systematic literature review conducted by Cabral and Dhar (2019) have identified several challenges due to slow progression of ecotourism initiatives such as (a) economic leakages, (b) lack of government involvement, (c) skill deficiency among the local communities,  (d) absence of disseminating environmental education, (e) sporadic increase in pollution, (f) conflict between  tourism management personnel and local communities and (g) inadequate infrastructure development.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Case_studies">Case studies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecotourism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Case studies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The purpose of ecotourism is to engage tourists in low impact, non-consumptive and locally oriented environments in order to maintain species and habitats — especially in underdeveloped regions.  While some ecotourism projects, including some found in the United States, can support such claims, many projects have failed to address some of the fundamental issues that nations face in the first place. Consequently, ecotourism may not generate the very benefits it is intended to provide to these regions and their people, and in some cases leaving economies in a state worse than before.<sup id="cite_ref-Fennell,_David_A._48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fennell,_David_A.-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The following case studies illustrate the rising complexity of ecotourism and its impacts, both positive and negative, on the environment and economies of various regions in the world.
