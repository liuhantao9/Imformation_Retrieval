<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slaithwaite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The village was part of the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Dartmouth" title="Earl of Dartmouth">Earl of Dartmouth</a> estates, a chapelry, in the parishes of Huddersfield and <a href="/wiki/Almondbury" title="Almondbury">Almondbury</a>, union of Huddersfield, Upper division of the <a href="/wiki/Wapentake" class="mw-redirect" title="Wapentake">wapentake</a> of <a href="/wiki/Agbrigg" title="Agbrigg">Agbrigg</a> and included the <a href="/wiki/Township" title="Township">township</a> of 'Lingarths' (Lingards)<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and the Township of Slaithwaite.
</p><p>In the early 19th century a local spring was discovered to contain <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulphurous</a> properties and minerals, similar to those found in <a href="/wiki/Harrogate" title="Harrogate">Harrogate</a>. Sometime after 1820 a bathing facility was built, along with a gardens and pleasure ground, with some visitor cottages. A free <a href="/wiki/School" title="School">school</a> was founded in 1721 and rebuilt twice: first in 1744, and again in 1842.
</p><p>In the 1848 edition of 'A Topographical Dictionary of England', Samuel Lewis (the editor) wrote:-
"<i>the lands are in meadow and pasture, with a small portion of arable; the scenery is bold and romantic. In the quarries of the district are found vegetable fossils, especially firs and other mountain trees. The village is beautifully seated in the valley of the river Colne; the inhabitants are chiefly employed in the woollen manufacture, in the spinning of cotton and silk, and in silk-weaving</i>"<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Slaithwaite Hall, thought to date from the mid-15th century, is located on a nearby hillside. It is one of a number of <a href="/wiki/Cruck" title="Cruck">cruck</a> framed buildings clustered in this area of West Yorkshire. After many years divided into <a href="/wiki/Cottages" class="mw-redirect" title="Cottages">cottages</a>, the building has been extensively restored and is now a single dwelling.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Legend" title="Legend">Legend</a> has it that local <a href="/wiki/Smugglers" class="mw-redirect" title="Smugglers">smugglers</a> caught by the <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Customs_and_Excise" class="mw-redirect" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Customs and Excise">excise men</a> tried to explain their <a href="/wiki/Nocturnal" class="mw-redirect" title="Nocturnal">nocturnal</a> activities as 'raking the moon from the canal' and definitely not as 'fishing out smuggled <a href="/wiki/Brandy" title="Brandy">brandy</a>'.<sup id="cite_ref-SM_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SM-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> A "Moonraker" is now the official<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (August 2010)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nickname</a> for a native of the village. Similar stories and nicknames exist for the neighbouring villages of <a href="/wiki/Golcar" title="Golcar">Golcar</a> ("Lillies"), <a href="/wiki/Marsden,_West_Yorkshire" title="Marsden, West Yorkshire">Marsden</a> ("Cuckoos") and <a href="/wiki/Linthwaite" title="Linthwaite">Linthwaite</a> ("Leadboilers"). The legend is also known in <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a>, where the locals are also known as 'Moonrakers'.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An <a href="/wiki/Apocrypha" title="Apocrypha">apocryphal</a> story associated with Slaithwaite is that in the 19th century, when tenants came to the village to pay their annual rent, The <a href="/wiki/William_Legge,_5th_Earl_of_Dartmouth" title="William Legge, 5th Earl of Dartmouth">5th Earl of Dartmouth</a> would provide 'rent dinners' and that the shoulder of mutton was the most favoured with the tables of hot roast beef, mutton and tongue (cooked together in the set pot) being available there at that time.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slaithwaite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Recent projects have seen a major restoration of the canal. That required a full re-<a href="/wiki/Earthworks_(engineering)" title="Earthworks (engineering)">excavation</a> and new <a href="/wiki/Lock_(water_transport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lock (water transport)">lock gates</a>. Following the emergence of the <a href="/wiki/Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway">railway</a> network they were little used and closed down then filled in during 1956. The refurbishment has revitalised the <a href="/wiki/High_street" class="mw-redirect" title="High street">high street</a> area and Slaithwaite is currently the only village in England with a canal running alongside its main street.
</p><p>There are several significant local employers, including <a href="/wiki/Thornton_%26_Ross" title="Thornton &amp; Ross">Thornton &amp; Ross</a> (a pharmaceuticals manufacturer), Shaw Pallets and Spectrum Yarns - one of a small number of remaining textiles businesses in the Colne Valley, once a major centre for wool and yarn.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shopping_and_entertainment">Shopping and entertainment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slaithwaite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Shopping and entertainment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Silent_Woman_Pub,_Slaithwaite,_Huddersfield_01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Silent_Woman_Pub%2C_Slaithwaite%2C_Huddersfield_01.jpg/220px-Silent_Woman_Pub%2C_Slaithwaite%2C_Huddersfield_01.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="138" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Silent_Woman_Pub%2C_Slaithwaite%2C_Huddersfield_01.jpg/330px-Silent_Woman_Pub%2C_Slaithwaite%2C_Huddersfield_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Silent_Woman_Pub%2C_Slaithwaite%2C_Huddersfield_01.jpg/440px-Silent_Woman_Pub%2C_Slaithwaite%2C_Huddersfield_01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2480" data-file-height="1551" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Silent_Woman_Pub,_Slaithwaite,_Huddersfield_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Silent Woman public house</div></div></div>
<p>There are several traditional <a href="/wiki/Public_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Public house">public houses</a> in Slaithwaite, including the Commercial, the Shoulder of Mutton, the Swan, and the 'Silent Woman' which came to the attention of the world media on 23 September 2007, when a man walked into the pub and ordered a pint of beer a few minutes after he had murdered his son and attacked his daughter with a knife.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  Alternative dining and drinking venues are the Little Bridge, Vanilla Bean, Om Is Where The Heart Is, Ashbys and Jax Bar tapas. There is a new pub and restaurant called Nobles on Manchester Road.
</p><p>Slaithwaite has a vibrant centre with many independent shops, a post office, <a href="/wiki/Restaurant" title="Restaurant">restaurants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coffeehouse" title="Coffeehouse">cafes</a>. Of particular note is the local butcher, E. Grange &amp; Son (Master Pie Makers), selling their own brand of pork pies, which have won some awards.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Other shops include the community-owned cooperative the 'Green Valley Grocer', the workers' cooperative the 'Handmade Bakery,' the 'Chocolate Corner' gift shop and cafe, and Mystical Moments - which hit national headlines in August 2016 as the Magic Wand shop'.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slaithwaite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Slaithwaite_viaduct_(5th_September_2010).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Slaithwaite_viaduct_%285th_September_2010%29.jpg/220px-Slaithwaite_viaduct_%285th_September_2010%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Slaithwaite_viaduct_%285th_September_2010%29.jpg/330px-Slaithwaite_viaduct_%285th_September_2010%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Slaithwaite_viaduct_%285th_September_2010%29.jpg/440px-Slaithwaite_viaduct_%285th_September_2010%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2226" data-file-height="1484" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Slaithwaite_viaduct_(5th_September_2010).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Slaithwaite Viaduct</div></div></div>
<p>More modern transport is provided by the <a href="/wiki/Slaithwaite_railway_station" title="Slaithwaite railway station">Slaithwaite railway station</a>, which has direct trains to <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>. The Colne Valley defines local geography by channelling the railway line, the canal and the <a href="/wiki/A62_road" title="A62 road">A62</a>: each of which has at one time been the primary means of transport across the <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennines</a>. The small <a href="/wiki/Humpback_Bridge" class="mw-redirect" title="Humpback Bridge">humpbacked bridge</a> over the canal is called 'Tim Brig'; it is said to be named after a local inn keeper who used the bridge during smuggling operations with the <a href="/wiki/Narrowboat" title="Narrowboat">narrowboats</a> passing through.
</p><p>There are several bus links from Slaithwaite to several places in the Huddersfield area. Services run by <a href="/wiki/First_West_Yorkshire" title="First West Yorkshire">First West Yorkshire</a> go from Slaithwaite to <a href="/wiki/Marsden,_West_Yorkshire" title="Marsden, West Yorkshire">Marsden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oldham" title="Oldham">Oldham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> and to Huddersfield. Other bus services are to <a href="/wiki/Holmfirth" title="Holmfirth">Holmfirth</a>, Blackmoorfoot and surrounding villages.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slaithwaite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In February, on alternate years, Slaithwaite celebrates a 19th-century legend of Moonraking<sup id="cite_ref-SM_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SM-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> with the Slaithwaite Moonraking Festival, with a week of lantern making and a programme of storytelling. The week-long celebration, which always takes place during the school half term week, ends with a parade of lanterns around the village, and a festival finale by the canal in the centre of the village. A heritage lottery funded project 'Wild about Wool', that is collating memories of the industrial heritage of the Colne Valley, is linked to the festival. 'Wool' was also the theme of the festival held in February 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Slaithwaite Philharmonic Orchestra<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> was founded here in 1891. An 80-strong amateur orchestra, the orchestra also plays an annual season of concerts in Huddersfield.
</p><p>Slaithwaite Brass Band<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> have been making music here since 1892. They perform at many concerts and events throughout the year and have had many successes over the years including being the first band to gain the Grand Shield twice.
</p><p>Parts of both Slaithwaite and <a href="/wiki/Marsden,_West_Yorkshire" title="Marsden, West Yorkshire">Marsden</a> were used to create the village called 'Skelthwaite' in the British TV show <i><a href="/wiki/Where_the_Heart_Is_(1997_TV_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Where the Heart Is (1997 TV series)">Where the Heart Is</a></i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slaithwaite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rugby League is the main sport played in the village. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20100216125804/http://www.slaithwaitesaracens.co.uk/">Slaithwaite Saracens</a> compete in the Pennine Rugby League premier division, as champions of Division One. They achieved the Huddersfield double by also winning the Huddersfield Holliday cup. Cricket is also very popular in the village. Slaithwaite Cricket &amp; Bowling Club, situated on Hill Top, during the summer becomes the heart of the community. It is a thriving club with many successful teams including winning Second XI Premiership Championship in the 2010 season.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The village also has its own running club named Slaithwaite Striders, which has a mixture of all abilities, meeting weekly to run and enjoy the surrounding roads, paths and of course the views.
</p>
