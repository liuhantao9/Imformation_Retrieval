<p><b>Eid al-Adha</b> (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl">عيد الأضحى</span>&#8206;, <small><a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Arabic" title="Romanization of Arabic">romanized</a>:&#160;</small><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">ʿīd al-ʾaḍḥā</i>, <small><a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Literal translation">lit.</a>&#160;</small>&#39;Feast of the Sacrifice&#39;, <small>IPA:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Arabic" title="Help:IPA/Arabic">[ʕiːd ælˈʔɑdˤħæː]</a></span>) or <b>Eid Qurban</b>  (<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>: <span lang="fa" dir="rtl">عيد قربان</span>&#8206;), also called the "<b>Festival of the Sacrifice</b>", is the second of two <a href="/wiki/Islamic_holidays" title="Islamic holidays">Islamic holidays</a> celebrated worldwide each year (the other being <a href="/wiki/Eid_al-Fitr" title="Eid al-Fitr">Eid al-Fitr</a>), and considered the holier of the two. It honours the willingness of <a href="/wiki/Abraham_in_Islam" title="Abraham in Islam">Ibrahim (Abraham)</a> to sacrifice his son as an act of obedience to <a href="/wiki/God_in_Islam" title="God in Islam">God's</a> command. But, before Abraham could sacrifice his son, God provided a lamb to sacrifice instead. In commemoration of this intervention, an animal is sacrificed ritually and divided into three parts. One share is given to the poor and needy, another is kept for home, and the third is given to relatives.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">Islamic lunar calendar</a>, <i>Eid al-Adha</i> falls on the 10th day of <a href="/wiki/Dhu_al-Hijjah" title="Dhu al-Hijjah">Dhu al-Hijjah</a>, and lasts for three days. In the international <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">(Gregorian) calendar</a>, the dates vary from year to year shifting approximately 11 days earlier each year.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_names">Other names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eid_al-Adha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Other names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In languages other than Arabic, the name is often simply translated into the local language, such as English <span lang="en" title="English language text">Feast of the Sacrifice</span>, German <i lang="de" title="German language text">Opferfest</i>, Dutch <i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text">Offerfeest</i>, <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a> <i lang="ro" title="Romanian language text">Sărbătoarea Sacrificiului</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a> <i lang="hu" title="Hungarian language text">Áldozati ünnep</i>. In Spanish it is known as <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">Fiesta del Cordero</i><sup id="cite_ref-Webislam_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Webislam-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> or <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">Fiesta del Borrego</i> (both meaning "festival of the lamb"). In <a href="/wiki/Kurdish_languages" title="Kurdish languages">Kurdish</a> it is known as (<i>Cejna Qurbanê /</i> جەژنی قوربان). It is also known as <i>Eid Qurban</i> (<span lang="fa" dir="rtl" title="Persian language text">عید قربان</span>) in <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian speaking countries</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <i lang="tr" title="Turkish language text">Kurban Bayramı</i><sup title="Turkey">Turkey</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-britannica_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <span lang="bn" title="Bengali language text">কোরবানীর ঈদ</span> in <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>, as <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">عید الكبير</span> the big Feast in the <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">Maghreb</a>, as <i>Iduladha</i>, <i>Hari Raya Aiduladha</i>, <i>Hari Raya Haji</i> or <i>Qurban</i> in <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, as <span lang="ur" dir="rtl" title="Urdu language text">بکرا عید</span>  "Goat Eid" or <span lang="ur" dir="rtl" title="Urdu language text">بڑی عید</span> "Greater Eid" in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a>, <i>Bakara Eid</i> in <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a> (most probably borrowed from the <a href="/wiki/Serer_language" title="Serer language">Serer language</a> – and an ancient <a Nigeria<sup id="cite_ref-Bianchi2004_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bianchi2004-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ramzy2012_14-0" class="reference"><a </p><p>The following names are used as other names of Eid al-Adha:
</p>
<ul><li><span lang="ur" dir="rtl" title="Urdu language text">عیدالاضحیٰ</span> (transliterations of the Arabic name)<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> is used in Urdu, Hindi, Assamese, Bengali, Gujarati, and <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">Austronesian languages</a> such as Malay and Indonesian.</li>
<li><span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">العيد الكبير</span> meaning "Greater Eid" (the "Lesser Eid" being Eid al-Fitr)<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> is used in Yemen, Syria, and North Africa (Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia, Libya, and Egypt). <li><span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">عید البقرة</span> (<i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic language text">eid al-baqara</i>) meaning "the Feast of Cows (also sheep or goats)" is used in Egypt, Saudi Arabia, and the Middle East. Although the word &#8207;<span lang="ar" dir="rtl"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D8%A8%D9%82%D8%B1%D8%A9#Arabic" class="extiw" title="wikt:بقرة">بقرة</a></span>&#8206; properly means a cow, it is also semantically extended to mean all livestock, especially sheep or goats. This extension is used in Hindi and Urdu as a very similar name <span lang="hi" title="Hindi language text">ईद-उल-अज़हा</span> (<i lang="hi-Latn" title="Hindi language text">īd-ul-azhā</i>, 'the Feast of goat') is used for the occasion.</li>
<li>The Feast of Sacrifice is used in Uzbekistan.</li>
<li>The Hajj Feast<sup id="cite_ref-Bianchi2004_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bianchi2004-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ramzy2012_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ramzy2012-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> is used in Malaysian and Indonesian, in the Philippines.</li>
<li>Big Sallah in Nigeria, as it is considered to be holier than Eid al-Fitr (which is locally known as the "Small Sallah").<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> "Ram Sallah" is also used, as it refers to the rams that are being sacrificed on that day.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eid_al-Adha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The word <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">عيد</span> (<i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic language text">ʿīd</i>) means 'festival', 'celebration', 'feast day', or 'holiday'. It itself is a <a href="/wiki/Semitic_root" title="Semitic root">triliteral root</a> &#8207;<span lang="ar" dir="rtl"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D8%B9%D9%8A%D8%AF#Arabic" class="extiw" title="wikt:عيد">عيد</a></span>&#8206; with associated root meanings of "to go back, to rescind, to accrue, to be accustomed, habits, to repeat, to be experienced; appointed time or place, anniversary, feast day."<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_Arabic_Dictionary_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_Arabic_Dictionary-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brill_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brill-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Jeffery" title="Arthur Jeffery">Arthur Jeffery</a> contests this etymology, and believes the term to have been borrowed into Arabic from Syriac, or less likely Targumic Aramaic.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The words <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">أضحى</span> (<i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic language text">aḍḥā</i>) and <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">قربان</span> (<i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic language text">qurbān</i>) are synonymous in meaning 'sacrifice' (animal sacrifice), 'offering' or 'oblation'. The first word comes from the triliteral root <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">ضحى</span> (<i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic language text">ḍaḥḥā</i>) with associated meanings of "immolate&#160;; offer up&#160;; sacrifice&#160;; victimize."<sup id="cite_ref-Almaany_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Almaany-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> No occurrence of this root with a meaning related to sacrifice occurs in the Qur'an<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_Arabic_Dictionary_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_Arabic_Dictionary-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> but in the Hadith literature. Arab Christians use the term to mean the <a href="/wiki/Sacramental_bread" title="Sacramental bread">Eucharistic host</a>. The second word derives from the triliteral root &#8207;<span lang="ar" dir="rtl"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D9%82_%D8%B1_%D8%A8#Arabic" class="extiw" title="wikt:ق ر ب">&#8207;قرب&#8206;</a></span>&#8206; (<i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic language text">qaraba</i>) with associated meanings of "closeness, proximity... to moderate; kinship...; to hurry; ...to seek, to seek water sources...; scabbard, sheath; small boat; sacrifice."<sup id="cite_ref-Brill_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brill-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Jeffery" title="Arthur Jeffery">Arthur Jeffery</a> recognizes the same Semitic root, but believes the sense of the term to have entered Arabic through <a href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">Aramaic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Compare Hebrew <i><a href="/wiki/Korban" title="Korban">korban</a></i> <span lang="he" dir="rtl" title="Hebrew language text">קָרבן</span> (<i lang="he-Latn" title="Hebrew language text">qorbān</i>).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eid_al-Adha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One of the main trials of Ibrahim's life was to face the command of God to sacrifice his dearest possession, his son.<sup id="cite_ref-britannica_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The earliest Islamic traditions identify Isma’il <a href="/wiki/Ishmael_in_Islam" title="Ishmael in Islam">(Ishmael</a>) as the son who was sacrificed. Upon hearing this command, Abraham prepared to submit to the <a href="/wiki/Will_of_God" title="Will of God">will of God</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bate_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bate-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> During this preparation, <a href="/wiki/Devil_(Islam)" class="mw-redirect" title="Devil (Islam)">Shaytaan (the Devil)</a> tempted Abraham and his family by trying to dissuade them from carrying out God's commandment, and Ibraham drove Satan away by throwing pebbles at him. In commemoration of their rejection of Satan, stones are thrown at symbolic pillars during the <a href="/wiki/Stoning_of_the_Devil" title="Stoning of the Devil">Stoning of the Devil</a> during Hajj rites.<sup id="cite_ref-SUNY_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SUNY-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When Ibraham attempted to cut his son's throat on mount Arafat,<sup id="cite_ref-Bate_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bate-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> he was astonished to see that his son was unharmed and instead, he found an animal<sup id="cite_ref-britannica_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britannica-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> which was slaughtered. Ibraham had passed the test by his willingness to carry out God's command.<sup id="cite_ref-Elias_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Elias-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This story is known as the <a href="/wiki/Binding_of_Isaac" title="Binding of Isaac">Akedah</a> in Judaism (Binding of Isaac) and originates in the <a href="/wiki/Torah" title="Torah">Torah</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Stephan_Huller_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stephan_Huller-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> the first book of Moses (<a href="/wiki/Book_of_Genesis" title="Book of Genesis">Genesis</a>, Ch. 22). The Quran refers to the <a href="/wiki/Akedah" class="mw-redirect" title="Akedah">Akedah</a> as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-Wiley-Blackwell_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wiley-Blackwell-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><div class="poem">
<p>100 "O my Lord! Grant me a righteous (son)!"<br />
101 So We gave him the good news of a boy ready to suffer and forbear.<br />
102 Then, when (the son) reached (the age of) (serious) work with him, he said: "O my son! I see in vision that I offer thee in sacrifice: Now see what is thy view!" (The son) said: "O my father! Do as thou art commanded: thou will find me if Allah so wills one practicing Patience and Constancy!"<br />
103 So when they had both submitted their wills (to Allah), and he had laid him prostrate on his forehead (for sacrifice),<br />
104 We called out to him "O Ibraham!<br />
105 "Thou hast already fulfilled the vision!"&#160;&#8211;&#32; thus indeed do We reward those who do right.<br />
106 For this was obviously a trial–<br />
107 And We ransomed him with a momentous sacrifice:<br />
108 And We left (this blessing) for him among generations (to come) in later times:<br />
109 "Peace and salutation to Abraham!"<br />
110 Thus indeed do We reward those who do right.<br />
111 For he was one of our believing Servants.<br />
112 And We gave him the good news of Isaac&#160;&#8211;&#32; a prophet&#160;&#8211;&#32; one of the Righteous.
</p>
</div><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite>Quran, sura 37 (<a href="/wiki/As-Saaffat" title="As-Saaffat">Aṣ-Ṣāffāt</a>), āyāt 100–112<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>The word "Eid" appears once in <a href="/wiki/Al-Ma%27ida" title="Al-Ma&#39;ida">Al-Ma'ida</a>, the fifth sura of the Quran, with the meaning "solemn festival".<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eid_prayers">Eid prayers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eid_al-Adha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Eid prayers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Eid_prayers" title="Eid prayers">Eid prayers</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Badshahi_in_all_its_glory_during_the_Eid_Prayers.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/The_Badshahi_in_all_its_glory_during_the_Eid_Prayers.JPG/220px-The_Badshahi_in_all_its_glory_during_the_Eid_Prayers.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/The_Badshahi_in_all_its_glory_during_the_Eid_Prayers.JPG/330px-The_Badshahi_in_all_its_glory_during_the_Eid_Prayers.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/The_Badshahi_in_all_its_glory_during_the_Eid_Prayers.JPG/440px-The_Badshahi_in_all_its_glory_during_the_Eid_Prayers.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Badshahi_in_all_its_glory_during_the_Eid_Prayers.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eid prayer at the <a href="/wiki/Badshahi_Mosque" title="Badshahi Mosque">Badshahi Mosque</a></div></div></div>
<p>Devotees offer the Eid al-Adha prayers at the mosque. The Eid al-Adha prayer is performed any time after the sun completely rises up to just before the entering of <i><a href="/wiki/Zuhr_prayer" title="Zuhr prayer">Zuhr</a></i> time, on the 10th of Dhu al-Hijjah. In the event of a <i><a href="/wiki/Force_majeure" title="Force majeure">force majeure</a></i> (e.g. natural disaster), the prayer may be delayed to the 11th of Dhu al-Hijjah and then to the 12th of Dhu al-Hijjah.<sup id="cite_ref-H._X._Lee_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H._X._Lee-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Eid prayers must be offered in congregation. Participation of women in the prayer congregation varies from community to community.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> It consists of two <i><a href="/wiki/Rakat" title="Rakat">rakats</a></i> (units) with seven <i><a href="/wiki/Takbir" title="Takbir">takbirs</a></i> in the first Raka'ah and five Takbirs in the second Raka'ah. For <a href="/wiki/Shia_Islam" title="Shia Islam">Shia Muslims</a>, Salat al-Eid differs from the five daily canonical prayers in that no <i><a href="/wiki/Adhan" title="Adhan">adhan</a></i> (call to prayer) or <i><a href="/wiki/Iqama" title="Iqama">iqama</a></i> (call) is pronounced for the two Eid prayers.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Salat" class="mw-redirect" title="Salat">salat</a></i> (prayer) is then followed by the khutbah, or sermon, by the <a href="/wiki/Imam" title="Imam">Imam</a>.
</p><p>At the conclusion of the prayers and sermon, Muslims embrace and exchange greetings with one another (<i><a href="/wiki/Eid_Mubarak" title="Eid Mubarak">Eid Mubarak</a></i>), give gifts and visit one another. Many Muslims also take this opportunity to invite their non-Muslim friends, neighbours, co-workers and classmates to their Eid festivities to better acquaint them about Islam and Muslim culture.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditions_and_practices">Traditions and practices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eid_al-Adha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Traditions and practices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Men, women, and children are expected to dress in their finest clothing to perform Eid prayer in a large congregation in an open <i><a href="/wiki/Waqf" title="Waqf">waqf</a></i> ("stopping") field called Eidgah or mosque. Affluent Muslims who can afford it sacrifice their best <a href="/wiki/Halal" title="Halal">halal</a> domestic animals (usually a cow, but can also be a camel, goat, sheep, or ram depending on the region) as a symbol of Abraham's willingness to sacrifice his only son.<sup id="cite_ref-Ekrem_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ekrem-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The sacrificed animals, called <i lang="ar-Latn" title="Hans Wehr transliteration">aḍḥiya</i> (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl">أضحية</span>&#8206;), known also by the Perso-Arabic term <i>qurbāni</i>, have to meet certain age and quality standards or else the animal is considered an unacceptable sacrifice.<sup id="cite_ref-CABI_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CABI-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In Pakistan alone nearly ten million animals are slaughtered on Eid days costing over US$2.0 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The meat from the sacrificed animal is preferred to be divided into three parts. The family retains one-third of the share; another third is given to relatives, friends, and neighbors; and the remaining third is given to the poor and needy.<sup id="cite_ref-Ekrem_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ekrem-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Muslims wear their new or best clothes. Women cook special sweets, including <a href="/wiki/Ma%27amoul" title="Ma&#39;amoul">ma'amoul</a> (filled shortbread cookies). They gather with family and friends.<sup id="cite_ref-H._X._Lee_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H._X._Lee-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eid_al-Adha_in_the_Gregorian_calendar">Eid al-Adha in the Gregorian calendar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eid_al-Adha&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Eid al-Adha in the Gregorian calendar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">Islamic calendar</a></div>
<p>While Eid al-Adha is always on the same day of the Islamic calendar, the date on the Gregorian calendar varies from year to year since the Islamic calendar is a lunar calendar and the Gregorian calendar is a <a href="/wiki/Solar_calendar" title="Solar calendar">solar calendar</a>. The lunar calendar is approximately eleven days shorter than the solar calendar.<sup id="cite_ref-Chris_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chris-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Each year, Eid al-Adha (like other Islamic holidays) falls on one of about two to four different Gregorian dates in different parts of the world, because the boundary of crescent visibility is different from the <a href="/wiki/International_Date_Line" title="International Date Line">International Date Line</a>.
</p><p>The following list shows the official dates of Eid al-Adha for Saudi Arabia as announced by the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Judicial_Council_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="Supreme Judicial Council of Saudi Arabia">Supreme Judicial Council</a>. Future dates are estimated according to the Umm al-Qura calendar of Saudi Arabia.<sup id="cite_ref-ummalqura_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ummalqura-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The Umm al-Qura is just a guide for planning purposes and not the absolute determinant or fixer of dates. Confirmations of actual dates by moon sighting are applied on the 29th day of the lunar month prior to Dhu al-Hijjah<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> to announce the specific dates for both Hajj rituals and the subsequent Eid festival. The three days after the listed date are also part of the festival. The time before the listed date the pilgrims visit the Mount Arafat and descend from it after sunrise of the listed day.
</p><p>In many countries, the start of any <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">lunar Hijri</a> month varies based on the observation of new moon by local religious authorities, so the exact day of celebration varies by locality.
</p>
