	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Tiriyó language</h1>

<p>The <b>Tiriyó</b> language is the everyday language of the <a href="/wiki/Tiriy%C3%B3" title="Tiriyó">Tiriyó</a> people, the majority of whom are monolingual.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Although Tiriyó is the preferred spelling, the Tiriyó refer to themselves as <i>tarëno</i>; other variations, including <i>tarano</i>, <i>tirió</i>, and <i>trio</i>, exist. The Tiriyó are located on both sides of the <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>-<a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> border in Lowland <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. Because Tiriyó is spoken by the entire Tiriyó population, its level of endangerment is low. However, it may be threatened by the presence of a newly installed radar station staffed by a considerable number of non-Indigenous people close to the main village.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The modern Tiriyó is formed from various different Indigenous communities; some of these, such as the Aramixó, are mentioned in European writings as early as 1609-1610.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Many of the now-Tiriyó groups lived between Brazil and <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a> until they were driven out by the <a href="/wiki/Wayampi" title="Wayampi">Oyampi</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Tupi_people" title="Tupi people">Tupi-Guaranian group</a> allied with the Portuguese. Together, the Portuguese and Oyampi drove these groups westward, and they mingled with the groups that were in the area to form the modern Tiriyó group.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As such, the Tiriyó established contact relatively early with runaway slave groups that settled in the area around the end of the 18th century. They maintained regular commercial relations with one group, the <a href="/wiki/Ndyuka_people" title="Ndyuka people">Ndyuka</a>, and for many years they were the only contact the Tiriyó had with foreign populations.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The first recorded contact between the Tiriyó and a European took place in 1843 between a ‘Drio’ village and Robert Schomburgk; this and the meeting between French explorer Jules Crevaux and a few ‘Trio’ were the only two points of contact between Tiriyó and Europeans in the 19th Century.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
Subsequent contact between Europeans and Tiriyó in the first half of the twentieth century produced ethnographic and linguistic studies of the region and Tiriyó subgroups in particular. After the ‘exploratory phase’ of contact came the ‘<a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionary</a> phase,’ wherein newly-built airstrips facilitated contact between missionaries and the Tiriyó.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> These missions tried to concentrate the Tiriyó population in larger villages to more easily convert them to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, and over time, other Indigenous groups such as the Akuriyó joined them here.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Today, the Tiriyó have a high degree of independence because their settlements are difficult to access. However, they are interested in reinforcing relationships with the foreign world.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tiriyó has been classified as belonging to the Taranoan group of the Guianan sub-branch of Cariban, together with <a href="/wiki/Karihona_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Karihona language">Karihona</a> (Carijona), in Colombia, and <a href="/wiki/Akuriy%C3%B3_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Akuriyó language">Akuriyó</a>, in Suriname, the former with a few, and the latter with apparently no, speakers left. Gildea (2012) lists <i>Tiriyó</i> and <i>Trió</i> as distinct languages.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_Research">Linguistic Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Linguistic Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first wordlist of Tiriyó was compiled by <a href="/wiki/Jules_Crevaux" title="Jules Crevaux">Jules Crevaux</a> in 1882, consisting of 31 entries including two sentences in Ndyuka-Tiriyó, a pidgin language.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In 1909, Claudius Henricus De Goeje wrote a short grammar of Tiriyó alongside a longer wordlist of around 500 entries that he had published previously in 1904.<sup id="cite_ref-Carlin_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carlin-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In-depth linguistic studies of Tiriyó were not written until later in the 20th century, when Ernest Migliazza published an investigation of the <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonology</a> of Tiriyó in 1965, as did Morgan Jones in 1972.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The two dialects of Tiriyó were first described in that work by Jones. A short morphological study by Ruth Wallace was published in 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sergio_Meira" class="mw-redirect" title="Sergio Meira">Sergio Meira</a> has conducted a great deal of research into Tiriyó, including in 1997, 1998, 1999, 2000, 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-Carlin_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carlin-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> His descriptive grammar of Tiriyó (1999) was the first major text on the language, and describes aspects of Tiriyó’s phonology, <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">morphology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">semantics</a>. It also provides a list of words commonly borrowed into Tiriyó, and a preliminary English-Tiriyó dictionary.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Eithne Carlin has also written a descriptive grammar of Tiriyó, that focuses on Tiriyó as spoken by people in Suriname.<sup id="cite_ref-Carlin_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carlin-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Carlin has also published other works about Tiriyó (Carlin 1997, 1999, 2003, 2006, 2011), primarily concerned with semantics and <a href="/wiki/Sociolinguistics" title="Sociolinguistics">sociolinguistics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Documentation">Documentation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Documentation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tiriyó has been partially documented as part of Meira’s research with the <a href="/wiki/Leiden_University" title="Leiden University">Leiden University</a>, in conjunction with the <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Institute_for_Psycholinguistics" title="Max Planck Institute for Psycholinguistics">Max Planck Institute for Psycholinguistics</a>. This documentation began in 1993 under Dr. Spike Gildea's Northern Brazilian Cariban Languages Documentation Project, and continued through 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Meira’s documentation included specific focus on stress patterns, contrastive demonstratives, and locative postpositions.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> There have been relatively few ethnographic studies on the Tiriyó, with the exception of the works by missionary Protasio Frikel and English anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Peter_Rivi%C3%A8re" title="Peter Rivière">Peter Rivière</a>. Between the 1950s and 1970s, Frikel wrote seven works (Frikel 1957, 1958, 1960, 1961a,b, 1964, 1971, 1973) relating to the Tiriyó.<sup id="cite_ref-Carlin_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carlin-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Rivière has published a number of works (Rivière 1963, 1966, 1969, 1970, 1971, 1981a,b, 1984, 1987, 1988, 1994, 1995a,b, 2000) beginning in 1963, notably Marriage Among the Trio.<sup id="cite_ref-Carlin_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carlin-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In his writing, he addresses errors made by Frikel.<sup id="cite_ref-Carlin_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carlin-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">Dialects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dialects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There seem to be two main dialects in the Tiriyó-speaking area, called by Jones (1972) <i>Eastern</i> or <i>Tapanahoni basin</i>, and <i>Western</i> or <i>Sipaliwini basin</i> dialects, and by Meira (2000, to appear) <i>K-Tiriyó</i> and <i>H-Tiriyó</i>. The main difference thus far reported is phonological: the different realization of what were (historically) clusters involving /h/ and a stop (see Phonology section below). Grammatical and/or lexical differences may also exist, but the examples thus far produced are disputed.
</p><p>Demographically, H-Tiriyó is the most important dialect (~ 60% of the speakers). It is the dialect spoken in the village of Kwamalasamutu, Suriname, and in the villages along the Western Paru river (Tawainen or Missão Tiriós, Kaikui Tëpu, Santo Antônio) and also along the Marapi river (Kuxare, Yawa, etc.). K-Tiriyó is spoken in the villages along the Eastern Paru river (Mataware, and some people at Bonna) in Brazil, and in the villages of Tepoe and Paloemeu in Suriname.
</p><p>Tiriyo was also a basis of the <a href="/wiki/Ndyuka-Tiriy%C3%B3_Pidgin" title="Ndyuka-Tiriyó Pidgin">Ndyuka-Tiriyó Pidgin</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stress">Stress</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Stress">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tiriyó <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">stress</a> follows a rhythmic pattern of the kind Hayes (1995) calls <a href="/wiki/Iamb_(foot)" class="mw-redirect" title="Iamb (foot)">iambic</a>. Phonetically:
</p>
<ul><li>In (C)V-only words, every second syllable from the beginning of the word is stressed, except the final syllable, which is never stressed (<i>extrametric</i>).</li>
<li>A non-(C)V syllable anywhere in the word attracts stress (except in the always unstressed final position) and disturbs the pattern, forcing it to restart as if a new word had begun.</li>
<li>Bisyllabic words do not have obvious stress.</li></ul>

<p>Note that some words apparently follow the opposite - <i><a href="/wiki/Troche" title="Troche">trochaic</a></i> - pattern (e.g., <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/meekane/</span> above). For these words, an underlying sequence of identical vowels is proposed. Cognate words from related languages provide evidence for this analysis: compare the Tiriyó stem <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/eeka/</span> 'bite' with e.g. Waiwai, Katxuyana, Hixkaryana <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/eska/</span>, Panare <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ehka/</span>, Karihona <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/eseka/</span>, suggesting a historical process of syllable reduction with subsequent compensatory lengthening of the preceding vowel.
</p><p>Since stress depends only on the type and number of syllables, morphological processes that involve syllabic prefixes or suffixes affect stress:
</p>
<center><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/pakoro/</span> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[pa.ˈkoː.ɽo]</span> 'house' → <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ji-pakoro/</span> 'my house' <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ji.ˈpaː.ko.ɽo]</span></center>
<p>In Hayes' framework, one could argue that stress placement is based on pairs of syllables (<i>feet</i>) consisting of either two (C)V (<i>light</i>) or one non-(C)V (<i>heavy</i>) syllables, except for the last syllable, which is extrametric, i.e. never forms a foot. This would explain the lack of stress in bisyllabic words: an initial light syllable, left alone by the extrametricity of the final syllable, cannot form a foot by itself and remains unstressed.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduplication">Reduplication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Reduplication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Reduplication" title="Reduplication">Reduplication</a></i> in Tiriyó affects <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verbs</a> (regularly) and also <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">nouns</a> and <a href="/wiki/Adverbial" title="Adverbial">adverbials</a> (irregularly: not all of them). On verbs, it usually marks iteration or repetition (e.g.: <i>wïtëe</i> 'I go, I am going', <i>wïtë-wïtëe</i> 'I keep going, I always go, I go again and again'); on nouns and adverbials, several examples of an entity, or several instances of a phenomenon (e.g.: <i>kutuma</i> 'painful', <i>kuu-kuutuma</i> 'painful all over, feeling pain all over one's body'; <i>sikinman</i> '(something) black', <i>siki-sikiman-ton</i> 'a number of black things' (including also the plural marker <i>-ton</i>; see below).
</p><p>Formally, there are two reduplicative patterns, termed <i>internal</i> and <i>external</i> reduplication. External reduplication is a regular process that copies the first two moras of a complete word (i.e., the first two syllables if they are light, or the first syllable if it is heavy). Coda consonants are not reduplicated: the preceding vowel is copied as long (i.e. as a VV sequence). If a syllable contains two vowels, some (older?) speakers copy both vowels, while other (younger?) speakers copy only the first vowel and lengthen it (i.e. turn it into a VV sequence).
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphophonology">Morphophonology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Morphophonology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are two general morphophonological processes that have important effects on the shapes of Tiriyó morphemes: <i>syllable reduction</i> and <i>ablaut</i>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Syllable_reduction">Syllable reduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Syllable reduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i>Syllable reduction</i> is the process whereby the final syllable of certain morphemes (mostly stems, though also sometimes affixes) is changed depending on the shape of the following element. These morphemes will typically have:
</p>
<ul><li>a <i>full</i> or <i>CV grade</i>, in which the final syllable occurs in its full form;</li>
<li>three <i>reduced grades</i>:
<ul><li>a <i>coda</i> or <i>C grade</i>, in which the final syllable is reduced to a coda consonant (<i>n</i> if the syllable had a nasal onset, <i>h</i> otherwise);<br />if the reducing syllable is not nasal (NV):</li>
<li>a <i>length</i> or <i>VV grade</i>, in which the final syllable is dropped, and the preceding vowel is 'compensatorily lengthened' (becomes VV);</li>
<li>a <i>zero grade</i>, in which the final syllable is dropped without any changes on the preceding vowel.</li></ul></li></ul>
<p>The table below illustrates the various grades of the verb stems <i>pono(pï)</i> 'to tell O' and <i>ona(mï)</i> 'to bury, hide O'.
</p>

<p>The reducing syllable can be the final one (<i>pono(pï)</i> 'to tell O', <i>ona(mï)</i> 'to bury/hide O'), or the initial one (<i>(pï)tai</i> 'shoes', <i>mïta</i> 'mouth'). The full form occurs when the following material (affix, stem, clitic) has a consonant cluster, i.e. is CCV-initial (the first consonant resyllabifies as the coda of the reducing syllable), or then starts with <i>r</i>.
The reduced forms occur when this is not the case: the coda grade when a possible cluster - <i>mp</i>, <i>nt</i>, <i>nk</i>, <i>ns</i>, <i>hp</i>, <i>hk</i>, <i>ht</i> - results, and the length grade in the other cases (the zero grade for verb stems, when no clitics follow). Reducing syllables generally consist of a stop or nasal and the vowels <i>ï</i> or <i>u</i> (<i>pï</i>, <i>pu</i>, <i>tï</i>, <i>tu</i>..., <i>mï</i>, <i>mu</i>,...); <i>rï</i> and <i>ru</i> syllables can also reduce, but with some irregularities; <i>wï</i> syllables only reduce stem-initially (and apparently never have a coda grade).
</p><p>Historically, syllable reduction results from the weakening and loss of the high vowels <i>ï</i> and <i>u</i>, leading to the formation of consonant clusters, in which the first element typically 'debuccalizes' to a glottal element (<i>h</i> or <i><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ʔ</span></i>) and later disappears, causing (when possible) the compensatory lengthening of the preceding vowel (cf. Gildea 1995). Comparative evidence suggests that many, perhaps all, morpheme-internal clusters in the Cariban family were formed as a result of this process.
</p>
<center><i>...CV.<u>CV</u>.CV...</i> &gt; <i>...CV<u>C</u>.CV...</i> &gt; <i>...CV<u>h</u>.CV...</i> or <i>...CV<u><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">ʔ</span></u>.CV...</i> &gt; <i>...CV<u>V</u>.CV...</i></center>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ablaut">Ablaut</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Ablaut">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Tiriyó, as in most Cariban languages, there is a class of stems which has two forms in different morphosyntactic environments: a form which is <i>e</i>-initial (the <i>e</i>- or <i>front grade</i>) and a form which is <i>ë</i>-initial (the <i>ë</i>- or <i>back grade</i>). With nouns, for instance, the back grade occurs with the inclusive (1+2) prefix <i>k-</i>, the third-person coreferential ('reflexive') prefix <i>t-</i>, and with the non-possessed form (prefixless); all other person-marked forms have the front grade.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">Morphology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Morphology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tiriyó morphology is in most respects typical of the Cariban family. It is neither highly <a href="/wiki/Polysynthetic_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Polysynthetic languages">polysynthetic</a> nor highly isolating. Tiriyó exhibits many forms of nominalization that distinguish between potential and actual <a href="/wiki/Agent_(Grammar)" class="mw-redirect" title="Agent (Grammar)">Agent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">Subject</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Object_(Grammar)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Object (Grammar) (page does not exist)">Object</a> as well as Circumstance and Event nominalizers. It marks for <a href="/wiki/Possession_(linguistics)" title="Possession (linguistics)">possession</a>, including past possession. Verbs also have derivational morphology. They mark for past, present, and future tense, as well as for certainty, doubt, and non-factual, hypothetical, incredulative, and admonative statements. Imperatives may also be conjugated as a <a href="/wiki/Hortative" title="Hortative">hortative</a>. Tiriyó has a wide variety of adverbial forms, and a variety of postpositions including directional, locative, perlative, relational, and experiencer. These mark for person and number. Interrogatives in Tiriyó consist of nominal, non-spatial adverbial, and spatial adverbial interrogatives.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Tiriyó is the only known Cariban language where almost all <a href="/wiki/Interrogative_word" title="Interrogative word">interrogatives</a> begin with the letter ‘a’, similar to ‘wh-words’ in English. The only exceptions, ‘eeke’ and ‘eekanmao’ (‘how’ and ‘when’, respectively) come from an earlier ‘aeke’.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> They are also the only words to be affected by the ‘_hpe’ particle, an <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)#Indefinite_article" title="Article (grammar)">indefinite</a>. ‘Akɨ’ and ‘atɨ’ have the same <a href="/wiki/Animacy" title="Animacy">animacy</a> distinction as certain pronouns; ‘Akɨ’ is similar to the English ‘who,’ but is used to ask about any animate being. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Possession">Possession</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Possession">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Possession in Tiriyó is denoted by the addition of a prefix that expresses the person of the possessor and a suffix that indicates possession to the stem of the noun being possessed. This suffix takes one of three forms: -ri, -hpɛ, or –ø.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Nouns in Tiriyó, like in all languages, can be classified according to possessibility. Some nouns may not be possessed, others must always be. These conditions exist along a spectrum, where the majority of nouns are optionally possessible.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Nouns that are never possessed include pronouns, proper nouns, human groups, animal names, and some <a href="/wiki/Nominalization" title="Nominalization">nominalizations</a>. These nominalizations are: “potential” Agents, Objects, and Subjects; generic infinitives; and adverbial nominalizers.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
This means that to indicate possession of an animal one must use indirect possession, where the inflection is not applied to the animal name, but to a generic noun.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Syntax">Syntax</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Syntax">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Case_and_Agreement">Case and Agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Case and Agreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Tiriyó belongs to the Cariban language family, whose <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a> is the least understood out of all its grammatical aspects. The <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">case marking patterns</a> of Tiriyó are no exception to this, as they vary considerably and “almost every possible combination of participants is instantiated in some construction”—the best way to describe the language is thus to say that Tiriyo is a complicated ‘split-participant’ system.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ergative_Patterns">Ergative Patterns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Ergative Patterns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

When the remote past form of a verb is used, the subject of a transitive clause is marked with the postposition _:ja; the subjects of intransitive clauses and objects of transitive sentences are both unmarked.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The first example below shows the marking of the transitive subject and the second shows the lack of marking of an intransitive subject:

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_Patterns">Other Patterns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Other Patterns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>According to Sergio Meira, two other forms of case agreement exist in the language. ‘Split-S systems’, where the subjects of intransitive verbs are sometimes marked the same way as the subjects of transitive ones, but sometimes are marked with objects instead, exist. Tripartite constructions, where subjects of transitive sentences, subjects of intransitive sentences, and objects are all marked differently, also exist in Tiriyó. Certain tenses even have more than one pattern at a time; one hypothesis to explain these variations is that the language’s case marking patterns are “fossil remnants of older constructions”.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In other words, the different constructions within each pattern are linked because of the history of the language, not because of their meaning.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tense">Tense</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Tense">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Verbs in Tiriyó distinguish between factual and non-factual <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_mood" title="Grammatical mood">moods</a>. The non-factual mood contains hypotheticals, incredulatives, and admonitives. The factual mood contains past, present, and future events, but does not imply that the speaker is necessarily certain that an event will occur or has occurred.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The tenses of Tiriyó, past; present; and future, have both <a href="/wiki/Perfect_(grammar)" title="Perfect (grammar)">perfect</a> and <a href="/wiki/Imperfect" title="Imperfect">imperfect</a> forms. Non-past tenses (present and future) distinguish between certainty and doubt on the part of the speaker.
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Certainty_and_Doubt">Certainty and Doubt</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Certainty and Doubt">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In present and future tenses, Tiriyó distinguish between things the speaker is certain of and things they are not. This distinction, represented as suffixes –e for certainty and –ne or –nɛ for doubt, is not present in collective forms.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> To use the certain form, a speaker must have absolute confidence in an event. For example, if there are rainclouds in the sky visible to both speaker and addressee and the speaker would like to say that it will rain, they must use the doubt form.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>It is important to note that the certainty and doubt forms do not express the source of the information; that is to say, they are not evidentials. They communicate how confident a speaker is in their assessment of a situation.<sup id="cite_ref-Meira_3-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Meira-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><br />
