<p><b>Indo-Surinamese</b> or <b>Indian-Surinamese</b>, are nationals of <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> with ancestry from <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>. They are mostly descendants of the <a href="/wiki/Indentured_servitude" title="Indentured servitude">indentured workers</a> brought by the Dutch and the British during the 19th-century. Per the 2012 Census of Suriname, 148,443 citizens of Suriname are of Indo-Surinamese origin,  constituting 27.4% of the total population, making them the largest ethnic group in Suriname.<sup id="cite_ref-Census_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Census-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indo-Surinamese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Indo-Surinamese are also known locally by the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> term  <i>Hindoestanen</i> (<small>Dutch pronunciation:&#32;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Dutch" title="Help:IPA/Dutch">[ˌɦɪnduˈstaːnə(n)]</a></span>), derived from the word <i><a href="/wiki/Hindustan#People" title="Hindustan">Hindustani</a></i>, lit., "someone from <a href="/wiki/Hindustan" title="Hindustan">Hindustan</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Hence, when Indians migrated to Suriname they were referred to as Hindustanis, people of Indian origin. They were also known as Girmiyas<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Naming_conventions_(geographic_names)" title="Wikipedia:Naming conventions (geographic names)"><span title="The geographic scope near this tag is ambiguous. (January 2018)">where?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, a term referring to the Agreements that the labourers had to sign regarding the work and the period of stay, and meaning "Someone with an Agreement."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indo-Surinamese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_60008924_Een_groep_Brits-Indische_immigranten.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_60008924_Een_groep_Brits-Indische_immigranten.jpg/220px-Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_60008924_Een_groep_Brits-Indische_immigranten.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="172" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_60008924_Een_groep_Brits-Indische_immigranten.jpg/330px-Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_60008924_Een_groep_Brits-Indische_immigranten.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_60008924_Een_groep_Brits-Indische_immigranten.jpg/440px-Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_60008924_Een_groep_Brits-Indische_immigranten.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="548" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tropenmuseum_Royal_Tropical_Institute_Objectnumber_60008924_Een_groep_Brits-Indische_immigranten.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Indian indentured labourers</div></div></div>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British Raj</a>, many Indians were sent to other British colonies for work. After the abolition of slavery in the Dutch colony of Suriname, the Dutch government signed <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Dutch_Treaties_of_1870%E2%80%9371" title="Anglo-Dutch Treaties of 1870–71">a treaty with the United Kingdom on the recruitment of contract workers</a>. Indians began migrating to Suriname in 1873 from what was then <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British India</a> as <a href="/wiki/Indenture" title="Indenture">indentured</a> labourers, mostly from the modern-day Indian states of <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bihar" title="Bihar">Bihar</a> and the surrounding regions.
</p><p>The first ship transporting Indian indentured labourers, the <i>Lalla Rookh</i>, arrived in the Paramaribo. Newly freed slaves in Suriname who witnessed Indian workers disembarking at the harbour, reportedly stated, "<i>Jobo tanbasi</i>", meaning "The white man is still the boss", suggesting that they viewed the development as a continuation of the slave trade. Initially, the transport and living conditions of Indian labourers in Suriname was worse than it had been prior to the abolition of the Dutch slave trade. The British Viceroy of India described it as "a new system of slavery". In 1870s, conditions were improved greatly following the passage of new legislation to protect the Indian workers. The Government of the United Kingdom and the colonial British Government in India feared comparisons to slavery would hurt their reputation, and enacted several legislations to make transportation of Indian workers safer and improve working conditions in plantations. The Dutch government, which had signed the agreement to recruit workers with the British after long and difficult negotiations, also feared jeopardizing the arrangement and meticulously followed the regulations imposed by the British. The Dutch were also concerned that they would be accused of reviving the slave trade.<sup id="cite_ref-Emmer_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emmer-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In order to reduce the mortality rate among workers being transported from India, the colonial British government required the presence of at least one doctor on every ship. As regulations required the doctor to be of European-origin, the regulations also required that one Indian indentured labourer be appointed as a translator and that he would be paid for his services at the end of the journey. Other regulations mandated that every ship have distilling apparatus with a capacity to produce at least 500 litres of drinking water from seawater daily, and also required ships to have a sickbay, male and female nursing staff, adequate food and medicine, and artificial ventilation in the passengers' quarters. Another regulation prohibited any ship transporting Indian indentured labourers from setting sail between the end of March and the beginning of August. Any shipping company that violated the regulations would be prohibited from transporting contact workers in the future. While the mortality rate among slaves working on plantations between 1680 and 1807 averaged 50.9 per thousand people, following the passage of the regulations post-1873, it dropped to 7.1 per thousand among Indian workers.<sup id="cite_ref-Emmer_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emmer-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Indo-Surinamese made up 37.6% of the population in the 1972 Census.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Just before and following the independence of Suriname on 25 November 1975, many Indo-Surinamese emigrated to the Netherlands, resulting in a decline in the population of the Indian community in Suriname.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indo-Surinamese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The majority religion among the Indo-Surinamese is <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, practiced by 78% of the people, followed by <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> (13%), <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> (7%), and <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">Jainism</a>. Among the <a href="/wiki/Hindu" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindu">Hindus</a> about 63% follow orthodox, traditional <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a> that they call <a href="/wiki/San%C4%81tan%C4%AB" title="Sanātanī">Sanātanī</a> to differentiate themselves from the 15% who belong to the reform movement <a href="/wiki/Arya_Samaj" title="Arya Samaj">Arya Samaj</a>, started by <a href="/wiki/Dayananda_Saraswati" title="Dayananda Saraswati">Dayananda Saraswati</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Among the Indo-Surinamese <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslims</a>, 60% follow <a href="/wiki/Sunni_Islam" title="Sunni Islam">Sunni Islam</a> while 40% identify as Ahmadiyya, of either the <a href="/wiki/Lahore_Ahmadiyya_Movement_for_the_Propagation_of_Islam" title="Lahore Ahmadiyya Movement for the Propagation of Islam">Lahore Ahmadiyya Movement for the Propagation of Islam</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Ahmadiyya" title="Ahmadiyya">Ahmadiyya</a> community.
</p>
