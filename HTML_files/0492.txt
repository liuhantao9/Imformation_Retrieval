	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Women's One Day International</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>
<b>Women's One Day International</b> (<b>WODI</b>) is the <a href="/wiki/Limited_overs_cricket" title="Limited overs cricket">limited overs</a> form of <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_cricket" title="Women&#39;s cricket">women's cricket</a>. Matches are scheduled for 50 <a href="/wiki/Over_(cricket)" title="Over (cricket)">overs</a>, equivalent to the <a href="/wiki/One_Day_International" title="One Day International">men's game</a>. The first women's ODIs were played in 1973, as part of the first <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_Cricket_World_Cup" title="Women&#39;s Cricket World Cup">Women's World Cup</a> which was held in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. The first ODI saw the hosts beat an <a href="/wiki/International_XI_women%27s_cricket_team" title="International XI women&#39;s cricket team">International XI</a>. The 1,000th women's ODI took place between <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_women%27s_cricket_team_in_South_Africa_in_2016%E2%80%9317" title="New Zealand women&#39;s cricket team in South Africa in 2016–17">South Africa and New Zealand</a> on 13 October 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-thousand_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thousand-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Involved_nations">Involved nations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Women%27s_One_Day_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Involved nations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2006, the <a href="/wiki/International_Cricket_Council" title="International Cricket Council">ICC</a> announced that only the top-10 ranked sides would have Test and ODI status. During the <a href="/wiki/2011_Women%27s_Cricket_World_Cup_Qualifier" title="2011 Women&#39;s Cricket World Cup Qualifier">2011 Women's Cricket World Cup Qualifier</a> Netherlands lost its One Day status by virtue of not finishing in the top 6 placings. As the top 4 teams with One Day status were not required to take part in this qualifying tournament, the top 6 in this tournament constituted the top 10 overall placings. Bangladesh replaced the Netherlands as one of the ten countries which currently have One Day status.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Countries which currently have One Day status are:

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rankings">Rankings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Women%27s_One_Day_International&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Rankings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before October 2018, ICC did not maintain a separate Twenty20 ranking for the women's game, instead aggregating performance over all three forms of the game into one overall women's teams ranking.<sup id="cite_ref-ICC_womens_ranking_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ICC_womens_ranking-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In January 2018, ICC granted international status to all matches between associate nations and announced plan to launch separate T20I rankings for women.<sup id="cite_ref-T20Istatus_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-T20Istatus-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In October 2018 the T20I rankings were launched with separate ODI rankings for Full Members.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
