<p><b>Lady Justice</b> (Latin: <i>Iustitia</i>) is an <a href="/wiki/Allegory" title="Allegory">allegorical</a> <a href="/wiki/Personification" title="Personification">personification</a> of the moral force in judicial systems.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Her attributes are a blindfold, a balance, and a sword. She often appears as a pair with <a href="/wiki/Prudentia" class="mw-redirect" title="Prudentia">Prudentia</a>, who holds a mirror and a snake.  
</p><p>Lady Justice originates from the personification of Justice in Ancient Roman art known as <b>Iustitia</b> or <b>Justitia</b> after <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">Iustitia</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-treccani.it_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-treccani.it-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> who is equivalent to the Greek goddesses <a href="/wiki/Themis" title="Themis">Themis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dike_(mythology)" title="Dike (mythology)">Dike</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_goddess_Iustitia">The goddess Iustitia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lady_Justice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The goddess Iustitia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origin of Lady Justice was Iustitia, the goddess of <a href="/wiki/Justice" title="Justice">Justice</a> within <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">Roman mythology</a>. Iustitia was introduced by emperor <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a>, and was thus not a very old deity in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_pantheon" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman pantheon">Roman pantheon</a>. 
</p><p>Justice was one of the virtues celebrated by emperor <a href="/wiki/Augustus" title="Augustus">Augustus</a> in his <i><a href="/wiki/Clipeus_virtutis" class="mw-redirect" title="Clipeus virtutis">clipeus virtutis</a></i>, and a <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Iustitia" class="mw-redirect" title="Temple of Iustitia">temple of Iustitia</a> was established in Rome on 8 January 13 BC by emperor <a href="/wiki/Tiberius" title="Tiberius">Tiberius</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-treccani.it_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-treccani.it-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  Iustitia became a symbol for the virtue of justice with which every emperor wished to associate his regime; emperor <a href="/wiki/Vespasian" title="Vespasian">Vespasian</a> minted coins with the image of the goddess seated on a throne called <i>Iustitia Augusta</i>, and many emperors after him used the image of the goddess to proclaim themselves protectors of justice.<sup id="cite_ref-treccani.it_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-treccani.it-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  
</p><p>Though formally called a goddess with her own temple and cult shrine in Rome, it appears that she was from the onset viewed more as an artistic symbolic personification rather than as an actual deity with religious significance.
</p>

<p>The personification of justice balancing the <a href="/wiki/Balance_scales" class="mw-redirect" title="Balance scales">scales</a> dates back to the goddess <a href="/wiki/Maat" title="Maat">Maat</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and later <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a>, of ancient Egypt. The <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Hellenic</a> deities <a href="/wiki/Themis" title="Themis">Themis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dike_(mythology)" title="Dike (mythology)">Dike</a> were later goddesses of justice. <a href="/wiki/Themis" title="Themis">Themis</a> was the embodiment of divine order, law, and custom, in her aspect as the personification of the divine rightness of law.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scales">Scales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lady_Justice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lady Justice is most often depicted with a set of scales typically suspended from one hand, upon which she measures the strengths of a case's support and opposition.
</p><p>The Greek goddess Dike is depicted holding a set of scales. 
</p><p>Bacchylides, Fragment 5 (trans. Campbell, Vol. <i>Greek Lyric IV</i>) (Greek lyric c. 5th B.C.):
</p>
<blockquote><p>If some god had been holding level the balance of Dike (Justice).</p></blockquote>
<p>The scales represent the weighing of evidence, and the scales lack a foundation in order to signify that evidence should stand on its own.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blindfold">Blindfold</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lady_Justice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Blindfold">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lady_Justice_at_Castallania,_Malta.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Lady_Justice_at_Castallania%2C_Malta.jpeg/220px-Lady_Justice_at_Castallania%2C_Malta.jpeg" decoding="async" width="220" height="295" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Lady_Justice_at_Castallania%2C_Malta.jpeg/330px-Lady_Justice_at_Castallania%2C_Malta.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Lady_Justice_at_Castallania%2C_Malta.jpeg/440px-Lady_Justice_at_Castallania%2C_Malta.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="1936" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lady_Justice_at_Castallania,_Malta.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>18th-century Lady Justice at the <a href="/wiki/Castellania_(Valletta)" title="Castellania (Valletta)">Castellania</a></div></div></div>
<p>Since the 16th century, Lady Justice has often been depicted wearing a blindfold. The blindfold represents <a href="/wiki/Impartiality" title="Impartiality">impartiality</a>, the ideal that justice should be applied without regard to wealth, power, or other status. The earliest Roman coins d as "blind" since the middle of the 16th century. The first known representation of blind Justice is <a href="/wiki/Hans_Gieng" title="Hans Gieng">Hans Gieng</a>'s 1543 statue on the <i><a href="/wiki/Gerechtigkeitsbrunnen_(Berne)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gerechtigkeitsbrunnen (Berne)">Gerechtigkeitsbrunnen</a></i> (Fountain of Justice) in <a href="/wiki/Berne" class="mw-redirect" title="Berne">Berne</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Instead of using the <a href="/wiki/Janus" title="Janus">Janus</a> approach, many sculptures simply leave out the blindfold altogether.  For example, atop the <a href="/wiki/Old_Bailey" title="Old Bailey">Old Bailey</a> courthouse in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>, a statue of Lady Justice stands without a blindfold;<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> the courthouse brochures explain that this is because Lady Justice was originally not blindfolded, and because her "maidenly form" is supposed to guarantee her impartiality which renders the blindfold redundant.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  Another variation is to depict a blindfolded Lady Justice as a human scale, weighing competing claims in each hand. An example of this can be seen at the Shelby County Courthouse in <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis, Tennessee</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sword">Sword</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lady_Justice&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sword">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The sword represented authority in ancient times, and conveys the idea that justice can be swift and final.<sup id="cite_ref-oureverydaylife_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oureverydaylife-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><br />
