<p>In general, a <b>rural area</b> or <b>countryside</b> is a geographic area that is located outside <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">towns</a> and <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">cities</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Health_Resources_and_Services_Administration" title="Health Resources and Services Administration">Health Resources and Services Administration</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services" title="United States Department of Health and Human Services">U.S. Department of Health and Human Services</a> defines the word <i>rural</i> as encompassing "...all population, housing, and territory not included within an <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban area</a>. Whatever is not urban is considered rural."<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Typical rural areas have a low <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> and small settlements. <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">Agricultural</a> areas are commonly rural, as are other types of areas such as <a href="/wiki/Forest" title="Forest">forest</a>. Different countries have varying definitions of <i>rural</i> for statistical and administrative purposes.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rural_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rural_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Canada, the <a href="/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development">Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development</a> defines a "predominantly rural region" as having more than 50% of the population living in rural communities where a "<a href="/wiki/Rural_community" class="mw-redirect" title="Rural community">rural community</a>" has a population density less than 150 people per square kilometre. In Canada, the census division has been used to represent "regions" and census consolidated sub-divisions have been used to represent "communities". Intermediate regions have 15 to 49 percent of their population living in a rural community. Predominantly <a href="/wiki/Urban_region" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban region">urban regions</a> have less than 15 percent of their population living in a rural community. Predominantly rural regions are classified as rural metro-adjacent, rural non-metro-adjacent and rural northern, following Ehrensaft and Beeman (1992). Rural metro-adjacent regions are predominantly rural <a href="/wiki/Census_division" title="Census division">census divisions</a> which are adjacent to metropolitan centres while rural non-metro-adjacent regions are those predominantly rural census divisions which are not adjacent to metropolitan centres. Rural northern regions are predominantly rural census divisions that are found either entirely or mostly above the following lines of parallel in each province: <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">Newfoundland and Labrador</a>, 50th; <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> 54th; <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, 54th; <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a>, 53rd; <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, 54th. As well, rural northern regions encompass all of the <a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">Yukon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">Northwest Territories</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nunavut" title="Nunavut">Nunavut</a>.
</p><p>Statistics Canada defines rural for their population counts. This definition has changed over time (see Appendix A in du Plessis et al., 2002). Typically, it has referred to the population living outside settlements of 1,000 or fewer inhabitants. The current definition states that census rural is the population outside settlements with fewer than 1,000 inhabitants and a population density below 400 people per square kilometre (Statistics Canada, 2007).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rural_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rural_areas_in_the_United_States" title="Rural areas in the United States">Rural areas in the United States</a></div>
<p>84% of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>' inhabitants live in <a href="/wiki/Suburban" class="mw-redirect" title="Suburban">suburban</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban areas</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> but cities occupy only 10 percent of the country. Rural areas (villages) occupy the remaining 90 percent. The <a href="/wiki/U.S._Census_Bureau" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Census Bureau">U.S. Census Bureau</a>, the <a href="/wiki/USDA" class="mw-redirect" title="USDA">USDA</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Economic_Research_Service" title="Economic Research Service">Economic Research Service</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Management_and_Budget" title="Office of Management and Budget">Office of Management and Budget</a> (OMB) have come together to help define rural areas. United States Census Bureau: The Census Bureau definitions (new to the 2000 census), which are based on population density, defines rural areas as all territory outside Census Bureau-defined urbanized areas and urban clusters. 
</p>
<ul><li>An urbanized area consists of a central surrounding areas whose population ("urban nucleus") is greater than 50,000. They may or may not contain individual cities with 50,000 or more; rather, they must have a core with a population density generally exceeding 1,000 persons per square mile; and may contain adjoining territory with at least 500 persons per square mile (other towns outside an urbanized area whose population exceeds 2,500).</li>
<li>Thus, rural areas comprise open country and settlements with fewer than 2,500 residents; areas designated as rural can have population densities as high as 999 per square mile or as low as 1 person per square mile.<sup id="cite_ref-ncseonline.org_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncseonline.org-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p><b>USDA</b>
</p>
<ul><li>The USDA's <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Rural_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="Office of Rural Development">Office of Rural Development</a> may define rural by various population thresholds. The <a href="/wiki/2002_farm_bill" class="mw-redirect" title="2002 farm bill">2002 farm bill</a> (P.L. 107-171, Sec. 6020) defined rural and rural area as any area other than (1) a city or town that has a population of greater than 50,000 inhabitants, and (2) the urbanized areas contiguous and adjacent to such a city or town.</li>
<li>The rural-urban continuum codes, urban influence code, and rural county typology codes developed by USDA's <a href="/wiki/Economic_Research_Service" title="Economic Research Service">Economic Research Service</a> (ERS) allow researchers to break out the standard metropolitan and non-metropolitan areas into smaller residential groups.<sup id="cite_ref-ncseonline.org_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncseonline.org-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> For example, a metropolitan county is one that contains an urbanized area, or one that has a twenty-five percent commuter rate to an urbanized area regardless of population.</li></ul>
<p><b>OMB</b>:
</p><p>Under the Core Based Statistical Areas used by the OMB (commonly referred to as 'CBSA Codes'),<sup id="cite_ref-GreatData.com_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GreatData.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<ul><li>a metropolitan county, or <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Statistical_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Metropolitan Statistical Area">Metropolitan Statistical Area</a>,(MSA) consists of (1) central counties with one or more urbanized areas (as defined by the <a href="/wiki/Census_Bureau" class="mw-redirect" title="Census Bureau">Census Bureau</a>) and (2) outlying counties that are economically tied to the core counties as measured by worker commuting data (i.e. if 25% of workers living there commute to the core counties, or if 25% of the employment in the county consists of workers coming from the central counties).</li>
<li>Non-metro counties are outside the boundaries of metro areas and are further subdivided into <a href="/wiki/Micropolitan_Statistical_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Micropolitan Statistical Area">Micropolitan Statistical Areas</a> centered on urban clusters of 10,000–50,000 residents, and all remaining non-core counties.<sup id="cite_ref-ncseonline.org_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ncseonline.org-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>In 2014, the USDA updated their rural / non-rural area definitions based on the 2010 Census counts.<sup id="cite_ref-GreatData.com_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GreatData.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rural_schools">Rural schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rural_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rural schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/National_Center_for_Education_Statistics" title="National Center for Education Statistics">National Center for Education Statistics</a> (NCES) revised its definition of rural schools in 2006 after working with the Census Bureau to create a new locale classification system to capitalize on improved geocoding technology.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rural_health">Rural health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rural_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Rural health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rural_health" title="Rural health">Rural health</a> definitions can be different for establishing under-served areas or health care accessibility in rural areas of the United States. According to the handbook, Definitions of Rural: A Handbook for Health Policy Makers and Researchers, "Residents of metropolitan counties are generally thought to have easy access to the relatively concentrated health services of the county's central areas. However, some metropolitan counties are so large that they contain small towns and rural, sparsely populated areas that are isolated from these central clusters and their corresponding health services by physical barriers." To address this type of rural area, "Harold Goldsmith, Dena Puskin, and Dianne Stiles (1992) described a methodology to identify small towns and rural areas within large metropolitan counties (LMCs) that were isolated from central areas by distance or other physical features." This became the Goldsmith Modification definition of rural. "Bhoomeet rural education The Goldsmith Modification has been useful for expanding the eligibility for federal programs that assist rural populations—to include the isolated rural populations of large metropolitan counties."
</p><p>Health care delivery in rural areas of the United States can be challenging. From 2005-2011, the rate of potentially preventable hospitalizations for acute conditions was highest in rural areas (as compared to large metropolitan, small metropolitan, or micropolitan areas).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rural_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rural_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Brazil, there are different notions of <i>rural area</i> and <i>countryside</i>. Rural areas are any place outside a municipality's urban development (buildings, streets) and it's carried by informal usage. Otherwise, countryside (<i>interior</i> in <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>) are officially defined as all municipalities outside the state/territory capital's metropolitan region. Some states as <a href="/wiki/Mato_Grosso_do_Sul" title="Mato Grosso do Sul">Mato Grosso do Sul</a> doesn't have any metropolitan region, thus all of the state, except its capital is officially <i>countryside</i>. <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro_(state)" title="Rio de Janeiro (state)">Rio de Janeiro</a> is singular in Brazil and it's de facto a <i>metropolitan state</i>, as circa 70% of its population are located in <a href="/wiki/Greater_Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Greater Rio de Janeiro">Greater Rio</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Federal_District_(Brazil)" title="Federal District (Brazil)">Federal District</a> it's not applicable and there's no countryside as all of it is treated as the federal capital. Brasília is <i>nominally</i> the capital, but the <i>capitality</i> is shared through all Federal District, because Brazil de facto defines its capital as a municipality, and in municipal matters, the Federal District is treated and governs as a single municipality, <a href="/wiki/City-state" title="City-state">city-state</a>-like (Brasília, DF).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rural_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rural_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="box-Tone plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-Tone" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png" decoding="async" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/60px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/80px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="48" data-file-height="48" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section's <b>tone or style may not reflect the <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Writing_better_articles#Tone" title="Wikipedia:Writing better articles">encyclopedic tone</a> used on Wikipedia</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> See Wikipedia's <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Writing_better_articles#Tone" title="Wikipedia:Writing better articles">guide to writing better articles</a> for suggestions.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">November 2018</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The country is divided into 402 administrative districts: 295 <a href="/wiki/List_of_rural_districts_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="List of rural districts of Germany">rural districts</a> and 107 urban districts. Germany is among the largest agricultural producers in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. More than half of Germany's territory – <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20120609065635/http://www.bmelv.de/SharedDocs/Standardartikel/EN/Agriculture/FarminginGermany.html">almost 19 million hectares</a> – is used for farming, and are located in the rural areas. Almost 10% of employees in Germany has work linked directly or indirectly with the agricultural, forest and fisheries sectors; approximately a fifth of them are employed in primary production.  The implication is that, unlike in some other European countries, where rural areas are known for being backward when compared to urban areas, in Germany, the trend is changing. Due to the country's policy of equal living conditions, this is not the case in Germany. Rural areas receive nearly equivalent attention as the urban areas do. Also, through a special approach to rural development, usually referred to as Village Renewal, the challenges of rural Germany are taken care of.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rural_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Malham_countryside.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Malham_countryside.jpg/220px-Malham_countryside.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Malham_countryside.jpg/330px-Malham_countryside.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Malham_countryside.jpg/440px-Malham_countryside.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Malham_countryside.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A typical countryside scene in rural <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Dales" title="Yorkshire Dales">Yorkshire Dales</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>In Britain, "rural" is defined<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> by the government <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Environment,_Food_and_Rural_Affairs" title="Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs">Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs</a> (DEFRA), using population data from the latest <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2001" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Census 2001">United Kingdom Census 2001</a>. These definitions have various grades, but the upper point is any local government area with more than 26% of its population living in a rural settlement or <a href="/wiki/Market_town" title="Market town">market town</a> ("market town" being defined as any settlement which has permission to hold a <a href="/wiki/Street_market" class="mw-redirect" title="Street market">street market</a>). A number of measures are in place to protect the British countryside, including <a href="/wiki/Green_belt_(United_Kingdom)" title="Green belt (United Kingdom)">green belts</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rural_health_2">Rural health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rural_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Rural health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In England an <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">NHS</a> patient is defined as rural if they live more than 1 mile (1.6 km) from either a doctor or a <a href="/wiki/Dispensing_chemist" class="mw-redirect" title="Dispensing chemist">dispensing chemist</a>. In Scotland a different definition of rural is used. This is important for defining whether the patient is expected to collect their own medicines. While doctors' surgeries in towns will not have a medicines dispensary instead expecting patients to use a high-street chemist to purchase their prescription medicines (in Scotland collection not purchase, as prescriptions are paid by the state), in rural village surgeries,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> an NHS medicines dispensary will be built into the same building.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rural_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rural_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Perumacheri_AUP_School.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Perumacheri_AUP_School.JPG/220px-Perumacheri_AUP_School.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Perumacheri_AUP_School.JPG/330px-Perumacheri_AUP_School.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Perumacheri_AUP_School.JPG/440px-Perumacheri_AUP_School.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Perumacheri_AUP_School.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A rural school in <a href="/wiki/Kannur" title="Kannur">Kannur</a>, India.</div></div></div>
<p>Rural areas are also known as the 'countryside' or a '<a href="/wiki/Village" title="Village">village</a>' in India. It has a very low population density. In rural areas, <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a> is the chief source of livelihood along with <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cottage_industries" class="mw-redirect" title="Cottage industries">cottage industries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a> etc.
</p><p>The quest to discover the real rural India still continues in great earnest. Almost every economic agency today has a definition of rural India. Here are a few definitions:
According to the Planning Commission, a town with a maximum population of 15,000 is considered rural in nature. In these areas the <a href="/wiki/Panchayat_raj" class="mw-redirect" title="Panchayat raj">panchayat</a> makes all the decisions. There are five persons in the <a href="/wiki/Panchayati_raj" title="Panchayati raj">panchayat</a>.
The National Sample Survey Organisation (NSSO) defines ‘rural’ as follows:
</p>
<ul><li>An area with a population density of up to 400 per square kilometer,</li>
<li>Villages with clear surveyed boundaries but no municipal board,</li>
<li>A minimum of 75% of male working population involved in agriculture and allied activities.<sup id="cite_ref-dhanbank.com_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dhanbank.com-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>RBI defines rural areas as those areas with a population of less than 49,000 (tier -3 to tier-6 cities).<sup id="cite_ref-dhanbank.com_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dhanbank.com-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is generally said that the rural areas house up to 70% of India's population. Rural India contributes a large chunk to India's GDP by way of agriculture, self-employment, services, construction etc. As per a strict measure used by the National Sample Survey in its 63rd round, called monthly per capita expenditure, rural expenditure accounts for 55% of total national monthly expenditure. The rural population currently accounts for one-third of the total Indian FMCG sales.<sup id="cite_ref-dhanbank.com_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dhanbank.com-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rural_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Japan, rural areas are referred to as "Inaka" which translates literally to "the countryside" or "one's native village."<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pakistan">Pakistan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rural_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Pakistan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Pakistani_village_life" title="Pakistani village life">Pakistani village life</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Amra_Kalan_Village.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Amra_Kalan_Village.JPG/220px-Amra_Kalan_Village.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Amra_Kalan_Village.JPG/330px-Amra_Kalan_Village.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Amra_Kalan_Village.JPG/440px-Amra_Kalan_Village.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Amra_Kalan_Village.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Amra Kalan village in <a href="/wiki/Kharian" title="Kharian">Kharian</a>, Pakistan</div></div></div>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/2017_Census_of_Pakistan" title="2017 Census of Pakistan">2017 census</a> about 64% of Pakistanis live in rural areas. Most rural areas in Pakistan tend to be near cities, and are <a href="/wiki/Peri-urbanisation" title="Peri-urbanisation">peri-urban</a> areas. This is due to the definition of a rural area in Pakistan being an area that does not come within an <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban</a> boundary.<sup id="cite_ref-Rethinking_urban_and_rural_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rethinking_urban_and_rural-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The remote rural villagers of Pakistan commonly live in houses made of bricks, clay or mud. <a href="/wiki/Socioeconomic_status" title="Socioeconomic status">Socioeconomic status</a> among rural Pakistani villagers is often based upon the ownership of <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_land" title="Agricultural land">agricultural land</a>, which also may provide <a href="/wiki/Social_status" title="Social status">social prestige</a> in village cultures. The majority of rural Pakistani inhabitants livelihoods is based upon the rearing of livestock, which also comprises a significant part of Pakistan's gross domestic product. Some <a href="/wiki/Livestock_in_Pakistan" class="mw-redirect" title="Livestock in Pakistan">livestock raised by rural Pakistanis</a> include cattle and goats. However rural areas in Pakistan that are near cities are considered <a href="/wiki/Suburbanization" title="Suburbanization">suburban areas</a> or <a href="/wiki/Suburb" title="Suburb">suburbs</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Human_fertility">Human fertility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rural_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Human fertility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rural residence is a <a href="/wiki/Fertility_factor_(demography)" title="Fertility factor (demography)">fertility factor</a>, with total fertility rates and <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnancy</a> being higher among women in rural areas than among women in urban areas and the rural population is much younger than urban areas.<sup id="cite_ref-rai2013_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rai2013-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
