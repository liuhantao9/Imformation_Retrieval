<p><b>Kickboxing</b> is a group of <a href="/wiki/Stand-up_fighting" title="Stand-up fighting">stand-up</a> <a href="/wiki/Combat_sport" title="Combat sport">combat sports</a> based on <a href="/wiki/Kicking" class="mw-redirect" title="Kicking">kicking</a> and <a href="/wiki/Punch_(strike)" class="mw-redirect" title="Punch (strike)">punching</a>, historically developed from <a href="/wiki/Karate" title="Karate">karate</a> mixed with <a href="/wiki/Boxing" title="Boxing">boxing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Kickboxing is practiced for <a href="/wiki/Self-defence" class="mw-redirect" title="Self-defence">self-defence</a>, general <a href="/wiki/Physical_fitness" title="Physical fitness">fitness</a>, or as a <a href="/wiki/Contact_sport" title="Contact sport">contact sport</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Japanese kickboxing originated in the late 1950s, with competitions held since then.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-O68_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-O68-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> American kickboxing originated in the 1970s and was brought to prominence in September 1974, when the Professional Karate Association (<a href="/wiki/Professional_Karate_Association" title="Professional Karate Association">PKA</a>) held the first World Championships. Historically, kickboxing can be considered a <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_martial_art" class="mw-redirect" title="Hybrid martial art">hybrid martial art</a> formed from the combination of elements of various traditional styles. This approach became increasingly popular since the 1970s, and since the 1990s, kickboxing has contributed to the emergence of <a href="/wiki/Mixed_martial_arts" title="Mixed martial arts">mixed martial arts</a> via further hybridization with <a href="/wiki/Ground_fighting" title="Ground fighting">ground fighting</a> techniques from <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_jiu-jitsu" title="Brazilian jiu-jitsu">Brazilian jiu-jitsu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Folk_wrestling" title="Folk wrestling">folk wrestling</a>.
</p><p>There is no single international governing body. International governing bodies include International Combat Organisation, <a href="/wiki/World_Association_of_Kickboxing_Organizations" title="World Association of Kickboxing Organizations">World Association of Kickboxing Organizations</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Kickboxing_Association" title="World Kickboxing Association">World Kickboxing Association</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_Sport_Karate_Association" title="International Sport Karate Association">International Sport Karate Association</a>, <a href="/wiki/International_Kickboxing_Federation" title="International Kickboxing Federation">International Kickboxing Federation</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Kickboxing_Network" title="World Kickboxing Network">World Kickboxing Network</a>, among others. Consequently, there is no single kickboxing <a href="/wiki/World_championship" title="World championship">world championship</a>, and champion titles are issued by individual <a href="/wiki/Boxing_promoter" class="mw-redirect" title="Boxing promoter">promotions</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/K-1" title="K-1">K-1</a>, <a href="/wiki/Glory_(kickboxing)" title="Glory (kickboxing)">Glory</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kunlun_Fight" title="Kunlun Fight">Kunlun Fight</a> among others. Bouts organised under different governing bodies apply different rules, such as allowing the use of knees or clinching, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The term "kickboxing" <i>kickboxing</i><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">キックボクシング</span>)</span> can be used in a narrow and in a broad sense.
</p>
<ul><li>The narrow use is restricted to the styles that self-identify as kickboxing, i.e. Japanese kickboxing (with its spin-off styles or rules such as <a href="/wiki/Shoot_boxing" title="Shoot boxing">shoot boxing</a> and K-1), Dutch kickboxing, and American kickboxing.</li>
<li>In the wider sense, it includes all <a href="/wiki/Stand-up_fighting" title="Stand-up fighting">stand-up</a> combat sports that allow both punching and kicking, including <a href="/wiki/Muay_Thai" title="Muay Thai">Muay Thai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kun_Khmer" class="mw-redirect" title="Kun Khmer">Kun Khmer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Savate" title="Savate">Savate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adithada" title="Adithada">Adithada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lethwei" title="Lethwei">Lethwei</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sanshou" title="Sanshou">Sanda</a>, and certain styles of karate (especially <a href="/wiki/Full_contact_karate" title="Full contact karate">full contact karate</a>).</li></ul>
<p>The term  itself was introduced in the 1960s as a <a href="/wiki/Wasei-eigo" title="Wasei-eigo">Japanese anglicism</a> by Japanese boxing promoter <a href="/wiki/Osamu_Noguchi" title="Osamu Noguchi">Osamu Noguchi</a> for a <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_martial_art" class="mw-redirect" title="Hybrid martial art">hybrid martial art</a> combining Muay Thai and karate which he had introduced in 1958. The term was later also adopted by the American variant. Since there has been a lot of cross-fertilization between these styles, with many practitioners training or competing under the rules of more than one style, the history of the individual styles cannot be seen in isolation from one another.
</p><p>The French term <i>Boxe pieds-poings</i> (literally "feet-fists-boxing") is also used in the sense of "kickboxing" in the general meaning, including French boxing (<a href="/wiki/Savate" title="Savate">Savate</a>) as well as American, Dutch and Japanese kickboxing, Burmese and Thai boxing, any style of <a href="/wiki/Full_contact_karate" title="Full contact karate">full contact karate</a>, etc.
</p><p>Arts labeled as <i>kickboxing</i> in the general sense include:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Indochinese_martial_arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Indochinese martial arts">Indochinese</a> family of kickboxing sports (also known generically as <i>muay</i><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>) including:<span id="Indochinese"></span>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Pradal_Serey" class="mw-redirect" title="Pradal Serey">Pradal Serey</a> – similar to <a href="/wiki/Muay_Thai" title="Muay Thai">Muay Thai</a> with an emphasis on elbow techniques (Ring-wise). Also known as Kun Khmer.</li>
<li>Thai <a href="/wiki/Muay_Boran" class="mw-redirect" title="Muay Boran">Muay Boran</a> (Ancient boxing) – Predecessor of Muay Thai, allows the use of headbutts.</li>
<li>Thai kickboxing or Muay Thai – the modern Thai martial art with strong emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Knee_(strike)" title="Knee (strike)">knee</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elbow_(strike)" title="Elbow (strike)">elbow strikes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Burmese <a href="/wiki/Lethwei" title="Lethwei">Lethwei</a>, a traditional <a href="/wiki/Burmese_martial_arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Burmese martial arts">Burmese martial art</a> of which has now grown into a popular kickboxing event with strong emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Knee_(strike)" title="Knee (strike)">knee</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elbow_(strike)" title="Elbow (strike)">elbow strikes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Headbutt" title="Headbutt">headbutt</a>. Any part of the body may be used to <a href="/wiki/Strike_(attack)" title="Strike (attack)">strike</a> and be struck. It is also known as <a href="/wiki/Bando" title="Bando">Bando</a> kickboxing.</li>
<li>Laotian <a href="/wiki/Muay_Lao" title="Muay Lao">Muay Lao</a> – Laotian boxing which is similar to Muay Thai</li>
<li>Filipino <a href="/wiki/Yaw-Yan" title="Yaw-Yan">Yaw-Yan</a> – Sayaw ng Kamatayan (Dance of Death) is the proper name for Yaw-Yan, a <a href="/wiki/Filipino_martial_arts" title="Filipino martial arts">Filipino martial art</a> developed by Napoleon Fernandez. The art resembles Muay Thai in a sense, but differs in the hip torquing motion as well as downward-cutting nature of its kicks and with strong emphasis on delivering attacks from long range.</li></ul></li>
<li>Indian <a href="/wiki/Musti_yuddha" class="mw-redirect" title="Musti yuddha">Musti yuddha</a> (also known as Muki boxing) and <a href="/wiki/Adithada" title="Adithada">Adithada</a>, a form of kickboxing that uses <a href="/wiki/Knee_(strike)" title="Knee (strike)">knee</a>, elbow and <a href="/wiki/Forehead" title="Forehead">forehead</a> strikes in <a href="/wiki/Southern_kalaripayattu" class="mw-redirect" title="Southern kalaripayattu">Southern kalaripayattu</a>.</li>
<li>French <a href="/wiki/Savate" title="Savate">Savate</a>, a historical sport which developed in the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> It is mostly known for its foot-kicking techniques.</li>
<li>Ukrainian <a href="/wiki/Combat_Hopak" title="Combat Hopak">Combat Hopak</a> is mostly built around punching and kicking techniques.</li>
<li>modern competition-oriented <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_martial_arts" title="Hybrid martial arts">hybrid martial arts</a> that developed in parallel with Japanese and American kickboxing:
<ul><li>Dutch Kickboxing — incorporate styles of Muay Thai, <a href="/wiki/Boxing" title="Boxing">Boxing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kyokushin" title="Kyokushin">Kyokushin</a> style of Karate.</li>
<li>Any style of <a href="/wiki/Full_contact_Karate" class="mw-redirect" title="Full contact Karate">Full contact Karate</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sanshou" title="Sanshou">Sanda</a> (Chinese Kickboxing ) – The applicable component of <a href="/wiki/Wushu_(term)" class="mw-redirect" title="Wushu (term)">wushu</a>/<a href="/wiki/Kung_fu" class="mw-redirect" title="Kung fu">kung fu</a> of which <a href="/wiki/Takedown_(grappling)" title="Takedown (grappling)">takedowns</a> and throws are legal in competition as well as all other sorts of striking (use of arms and legs).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Shoot_boxing" title="Shoot boxing">Shoot boxing</a> – A Japanese form of kickboxing which allows throwing and <a href="/wiki/Submission_hold" class="mw-redirect" title="Submission hold">submission</a> while standing, similar to Sanda.</li></ul></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kickboxing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kickboxing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since kickboxing is a broad term, understanding the history can be somewhat difficult. Some of the earliest forms of kickboxing included the various <a href="/wiki/Indochinese_martial_arts" class="mw-redirect" title="Indochinese martial arts">Indochinese martial arts</a> especially <a href="/wiki/Muay_Boran" class="mw-redirect" title="Muay Boran">muay boran</a>, which developed into modern <a href="/wiki/Muay_Thai" title="Muay Thai">Muay Thai</a>.
</p><p>However, in terms of modern competition, it was during the 1950s that a Japanese karateka named <a href="/wiki/Tatsuo_Yamada_(karate)" title="Tatsuo Yamada (karate)">Tatsuo Yamada</a> first established an outline of a new sport that combined karate and Muay Thai.
</p><p>This was further explored during the early 1960s, when competitions between karate and Muay Thai began, which allowed for rule modifications to take place. By the middle of the decade the first true kickboxing events were being held in <a href="/wiki/Osaka" title="Osaka">Osaka</a>.
</p><p>By the 1970s and 1980s, the sport had expanded beyond Japan and had reached North America and Europe. It was during this time that many of the most prominent <a href="/wiki/Sport_governing_body" class="mw-redirect" title="Sport governing body">governing bodies</a> were formed.
</p>
<ul><li>In Japan the sport was widely popular and was regularly broadcast on <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> before going into a dark period during the 1980s.</li>
<li>In North America the sport had unclear rules so kickboxing and <a href="/wiki/Full_contact_karate#American" title="Full contact karate">full contact karate</a> were essentially the same sport.</li>
<li>In Europe the sport found marginal success but did not thrive until the 1990s.</li></ul>
<p>Since the 1990s the sport has been mostly dominated by the Japanese K-1 promotion, with some competition coming from other promotions and mostly pre-existing governing bodies.
</p><p>Along with the growing popularity in competition, there has been an increased amount of participation and exposure in the <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">mass media</a>, <a href="/wiki/Physical_fitness" title="Physical fitness">fitness</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Self-defense" title="Self-defense">self-defense</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kickboxing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kumite_of_Motobu_Choki.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Kumite_of_Motobu_Choki.jpg/220px-Kumite_of_Motobu_Choki.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="333" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Kumite_of_Motobu_Choki.jpg/330px-Kumite_of_Motobu_Choki.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/53/Kumite_of_Motobu_Choki.jpg 2x" data-file-width="350" data-file-height="529" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kumite_of_Motobu_Choki.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tatsuo Yamada (left) and his master, <a href="/wiki/Choki_Motobu" class="mw-redirect" title="Choki Motobu">Choki Motobu</a> (right)</div></div></div>
<p>On December 20, 1959, a Muay Thai match among Thai fighters was held at <a href="/wiki/Asakusa" title="Asakusa">Asakusa</a> town hall in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>. Tatsuo Yamada, who established "<i>Nihon Kempo Karate-do</i>", was interested in Muay Thai because he wanted to perform karate matches with full-contact rules since practitioners are not allowed to hit each other directly in karate matches. At this time, it was unimaginable to hit each other in karate matches in Japan. He had already announced his plan which was named <i>"The draft principles of project of establishment of a new sport and its industrialization"</i> in November 1959, and he proposed the tentative name of "karate-boxing" for this new sport.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> It is still unknown whether Nak Muay were invited by Yamada, but it is clear that Yamada was the only <a href="/wiki/Karateka" class="mw-redirect" title="Karateka">karateka</a> who was really interested in Muay Thai. Yamada invited a champion Nak Muay (and formerly his son Kan Yamada's sparring partner), and started studying Muay Thai. At this time, the Thai fighter was taken by <a href="/wiki/Osamu_Noguchi" title="Osamu Noguchi">Osamu Noguchi</a> who was a promoter of boxing and was also interested in Muay Thai.<sup id="cite_ref-O68_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-O68-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The Thai fighter's photo was on the magazine "The Primer of Nihon Kempo Karate-do, the first number" which was published by Yamada.
</p><p>There were "Karate vs. Muay Thai fights" on February 12, 1963. The three karate fighters from <i>Oyama dojo</i> (<a href="/wiki/Kyokushin" title="Kyokushin">kyokushin</a> later) went to the Lumpinee Boxing Stadium in Thailand and fought against three Muay Thai fighters. The three kyokushin karate fighters' names are <a href="/wiki/Tadashi_Nakamura_(martial_artist)" title="Tadashi Nakamura (martial artist)">Tadashi Nakamura</a>, Kenji Kurosaki and Akio Fujihira (also known as Noboru Osawa). The Muay Thai team were composed of only one authentic Thai fighter.<sup id="cite_ref-8_Limbs_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8_Limbs-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Japan won by 2–1: Tadashi Nakamura and Akio Fujihira both KOed opponents by punch while Kenji Kurosaki, who fought the Thai, was KOed by elbow. The only Japanese loser Kenji Kurosaki was then a kyokushin instructor rather than a contender and temporarily designated as a substitute for the absent chosen fighter. On June of the same year, karateka and future kickboxer <a href="/wiki/Tadashi_Sawamura" title="Tadashi Sawamura">Tadashi Sawamura</a> faced against top Thai fighter Samarn Sor Adisorn, in which Sawamura was knocked down 16 times and defeated.<sup id="cite_ref-8_Limbs_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8_Limbs-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Sawamura would use what he learned in that fight to incorporate in the evolving kickboxing tournaments.
</p><p>Noguchi studied <a href="/wiki/Muay_Thai" title="Muay Thai">Muay Thai</a> and developed a combined martial art which Noguchi named <i>kick boxing</i>, which absorbed and adopted more rules than techniques from Muay Thai. The main techniques of kickboxing are still derived from Japanese full contact karate (<a href="/wiki/Kyokushin_kaikan" class="mw-redirect" title="Kyokushin kaikan">kyokushin</a>). However, throwing and <a href="/wiki/Headbutt" title="Headbutt">butting</a> were allowed in the beginning to distinguish it from Muay Thai. This was later repealed. The <i>Kickboxing Association</i>, the first kickboxing sanctioning body, was founded by Osamu Noguchi in 1966 soon after that. Then the first kickboxing event was held in Osaka on April 11, 1966.
</p><p>Tatsu Yamada died in 1967, but his dojo changed its name to <i>Suginami Gym</i>, and kept sending kickboxers off to support kickboxing.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kickboxing boomed and became popular in Japan as it began to be broadcast on <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">TV</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> By 1970, kickboxing was telecast in Japan on three different channels three times weekly. The fight cards regularly included bouts between Japanese (kickboxers) and Thai (Muay Thai) boxers. <a href="/wiki/Tadashi_Sawamura" title="Tadashi Sawamura">Tadashi Sawamura</a> was an especially popular early kickboxer. In 1971 the All Japan Kickboxing Association (AJKA) was established and it registered approximately 700 kickboxers. The first AJKA Commissioner was Shintaro Ishihara, the longtime Governor of Tokyo. Champions were in each weight division from fly to middle. Longtime Ilyushiner Noboru Osawa won the AJKA bantamweight title, which he held for years. Raymond Edler, an American university student studying at Sophia University in Tokyo, took up kickboxing and won the AJKC middleweight title in 1972; he was the first non-Thai to be officially ranked in the sport of Thai boxing, when in 1972 Rajadamnern ranked him no. 3 in the Middleweight division. Edler defended the All Japan title several times and abandoned it. Other popular champions were Toshio Fujiwara and Mitsuo Shima. Most notably, Fujiwara was the first non-Thai to win an official Thai boxing title, when he defeated his Thai opponent in 1978 at Rajadamnern Stadium winning the lightweight championship bout.
</p><p>By 1980, due to poor ratings and then infrequent television coverage, the golden-age of kickboxing in Japan was suddenly finished.
Kickboxing had not been seen on TV until K-1 was founded in 1993.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In 1993, as <a href="/wiki/Kazuyoshi_Ishii" title="Kazuyoshi Ishii">Kazuyoshi Ishii</a> (founder of <a href="/wiki/Seidokaikan" title="Seidokaikan">Seidokaikan</a> karate) produced K-1 under special kickboxing rules (no elbow and <a href="/wiki/Tachi_waza" class="mw-redirect" title="Tachi waza">neck wrestling</a>) in 1993, kickboxing became famous again.<sup id="cite_ref-Maylam2001_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maylam2001-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TashiroTyrangiel2001_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TashiroTyrangiel2001-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
In the mid-1980s to early 1990s, before the first k-1, Kazuyoshi Ishii also partook in the formation of glove karate as an amateur sport in Japan. <a href="/wiki/Full_contact_karate#Gloved" title="Full contact karate">Glove karate</a> is based on knockdown karate rules, but wearing boxing gloves and allowing punches to the head. In effect, it is oriental rules kickboxing with scoring based on knockdowns and aggression rather than the number of hits. As K-1 grew in popularity, Glove karate for a while became the fastest growing amateur sport in Japan.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kickboxing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: North America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Full_contact_karate#American" title="Full contact karate">Full contact karate §&#160;American</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kickboxing_right_hook_landing.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Kickboxing_right_hook_landing.jpg/250px-Kickboxing_right_hook_landing.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Kickboxing_right_hook_landing.jpg/375px-Kickboxing_right_hook_landing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Kickboxing_right_hook_landing.jpg/500px-Kickboxing_right_hook_landing.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="554" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kickboxing_right_hook_landing.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hook-punch</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Count_Dante" title="Count Dante">Count Dante</a>, Ray Scarica and <a href="/wiki/Maung_Gyi" title="Maung Gyi">Maung Gyi</a> held the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>' earliest
cross-style full-contact style martial arts tournaments as early as 1962.
Between 1970 and 1973 a handful of kickboxing promotions were staged across the USA. The first recognized bout of this kind occurred on January 17, 1970, and came about when Joe Lewis, a Shorin Ryu stylist who had also studied <a href="/wiki/Jeet_Kune_Do" title="Jeet Kune Do">Jeet Kune Do</a> with the legendary <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Lee" title="Bruce Lee">Bruce Lee</a>, and noted champion in the Karate tournament circuit, grew disillusioned with the point-sparring format and sought to create an event that would allow martial artists to fight to the knock out. Enlisting the help of promoter Lee Faulkner,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> training in boxing and combining the techniques of boxing and Karate for the first time in America, Lewis arranged the bout to be held at the 1st Pro Team Karate Championships. Lewis faced Kenpo stylist Greg "Om" Baines,<sup id="cite_ref-starsystemkickboxing.net_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-starsystemkickboxing.net-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> who had defeated two opponents in years pasts. Lewis won the fight by knockout in the second round. The event was advertised as "Full contact" but the announcers referred to it as Kickboxing, and rules included knees, elbows and sweeps.<sup id="cite_ref-starsystemkickboxing.net_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-starsystemkickboxing.net-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Lewis would defend his U.S Heavyweight champion title 10 times, remaining undefeated until he came back from his retirement. In the early days, the rules were never clear; one of the first tournaments had no weight divisions and all the competitors fought off until one was left. During this early time, kickboxing and <a href="/wiki/Full_contact_karate" title="Full contact karate">full contact karate</a> are essentially the same sport.
</p><p>The institutional separation of American full-contact karate from kickboxing occurred with the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Professional_Karate_Association" title="Professional Karate Association">Professional Karate Association (PKA)</a> in 1974 and of the <a href="/wiki/World_Kickboxing_Association" title="World Kickboxing Association">World Kickboxing Association (WKA)</a> in 1976.
They were the first organised body of martial arts on a global scale to sanction fights, create ranking systems, and institute a development programme.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Kickboxing_Federation" title="International Kickboxing Federation">International Kickboxing Federation (IKF)</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Sport_Kickboxing_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="International Sport Kickboxing Association">International Sport Kickboxing Association (ISKA)</a> have been the only organizations to have thrived in the modern era.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/International_Kickboxing_Federation" title="International Kickboxing Federation">International Kickboxing Federation (IKF)</a> was founded in 1992 by Steve Fossum and Dan Stell. Stell eventually stepped down to go back to fighting while Fossum continued with the organization. In 1999 Fossum and Joe Taylor of Ringside Products created the first amateur open North American tournament for Kickboxing and Muay Thai, now the IKF World Classic.
</p><p>After ending its venture with K-1 in 2006, ISKA co-operated the <a href="/wiki/World_Combat_League" title="World Combat League">World Combat League</a> with <a href="/wiki/Chuck_Norris" title="Chuck Norris">Chuck Norris</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Strikeforce_(mixed_martial_arts)" title="Strikeforce (mixed martial arts)">Strikeforce</a> MMA in partnership with Silicon Valley Entertainment (SVE), an investor group who also own the San Jose Sharks. Norris passed the WCL to his son-in-law Damien Diciolli in 2007, and it has since become inactive. Strikeforce MMA was sold to UFC in 2011.
</p><p>The ISKA expanded into sport (tournament) martial arts about 15 years ago,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (November 2014)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and is a co-operator along with WAKO and Global Marketing Ventures (GMV) in the global Open World Tour (OWT) the first worldwide pro circuit of sport karate professional competitors. It sanctions and assists in the annual US Open &amp; ISKA World Championships that anchors the OWT and the North American-based NASKA Tour. The US Open &amp; ISKA World Championships is broadcast live on ESPN2 and ESPN3 each year.
</p><p>Other kickboxing sanctioning bodies include <a href="/wiki/World_Association_of_Kickboxing_Organizations" title="World Association of Kickboxing Organizations">World Association of Kickboxing Organizations</a> (primarily amateurs) and KICK International.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kickboxing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/World_Association_of_Kickboxing_Organizations" title="World Association of Kickboxing Organizations">World Association of Kickboxing Organizations</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>, American-styled kickboxing was promulgated from its inception in the 1970s by <a href="/wiki/Georg_F_Brueckner" title="Georg F Brueckner">Georg F. Bruckner</a>, who in 1976 was the co-founder of the <a href="/wiki/World_Association_of_Kickboxing_Organizations" title="World Association of Kickboxing Organizations">World Association of Kickboxing Organizations</a>. The term "kickboxing" as used in <a href="/wiki/German-speaking_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="German-speaking Europe">German-speaking Europe</a> is therefore mostly synonymous with American kickboxing. The elbow and knee techniques allowed in Japanese kickboxing, by contrast, were associated with Muay Thai, and Japanese kickboxing went mostly unnoticed in German-speaking Europe before the launch of K-1 in 1993.
</p><p>By contrast, in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> kickboxing was introduced in its Japanese form, by <a href="/wiki/Jan_Plas" title="Jan Plas">Jan Plas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thom_Harinck" title="Thom Harinck">Thom Harinck</a> who founded NKBB (The Dutch Kickboxing Association) in 1976.
Harinck also founded the MTBN (Dutch Muay Thai Association) in 1983, and the WMTA (World Muay Thai Association) and the EMTA (European Muay Thai Association) in 1984.
The most prominent kickboxing gyms in the Netherlands, <a href="/wiki/Mejiro_Gym" title="Mejiro Gym">Mejiro Gym</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chakuriki_Gym" title="Chakuriki Gym">Chakuriki Gym</a> and <a href="/wiki/Golden_Glory" title="Golden Glory">Golden Glory</a>, were all derived from or were significantly influenced by Japanese kickboxing and kyokushin karate.
</p><p>Dutch athletes have been very successful in the K-1 competitions.
Out of the 19 <a href="/wiki/K-1_World_Grand_Prix" title="K-1 World Grand Prix">K-1 World Grand Prix</a> championship titles issued from 1993 to 2012, 15 went to Dutch participants (<a href="/wiki/Peter_Aerts" title="Peter Aerts">Peter Aerts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernesto_Hoost" title="Ernesto Hoost">Ernesto Hoost</a>, <a href="/wiki/Remy_Bonjasky" title="Remy Bonjasky">Remy Bonjasky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Semmy_Schilt" title="Semmy Schilt">Semmy Schilt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alistair_Overeem" title="Alistair Overeem">Alistair Overeem</a>). The remaining four titles were won by <a href="/wiki/Branko_Cikati%C4%87" title="Branko Cikatić">Branko Cikatić</a> of Croatia in 1993, <a href="/wiki/Andy_Hug" title="Andy Hug">Andy Hug</a> of Switzerland in 1996, <a href="/wiki/Mark_Hunt" title="Mark Hunt">Mark Hunt</a> of New Zealand in 2001 and <a href="/wiki/Mirko_Filipovi%C4%87" class="mw-redirect" title="Mirko Filipović">Mirko Filipović</a> of Croatia in 2012.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Individual_rulesets">Individual rulesets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kickboxing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Individual rulesets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kickboxing has a number of different rulesets. For example, American kickboxing and/or <a href="/wiki/Full_contact_karate#American" title="Full contact karate">American full contact karate</a> restricts to strikes using punches and higher kicks; whereas some other arts often regarded as "kickboxing" allow <a href="/wiki/Low_kick" title="Low kick">low kicks</a> and even <a href="/wiki/Knee_(strike)" title="Knee (strike)">knee strikes</a>, elbows, and grappling maneuvers. All forms of kickboxing use an identical scoring system, however. A winner is declared during the bout if there is a <a href="/wiki/Submission_(combat_sport_term)" class="mw-redirect" title="Submission (combat sport term)">submission</a> (fighter quits or fighter's corner throws in the towel), <a href="/wiki/Knockout" title="Knockout">knockout</a> (KO), or referee stoppage (<a href="/wiki/Technical_knockout" class="mw-redirect" title="Technical knockout">technical knockout</a>, or TKO). If all of the rounds expire with no knockout then the fight is scored by a team of 3 judges. The judges determine a winner based on their scoring of each round. A <a href="/wiki/Split_decision" title="Split decision">split decision</a> indicates a disagreement between the judges, while a <a href="/wiki/Unanimous_decision" title="Unanimous decision">unanimous decision</a> indicates that all judges saw the fight the same way and all have declared the same winner.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Full_contact">Full contact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kickboxing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Full contact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Full contact rules</b>, or <b>American kickboxing</b>, is essentially a mixture of Western boxing and traditional karate.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The male kickboxers are bare-chested wearing kickboxing trousers and protective gear including: mouth-guard, hand-wraps, 10&#160;oz (280&#160;g). boxing gloves, groin-guard, shin-pads, and kick-boots and protective helmet (for amateurs and those under 16). The female kickboxers will wear a <a href="/wiki/Sports_bra" title="Sports bra">sports bra</a> and chest protection in addition to the male clothing/protective gear.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> also although there have been many organizations the PKA was the first and adopted the above waist kick rule only to create a more appealing option for TV and mainstream Americans with the hope that using a lot of flashy movie type kicks would draw in crowds and fans which it did in ways but it was the inception of the WKA founded by Howard Hanson and Arnold Urquidez which went internationally and started allowing the use of low kicks after the Urquidez brothers were challenged by Thai fighters living in California looking to spread Muay Thai in the USA in the mid 70s which helped the most notable and internationally famous younger brother Benny "the jet" Urquidez which his early fights with Muay Thai fighters helped him learn and adapt helping him become very successful against the elite Japanese fighters.
</p><p>Notable fighters under full contact rules include, <a href="/wiki/Dennis_Alexio" title="Dennis Alexio">Dennis Alexio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joe_Lewis_(martial_arts)" class="mw-redirect" title="Joe Lewis (martial arts)">Joe Lewis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rick_Roufus" title="Rick Roufus">Rick Roufus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean-Yves_Th%C3%A9riault_(kickboxer)" title="Jean-Yves Thériault (kickboxer)">Jean-Yves Thériault</a>, <a href="/wiki/Benny_Urquidez" title="Benny Urquidez">Benny Urquidez</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Wallace_(martial_arts)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bill Wallace (martial arts)">Bill Wallace</a>, <a href="/wiki/Demetrius_Havanas" title="Demetrius Havanas">Demetrius Havanas</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Billy_Jackson_(kickboxer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Billy Jackson (kickboxer) (page does not exist)">Billy Jackson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pete_Cunningham_(kickboxer)" title="Pete Cunningham (kickboxer)">Pete Cunningham</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Don_%22The_Dragon%22_Wilson" class="mw-redirect" title="Don &quot;The Dragon&quot; Wilson">Don "The Dragon" Wilson</a>
</p><p>Rules:
</p>
<ul><li>Opponents are allowed to hit each other with <a href="/wiki/Punch_(combat)" title="Punch (combat)">punches</a> and kicks, striking above the waist.</li>
<li>Elbows and <a href="/wiki/Knee_(strike)" title="Knee (strike)">knees</a> are forbidden and the use of the <a href="/wiki/Tibia" title="Tibia">shins</a> is seldom allowed.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Clinch_fighting" title="Clinch fighting">Clinch fighting</a> and grappling are forbidden, sweeps are legal but vary depending on the referee.</li>
<li>Bouts are usually 3 to 10 rounds (lasting 2–3 minutes each) with a 1-minute rest in between rounds.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Semi-Contact">Semi-Contact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kickboxing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Semi-Contact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Semi-contact rules</b> or <b>Points Fighting</b>, is the variant of American kickboxing most similar to karate, since it consists in fighting for the purpose of scoring points with an emphasis on delivery, speed, and technique. Under such rules, fights are held on the <a href="/wiki/Tatami" title="Tatami">tatami</a>, presenting the belts to classify the fighters in order of experience and ability. The male kickboxers wear shirts and kickboxing trousers as well as protective gear including: mouth-guard, hand-wraps, 10&#160;oz (280&#160;g). boxing gloves, groin-guard, shin-pads, kick-boots, and headgear. The female kickboxers will wear a sports bra and chest protection in addition to the male clothing/protective gear.
</p><p>Notable fighters under semi-contact rules include <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Daniels_(martial_artist)" title="Raymond Daniels (martial artist)">Raymond Daniels</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Page_(fighter)" title="Michael Page (fighter)">Michael Page</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gregorio_Di_Leo" title="Gregorio Di Leo">Gregorio Di Leo</a>.
There is also a guinness world record for semi contact also known as light continuous, the world record is for the most consecutive three minute rounds with one minute rest between the record stands at 135 and took nine hours to complete. The record used a modified rule set (known as the world record rules) from the British Kickboxing Council or BKC. The record was set in 2017 as part of a fund raiser for charity and has yet to be beaten.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> 
</p><p>Rules:
</p>
<ul><li>Fighters can score through punches or kicks, striking above the waist and <a href="/wiki/Sweep_(martial_arts)" title="Sweep (martial arts)">foot sweeps</a>, executed below the ankle.</li>
<li>Punches, kicks, and foot sweeps are awarded 1 point. Kicks to the head or jumping kicks to the body are awarded 2 points. Jumping kicks to the head are awarded 3 points.</li>
<li>Hook kicks and Axe kicks are allowed but must be executed with the sole of the foot.</li>
<li>The use of the <a href="/wiki/Tibia" title="Tibia">shins</a> is seldom allowed, save for jumping and spinning techniques.</li>
<li>Elbows, <a href="/wiki/Knee_(strike)" title="Knee (strike)">knees</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Punch_(combat)" title="Punch (combat)">spinning backfists</a> are forbidden.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Clinch_fighting" title="Clinch fighting">Clinch fighting</a>, throws and <a href="/wiki/Sweep_(martial_arts)" title="Sweep (martial arts)">sweeps</a> (with the exception of foot sweeps) are forbidden.</li>
<li>Bouts are usually 3 rounds (lasting 2–3 minutes each) with a 1-minute rest in between rounds.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International">International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kickboxing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: International">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>International rules</b>, or <b>freestyle rules</b> (also known simply as Kickboxing in Europe and <b>Low Kick</b> in the United States), contrasts with full contact rules in that it also allows low kicks. The male kickboxers are bare-chested wearing kickboxing trousers or shorts and protective gear including: mouth-guard, hand-wraps, shin-wraps, 10&#160;oz (280&#160;g). boxing gloves and groin-guard. The female kickboxers will wear a sports bra and chest protection in addition to the male clothing/protective gear.
</p><p>Notable fighters under international rules include <a href="/wiki/Rick_Roufus" title="Rick Roufus">Rick Roufus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Roque%C3%B1i" title="Abraham Roqueñi">Abraham Roqueñi</a>.
</p><p>Rules:
</p>
<ul><li>Fighters are allowed to strike their opponent with punches and kicks, including kicks below the waist, except for the groin.</li>
<li>Elbows and <a href="/wiki/Knee_(strike)" title="Knee (strike)">knees</a> are forbidden.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Clinch_fighting" title="Clinch fighting">Clinch fighting</a>, throws and sweeps are forbidden.</li>
<li>Bouts are 3 to 5 rounds for amateurs and 3 to 10 rounds for professionals, all rounds lasting 2-3 minutes each. Each round has a 1-minute rest in between rounds.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Muay_Thai">Muay Thai</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kickboxing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Muay Thai">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Muay_Thai" title="Muay Thai">Muay Thai</a></div>
<p><b>Muay Thai</b>, or <b>Thai boxing</b>, rules usually sees bouts contested over 5, 3 minute rounds and male fighters bare-chested wearing shorts and protective gear including: mouth-guard, hand-wraps, shin-wraps, 10&#160;oz (280&#160;g). boxing gloves, groin-guard and sometimes <i>prajioud</i> arm bands. The female Thaiboxers will wear a sports bra and chest protection in addition to the male clothing/protective gear. Muay Thai is unique in that it is the only style of kickboxing that allows elbows, <a href="/wiki/Knee_(strike)" title="Knee (strike)">knees</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clinch_fighting" title="Clinch fighting">clinch fighting</a>, throws, sweeps and low kicks. <a href="/wiki/Groin_attack" title="Groin attack">Groin strikes</a> were allowed until the 1980s in international Muay Thai and are still permitted in Thailand itself (though the boxers wear cups to lessen the impact). Kicking to mid-body and head are scored highly generating a large number of points on judges' scorecards. Moreover, kicking is still judged highly even if the kick was blocked. In contrast, punching is worth fewer points.
</p><p>Notable fighters under Muay Thai rules include <a href="/wiki/Apidej_Sit_Hrun" class="mw-redirect" title="Apidej Sit Hrun">Apidej Sit Hrun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buakaw_Por._Pramuk" class="mw-redirect" title="Buakaw Por. Pramuk">Buakaw Por. Pramuk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Changpuek_Kiatsongrit" title="Changpuek Kiatsongrit">Changpuek Kiatsongrit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rob_Kaman" title="Rob Kaman">Rob Kaman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ramon_Dekkers" title="Ramon Dekkers">Ramon Dekkers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coban_Lookchaomaesaitong" title="Coban Lookchaomaesaitong">Coban Lookchaomaesaitong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dieselnoi_Chor_Thanasukarn" title="Dieselnoi Chor Thanasukarn">Dieselnoi Chor Thanasukarn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saenchai" title="Saenchai">Saenchai P.K. Saenchaimuaythaigym</a>, <a href="/wiki/Samart_Payakaroon" title="Samart Payakaroon">Samart Payakaroon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yodsanklai_Fairtex" title="Yodsanklai Fairtex">Yodsanklai Fairtex</a>.
</p><p>Rules:
</p>
<ul><li>Fighters are allowed to strike their opponent with punches, kicks, including kicks below the waist, elbows and <a href="/wiki/Knee_(strike)" title="Knee (strike)">knees</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Clinch_fighting" title="Clinch fighting">Clinch fighting</a>, throws and sweeps are allowed.</li>
<li>Bouts are generally 5, 3-minute rounds with 2 minutes rest in between, but 3 round fights are used.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oriental">Oriental</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kickboxing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Oriental">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Oriental rules</b>, also known as <b>Japanese kickboxing</b> and <b>K-1 rules</b>, is a combat sport created by the Japanese boxing promoter <a href="/wiki/Osamu_Noguchi" title="Osamu Noguchi">Osamu Noguchi</a> and Karate practitioner <a href="/wiki/Tatsuo_Yamada_(karate)" title="Tatsuo Yamada (karate)">Tatsuo Yamada</a>. It was the first combat sport that adopted the name of "kickboxing" in 1966, later termed "Japanese kickboxing" as a <a href="/wiki/Retronym" title="Retronym">retronym</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Oriental rules bouts were traditionally fought over 5, 3-minute rounds but 3 round bouts have since become popular since their inception in the K-1 promotion. The male kickboxers are bare-chested wearing shorts (although trousers and <a href="/wiki/Karate_gi" title="Karate gi">karate gis</a> have been worn) and protective gear including: mouth-guard, hand-wraps, shin-wraps, 10&#160;oz (280&#160;g). boxing gloves and groin-guard. The female kickboxers will wear a sports bra and chest protection in addition to the male clothing/protective gear.
</p><p>Notable fighters under Oriental rules include <a href="/wiki/Peter_Aerts" title="Peter Aerts">Peter Aerts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Remy_Bonjasky" title="Remy Bonjasky">Remy Bonjasky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toshio_Fujiwara" title="Toshio Fujiwara">Toshio Fujiwara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernesto_Hoost" title="Ernesto Hoost">Ernesto Hoost</a>, <a href="/wiki/Albert_Kraus" title="Albert Kraus">Albert Kraus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Masato_(kickboxer)" title="Masato (kickboxer)">Masato</a>, <a href="/wiki/Giorgio_Petrosyan" title="Giorgio Petrosyan">Giorgio Petrosyan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tadashi_Sawamura" title="Tadashi Sawamura">Tadashi Sawamura</a> and <a href="/wiki/Semmy_Schilt" title="Semmy Schilt">Semmy Schilt</a>
</p><p>Rules:
</p>
<ul><li>Fighters are allowed to strike their opponent with punches, kicks and <a href="/wiki/Knee_(strike)" title="Knee (strike)">knees</a> including kicks below the waist, except for the groin.</li>
<li>Elbow strikes on the head are forbidden.</li>
<li>Limited <a href="/wiki/Clinch_fighting" title="Clinch fighting">clinch fighting</a> is allowed.</li>
<li>Bouts are 3 to 5 rounds (lasting 3 minutes each) with a 1-minute rest in between rounds.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Headbutt" title="Headbutt">Headbutts</a>, throws and sweeps were banned in 1966 for fighters' safety.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sanshou">Sanshou</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kickboxing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Sanshou">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sanshou" title="Sanshou">Sanshou</a></div>
<p><b>Sanshou</b>, or <b>Sanda</b>, (also known as <b>Chinese boxing</b>) is a form of kickboxing originally developed by the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_military" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese military">Chinese military</a> based upon the study and practices of traditional <a href="/wiki/Kung_fu" class="mw-redirect" title="Kung fu">Kung fu</a> and modern combat fighting techniques; it combines traditional kickboxing, which include close range and rapid successive <a href="/wiki/Punch_(strike)" class="mw-redirect" title="Punch (strike)">punches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kicks" class="mw-redirect" title="Kicks">kicks</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Wrestling" title="Wrestling">wrestling</a>, <a href="/wiki/Takedown_(grappling)" title="Takedown (grappling)">takedowns</a>, throws, sweeps, kick catches, and in some competitions, even elbow and <a href="/wiki/Knee_(strike)" title="Knee (strike)">knee</a> strikes. The male fighters are bare-chested wearing shorts and protective gear including: mouth-guard, hand-wraps, 10&#160;oz (280&#160;g) boxing gloves and groin-guard. The female kickboxers will wear a sports bra and chest protection in addition to the male clothing/protective gear.
</p><p>Notable fighters under Sanshou rules include <a href="/wiki/Pat_Barry_(fighter)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pat Barry (fighter)">Pat Barry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Tiequan" title="Zhang Tiequan">Zhang Tiequan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liu_Hailong" title="Liu Hailong">Liu Hailong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cung_Le" title="Cung Le">Cung Le</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shahbulat_Shamhalaev" title="Shahbulat Shamhalaev">Shahbulat Shamhalaev</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shamil_Zavurov" title="Shamil Zavurov">Shamil Zavurov</a>.
</p><p>Rules:
</p>
<ul><li>Fighters are allowed to strike their opponent with punches and kicks including kicks below the waist, except for the groin.</li>
<li>Elbows and <a href="/wiki/Knee_(strike)" title="Knee (strike)">knees</a> are forbidden (with the exception of some competitions).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Clinch_fighting" title="Clinch fighting">Clinch fighting</a>, throws and sweeps are allowed.</li>
<li>Bouts are 5 rounds (lasting 3 minutes each) with a 1-minute rest in between rounds.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shoot_boxing">Shoot boxing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kickboxing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Shoot boxing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Shoot_boxing" title="Shoot boxing">Shoot boxing</a></div>
<p><b>Shoot boxing</b> is a unique style of kickboxing popular in Japan that utilizes <a href="/wiki/Standing" title="Standing">standing</a> <a href="/wiki/Submission_(combat_sports)" title="Submission (combat sports)">submissions</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Chokehold" title="Chokehold">chokeholds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armlock" title="Armlock">armlocks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wristlock" title="Wristlock">wristlocks</a> in addition to kicks, punches, <a href="/wiki/Knee_(strike)" title="Knee (strike)">knees</a> and <a href="/wiki/Throw_(grappling)" title="Throw (grappling)">throws</a>. The male fighters are bare-chested wearing skin tight trousers and protective gear including: mouth-guard, hand-wraps, 10&#160;oz (280&#160;g). boxing gloves and groin-guard. The female kickboxers will wear a sports bra and chest protection in addition to the male clothing/protective gear.
</p><p>Notable fighters under shoot boxing rules include <a href="/wiki/Rena_Kubota" title="Rena Kubota">Rena Kubota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kenichi_Ogata_(shoot_boxer)" title="Kenichi Ogata (shoot boxer)">Kenichi Ogata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hiroki_Shishido" title="Hiroki Shishido">Hiroki Shishido</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andy_Souwer" title="Andy Souwer">Andy Souwer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ai_Takahashi_(kickboxer)" title="Ai Takahashi (kickboxer)">Ai Takahashi</a>.
</p><p>Rules:
</p>
<ul><li>Opponents are allowed to strike each other with punches, <a href="/wiki/Kick" title="Kick">kicks</a>, including kicks below the waist, except for the groin, and <a href="/wiki/Knee_(strike)" title="Knee (strike)">knees</a>.</li>
<li>Elbows are forbidden.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Clinch_fighting" title="Clinch fighting">Clinch fighting</a>, <a href="/wiki/Throw_(grappling)" title="Throw (grappling)">throws</a> and sweeps are allowed.</li>
<li>Standing <a href="/wiki/Submission_(combat_sports)" title="Submission (combat sports)">submissions</a> are allowed.</li>
<li>Bouts are 3 rounds (lasting 3 minutes each) with a 1-minute rest in between rounds.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Techniques">Techniques</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kickboxing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Techniques">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Punching">Punching</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kickboxing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Punching">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Punching techniques are very much identical to <a href="/wiki/Boxing_punches" class="mw-redirect" title="Boxing punches">boxing punches</a>, including
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Jab" title="Jab">Jab</a> – straight punch from the front hand. The arm extends from the side of the torso which is quickly turned concurrently with this action. A jab may be directed at an opponent's head or body, and is often used in conjunction with the cross.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cross_(boxing)" title="Cross (boxing)">Cross</a> – straight punch from the back hand</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hook_(boxing)" title="Hook (boxing)">Hook</a> – rounded punch to either the head or body in an arching motion, usually not scored in points scoring</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Uppercut" title="Uppercut">Uppercut</a> – rising punch striking to the chin</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Short_straight-punch" title="Short straight-punch">Short straight-punch</a> usually striking to the chin</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Backfist" class="mw-redirect" title="Backfist">Backfist</a> usually from the front hand, <b>reverse-back fist</b> and <b> spinning back-fist </b> both usually from the back hand – are strikes to the head, raising the arm and bending the arm at the elbow and then straightening the arm quickly to strike to the side of the head with the rear of the knuckles.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Superman_punch" title="Superman punch">Flying-punch</a> struck usually from the rear hand, the combatant hops on the front foot, kicking back with the rear foot and simultaneously extending the rear hand as a punch, in the form of "superman" flying through the sky.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Cross-counter" title="Cross-counter">Cross-counter</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Counterpunch_(boxing)" title="Counterpunch (boxing)">counterpunch</a> begun immediately after an opponent throws a jab, exploiting the opening in the opponent's position.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Drop_(boxing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Drop (boxing)">Overhand</a> (<i>overcut</i> or <i>drop</i>) – a semi-circular and vertical punch thrown with the rear hand. It is usually when the opponent <a href="/wiki/Bobbing_(boxing)" title="Bobbing (boxing)">bobbing</a> or <a href="/wiki/Slipping" title="Slipping">slipping</a>. The strategic utility of the drop relying on body weight can deliver a great deal of power.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bolo_punch" title="Bolo punch">Bolo punch</a> – a combination of a wide <a href="/wiki/Uppercut" title="Uppercut">uppercut</a>/right cross/<a href="/wiki/Swing_(boxing)" title="Swing (boxing)">swing</a> that was delivered seemingly from the floor</li>
<li>Half-hook – a combination of a wide <a href="/wiki/Jab" title="Jab">jab</a>/hook or <a href="/wiki/Cross" title="Cross">cross</a>/hook</li>
<li>Half-swing – a combination of a wide hook/<a href="/wiki/Swing_(boxing)" title="Swing (boxing)">swing</a></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kicking">Kicking</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kickboxing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Kicking">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Roundhouse-kick.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Roundhouse-kick.jpg/220px-Roundhouse-kick.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="175" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Roundhouse-kick.jpg/330px-Roundhouse-kick.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Roundhouse-kick.jpg/440px-Roundhouse-kick.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1104" data-file-height="880" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Roundhouse-kick.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Roundhouse kick</div></div></div>
<p>The standard kicking techniques are:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Front_kick" title="Front kick">Front kick</a> or <i>push Kick/high Kick</i> – Striking face or chest on with the balls of the foot</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Side_kick" class="mw-redirect" title="Side kick">Side kick</a> – Striking with the heel of the foot with leg parallel to the ground, can be performed to either the head, leg or body</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Semi-circular_kick" class="mw-redirect" title="Semi-circular kick">Semi-circular kick</a> or <i>forty five degree roundhouse kick</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Roundhouse_kick" title="Roundhouse kick">Roundhouse kick</a> or <i>circle kick</i> – Striking with the front of the foot or the shin to the head or the body in a <i>chopping</i> motion</li></ul>
<p>There are a large number of special or variant kicking techniques, including spinning kicks, jumping kicks, and other variants such as
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Hook_kick" class="mw-redirect" title="Hook kick">Hook kick</a> (heel kick) – Extending the leg out to the side of the body, and hooking the leg back to strike the head with either the heel or sole</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Crescent_kick" class="mw-redirect" title="Crescent kick">Crescent kick</a> and <i>forward crescent kick</i></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Axe_kick" class="mw-redirect" title="Axe kick">Axe kick</a> – is a <i>stomp out kick</i> or <i> axe kick</i>. The stomp kick normally travels downward, striking with the side or base heel.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Back_kick" class="mw-redirect" title="Back kick">Back kick</a> – is delivered with the base heel of the foot.</li>
<li>Sweeping – One foot or both feet of an opponent may be swept depending upon their position, balance and strength.</li></ul>
<p>Spinning versions of the back, side, hook and axe kicks can also be performed along with jumping versions of all kicks.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Knee_and_elbow_strikes">Knee and elbow strikes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kickboxing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Knee and elbow strikes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The knee and elbow techniques in Japanese kickboxing, indicative of its Muay Thai heritage, are the main difference that separates this style from other kickboxing rules.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
See <i><a href="/wiki/Muay_Thai#Elbow" title="Muay Thai">ti sok</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Muay_Thai#Knee" title="Muay Thai">ti khao</a></i> for details.
</p>
<ul><li><b>Straight knee</b> (long-range knee kick or front heel kick). This knee strike is delivered with the back or reverse foot against an opponent's stomach, groin, hip or spine an opponent forward by the neck, shoulder or arm.</li>
<li><b>Flying knee</b> – can be delivered with the front or back foot. It makes an explosive snap upwards to strike an opponent's face, chin, throat or chest.</li>
<li><b>Hook knee</b> – can be delivered with the front or back foot. It makes a half circle spin and strikes the sides of an opponent.</li>
<li><b>Side knee</b> – is a highly-deceptive knee technique used in close-range fighting. The knee is lifted to the toes or lifted up, and is snapped to left and right, striking an opponent's sensitive knee joints, insides of thighs, groin.</li>
<li><b>Side elbow</b> - most commonly used elbow, commonly seen in Thai boxing fights</li>
<li><b>Upward elbow</b> - used in the clinch to strike the opponent’s nose, chin or both</li>
<li><b>Downward elbow</b> - used as a replacement to an overhand striking down from just under the forehead to the chin</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defense">Defense</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kickboxing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Defense">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are three main defensive positions (guards or styles) used in kickboxing. Within each style, there is considerable variation among fighters, as some fighters may have their guard higher for more head protection while others have their guard lower to provide better protection against body punches. Many fighters vary their defensive style throughout a bout in order to adapt to the situation of the moment, choosing the position best suited to protect them.
</p>
<ul><li>Slip – <b><a href="/wiki/Slipping" title="Slipping">Slipping</a></b> rotates the body slightly so that an incoming punch passes harmlessly next to the head. As the opponent's punch arrives, the boxer sharply rotates the hips and shoulders. This turns the chin sideways and allows the punch to "slip" past. Muhammad Ali was famous for extremely fast and close slips.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Bob_and_weave" title="Bob and weave">Bob and weave</a></b> – <b><a href="/wiki/Bobbing_(boxing)" title="Bobbing (boxing)">bobbing</a></b> moves the head laterally and beneath an incoming punch. As the opponent's punch arrives, the kickboxer bends the legs quickly and simultaneously shifts the body either slightly right or left. Once the punch has been evaded, the kickboxer "weaves" back to an upright position, emerging on either the outside or inside of the opponent's still-extended arm. To move outside the opponent's extended arm is called "bobbing to the outside". To move inside the opponent's extended arm is called "bobbing to the inside".</li>
<li>Parry/Block – <b><a href="/wiki/Parrying_(boxing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Parrying (boxing)">Parrying</a></b> or <b><a href="/wiki/Blocking_(martial_arts)" title="Blocking (martial arts)">blocking</a></b> uses the kickboxer's hands as defensive tools to deflect incoming attacks. As the opponent's punch arrives, the boxer delivers a sharp, lateral, open-handed blow to the opponent's wrist or forearm, redirecting the punch.</li>
<li>The cover-up – <b><a href="/wiki/Blocking_(martial_arts)" title="Blocking (martial arts)">Covering</a> up</b> is the last opportunity to avoid an incoming strike to an unprotected face or body. Generally speaking, the hands are held high to protect the head and chin and the forearms are tucked against the torso to impede body shots. When protecting the body, the kickboxer rotates the hips and lets incoming punches "roll" off the guard. To protect the head, the kickboxer presses both fists against the front of the face with the forearms parallel and facing outwards. This type of guard is weak against attacks from below.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Stand-up_grappling_position" class="mw-redirect" title="Stand-up grappling position">clinch</a> – <b><a href="/wiki/Clinching_position" class="mw-redirect" title="Clinching position">Clinching</a></b> is a form of standing <a href="/wiki/Grappling" title="Grappling">grappling</a> and occurs when the distance between both fighters has closed and straight punches cannot be employed. In this situation, the kickboxer attempts to <a href="/wiki/Grappling_hold" title="Grappling hold">hold</a> or "tie up" the opponent's hands or enter <a href="/wiki/Clinch_fighting" title="Clinch fighting">neck wrestling</a> position. In one way to perform a clinch, the kickboxer loops both hands around the outside of the opponent's shoulders, scooping back under the forearms to grasp the opponent's arms tightly against his own body. In this position, the opponent's arms are pinned and cannot be used to attack. Other forms of clinch involve getting control of opponents neck by <a href="/wiki/Collar_tie" title="Collar tie">collar tie</a> or upper body by <a href="/wiki/Underhook" title="Underhook">underhooks</a>, <a href="/wiki/Overhook" title="Overhook">overhooks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bear_hug" title="Bear hug">body lock</a>. It is often in the clinch where knee, elbow, sweep and throw techniques are used.</li></ul>
