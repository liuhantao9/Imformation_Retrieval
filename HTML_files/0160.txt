	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Polish name</h1>

<p><b>Polish names</b>  have two main elements: the <i>imię</i>, the first name, or <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>; and the <i>nazwisko</i>, the last name, <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">family name</a> (surname).
</p><p>The usage of <a href="/wiki/Personal_name" title="Personal name">personal names</a> in <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> is generally governed by <a href="/wiki/Civil_law_(legal_system)" title="Civil law (legal system)">civil law</a>, church law, personal taste and family custom.
</p><p>The law requires a given name (<i>imię</i>) to indicate the person's gender. Almost all Polish female names end in a vowel <i>-a</i>, and most male names end in a consonant or a vowel other than <i>a</i>. There are, however, a few male names that end in <i>a</i>, which are very old and uncommon, such as Barnaba, Bonawentura, Boryna, Jarema, Kosma, Kuba (a diminutive of Jakub) and Saba. <a href="/wiki/Maria_(given_name)" title="Maria (given name)">Maria</a> is a female name that can be used also as a middle (second) name for males.
</p><p>Since the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a>, Polish-sounding surnames ending with the masculine <i>-ski</i> <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffix</a>, including <i>-cki</i> and <i>-dzki</i>, and the corresponding feminine suffix <i>-ska/-cka/-dzka</i> were associated with the <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobility</a> (Polish <i><a href="/wiki/Szlachta" title="Szlachta">szlachta</a></i>), which alone, in the early years, had such suffix distinctions.<sup id="cite_ref-ZKHP_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZKHP-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> They are widely popular today.
</p><p>Minor regional spelling differences also exist depending on whether the surname originated in <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a> or <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">Slovak</a> (<i>-sky/-ský</i>).
</p>

<h2><span id="Imi.C4.99_.28given_name.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Imię_(given_name)"><i>Imię</i> (given name)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Imię (given name)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A child in Poland is usually given one or two names; Polish registry offices do not register more than two. Among Catholics, who form the vast majority of the population, it is customary to adopt the name of a saint as an informal, third given name at <a href="/wiki/Confirmation" title="Confirmation">confirmation</a>, however, this does not have any legal effect. (This is reminiscent of the pre-Christian rite of the "first haircut" (<a href="/wiki/First_haircut#Polish_boys" title="First haircut">postrzyżyny</a>), which also involved giving the child a new name.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>)
</p><p>Parents normally choose from a long list of traditional names which may come from:<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>a <a href="/wiki/Christian_name" title="Christian name">Christian name</a>, i.e., a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Biblical_names" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Biblical names">Biblical name</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Saint%27s_name" title="Saint&#39;s name">saint's name</a></li>
<li>a <a href="/wiki/Slavic_names" title="Slavic names">Slavic name of pre-Christian origin</a>.</li></ul>
<p>The names of Slavic saints, such as <a href="/wiki/Wojciech" title="Wojciech">Wojciech</a> (<a href="/wiki/Adalbert_of_Prague" title="Adalbert of Prague">St Adalbert</a>), <a href="/wiki/Stanislav_(given_name)" title="Stanislav (given name)">Stanisław</a> (<a href="/wiki/St_Stanislaus" class="mw-redirect" title="St Stanislaus">St Stanislaus</a>), or <a href="/wiki/Casimir" title="Casimir">Kazimierz</a> (<a href="/wiki/St_Casimir" class="mw-redirect" title="St Casimir">St Casimir</a>), belong to both of these groups. Slavic names used by historical <a href="/wiki/List_of_Polish_monarchs" title="List of Polish monarchs">Polish monarchs</a>, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Boleslaus" class="mw-redirect" title="Boleslaus">Bolesław</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lech_(name)" title="Lech (name)">Lech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mieszko_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mieszko (disambiguation)">Mieszko</a>, <a href="/wiki/W%C5%82adys%C5%82aw_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Władysław (disambiguation)">Władysław</a>, are common as well. Additionally, a few names of <a href="/wiki/Lithuania" title="Lithuania">Lithuanian</a> origin, such as <a href="/wiki/Algirdas" title="Algirdas">Olgierd</a> (<a href="/wiki/Algirdas" title="Algirdas">Algirdas</a>), <a href="/wiki/Witold_(given_name)" title="Witold (given name)">Witold</a> (<a href="/wiki/Vytautas" title="Vytautas">Vytautas</a>) or <a href="/wiki/Danuta_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Danuta (disambiguation)">Danuta</a>, are quite popular in Poland.
</p><p>Traditionally, the names are given at a child's <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptism</a>. Non-Christian, but traditional, Slavic names are usually accepted, but the priest may encourage parents to pick at least one Christian name. In the past, two Christian names were given to a child so that he or she had two <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saints</a> instead of just one. At <a href="/wiki/Confirmation_(sacrament)" class="mw-redirect" title="Confirmation (sacrament)">confirmation</a>, people usually adopt yet another (second or third) Christian name, however, it is never used outside church documents.
</p><p>In Eastern Poland, as in many other Catholic countries, people celebrate <a href="/wiki/Namesdays" class="mw-redirect" title="Namesdays">name days</a> (<i>imieniny</i>) on the day of their patron saint. On the other hand, in Western Poland, <a href="/wiki/Birthday" title="Birthday">birthdays</a> are more popular. Today, in Eastern Poland, birthdays remain relatively intimate celebrations, as often only relatives and close friends know a person's date of birth. Name days, on the other hand, are often celebrated together with co-workers and other less-intimate friends. Information about whose name is associated with a given day can be found in most Polish calendars and on the internet.
</p><p>The choice of a given name is largely influenced by fashion. Many parents name their child after a national hero or heroine, or a character from a book, film, or TV show. In spite of this, a great number of popular names have been in use since the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">Diminutives</a> are popular in everyday usage, and are by no means reserved for children. The Polish language allows for a great deal of creativity in this field. Most diminutives are formed by adding a suffix. For male names it may be <i>-ek</i> or the more affectionate <i>-uś</i>; for female names it may be <i>-ka</i>, or <i>-nia</i> / <i>-dzia</i> / <i>-sia</i> / <i>cia</i> respectively. For example, <a href="/wiki/Maria_(given_name)" title="Maria (given name)">Maria</a> (a name which was once reserved to refer to the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Mary" class="mw-redirect" title="Virgin Mary">Virgin Mary</a>; now the archaic form "Maryja" is used for this), has diminutives Marysia, Maryśka, Marysieńka,  Mania, Mańka, Maniusia, etc.
</p><p>Alternatively, <a href="/wiki/Augmentative" title="Augmentative">augmentative</a> forms (Polish: <i>zgrubienie</i>) may be colloquially used, often with scornful or disdainful intention. For example, Maria may be called Marycha or Marychna.
</p><p>As in many other cultures, a person may informally use a <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nickname</a> (<i>pseudonim, ksywa</i>) or instead of a given name.
</p><p>In 2009, the most popular female names in Poland were Anna, Maria, and Katarzyna (Katherine). The most popular male names were Piotr (Peter), Krzysztof (Christopher), and Andrzej (Andrew).<sup id="cite_ref-20_most_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20_most-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Nazwisko_.28surname.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Nazwisko_(surname)"><i>Nazwisko</i> (surname)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nazwisko (surname)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Polish surnames</b>, like those in most of Europe, are hereditary and generally <a href="/wiki/Patrilineality" title="Patrilineality">patrilineal</a> (passed from the father to his children).
</p><p>A Polish marriage certificate lists three fields, the surnames for the husband, wife, and children. The partners may choose to retain their surnames, or both adopt the surname of either partner, or a combination of both; the children must receive either the joint surname or the surname of one of the partners. However, a married woman usually adopts her husband's name and the children usually bear the surname of the father. The wife may keep her <a href="/wiki/Maiden_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Maiden name">maiden name</a> (<i>nazwisko panieńskie</i>) or add her husband's surname to hers, thus creating a <a href="/wiki/Double-barrelled_name" title="Double-barrelled name">double-barrelled name</a> (<i>nazwisko złożone</i>). However, if she already has a double-barrelled name, she must leave one of the parts out—it is illegal to use a triple- or more-barrelled name. An exception is when one of the surnames is composed of a surname proper plus <a href="/wiki/Agnomen" title="Agnomen">agnomen</a> (<i>przydomek</i>), e.g., <a href="/wiki/Maria_G%C4%85sienica_Daniel-Szatkowska" title="Maria Gąsienica Daniel-Szatkowska">Maria Gąsienica Daniel-Szatkowska</a>, where "<a href="/wiki/G%C4%85sienica" title="Gąsienica">Gąsienica</a> Daniel is her husband's surname. It is also possible, though rare, for the husband to adopt his wife's surname or to add his wife's surname to his family name (an example is businessman <a href="/wiki/Zygmunt_Solorz-%C5%BBak" title="Zygmunt Solorz-Żak">Zygmunt Solorz-Żak</a>, who did both, taking his wife's name on his first marriage, and later appending his second wife's name to it).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The most widespread Polish surnames are <a href="/wiki/Nowak" class="mw-redirect" title="Nowak">Nowak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kowalski" title="Kowalski">Kowalski</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wi%C5%9Bniewski" title="Wiśniewski">Wiśniewski</a> and <a href="/wiki/W%C3%B3jcik" title="Wójcik">Wójcik</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20_most_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20_most-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Suffix_-ski.2F-ska"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Suffix_-ski/-ska">Suffix -ski/-ska</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Suffix -ski/-ska">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Ski" (also "Sky" in other regions) is a formative adjective, from the <a href="/wiki/Proto-Slavic" title="Proto-Slavic">Proto-Slavic</a> "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Slavic/-%D1%8Csk%D1%8A" class="extiw" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Slavic/-ьskъ">ьskъ</a>", which defined affiliation to something. It was also used with names of territories and settlements to denote possession or place of origin.<sup id="cite_ref-SCL_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SCL-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SR_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SR-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The suffix, <i>-ski</i> (feminine: <i>-ska</i>), has been restricted to the nobility in eastern Europe and some parts of central Europe since the High Middle Ages.<sup id="cite_ref-ZKHP_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ZKHP-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It was the equivalent to <a href="/wiki/Nobiliary_particle" title="Nobiliary particle">nobiliary particles</a> appearing in the names of nobility, such as in the Germanic <i><a href="/wiki/Von" title="Von">von</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Nobiliary_particle#Germany_and_Austria" title="Nobiliary particle">zu</a></i>. Almost all surnames borne by the nobility with the <i>-ski</i> (or <i>-sky</i>) suffix are preceded by a place name (toponymic) or other territorial designation derived from their main court, holdings, castle, manor or estate. For example, the Polish nobleman <a href="/wiki/Jan_of_Tarn%C3%B3w" title="Jan of Tarnów">Jan of Tarnów</a> whose name in Polish is "Jan z Tarnowa" was equally known by the name "Jan Tarnowski"; this highlighted his nobility unlike the preposition of "z" alone which could be construed as a regular <a href="/wiki/Preposition_and_postposition" title="Preposition and postposition">prepositional particle</a>.
</p><p>In the 19th century, a wave of seemingly noble sounding surnames began to appear among the <a href="/wiki/Commoner" title="Commoner">common population</a>, where a significant number of the <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">bourgeoisie</a> class, and even the <a href="/wiki/Peasantry" class="mw-redirect" title="Peasantry">peasantry</a>, began to adopt or bear the noble <i>-ski</i> suffix.<sup id="cite_ref-PSTA_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PSTA-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The <i>-ski</i> suffix was thus attached to surnames derived from a person's occupation, characteristics, <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> surnames, or <a href="/wiki/Toponymic_surname" title="Toponymic surname">toponymic surnames</a> (from a person's place of residence, birth or family origin).<sup id="cite_ref-PSTA_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PSTA-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This caused a blur between the <i>-ski</i> bearing territorial toponymic surnames once a characteristic only borne by the nobility. As such, and contrary to a popular modern-day misconception, a person simply bearing the <i>-ski</i> suffix in their family surname or merely sharing the same toponymic surname as members of Poland's nobility, does not in itself denote that person too is a member of the nobility, of noble origin, or indeed connected to that particular family.<sup id="cite_ref-PSTA_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PSTA-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When referring to two or more members of the same family and surname, the suffix <i>-ski</i> is replaced with the plural <i>-skich</i>, <i>-scy</i> or <i>-ccy</i> (plural masculine or both masculine and feminine) as well as <i>-skie</i> or <i>-ckie</i> (plural feminine).<sup id="cite_ref-LEGTIN_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LEGTIN-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i>-ski</i> ending and its derivations are the only ones in Polish that have feminine forms, where women have the feminine version ending in <i>-ska</i> instead. Historically, female versions of surnames were more complex, often formed by adding the suffix <i>-owa</i> for married women and <i>-ówna</i> for unmarried women. In most cases, this practice is now considered archaic or rustic.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="History.2C_heraldry.2C_and_clan_names"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="History,_heraldry,_and_clan_names">History, heraldry, and clan names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History, heraldry, and clan names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Family names first appeared in Poland around the 13th century and were only used by the upper <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">social classes</a> of society. Originally the Polish nobility belonged to <a href="/wiki/Heraldic_family" class="mw-redirect" title="Heraldic family">heraldic clans</a> (Polish <i>ród herbowy</i>) whose names survived in their shared <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coats of arms</a>. Eventually, members of one clan would split into separate families with different surnames, usually derived from the name of their holdings or estates. Sometimes the family name and the clan name (associated with the arms) would be used together and form a double-barrelled name.
</p><p>To explain the formation of a particular Polish nobleman's name, e.g. Jakub Dąbrowski, <a href="/wiki/Radwan_coat_of_arms" title="Radwan coat of arms">Radwan coat of arms</a>, the process might be as follows:  
In Polish <i>dąb</i> means "oak".<sup id="cite_ref-hoffman--william_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoffman--william-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>157</span></sup> <i>Dąbrów</i> means "oak forest" and <i>Dąbrówka</i> means "oak grove".  Then, by analogy with German surnames associated with noble provenance using <a href="/wiki/German_nobility#Nobiliary_particles" title="German nobility">von</a>, the equivalent Polish preposition is, <i><a href="/wiki/German_nobility#Nobiliary_particles" title="German nobility">z</a></i>, which means "from"  followed by the name of the <a href="/wiki/Family_patrimony" title="Family patrimony">patrimony</a> or <a href="/wiki/Estate_(land)" title="Estate (land)">estate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In Polish the expressions, <i>z Dąbrówki</i> and <i>Dąbrowski</i> mean the same thing: hailing "from Dąbrówka".<sup id="cite_ref-hoffman--william_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hoffman--william-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>60</span></sup> More precisely, <i>z Dąbrówki</i> actually means owner of the estate, <i>Dąbrówka</i>, but not necessarily originating from there. <sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-radwan-family-line_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-radwan-family-line-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Thus <i>Jakub z Dąbrówki herbu Radwan</i> translates as "Jacob from Dąbrówka, with the Radwan coat of arms". But with the later addition of his <a href="/wiki/Cognomen" title="Cognomen">cognomen</a> or nickname, <i>Żądło</i>, he would become known as, <i>Jakub z Dąbrówki, Żądło, herbu Radwan</i> - or he could be called just plain, <i>Jakub Żądło</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-boniecki_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boniecki-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-zadlo-cognomen_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zadlo-cognomen-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-zadlo-cognomen_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zadlo-cognomen-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The most striking concept of the Polish heraldic system is that a coat of arms may originate from a single family, but come to be carried by several non-related families of the Polish <i>szlachta</i> (nobility). Unrelated families who have joined the nobility by <a href="/wiki/Heraldic_adoption" title="Heraldic adoption">heraldic adoption</a> can share the same coat of arms, even though that coat of arms bears the surname of the family who created it. Thus the total number of coats of arms in this system was relatively low — about 200 in the late Middle Ages. One side-effect of this unique arrangement was that it became customary to refer to noblemen by both their family name and their coat of arms/clan name. For example: <a href="/wiki/Jan_Zamoyski" title="Jan Zamoyski">Jan Zamoyski</a> <i>herbu</i> <a href="/wiki/Jelita" class="mw-redirect" title="Jelita">Jelita</a> means <i>Jan Zamoyski of the clan Jelita</i>.
</p><p>From the 15th to the 17th century, the formula seems to copy the ancient <a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_convention" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman naming convention">Roman naming convention</a> with the classic <i>tria nomina</i> used by the <a href="/wiki/Patrician_(post-Roman_Europe)" title="Patrician (post-Roman Europe)">Patricians</a>: praenomen (or given name), nomen gentile (or <a href="/wiki/Gens" title="Gens">gens</a>/Clan name) and <a href="/wiki/Cognomen" title="Cognomen">cognomen</a> (surname), following the Renaissance fashion. Thus, <i>Jan Jelita Zamoyski</i>, forming a double-barrelled name (nazwisko złożone). Later, the double-barrelled name would be joined with a hyphen: <i>Jan Jelita-Zamoyski</i>.
</p><p>Example - Jakub: Radwan Żądło-Dąbrowski<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> (sometimes Jakub: Radwan Dąbrowski-Żądło)
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions#Praenomen" title="Roman naming conventions">Praenomen</a></b>
</p><p>Jakub
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions#Nomen" title="Roman naming conventions">Nomen</a></b> (nomen gentile—name of the <a href="/wiki/Gens" title="Gens">gens</a><sup id="cite_ref-radwan-family-line_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-radwan-family-line-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>/<a href="https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/R%C3%B3d" class="extiw" title="pl:Ród">ród</a> or knights' clan):
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Radwan_coat_of_arms" title="Radwan coat of arms">Radwan</a><sup id="cite_ref-radwan_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-radwan-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions#Cognomen" title="Roman naming conventions">Cognomen</a></b> (name of the family branch/<a href="/wiki/Sept" title="Sept">sept</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Radwan_coat_of_arms" title="Radwan coat of arms">Radwan</a> gens):
</p><p>For example—Braniecki, Dąbrowski<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>, Czcikowski, Dostojewski, Górski, Nicki, <a href="/wiki/House_of_Zebrzydowski" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Zebrzydowski"> Zebrzydowski</a>, etc.
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Agnomen" title="Agnomen">Agnomen</a></b> (nickname, Polish <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/przydomek" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:przydomek">wiktionary:przydomek</a>):
</p><p>Żądło (prior to the 17th century, was a <a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions#Cognomen" title="Roman naming conventions">cognomen</a><sup id="cite_ref-zadlo-cognomen_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zadlo-cognomen-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>)
</p><p>Gradually the use of family names spread to other social groups: the <a href="/wiki/Townsfolk" class="mw-redirect" title="Townsfolk">townsfolk</a> (<i>burghers</i>) by the end of the 17th century, then the peasantry, and finally the <a href="/wiki/Jew" class="mw-redirect" title="Jew">Jews</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The process ended only in the mid-19th century.
</p><p>After the First and Second World Wars some <a href="/wiki/Resistance_movement" title="Resistance movement">resistance</a> fighters added their wartime <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym#Nom_de_guerre" title="Pseudonym"><i>noms de guerre</i></a> to their original family names. This was yet another reason for creating double-barrelled names. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Edward_Rydz-%C5%9Amig%C5%82y" title="Edward Rydz-Śmigły">Edward Rydz-Śmigły</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jan_Nowak-Jeziora%C5%84ski" title="Jan Nowak-Jeziorański">Jan Nowak-Jeziorański</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tadeusz_B%C3%B3r-Komorowski" title="Tadeusz Bór-Komorowski">Tadeusz Bór-Komorowski</a>. Some artists, such as <a href="/wiki/Tadeusz_Boy-%C5%BBele%C5%84ski" title="Tadeusz Boy-Żeleński">Tadeusz Boy-Żeleński</a>, also added their <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym#Pseudonyms_in_print" title="Pseudonym"><i>noms de plume</i></a> to their surnames.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Change_of_name">Change of name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Change of name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A Polish citizen may apply to the registry office (<i>Urząd Stanu Cywilnego</i>) with a request for a change of name or surname alongside the payment of a small administrative fee.<sup id="cite_ref-Gazeta_Wrocławska_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gazeta_Wrocławska-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> If the change of surname is not linked to marriage, the family surname is also changed in the successful's applicant's documents. A note is added to the applicant's birth certificate in the system, informing of the subsequent change of name.
</p><p>The request need not be successful. Certain types of request are certain to be refused: for example, the surname of a famous historical figure (where the applicant cannot demonstrate a close family connection to the surname), or where there is concern that the applicant is applying with the aim of evading criminal or civil responsibility.<sup id="cite_ref-Gazeta_Wrocławska_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gazeta_Wrocławska-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>  Every application must give a motivation for the change of name; for example, the existing name being offensive or funny, the desire to revert to a previous name, a close attachment to family members (e.g. parent, step-parent) bearing a different surname, or being commonly known in unofficial contexts by a different name.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Polish_names_in_diasporas">Polish names in diasporas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Polish names in diasporas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="rquote pullquote floatright" role="presentation" style="border-collapse:collapse; border-style:none; float:right; margin:0.5em 0.75em; width:33%;">

<tbody><tr style="text-align:left; vertical-align:top;">
<td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:&#39;Times New Roman&#39;,serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">“
</td>
<td style="padding:0 10px;">The Polish names, of course, are unpronounceable
</td>
<td style="color:#b2b7f2; font-size:3.3em; font-family:&#39;Times New Roman&#39;,serif; font-weight:bold; padding:4px 2px 2px; text-align:right; vertical-align:bottom; width:0.5em; line-height: 1em;">”
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td class="rquotecite" colspan="3" style="padding-top:10px; font-size:smaller; line-height:normal; text-align:right;"><cite>—&#160;Why <a href="/wiki/Polish_Air_Forces_in_France_and_Great_Britain" title="Polish Air Forces in France and Great Britain">Polish RAF pilots</a> received nicknames during World War II<sup id="cite_ref-ingersoll1940_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ingersoll1940-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></cite>
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>When Polish individuals emigrate to countries with different languages and cultures, the often-difficult spelling and pronunciation of Polish names commonly cause them to be misspelled or changed,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> sometimes by transliteration into, for example, <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">Cyrillic</a>.
</p><p>For example, in <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, <i>w</i> is often changed to <i>v</i> and <i>sz</i> to <i>sh</i>. Similar changes occur in <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>. Changes in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> can be even more extreme; a <i>Spiczyński</i> may become simply <i>Spika</i>, for example, where a more rigorous transcription would produce <i>de Spiczyñ</i>.
</p><p>Another typical change is the loss of the gender distinction in adjectival surnames, especially visible for those ending in <i>-ski</i> (fem.: <i>-ska</i>), <i>-cki</i> (fem.: <i>-cka</i>) and <i>-dzki</i> (fem.: <i>-dzka</i>). Western languages do not distinguish between male and female surnames, even if the language has gender-specific adjectives (like German, French or Spanish). As the surname is, in most cases, inherited from the father (or accepted from the husband), the Western registries of birth and marriage ascribe the masculine form (the one ending in <i>-i</i>) to the female members of the family. Slavic countries, in contrast, would use the feminine form of the surname (the one ending in <i>-a</i>). So the form <i>Anna Kowalski</i> would never be met within Poland, whereas it is commonly found in the US, Germany or Argentina.
</p><p>Another change is changing the final vowel <i>-i</i> of the endings <i>-ski</i>, <i>-cki</i> and <i>-dzki</i> into <i>-y</i>. These endings are common in Czech, Slovak and Ukrainian languages, but they never occur in Polish.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">Classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Based on grammatical features, Polish surnames may be divided into:
</p>
<ul><li><i>nominal</i>, derived from and <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">declined</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">noun</a></li>
<li><i>adjectival</i>, derived from and declined as an <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjective</a>.</li></ul>
<p>Adjectival names very often end in the suffixes, <i>-ski</i>, <i>-cki</i> and <i>-dzki</i> (feminine <i>-ska</i>, <i>-cka</i> and <i>-dzka</i>), and are considered to be either typically Polish or typical for the Polish nobility. In the case of '-ski', it holds true if the surname contains the name of a city, town, village or other geographical location.
</p><p>Based on origin, Polish family names may be generally divided into three groups: cognominal, toponymic and patronymic.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cognominal">Cognominal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cognominal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Cognominal_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="Cognominal surname">cognominal surname</a> (<i>nazwisko przezwiskowe</i>) derives from a person's nickname, usually based on his profession, occupation, physical description,  character trait, etc.
</p><p>Examples of <a href="/wiki/Cognominal_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="Cognominal surname">cognominal surnames</a>:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Kowal_(surname)" title="Kowal (surname)">Kowal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kowalski" title="Kowalski">Kowalski</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kowalczyk" title="Kowalczyk">Kowalczyk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kowalewski" title="Kowalewski">Kowalewski</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kowalewicz" class="mw-redirect" title="Kowalewicz">Kowalewicz</a> – from <i>kowal</i> (blacksmith).</li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C5%82ynarz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Młynarz (page does not exist)">Młynarz</a>, <a href="/wiki/M%C5%82ynarski" title="Młynarski">Młynarski</a>, <a href="/wiki/M%C5%82ynarczyk" title="Młynarczyk">Młynarczyk</a> – from <i>młynarz</i> (miller).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nowak" class="mw-redirect" title="Nowak">Nowak</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nowakowski" title="Nowakowski">Nowakowski</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nowicki" title="Nowicki">Nowicki</a> – from <i>nowy</i> (the new one). (Nowak is the most popular Polish surname.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lis_(surname)" title="Lis (surname)">Lis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lisiewicz" class="mw-redirect" title="Lisiewicz">Lisiewicz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lisowski" title="Lisowski">Lisowski</a> – from <i>lis</i> (fox).</li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Toponymic">Toponymic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Toponymic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Toponymic_surname" title="Toponymic surname">Toponymic surnames</a> (<i>nazwisko odmiejscowe</i>) usually derive from the name of a village or town, or the name of a topographic feature. These names are almost always of the adjectival form. Originally they referred to the village owner (lord). In the 19th century, however, surnames were often taken from the name of a person's town.<sup id="cite_ref-PSTA_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PSTA-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Patronymic">Patronymic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Patronymic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> surname (<i>nazwisko odimienne</i>) derives from the given name of a person, and usually ends in a suffix suggesting a family relation.
</p><p>Examples of <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> surnames:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Adamczyk" title="Adamczyk">Adamczyk</a>—derived from Adam</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/%C5%81ukaszewicz" title="Łukaszewicz">Łukaszewicz</a>—derived from Łukasz (Luke).</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>There is a class of surnames derived from past tense participles. These names usually have the formally feminine (-ła) or neutral (-ło) ending of the (ancient, now obsolete) gender-neutral active past participle, meaning "the one who has ...[come, applied, accomplished, settled, searched, found, etc.]". <a href="/wiki/Domaga%C5%82a" title="Domagała">Domagała</a>, <a href="/wiki/Przyby%C5%82a" title="Przybyła">Przybyła</a>, <a href="/wiki/Napiera%C5%82a" title="Napierała">Napierała</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dopiera%C5%82a" class="mw-redirect" title="Dopierała">Dopierała</a>, <a href="/wiki/Szuka%C5%82a" title="Szukała">Szukała</a> or <a href="/wiki/Podsiad%C5%82o" title="Podsiadło">Podsiadło</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wcis%C5%82o" title="Wcisło">Wcisło</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wlaz%C5%82o" class="mw-redirect" title="Wlazło">Wlazło</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Przyby%C5%82o&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Przybyło (page does not exist)">Przybyło</a> are examples of these names. A smaller number of surnames use the masculine ending, for example, <a href="/wiki/Musia%C5%82" title="Musiał">Musiał</a> or Niechciał.</li></ul>
<ul><li>Another class of surnames uses the latin <a href="/wiki/Disjunct_(linguistics)" title="Disjunct (linguistics)">disjunct</a> <i>vel</i>. This arises when an ancestor was known by a given family name and under an <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">alias</a>. A case in point was when a soldier took part in an <a href="/wiki/Polish_uprising" class="mw-redirect" title="Polish uprising">uprising</a> and then pursued by the authorities, assumed another name. Subsequently, rather than lose one of his identities, he merged them using the disjunct, <i>vel</i> to indicate he was known under one <i>or</i> other name. For example, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Przemys%C5%82aw_%C5%BBurawski_vel_Grajewski&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Przemysław Żurawski vel Grajewski (page does not exist)">Przemysław Żurawski vel Grajewski</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;&#91;<a href="https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Przemys%C5%82aw_%C5%BBurawski_vel_Grajewski" class="extiw" title="pl:Przemysław Żurawski vel Grajewski">pl</a>&#93;</span>. The family then kept the form.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Feminine_forms">Feminine forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Feminine forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Adjectival surnames, like all Polish adjectives, have <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">masculine and feminine</a> forms. If a masculine surname ends in <i>-i</i> or <i>-y</i>; its feminine equivalent ends in <i>-a</i>.
</p><p>The feminine form is not just a common usage form, it is also the form of the surname that appears in all official records, such as birth, death and marriage certificates, identity cards, and passports. A female first name coupled with a male surname or vice versa sounds incongruous and wrong to the Polish ear.
</p><p>Surnames ending with consonants usually have no additional feminine form. In the past, when the masculine form ended in a consonant, the feminine surname could have been derived by adding the suffix, <i>-owa,</i> for married women, indicating ownership and the suffix <i>-ówna</i> for maiden surname, indicating fatherhood.<sup id="cite_ref-php_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-php-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Cezaria_Baudouin_de_Courtenay" class="mw-redirect" title="Cezaria Baudouin de Courtenay">Cezaria Baudouin de Courtenay</a>, after her marriage to <a href="/wiki/Janusz_J%C4%99drzejewicz" title="Janusz Jędrzejewicz">Janusz Jędrzejewicz</a>, was named <a href="/wiki/Cezaria_Baudouin_de_Courtenay_Ehrenkreutz_J%C4%99drzejewiczowa" class="mw-redirect" title="Cezaria Baudouin de Courtenay Ehrenkreutz Jędrzejewiczowa">Cezaria Baudouin de Courtenay Ehrenkreutz Jędrzejewiczowa</a>. The unmarried daughter of Jędrzejewicz would have the official surname Jędrzejewiczówna. In modern times, Jędrzejewicz may be both a masculine and a feminine surname.
</p><p>Exceptions from the above (archaic) rule are surnames ending in <i>-g</i> or syllables starting with '-g': in this case the unmarried feminine form would use the suffix <i>-żanska</i>: <a href="/wiki/Fertig_(surname)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fertig (surname)">Fertig</a>-&gt;Fertiżanska, <a href="/wiki/Szeliga_(surname)" title="Szeliga (surname)">Szeliga</a>-&gt;Szeliżanska.<sup id="cite_ref-php_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-php-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neutral_form">Neutral form</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Neutral form">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The neutral form ("rodzaj nijaki") may be used for neutral gender. For example, when talking about a child of the neighbours one may say "To małe Kowalskie jest bardzo spokojnym dzieckiem" ("That Kowalski little one is a very quiet child"), or in plural: "Wasilewskie wyjechały do babci" ("The Wasilewskis children went away to see their grandma"). Unlike the feminine form, this form is never used in official documents; it is an informal form used mostly in spoken language.
</p>

<p>Nominal surnames may or may not change with gender. Like other Slavic languages, Polish has special feminine suffixes which were added to a woman's surname. A woman who was never married used her father's surname with the suffix <i>-ówna</i> or <i>-'anka</i>. A married woman or a widow used her husband's surname with the suffix <i>-owa</i> or <i>-'ina</i> / <i>-'yna</i> (the apostrophe means that the last consonant in the base form of the surname is softened). Although these suffixes are still used by some people, mostly the elderly and in rural areas, they are now becoming outdated and there is a tendency to use the same form of a nominal surname for both a man and a woman. Furthermore, the forms "-anka" and "-ina/-yna" are going out of fashion and being replaced by "-ówna" and "-owa" respectively.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formal_and_informal_use">Formal and informal use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Formal and informal use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/T-V_distinction#Polish" class="mw-redirect" title="T-V distinction">T-V distinction in Polish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Honorifics_(linguistics)#Polish" title="Honorifics (linguistics)">Honorifics in Polish</a></div>
<p>Poles pay great attention to the correct way of referring to, or addressing other people, depending on the level of social distance, familiarity and politeness. The differences between formal and informal language include:
</p>
<ul><li>using surnames or given names</li>
<li>using or not using honorific titles, such as <i>Pan</i> / <i>Pani</i> / <i>Państwo</i>;</li>
<li>using the third person singular (formal) or the second person singular (informal) forms.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formal_language">Formal language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Formal language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="Pan_.2F_Pani_.2F_Pa.C5.84stwo"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Pan_/_Pani_/_Państwo"><i>Pan</i> / <i>Pani</i> / <i>Państwo</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Pan / Pani / Państwo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><i>Pan</i> and <i>Pani</i> are the basic honorific styles used in Polish to refer to a man or woman, respectively. In the past, these styles were reserved for hereditary nobles, and played more or less the same role as "Lord" or "Sir" and "Lady" or "Madame" in English. Since the 19th century, they have come to be used in all strata of society and may be considered equivalent to the English "Mr." and "Ms." or the Japanese "san" suffix, while nobles would be addressed "Jego/Jej Miłość Pan/Pani" (His/Her Grace Lord/Lady). There used to be a separate style, <i>Panna</i> ("Miss"), applied to unmarried women, but this is now outdated and mostly replaced by <i>Pani</i>.
</p><p>"Państwo" is widely used when referring to a married couple (instead of using <i>Pan</i> and <i>Pani</i>) or even a whole family.
</p><p>Examples:
</p>
<ul><li>Pan Kowalski + Pani Kowalska = Państwo Kowalscy</li>
<li>Pan Nowak + Pani Nowak = Państwo Nowakowie</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Titles">Titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>When addressing people, scientific and other titles are always used together with "Pan" and "Pani" and the name itself is dropped. However, when a person is spoken of but not addressed directly, then both the title and the name are used and the words "Pan"/"Pani" are often omitted.
</p><p>Examples:
</p>
<ul><li>"Panie profesorze" ("Professor"), "Pan profesor powiedział" ("Professor (X) said" or "you have said, professor")</li>
<li>"Pani doktor" ("Doctor"), "Pani doktor powiedziała" ("Doctor (X) said" or "you have said, doctor")</li></ul>
<p>but:
</p>
<ul><li>"Pan profesor Jan Nowak" or "profesor Jan Nowak" or "profesor Nowak",</li>
<li>"Pani doktor Maria Kowalska" or "doktor Maria Kowalska" or "doktor Kowalska"</li></ul>
<h4><span id="Given_name_.2F_surname_order"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Given_name_/_surname_order">Given name / surname order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Given name / surname order">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The given name(s) normally comes before the surname. However, in a list of people sorted alphabetically by surname, the surname usually comes first. Hence some people may also use this order in spoken language (e.g. introducing themselves as <i>Kowalski Jan</i> instead of <i>Jan Kowalski</i>), but this is generally considered incorrect or a throwback to the Communist era when this order was sometimes heard in official situations. In many formal situations, the given name is omitted altogether.
</p><p>Examples:
</p>
<ul><li>Pan Włodzimierz Malinowski</li>
<li>Pani Jadwiga Kwiatkowska</li></ul>
<p>On the other hand, it is not common to refer to public figures, while not addressing them, with "Pan" or "Pani". This is true for politicians, artists, and athletes.
</p>
<ul><li>"Jan Kowalski był dziś w Gdańsku." ("Jan Kowalski was in Gdansk today") and not "Pan Jan Kowalski był dziś w Gdańsku."</li>
<li>"Jan Kowalski uważa, że" or "Minister Kowalski uważa, że" ("Mr Kowalski maintains that") is better than "Pan Kowalski uważa, że".</li>
<li>"Film reżyserował Jan Kowalski." ("The film was directed by Jan Kowalski.") and not: "Film reżyserował pan Jan Kowalski."</li>
<li>"Złoty medal zdobyła Anna Kowalska." ("The gold medal was won by Anna Kowalska.") and never: "Złoty medal zdobyła pani Anna Kowalska."</li></ul>
<p>In such circumstances, preceding a name with "Pan" or "Pani" would usually be seen as being ironical.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Semi-formal_levels_of_address">Semi-formal levels of address</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Semi-formal levels of address">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In situations of frequent contact, for example, at work, people who do not change their status from formal into familiar, may remain for years at a semi-formal level, using the formal "Pan"/"Pani" form followed by the given name. This way of calling people is used not only when addressing them but also when referring to them to a third person with whom one remains at the same level of semi-formal contact.
</p><p>In a situation where two people do not have the same status, for example an employer and employee, a subordinate person is addressed by his or her given name by their superior, but the subordinate never uses the given name of the superior, using his or her title instead.
</p>
<ul><li>the superior to a subordinate: "Panie Włodzimierzu!", "Pani Jadwigo!";</li>
<li>a subordinate to the superior: "Panie Dyrektorze!" (literally: "Mr Principal!"), "Pani Kierownik!" (literally: "Mrs Manager!").</li></ul>
<p>This style is to a certain degree similar to the <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_name" title="Vietnamese name">Vietnamese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_name" title="Japanese name">Japanese</a> or <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_name" title="Icelandic name">Icelandic</a> usage.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>NB. If the superior wants to behave more politely or show his or her friendly attitude towards the subordinate, a diminutive form of the given name may be used: "Panie Włodku!", "Pani Jadziu!". This, however, is usually not practised when the subordinate is much older than the superior, as it may be felt by the subordinate as being overly patronised by his/her superior.
</p><p>It is rude to call a person by his/hers surname in the presence of unknown people. In a random crowd, a person calling another person should use a form of "Proszę Pana/Pani" ("I'm asking you, Sir/Madam") or use the semi-formal form with first name, like "Panie Włodzimierzu" ("Mr. Włodzimierz"). This comes from a general rule that one has the right to be anonymous in a crowd of unknown people; this rule is observed in most countries of Western culture. To disclose one's given name does not fall under this rule, as many people can be named Włodzimierz for instance.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Semi-informal_and_informal_language">Semi-informal and informal language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Polish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Semi-informal and informal language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Informal forms of address are normally used only by relatives, close friends and co-workers. In such situations diminutives are generally preferred to the standard forms of given names. At an intermediate level of familiarity (for example, among co-workers) a diminutive given name may be preceded by formal the <i>Pan</i> or <i>Pani</i> (semi-informal form of address).
</p>
<ul><li>Pan Włodek (but also standard semi-formal form "Pan Włodzimierz") - in direct address "Panie Włodku" (standard: "Panie Włodzimierzu")</li>
<li>Pani Jadzia (but also "Pani Jadwiga") - in direct address "Pani Jadziu" (standard: "Pani Jadwigo")</li></ul>
<p>Using the honorific style with a surname only, if used to refer to a given person directly, is generally perceived as rude. In such case, it is more polite to use just the form "Pan", without given or family name.
</p><p>It is very rude to address someone whom one does not know well without using "Pan" or "Pani", and with the second person singular instead of the polite third person singular pronouns and verb forms. Traditionally, the act of moving from this form to a friendly "you" must be acknowledged by both parties and it is usually a mark of a close friendly relationship between the two people. The change can only be proposed by the older or more respected person; a similar suggestion initiated by the younger or less respected person will usually be perceived as presumptuous and arrogant.
</p><p>There is a clear distinction between "friends" and "colleagues". For example, co-workers will rarely be referred to as friends, but often as colleagues. Being a colleague in Polish means that people share their time or aims to some extent. To be considered a "friend", they have to feel a closer relation, such as sharing secrets or feeling that they can depend on one another. Thus "przyjaciel" ("friend") in Polish has a narrower meaning than its counterpart in English.
</p><p>There is yet another type of relation – "znajomy" which means "acquaintance"; this is distinct from "kolega" (a colleague) in that it implies a social, not professional, relationship.
</p><p>It is not uncommon to use a half-informal title with the name omitted. This is, however, usually found only in the vocative case: "Panie Kolego!" (much less common: "Pani Koleżanko!") which literally means "Mr. Mate!".
</p>
