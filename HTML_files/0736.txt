<p><b>House of Fraser</b> is a British <a href="/wiki/Department_store" title="Department store">department store</a> group with 54 stores across the United Kingdom and Ireland. It was established in <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>, Scotland in 1849 as <b>Arthur and Fraser</b>. By 1891, it was known as <b>Fraser &amp; Sons</b>. The company grew steadily during the early 20th century, and after the Second World War a large number of acquisitions transformed the company into a national chain.
</p><p>From 1936 onwards the company expanded substantially through acquisitions, including <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Drapery_Corporation" title="Scottish Drapery Corporation">Scottish Drapery Corporation</a> (1952), <a href="/wiki/Binns_(department_store)" title="Binns (department store)">Binns</a> (1953), <a href="/wiki/Barkers_of_Kensington" title="Barkers of Kensington">Barkers of Kensington</a> (1957), and <a href="/wiki/Dickins_%26_Jones" title="Dickins &amp; Jones">Dickins &amp; Jones</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Harrods" title="Harrods">Harrods</a> group (1959). In 1948, the company was first listed on the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a>. Later acquisitions included <a href="/wiki/Howells_(department_store)" title="Howells (department store)">Howells</a> (1972) and <a href="/wiki/Army_%26_Navy_Stores_(United_Kingdom)" title="Army &amp; Navy Stores (United Kingdom)">Army &amp; Navy Stores</a> (1973).
</p><p>Ownership of the group passed to the <a href="/wiki/Al_Fayed" class="mw-redirect" title="Al Fayed">Al Fayed</a> family in 1985 (£615million), and in 1995 it was listed in the <a href="/wiki/FTSE_Group" title="FTSE Group">FTSE Index</a> as House of Fraser plc, with Harrods moved into the private ownership of the Al Fayeds. In the 1990s several stores were closed and fifteen stores transferred to a joint venture with <a href="/wiki/British_Land_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="British Land Company">British Land Company</a>, which then continued operating under their old name. The former Harrod group store <a href="/wiki/D_H_Evans" title="D H Evans">D H Evans</a> on Oxford Street, London was re-branded as House of Fraser in 2001 and became the chain's flagship store.
</p><p>In 2005, the group acquired <a href="/wiki/Jenners" title="Jenners">Jenners</a> (£46m), and <a href="/wiki/Beatties" title="Beatties">Beatties</a> (£69m). In 2006, group was acquired by a consortium of investors (Highland Group Holdings) including Icelandic based <a href="/wiki/Landsbanki" title="Landsbanki">Landsbanki</a> (35%). An online store was launched in 2007. In 2014 the group (as Highland Group Holdings Ltd) was sold to Nanjing Xinjiekou Department Store Co. (Sanpower Group), a leading chain of Chinese department stores for approximately £480 million.
</p><p>In May 2018, the group entered a <a href="/wiki/Company_voluntary_arrangement" title="Company voluntary arrangement">company voluntary arrangement</a>, and in June the closure of 31 stores was announced. On 10 August 2018 <a href="/wiki/Mike_Ashley_(businessman)" title="Mike Ashley (businessman)">Mike Ashley</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Sports_Direct" title="Sports Direct">Sports Direct</a> chain agreed to buy the business (stores, stock, brand) for £90 million after the chain went into administration earlier that day.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_early_years">The early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_of_Fraser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The company was founded by <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Fraser_(retailer)" title="Hugh Fraser (retailer)">Hugh Fraser</a> and James Arthur in 1849 as a small drapery shop on the corner of <a href="/wiki/Argyle_Street,_Glasgow" title="Argyle Street, Glasgow">Argyle Street</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buchanan_Street" title="Buchanan Street">Buchanan Street</a> in Glasgow, Scotland trading as Arthur and Fraser.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hugh Fraser had been apprenticed to Stewart &amp; McDonald Ltd, a <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> <a href="/wiki/Draper" title="Draper">drapery</a> warehouse where he rose to the position of warehouse manager and from where he brought many of initial customers.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>James Arthur also owned a retail drapery business in <a href="/wiki/Paisley,_Renfrewshire" title="Paisley, Renfrewshire">Paisley</a>, near Glasgow: he appointed a manager to oversee the Paisley business while he focused on his new business.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The company established a <a href="/wiki/Wholesale" class="mw-redirect" title="Wholesale">wholesale</a> trade in adjoining premises in Argyle Street. In 1856 the wholesale business moved to a larger site in Miller Street, <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a> and started to trade under the name Arthur &amp; Co. The retail side of the business expanded into the vacant buildings left by the wholesale side.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the late 1850s and early 1860s the retail business was run by a professional manager – first Thomas Kirkpatrick and then Alexander McLaren.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 1865 the partnership between the partners was dissolved and Fraser assumed control of the retail business leaving Arthur with the wholesale business. In 1865 McLaren joined the retail business and the name was changed to Fraser &amp; McLaren.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Fraser_.26_Sons"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Fraser_&amp;_Sons">Fraser &amp; Sons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_of_Fraser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fraser &amp; Sons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When the first Hugh Fraser died in 1873, his three eldest sons, James, John and Hugh, acquired stakes in the business. James and John Fraser were initially directors in the business and employed Alexander McLaren and later John Towers to manage it for them. In 1891 Hugh also joined the partnership which by then was called Fraser &amp; Sons.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1879, the current flagship store on Oxford Street in London was opened by Dan Harries Evans, a 23-year-old from Whitemill in Carmarthenshire, Wales who had previously been apprenticed to a <a href="/wiki/Draper" title="Draper">draper</a> in Forest Hamlet near <a href="/wiki/Merthyr_Tydfil" title="Merthyr Tydfil">Merthyr Tydfil</a>, Wales. He moved to London in 1878 to set up his own business in <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Bridge_Road" title="Westminster Bridge Road">Westminster Bridge Road</a>. The store traded under the <a href="/wiki/D_H_Evans" title="D H Evans">D H Evans</a> name until 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By 1900, Hugh Fraser II was in charge: he incorporated the business as Fraser &amp; Sons Ltd in 1909 and introduced the famous stag's head motif.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After Hugh Fraser II died in 1927, his son <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Fraser,_1st_Baron_Fraser_of_Allander" title="Hugh Fraser, 1st Baron Fraser of Allander">Hugh Fraser III</a>, an accountant, became Chairman of the business.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He opened new departments, enlarged the tearoom, opened a restaurant and also began to look at possible acquisitions.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 1936 he purchased Arnott &amp; Co Ltd and its neighbour Robert Simpson &amp; Sons Ltd in nearby Argyle Street, merging the companies to help improve trade.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 1948 the Company, now named House of Fraser, was first listed on the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1950s_to_1970s">1950s to 1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_of_Fraser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1950s to 1970s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kendal_Milne%27s_Deansgate.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Kendal_Milne%27s_Deansgate.jpg/220px-Kendal_Milne%27s_Deansgate.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="190" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Kendal_Milne%27s_Deansgate.jpg/330px-Kendal_Milne%27s_Deansgate.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Kendal_Milne%27s_Deansgate.jpg/440px-Kendal_Milne%27s_Deansgate.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="553" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kendal_Milne%27s_Deansgate.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Art_Deco" title="Art Deco">Art Deco</a> <a href="/wiki/Kendals" title="Kendals">Kendals</a> building on <a href="/wiki/Deansgate" title="Deansgate">Deansgate</a>, Manchester, England – a House of Fraser store since 1959</div></div></div>
<p>In 1951, the Company purchased McDonald's Ltd, and with it a branch in <a href="/wiki/Harrogate" title="Harrogate">Harrogate</a>. Fraser then purchased the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Drapery_Corporation" title="Scottish Drapery Corporation">Scottish Drapery Corporation</a> in 1952, followed by the <a href="/wiki/Sunderland,_Tyne_and_Wear" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunderland, Tyne and Wear">Sunderland</a> based <a href="/wiki/Binns_(department_store)" title="Binns (department store)">Binns</a> group of stores in 1953.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Fraser sold the property sites to insurance companies, leasing them back for long terms at advantageous rates. This enabled the release of capital for the purchase of new premises and the modernisation of existing stores. In 1957 the <a href="/wiki/Kensington" title="Kensington">Kensington</a> store group of <a href="/wiki/Barkers_of_Kensington" title="Barkers of Kensington">John Barker &amp; Co Ltd</a> was acquired and in 1959 <a href="/wiki/Harrods" title="Harrods">Harrods</a><sup id="cite_ref-history_1-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Dickins_%26_Jones" title="Dickins &amp; Jones">Dickins &amp; Jones</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> also joined the Group.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sir_Hugh_Fraser,_2nd_Baronet" title="Sir Hugh Fraser, 2nd Baronet">Sir Hugh Fraser</a> succeeded his father as Chairman of the company when his father died in 1966.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Sir Hugh resumed the expansion of the company in 1969 with the takeover of <a href="/wiki/J_J_Allen_(department_store)" class="mw-redirect" title="J J Allen (department store)">J. J. Allen Ltd</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Bournemouth" title="Bournemouth">Bournemouth</a> based group.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the 1970s, the House of Fraser Group acquired more companies including: T. Baird &amp; Sons Ltd of Scotland, Switzer &amp; Co. Ltd of Dublin, Ireland, and <a href="/wiki/Dingles_(department_store)" title="Dingles (department store)">E. Dingle &amp; Co. Ltd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chiesmans_(department_store)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chiesmans (department store)">Chiesmans Ltd</a>, Hide &amp; Co and the <a href="/wiki/Army_%26_Navy_Stores_(United_Kingdom)" title="Army &amp; Navy Stores (United Kingdom)">Army &amp; Navy Stores</a> in southern England, as well as a number of independent stores, totaling over fifty stores during the decade.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 1973 the House of Fraser Group was considering merging with the British pharmacy company <a href="/wiki/Boots_UK" title="Boots UK">Boots</a>, and was even subject to a written answer in the House of Commons.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The government decided to ban the proposed merger in 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980-1985">1980-1985</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_of_Fraser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1980-1985">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Former_House_of_Fraser_logo.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Former House of Fraser logo with jumping stag and F shadow." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d6/Former_House_of_Fraser_logo.jpg/220px-Former_House_of_Fraser_logo.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="132" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d6/Former_House_of_Fraser_logo.jpg/330px-Former_House_of_Fraser_logo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/d6/Former_House_of_Fraser_logo.jpg 2x" data-file-width="407" data-file-height="245" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Former_House_of_Fraser_logo.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Former House of Fraser logo with jumping stag and "F" shadow</div></div></div>
<p>In 1981, Prof. Roland Smith succeeded Sir Hugh Fraser as chairman. A takeover bid by <a href="/wiki/Lonrho" title="Lonrho">Lonrho</a> was referred to the <a href="/wiki/Monopolies_and_Mergers_Commission" class="mw-redirect" title="Monopolies and Mergers Commission">Monopolies and Mergers Commission</a> and declared to be contrary to the public interest.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Four new stores opened between 1980 and 1984,<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> including D H Evans in Wood Green, North London in 1980, Dickins &amp; Jones in Milton Keynes in 1981, Frasers in Perth in April 1984, and Dickins &amp; Jones in Epsom, Surrey in May 1984.
</p><p>The company, by then House of Fraser PLC, diversified into sports goods under the name of Astral Sports and Leisure (subsequently sold to <a href="/wiki/Sears_plc" title="Sears plc">Sears plc</a>, owned Olympus Sport division) and into funerals with Wylie &amp; Lochhead. It also launched the 'You' range of cosmetics and jewellery shops, and in 1985 acquired Turnbull &amp; Asser Holdings Ltd, shirt makers of <a href="/wiki/Jermyn_Street" title="Jermyn Street">Jermyn Street</a>, London and Kurt Geiger Holdings Ltd, shoe retailers.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Other developments during the 1980s included the introduction of "Lifestyle" merchandise ranges and a huge investment in store refurbishment nationwide. In 1983 the Company introduced the Frasercard (later renamed Recognition), valid at all stores and administered from a central facility based in <a href="/wiki/Swindon" title="Swindon">Swindon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1985-2006:_Al_Fayed_ownership">1985-2006: Al Fayed ownership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_of_Fraser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1985-2006: Al Fayed ownership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1985, the <a href="/wiki/Al_Fayed" class="mw-redirect" title="Al Fayed">Al Fayed</a> family bought the business for £615 million. The Al Fayeds supported the continuing expansion of the Company and replaced the stag's head logo with a stag leaping from a green triangle with shop signs of this period using a double layered san serif typeface.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wikimedia1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wikimedia1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 1988, a five-year strategic business plan was announced which saw a rationalisation of stores. Small branches were to be relinquished and replaced with larger units.
</p><p>In September 1990, two new department stores were opened, a House of Fraser in the <a href="/wiki/Meadowhall_Shopping_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Meadowhall Shopping Centre">Meadowhall Shopping Centre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>, and Schofields in Leeds. In 1991, a new House of Fraser store was opened at the Lakeside Shopping Centre in West Thurrock, Essex.
</p><p>In 1994, before House of Fraser PLC was relisted on the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a>, Harrods was moved out of the Group so that it could remain under the private ownership of the Al Fayed family.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> John Coleman, who was appointed chief executive of the House of Fraser Group in 1996, launched the Linea brand in 1997, along with Platinum and Fraser the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The House of Fraser logo was revised in 1996 with the leaping stag now going over an "F" shadow and shop signs using a serif typeface.<sup id="cite_ref-wikimedia1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wikimedia1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> There were many store closures in this period which included the closure or selling off of branches in locations including Sheffield (House of Fraser), Newcastle (Binns), Sunderland (Binns), Bradford (Rackhams) and Leeds (Schofields which had closed only six years after opening although House of Fraser continued to have a presence with their Rackhams (now House of Fraser store) in the city) with the loss of around 1,000 jobs.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>House of Fraser set up BL Fraser, a 50–50 joint venture with the <a href="/wiki/British_Land_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="British Land Company">British Land Company</a>, in 1999 to buy 15 House of Fraser stores that would continue to be operated by House of Fraser.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The Company added to its private-label brands in 2000 with House of Fraser womenswear, The Collection menswear, and a Linea Home.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2003, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hunter" title="Tom Hunter">Tom Hunter</a> put forward a hostile bid for the Group, with the possible intention to merge with <a href="/wiki/Allders" title="Allders">Allders</a>, another department store he had shareholdings in.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In addition there was a large reduction in the number of House of Fraser stores in Scotland which included the sell off or closure of branches in Aberdeen (Frasers), Dundee (Arnotts), Inverness (Frasers), Paisley (Arnotts) and Perth (Frasers).
</p><p>2005 was a year of growth for House of Fraser with the acquisition of the four <a href="/wiki/Jenners" title="Jenners">Jenners</a> department stores in April for £46m,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Beatties" title="Beatties">Beatties</a>, a mainly <a href="/wiki/English_Midlands" class="mw-redirect" title="English Midlands">Midlands</a> based department store chain of 12 sites, for £69.3m in the summer of 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In addition to buying companies, House of Fraser continued its own development programme and opened several more stores including its first store outside the UK in <a href="/wiki/Dundrum_Town_Centre" title="Dundrum Town Centre">Dundrum Town Centre</a>, Dublin, Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> as well as stores in Maidstone and Norwich.
</p><p>In 2006, the Company consolidated its portfolio by closing the 135-year-old Barkers business in <a href="/wiki/Kensington_High_Street" title="Kensington High Street">Kensington High Street</a> on 2 January 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and on 14 January 2006, closed its <a href="/wiki/Dickins_%26_Jones" title="Dickins &amp; Jones">Dickins &amp; Jones</a> store in London's Regent Street following a substantial rent increase.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In addition, the Company closed its <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> Beatties store in January 2006 (although retained the House of Fraser store in Birmingham).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2006-2014:_Highland_Group_Holdings">2006-2014: Highland Group Holdings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_of_Fraser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2006-2014: Highland Group Holdings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 2006, the Group announced that it had received a preliminary bid approach valuing it at £300 million, and in August 2006 House of Fraser confirmed a takeover approach from the Highland consortium who acquired the Company for £351.4 million in November 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Highland Group Holdings Limited was 35% owned by <a href="/wiki/Landsbanki" title="Landsbanki">Landsbanki</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>As part of the Highland takeover all brand names for their stores, including most of the Beatties branches, will be replaced with the House of Fraser name (with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Jenners" title="Jenners">Jenners</a>) with the stag logo axed and a new san serif typeface used on shop signs.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In September 2007, House of Fraser launched its online store.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Company had four major openings in 2008, including its first store in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> in the newly built <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Square_Shopping_Centre" title="Victoria Square Shopping Centre">Victoria Square Shopping Centre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a> in March. At 120,000&#160;sq&#160;ft (11,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) it was the largest store that House of Fraser had opened (as opposed to taken over) in the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Also in March 2008, the Company opened a 95,000&#160;sq&#160;ft (8,800&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) store in High Wycombe. On 25 September 2008 the Company opened a 100,000&#160;sq&#160;ft (9,300&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) store in the <a href="/wiki/Cabot_Circus" title="Cabot Circus">Cabot Circus</a> development in <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and a branch in <a href="/wiki/Westfield_London" title="Westfield London">Westfield London</a>, a new 70,000&#160;sq&#160;ft (6,500&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) store, on 30 October 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>House of Fraser launched the HouseofFraser.com "Buy &amp; Collect" concept shop in October 2011 with its first location in Aberdeen. A further site, in Liverpool, opened in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> These small shops were equipped with computer terminals to allow customers to order from the House of Fraser website. Both shops had closed by the summer of 2016.
</p><p>In December 2013, talks to takeover House of Fraser were held by French department store <a href="/wiki/Galeries_Lafayette" title="Galeries Lafayette">Galeries Lafayette</a> with House of Fraser also exploring a floating on the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a> once more in the summer of 2014 if the takeover was to be abandoned.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2014-2018:_Sanpower_Group_ownership">2014-2018: Sanpower Group ownership</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_of_Fraser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2014-2018: Sanpower Group ownership">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In April 2014, it was reported by the BBC that House of Fraser would be sold to Chinese conglomerate Sanpower Group, who would obtain 89% share in the company which would value the business at about £450 million.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Nanjing Xinjiekou Department Store Co will buy an 89% stake in Highland Group Holdings Ltd, which owns House of Fraser.<sup id="cite_ref-China&#39;s_Nanjing_Xinjiekou_to_Buy_Stake_in_House_of_Fraser_Owner_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-China&#39;s_Nanjing_Xinjiekou_to_Buy_Stake_in_House_of_Fraser_Owner-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The purchase is worth £450 million.<sup id="cite_ref-China&#39;s_Nanjing_Xinjiekou_to_Buy_Stake_in_House_of_Fraser_Owner_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-China&#39;s_Nanjing_Xinjiekou_to_Buy_Stake_in_House_of_Fraser_Owner-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Sanpower Group is a 22 percent shareholder of the Nanjing Xinjiekou Department Store Co.<sup id="cite_ref-China&#39;s_Nanjing_Xinjiekou_to_Buy_Stake_in_House_of_Fraser_Owner_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-China&#39;s_Nanjing_Xinjiekou_to_Buy_Stake_in_House_of_Fraser_Owner-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> On 2 September 2014 Don McCarthy, retiring Executive Chairman of House of Fraser, announced the completion of the sale of 100% of the preferred ordinary shares and B ordinary shares, and approximately 89% of the A ordinary shares and preference shares of Highland Group Holdings Ltd, to Nanjing Xinjiekou Department Store Co, a leading chain of Chinese department stores and part of the Sanpower Group, for an enterprise value of approximately £480 million.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>In 2017, a new department store opened at the Rushden Lakes development in <a href="/wiki/Rushden" title="Rushden">Rushden, Northamptonshire</a>. The closure of House of Fraser Outlet in Leicester also took place during the year and a further closure, in Aylesbury, was announced for 2018, however, this never materialised following the acquisition by <a href="/wiki/Sports_Direct_International" class="mw-redirect" title="Sports Direct International">Sports Direct International</a>. A new store in Chester was announced in February 2017 with construction due to start in mid-2018. It was announced later in 2018 that House of Fraser had pulled out of these plans due to their financial issues.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2018:_Administration">2018: Administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_of_Fraser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2018: Administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 2 May 2018, the company announced that it was to be entering into a conditional sale of a controlling stake in the firm to Nanjing Cenbest (another Sanpower Group subsidiary) to <a href="/wiki/Hamleys" title="Hamleys">Hamleys</a> owner <a href="/wiki/C.banner" title="C.banner">C.banner</a>, another Chinese firm. A condition of the sale is that the company streamline its existing store portfolio and cost base. The intention to launch a <a href="/wiki/Company_voluntary_arrangement" title="Company voluntary arrangement">company voluntary arrangement</a> (CVA) was announced on the same day.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> However, C.banner later pulled out.<sup id="cite_ref-closures31_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-closures31-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Datitude_080618_Location_of_House_of_Fraser_UK_Store_Closures.png" class="image"><img alt="Location of closing House of Fraser stores" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Datitude_080618_Location_of_House_of_Fraser_UK_Store_Closures.png/220px-Datitude_080618_Location_of_House_of_Fraser_UK_Store_Closures.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="135" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Datitude_080618_Location_of_House_of_Fraser_UK_Store_Closures.png/330px-Datitude_080618_Location_of_House_of_Fraser_UK_Store_Closures.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Datitude_080618_Location_of_House_of_Fraser_UK_Store_Closures.png/440px-Datitude_080618_Location_of_House_of_Fraser_UK_Store_Closures.png 2x" data-file-width="3994" data-file-height="2454" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Datitude_080618_Location_of_House_of_Fraser_UK_Store_Closures.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Location of House of Fraser UK Store Estate and those planned for closure in the announcement on 7 June 2018</div></div></div>
<p>On 7 June 2018 the company announced that it would close 31 of its 58 UK stores:<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Altrincham • Aylesbury • Birkenhead • Birmingham • Bournemouth • Camberley • Cardiff • Carlisle • Chichester • Cirencester • Cwmbran • Darlington • Doncaster • Edinburgh Frasers • Epsom • Grimsby • High Wycombe • Hull • Leamington Spa • Lincoln • London Oxford Street • London King William Street • Middlesbrough • Milton Keynes • Plymouth • Shrewsbury • Skipton • Swindon • Telford • Wolverhampton • Worcester
</p><p>This includes the flagship <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Street" title="Oxford Street">Oxford Street</a> branch and the large Birmingham store in January 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Richard Lim of Retail Economics said that it remained "hard to know with any certainty just what will happen next at House of Fraser" but that without any external funding within a matter of weeks it would inevitably fall into <a href="/wiki/Administration_(law)" title="Administration (law)">administration</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-closures31_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-closures31-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Before the closures the company employed 6,000 people directly, and another 11,500 concession staff.<sup id="cite_ref-closures31_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-closures31-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Minority shareholder <a href="/wiki/Sports_Direct_International" class="mw-redirect" title="Sports Direct International">Sports Direct International</a> announced on 6 May 2018 that it was to launch legal action against the company and its leadership team. Sports Direct's representative stating that "We have been frozen out by House of Fraser. Their dealings in China are opaque, and it is blatant that we have been unfairly prejudiced. We have no option other than litigation to protect the interests of Sports Direct and its shareholders."<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2018-present:_Sports_Direct">2018-present: Sports Direct</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=House_of_Fraser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2018-present: Sports Direct">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 10 August 2018, House of Fraser entered <a href="/wiki/Administration_(law)" title="Administration (law)">administration</a>. Later that day <a href="/wiki/Sports_Direct_International" class="mw-redirect" title="Sports Direct International">Sports Direct International</a> <a href="/wiki/Pre-packaged_insolvency" title="Pre-packaged insolvency">agreed to buy the assets of the business</a> – the House of Fraser stores, brand and the stock – for £90 million in cash.<sup id="cite_ref-purchasebySportsDirect_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-purchasebySportsDirect-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Soon after the Sports Direct acquisition, many shock store closures were announced including the previously safe Manchester store, as well as Nottingham, Lakeside, Gateshead and Norwich, among others. However, after months of negotiations, almost all stores were saved, with the exception of Shrewsbury, Cirencester, Edinburgh Frasers, Chichester and London King William Street, all of which closed during December 2018 and January 2019.
</p><p>On 19 April 2019, it was revealed that a sixth store, in Altrincham, would shut due to the landlords, M&amp;M, served Ashley notice to quit, with the intention of turning it into residential area. It is however, unknown when it will close. On 14 June 2019, it was confirmed that the store in Hull, which nearly closed in December, would close in Summer 2019 as a result of failing to agree on redevelopments to the site.
Due to many stores being saved on one year leases often on no or little rent, it is unknown whether they’ll be more like Altrincham. There is also rumours that Philip Day, bidder for House of Fraser, will open some stores up as Days.
</p><p>On 26 July 2019, it was revealed that <a href="/wiki/Sports_Direct" title="Sports Direct">Sports Direct</a> had been hit by a £605 million bill from the Belgian tax authorities. There had been delays to the publication of its results throughout the day and it was now in talks with the Belgian authorities to resolve the bill. Sports Direct described the problems at House of Fraser as "nothing short of terminal." The cost of keeping the group running has been £51 million so far. Its owner said there were going to be a lot of store closures and added that there were a number of stores that, despite paying no rent, were still unprofitable.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
