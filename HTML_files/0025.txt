	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Prequel</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>prequel</b> is a literary, dramatic, or cinematic work whose story precedes that of a previous work,<sup id="cite_ref-mw_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mw-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-oxford_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> by focusing on events that occur before the original narrative.<sup id="cite_ref-Silverblatt_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Silverblatt-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> A prequel is a work that forms part of a <a href="/wiki/Backstory" title="Backstory">backstory</a> to the preceding work.
</p><p>All "prequels" are, by definition, essentially <a href="/wiki/Sequel" title="Sequel">sequels</a> in that they "expand on a <i>previous</i> or <i>preceding</i> work."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The term is a 20th-century <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">neologism</a> that is a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of the prefix "pre-" (from <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>prae</i>, "before") and "sequel".<sup id="cite_ref-mw_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mw-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-oxford_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Like other sequels, prequels may or may not concern the same plot as the work from which they are derived. Often, they explain the background which led to the events in the original, but sometimes the connections are not as explicit. Sometimes, prequels play on the audience's knowledge of what will happen next, using deliberate references to create <a href="/wiki/Dramatic_irony" class="mw-redirect" title="Dramatic irony">dramatic irony</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prequel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though the word "prequel" is of recent origin, works fitting this concept existed long before. The <i><a href="/wiki/Cypria" title="Cypria">Cypria</a></i>, presupposing hearers' acquaintance with the events of the Homeric epic, confined itself to what preceded the <i><a href="/wiki/Iliad" title="Iliad">Iliad</a></i>, and thus formed a kind of introduction.
</p><p>According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> the word "prequel" first appeared in print in 1958 in an article by <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Boucher" title="Anthony Boucher">Anthony Boucher</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Magazine_of_Fantasy_%26_Science_Fiction" title="The Magazine of Fantasy &amp; Science Fiction">The Magazine of Fantasy &amp; Science Fiction</a></i>, used to describe <a href="/wiki/James_Blish" title="James Blish">James Blish</a>'s 1956 story <i><a href="/wiki/They_Shall_Have_Stars" class="mw-redirect" title="They Shall Have Stars">They Shall Have Stars</a>,</i> which expanded on the story introduced in his earlier 1955 work, <i>Earthman Come Home</i>. The term came into general usage in the 1970s and 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-oxford_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Butch_and_Sundance:_The_Early_Days" title="Butch and Sundance: The Early Days">Butch and Sundance: The Early Days</a></i> (1979) may have introduced the term "prequel" into the mainstream.<sup id="cite_ref-salon_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-salon-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The term has since been popularized by the <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars#Prequel_trilogy" title="Star Wars"><i>Star Wars</i> prequel trilogy</a> (1999-2005).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (April 2012)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/The_Magician%27s_Nephew" title="The Magician&#39;s Nephew">The Magician's Nephew</a>, a 1955 novel by <a href="/wiki/C.S._Lewis" class="mw-redirect" title="C.S. Lewis">C.S. Lewis</a>, was written as a prequel to <a href="/wiki/The_Lion_the_Witch_and_the_Wardrobe" class="mw-redirect" title="The Lion the Witch and the Wardrobe">The Lion the Witch and the Wardrobe</a> and is the earliest example in literature.
</p><p><i>The Adventures of Ben Gunn</i>, a 1956 novel by <a href="/wiki/R._F._Delderfield" title="R. F. Delderfield">R. F. Delderfield</a> was written as a prequel to the novel <i>Treasure Island</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prequel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rather than being a concept distinct from that of a <i>sequel</i>, a <i>prequel</i> still adheres to the general principle of serialization, defined only by its internal chronology and publication order. For example, <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_Episode_I_%E2%80%93_The_Phantom_Menace" title="Star Wars: Episode I – The Phantom Menace">Star Wars: Episode I – The Phantom Menace</a></i> (1999) is a prequel to <i><a href="/wiki/Return_of_the_Jedi" title="Return of the Jedi">Star Wars: Episode VI – Return of the Jedi</a></i> (1983) but is only a <i>predecessor</i> rather than a <i>prequel</i> of <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars:_Episode_II_%E2%80%93_Attack_of_the_Clones" title="Star Wars: Episode II – Attack of the Clones">Star Wars: Episode II – Attack of the Clones</a></i> (2002) because of the release order. Likewise, 1984's <i><a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones_and_the_Temple_of_Doom" title="Indiana Jones and the Temple of Doom">Indiana Jones and the Temple of Doom</a></i> is a prequel to 1981's <i><a href="/wiki/Raiders_of_the_Lost_Ark" title="Raiders of the Lost Ark">Indiana Jones and the Raiders of the Lost Ark</a>,</i> in that it is set in 1935, one year before the first film.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Complications">Complications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prequel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Complications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sometimes "prequel" describes followups where it is not always possible to apply a label defined solely in terms of <a href="/wiki/Intertextuality" title="Intertextuality">intertextuality</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jess-Cooke_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jess-Cooke-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In the case of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Godfather_Part_II" title="The Godfather Part II">The Godfather Part II</a></i>, the narrative combines elements of a prequel with those of a more generalized sequel by having two intercut narrative strands, one continuing from the first film (<a href="/wiki/The_mafia" class="mw-redirect" title="The mafia">the mafia</a> family story under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Michael_Corleone" title="Michael Corleone">Michael Corleone</a>), and one, completely separate, detailing events that precede it (the story of his father <a href="/wiki/Vito_Corleone" title="Vito Corleone">Vito Corleone</a> in his youth). In this sense the film can be regarded as both a "prequel and a sequel" (i.e., both a prior and a continuing story), and is often referred to in this manner.<sup id="cite_ref-Jess-Cooke_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jess-Cooke-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the original <a href="/wiki/Planet_of_the_Apes" title="Planet of the Apes"><i>Planet of the Apes</i> series</a>, even though the latter three films depict world events chronologically prior to those of the first two films, the narrative itself is continuous for the main characters, as three apes from the first two films go back in time. The later installments (<i><a href="/wiki/Escape_from_the_Planet_of_the_Apes" title="Escape from the Planet of the Apes">Escape from...</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-dirks_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dirks-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_the_Planet_of_the_Apes" title="Conquest of the Planet of the Apes">Conquest of...</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-britt_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britt-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-matheou_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-matheou-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Battle_for_the_Planet_of_the_Apes" title="Battle for the Planet of the Apes">Battle for...</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-chappell_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chappell-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>) are sometimes called "prequels" in a broad sense of the word, but they are primarily sequels, as defined both broadly (as later installments) and narrowly (as continuation of the previously established storyline).<sup id="cite_ref-mw_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mw-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In recent times the term "prequel" has also been applied to <a href="/wiki/Origin-story" class="mw-redirect" title="Origin-story">origin-story</a> <a href="/wiki/Reboot_(fiction)" title="Reboot (fiction)">reboots</a>, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Rise_of_the_Planet_of_the_Apes" title="Rise of the Planet of the Apes">Rise of the Planet of the Apes</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Batman_Begins" title="Batman Begins">Batman Begins</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Casino_Royale_(2006_film)" title="Casino Royale (2006 film)">Casino Royale</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-rota_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rota-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Sutton_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The creators of both <i>Batman Begins</i> and <i>Rise of the Planet of the Apes</i> also stated their intent to dispense with the continuity of the previous films so they would exist as separate pieces of work, with <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Nolan" title="Christopher Nolan">Christopher Nolan</a>—director of <i>Batman Begins</i>—explicitly stating he does not consider it a prequel.<sup id="cite_ref-Sutton_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Here, "prequel" denotes status as a "franchise-renewing original" that depicts events earlier in the (internally inconsistent) narrative cycle than those of a previous installment.<sup id="cite_ref-Sutton_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sutton-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Most reviewers require that a prequel must lead up to the beginning of its original work,<sup id="cite_ref-Silverblatt_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Silverblatt-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> which is inconsistent with works that dispense with the narrative of previous work and are not significantly within the same <a href="/wiki/Continuity_(fiction)" title="Continuity (fiction)">continuity</a>. At times, the term has been used to refer to a work that was released, as well as chronologically set, before any other work.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> However, that usage conflicts with the fact that a prequel is a type of sequel.
</p><p>The 2009 film <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek_(film)" title="Star Trek (film)">Star Trek</a></i> features characters from the 1960s TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Original_Series" title="Star Trek: The Original Series">Star Trek</a></i>, but earlier in their careers. However, the film is set in an alternate timeline caused by a <a href="/wiki/Romulan" title="Romulan">Romulan</a> captain from the universe of the original series going back in time and interfering with history. Thus, the film has been described as simultaneously a prequel and a reboot.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
