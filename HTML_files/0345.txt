Law of the Sea</a> at <a href="/wiki/Wikisource" title="Wikisource">Wikisource</a></td></tr></tbody></table><p>The <b>United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea</b> (<b>UNCLOS</b>), also called the <b>Law of the Sea Convention</b> or the <b>Law of the Sea treaty</b>, is the international agreement that resulted from the third United Nations Conference on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS III), which took place between 1973 and 1982. The Law of the Sea Convention defines the rights and responsibilities of nations with respect to their use of the world's oceans, establishing guidelines for businesses, the environment, and the management of marine <a href="/wiki/Natural_resource" title="Natural resource">natural resources</a>. The Convention, concluded in 1982, replaced the quad-treaty 1958 <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_High_Seas" title="Convention on the High Seas">Convention on the High Seas</a>. UNCLOS came into force in 1994, a year after Guyana became the 60th nation to ratify the treaty.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> As of June&#160;2016<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, 167 countries and the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> have joined in the Convention. It is uncertain as to what extent the Convention codifies <a href="/wiki/Customary_international_law" title="Customary international law">customary international law</a>.
</p><p>While the <a href="/wiki/Secretary-General_of_the_United_Nations" title="Secretary-General of the United Nations">Secretary-General of the United Nations</a> receives instruments of <a href="/wiki/Ratification" title="Ratification">ratification</a> and accession and the UN provides support for meetings of states party to the Convention, the UN has no direct operational role in the implementation of the Convention. There is, however, a role played by organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/International_Maritime_Organization" title="International Maritime Organization">International Maritime Organization</a>, the <a href="/wiki/International_Whaling_Commission" title="International Whaling Commission">International Whaling Commission</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/International_Seabed_Authority" title="International Seabed Authority">International Seabed Authority (ISA)</a>. (The ISA was established by the UN Convention.)
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>UNCLOS replaces the older '<a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_the_seas" title="Freedom of the seas">freedom of the seas</a>' concept, dating from the 17th century: national rights were limited to a specified belt of water extending from a nation's <a href="/wiki/Coast" title="Coast">coastlines</a>, usually 3 nautical miles (5.6&#160;km) (<a href="/wiki/Three-mile_limit" title="Three-mile limit">Three-mile limit</a>), according to the '<a href="/wiki/Cannon" title="Cannon">cannon</a> shot' rule developed by the Dutch jurist <a href="/wiki/Cornelius_van_Bynkershoek" title="Cornelius van Bynkershoek">Cornelius van Bynkershoek</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Akashi-Bynkershoek_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Akashi-Bynkershoek-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> All waters beyond national boundaries were considered <a href="/wiki/International_waters" title="International waters">international waters</a>: free to all nations, but belonging to none of them (the <i><a href="/wiki/International_waters" title="International waters">mare liberum</a></i> principle promulgated by <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Grotius" title="Hugo Grotius">Hugo Grotius</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the early 20th century, some nations expressed their desire to extend national claims: to include mineral resources, to protect <a href="/wiki/Fish_stock" title="Fish stock">fish stocks</a>, and to provide the means to enforce <a href="/wiki/Pollution#Pollution_control" title="Pollution">pollution controls</a>. (The <a href="/wiki/League_of_Nations" title="League of Nations">League of Nations</a> called a 1930 conference at <a href="/wiki/The_Hague" title="The Hague">The Hague</a>, but no agreements resulted.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>) Using the customary international law principle of a nation's right to protect its natural resources, President <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">Harry S. Truman</a> in 1945 extended United States control to all the natural resources of its <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a>. Other nations were quick to follow suit. Between 1946 and 1950, Chile, Peru, and Ecuador extended their rights to a distance of 200 nautical miles (370&#160;km) to cover their <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_Current" title="Humboldt Current">Humboldt Current</a> fishing grounds. Other nations extended their territorial seas to 12 nautical miles (22&#160;km).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>By 1967, only 25 nations still used the old 3-mile (4.8&#160;km) limit,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> while 66 nations had set a 12-nautical-mile (22&#160;km) territorial limit<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and eight had set a 200-nautical-mile (370&#160;km) limit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> As of 28&#160;May&#160;2008<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, only two countries still use the 3-mile (4.8&#160;km) limit: Jordan and Palau.<sup id="cite_ref-untableofclaims_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-untableofclaims-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> That limit is also used in certain Australian islands, an area of Belize, some Japanese <a href="/wiki/Strait" title="Strait">straits</a>, certain areas of Papua New Guinea, and a few <a href="/wiki/British_Overseas_Territories" title="British Overseas Territories">British Overseas Territories</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Anguilla" title="Anguilla">Anguilla</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<p>In 1956, the United Nations held its first Conference on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS I) at <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a>, Switzerland. UNCLOS I<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> resulted in four treaties concluded in 1958:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Territorial_Sea_and_Contiguous_Zone" class="mw-redirect" title="Convention on the Territorial Sea and Contiguous Zone">Convention on the Territorial Sea and Contiguous Zone</a>, entry into force: 10 September 1964</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Continental_Shelf" title="Convention on the Continental Shelf">Convention on the Continental Shelf</a>, entry into force: 10 June 1964</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_High_Seas" title="Convention on the High Seas">Convention on the High Seas</a>, entry into force: 30 September 1962</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Fishing_and_Conservation_of_Living_Resources_of_the_High_Seas" class="mw-redirect" title="Convention on Fishing and Conservation of Living Resources of the High Seas">Convention on Fishing and Conservation of Living Resources of the High Seas</a>, entry into force: 20 March 1966</li></ul>
<p>Although UNCLOS I was considered a success, it left open the important issue of breadth of territorial waters.<sup id="cite_ref-mlr82-behuniak_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mlr82-behuniak-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="UNCLOS_II">UNCLOS II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: UNCLOS II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1960, the United Nations held the second Conference on the Law of the Sea ("UNCLOS II"); however, the six-week Geneva conference did not result in any new agreements.<sup id="cite_ref-mlr82-behuniak_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mlr82-behuniak-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Generally speaking, developing nations and third world countries participated only as clients, allies, or dependents of the United States or the Soviet Union, with no significant voice of their own.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="UNCLOS_III">UNCLOS III</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: UNCLOS III">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Zonmar-en.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Zonmar-en.svg/300px-Zonmar-en.svg.png" decoding="async" width="300" height="447" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Zonmar-en.svg/450px-Zonmar-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Zonmar-en.svg/600px-Zonmar-en.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="493" data-file-height="735" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Zonmar-en.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sea areas in international rights (Top view)</div></div></div>
<p>The issue of varying claims of territorial waters was raised in the UN in 1967 by <a href="/wiki/Arvid_Pardo" title="Arvid Pardo">Arvid Pardo</a> of Malta, and in 1973 the <i>Third United Nations Conference on the Law of the Sea</i> was convened in New York. In an attempt to reduce the possibility of groups of nation-states dominating the negotiations, the conference used a consensus process rather than majority vote. With more than 160 nations participating, the conference lasted until 1982. The resulting convention came into force on 16 November 1994, one year after the 60th state, Guyana, ratified the treaty.
</p><p>The convention introduced a number of provisions. The most significant issues covered were setting limits, navigation, archipelagic status and transit regimes, <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zones" class="mw-redirect" title="Exclusive economic zones">exclusive economic zones</a> (EEZs), continental shelf jurisdiction, deep seabed mining, the exploitation regime, protection of the marine environment, scientific research, and settlement of disputes.
</p><p>The convention set the limit of various areas, measured from a carefully defined <a href="/wiki/Baseline_(sea)" title="Baseline (sea)">baseline</a>. (Normally, a sea baseline follows the low-water line, but when the coastline is deeply indented, has fringing islands or is highly unstable, straight baselines may be used.) The areas are as follows:
</p>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Internal_waters" title="Internal waters">Internal waters</a></dt>
<dd>Covers all water and waterways on the landward side of the baseline. The coastal state is free to set laws, regulate use, and use any resource. Foreign vessels have no right of passage within internal waters. A vessel in the high seas assumes jurisdiction under the internal laws of its flag State. Pursuit of a ship by the Coastal State may only take place in the internal waters and is required to end when reaching the contiguous zone.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Territorial_waters" title="Territorial waters">Territorial waters</a></dt>
<dd>Out to 12 nautical miles (22 kilometres; 14 miles) from the baseline, the coastal state is free to set laws, regulate use, and use any resource. Vessels were given the right of <a href="/wiki/Innocent_passage" title="Innocent passage">innocent passage</a> through any territorial waters, with strategic straits allowing the passage of military craft as <a href="/wiki/Transit_passage" title="Transit passage">transit passage</a>, in that naval vessels are allowed to maintain postures that would be illegal in territorial waters. "Innocent passage" is defined by the convention as passing through waters in an expeditious and continuous manner, which is not "prejudicial to the peace, good order or the security" of the coastal state. Fishing, polluting, weapons practice, and spying are not "innocent", and submarines and other underwater vehicles are required to navigate on the surface and to show their flag. Nations can also temporarily suspend innocent passage in specific areas of their territorial seas, if doing so is essential for the protection of their security.</dd>
<dt>Archipelagic waters</dt>
<dd>The convention set the definition of Archipelagic States in Part IV, which also defines how the state can draw its territorial borders. A baseline is drawn between the outermost points of the outermost islands, subject to these points being sufficiently close to one another. All waters inside this baseline are designated <i>Archipelagic Waters</i>. The state has sovereignty over these waters (like internal waters), but subject to existing rights including traditional fishing rights of immediately adjacent states.<sup id="cite_ref-UNCLOS3_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNCLOS3-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Foreign vessels have right of innocent passage through archipelagic waters (like territorial waters).</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Contiguous_zone" class="mw-redirect" title="Contiguous zone">Contiguous zone</a></dt>
<dd>Beyond the 12-nautical-mile (22&#160;km) limit, there is a further 12 nautical miles (22&#160;km) from the territorial sea <a href="/wiki/Baseline_(sea)" title="Baseline (sea)">baseline</a> limit, the contiguous zone, in which a state can continue to enforce laws in four specific areas: customs, taxation, immigration and pollution, if the infringement started within the state's territory or territorial waters, or if this infringement is about to occur within the state's territory or territorial waters.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> This makes the contiguous zone a <a href="/wiki/Hot_pursuit" title="Hot pursuit">hot pursuit</a> area.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone" title="Exclusive economic zone">Exclusive economic zones</a> (EEZs)</dt>
<dd>These extend 200 nautical miles (370 kilometres; 230 miles) from the <a href="/wiki/Baseline_(sea)" title="Baseline (sea)">baseline</a>. Within this area, the coastal nation has sole exploitation rights over all natural resources. In casual use, the term may include the territorial sea and even the continental shelf. The EEZs were introduced to halt the increasingly heated clashes over fishing rights, although <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a> was also becoming important. The success of an offshore <a href="/wiki/Oil_platform" title="Oil platform">oil platform</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Mexico" title="Gulf of Mexico">Gulf of Mexico</a> in 1947 was soon repeated elsewhere in the world, and by 1970 it was technically feasible to operate in waters 4,000 metres deep. Foreign nations have the freedom of navigation and overflight, subject to the regulation of the coastal states. Foreign states may also lay submarine pipes and cables.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">Continental shelf</a></dt>
<dd>The continental shelf is defined as the <a href="/wiki/Natural_prolongation" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural prolongation">natural prolongation</a> of the land territory to the <a href="/wiki/Continental_margin" title="Continental margin">continental margin</a>'s outer edge, or 200 nautical miles (370&#160;km) from the coastal state's baseline, whichever is greater. A state's continental shelf may exceed 200 nautical miles (370&#160;km) until the natural prolongation ends. However, it may never exceed 350 nautical miles (650 kilometres; 400 miles) from the baseline; or it may never exceed 100 nautical miles (190 kilometres; 120 miles) beyond the 2,500-meter <a href="/wiki/Isobath" class="mw-redirect" title="Isobath">isobath</a> (the line connecting the depth of 2,500 meters). Coastal states have the right to harvest mineral and non-living material in the subsoil of its continental shelf, to the exclusion of others. Coastal states also have exclusive control over living resources "attached" to the continental shelf, but not to creatures living in the water column beyond the exclusive economic zone.</dd></dl>
<p>Aside from its provisions defining ocean boundaries, the convention establishes general obligations for safeguarding the marine environment and protecting freedom of scientific research on the high seas, and also creates an innovative legal regime for controlling mineral resource exploitation in deep seabed areas beyond national jurisdiction, through an <a href="/wiki/International_Seabed_Authority" title="International Seabed Authority">International Seabed Authority</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Common_heritage_of_mankind" title="Common heritage of mankind">Common heritage of mankind</a> principle.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Landlocked_country" title="Landlocked country">Landlocked</a> states are given a right of access to and from the sea, without taxation of traffic through transit states.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Part_XI_and_the_1994_Agreement">Part XI and the 1994 Agreement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Part XI and the 1994 Agreement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Part XI of the Convention provides for a regime relating to minerals on the seabed outside any state's territorial waters or EEZ (Exclusive Economic Zones). It establishes an <a href="/wiki/International_Seabed_Authority" title="International Seabed Authority">International Seabed Authority</a> (ISA) to authorize seabed exploration and mining and collect and distribute the seabed mining royalty.
</p><p>The United States objected to the provisions of Part XI of the Convention on several grounds, arguing that the treaty was unfavorable to American economic and security interests. Due to Part XI, the United States refused to ratify the UNCLOS, although it expressed agreement with the remaining provisions of the Convention.
</p><p>From 1982 to 1990, the United States accepted all but Part XI as customary international law, while attempting to establish an alternative regime for exploitation of the minerals of the deep seabed. An agreement was made with other seabed mining nations and licenses were granted to four international consortia. Concurrently, the Preparatory Commission was established to prepare for the eventual coming into force of the Convention-recognized claims by applicants, sponsored by signatories of the Convention. Overlaps between the two groups were resolved, but a decline in the demand for minerals from the seabed made the seabed regime significantly less relevant. In addition, the decline of Socialism and the fall of Communism in the late 1980s had removed much of the support for some of the more contentious Part XI provisions.
</p><p>In 1990, consultations were begun between signatories and non-signatories (including the United States) over the possibility of modifying the Convention to allow the industrialized countries to join the Convention. The resulting 1994 Agreement on Implementation was adopted as a binding international Convention. It mandated that key articles, including those on limitation of seabed production and mandatory technology transfer, would not be applied, that the United States, if it became a member, would be guaranteed a seat on the Council of the International Seabed Authority, and finally, that voting would be done in groups, with each group able to block decisions on substantive matters. The 1994 Agreement also established a Finance Committee that would originate the financial decisions of the Authority, to which the largest donors would automatically be members and in which decisions would be made by consensus.
</p><p>On 1 February 2011, the Seabed Disputes Chamber of the International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea (ITLOS) issued an advisory opinion concerning the legal responsibilities and obligations of States Parties to the Convention with respect to the sponsorship of activities in the Area in accordance with Part XI of the Convention and the 1994 Agreement.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The advisory opinion was issued in response to a formal request made by the International Seabed Authority following two prior applications the Authority's Legal and Technical Commission had received from the Republics of Nauru and Tonga regarding proposed activities (a plan of work to explore for polymetallic nodules) to be undertaken in the Area by two State-sponsored contractors – Nauru Ocean Resources Inc. (sponsored by the Republic of Nauru) and Tonga Offshore Mining Ltd. (sponsored by the Kingdom of Tonga). The advisory opinion set forth the international legal responsibilities and obligations of Sponsoring States AND the Authority to ensure that sponsored activities do not harm the marine environment, consistent with the applicable provisions of UNCLOS Part XI, Authority regulations, ITLOS case law, other international environmental treaties, and Principle 15 of the UN Rio Declaration.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biodiversity_Beyond_National_Jurisdiction">Biodiversity Beyond National Jurisdiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Biodiversity Beyond National Jurisdiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2017, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> (UNGA) voted to convene an <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_conference" class="mw-redirect" title="Intergovernmental conference">intergovernmental conference</a> (IGC) to consider establishing an international legally-binding instrument on the conservation and sustainable use of biodiversity beyond national jurisdiction (BBNJ). Over a series of four sessions between 2018 and 2020, the IGC will convene for negotiations towards a BBNJ treaty.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The convention was opened for signature on 10 December 1982 and entered into force on 16 November 1994 upon deposition of the 60th instrument of ratification.<sup id="cite_ref-history_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The convention has been ratified by 168 parties, which includes 167 states (164 member states of the United Nations plus the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly_observers" title="United Nations General Assembly observers">UN Observer state</a> <a href="/wiki/State_of_Palestine" title="State of Palestine">Palestine</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Cook_Islands" title="Cook Islands">Cook Islands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niue" title="Niue">Niue</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-rat_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rat-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In early August 2019 United States created <a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Maritime_Security_Construct&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="International Maritime Security Construct (page does not exist)">International Maritime Security Construct</a> to prevent escalation of the new <a href="/wiki/2019_Persian_Gulf_crisis" title="2019 Persian Gulf crisis">Gulf crisis</a>.
</p>
