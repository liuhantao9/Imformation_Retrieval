	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Turkish name</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>Turkish name</b> consists of an <i>ad</i> or an <i>isim</i> (given name; plural <i>adlar</i> and <i>isimler</i>) and a <i>soyadı</i> or <i>soyisim</i> (surname).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Turkish names exist in a "full name" format. While there is only one <i>soyadı</i> (surname) in the full name there may be more than one <i>ad</i> (given name). Married women may carry both their maiden and husband's surnames. The <i>soyadı</i> is written as the last element of the full name, after all given names (except that official documents related to registration matters often use the format "Soyadı, Adı").
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Given_names">Given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Turkish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="given"></span><span id="Given"></span>
</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Turkish_masculine_given_names" title="Category:Turkish masculine given names">Category:Turkish masculine given names</a>, <a href="/wiki/Category:Turkish_feminine_given_names" title="Category:Turkish feminine given names">Category:Turkish feminine given names</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Category:Turkish_unisex_given_names" title="Category:Turkish unisex given names">Category:Turkish unisex given names</a></div>
<p>At least one name, often two but very rarely more, are given to a person at birth. Newly given names are allowed up to three words. Most names are gender-specific: <a href="/wiki/O%C4%9Fuz_(name)" title="Oğuz (name)">Oğuz</a> is strictly for males, <a href="/wiki/Tu%C4%9F%C3%A7e" title="Tuğçe">Tuğçe</a> only for females. But there are many Turkish names which are <a href="/wiki/Unisex_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Unisex names">unisex</a>. Many modern given names (such as <a href="/wiki/Deniz_(given_name)" title="Deniz (given name)">Deniz</a>, "sea"; or Ülkü, "ideal") are given to newborns of either sex. 
</p><p>Among the common examples of the many <a href="/wiki/Unisex_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Unisex names">unisex names</a> in Turkey include <a href="/wiki/Ayta%C3%A7" title="Aytaç">Aytaç</a>, <a href="/wiki/Deniz_(given_name)" title="Deniz (given name)">Deniz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derya" title="Derya">Derya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evren_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Evren (disambiguation)">Evren</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evrim" title="Evrim">Evrim</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C3%96zg%C3%BCr" title="Özgür">Özgür</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Y%C3%BCcel" title="Yücel">Yücel</a>. Unlike English unisex names, most Turkish unisex names have been traditionally used for both genders. However, some unisex names are used more for one gender (Derya is used more for girls, whereas Aytaç is used more for boys). Names are given to babies by their parents and then registered in "The Central Civil Registration System" (MERNIS)<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> while preparing the baby's <a href="/wiki/Identity_document" title="Identity document">identity document</a> at the birth registration office of the district's governorship.
</p><p>Turkish names are often words with specific meanings in the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish language</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most Turkish names can easily be differentiated from others,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> except those of other Turkic nations, particularly <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijani_name" title="Azerbaijani name">Azerbaijani name</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> especially if they are of pure Turkic origin. The <i>Law on the Adoption and Implementation of the Turkish Alphabet</i> of 1928, in force as decreed by article 174 of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Turkey" title="Constitution of Turkey">Constitution of Turkey</a>, prescribes that only letters in the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_alphabet" title="Turkish alphabet">Turkish alphabet</a> may be used on birth certificates. As the <a href="/wiki/Turkish_alphabet" title="Turkish alphabet">Turkish alphabet</a> has no Q, W, X, or other symbols, names including those be cannot be officially given unless they are transliterated into Turkish.
</p><p>Ideological concerns of the families can also affect naming behaviour.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Some religious families give second names of <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> origin, which can be names of important figures in the religion of Islam such as <a href="/wiki/Muhammed" class="mw-redirect" title="Muhammed">Muhammed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ali" title="Ali">Ali</a>. Some of these names have evolved in time, differentiating from the Arabic original, as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Mehmet" class="mw-redirect" title="Mehmet">Mehmet</a> (although the original name (Muhammed) also began to be used after the switch to the Latin alphabet distinguished the two spellings). Another change is for linguistic reasons such as in the case of Vahdettin (from Vahideddin), Sadettin (from Sa'adeddin) or Nurettin (from Nureddin).
</p><p>Some Turkish people with two given names are commonly referred to with just one of these names while others are referred to with both. For example, the writer Ferit Orhan Pamuk is commonly known simply as <a href="/wiki/Orhan_Pamuk" title="Orhan Pamuk">Orhan Pamuk</a>, but another writer, <a href="/wiki/Ahmet_Hamdi_Tanp%C4%B1nar" title="Ahmet Hamdi Tanpınar">Ahmet Hamdi Tanpınar</a>, is known with both given names. Many Turkish people with two given names, like Orhan Pamuk, are often known and called by their first name, which is placed between the middle and last names, as opposed to Western naming conventions.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surnames">Surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Turkish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Turkish-language_surnames" title="Category:Turkish-language surnames">Category:Turkish-language surnames</a></div>
<p>Until the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Surname_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Surname Law">Surname Law</a> in 1934, as part of <a href="/wiki/Atat%C3%BCrk%27s_Reforms" title="Atatürk&#39;s Reforms">Atatürk's Reforms</a>, Turkish citizens had no surnames. The law required all citizens of Turkey to adopt an official surname. Before that, male Turks often used their father's name followed by <i>-oğlu</i> ("son of"), or a nickname of the family, before their given name (e.g. <i>Mustafa-oğlu</i> Mehmet, <i>Köselerin</i> Hasan) before the modern era. The Turks who descended from a ruling house used <i>-zade</i> ("descendant in the male line"), e.g. Sami Paşazade Mehmet Bey ("Mehmet <a href="/wiki/Bey" title="Bey">Bey</a>, descendant/son of Sami <a href="/wiki/Pasha" title="Pasha">Pasha</a>").
</p><p>The surname (<i>soyad</i>, literally "lineage name" or "family name") is an ancestry-based name following a person's given names, used for addressing people or the family.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The surname (<i>soyadı</i>) is a single word according to Turkish law. It is not gender-specific and has no gender-dependent modifications. The <i>soyadı</i> is neither <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> nor <a href="/wiki/Matronymic" title="Matronymic">matronymic</a>. Surnames in Turkey are <a href="/wiki/Patrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrilineal">patrilineal</a>: they pass in the male line from father to his legal children without any change in form. Turkey has abolished all notions of nobility; thus, there is no noble form or type of surname.
</p><p>Since 2014, women in Turkey are allowed to keep their birth names alone for their whole life instead of using their husbands' names.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Prior to this date, the Turkish Code of Civil Law Article 187 required a married woman to compulsorily obtain her husband's surname after the marriage; or otherwise, to use her birth name in front of her husband’s name by giving a written application to the marriage officer or the civil registry office. In 2014, the Constitutional Court ruled that prohibiting married women from retaining only maiden names is a violation of their rights.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
After divorce, the woman returns to her pre-marriage surname. The court may grant a woman the right to keep her ex-husband's surname after divorcing; the court's decision must consider both the man's and the woman's situations.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> A woman may have only two surnames due to marriage. Thus, a woman who continues to use double surname after divorcing, cannot take a third surname by marrying again.<sup id="cite_ref-tbmm.gov.tr_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tbmm.gov.tr-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> 
The child of a family takes the "family name", which is his or her father's surname. A child takes their mother's surname if the mother is not married, or if the father is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Turkish citizens may change their surnames according to Turkish Civil Law<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and Turkish Law on Population Services via court decision of "civil court of first instance".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
