<p><b>Cleckheaton</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">town</a> in the Metropolitan borough of <a href="/wiki/Kirklees" title="Kirklees">Kirklees</a>, in <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, England. <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">Historically</a> part of the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>, it is situated south of <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> east of <a href="/wiki/Brighouse" title="Brighouse">Brighouse</a>, west of <a href="/wiki/Batley" title="Batley">Batley</a> and south-west of <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>.  It is at the centre of the <a href="/wiki/River_Spen" title="River Spen">Spen Valley</a> and was the major town in the former borough of <a href="/wiki/Spenborough" title="Spenborough">Spenborough</a>.
Cleckheaton has a history as a mill town, although this industry has now all but vanished.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleckheaton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cleckheaton_Whitechapel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Cleckheaton_Whitechapel.jpg/220px-Cleckheaton_Whitechapel.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Cleckheaton_Whitechapel.jpg/330px-Cleckheaton_Whitechapel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Cleckheaton_Whitechapel.jpg/440px-Cleckheaton_Whitechapel.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cleckheaton_Whitechapel.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Whitechapel Church</div></div></div>
<p>The Spen Valley was once heavily wooded.  Evidence of human habitation in <a href="/wiki/Mesolithic" title="Mesolithic">Mesolithic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> times has been found in the area.  Roman remains have been found in the valley and it is thought that roads from <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> to <a href="/wiki/Chester" title="Chester">Chester</a>, and from settlements in <a href="/wiki/Halifax,_West_Yorkshire" title="Halifax, West Yorkshire">Halifax</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a>, passed through Cleckheaton and the junction gave rise to a staging post.
</p><p>Cleckheaton was in the ancient parish of <a href="/wiki/Birstall,_West_Yorkshire" title="Birstall, West Yorkshire">Birstall</a>. A chapel-of-ease, known as the White Chapel (later Whitechapel) was established.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Textile_working">Textile working</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleckheaton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Textile working">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The area was very disorganised for a long time after the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a> and the richest townships at that time were still the richest 300 years later as the Poll Tax returns of 1379 show.  They also demonstrate the lack of administration as only the richest four of the 227 families living in the Spen Valley were made to pay more than the 4d (approx. 2p) minimum tax. These tax returns also show the recent deviation from the traditional sources of wealth in the area (i.e. farming and allied trades). These were centred on textiles and included dyeing, weaving and fulling (common names in the area nowadays still recall these early trades: Lister- dyer, Webster- weaver, Walker- fuller).  The spread of these trades was also a result of the absence of regulation of the area.  Due to the lack of manorial control, land was divided between all the sons in a family rather than just passing to the eldest. As the farmland owned by a family got smaller they became unable to support the family and so people turned to production of woollens to gain extra income.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cleckheaton_Priory_pub.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Cleckheaton_Priory_pub.jpg/220px-Cleckheaton_Priory_pub.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Cleckheaton_Priory_pub.jpg/330px-Cleckheaton_Priory_pub.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Cleckheaton_Priory_pub.jpg/440px-Cleckheaton_Priory_pub.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cleckheaton_Priory_pub.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Old pub named after Kirklees Priory</div></div></div>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/English_Reformation" title="English Reformation">Reformation</a>, Kirklees Priory was largely destroyed, many families were driven from the area and new non-aristocratic lords of the manor who were sympathetic to Protestantism were introduced by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I</a>, as was a puritan clergyman who was installed at Birstall Church. By 1570, at the time of the <a href="/wiki/Rising_of_the_North" title="Rising of the North">Rising of the Northern Earls</a>, the last of the old Norman noble families had been swept away. Sir John Neville went into exile and forfeited his estate and Thomas Hussey (heir to the de Tilly family of Oakwell Hall) was imprisoned in the <a href="/wiki/Tower_of_London" title="Tower of London">Tower of London</a> for some time before being pardoned.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>By the 17th century land-owning farmers were finding it increasingly difficult as were their landlords and some payments were still in kind as farmers had no money to pay their debts.  Meanwhile, the textile workers were becoming more and more prosperous and paid less and less attention to their hard up and increasingly impotent landlords. During the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> the clothiers were on one side and the landlords on the other. Lords of the area were made Royalist officers and made some progress such as at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Adwalton_Moor" title="Battle of Adwalton Moor">Battle of Adwalton Moor</a> about a mile east of <a href="/wiki/Birkenshaw,_West_Yorkshire" title="Birkenshaw, West Yorkshire">Birkenshaw</a> and the siege of <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a>, before the Parliamentarians took control of the area. Royalist families were forced, after the war, to pay large fines to keep their lands and avoid imprisonment. All the time clothiers were growing wealthier and by the end of the 17th century more than half of the wills in the parish of Birstall came from men whose wealth came from textiles.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nonconformity">Nonconformity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleckheaton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nonconformity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Independent_Methodist_Chapel_Cleckheaton.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Independent_Methodist_Chapel_Cleckheaton.jpg/220px-Independent_Methodist_Chapel_Cleckheaton.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Independent_Methodist_Chapel_Cleckheaton.jpg/330px-Independent_Methodist_Chapel_Cleckheaton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Independent_Methodist_Chapel_Cleckheaton.jpg/440px-Independent_Methodist_Chapel_Cleckheaton.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="451" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Independent_Methodist_Chapel_Cleckheaton.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Independent Methodist Chapel, Chapel Street</div></div></div>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Restoration_(England)" title="Restoration (England)">restoration of the monarchy</a> in 1660, <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">Anglicanism</a> was reintroduced. But many people had found <a href="/wiki/Puritan" class="mw-redirect" title="Puritan">Puritan</a> teachings more to their taste, and it took many years to re-install an Anglican vicar to Whitechapel. Despite the draconian nonconformist laws, there were many <a href="/wiki/Nonconformist" title="Nonconformist">nonconformist</a> meeting houses and nonconformity flourished; a fifth of the population of the Birstall Parish was estimated to be nonconformist. <a href="/wiki/Quaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Quaker">Quakers</a> were widespread, and even now a number of 17th and 18th century Quaker burial grounds remain in the area. In the 18th century <a href="/wiki/Presbyterianism" title="Presbyterianism">Presbyterianism</a> was widespread, but then it lost a large minority of its flock to <a href="/wiki/Unitarianism" title="Unitarianism">Unitarianism</a> and to the <a href="/wiki/Baptist" class="mw-redirect" title="Baptist">Baptist</a> church. <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">Methodism</a> also flourished from the 1740s after visits from <a href="/wiki/John_Wesley" title="John Wesley">John Wesley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wesley" title="Charles Wesley">Charles Wesley</a>, as did the <a href="/wiki/Moravian_Church" title="Moravian Church">Moravian Church</a>. Indeed, John Wesley lived in Birstall for some time as it was near many large towns in the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In spite of the religious strength in the valley, the inhabitants were somewhat unconventional and still went to astrologers, quack doctors and prophets. Men like Eli Collins, the "Wizard of Wyke", and Alvery Newsome, the "Wise Man of Heckmondwike", were widespread. Furthermore, to increase the isolation, the area had no canals and had few roads, apart from a few turnpike roads, including the major ones from <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> to <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a> and Bradford to Halifax.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_Revolution">Industrial Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleckheaton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Industrial Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Prospect_Mills_Cleckheaton.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Prospect_Mills_Cleckheaton.jpg/220px-Prospect_Mills_Cleckheaton.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="161" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Prospect_Mills_Cleckheaton.jpg/330px-Prospect_Mills_Cleckheaton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Prospect_Mills_Cleckheaton.jpg/440px-Prospect_Mills_Cleckheaton.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="468" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Prospect_Mills_Cleckheaton.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Prospect Mills in Prospect Road.</div></div></div>
<p>After the discovery of good quality coal in the north of the valley, industry spread quickly.
</p><p>In 1804 the Reverend Hammond Roberson, annoyed that the administration of Liversedge was disorganised, promoted a system of reform (the <a href="/wiki/Select_vestries" class="mw-redirect" title="Select vestries">select vestry</a>) which quickly spread to Cleckheaton and Heckmondwike. In 1810, after his wife's death, Roberson turned his attention to church building in the area. In 1818 Parliament voted £1 million for the building of new churches in the country, and Roberson was able to secure funding to build Cleckheaton Church: <a href="/wiki/William_Swinden_Barber#Church_of_St_John_the_Evangelist,_Cleckheaton,_1886" title="William Swinden Barber">St John the Evangelist</a> in Church Street.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spen_Valley">Spen Valley</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleckheaton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Spen Valley">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By the mid 19th century the Spen Valley entered its golden era. In 1800 children were paid starvation wages for putting staples into leather for carding wool, but by 1838 there were eleven carding factories in Cleckheaton and by 1893 the town was recognised as the carding capital of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><br />
The mill owners built turnpike roads between the villages to enable their employees to get to work and lobbied to get railways built to get their products to reach customers. After a great deal of wrangling with the various railway companies in the 1840s railways were finally built which enabled the textile mills, ironworks, chemical factories and collieries to compete with those around the country. More than this, improved local roads and transport brought together the people of the individual villages of the valley and quickly the villages grew until they merged.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleckheaton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 20th century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lion_Sweet_Factory_Cleckheaton.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Lion_Sweet_Factory_Cleckheaton.jpg/220px-Lion_Sweet_Factory_Cleckheaton.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Lion_Sweet_Factory_Cleckheaton.jpg/330px-Lion_Sweet_Factory_Cleckheaton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Lion_Sweet_Factory_Cleckheaton.jpg/440px-Lion_Sweet_Factory_Cleckheaton.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lion_Sweet_Factory_Cleckheaton.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Lion sweet factory, Westgate</div></div></div>
<p>Around 1900, many large and expensive buildings were erected and became symbols of the area's wealth; massive chapels and a new grammar school were built in Cleckheaton, and to mark the new <a href="/wiki/Urban_district_(Great_Britain_and_Ireland)" title="Urban district (Great Britain and Ireland)">urban district</a> and the fact that it was the centre of it, Cleckheaton built a town hall in 1892, paid for in part by public subscription.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1903 Lion Confectionery began making "Midget Gems" in Cleckheaton, and 1904 saw the opening of the <a href="/wiki/Phelon_%26_Moore" title="Phelon &amp; Moore">Phelon &amp; Moore</a> (Panther) motorcycle factory in the town, soon followed by a car factory.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> BBA (formerly British Belting &amp; Asbestos), the large asbestos, friction material, and conveyor belting firm, built its headquarters at Moorend, where they manufactured automotive disc brake pads under the Mintex banner. A tourist industry developed to serve visitors to the area made famous by <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Bront%C3%AB" title="Charlotte Brontë">Charlotte Brontë</a>'s "Shirley" and by the <a href="/wiki/Luddite" title="Luddite">Luddite</a> attacks. The Mowatt family paid for Cleckheaton Library and also the reference library at Cleckheaton's grammar (later secondary) school, <a href="/wiki/Whitcliffe_Mount_School" title="Whitcliffe Mount School">Whitcliffe Mount</a>, which bears their name.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>From the 1920s, Spenborough's fortunes began to decline. Pits began to close and trade waned. Slowly, central government took on responsibilities previously held by <a href="/wiki/Spenborough" title="Spenborough">Spenborough</a> such as water supply, gas production, public health and education. By this time the level of industry was in serious decline as the textile mills, foundries and other factories gradually closed.
</p><p>In the 1960s and 1970s many new houses were built in Cleckheaton, as the town attracted commuters to <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ashtons" title="Ashtons">Ashtons</a> built many houses around Cleckheaton and neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Mirfield" title="Mirfield">Mirfield</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cleckheaton_railway_station">Cleckheaton railway station</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleckheaton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cleckheaton railway station">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cleckheaton_Station_Tavern.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Cleckheaton_Station_Tavern.jpg/220px-Cleckheaton_Station_Tavern.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Cleckheaton_Station_Tavern.jpg/330px-Cleckheaton_Station_Tavern.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Cleckheaton_Station_Tavern.jpg/440px-Cleckheaton_Station_Tavern.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cleckheaton_Station_Tavern.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Station Tavern. Station was to the left; sidings were straight ahead.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Cleckheaton_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Cleckheaton railway station">Cleckheaton railway station</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cleckheaton_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Cleckheaton railway station">railway station</a> closed to passenger traffic in 1965 and to goods four years later.
</p><p>In 1972 a singular case was heard at Wakefield Crown Court. A <a href="/wiki/Dewsbury" title="Dewsbury">Dewsbury</a> man was accused of, as counsel for the prosecution put it, effectively stealing the station. <a href="/wiki/British_Rail" title="British Rail">British Rail</a> had contracted for the clearing of the site; part of the deal was that the contractors would sell and retain the proceeds from disposal of the materials and scrap. On arrival, they discovered that the station and most of the material were already gone. It transpired that the defendant had been contracted by another firm to clear the site, had been advanced a sum for hire of plant, and had spent three weeks clearing the site. Subsequent efforts to trace the second firm failed, and the court found the man not guilty, deciding that he had been duped and left significantly out of pocket.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Cleckheaton has a bus station in the town centre. It has six stands, and the main operator is Arriva. It is owned and maintained by <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire_Passenger_Transport_Executive" class="mw-redirect" title="West Yorkshire Passenger Transport Executive">Metro (WYPTE)</a>, who rebuilt it in April 2005, replacing the previous site owned by <a href="/wiki/Arriva_Yorkshire" title="Arriva Yorkshire">Arriva Yorkshire</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleckheaton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_government">Local government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleckheaton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Local government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Town_Hall_Cleckheaton.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Town_Hall_Cleckheaton.jpg/220px-Town_Hall_Cleckheaton.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Town_Hall_Cleckheaton.jpg/330px-Town_Hall_Cleckheaton.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/Town_Hall_Cleckheaton.jpg/440px-Town_Hall_Cleckheaton.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Town_Hall_Cleckheaton.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cleckheaton Town Hall (1892) by Mawson &amp; Hudson of Bradford</div></div></div>
<p>Cleckheaton adopted the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1858" class="mw-redirect" title="Local Government Act 1858">Local Government Act 1858</a> in 1864 and a <a href="/wiki/Local_board_of_health" title="Local board of health">local board</a> was formed. The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1894" title="Local Government Act 1894">Local Government Act 1894</a> reconstituted the area of the local board as Cleckheaton <a href="/wiki/Urban_District" class="mw-redirect" title="Urban District">Urban District</a>. There were at this time attempts to involve all the local authorities in the valley in joint projects such as installation of sewers and water. In 1915, the three <a href="/wiki/Urban_district_(Great_Britain_and_Ireland)" title="Urban district (Great Britain and Ireland)">urban districts</a> of Cleckheaton, <a href="/wiki/Liversedge" title="Liversedge">Liversedge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gomersal" title="Gomersal">Gomersal</a> were amalgamated to form <a href="/wiki/Spenborough" title="Spenborough">Spenborough Urban District</a>. In 1937 a <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1929" title="Local Government Act 1929">county review order</a> enlarged the urban district to include Birkenshaw, Hunsworth and <a href="/wiki/Hartshead" title="Hartshead">Hartshead</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-youngs_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-youngs-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Spenborough (which included all the Spen Valley except for Heckmondwike) was granted a charter of incorporation and became a <a href="/wiki/Municipal_borough" title="Municipal borough">municipal borough</a> on 23 May 1955. The borough used the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a> which bore the motto "Industry Enriches" which it had been granted in 1949.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 1 April 1974 the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a> reorganised administration throughout <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>. The borough of Spenborough was abolished, and its area combined with that of ten other local authorities to form the Metropolitan Borough of Kirklees, one of five <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan boroughs</a> of <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
Cleckheaton is an <a href="/wiki/Unparished_area" title="Unparished area">unparished area</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ward_(politics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ward (politics)">ward</a> of Kirklees. The population of this ward as taken at the 2011 Census was 16,622.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> It elects three of the borough council's 69 councillors. In 2007 all three councillors were members of the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Democrats_(UK)" title="Liberal Democrats (UK)">Liberal Democrats</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Under the borough council's <a href="/wiki/Decentralisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Decentralisation">decentralisation</a> scheme, Cleckheaton's councillors and those from Heckmondwike and Liversedge and Gomersal form the Spen Valley Area Committee.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In August 2014, it was announced that Cleckheaton councillor <a href="/wiki/Kath_Pinnock" class="mw-redirect" title="Kath Pinnock">Kath Pinnock</a> was to be elevated to the House of Lords as a life peer.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parliamentary_representation">Parliamentary representation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleckheaton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Parliamentary representation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1885 Cleckheaton and the three neighbouring townships, Gomersal, Heckmondwike and Liversedge, were grouped to form the Parliamentary <a href="/wiki/County_constituency" class="mw-redirect" title="County constituency">county constituency</a> of <a href="/wiki/Spen_Valley_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Spen Valley (UK Parliament constituency)">Spen Valley</a> and remained in that constituency until 1950.<sup id="cite_ref-youngs_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-youngs-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The seat was held at various times by the <a href="/wiki/Liberal_Party_(UK)" title="Liberal Party (UK)">Liberal</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Liberal_Party_(UK,_1931)" title="National Liberal Party (UK, 1931)">National Liberal</a> and Labour parties. From 1950 to 1983 Cleckheaton was included in the <a href="/wiki/Borough_constituency" class="mw-redirect" title="Borough constituency">borough constituency</a> of <a href="/wiki/Brighouse_and_Spenborough_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Brighouse and Spenborough (UK Parliament constituency)">Brighouse and Spenborough</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-youngs_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-youngs-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> It was a marginal seat, changing hands between the Conservative and Labour parties on six occasions. Cleckheaton is now in the <a href="/wiki/Batley_and_Spen_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Batley and Spen (UK Parliament constituency)">Batley and Spen</a> borough constituency,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> created in 1983. It was represented by a <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> from 1983 to 1997, then by <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour</a> MP <a href="/wiki/Mike_Wood_(Labour_politician)" title="Mike Wood (Labour politician)">Mike Wood</a> until 2015, when Wood retired and was succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Jo_Cox" title="Jo Cox">Jo Cox</a> (Labour). After Jo Cox was murdered in 2016, she was replaced in a by-election (which was uncontested by the other major parties) by <a href="/wiki/Tracy_Brabin" title="Tracy Brabin">Tracy Brabin</a>, who retained the seat in 2017.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleckheaton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cleckheaton is at the centre of a number of villages which together form the Spen Valley:
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Oakenshaw,_West_Yorkshire" title="Oakenshaw, West Yorkshire">Oakenshaw</a>, East Bierley, Hunsworth, <a href="/wiki/Birkenshaw,_West_Yorkshire" title="Birkenshaw, West Yorkshire">Birkenshaw</a>, Drub, <a href="/wiki/Gomersal" title="Gomersal">Gomersal</a>, Little Gomersal, Littletown, Millsbridge, <a href="/wiki/Liversedge" title="Liversedge">Liversedge</a>, Roberttown, Hartshead, Clifton, <a href="/wiki/Hightown,_West_Yorkshire" title="Hightown, West Yorkshire">Hightown</a>, Heckmondwike and Scholes.
</p><p>The town itself is made up of areas such as Moorend, Whitechapel, Whitcliffe, Moorbottom, Moorside, the Marsh and Rawfolds.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cleckheaton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cleckheaton Sports Club, based at Moorend, is the home of the town's <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a> team, <a href="/wiki/Cleckheaton_RUFC" title="Cleckheaton RUFC">Cleckheaton RUFC</a>., who play in the <a href="/wiki/National_League_3_North" class="mw-redirect" title="National League 3 North">National 3 North</a> league at the fifth tier of English domestic rugby. The club is also home to Cleckheaton Cricket Club, who play in the <a href="/wiki/Bradford_Cricket_League" class="mw-redirect" title="Bradford Cricket League">Bradford Premier Cricket League</a> Division One.  Yorkshire Captain <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Gale" title="Andrew Gale">Andrew Gale</a> is a member of Cleckheaton and occasionally plays, depending on his availability and commitments for Yorkshire. Also the club, are two <a href="/wiki/Bowls" title="Bowls">bowling</a> greens, home to Cleckheaton Sports Bowls Club, which has hosted the BCGBA Senior Merit "All England" Finals four times.  It also hosts lesser annual competitions such as the John Smith's Classic.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Liversedge_F.C." title="Liversedge F.C.">Liversedge F.C.</a> are the town's highest football club in the football league pyramid, currently playing in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Counties_East_Football_League" title="Northern Counties East Football League">Northern Counties East Football League</a> Premier Division as of the <a href="/wiki/2012-13_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="2012-13 in English football">2012–13 season</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Cleanup" title="Wikipedia:Cleanup"><span title="Wikipedia:Cleanup">out of date</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> They play their home games at the Clayborn Ground, 1&#160;km from the town centre.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other cricket clubs from the local area include Spen Victoria, Gomersal, Hartshead Moor and East Bierley from the <a href="/wiki/Bradford_Cricket_League" class="mw-redirect" title="Bradford Cricket League">Bradford Cricket League</a>, and Scholes and Liversedge from the Central Yorkshire Cricket League.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
