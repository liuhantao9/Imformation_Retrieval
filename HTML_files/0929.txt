	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Lem Billings</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>

		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><table class="infobox biography vcard" style="width:22em"><tbody><tr><th colspan="2" style="text-align:center;font-size:125%;font-weight:bold"><div class="fn" style="display:inline">Lem Billings</div></th></tr><tr><th scope="row">Born</th><td><div style="display:inline" class="nickname">Kirk LeMoyne Billings</div><br /><span style="display:none">(<span class="bday">1916-04-15</span>)</span>April 15, 1916<br /><div style="display:inline" class="birthplace"><a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>, Pennsylvania, U.S.</div></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Died</th><td>May 28, 1981<span style="display:none">(1981-05-28)</span> (aged&#160;65)<br /><div style="display:inline" class="deathplace"><a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>, New York, U.S.</div></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Resting place</th><td class="label"><a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Cemetery" title="Allegheny Cemetery">Allegheny Cemetery</a></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Nationality</th><td class="category">American</td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Education</th><td><a href="/wiki/Choate_Rosemary_Hall" title="Choate Rosemary Hall">Choate Rosemary Hall</a></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Alma&#160;mater</th><td><a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">Princeton University</a><br /><a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School" title="Harvard Business School">Harvard Business School</a></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><b>Kirk LeMoyne "Lem" Billings</b> (April 15, 1916 – May 28, 1981) was a close and long-time friend of President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> and the Kennedy family.  Billings was a prep school roommate of Kennedy, an usher at his wedding and a campaigner for his successful 1960 presidential bid. <a href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Kennedy_Sr." title="Joseph P. Kennedy Sr.">Joseph Kennedy Sr.</a> called him "my second son," and he sometimes acted as escort for several of the Kennedy women. Billings served with <a href="/wiki/Sargent_Shriver" title="Sargent Shriver">Sargent Shriver</a> as a trustee for the Kennedy family trusts.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lem_Billings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Billings was born in <a href="/wiki/Pittsburgh" title="Pittsburgh">Pittsburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>, on April 15, 1916, the third child of Frederic Tremaine Billings (1873–1933) and Romaine LeMoyne (1882–1970). His father was a prominent physician and a graduate of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Academy" title="United States Naval Academy">U.S. Naval Academy</a>. His mother was a <i><a href="/wiki/Mayflower" title="Mayflower">Mayflower</a></i> descendant and his great-grandfather <a href="/wiki/Francis_Julius_LeMoyne" title="Francis Julius LeMoyne">Francis Julius LeMoyne</a> was a prominent <a href="/wiki/Abolitionist" class="mw-redirect" title="Abolitionist">abolitionist</a> linked to the underground railroad who helped establish what is today known as <a href="/wiki/LeMoyne%E2%80%93Owen_College" title="LeMoyne–Owen College">LeMoyne-Owen College</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The Billings family were Episcopalians and Republicans.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Billings, a 16-year-old third-year student, and Kennedy, a 15-year-old second-year student, met at <a href="/wiki/Choate_Rosemary_Hall" title="Choate Rosemary Hall">Choate</a>, an elite preparatory school, in the fall of 1933. Billings as a teenager was 6' 2", weighed 175 pounds, and was the strongest member of the Choate crew.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> They became fast friends, drawn to each other by their mutual distaste for their strict school. From Billings's first visit with the Kennedy family for Christmas in <a href="/wiki/Palm_Beach,_Florida" title="Palm Beach, Florida">Palm Beach</a> in 1933, he joined them for holidays, participated in family events, and was treated like a member of the family. The <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Depression</a> had hurt the Billings family financially, and Lem Billings was at Choate on scholarship. Billings repeated his senior year so that he and Kennedy could graduate from Choate together in 1935.
</p><p>Billings and Kennedy took a summer trip through Europe in the summer of 1937. Between Munich and Nuremberg, they bought a <a href="/wiki/Dachshund" title="Dachshund">dachshund</a> they named Dunker, but had to give him up because of Kennedy's allergies.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1939, Billings graduated from Princeton where he majored in art and architecture<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and wrote his senior thesis on <a href="/wiki/Tintoretto" title="Tintoretto">Tintoretto</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1941, Billings failed medical tests required by the military.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In 1942, supported by a recommendation from <a href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Kennedy_Sr." title="Joseph P. Kennedy Sr.">Joseph Kennedy Sr.</a>, his friend's father, who called him "my second son," he won admission to the <a href="/wiki/AFS_Intercultural_Programs#Early_years" title="AFS Intercultural Programs">American Ambulance Field Service</a>, where his poor eyesight was not a disqualification.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> He saw action in North Africa in 1942–43.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In 1944 he received a commission in the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Naval_Reserve" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Naval Reserve">U.S. Naval Reserve</a> and served in the South Pacific until being discharged in 1946.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After working on Kennedy's successful campaign for Congress in 1946, Billings toured seven Latin American countries with <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lem_Billings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>From 1946 to 1948, Billings attended <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Business_School" title="Harvard Business School">Harvard Business School</a> and earned an MBA.<sup id="cite_ref-smith30_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smith30-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  He later had several jobs, including selling <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">Coca-Cola</a> dispensers to drugstores and working at a <a href="/wiki/Genesco" title="Genesco">General</a> Shoe store. As Vice President at the Emerson Drug Company in Baltimore, he was responsible for inventing the 1950s fad drink <a href="/wiki/Fizzies" title="Fizzies">Fizzies</a> by adding a fruit flavor to disguise the <a href="/wiki/Sodium_citrate" title="Sodium citrate">sodium citrate</a> taste.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In 1958, he moved to the Manhattan advertising firm Lennen &amp; Newell<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> as an advertising executive.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On September 12, 1953, Billings was an usher at the wedding of Kennedy and <a href="/wiki/Jacqueline_Kennedy_Onassis" title="Jacqueline Kennedy Onassis">Jacqueline Lee Bouvier</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In 1956 he was an usher at the wedding of Kennedy's sister <a href="/wiki/Jean_Kennedy_Smith" title="Jean Kennedy Smith">Jean</a> to <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Edward_Smith" title="Stephen Edward Smith">Stephen Edward Smith</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1960, on leave from his job, he worked on Kennedy's presidential campaign. He managed the campaign in the Third Congressional District in the Wisconsin primary and then served as general troubleshooter and coordinator of television in the West Virginia primary.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kennedy_administration">Kennedy administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lem_Billings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Kennedy administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1961, Billings declined Kennedy's offer to appoint him the first head of the <a href="/wiki/Peace_Corps" title="Peace Corps">Peace Corps</a>, director of a new agency to promote tourism, the U.S. Travel Service, or ambassador to <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> He later said: "I realized that I did not want to work for the president — because I felt it would change our relationship."<sup id="cite_ref-pitts187_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitts187-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>  In September 1961, he accepted an appointment to the board of trustees of the planned National Cultural Center, which later became the <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Kennedy Center">Kennedy Center</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-pitts187_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitts187-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The next year, Kennedy named him to a board to plan America's participation in the <a href="/wiki/1964_New_York_World%27s_Fair" title="1964 New York World&#39;s Fair">New York World's Fair of 1964–5</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> He represented the President when the alumni association unveiled Kennedy's portrait at Choate in May 1963.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Billings visited the White House for most weekends during the Kennedy Administration. When a butler commented on the fact that Billings was leaving his belongings in one of the third-floor guest rooms, the First Lady replied: "He's been my house guest since I was married."<sup id="cite_ref-collier_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collier-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>244</span></sup> Sometimes he stayed for longer periods.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> When the First Lady was away, Billings organized White House dinner parties for the President and old friends,<sup id="cite_ref-pitts192_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitts192-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and when the President traveled he kept the First Lady company.<sup id="cite_ref-smith29_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smith29-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> One presidential aide later said that "some people saw him so much they thought he was the Secret Service."<sup id="cite_ref-pitts192_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitts192-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Billings never had a White House pass and said: "Jack and Jackie were so nice about this that I didn't even have to tell them whether I was coming or going."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Historian Sally Bedell Smith compared him to <a href="/wiki/Zelig" title="Zelig">Leonard Zelig</a>, a nondescript character in <a href="/wiki/Woody_Allen" title="Woody Allen">Woody Allen</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Zelig" title="Zelig">1983 film</a> who is always present in the back row at major events.<sup id="cite_ref-smith29_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smith29-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> He sat with the President's family at the <a href="/wiki/Inauguration_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="Inauguration of John F. Kennedy">Kennedy inauguration</a> and walked not far behind his widow at the <a href="/wiki/State_funeral_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="State funeral of John F. Kennedy">Kennedy funeral</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The press frequently reported on his presence at Kennedy family events, such as the arrival of the Kennedy children in Washington in February 1961.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> He accompanied the President to church,<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> launched a kite for the President's daughter <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Kennedy" title="Caroline Kennedy">Caroline</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> and delivered pet hamsters to the Kennedy children.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> He joined the President's entourage for his tours of Europe in both 1961 and 1963.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> In 1962 he escorted two of the President's sisters, <a href="/wiki/Eunice_Kennedy_Shriver" title="Eunice Kennedy Shriver">Eunice Shriver</a> and Jean Kennedy Smith, around Europe for more than two weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>  When the Kennedys spent the weekend at Glen Ora, their <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> estate, Jacqueline Kennedy invited Billings to join them more often than the President did. She needed Billings to keep the President company while she went horseback riding.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Billings' role as "first friend" was assessed by many observers at the time and since. <a href="/wiki/Ted_Sorensen" title="Ted Sorensen">Ted Sorensen</a> called him "an admirer — almost a fawning admirer — of his friend."<sup id="cite_ref-pitts192_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitts192-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Arthur_M._Schlesinger_Jr." title="Arthur M. Schlesinger Jr.">Arthur Schlesinger</a> thought Billings "used to glare at me when we occasionally encountered each other in the company of JFK, and for a time I took this rather personally. Soon I discovered that he glared with equal suspicion at anyone whose friendship with JFK postdated his own."<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Another said: "Members of the president's staff thought of him as a 'handy old piece of furniture.'"<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most recognized that Billings and Kennedy had been friends from youth and did not question their relationship or Billings' presence. <a href="/wiki/Ben_Bradlee" title="Ben Bradlee">Ben Bradlee</a>, a Kennedy friend who worked at <i>Newsweek</i> during the Kennedy Administration, and no friend of Billings,<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> said "they were childhood friends and stayed loyal to each other forever." Billings, he said, "had a natural jealousy. He didn't want to share his friendship with Jack."<sup id="cite_ref-pitt197_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitt197-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gore_Vidal" title="Gore Vidal">Gore Vidal</a>, who was banned from the White House after a run-in with Billings,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> was critical of Billings,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> but also thought Billings played an important role as an aide to Kennedy, who was often ill or in pain. "He needed Lem Billings to get around — better than a trained nurse" that would have made his political career impossible. Vidal thought Jacqueline Kennedy thought Billings "was kind of a nothing ... but Jack needed him and she was practical."<sup id="cite_ref-pitts232_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitts232-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many testify to Billings' wit and ability to help the President relax. He once described the Kennedy family's lack of business awareness: "Listening to the Kennedy brothers talk about business was like hearing nuns talk about sex."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Billings also served the President as an artistic adviser, selecting <a href="/wiki/Scrimshaw" title="Scrimshaw">scrimshaw</a> for display in the Oval Office<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> and, on one European tour, quickly assembling a selection of artworks to be presented as gifts.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Billings spent less time with the President in the fall of 1963. One of their friends thought "that Jackie was trying to close Lem out."<sup id="cite_ref-smith510_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smith510-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Billings spent the last weekend of October 1963 with the couple, the last time he saw them together.<sup id="cite_ref-smith510_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-smith510-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Billings saw the President for the last time when they dined at the White House with <a href="/wiki/Greta_Garbo" title="Greta Garbo">Greta Garbo</a> on November 13, 1963, nine days before the President's <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="Assassination of John F. Kennedy">assassination in Dallas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lem_Billings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Friends from the 1970s confirmed that Billings was homosexual, but not open to discussing it.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> In 2006, looking back to the Kennedy Administration, Ben Bradlee said: "I suppose it's known that Lem was gay....It impressed me that Jack had gay friends."<sup id="cite_ref-pitt197_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitt197-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> At the same time, he admitted that no one ever expressed the idea aloud during Kennedy's White House years.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Red Fay, a friend of the President from his World War II service, said of Billings: "I didn't see anything overtly gay about him; I think he was neutral."<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> One historian wrote that after the 1963 assassination Billings was:  "probably the saddest of the Kennedy 'widows'."<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Though newspapers often mentioned Billings' attendance at major social events, they identified him either as the escort of a member of the Kennedy family or included him in a list of Kennedy friends. Otherwise he attended without female accompaniment.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Though there has never been any proof of homosexual activity between them, John F. Kennedy would often, and even when married, share a bedroom with Billings.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some historians believe that Billings expressed his sexual interest in Kennedy in writing in 1934 and that Kennedy rebuffed his advances.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy knew Billings had rightly been accused of homosexual behavior by a fellow Choate student during their years there.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Charles_L._Bartlett_(journalist)" title="Charles L. Bartlett (journalist)">Charles L. Bartlett</a>, a journalist who introduced Kennedy to <a href="/wiki/Jacqueline_Kennedy_Onassis" title="Jacqueline Kennedy Onassis">Jacqueline Bouvier</a><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> and friend of both Billings and Kennedy, described their relationship: "Lem was a stable presence for Jack. Lem's raison d'être was Jack Kennedy. I don't think it's true that he did not have views of his own, as some have said. He had a very independent mind. He had interests of his own that Jack didn't necessarily share. He certainly didn't have the same interest in politics and women that Jack had."<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Though Gore Vidal thought Billings was "absolutely nobody," he also believed "it was a good idea that Jack had somebody he could trust like that around him." He believed Billings loved Kennedy.<sup id="cite_ref-pitts232_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitts232-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> "Jack made a big difference in my life," Billings said. "Because of him, I was never lonely. He may have been the reason I never got married."<sup id="cite_ref-nhr_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhr-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lem_Billings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1964, Billings was named to select a memorial to Kennedy to be placed in the Kennedy Center.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> In 1965, Jacqueline Kennedy invited Billings to accompany her and her children to England for the unveiling of a memorial to President Kennedy at <a href="/wiki/Runnymede" title="Runnymede">Runnymede</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nhr_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nhr-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He escorted Jean Kennedy Smith, sister of the President, to a gala ballet performance in 1966<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ethel_Kennedy" title="Ethel Kennedy">Mrs. Robert Kennedy</a> to the 1971 opening of the Kennedy Center.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the assassination of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Kennedy">Robert Kennedy</a> in 1968, Billings became depressed and started to drink in excess, an addiction that plagued him for the majority of his life.<sup id="cite_ref-pitts_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitts-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-collier_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collier-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>505</span></sup> He maintained close ties to the Kennedys and their children for the rest of his life, frequently socializing with <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy_Jr." title="Robert F. Kennedy Jr.">Bobby Kennedy Jr.</a>, to whom he became almost a surrogate father, and <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Lawford" title="Christopher Lawford">Christopher Lawford</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pitts,_293_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pitts,_293-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Billings' behavior changed drastically in the late 1960s.<sup id="cite_ref-collier_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collier-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>364</span></sup> According to Kennedy family members, such as Peter Lawford, and others, Billings began using drugs<sup id="cite_ref-collier_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collier-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>365</span></sup> due to the influence of the Kennedy and Lawford boys. The elder Kennedys began to discourage the boys from associating with Billings because of his excessive recreational drug use (including alcohol).<sup id="cite_ref-pitts_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitts-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Billings served for many years along with <a href="/wiki/Sargent_Shriver" title="Sargent Shriver">Sargent Shriver</a> as a trustee for the Kennedy family trusts,<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> working from an office in the <a href="/wiki/Pan_Am_Building" class="mw-redirect" title="Pan Am Building">Pan Am Building</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pitts,_293_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pitts,_293-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Jacqueline Kennedy Onassis included Billings as a guest at a party marking the birthdays of her children <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Kennedy" title="Caroline Kennedy">Caroline</a> (21st) and <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_Jr." title="John F. Kennedy Jr.">John Jr.</a> (18th) in 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1987, historian <a href="/wiki/Doris_Kearns_Goodwin" title="Doris Kearns Goodwin">Doris Kearns Goodwin</a> described how Billings structured her interviews with him. She had to submit questions in advance. Billings then prepared responses and read them aloud to her.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lem_Billings&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On May 28, 1981, Billings died in his sleep following a heart attack in his Manhattan apartment.<sup id="cite_ref-pitts306_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitts306-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>  His dying wish was for the young Kennedy men to carry his casket to its final resting place. When they arrived at the cemetery, it was already in place to be lowered. The young Kennedys took the casket and carried it around the gravesite before returning it to the burial plot.<sup id="cite_ref-pitts306_69-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pitts306-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> He is buried in <a href="/wiki/Allegheny_Cemetery" title="Allegheny Cemetery">Allegheny Cemetery</a> in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
