<p>The <b>Purple Heart</b> is a <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> <a href="/wiki/Awards_and_decorations_of_the_United_States_military" class="mw-redirect" title="Awards and decorations of the United States military">military decoration</a> awarded in the name of the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> to those wounded or killed while serving, on or after April 5, 1917, with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Armed_Forces" title="United States Armed Forces">U.S. military</a>. With its forerunner, the <a href="/wiki/Badge_of_Military_Merit" title="Badge of Military Merit">Badge of Military Merit</a>, which took the form of a heart made of purple cloth, the Purple Heart is the oldest <a href="/wiki/Military_award" class="mw-redirect" title="Military award">military award</a> still given to U.S. military members &#8211; the only earlier award being the obsolete <a href="/wiki/Fidelity_Medallion" title="Fidelity Medallion">Fidelity Medallion</a>. The <a href="/wiki/National_Purple_Heart_Hall_of_Honor" title="National Purple Heart Hall of Honor">National Purple Heart Hall of Honor</a> is located in <a href="/wiki/New_Windsor,_New_York" title="New Windsor, New York">New Windsor, New York</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_Heart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original Purple Heart, designated as the <a href="/wiki/Badge_of_Military_Merit" title="Badge of Military Merit">Badge of Military Merit</a>, was established by <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a> &#8211; then the <a href="/wiki/Commander-in-chief" title="Commander-in-chief">commander-in-chief</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Army" title="Continental Army">Continental Army</a> &#8211; by order from his <a href="/wiki/Washington%27s_Headquarters_State_Historic_Site" title="Washington&#39;s Headquarters State Historic Site">Newburgh, New York headquarters</a> on August 7, 1782. The Badge of Military Merit was only awarded to three <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">Revolutionary War</a> soldiers by Gen. George Washington himself. General Washington authorized his subordinate officers to issue Badges of Merit as appropriate. From then on, as its legend grew, so did its appearance. Although never abolished, the award of the badge was not proposed again officially until after <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tioh_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tioh-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Purple_Heart_History_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purple_Heart_History-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On October 10, 1927, <a href="/wiki/Chief_of_Staff_of_the_United_States_Army" title="Chief of Staff of the United States Army">Army Chief of Staff</a> General <a href="/wiki/Charles_Pelot_Summerall" title="Charles Pelot Summerall">Charles Pelot Summerall</a> directed that a draft bill be sent to Congress "to revive the Badge of Military Merit". The bill was withdrawn and action on the case ceased January 3, 1928, but the office of the <a href="/wiki/Adjutant_General" class="mw-redirect" title="Adjutant General">Adjutant General</a> was instructed to file all materials collected for possible future use. A number of private interests sought to have the medal re-instituted in the Army; this included the board of directors of the <a href="/wiki/Fort_Ticonderoga" title="Fort Ticonderoga">Fort Ticonderoga Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ticonderoga,_New_York" title="Ticonderoga, New York">Ticonderoga, New York</a>.
</p><p>On January 7, 1931, Summerall's successor, General <a href="/wiki/Douglas_MacArthur" title="Douglas MacArthur">Douglas MacArthur</a>, confidentially reopened work on a new design, involving the Washington Commission of Fine Arts. Elizabeth Will, an Army <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">heraldic</a> specialist in the Office of the <a href="/wiki/Quartermaster_General" class="mw-redirect" title="Quartermaster General">Quartermaster General</a>, was named to redesign the newly revived medal, which became known as the Purple Heart. Using general specifications provided to her, Will created the design sketch for the present medal of the Purple Heart. The new design, which exhibits a bust and profile of <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>, was issued on the bicentennial of Washington's birth. Will's <a href="/wiki/Obituary" title="Obituary">obituary</a>, in the edition of February 8, 1975 of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">The Washington Post</a></i> newspaper, reflects her many contributions to military heraldry.
</p>

<p>The Commission of Fine Arts solicited plaster models from three leading sculptors for the medal, selecting that of <a href="/wiki/John_R._Sinnock" title="John R. Sinnock">John R. Sinnock</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_Mint" title="Philadelphia Mint">Philadelphia Mint</a> in May 1931. By Executive Order of the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a>, the Purple Heart was revived on the 200th Anniversary of George Washington's birth, out of respect to his memory and military achievements, by War Department <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/General_Order_No._3" class="extiw" title="s:General Order No. 3">General Order No. 3</a>, dated February 22, 1932.  
</p><p>The criteria were announced in a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_War" title="United States Department of War">War Department</a> circular dated February 22, 1932, and authorized award to soldiers, upon their request, who had been awarded the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Meritorious_Service_Citation_Certificate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Meritorious Service Citation Certificate (page does not exist)">Meritorious Service Citation Certificate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Army_Wound_Ribbon" title="Army Wound Ribbon">Army Wound Ribbon</a>, or were authorized to wear <a href="/wiki/Wound_Chevron" title="Wound Chevron">Wound Chevrons</a> subsequent to April 5, 1917, the day before the United States entered <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. The first Purple Heart was awarded to MacArthur. During the early period of American involvement in <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> (December 8, 1941 – September 22, 1943), the Purple Heart was awarded both for wounds received in action against the enemy and for meritorious performance of duty. With the establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Merit" title="Legion of Merit">Legion of Merit</a>, by an Act of Congress, the practice of awarding the Purple Heart for meritorious service was discontinued. By <a href="/wiki/Executive_order_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive order (United States)">Executive Order</a> <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Executive_Order_9277" class="extiw" title="s:Executive Order 9277">9277</a>, dated December 3, 1942, the decoration was applied to all services; the order required reasonable uniform application of the regulations for each of the Services. This executive order also authorized the award only for wounds received. For both military and civilian personnel during the World War II era, to meet eligibility for the Purple Heart, AR 600-45, dated September 22, 1943, and May 3, 1944, required identification of circumstances.
</p><p>After the award was re-authorized in 1932 some U.S. Army wounded from conflicts prior to the first World War applied for, and were awarded, the Purple Heart: "...veterans of the Civil War and Indian Wars, as well as the Spanish–American War, China Relief Expedition (Boxer Rebellion), and Philippine Insurrection also were awarded the Purple Heart. This is because the original regulations governing the award of the Purple Heart, published by the Army in 1932, provided that any soldier who had been wounded in any conflict involving U.S. Army personnel might apply for the new medal. There were but two requirements: the applicant had to be alive at the time of application (no posthumous awards were permitted) and he had to prove that he had received a wound that necessitated treatment by a medical officer."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Subject to approval of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">Secretary of Defense</a>, <a href="/wiki/Executive_order_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive order (United States)">Executive Order</a> <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Executive_Order_10409" class="extiw" title="s:Executive Order 10409">10409</a>, dated February 12, 1952, revised authorizations to include the Service Secretaries. Dated April 25, 1962, <a href="/wiki/Executive_order_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive order (United States)">Executive Order</a> <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Executive_Order_11016" class="extiw" title="s:Executive Order 11016">11016</a>, included provisions for posthumous award of the Purple Heart. Dated February 23, 1984, <a href="/wiki/Executive_order_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive order (United States)">Executive Order</a> <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Executive_Order_12464" class="extiw" title="s:Executive Order 12464">12464</a>, authorized award of the Purple Heart as a result of terrorist attacks, or while serving as part of a peacekeeping force, subsequent to March 28, 1973.
</p><p>On June 13, 1985, the Senate approved an amendment to the 1985 Defense Authorization Bill, which changed the precedence of the Purple Heart award, from immediately above the <a href="/wiki/Good_Conduct_Medal_(United_States)" title="Good Conduct Medal (United States)">Good Conduct Medal</a> to immediately above the <a href="/wiki/Meritorious_Service_Medal_(United_States)" title="Meritorious Service Medal (United States)">Meritorious Service Medals</a>. Public Law 99-145 authorized the award for wounds received as a result of <a href="/wiki/Friendly_fire" title="Friendly fire">friendly fire</a>. Public Law 104-106 expanded the eligibility date, authorizing award of the Purple Heart to a former <a href="/wiki/Prisoner_of_war" title="Prisoner of war">prisoner of war</a> who was wounded after April 25, 1962. The National Defense Authorization Act for Fiscal Year 1998 (Public Law 105-85) changed the criteria to delete authorization for award of the Purple Heart to any non-military U.S. national serving under competent authority in any capacity with the Armed Forces. This change was effective May 18, 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During World War II, 1,506,000 Purple Heart medals were manufactured, many in anticipation of the estimated casualties resulting from the planned Allied <a href="/wiki/Operation_Downfall" title="Operation Downfall">invasion of Japan</a>. By the end of the war, even accounting for medals lost, stolen or wasted, nearly 500,000 remained. To the present date, total combined American military casualties of the seventy years following the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>—including the <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam Wars</a>—have not exceeded that number. In 2000, there remained 120,000 Purple Heart medals in stock. The existing surplus allowed combat units in <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">Iraq</a> and <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> to keep Purple Hearts on-hand for immediate award to soldiers wounded in the field.<sup id="cite_ref-surplus_hearts_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-surplus_hearts-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The "History" section of the November 2009 edition of <i><a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_(magazine)" class="mw-redirect" title="National Geographic (magazine)">National Geographic</a></i> estimated the number of Purple Hearts given. Above the estimates, the text reads, "Any tally of Purple Hearts is an estimate. Awards are often given during conflict; records aren't always exact" (page 33).<sup id="cite_ref-natgeo_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-natgeo-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The estimates are as follows:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>: 320,518</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>: 1,076,245</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>: 118,650</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>: 351,794</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Persian_Gulf_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Persian Gulf War">Persian Gulf War</a>: 607</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/War_in_Afghanistan_(2001%E2%80%93present)" title="War in Afghanistan (2001–present)">Afghanistan War</a>: 7,027 (as of June 5, 2010)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">Iraq War</a>: 35,321 (as of June 5, 2010)</li></ul>
<p>August 7 of every year is recognized as "National Purple Heart Day."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criteria">Criteria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_Heart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Criteria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flickr_-_The_U.S._Army_-_Purple_Heart_honors.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Flickr_-_The_U.S._Army_-_Purple_Heart_honors.jpg/220px-Flickr_-_The_U.S._Army_-_Purple_Heart_honors.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Flickr_-_The_U.S._Army_-_Purple_Heart_honors.jpg/330px-Flickr_-_The_U.S._Army_-_Purple_Heart_honors.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Flickr_-_The_U.S._Army_-_Purple_Heart_honors.jpg/440px-Flickr_-_The_U.S._Army_-_Purple_Heart_honors.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1947" data-file-height="1298" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flickr_-_The_U.S._Army_-_Purple_Heart_honors.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Admiral <a href="/wiki/Michael_Mullen" title="Michael Mullen">Mike Mullen</a> reads the citations for seven soldiers receiving Purple Hearts for wounds sustained in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Army.mil-30837-2009-02-20-130233.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Army.mil-30837-2009-02-20-130233.jpg/220px-Army.mil-30837-2009-02-20-130233.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="157" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Army.mil-30837-2009-02-20-130233.jpg/330px-Army.mil-30837-2009-02-20-130233.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Army.mil-30837-2009-02-20-130233.jpg/440px-Army.mil-30837-2009-02-20-130233.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2100" data-file-height="1500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Army.mil-30837-2009-02-20-130233.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A soldier is awarded the Purple Heart during a ceremony on <a href="/wiki/Coleman_Army_Airfield" title="Coleman Army Airfield">Coleman Barracks</a></div></div></div>
<p>The Purple Heart is awarded in the name of the President of the United States to any member of the Armed Forces of the United States who, while serving under competent authority in any capacity with one of the U.S. Armed Services after April 5, 1917, has been wounded or killed. Specific examples of services which warrant the Purple Heart includes:
</p>
<dl><dd>a) any action against an enemy of the United States;</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>b) any action with an opposing armed force of a foreign country in which the Armed Forces of the United States are or have been engaged;</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>c) while serving with friendly foreign forces engaged in an armed conflict against an opposing armed force in which the United States is not a belligerent party;</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>d) as a result of an act of any such enemy or opposing armed forces; or</dd></dl>
<dl><dd>e) as a result of an act of any hostile foreign force.</dd></dl>
<p>The two letters c) and e) were added by <a href="/wiki/Executive_order_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive order (United States)">Executive Order</a> <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Executive_Order_11016" class="extiw" title="s:Executive Order 11016">11016</a> on April 25, 1962, as U.S. service personnel were being sent to <a href="/wiki/South_Vietnam" title="South Vietnam">South Vietnam</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> as <a href="/wiki/Military_advisor" title="Military advisor">military advisors</a> rather than <a href="/wiki/Combatant" title="Combatant">combatants</a>. As many were being killed or wounded while serving in that capacity in South Vietnam, and because the United States was not formally a participant of the war (until 1965), there was no “enemy” to satisfy the requirement of a wound or death received “in action against an enemy.” In response, President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> signed the executive order that awarded to any person wounded or killed “while serving with friendly foreign forces” or “as a result of action by a hostile foreign force.”<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After March 28, 1973, it may be awarded as a result of an international terrorist attack against the United States or a foreign nation friendly to the United States, recognized as such an attack by the Secretary of the Army, or jointly by the Secretaries of the separate armed services concerned if persons from more than one service are wounded in the attack. Also, it may be awarded as a result of military operations while serving outside the territory of the United States as part of a peacekeeping force.<sup id="cite_ref-AR600–8–22_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AR600–8–22-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Purple Heart differs from most other decorations in that an individual is not "recommended" for the decoration; rather he or she is entitled to it upon meeting specific criteria. A Purple Heart is awarded for the first wound suffered under conditions indicated above, but for each subsequent award an <a href="/wiki/Oak_leaf_cluster" title="Oak leaf cluster">oak leaf cluster</a> or <a href="/wiki/5/16_inch_star" title="5/16 inch star">5/16 inch star</a> is worn in lieu of another medal. Not more than one award will be made for more than one wound or injury received at the same instant.
</p><p>A "wound" is defined as an injury to any part of the body from an outside force or agent sustained under one or more of the conditions listed above. A physical lesion is not required; however, the wound for which the award is made must have required treatment by a medical officer and records of medical treatment for wounds or injuries received in action must have been made a matter of official record. When contemplating an award of this decoration, the key issue that commanders must take into consideration is the degree to which the enemy caused the injury. The fact that the proposed recipient was participating in direct or indirect combat operations is a necessary prerequisite, but is not sole justification for award. The Purple Heart is not awarded for non-combat injuries.<sup id="cite_ref-AR600–8–22_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AR600–8–22-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Enemy-related injuries which <i>justify</i> the award of the Purple Heart include: injury caused by enemy <a href="/wiki/Bullet" title="Bullet">bullet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fragmentation_(weaponry)" title="Fragmentation (weaponry)">shrapnel</a>, or other projectile created by enemy action; injury caused by enemy placed <a href="/wiki/Land_mine" title="Land mine">land mine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naval_mine" title="Naval mine">naval mine</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Booby_trap" title="Booby trap">trap</a>; injury caused by enemy released <a href="/wiki/Chemical,_biological,_radiological,_and_nuclear" class="mw-redirect" title="Chemical, biological, radiological, and nuclear">chemical, biological, or nuclear agent</a>; injury caused by vehicle or aircraft accident resulting from enemy fire; and, concussion injuries caused as a result of enemy generated explosions.
</p><p>Injuries or wounds which <i>do not qualify</i> for award of the Purple Heart include <a href="/wiki/Frostbite" title="Frostbite">frostbite</a> or <a href="/wiki/Trench_foot" title="Trench foot">trench foot</a> injuries; <a href="/wiki/Heat_stroke" title="Heat stroke">heat stroke</a>; <a href="/wiki/Food_poisoning" class="mw-redirect" title="Food poisoning">food poisoning</a> not caused by enemy agents; chemical, biological, or nuclear agents not released by the enemy; <a href="/wiki/Posttraumatic_stress_disorder" title="Posttraumatic stress disorder">battle fatigue</a>; disease not directly caused by enemy agents; accidents, to include explosive, aircraft, vehicular, and other accidental wounding not related to or caused by enemy action; self-inflicted wounds (e.g., a soldier accidentally or intentionally fires their own gun and the bullet strikes his or her leg), except when in the heat of battle, and not involving gross negligence; <a href="/wiki/Post-traumatic_stress_disorders" class="mw-redirect" title="Post-traumatic stress disorders">post-traumatic stress disorders</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and jump injuries not caused by enemy action.
</p><p>It is not intended that such a strict interpretation of the requirement for the wound or injury to be caused by direct result of hostile action be taken that it would preclude the award being made to deserving personnel. Commanders must also take into consideration the circumstances surrounding an injury, even if it appears to meet the criteria. In the case of an individual injured while making a parachute landing from an aircraft that had been brought down by enemy fire; or, an individual injured as a result of a vehicle accident caused by enemy fire, the decision will be made in favor of the individual and the award will be made. As well, individuals wounded or killed as a result of "<a href="/wiki/Friendly_fire" title="Friendly fire">friendly fire</a>" in the "heat of battle" will be awarded the Purple Heart as long as the "friendly" projectile or agent was released with the full intent of inflicting damage or destroying enemy troops or equipment. Individuals injured as a result of their own negligence, such as by driving or walking through an unauthorized area known to have been mined or placed off limits or searching for or picking up unexploded munitions as war souvenirs, will not be awarded the Purple Heart as they clearly were not injured as a result of enemy action, but rather by their own negligence.
</p><p>Animals are generally not eligible for the Purple Heart; however, there have been rare instances when animals holding military rank were honored with the award. An example includes the horse <a href="/wiki/Sergeant_Reckless" title="Sergeant Reckless">Sergeant Reckless</a> during the Korean War.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Former_eligibility">Former eligibility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_Heart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Former eligibility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1942 to 1997, non-military personnel serving or closely affiliated with the armed forces—as government employees, <a href="/wiki/Red_Cross" class="mw-redirect" title="Red Cross">Red Cross</a> workers, <a href="/wiki/War_correspondent" title="War correspondent">war correspondents</a>, and the like—were eligible to receive the Purple Heart whether in peacetime or armed conflicts. Among the earliest to receive the award were nine <a href="/wiki/Honolulu_Fire_Department" title="Honolulu Fire Department">Honolulu Fire Department</a> (HFD) firefighters killed or wounded in peacetime while fighting fires at <a href="/wiki/Hickam_Field" class="mw-redirect" title="Hickam Field">Hickam Field</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Attack_on_Pearl_Harbor" title="Attack on Pearl Harbor">attack on Pearl Harbor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> About 100 men and women received the award, the most famous being newspaperman <a href="/wiki/Ernie_Pyle" title="Ernie Pyle">Ernie Pyle</a> who was awarded a Purple Heart posthumously by the Army after being killed by Japanese machine gun fire in the Pacific Theater, near the end of World War II. Before his death, Pyle had seen and experienced combat in the European Theater, while accompanying and writing about infantrymen for the folks back home.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  Those serving in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Merchant_Marine" title="United States Merchant Marine">Merchant Marine</a> are not eligible for the award.  During World War II, members of this service who met the Purple Heart criteria received a <a href="/wiki/Merchant_Marine_Mariner%27s_Medal" title="Merchant Marine Mariner&#39;s Medal">Merchant Marine Mariner's Medal</a> instead.
</p><p>The most recent Purple Hearts presented to non-military personnel occurred after the <a href="/wiki/Khobar_Towers_bombing" title="Khobar Towers bombing">terrorist attacks at Khobar Towers</a>, Saudi Arabia, in 1996—for their injuries, about 40 <a href="/wiki/United_States_federal_civil_service" title="United States federal civil service">U.S. civil service</a> employees received the award.
</p><p>However, in 1997, at the urging of the <a href="/wiki/Military_Order_of_the_Purple_Heart" title="Military Order of the Purple Heart">Military Order of the Purple Heart</a>, Congress passed legislation prohibiting future awards of the Purple Heart to non-military personnel. Civilian employees of the U.S. Department of Defense who are killed or wounded as a result of hostile action may receive the new <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_Defense_Medal_for_the_Defense_of_Freedom" title="Secretary of Defense Medal for the Defense of Freedom">Defense of Freedom Medal</a>. This award was created shortly after the <a href="/wiki/September_11_attacks" title="September 11 attacks">terrorist attacks of September 11, 2001</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">Appearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_Heart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Appearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Purple Heart award is a <a href="/wiki/Heart_(symbol)" title="Heart (symbol)">heart</a>-shaped medal within a gold border, <span class="frac nowrap">1<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>3</sup>&#8260;<sub>8</sub></span> inches (35&#160;mm) wide, containing a profile of General <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>. Above the heart appears a shield of the <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_Arms_of_George_Washington" class="mw-redirect" title="Coat of Arms of George Washington">coat of arms of George Washington</a> (a white shield with two red bars and three red stars in chief) between sprays of green leaves. The reverse consists of a raised bronze heart with the words <small>FOR MILITARY MERIT</small> below the coat of arms and leaves.
</p><p>The ribbon is <span class="frac nowrap">1<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>3</sup>&#8260;<sub>8</sub></span> inches (35&#160;mm) wide and consists of the following stripes: <span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>8</sub></span> inch (3.2&#160;mm) white 67101; <span class="frac nowrap">1<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>8</sub></span> inches (29&#160;mm) purple 67115; and <span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>8</sub></span> inch (3.2&#160;mm) white 67101.<sup id="cite_ref-tioh_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tioh-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

Grosso</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joint_Base_McGuire-Dix-Lakehurst" class="mw-redirect" title="Joint Base McGuire-Dix-Lakehurst">Joint Base McGuire-Dix-Lakehurst</a> commander</div></div></div>
<p>Current active duty personnel are awarded the Purple Heart upon recommendation from their <a href="/wiki/Command_hierarchy" title="Command hierarchy">chain of command</a>, stating the injury that was received and the action in which the service member was wounded. The award authority for the Purple Heart is normally at the level of an <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">Army</a> <a href="/wiki/Brigade" title="Brigade">Brigade</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">Marine Corps</a> <a href="/wiki/Division_(military)" title="Division (military)">Division</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">Air Force</a> Wing, or <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">Navy</a> Task Force. While the award of the Purple Heart is considered automatic for all wounds received in combat, each award presentation must still be reviewed to ensure that the wounds received were as a result of enemy action. Modern day Purple Heart presentations are recorded in both hardcopy and electronic service records. The annotation of the Purple Heart is denoted both with the service member's parent command and at the headquarters of the military service department. An original citation and award certificate are presented to the service member and filed in the field service record.
</p>

<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korean_War" title="Korean War">Korean War</a>, and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the Purple Heart was often awarded on the spot, with occasional entries made into service records. In addition, during mass demobilizations following each of America's major wars of the 20th century, it was common occurrence to omit mention from service records of a Purple Heart award. This occurred due to clerical errors, and became problematic once a service record was closed upon discharge. In terms of keeping accurate records, it was commonplace for some field commanders to engage in bedside presentations of the Purple Heart. This typically entailed a <a href="/wiki/General_officer" title="General officer">general</a> entering a hospital with a box of Purple Hearts, pinning them on the pillows of wounded service members, then departing with no official records kept of the visit, or the award of the Purple Heart. Service members, themselves, complicated matters by unofficially leaving hospitals, hastily returning to their units to rejoin battle so as to not appear a malingerer. In such cases, even if a service member had received actual wounds in combat, both the award of the Purple Heart, as well as the entire visit to the hospital, was unrecorded in official records.
</p><p>Service members requesting retroactive awards of the Purple Heart must normally apply through the <a href="/wiki/National_Personnel_Records_Center" title="National Personnel Records Center">National Personnel Records Center</a>. Following a review of service records, qualified Army members are awarded the Purple Heart by the U.S. Army Human Resources Command in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Knox" title="Fort Knox">Fort Knox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a>. Air Force veterans are awarded the Purple Heart by the Awards Office of <a href="/wiki/Randolph_Air_Force_Base" title="Randolph Air Force Base">Randolph Air Force Base</a>, while Navy, Marine Corps, and Coast Guard, present Purple Hearts to veterans through the Navy Liaison Officer at the National Personnel Records Center. Simple clerical errors, where a Purple Heart is denoted in military records, but was simply omitted from a (WD AGO Form 53-55 (predecessor to the) <a href="/wiki/DD_Form_214" title="DD Form 214">DD Form 214</a> (Report of Separation), are corrected on site at the National Personnel Records Center through issuance of a DD-215 document.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Retroactive_requests">Retroactive requests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_Heart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Retroactive requests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Because the Purple Heart did not exist prior to 1932, decoration records are not annotated in the service histories of veterans wounded, or killed, by enemy action, prior to establishment of the medal. The Purple Heart is, however, retroactive to 1917 meaning it may be presented to veterans as far back as  the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>. Prior to 2006, service departments would review all available records, including older service records, and service histories, to determine if a veteran warranted a retroactive Purple Heart. As of 2008, such records are listed as "Archival", by the National Archives and Records Administration, meaning they have been transferred from the custody of the military, and can no longer be loaned and transferred for retroactive medals determination. In such cases, requestors asking for a Purple Heart (especially from records of the First World War) are provided with a complete copy of all available records (or reconstructed records in the case of the <a href="/wiki/1973_National_Archives_Fire" class="mw-redirect" title="1973 National Archives Fire">1973 fire</a>) and advised the Purple Heart may be privately purchased if the requestor feels it is warranted.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>A clause to the archival procedures was revised in mid-2008, where if a veteran, or, if deceased, an immediate member of the family, requested the Purple Heart, on an Army or Air Force record, the medal could still be granted by the National Archives. In such cases, where a determination was required made by the military service department, photocopies of the archival record, (but not the record itself), would be forwarded to the headquarters of the military branch in question. This stipulation was granted only for the Air Force and Army; Marine Corps, Navy, and Coast Guard archival medals requests are still typically only offered a copy of the file and told to purchase the medal privately. For requests directly received from veterans, these are routed through a Navy Liaison Office, on site at 9700 Page Avenue, St. Louis, MO 63132-5100 (the location of the Military Personnel Records Center).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Destroyed_record_requests">Destroyed record requests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Purple_Heart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Destroyed record requests">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to the <a href="/wiki/1973_National_Archives_Fire" class="mw-redirect" title="1973 National Archives Fire">1973 National Archives Fire</a>, a large number of retroactive Purple Heart requests are difficult to verify because all records to substantiate the award may have been destroyed. As a solution to deal with Purple Heart requests, where service records were destroyed in the 1973 fire, the National Personnel Records Center maintains a separate office. In such cases, NPRC searches through unit records, military pay records, and records of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Veterans_Affairs" title="United States Department of Veterans Affairs">Department of Veterans Affairs</a>. If a Purple Heart is warranted, all available alternate records sources are forwarded to the military service department for final determination of issuance.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The loaning of fire related records to the military has declined since 2006 because a large number of such records now fall into the "archival records" category of military service records. This means the records were transferred from the military to the National Archives, and in such cases, the Purple Heart may be privately purchased by the requestor (see above section of retroactive requests for further details) but is no longer provided by the military service department.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
