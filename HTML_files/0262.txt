<p><b>Decolonization of the Americas</b> refers to the process by which the countries in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a> gained their independence from <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> rule. <a href="/wiki/Decolonization" title="Decolonization">Decolonization</a> began with a series of revolutions in the late 18th and early to mid-19th centuries. The status quo then prevailed for more than a century, excepting the independence of Cuba (whose war for independence culminated in the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">Spanish–American War</a>).
</p><p>Peaceful independence by voluntary withdrawal of colonial powers then became the norm in the second half of the 20th century. However, there are still many British and Dutch colonies in North America (mostly Caribbean islands), and France has fully integrated most of its former colonies as fully constituent <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">departments</a> of France.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decolonization_of_the_Americas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a> and <a href="/wiki/History_of_United_States_continental_expansion" class="mw-redirect" title="History of United States continental expansion">History of United States continental expansion</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States of America</a> declared independence from <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Great Britain</a> on July 4, 1776, thus becoming the first independent, foreign-recognized nation in the Americas and the first European colonial entity to break from its mother country. Britain formally acknowledged American independence in 1783 after its defeat in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>.
</p><p>Although initially occupying only the land east of the <a href="/wiki/Mississippi_River" title="Mississippi River">Mississippi</a> between Canada and Florida, the United States would later eventually <a href="/wiki/Territorial_evolution_of_the_United_States" title="Territorial evolution of the United States">acquire various other North American territories</a> from the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_colonial_empire" title="French colonial empire">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russians</a> in succeeding years, effectively decolonizing these areas formerly under European control.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Haiti_and_the_French_Antilles">Haiti and the French Antilles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decolonization_of_the_Americas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Haiti and the French Antilles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Revolution" title="Haitian Revolution">Haitian Revolution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latin_American_wars_of_independence" title="Latin American wars of independence">Latin American wars of independence</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolutions</a> had profound effects on the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spanish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">Portuguese</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_colonial_empire" title="French colonial empire">French</a> colonies in the Americas. <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a>, a French slave colony, was the first to follow the United States to independence, during the <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Revolution" title="Haitian Revolution">Haitian Revolution</a>, which lasted from 1791 to 1804. Thwarted in his attempt to rebuild a French empire in North America, <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoleon Bonaparte</a> sold <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Purchase" title="Louisiana Purchase">Louisiana</a> to the United States and from then on focused on the European theater, marking the end of France's ambitions of building a colonial empire in the Western Hemisphere.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_America">Spanish America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decolonization_of_the_Americas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spanish America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Americas_independence_map.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Americas_independence_map.PNG/220px-Americas_independence_map.PNG" decoding="async" width="220" height="324" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Americas_independence_map.PNG/330px-Americas_independence_map.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Americas_independence_map.PNG/440px-Americas_independence_map.PNG 2x" data-file-width="862" data-file-height="1270" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Americas_independence_map.PNG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Places in the Americas by date of independence. Note that the United States did not complete its continental territorial expansion until 1867; Canada did not complete sovereignty as an independent country until 1982.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_American_wars_of_independence" title="Spanish American wars of independence">Spanish American wars of independence</a></div>
<p>The Spanish Kingdoms in the Americas won their independence in the first quarter of the 19th century.
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">Peninsular War</a>, Napoleon installed his own brother, Joseph Bonaparte on the Spanish Throne and captured the King Fernando VII. Several assemblies were established after 1810 by the <i><a href="/wiki/Spanish_Criollo_peoples" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish Criollo peoples">Criollos</a></i> to recover the sovereignty and self-government based in <a href="/wiki/Siete_Partidas" title="Siete Partidas">Seven-Part Code</a>, for restoration the laws of Castilian succession to rule the lands in the name of <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_VII_of_Spain" title="Ferdinand VII of Spain">Ferdinand VII of Spain</a>.
</p><p>This experience of self-government, along with the influence of <a href="/wiki/Liberalism" title="Liberalism">Liberalism</a> and the ideas of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French</a> and <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolutions</a>, brought about a struggle for independence, led by the <i><a href="/wiki/Libertadores" title="Libertadores">Libertadores</a></i>. The territories freed themselves, often with help from foreign mercenaries and privateers. United States, Europe and the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> were neutral, aimed to achieve political influence and trade without the Spanish monopoly.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sim%C3%B3n_Bol%C3%ADvar" title="Simón Bolívar">Simón Bolívar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_de_San_Mart%C3%ADn" title="José de San Martín">José de San Martín</a> led the final phase of the independence struggle. Although Bolívar attempted to keep the Spanish-speaking parts of the continent politically unified, they rapidly became independent of one another as well, and several further wars were fought, such as the <a href="/wiki/Paraguayan_War" title="Paraguayan War">Paraguayan War</a> and the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Pacific" title="War of the Pacific">War of the Pacific</a>.
</p><p>A related process took place in what is now Mexico, Central America, and parts of North America between 1810 and 1821 with the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_War_of_Independence" title="Mexican War of Independence">Mexican War of Independence</a>. Independence was achieved in 1821 by a coalition uniting under <a href="/wiki/Agust%C3%ADn_de_Iturbide" title="Agustín de Iturbide">Agustín de Iturbide</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Army_of_the_Three_Guarantees" title="Army of the Three Guarantees">Army of the Three Guarantees</a>. Unity was maintained for a short period under the <a href="/wiki/First_Mexican_Empire" title="First Mexican Empire">First Mexican Empire</a>, but within a decade the region fought against the United States over the border lands (losing bordering lands of California and Texas). Most of the heat was during the official <a href="/wiki/Mexican%E2%80%93American_War" title="Mexican–American War">Mexican-American War</a> from 1846 to 1848.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1898, in the <a href="/wiki/Greater_Antilles" title="Greater Antilles">Greater Antilles</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> won the <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">Spanish–American War</a> and occupied <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a> and <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, ending Spanish territorial control in the Americas.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decolonization_of_the_Americas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Independence_of_Brazil" title="Independence of Brazil">Independence of Brazil</a></div>
<p>Unlike the Spanish, the Portuguese did not divide their colonial territory in the Americas. The captaincies they created were subdued to a centralized administration in Salvador which reported directly to the Crown in Lisbon. Therefore, it is not common to refer to "Portuguese America" (like Spanish America, Dutch America, etc.), but rather to Brazil, as a unified colony since its very beginnings.
</p><p>As a result, Brazil did not split into several states by the time of Independence (1822), as happened to its Spanish-speaking neighbors. The adoption of monarchy instead of federal republic in the first six decades of Brazilian political sovereignty also contributed to the nation's unity.
</p><p>In the Portuguese colony <a href="/wiki/Dom_Pedro_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Dom Pedro I">Dom Pedro I</a> (also Pedro IV of Portugal), son of the Portuguese <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">king</a> <a href="/wiki/Dom_Jo%C3%A3o_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="Dom João VI">Dom João VI</a>, proclaimed the country's independence in 1822 and became Brazil's first <a href="/wiki/Emperor" title="Emperor">Emperor</a>. This was generally peacefully accepted by the crown in Portugal, although some <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla" class="mw-redirect" title="Guerrilla">guerrillas</a> <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_War_of_Independence" class="mw-redirect" title="Brazilian War of Independence">were fought</a> between Portuguese troops and civilians. Portugal recognized Brazil's independence three years later upon compensation.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decolonization_of_the_Americas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Confederation" title="Canadian Confederation">Canadian Confederation</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>'s transition from colonial rule to independence occurred gradually over many decades and was achieved mostly through political means, as opposed to the violent revolutions that marked the end of colonialism in other North and South American countries. Attempts at revolting against the British, such as the <a href="/wiki/Rebellion_of_1837" class="mw-redirect" title="Rebellion of 1837">Rebellion of 1837</a>, were brief and quickly put down. Canada was declared a dominion within the British Empire in 1867. Originally, the Canadian Confederation included just a few of what are now Canada's eastern provinces; other British colonies in modern-day Canada, such as <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward_Island" title="Prince Edward Island">Prince Edward Island</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland" class="mw-redirect" title="Newfoundland">Newfoundland</a>, would join later. Additionally, Britain's and Norway's claims to Arctic lands were ceded to Canada in the late 19th and early 20th century. By 1931, the United Kingdom had relinquished its control over Canada's foreign policy. What few political links that remained between Canada and the UK were formally severed in 1982 with the <a href="/wiki/Canada_Act_1982" title="Canada Act 1982">Canada Act</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_non-sovereign_territories">Current non-sovereign territories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Decolonization_of_the_Americas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Current non-sovereign territories">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some parts of the Americas are still administered by European countries or the USA:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Anguilla" title="Anguilla">Anguilla</a> (United Kingdom)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Aruba" title="Aruba">Aruba</a> (Netherlands)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a> (United Kingdom)</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Falkland_Islands" title="Falkland Islands">Falkland Islands</a> (United Kingdom)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a> (France)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> (Kingdom of Denmark)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> (France)</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Sint_Eustatius" title="Sint Eustatius">Sint Eustatius</a> (Netherlands)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sint_Marteen" class="mw-redirect" title="Sint Marteen">Sint Marteen</a> (Netherlands)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/South_Georgia_and_South_Sandwich_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="South Georgia and South Sandwich Islands">South Georgia and South Sandwich Islands</a> (United Kingdom)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Turks_and_Caicos_Islands" title="Turks and Caicos Islands">Turks and Caicos Islands</a> (United Kingdom)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/U.S._Virgin_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Virgin Islands">U.S. Virgin Islands</a> (United States)</li></ul>
<p>Some of the remaining non-sovereign territories of the Americas have retained this status by choice, and enjoy a significant degree of self-government. (Some have nevertheless been placed on the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_list_of_Non-Self-Governing_Territories#Atlantic_Ocean_and_Caribbean_Sea" title="United Nations list of Non-Self-Governing Territories">U.N. list of Non-Self-Governing Territories</a>, an ongoing subject of controversy.) Aruba, for example, seceded from the Netherlands Antilles on January 1, 1986, and became a separate, self-governing member of the Kingdom of the Netherlands. Movement toward full independence by 1996 was halted at Aruba's request in 1990. <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">Guadeloupe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martinique" title="Martinique">Martinique</a> are not considered <a href="/wiki/Dependent_territories" class="mw-redirect" title="Dependent territories">dependent territories</a> of <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, but have been "incorporated" into France itself, as overseas <i><a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9partement_in_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Département in France">départements</a></i> (<i><a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9partements_d%27outre-mer" class="mw-redirect" title="Départements d&#39;outre-mer">départements d'outre-mer</a>, or DOM</i>). Other regions however have had or currently have movements to change their political status, for example different movements to change the <a href="/wiki/Political_status_of_Puerto_Rico" title="Political status of Puerto Rico">political status of Puerto Rico</a> and intermittent calls for independence in other non-sovereign territories such as <a href="/wiki/Martinican_Independence_Movement" title="Martinican Independence Movement">Martinique</a> and others, with differing amounts of support.
</p>

</li>
<li id="cite_note-22"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-22">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">On November 15, 1838, Honduras formally withdrew from the Federal Republic of Central America and became an independent state.</span>

<li id="cite_note-24"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-24">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">On February 18, 1841, El Salvador (by then the only member remaining in the Federal Republic of Central America), formally withdrew and became an independent state.</span>

On November 6, 1844, <a href="/wiki/Dominican_War_of_Independence" title="Dominican War of Independence">after months of fighting</a> starting from February 27 of that year, the Dominican Republic regained sovereignty upon the ratification of the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Dominican_Republic" title="Constitution of the Dominican Republic">Constitution of the Dominican Republic</a>. The February 27 date is celebrated as the Dominican Republic's date of independence.</span>

On December 11, 1931 it got increased self-governance powers through the <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Westminster_1931" title="Statute of Westminster 1931">Statute of Westminster</a>, followed by complete sovereignty on April 17, 1982 after the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Canada_Act_1982" title="Canada Act 1982">Canada Act 1982</a>. This dominance would last until the end of the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Revolution" title="Cuban Revolution">Cuban Revolution</a> on January 1, 1959. As a result, Cuba celebrates the date October 10, 1868 as its date of independence, when <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Manuel_de_C%C3%A9spedes" title="Carlos Manuel de Céspedes">Carlos Manuel de Céspedes</a> formally declared Cuba's independence and launched the <a href="/wiki/Ten_Years%27_War" title="Ten Years&#39; War">Ten Years' War</a>.</span>
