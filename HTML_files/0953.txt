	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Dean's List</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>Dean's List</b> is an academic award, or distinction, used to recognize the level of highest scholarship demonstrated by students in a <a href="/wiki/College" title="College">college</a> or <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a>. This system is most often used in North America,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ryerson_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ryerson-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> though institutions in Europe,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Asia,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and Australia<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> may also employ similar measures. It is often synonymous with <b>honor roll</b> and <b>honor list</b>, but should not be confused with <a href="/wiki/Honours_degree" title="Honours degree">honours degrees</a>.
</p><p>Generally, students enrolled in college or university would need to satisfy a series of specific requirements before receiving the Dean's List. These requirements may differ across institutions, but in most cases will require students to enroll in a <a href="/wiki/Full-time" title="Full-time">full-time</a> capacity, to achieve a specific <a href="/wiki/Grade_Point_Average" class="mw-redirect" title="Grade Point Average">grade point average</a> within the <a href="/wiki/Academic_term" title="Academic term">academic term</a>, and to maintain a specific <a href="/wiki/Cumulative_grade_point_average" class="mw-redirect" title="Cumulative grade point average">cumulative grade point average</a> throughout enrollment. Universities may often establish further rewards, such as Annual Dean's Lists, for students that demonstrate even greater academic distinction. As such, a Dean's List and its direct derivatives are usually intended for students that demonstrate highest scholarship across multiple disciplines, and not necessarily bound to a specific field of study.
</p><p>Over time, the Dean's List has also led to the creation of additional rewards with similar nomenclatures, including the <a href="/wiki/Provost_(education)" title="Provost (education)">Provost's</a> List,<sup id="cite_ref-Wyoming_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wyoming-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_(education)" title="Chancellor (education)">Chancellor's</a> List,<sup id="cite_ref-UNC-Asheville_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNC-Asheville-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> the Rector's List (mostly limited to within Europe),<sup id="cite_ref-WU-Vienna_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WU-Vienna-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and the Director's List.<sup id="cite_ref-Wharton_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wharton-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The additional requirements to be met for such honors may include academic distinction within a target field of study, extracurricular achievements, continued high scholarship throughout enrollment or other noteworthy accomplishments.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distinction">Distinction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dean%27s_List&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distinction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_to_North_American_Latin_honors">Comparison to North American Latin honors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dean%27s_List&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Comparison to North American Latin honors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Latin_honors" title="Latin honors">Latin honors</a></div>
<p>Although the Dean's List and Latin honors may both be issued in recognition of high scholarship, they are mostly separate awards not intended for direct comparison.
Many institutions confer three levels of Latin honors, as follows:
</p>
<ul><li><i>cum laude</i>, meaning "with praise" (or "with honor")<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
<ul><li>In North America, this honor is typically awarded to graduates in the top 25% (or 30%) of their class.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li><i>magna cum laude</i>, meaning "with great honor"
<ul><li>In North America, this honor is typically awarded to graduates in the top 10% (or 15%) of their class; this is the highest honor awarded at some institutions.</li></ul></li>
<li><i>summa cum laude</i>, meaning "with highest honor"
<ul><li>In North America, this honor is typically awarded to graduates in the top 1-5% of their class.</li></ul></li></ul>
<p>Because Latin honors are often conferred to the approximate class rank whereby students also receive the Dean's List (as the top 10-15%), <i><b>magna</b></i> and <i><b>summa cum laude</b></i> are usually held in higher regard. A Dean's List may therefore be seen as equal to (or more prestigious than) <i><b>cum laude</b>,</i> depending on the specific requirements involved.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_to_GPA">Comparison to GPA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dean%27s_List&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Comparison to GPA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Dean's List <a href="/wiki/GPA" class="mw-redirect" title="GPA">GPA</a> requirements vary from school to school, although there are general standards:
</p>
<ul><li>With the GPA scale from 0 to 4, 4 being an A, the dean's list cutoff is typically around 3.5-3.7.<sup id="cite_ref-Wyoming_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wyoming-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OSU_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OSU-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cornell_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cornell-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Falk_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Falk-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Washington_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Washington-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Top 10% of the class in GPA<sup id="cite_ref-Wharton_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wharton-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Some schools maintain two lists for two different levels of GPA. For example, the dean's list records students with at least a 3.5 GPA whereas the chancellor's list records students with a higher 4.0 GPA.<sup id="cite_ref-IU-Southeast_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IU-Southeast-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-UNC-Asheville_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNC-Asheville-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gonzaga-Honors_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gonzaga-Honors-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Different from an academic <a href="/wiki/Scholarship" title="Scholarship">scholarship</a>, the dean's list award does not come with any <a href="/wiki/Student_financial_aid_(United_States)" title="Student financial aid (United States)">financial aid</a>. However, students high on the dean's list are often offered extra financial aid, especially if they express a desire to transfer or exhibit more financial need.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_to_European_classifications">Comparison to European classifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Dean%27s_List&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Comparison to European classifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Europe, <a href="/wiki/College" title="College">colleges</a> and <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">universities</a> may utilize variations of the <a href="/wiki/British_undergraduate_degree_classification" title="British undergraduate degree classification">British degree classifications</a> to distinguish students of high scholarship. The classification system as currently used in the United Kingdom, for example, was developed in 1918.<sup id="cite_ref-Alderman2_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alderman2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The system was implemented based on one from the 16th century, when the <a href="/wiki/Regius_Professor_of_Divinity" title="Regius Professor of Divinity">Regius Professor of Divinity</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a> implemented <a href="/wiki/Norm-referenced_test" title="Norm-referenced test">norm referencing</a> to distinguish the top 25% of candidates, the next 50%, and the bottom 25%.<sup id="cite_ref-Alderman2_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Alderman2-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Today, <b>first-class honours</b> (commonly referred to as "<b>first-hons</b>") is awarded, by most institutions, to any student that receives a grade of 70% or higher (as opposed to classification by class rank). The number of first-class honours degrees has reportedly tripled since the 1990s,<sup id="cite_ref-Paton_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Paton-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and there has been concern over possible <a href="/wiki/Grade_inflation" title="Grade inflation">grade inflation</a> due to increasing numbers of higher-class honours degrees awarded per annum.<sup id="cite_ref-Shepherd_1_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shepherd_1-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Shepherd_2_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shepherd_2-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
