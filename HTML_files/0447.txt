<p>A <b>firecracker</b> (<b>cracker</b>, <b>noise maker</b>,  <b>banger</b>, or <b>bunger</b><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) is a small <a href="/wiki/Explosive" title="Explosive">explosive</a> device primarily designed to produce a large amount of noise, especially in the form of a loud bang; any visual effect is incidental to this goal. They have fuses, and are wrapped in a heavy paper casing to contain the explosive compound.  Firecrackers, along with <a href="/wiki/Fireworks" title="Fireworks">fireworks</a>, originated in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ming_Dynasty_Jin_Ping_Mei_fireworks.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Ming_Dynasty_Jin_Ping_Mei_fireworks.jpg/220px-Ming_Dynasty_Jin_Ping_Mei_fireworks.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="282" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Ming_Dynasty_Jin_Ping_Mei_fireworks.jpg/330px-Ming_Dynasty_Jin_Ping_Mei_fireworks.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Ming_Dynasty_Jin_Ping_Mei_fireworks.jpg/440px-Ming_Dynasty_Jin_Ping_Mei_fireworks.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="896" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ming_Dynasty_Jin_Ping_Mei_fireworks.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An illustration of a fireworks display from the 1628–1643 edition of the <a href="/wiki/Ming_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Ming Dynasty">Ming Dynasty</a> novel <i><a href="/wiki/Jin_Ping_Mei" title="Jin Ping Mei">Jin Ping Mei</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Science_and_Civilisation_in_China_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Science_and_Civilisation_in_China-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The predecessor of the firecracker was a type of heated bamboo, used as early as 200 BC, that exploded when heated continuously. The Chinese name for firecrackers, 爆竹(<i>baozhu</i>), literally means "exploding bamboo."<sup id="cite_ref-mtmenc_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtmenc-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> After the invention of gunpowder, gunpowder firecrackers had a shape that resembled bamboo and produced a similar sound, so the name "exploding bamboo" was retained.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In traditional Chinese culture, firecrackers were used to scare off evil spirits.<sup id="cite_ref-Baker_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Baker-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contents_of_firecrackers">Contents of firecrackers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firecracker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Contents of firecrackers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Firecrackers are generally made of cardboard or plastic, with <a href="/wiki/Flash_powder" title="Flash powder">flash powder</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cordite" title="Cordite">cordite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smokeless_powder" title="Smokeless powder">smokeless powder</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Black_powder" class="mw-redirect" title="Black powder">black powder</a> as the propellant. This is not always the case, however. Anything from match heads, kerosene to lighter fluid have been used successfully in making firecrackers. The key to loud firecrackers, however, although in part lying in the propellant substance, is pressure. The entire firecracker must be very tightly packed in order for it to work best. Flash powder, however, does not need to be packed tightly, and should not be.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="How_firecrackers_were_made">How firecrackers were made</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firecracker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: How firecrackers were made">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Dyer_Ball_Things_Chinese-295-295.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Dyer_Ball_Things_Chinese-295-295.jpg/200px-Dyer_Ball_Things_Chinese-295-295.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="338" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Dyer_Ball_Things_Chinese-295-295.jpg/300px-Dyer_Ball_Things_Chinese-295-295.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Dyer_Ball_Things_Chinese-295-295.jpg/400px-Dyer_Ball_Things_Chinese-295-295.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1185" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Dyer_Ball_Things_Chinese-295-295.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Dyer Ball on firecrackers</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/James_Dyer_Ball" title="James Dyer Ball">James Dyer Ball</a>, in his book <i>Things Chinese,</i> has a detailed description about the process and material used for making firecrackers at the end of the 19th century. At that time, firecrackers were made by female and child workers, using straw paper to make the body of the firecracker, while the fuse was made of bamboo paper imported from Japan, then stiffened with buckwheat paste. The bamboo paper was cut into strips of 14 inches (360&#160;mm) long and <span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>3</sub></span> inch (8.5&#160;mm) wide, laid on a table; a string of gunpowder was placed at the center with a hollow tube, then twisted up to make a piece of fuse. The firecracker tubes were made from pieces of straw paper wrapped around iron rods of various diameters and then tightened with a special tool. 200 to 300 firecrackers were tied up in a bunch, then red clay was spread at the bottom of the bunch and forced into each end of the firecracker with a punch; gunpowder was poured in, then the other end was sealed with an awl by turning the tube inward, and a fuse inserted.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firecracker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lewes_Bonfire,_LBBS,_Banger_blitz.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Lewes_Bonfire%2C_LBBS%2C_Banger_blitz.jpg/220px-Lewes_Bonfire%2C_LBBS%2C_Banger_blitz.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Lewes_Bonfire%2C_LBBS%2C_Banger_blitz.jpg/330px-Lewes_Bonfire%2C_LBBS%2C_Banger_blitz.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Lewes_Bonfire%2C_LBBS%2C_Banger_blitz.jpg/440px-Lewes_Bonfire%2C_LBBS%2C_Banger_blitz.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1280" data-file-height="853" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lewes_Bonfire,_LBBS,_Banger_blitz.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Two men dressed as colonial soldiers carry a banner, exploding firecrackers, commemorating <a href="/wiki/Guy_Fawkes" title="Guy Fawkes">Guy Fawkes</a> and his co-conspirators as part of <a href="/wiki/Lewes" title="Lewes">Lewes</a> Bonfire Night celebrations.</div></div></div>
<p>Firecrackers are commonly used in celebration of holidays or festivals, such as <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a>, <a href="/wiki/Independence_Day_(United_States)" title="Independence Day (United States)">Independence Day</a> (also known as the Fourth of July) in the United States, <a href="/wiki/Diwali" title="Diwali">Diwali</a> in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eid_al-Fitr" title="Eid al-Fitr">Eid al-Fitr</a> in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tihar_(festival)" title="Tihar (festival)">Tihar</a> in <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Day_of_Ashura" class="mw-redirect" title="Day of Ashura">Day of Ashura</a> in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guy_Fawkes_Night" title="Guy Fawkes Night">Guy Fawkes Night</a> or Bonfire Night in the United Kingdom, Halloween in Ireland, Bastille Day in France, Spanish <a href="/wiki/Fallas" class="mw-redirect" title="Fallas">Fallas</a>, in almost every cultural festival of <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">Sri Lanka</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Sinhala_and_Tamil_new_year" class="mw-redirect" title="Sinhala and Tamil new year">Sri Lankan New Year</a>), <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Eve" title="New Year&#39;s Eve">New Year's Eve</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Day" title="New Year&#39;s Day">New Year's Day</a>, and in the celebration of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_New_Year" title="Chinese New Year">Chinese New Year</a> by Chinese communities around the world.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> the slang term for a firecracker, typically used on Guy Fawkes Night, is a "jacky-jumper".<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legality_of_firecrackers">Legality of firecrackers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firecracker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legality of firecrackers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Firecrackers, as well as other types of explosives, are subject to various laws in many countries, although firecrackers themselves are not usually considered illegal contraband material.  It is usually the manufacture, sale, storage, and use of firecrackers that are subject to laws including safety requirements for manufacture, the requirement of a permit to sell or store, or restrictions on the use of firecrackers.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Firecracker_ban">Firecracker ban</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firecracker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Firecracker ban">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The use of firecrackers, although a traditional part of the celebration, has over the years led to many injuries and deaths. There have been incidents every year of users being blinded, losing body parts, or suffering other injuries, especially during festivities that customarily involve firecrackers such as Chinese New Year season. Hence, many governments and authorities have enacted laws completely banning the sale or use of firecrackers, or banning the use of firecrackers in the street, primarily because of safety or environmental reasons.
These rules also require a permit from the local government, as well as any relevant local bodies such as maritime or aviation authorities (as relevant to the types of fireworks being used) and hospitals, schools, etc. within a certain range.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> — Firecrackers are not authorized under the Explosives Act, thus making importation, possession, transportation, storage, or manufacturing illegal in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Canada banned firecrackers on September 27, 1972, after media reports that two children were killed and three others severely burned when some older children were playing with firecrackers outside their tent. It later came out that the children inside the tent had actually been smoking and, not wanting to tell their parents, had told them they had been playing with firecrackers.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Fireworks are still legal to buy for anyone 18 years of age or over.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a> - The use of firecrackers is regulated by the <i>Law on Explosive Substances and the Production and Trading of Weapons</i> (<a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">Croatian</a>: <i lang="hr">Zakon o eksplozivnim tvarima te proizvodnji i prometu oružja</i>). According to the Law, firecrackers (including other pyrotechnic articles) are divided into three classes: <i>Class 1 Pyrotechnics</i> - pyrotechnic articles for fireworks which represent a very low risk, have negligible noise level and are intended for use in restricted areas, including fireworks intended for use within residential buildings; <i>Class 2 and 3 Pyrotechnics</i> - pyrotechnic articles for fireworks which represent low to medium risk, have medium to high noise level and are intended for outdoor use in restricted areas and large open areas. Class 1 Pyrotechnics can be sold all year round to people over the age of 14 in general stores and newsstands, while Class 2 and 3 Pyrotechnics can only be sold between 15 December and 1 January each year at gun shops and stores with special permits to the people over the age of 14 (class 2) or over the age of 18 (class 3). Use of Class 2 and 3 Pyrotechnics is allowed only in the period between 27 December and 1 January.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Each year since 1993, between 15 December and 8 January, the police carries out the "Peace and good" action with the aim of prevention of people from getting hurt and violating public order. The penalties for unauthorized use of pyrotechnic articles range between 1,000 and 15,000 <a href="/wiki/Croatian_kuna" title="Croatian kuna">kunas</a> (<a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">€</a>135 - €2,035) for people, and between 10,000 and 80,000 kunas (€1,355 - €10,850) for companies. For a legal guardian of a child under the age of 14 who have used pyrotechnic articles, fine ranges between 1,000 and 3,000 kunas (€135 - €405).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">Mainland China</a> — As of 2008, most urban areas in mainland China permit firecrackers. In the first three days of the traditional New Year, it is a tradition that people compete with each other by playing with firecrackers. However, many urban areas banned them in the 1990s. For example, they were banned in <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>'s <a href="/wiki/List_of_administrative_divisions_of_Beijing" title="List of administrative divisions of Beijing">urban districts</a> from 1993 to 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In 2004, 37 people were killed in a stampede when four million<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> people gathered for a rumored <a href="/wiki/Lantern_Festival" title="Lantern Festival">Lantern Festival</a> firework display in nearby <a href="/wiki/Miyun" class="mw-redirect" title="Miyun">Miyun</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Since the ban was lifted, the firecracker barrage has been tremendous. An unusual<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2010)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> feature is that many residents in major cities look down on street-level fireworks from their tower blocks. Bans are rare in rural areas.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a> — Firecrackers are legal in the Czech Republic.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> - Fireworks are classified. Some fireworks are restricted to adults (18 years), may be sold to private persons only on three days before Silvester and may be used only on Silvester or New Year (special permits can be obtained to use these on other days of the year). Other fireworks have no such restrictions, however, they are not recommended for children below 12 years and guidance by adults is advised. Certain fireworks are available only to professionals.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> - Firecrackers sold to consumers are restricted to 0.95 grams of black powder. The ban on larger firecrackers went into effect in 1996. Before, mid-size <i>kiinanpommi</i> or <i>kiinari</i> ("Chinese bomb") and <i>tykinlaukaus</i> and large <i>tykäri</i> ("artillery shot") had become cheap and common products that were often used by children after the celebration, leading to accidents.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> — <a href="/wiki/Fireworks" title="Fireworks">Fireworks</a> are banned for security reasons, as some speculate a connection between firework use and the <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_1967_Leftist_Riots" class="mw-redirect" title="Hong Kong 1967 Leftist Riots">1967 Leftist Riot</a>. However, the government stages a fireworks display in <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Harbour" title="Victoria Harbour">Victoria Harbour</a> for New Year countdown and the second day of the Chinese New Year.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> — Firecrackers are banned in Hungary since 2005. However, they are legal in Slovakia and people smuggle them.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> — Firecrackers and fireworks are generally forbidden in public during the Chinese New Year, especially in areas with significant non-Chinese populations, to avoid conflict between the two. However, there are some exceptions. The usage of firecrackers is legal in some metropolitan areas such as <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">Jakarta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Medan" title="Medan">Medan</a>, where the degree of racial and cultural tolerance is higher.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> — Firecrackers are legal and can be bought without a licence by anyone 18 years of age or over.</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sparkles_phuljhari_fireworks_on_DIWALI,_festival_of_lights.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Sparkles_phuljhari_fireworks_on_DIWALI%2C_festival_of_lights.jpg/220px-Sparkles_phuljhari_fireworks_on_DIWALI%2C_festival_of_lights.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="177" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Sparkles_phuljhari_fireworks_on_DIWALI%2C_festival_of_lights.jpg/330px-Sparkles_phuljhari_fireworks_on_DIWALI%2C_festival_of_lights.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Sparkles_phuljhari_fireworks_on_DIWALI%2C_festival_of_lights.jpg/440px-Sparkles_phuljhari_fireworks_on_DIWALI%2C_festival_of_lights.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2914" data-file-height="2348" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sparkles_phuljhari_fireworks_on_DIWALI,_festival_of_lights.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Diwali fireworks is a family event in many parts of India. Above: <i>Phuljhari</i>, sparklers that are popular with some children on Diwali nights.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> — Firecrackers are easily available in India. Firecrackers are used in the Indian festival of light(<a href="/wiki/Diwali" title="Diwali">Diwali</a>), and also for marriage ceremonies. <a href="/wiki/Sivakasi" title="Sivakasi">Sivakasi</a> a city located in South India supply Firecrackers to all over India. In October 2017 Supreme Court banned firecrackers in Delhi, Industry says it stares at Rs 1,000-crore loss and layoffs.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a> — Fireworks and firecrackers are not permitted in the Republic of Ireland; however, many people smuggle them from <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, where they are legal. They are most common around <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Irish anti-firecracker laws are considered among the strictest in the world, equal to Chile's.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> — Banned in Minor Offences Act 1955 and Explosives Act 1957. However, many Malaysians smuggle them from <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> and the Philippines.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> — The government of Norway decided to ban rockets in early 2009. Other types of fireworks are still allowed.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> — Fireworks and firecrackers are widely available throughout the Philippines and both <a href="/wiki/List_of_Philippine_laws" title="List of Philippine laws">Republic Act</a> 7183 and Executive Order 28 were enacted to regulate and to control the sale, distribution, manufacture and use of firecrackers for <a href="/wiki/Public_safety" class="mw-redirect" title="Public safety">public safety</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ra7183_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ra7183-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Under Republic Act 7183, there are certain firecrackers that are legal such as <a href="/wiki/Judas%27_belt" class="mw-redirect" title="Judas&#39; belt">Judas' belt</a> and the prohibition of a certain firecracker and pyrotechnic devices shall be determined by the Director-General of the <a href="/wiki/Philippine_National_Police" title="Philippine National Police">Philippine National Police</a>.  Although, there are <a href="/wiki/Cities_of_the_Philippines" title="Cities of the Philippines">cities in the Philippines</a> banning all kinds of firecrackers and those cities are <a href="/wiki/Muntinlupa" title="Muntinlupa">Muntinlupa</a> (since 2013), <a href="/wiki/Olongapo" title="Olongapo">Olongapo</a> (since 2008)<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Davao_City" title="Davao City">Davao City</a> (since 2001).<sup id="cite_ref-Geronimo_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Geronimo-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Lacorte_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lacorte-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Injuries involving the use or ingestion of "<a href="/wiki/Piccolo_(firecracker)" title="Piccolo (firecracker)">Piccolo</a>" sticks account for the majority of firecracker-related incidents in the country.<sup id="cite_ref-Uy_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Uy-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-piccoloGMA_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-piccoloGMA-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> — A partial ban on firecrackers was imposed in March 1970 after a fire killed six people and injured 68.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> This was extended to a total ban in August 1972, after an explosion that killed two people.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> and an attack on two police officers attempting to stop a group from letting off firecrackers in February 1972.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> However, in 2003, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Singapore" title="Government of Singapore">government</a> allowed firecrackers to be set off during the festive season.  At the Chinese New Year light-up in <a href="/wiki/Chinatown,_Singapore" title="Chinatown, Singapore">Chinatown</a>, at the stroke of midnight on the first day of the Lunar New Year, firecrackers are set off under controlled conditions by the <a href="/wiki/Singapore_Tourism_Board" title="Singapore Tourism Board">Singapore Tourism Board</a>. Other occasions where firecrackers are allowed to be set off are determined by the <a href="/wiki/Singapore_Tourism_Board" title="Singapore Tourism Board">tourism board</a> or other government organizations. However, their sale is not allowed.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> — Only rocket-type fireworks are allowed in Sweden. The ban of firecrackers was effectuated by the <a href="/wiki/EU_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="EU Parliament">EU Parliament</a> and Swedish government effective December 1, 2001, but in 2006 the EU Parliament changed the laws, allowing smaller types of firecrackers. By 2008, the law had to be in effect in all EU member countries, including Sweden.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a> — Beginning in 2008, firecrackers are banned in urban areas, but still allowed in rural areas.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> — In 1997, firecrackers became illegal, but most other consumer fireworks are legal.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> — In 2007, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> lifted its decade-old ban on firecrackers, allowing a display of 300,000 firecrackers to be set off in <a href="/wiki/Chinatown,_Manhattan" title="Chinatown, Manhattan">Chinatown</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Chatham_Square_(Manhattan)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chatham Square (Manhattan)">Chatham Square</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Under the supervision of the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Fire_Department" title="Los Angeles Fire Department">fire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Police_Department" title="Los Angeles Police Department">police</a> departments, <a href="/wiki/Chinatown,_Los_Angeles,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinatown, Los Angeles, California">Los Angeles</a> regularly lights firecrackers every New Year's Eve, mostly at temples and the shrines of benevolent associations. The <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">San Francisco</a> Chinese New Year Parade, the largest outside China, is accompanied by numerous firecrackers, both officially sanctioned and illicit.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> — In 1994, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Vietnam" title="Government of Vietnam">Government</a> decided to ban firecrackers nationwide. Only fireworks displays produced and performed by the government are permitted.</li></ul>
<h2><span id="Firecracker_brands.2C_packs_and_labels"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Firecracker_brands,_packs_and_labels">Firecracker brands, packs and labels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Firecracker&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Firecracker brands, packs and labels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Early (pre-1920s) Chinese firecrackers (also known as "Mandarin firecrackers") were typically <span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span> to 2 inches (13 to 51&#160;mm) long, and approximately <span class="frac nowrap"><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>4</sub></span> inch (6.4&#160;mm) in diameter, and were charged with <a href="/wiki/Black_powder" class="mw-redirect" title="Black powder">black powder</a>. Mandarin crackers produced a less loud, duller thud compared to modern flash-light crackers (which utilize a different explosive known as <a href="/wiki/Flash_powder" title="Flash powder">flash powder</a>). Mandarin crackers produced a dimmer, less brilliant flash when they exploded also. Individual Mandarin crackers were most often braided into "strings" of varying lengths, which, when ignited, exploded in rapid sequence.  Generally, the strings (sometimes containing as many as several thousand crackers) would be hung from an overhead line before ignition. Most Mandarin crackers were colored all red and did not generally have designs or logos decorating their exterior surface (or "shell wraps").  Occasionally a few yellow and green Mandarin crackers were created and would be braided into the predominantly all-red strings to symbolize the emperor and the ruling class, while the numerous red crackers symbolized the common man.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Firecracker_roll.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Firecracker_roll.jpeg/220px-Firecracker_roll.jpeg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Firecracker_roll.jpeg/330px-Firecracker_roll.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Firecracker_roll.jpeg/440px-Firecracker_roll.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="2248" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Firecracker_roll.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A firecracker roll containing 10,000 firecrackers</div></div></div>
<p>Once flash powder, which produces a significantly sharper and brighter bang, replaced black powder as a firecracker's explosive charge (circa 1924), manufacturers began competing to gain loyalty of typical consumers (that is, mainly boys eight to 16 years old). Thousands of brands were created during the flash-light cracker's heyday from the 1920s through the early 1970s. Only a small percentage of brands lasted more than a year or two. Collectors now seek the various labels from the era.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Until the mid-1980s, firecracker production was low-tech. They were handmade, beginning with rolling tubes. Once the firecracker tubes were rolled by hand (commonly from newspaper) and labelled, and then filled with powder, their ends were crimped and fuses inserted, all by hand. These finished firecrackers were usually braided into "strings" and sold in packs which came in many sizes, from the very small ("penny packs" containing as few as four to six firecrackers) to the most common size packs (containing 16 and 20 crackers per pack), to larger packs (containing 24, 30, 32, 40, 50, 60, 72, 90, 100, and 120 firecrackers), to huge "belts" and "rolls" (packages containing strings of several hundred to several thousand crackers—Phantom Fireworks sells rolls as large as 16,000 firecrackers<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>). Firecracker packages were wrapped in colourful and translucent <a href="/wiki/Glassine" title="Glassine">glassine</a> paper, as well as clear cellophane, with glassine the most popular.
</p><p>The final operation involved applying a branded label on each pack, then bundling finished packs into wholesale lots called "bricks" which contained an average of 80 packs each (varying according to the size of the packs being bundled; for example, packs of 32 crackers might have 40 packs per brick, compared to packs of 16 or 20 with 80 packs per brick).
</p>
