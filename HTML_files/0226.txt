<p>The <b>Tiriyó</b> (also known as <b>Trio</b>) are a <a href="/wiki/Amerindian" class="mw-redirect" title="Amerindian">Amerindian</a> ethnic group native to parts of northern <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>. In 2005, there were approximately 2,000 Tiriyó in the two countries. They live in several major villages and a number of minor villages in the border zone between <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>. They speak the <a href="/wiki/Tiriy%C3%B3_language" title="Tiriyó language">Tiriyó language</a>, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Cariban_languages" title="Cariban languages">Cariban language family</a> and refer to themselves as <i>tarëno</i>, etymologically 'people from here' or 'local people'.
</p><p>About 30% are Christians, while 70% follow indigenous religions.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tiriy%C3%B3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The modern Tiriyó are formed from various different indigenous communities; some of these, such as the Aramixó, are mentioned in European writings as early as 1609-1610.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeira1999_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeira1999-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Many of the now-Tiriyó groups lived between Brazil and <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a> until they were driven out by the <a href="/wiki/Wayampi" title="Wayampi">Oyampi</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Tupi_people" title="Tupi people">Tupi-Guaranian group</a> allied with the Portuguese. Together, the Portuguese and Oyampi drove these groups westward, and they mingled with the groups that were in the area to form the modern Tiriyó group.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeira1999_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeira1999-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As such, the Tiriyó established contact relatively early with runaway slave groups that settled in the area around the end of the 18th century. They maintained regular commercial relations with one group, the <a href="/wiki/Ndyuka_people" title="Ndyuka people">Ndyuka</a>, and for many years they were the only contact the Tiriyó had with foreign populations.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeira1999_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeira1999-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The first recorded contact between the Tiriyó and a European took place in 1843 between a ‘Drio’ village and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Schomburgk" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Schomburgk">Robert Schomburgk</a>; this and the meeting between French explorer Jules Crevaux and a few ‘Trio’ were the only two points of contact between Tiriyó and Europeans in the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeira1999_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeira1999-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Subsequent contact between Europeans and Tiriyó in the first half of the twentieth century produced ethnographic and linguistic studies of the region and Tiriyó subgroups in particular. After the ‘exploratory phase’ of contact came the ‘<a href="/wiki/Missionary" title="Missionary">missionary</a> phase,’ wherein newly-built airstrips facilitated contact between missionaries and the Tiriyó.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeira1999_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeira1999-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> These missions tried to concentrate the Tiriyó population in larger villages to more easily convert them to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>, and over time, other Indigenous groups such as the Akuriyó joined them here.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeira1999_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeira1999-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Today, the Tiriyó have a high degree of independence because their settlements are difficult to access. However, they are interested in reinforcing relationships with the foreign world.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeira1999_2-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeira1999-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
