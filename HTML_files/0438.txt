<p><b>Boxing Day</b> is a secular holiday celebrated the day after <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Christmas Day">Christmas Day</a>. It originated in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and is celebrated in a number of countries that previously formed part of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a>. Boxing Day is on 26 December, although the attached <a href="/wiki/Bank_holiday" title="Bank holiday">bank holiday</a> or <a href="/wiki/Public_holiday" title="Public holiday">public holiday</a> may take place either on that day or two days later.
</p><p>In some <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> countries such as <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, 26 December is celebrated as a Second Christmas Day.<sup id="cite_ref-Second_Christmas_Day_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Second_Christmas_Day-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boxing_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are competing theories for the origins of the term, none of which are definitive.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> gives the earliest attestations from Britain in the 1830s, defining it as "the first weekday after Christmas day, observed as a holiday on which postmen, errand boys, and servants of various kinds expect to receive a Christmas box".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "Christmas box" dates back to the 17th century, and among other things meant:
</p>
<blockquote><p>A present or gratuity given at Christmas: in Great Britain, usually confined to gratuities given to those who are supposed to have a vague claim upon the donor for services rendered to him as one of the general public by whom they are employed and paid, or as a customer of their legal employer; the undefined theory being that as they have done offices for this person, for which he has not directly paid them, some direct acknowledgement is becoming at Christmas.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>In Britain, it was a custom for tradesmen to collect "Christmas boxes" of money or presents on the first weekday after Christmas as thanks for good service throughout the year.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This is mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Pepys" title="Samuel Pepys">Samuel Pepys</a>' diary entry for 19 December 1663.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> This custom is linked to an older British tradition where  the servants of the wealthy were allowed the next day to visit their families since they would have to serve their masters on Christmas Day. The employers would give each servant a box to take home containing gifts, bonuses, and sometimes leftover food. Until the late 20th century there continued to be a tradition among many in the UK to give a Christmas gift, usually cash,  to vendors although not on Boxing Day as many would not work on that day.
</p><p>In South Africa as recently as the 1980s, vendors who normally had little interaction with those they served were accustomed to knock on their doors asking for a "Christmas box", being a small cash donation, in the weeks before or after Christmas.
</p><p>The European tradition of giving money and other gifts to those in need and in service positions has been dated to the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, but the exact origin is unknown. It is believed to be in reference to the Alms Box placed in areas of worship to collect donations to the poor. The tradition may come from a custom in the late Roman/early Christian era wherein metal boxes placed outside churches were used to collect special offerings tied to the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Stephen%27s_Day" title="Saint Stephen&#39;s Day">Feast of Saint Stephen</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> which in the <a href="/wiki/Latin_Church" title="Latin Church">Western church</a> falls on the same day as Boxing Day.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Date">Date</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boxing_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Date">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Boxing Day is a <a href="/wiki/Secular_holiday" class="mw-redirect" title="Secular holiday">secular holiday</a> traditionally celebrated on 26 December, the day after Christmas Day. December 26 is also <a href="/wiki/Saint_Stephen%27s_Day" title="Saint Stephen&#39;s Day">Saint Stephen's Day</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Religious_holiday" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious holiday">religious holiday</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Status_by_country">Status by country</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boxing_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Status by country">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the UK, Boxing Day has been a <a href="/wiki/Bank_holiday" title="Bank holiday">bank holiday</a> since 1871. When 26 December falls on a Saturday, Boxing Day is moved to the following Monday. If 26 December falls on a Sunday, the substitute <a href="/wiki/Public_holiday" title="Public holiday">public holiday</a> is the following Tuesday.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> However, 26 December is referred to as Boxing Day even when it falls on a Sunday.
</p><p>In Scotland, Boxing Day has been specified as an additional bank holiday since 1974,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Royal_Proclamation" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Proclamation">Royal Proclamation</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Banking_and_Financial_Dealings_Act_1971" class="mw-redirect" title="Banking and Financial Dealings Act 1971">Banking and Financial Dealings Act 1971</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>, when the island <a href="/wiki/History_of_Ireland_(1801%E2%80%931922)" class="mw-redirect" title="History of Ireland (1801–1922)">was part of</a> the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">United Kingdom</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bank_Holidays_Act_1871" title="Bank Holidays Act 1871">Bank Holidays Act 1871</a> established the feast day of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Stephen" title="Saint Stephen">Saint Stephen</a> as a non-moveable public holiday on 26 December.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Following <a href="/wiki/Partition_of_Ireland" title="Partition of Ireland">partition in 1920</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> reverted to the British name, Boxing Day.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, despite the <a href="/wiki/Handover_of_Hong_Kong" title="Handover of Hong Kong">transfer of sovereignty</a> from the UK to China in 1997, Boxing Day continues to be a public holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> If Boxing Day falls on a Sunday, a compensation day is given on the next weekday.
</p><p>In Australia, Boxing Day is a public holiday in all jurisdictions except the state of <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a>, where a public holiday known as <a href="/wiki/Proclamation_Day_(South_Australia)" class="mw-redirect" title="Proclamation Day (South Australia)">Proclamation Day</a> is celebrated on the first weekday after Christmas Day or the Christmas Day holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In New Zealand, Boxing Day is a statutory holiday. On these holidays, people who must work receive 1 1/2 times their salaries and a lieu day is provided to employees who work.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Canada, Boxing Day (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">le Lendemain de Noel</i>) is a federal statutory holiday. Government offices, banks and post offices/delivery are closed. In some Canadian provinces, Boxing Day is a statutory holiday<sup id="cite_ref-Manitoba_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Manitoba-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> that is always celebrated on 26 December. In Canadian provinces where Boxing Day is a statutory holiday and it falls on a Saturday or Sunday, compensation days are given in the following week.<sup id="cite_ref-Manitoba_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Manitoba-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While not generally observed in the United States, on 5 December 1996, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> Gov. <a href="/wiki/William_F._Weld" class="mw-redirect" title="William F. Weld">William F. Weld</a> declared 26 December as Boxing Day in <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> in response to the efforts of a local coalition of British citizens to "transport the English tradition to the United States",<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> but not as an employee holiday.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Nigeria, Boxing Day is a public holiday for working people and students. When it falls on a Saturday or Sunday, there is always a holiday on Monday.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, Boxing Day is a public holiday.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, Boxing Day was a public holiday for working people and students; when it fell on a Saturday or Sunday, there was a holiday on Monday. However, in recent years this tradition has ceased in Singapore.
</p><p>In the British overseas territory of <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">Bermuda</a>, the costumed <a href="/wiki/Gombey" title="Gombey">Gombey dancers</a> perform throughout the mid-Atlantic island on Boxing Day, a tradition believed to date back to the 18th century when slaves were permitted to gather at Christmastime.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Shopping">Shopping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boxing_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Shopping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In the UK,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Canada,<sup id="cite_ref-ctv.ca_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctv.ca-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Australia,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Trinidad and Tobago, and New Zealand,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Boxing Day is primarily known as a shopping holiday, much like <a href="/wiki/Black_Friday_(shopping)" title="Black Friday (shopping)">Black Friday</a> (the day after <a href="/wiki/Thanksgiving_(United_States)" title="Thanksgiving (United States)">Thanksgiving</a>) in the United States. Boxing Day sales are common and shops often allow dramatic price reductions. For many merchants, Boxing Day has become the day of the year with the greatest revenue. In the UK in 2009 it was estimated that up to 12 million shoppers appeared at the sales (a rise of almost 20% compared to 2008, although this was also affected by the fact that the <a href="/wiki/VAT" class="mw-redirect" title="VAT">VAT</a> was about to revert to 17.5% from 1 January, following the temporary reduction to 15%).<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many retailers open very early (typically 5&#160;am or even earlier) and offer <a href="/wiki/Doorbuster" title="Doorbuster">doorbuster</a> deals and <a href="/wiki/Loss_leader" title="Loss leader">loss leaders</a> to draw people to their stores. It is not uncommon for long queues to form early in the morning of 26 December, hours before the opening of shops holding the big sales, especially at <a href="/wiki/Big-box_store" title="Big-box store">big-box consumer electronics</a> retailers.<sup id="cite_ref-ctv.ca_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ctv.ca-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Many stores have a limited quantity of big draw or deeply discounted items.<sup id="cite_ref-toronto.ctv.ca_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-toronto.ctv.ca-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Because of the shoulder-to-shoulder crowds, many choose to stay at home and avoid the hectic shopping experience. Local media often covers the event, mentioning how early the shoppers began queuing up, and showing video of shoppers queuing and later leaving with their purchased items.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Many retailers have implemented practices aimed at managing large numbers of shoppers. They may limit entrances, restrict the number of patrons in a store at a time, provide tickets to people at the head of the queue to guarantee them a hot ticket item, or canvass queued-up shoppers to inform them of inventory limitations.<sup id="cite_ref-toronto.ctv.ca_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-toronto.ctv.ca-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In recent years, retailers have expanded deals to "<a href="/wiki/Boxing_Week" title="Boxing Week">Boxing Week</a>". While Boxing Day is 26 December, many retailers will run the sales for several days before or after 26 December, often up to New Year's Eve. Notably, in <a href="/wiki/Late_2000s_recession" class="mw-redirect" title="Late 2000s recession">the recession of late 2008</a>, a record number of retailers held early promotions due to a weak economy.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Canada's Boxing Day has often been compared with the American <a href="/wiki/Super_Saturday" title="Super Saturday">Super Saturday</a> (the Saturday before Christmas) and <a href="/wiki/Black_Friday_(shopping)" title="Black Friday (shopping)">Black Friday</a>. From 2009 onward, Black Friday deals became more prominent among Canadian retailers to discourage shoppers from crossing the border to the US when the Canadian and USA dollars were close to parity. This has lessened the appeal of Boxing Day in Canada somewhat as it was overtaken by Black Friday in terms of sales in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Boxing Day is not and has never been a shopping holiday in the USA; however, 26 December often starts many U.S. retailers' after-Christmas sales and the earliest starting day after Christmas of many people, ex. Americans, returning unwanted gifts for exchanges or refunds.
</p><p>In some areas of Canada, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Canada" title="Atlantic Canada">Atlantic Canada</a> and parts of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ontario" title="Northern Ontario">Northern Ontario</a>, most retailers are prohibited from opening on Boxing Day, either by provincial law or by municipal <a href="/wiki/Bylaw" class="mw-redirect" title="Bylaw">bylaw</a>, or by informal agreement among major retailers, in order to provide a day of relaxation following Christmas Day. In these areas, sales otherwise scheduled for 26 December are moved to the 27th.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The city council of <a href="/wiki/Greater_Sudbury" title="Greater Sudbury">Greater Sudbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a>, which was the largest city in Canada to maintain this restriction as of the early 2010s, formally repealed its store hours bylaw on 9 December 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2009, many retailers with both online and <a href="/wiki/High_Street" title="High Street">High Street</a> stores launched their online sales on Christmas Eve and their High Street sales on Boxing Day.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Boxing_Day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MCG_stands.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/MCG_stands.jpg/220px-MCG_stands.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/MCG_stands.jpg/330px-MCG_stands.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/MCG_stands.jpg/440px-MCG_stands.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1025" data-file-height="672" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MCG_stands.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Boxing_Day_Test" title="Boxing Day Test">Boxing Day Test</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Melbourne_Cricket_Ground" title="Melbourne Cricket Ground">Melbourne Cricket Ground</a>, 2006</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Keswick_Boxing_Day_hunt_1962.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Keswick_Boxing_Day_hunt_1962.jpg/220px-Keswick_Boxing_Day_hunt_1962.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Keswick_Boxing_Day_hunt_1962.jpg/330px-Keswick_Boxing_Day_hunt_1962.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Keswick_Boxing_Day_hunt_1962.jpg/440px-Keswick_Boxing_Day_hunt_1962.jpg 2x" data-file-width="786" data-file-height="518" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Keswick_Boxing_Day_hunt_1962.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Boxing Day Meet of the Blencathra Foxhounds in Keswick, 1962</div></div></div>
<p>In the United Kingdom, it is traditional for all top-tier <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> leagues in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> – the <a href="/wiki/Premier_League" title="Premier League">Premier League</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Premiership" title="Scottish Premiership">Scottish Premiership</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/NIFL_Premiership" title="NIFL Premiership">NIFL Premiership</a> – and the lower ones, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby</a> leagues, to hold a full programme of <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> matches on Boxing Day. Originally, matches on Boxing Day were played against <a href="/wiki/Local_derby" class="mw-redirect" title="Local derby">local rivals</a> to avoid teams and their fans having to travel a long distance to an away game on the day after Christmas Day.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Prior to the formation of leagues, a number of traditional <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a> fixtures took place on Boxing Day each year, notably <a href="/wiki/Llanelli_RFC" title="Llanelli RFC">Llanelli</a> v <a href="/wiki/London_Welsh_RFC" title="London Welsh RFC">London Welsh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leicester_Tigers" title="Leicester Tigers">Leicester</a> v <a href="/wiki/Barbarian_F.C." title="Barbarian F.C.">The Barbarians</a>.
</p><p>In Italy, Boxing Day football was played for the first time in the <a href="/wiki/2018%E2%80%9319_Serie_A" title="2018–19 Serie A">2018/19 Serie A season</a>. The experiment was successful, with Italian stadiums 69% full on average – more than any other matchday in December 2018.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Rugby_league" title="Rugby league">rugby league</a>, festive fixtures were a staple of the traditional winter season. Since the transition to a summer season in the 1990s, no formal fixtures are now arranged on Boxing Day but some clubs, such as <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Trinity" title="Wakefield Trinity">Wakefield Trinity</a>, arrange a traditional <a href="/wiki/Local_derby" class="mw-redirect" title="Local derby">local derby</a> friendly fixture instead.
</p><p>In Australia, New Zealand and South Africa, <a href="/wiki/Test_cricket" title="Test cricket">Test cricket</a> matches are played on Boxing Day. For more details see <a href="/wiki/Boxing_Day_Test" title="Boxing Day Test">Boxing Day Test</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Australia, the first day of the <a href="/wiki/Boxing_Day_Test" title="Boxing Day Test">Boxing Day Test</a> in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a> and the start of the <a href="/wiki/Sydney_to_Hobart_Yacht_Race" title="Sydney to Hobart Yacht Race">Sydney to Hobart Yacht Race</a> are on Boxing Day.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In horse racing, there is the <a href="/wiki/King_George_VI_Chase" title="King George VI Chase">King George VI Chase</a> at <a href="/wiki/Kempton_Park_Racecourse" title="Kempton Park Racecourse">Kempton Park Racecourse</a> in Surrey, England. It is the second most prestigious <a href="/wiki/National_Hunt_racing" title="National Hunt racing">chase</a> in Britain, after the <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_Gold_Cup" title="Cheltenham Gold Cup">Cheltenham Gold Cup</a>. In addition to the prestigious race at Kempton, in Britain, it is usually the day with the highest number of racing meetings of the year, with eight in 2016, in addition to three more in Ireland.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> In Barbados, the final day of horse racing is held on Boxing Day at The Historic Garrison Savannah, a UNESCO world heritage site. This tradition has been going on for decades in this former British colony.
</p><p>Boxing Day is one of the main days in the hunting calendar for <a href="/wiki/List_of_foxhound_packs_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="List of foxhound packs of the United Kingdom">hunts</a> in the UK and US, with most hunts (both mounted foxhound or harrier packs and foot packs of beagles or bassets) holding meets, often in town or village centres.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several <a href="/wiki/Ice_hockey" title="Ice hockey">ice hockey</a> contests are associated with the day. The <a href="/wiki/IIHF_World_U20_Championship" title="IIHF World U20 Championship">IIHF World U20 Championship</a> typically begins on 26 December, while the <a href="/wiki/Spengler_Cup" title="Spengler Cup">Spengler Cup</a> also begins on 23 December in <a href="/wiki/Davos" title="Davos">Davos</a>, Switzerland; the Spengler Cup competition includes <a href="/wiki/HC_Davos" title="HC Davos">HC Davos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada_men%27s_national_ice_hockey_team" title="Canada men&#39;s national ice hockey team">Team Canada</a>, and other top European Hockey teams. The <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">National Hockey League</a> traditionally had close to a full slate of games (10 were played in 2011<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>), following the league-wide days off given for Christmas Eve and Christmas Day. However, the 2013 collective bargaining agreement (which followed <a href="/wiki/2012-13_NHL_lockout" class="mw-redirect" title="2012-13 NHL lockout">a lock-out</a>) extended the league mandate of Christmas Eve and Christmas Day off to include Boxing Day, except when it falls on a Saturday, in which case the league can choose to make 23 December a league-wide off day instead for that year.<sup id="cite_ref-NHL_CBA_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHL_CBA-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In some African <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> nations, particularly Ghana, Uganda, Malawi, Zambia and Tanzania, <a href="/wiki/Professional_boxing" title="Professional boxing">professional boxing</a> contests are held on Boxing Day. This practice has also been followed for decades in Guyana and Italy.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A notable tradition in Sweden is <i><i lang="sv" title="Swedish language text"><a href="/wiki/Saint_Stephen%27s_Day_bandy" title="Saint Stephen&#39;s Day bandy">Annandagsbandy</a></i></i>, which formerly marked the start of the <a href="/wiki/Bandy" title="Bandy">bandy</a> season and always draws large crowds. Games traditionally begin at 1:15&#160;pm.<sup id="cite_ref-annandagsbandy_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-annandagsbandy-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
