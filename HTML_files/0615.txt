<p><b>Free public transport</b>, often called <b>fare-free public transit</b> or <b>zero-fare public transport</b>, refers to <a href="/wiki/Public_transport" title="Public transport">public transport</a> funded in full by means other than by collecting <a href="/wiki/Fare" title="Fare">fares</a> from passengers. It may be funded by national, regional or local <a href="/wiki/Government" title="Government">government</a> through <a href="/wiki/Taxation" class="mw-redirect" title="Taxation">taxation</a>, or by commercial <a href="/wiki/Sponsor_(commercial)" title="Sponsor (commercial)">sponsorship</a> by businesses.  Alternatively, the concept of "free-ness" may take other forms, such as <a href="/wiki/Free_travel_pass" title="Free travel pass">no-fare access via a card</a> which may or may not be paid for in its entirety by the user. Luxembourg is set to be the first country in the world to make all public transport free<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> from 1 March 2020.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Germany is considering making their public transit system fare-free in response to the EU's threatening to fine them for their air pollution levels.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_public_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="City-wide_systems">City-wide systems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_public_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: City-wide systems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tallinn" title="Tallinn">Tallinn</a>, capital city of <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> with more than 420,000 inhabitants, and several mid-size European cities and many smaller towns around the world have converted their <a href="/wiki/Public_transportation" class="mw-redirect" title="Public transportation">public transportation</a> networks to zero-fare. The city of <a href="/wiki/Hasselt" title="Hasselt">Hasselt</a> in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> is a notable example: fares were abolished in 1997 and <a href="/wiki/Ridership" class="mw-redirect" title="Ridership">ridership</a> was as much as "13 times higher" by 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-stad_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stad-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_services">Local services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_public_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Local services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Local zero-fare shuttles or inner-city loops are far more common than citywide systems. They often use <a href="/wiki/Buses" class="mw-redirect" title="Buses">buses</a> or <a href="/wiki/Trams" class="mw-redirect" title="Trams">trams</a>. These may be set up by a city government to ease <a href="/wiki/Traffic_bottleneck" title="Traffic bottleneck">bottlenecks</a> or fill short gaps in the transport network.
</p>
<dl><dd><i>See <a href="/wiki/List_of_free_public_transport_routes" title="List of free public transport routes">List of free public transport routes</a> for a list of zero-fare routes within wider (fare-paying) networks</i></dd></dl>
<p>Zero-fare transport is often operated as part of the services offered within a public facility, such as a hospital or university <a href="/wiki/Campus" title="Campus">campus</a> shuttle or an <a href="/wiki/Airport" title="Airport">airport</a> inter-terminal shuttle.
</p><p>Some zero-fare services may be built to avoid the need for large transport construction. <a href="/wiki/Port_city" class="mw-redirect" title="Port city">Port cities</a> where shipping would require very high <a href="/wiki/Bridge" title="Bridge">bridges</a> might provide zero-fare <a href="/wiki/Ferries" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferries">ferries</a> instead. These are free at the point of use, just as the use of a bridge might have been.
Machinery installed within a building or shopping centre can be seen as 'zero-fare transport': <a href="/wiki/Elevator" title="Elevator">elevators</a>, <a href="/wiki/Escalators" class="mw-redirect" title="Escalators">escalators</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moving_sidewalk" class="mw-redirect" title="Moving sidewalk">moving sidewalks</a> are often provided by property owners and funded through the sales of goods and services. <a href="/wiki/Community_bicycle_program" class="mw-redirect" title="Community bicycle program">Community bicycle programs</a>, providing free <a href="/wiki/Bicycles" class="mw-redirect" title="Bicycles">bicycles</a> for short-term public use could be thought of as zero-fare transport.
</p><p>A common example of zero-fare transport is <a href="/wiki/Student_transport" title="Student transport">student transport</a>, where students travelling to or from school do not need to pay. A notable example is the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Wisconsin-Stevens_Point" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Wisconsin-Stevens Point">University of Wisconsin-Stevens Point</a>, which provides much of the funding to operate the <a href="/wiki/Stevens_Point_Transit" title="Stevens Point Transit">Stevens Point Transit</a> system. All students at the university can use any of the four citywide campus routes and the other four bus routes throughout the city free of charge. The university also funds two late night bus routes to serve the downtown free of charge with a goal of cutting down drunk driving.
</p><p>In some regions transport is free because the revenues are lower that expenses from fare collection is already partially paid by government or company or service (for example BMO railway road in Moscow, most part of is used to as service transport and officially pick up passengers).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="this sentence is very confusing, where did you learn English?&#32;:-) (May 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Many large <a href="/wiki/Amusement_park" title="Amusement park">amusement parks</a> will have trams servicing large parking lots or distant areas. <a href="/wiki/Disneyland" title="Disneyland">Disneyland</a> in <a href="/wiki/Anaheim,_California" title="Anaheim, California">Anaheim, California</a>, runs a tram from its entrance, across the parking lot, and across the street to its hotel as well as the bus stop for <a href="/wiki/Orange_County_Transportation_Authority" title="Orange County Transportation Authority">Orange County</a> and <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_County_Metropolitan_Transportation_Authority" title="Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority">Los Angeles</a> local transit buses. <a href="/wiki/Six_Flags_Magic_Mountain" title="Six Flags Magic Mountain">Six Flags Magic Mountain</a> in <a href="/wiki/Valencia,_California" class="mw-redirect" title="Valencia, California">Valencia, California</a>, provides tram service throughout its parking lot.
</p><p>In July 2017, <a href="/wiki/Dubai" title="Dubai">Dubai</a> announced it would offer free bus services for a short period of time on selected days.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Benefits">Benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_public_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Operational_benefits">Operational benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_public_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Operational benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Transport operators can benefit from faster boarding and shorter dwell times, allowing faster <a href="/wiki/Public_transport_timetable" title="Public transport timetable">timetabling</a> of services. Although some of these benefits can be achieved in other ways, such as off-vehicle ticket sales and modern types of electronic <a href="/wiki/Fare" title="Fare">fare</a> collection, zero-fare transport avoids equipment and personnel costs.
</p><p>Passenger aggression may be reduced. In 2008 bus drivers of Société des Transports Automobiles (STA) in <a href="/wiki/Essonne" title="Essonne">Essonne</a> held strikes demanding zero-fare transport for this reason. They claim that 90% of the aggression is related to refusal to pay the fare.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_benefits">Commercial benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_public_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Commercial benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some zero-fare transport services are funded by private businesses, such as the merchants in a <a href="/wiki/Shopping_mall" title="Shopping mall">shopping mall</a>, in the hope that doing so will increase sales or other revenue from increased foot traffic or ease of travel. Employers often operate free shuttles as a benefit to their employees, or as part of a <a href="/wiki/Traffic_congestion" title="Traffic congestion">congestion</a> mitigation agreement with a local government.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Community_benefits">Community benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_public_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Community benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Zero-fare transport can make the system more accessible and fair for low-income residents. Other benefits are the same as those attributed to public transport generally:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">Road</a> traffic can benefit from decreased <a href="/wiki/Traffic_congestion" title="Traffic congestion">congestion</a> and faster average road speeds, fewer traffic accidents, easier <a href="/wiki/Parking" title="Parking">parking</a>, savings from reduced wear and tear on roads</li>
<li>Increased public access, especially for the poor and low waged, which can in turn benefit <a href="/wiki/Social_integration" title="Social integration">social integration</a>, businesses and those looking for work</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Environment_(biophysical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Environment (biophysical)">Environmental</a> and <a href="/wiki/Public_health" title="Public health">public health</a> benefits including decreased <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise pollution</a> from road traffic</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Global_benefits">Global benefits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_public_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Global benefits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Global benefits of zero-fare transport are also the same as those attributed to public transport generally. If use of personal <a href="/wiki/Cars" class="mw-redirect" title="Cars">cars</a> is discouraged, zero-fare public transport could mitigate the problems of <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oil_depletion" title="Oil depletion">oil depletion</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disadvantages">Disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_public_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several large U.S. municipalities have attempted zero-fare systems, but many of these implementations have been judged unsuccessful by policy makers. A 2002 National Center for Transportation Research report suggests that, while transit ridership does tend to increase, there are also some disadvantages:<sup id="cite_ref-nctr_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nctr-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>An increase in vandalism, resulting in increased costs for security and vehicle-maintenance</li>
<li>In large transit systems, significant revenue shortfalls unless additional funding was provided</li>
<li>An increase in driver complaints and staff turnover, although farebox-related arguments were eliminated</li>
<li>Slower service overall (not collecting fares has the effect of speeding boarding, but increased crowding tends to swamp out this effect unless additional vehicles are added)</li>
<li>Declines in schedule adherence</li></ul>
<p>This U.S. report suggests that, while ridership does increase overall, the goal of enticing drivers to take transit instead of driving is not necessarily met: because fare-free systems tend to attract a certain number of "problem riders", zero-fare systems may have the unintended effect of convincing some 'premium' riders to go back to driving their cars. It should be kept in mind that this was a study that only looked at U.S. cities, and the author's conclusions may be less applicable in other countries that have better social safety nets and less crime than the large U.S. cities studied.<sup id="cite_ref-nctr_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nctr-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Perception_and_analysis">Perception and analysis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Free_public_transport&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Perception and analysis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-More_citations_needed_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Free_public_transport&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed.<br /><small><span class="plainlinks"><i>Find sources:</i>&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="//www.google.com/search?as_eq=wikipedia&amp;q=%22Free+public+transport%22">"Free public transport"</a>&#160;–&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="//www.google.com/search?tbm=nws&amp;q=%22Free+public+transport%22+-wikipedia">news</a>&#160;<b>·</b> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="//www.google.com/search?&amp;q=%22Free+public+transport%22+site:news.google.com/newspapers&amp;source=newspapers">newspapers</a>&#160;<b>·</b> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="//www.google.com/search?tbs=bks:1&amp;q=%22Free+public+transport%22+-wikipedia">books</a>&#160;<b>·</b> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="//scholar.google.com/scholar?q=%22Free+public+transport%22">scholar</a>&#160;<b>·</b> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.jstor.org/action/doBasicSearch?Query=%22Free+public+transport%22&amp;acc=on&amp;wc=on">JSTOR</a></span></small></span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">August 2018</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Free public transport creates the perception of a no-cost service, just as car drivers commonly perceive no cost to deciding to take their car somewhere. The catch of the car-based system is that the car trip is <a href="/wiki/Car_costs" title="Car costs">not in fact free</a>, but it is generally perceived as such.
</p><p>Likewise, this <i>perception</i> of freeness is important for public transport, which is far more environmentally and resource efficient than own-car travel – which means in this case that full access to the system need not be altogether “free” for its users but that from a financial perspective it becomes front-loaded and affordable. The invariable fact of life of delivering any public service is that the money to do so must come from somewhere – and of “free” public transport that once the user has entered into some kind of “contract” with her or his city – for example a monthly or annual transit pass that opens up the public system to unlimited use for those who pay for it. Now, how they pay and how much will be part of the overall political/economic package (“contract”) of their community.  In cities that offer such passes – as is the case to take but one example in most cities in France that since the mid-seventies have had their own <a href="/wiki/Carte_Orange" class="mw-redirect" title="Carte Orange">Carte Orange</a> – the remainder of the funds needed to pay for these services comes from other sources (mainly in this case from employers, <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">local government</a>).
</p><p>Social-justice advocacy groups, such as the <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> network <a href="/wiki/Planka.nu" title="Planka.nu">Planka.nu</a>, see zero-fare public transport as an effort in the <a href="/wiki/Redistribution_of_wealth" class="mw-redirect" title="Redistribution of wealth">redistribution of wealth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> It is also argued that transportation to and from <a href="/wiki/Employment" title="Employment">work</a> is a necessary part of the work day, and is essential to the <a href="/wiki/Employer" class="mw-redirect" title="Employer">employer</a> in the managing of work hours. It is thus argued that financing of public transportation should fall to employers rather than private citizens.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
