<p>This article features the <b>naming culture</b> of <b><a href="/wiki/Personal_name" title="Personal name">personal names</a></b> of ethnic <a href="/wiki/Serbs" title="Serbs">Serbs</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">Serbian language</a>. Serbian names are rendered in the "Western name order" with the <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> placed after the <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>. "Eastern name order" may be used when multiple names appear in a sorted list, particularly in official notes and legal documents when the last name is capitalized (e.g. MILOVANOVIĆ Janko).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Given_names">Given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serbian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Serbian_given_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Serbian given names">Serbian given names</a></div>
<p>As with most European cultures, a child is <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given a first name</a> chosen by their parents or <a href="/wiki/Godparent" title="Godparent">godparents</a>. The given name comes first, the surname last, e.g. "Željko Popović", where "Željko" is a first name and "Popović" is a family name.
</p><p>Serbian first names largely originate from <a href="/wiki/Slavic_names" title="Slavic names">Slavic roots</a>: e.g. <a href="/wiki/Miroslav_(given_name)" title="Miroslav (given name)">Miroslav</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_(name)" title="Vladimir (name)">Vladimir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zoran" title="Zoran">Zoran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lubomir_(given_name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lubomir (given name)">Ljubomir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vesna_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Vesna (name)">Vesna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radmila" title="Radmila">Radmila</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milica" title="Milica">Milica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Svetlana" title="Svetlana">Svetlana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slavica_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Slavica (disambiguation)">Slavica</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bo%C5%BEidarka&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Božidarka (page does not exist)">Božidarka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milorad" title="Milorad">Milorad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dragan" title="Dragan">Dragan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milan_(given_name)" title="Milan (given name)">Milan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goran_(Slavic_name)" title="Goran (Slavic name)">Goran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radomir_(given_name)" title="Radomir (given name)">Radomir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vuka%C5%A1in" title="Vukašin">Vukašin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Miomir" title="Miomir">Miomir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Branimir" title="Branimir">Branimir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Budimir_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Budimir (disambiguation)">Budimir</a>; see also <a href="/wiki/Slavic_names" title="Slavic names">Slavic names</a>, or the <a href="https://sr.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%A1%D0%BF%D0%B8%D1%81%D0%B0%D0%BA_%D1%81%D0%BB%D0%BE%D0%B2%D0%B5%D0%BD%D1%81%D0%BA%D0%B8%D1%85_%D0%B8%D0%BC%D0%B5%D0%BD%D0%B0" class="extiw" title="sr:Списак словенских имена">list of Slavic names in the Serbian Wikipedia</a>)
</p><p>Some may be non-<a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic</a> but chosen to reflect <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> faith. Names of this nature may often originate from <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> for <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Biblical</a> reasons. Christian names include: e.g. <a href="/wiki/Nikola" title="Nikola">Nikola</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ivan_(name)" title="Ivan (name)">Ivan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jovan" title="Jovan">Jovan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maria_(given_name)" title="Maria (given name)">Marija</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anna_(given_name)" title="Anna (given name)">Ana</a>, Mihailo. Along similar lines of non-Slavic names among Christians, the origins for many such names are <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">Greek</a>: e.g. <a href="/wiki/Aleksandar" class="mw-redirect" title="Aleksandar">Aleksandar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andrej" class="mw-redirect" title="Andrej">Andrej</a>, <a href="/wiki/Teodora" title="Teodora">Teodora</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jelena_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jelena (disambiguation)">Jelena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sophia_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sophia (name)">Sofija</a>, <a href="/wiki/Katarina_(given_name)" title="Katarina (given name)">Katarina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nikola" title="Nikola">Nikola</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C4%90or%C4%91e" title="Đorđe">Đorđe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stefan_(given_name)" title="Stefan (given name)">Stefan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petar" class="mw-redirect" title="Petar">Petar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vasilije" title="Vasilije">Vasilije</a>, <a href="/wiki/Todor" title="Todor">Todor</a>. Names of <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> origin include: e.g. <a href="/wiki/Marko_(given_name)" title="Marko (given name)">Marko</a>, <a href="/wiki/Angela_(given_name)" title="Angela (given name)">Anđelka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthony_(given_name)" title="Anthony (given name)">Antonije</a>, <a href="/wiki/Paul_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul (name)">Pavle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sr%C4%91an" title="Srđan">Srđan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marina_(given_name)" title="Marina (given name)">Marina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Natalia_(given_name)" title="Natalia (given name)">Natalija</a>, Kornelije. Names of <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a> origin, entering through Russian, include: e.g. <a href="/wiki/Igor_(given_name)" title="Igor (given name)">Igor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oliver_(given_name)" title="Oliver (given name)">Oliver</a>, <a href="/wiki/Olga_(name)" title="Olga (name)">Olga</a>.
</p><p>In Serbian naming culture, apotropaic names (<i>zaštitne ime</i>, "protective names") include <a href="/wiki/Vuk_(name)" title="Vuk (name)">Vuk</a> (and its many derivatives), <a href="/wiki/Nenad" title="Nenad">Nenad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prodan" title="Prodan">Prodan</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sredoje&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Sredoje (page does not exist)">Sredoje</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stani%C5%A1a" title="Staniša">Staniša</a>, and others.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surnames">Surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serbian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most <b>Serbian surnames</b> have the surname <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffix</a> <i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text"><a href="/wiki/-i%C4%87" class="mw-redirect" title="-ić">-ić</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Serbian_Cyrillic_alphabet" title="Serbian Cyrillic alphabet">Serbian Cyrillic</a>: <span lang="sr-Cyrl" title="Serbian language text">-ић</span>) (<small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Serbo-Croatian" title="Help:IPA/Serbo-Croatian">[itɕ]</a></span>). This can sometimes further be transcribed as <i>-ic</i>, but in history, Serbian names have often been transcribed with a phonetic ending, <i>-ich</i> or <i>-itch</i>.
</p><p>This form is often associated with <a href="/wiki/Serbs" title="Serbs">Serbs</a> from before the early 20th century: hence <a href="/wiki/Milutin_Milankovi%C4%87" title="Milutin Milanković">Milutin Milanković</a> is usually referred to, for historical reasons, as Milutin Milankovitch, and <a href="/wiki/Mileva_Mari%C4%87" title="Mileva Marić">Mileva Marić</a>, born in <a href="/wiki/Vojvodina" title="Vojvodina">Vojvodina</a> (then a part of Hungary) has sometimes been rendered as Marity (e.g. in the claim of "Einstein-Marity" theory).
</p><p>The <i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text">-ić</i> suffix is a <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic</a> <a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">diminutive</a>, originally functioning to create <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymics</a>. Thus the surname <a href="/wiki/Petrovi%C4%87" title="Petrović">Petrović</a> means the little son of Petar (Petrić signifies the little son of Petra, the widow).
</p><p>Most Serbian surnames are paternal (father), maternal (mother), occupational, or derived from personal traits.
</p><p>Other common surname suffixes are <i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text">-ov</i> (<span lang="sr-Cyrl" title="Serbian language text">-ов</span>), <i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text">-ev</i> (<span lang="sr-Cyrl" title="Serbian language text">-ев</span>), <i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text">-in</i> (<span lang="sr-Cyrl" title="Serbian language text">-ин</span>) and <i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text">-ski</i> (<span lang="sr-Cyrl" title="Serbian language text">-ски</span>; also <i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text">-cki</i>(<span lang="sr-Cyrl" title="Serbian language text">-цки</span>)/<i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text">čki</i>(<span lang="sr-Cyrl" title="Serbian language text">чки</span>)/<i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text">ški</i>(<span lang="sr-Cyrl" title="Serbian language text">шки</span>)) which is the Slavic <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">possessive case</a> suffix, thus Nikola's son becomes Nikolin, Petar's son Petrov, and Jovan's son Jovanov. The two suffixes are often combined, most commonly as <i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text">-ović</i> (<span lang="sr-Cyrl" title="Serbian language text">-овић</span>). Other, less common suffices are <i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text">-alj</i>(<span lang="sr-Cyrl" title="Serbian language text">-аљ</span>)/<i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text">olj</i>(<span lang="sr-Cyrl" title="Serbian language text">ољ</span>)/<i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text">elj</i>(<span lang="sr-Cyrl" title="Serbian language text">ељ</span>), <i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text">-ija</i> (<span lang="sr-Cyrl" title="Serbian language text">-ија</span>), <i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text">-ica</i> (<span lang="sr-Cyrl" title="Serbian language text">-ица</span>), <i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text">-ar</i>(<span lang="sr-Cyrl" title="Serbian language text">-ар</span>)/<i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text">ac</i>(<span lang="sr-Cyrl" title="Serbian language text">ац</span>)/<i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text">an</i>(<span lang="sr-Cyrl" title="Serbian language text">ан</span>).
</p><p>When marrying, the woman most often adopts her husband's family name, though she can also keep both of her last names or not change her last name at all.
</p><p>It is estimated that some two thirds of all Serbian surnames end in <i lang="sr-Latn" title="Serbian language text">-ić</i>. The ten most common surnames in <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>, in order, are <a href="/wiki/Jovanovi%C4%87" title="Jovanović">Jovanović</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petrovi%C4%87" title="Petrović">Petrović</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nikoli%C4%87" title="Nikolić">Nikolić</a>, <a href="/wiki/Markovi%C4%87" title="Marković">Marković</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C4%90or%C4%91evi%C4%87" title="Đorđević">Đorđević</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stojanovi%C4%87" title="Stojanović">Stojanović</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ili%C4%87" title="Ilić">Ilić</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stankovi%C4%87" title="Stanković">Stanković</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pavlovi%C4%87" title="Pavlović">Pavlović</a> and <a href="/wiki/Milo%C5%A1evi%C4%87" class="mw-redirect" title="Milošević">Milošević</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Politika_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Politika-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Outside Serbian countries, Slavic suffixes have been transliterated. <a href="/wiki/Serbs_in_Hungary" title="Serbs in Hungary">Serbs in Hungary</a> have the endings <i lang="hu" title="Hungarian language text">-ity</i>, <i lang="hu" title="Hungarian language text">-ics</i> <i lang="hu" title="Hungarian language text">-its</i>, <a href="/wiki/Serbs_in_North_Macedonia" class="mw-redirect" title="Serbs in North Macedonia">Serbs in North Macedonia</a> <i lang="mk-Latn" title="Macedonian-language romanization">-ikj</i> (or <i lang="mk-Latn" title="Macedonian-language romanization">iḱ</i>; <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_language" title="Macedonian language">Macedonian</a>: <span lang="mk" title="Macedonian language text">иќ</span>), and <a href="/wiki/Serbs_in_Romania" class="mw-redirect" title="Serbs in Romania">Serbs in Romania</a> <i lang="ro" title="Romanian language text">-ici</i>.
</p><p>Although far less common than patronymic surnames, matronymic surnames are widespread both in Serbia and in neighboring countries where Serbs live. Examples include surnames such as Katić, Sinđelić, Nedić, Marić, Višnjić, Janjić, Sarić, Miličić, Milenić, Natalić, Zorić, Smiljić, Anđelić and many others. Sometimes it is difficult to ascertain if the name of a specific family is patronymic or matronymic considering many Serbian names have both male and female version (for example, surname Miljanić could come from both m.- Miljan and f.- Miljana). Cases where widows had to become heads of households were not uncommon during 18th and 19th century and when surnames were first standardized in Serbia in 1851 it was decided they would be based on the names of eldest living heads of households which in some cases were women. People who did not know their father well would also often take matronymic surnames, with a notable case being the hero of the First Serbian Uprising Stevan Sinđelić, who took that surname in honor of his mother Sinđelija.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Serbian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The names of early Serbian rulers like <a href="/wiki/Mutimir" class="mw-redirect" title="Mutimir">Mutimir</a> are <a href="/wiki/Slavic_dithematic_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Slavic dithematic names">Slavic dithematic names</a>, as per Old Slavic tradition, until the 9th century and Christianization after which Christian names appear.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Demetrios_Chomatenos" title="Demetrios Chomatenos">Demetrios Chomatenos</a> (<a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Ohrid" title="Archbishop of Ohrid">Archbishop of Ohrid</a> from 1216 to 1236) registered the naming culture of the <a href="/wiki/South_Slavs" title="South Slavs">South Slavs</a> in <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_lands" class="mw-redirect" title="Byzantine lands">Byzantine lands</a>. In the 11th and 12th century, family names became more common and stable in Byzantium, adapted by the majority of people in <a href="/wiki/Macedonia_(region)#Medieval_Macedonia" title="Macedonia (region)">Byzantine Macedonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Epirus" title="Epirus">Epirus</a> and other regions (including women, sometimes even monks), not only aristocrats. The South Slavs, however, maintained the tradition of only giving a personal name, sometimes with a <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">Patronymic</a>. There are only 2 cases of family names used by South Slavs during this time; Bogdanopoulos and Serbopoulos, both Serbian names with the Greek suffix -opoulos (όπουλος, originating in <a href="/wiki/Peloponnese" title="Peloponnese">Peloponnese</a> in the 10th century)<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>. Patronymics ending on -ić, on the other hand, seem to have been the norm by late 14th and early 15th century because nearly all letters of correspondence between Dubrovnik and Serbia and Bosnia from that period contain them. In that same period proper family names of Slavic origin, not just patronymics, appear in Dubrovnik and soon in Hum and then in Serbia and Bosnia where during 15th-century nobles start using proper surnames. However this never became common among ordinary people and since nobility of Serbia and Bosnia was mostly wiped out by 16th century, only their remnants in Venice, Hungary and later Habsburg monarchy as well as some members of high clergy used standard surnames during following centuries. Due to general lack of safety clans started to form in regions of Montenegro and Herzegovina from 15th century onwards. These clans were territorially based but each was subdivided into fraternities so some people used names of these fraternities as a surname in those regions but only when speaking to outsiders and more as a toponymic rather than proper surname.
</p><p>In older naming convention which was common in Serbia up until the mid 19th century a person's name would consist of three distinct parts: the person's given name, the patronymic derived from father's personal name, and the family or fraternity name, as seen in for example in the name of language reformer Vuk Stefanović Karadžić. However, use of this convention depended on a person's education and interest in his ancestry. Most ordinary people were still referred to mostly by their given name and sometimes with a patronymic, profession or toponymic. Serbian surnames as used today were first standardized in Principality of Serbia during 1851 and on the census of 1854, the population was recorded by their fixed surnames for the first time. Surnames were mostly formed as patronymics (or in some cases matronymics) derived from names of at the time eldest living heads of households rather than distant ancestors, though there were exceptions. In most cases, such patronymics were already in use so they were simply "frozen" and turned into surnames that carried on into future generations. This swift introduction of surnames is one of the reasons why, in comparison to other regions where Serbs live, there is less variation in forms of surnames within central Serbia, where vast majority of surnames ends with suffixes -ović (in patronymic surnames) and -ić (can be used both for patronymic and for matronymic surnames).
</p><p>Among Serbs that lived across the rivers Drina, Sava and Danube, in addition to surnames with these most common suffixes there were many surnames based on professions, nicknames, toponymics, traits, etc. In case of what was then Southern Hungary, Serb suffixes were often intentionally changed by Austro-Hungarian administrators from -ović, -ević and -ić into -ov, -ev, -in and -ski which in their mind sounded less typically Serbian. This process started around 1817 but was particularly intensified after 1860/61 when Duchy of Serbia and Tamiš Banate was abolished and 1867 when Habsburg monarchy was reformed into Austro-Hungary. By 1900s it had only moderate success and it never achieved its true goal of culturally separating Vojvodina Serbs from their brethren to the south. In some regions with Serbian majority which were only liberated during wars of 1912–1918, standardized surnames were finally introduced with the creation of Kingdom of Serbs, Croats and Slovenes and recorded for the first time during population census of 1921.
</p>
