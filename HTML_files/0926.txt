	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Appendectomy</h1>

<p>An <b>appendectomy</b>, also spelled appendicectomy<b>,</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Surgery" title="Surgery">surgical operation</a> in which the <a href="/wiki/Vermiform_appendix" class="mw-redirect" title="Vermiform appendix">vermiform appendix</a> (a portion of the intestine) is removed. Appendectomy is normally performed as an urgent or emergency procedure to treat acute <a href="/wiki/Appendicitis" title="Appendicitis">appendicitis</a>.
</p><p>Appendectomy may be performed <a href="/wiki/Laparoscopic_surgery" class="mw-redirect" title="Laparoscopic surgery">laparoscopically</a> (as <a href="/wiki/Minimally_invasive_surgery" class="mw-redirect" title="Minimally invasive surgery">minimally invasive surgery</a>) or as an open operation. Laparoscopy is often used if the diagnosis is in doubt, or in order to leave a less visible surgical scar. Recovery may be slightly faster after laparoscopic surgery, although the laparoscopic procedure itself is more expensive and resource-intensive than open surgery and generally takes longer. Advanced pelvic sepsis occasionally requires a lower midline <a href="/wiki/Laparotomy" title="Laparotomy">laparotomy</a>. In US adults, the 30-day mortality after appendectomy was 1.8%.<sup id="cite_ref-Margenthaler_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Margenthaler-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Procedure">Procedure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Appendectomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Procedure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Young_nurses_assist_at_an_appendectomy_8b07788v.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Young nurses assist at an appendectomy 8b07788v.jpg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Young_nurses_assist_at_an_appendectomy_8b07788v.jpg/220px-Young_nurses_assist_at_an_appendectomy_8b07788v.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="230" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Young_nurses_assist_at_an_appendectomy_8b07788v.jpg/330px-Young_nurses_assist_at_an_appendectomy_8b07788v.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Young_nurses_assist_at_an_appendectomy_8b07788v.jpg/440px-Young_nurses_assist_at_an_appendectomy_8b07788v.jpg 2x" data-file-width="981" data-file-height="1024" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Young_nurses_assist_at_an_appendectomy_8b07788v.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div></div></div></div>

<p>In general terms, the procedure for an open appendectomy is:
</p>
<ol><li>Antibiotics are given immediately if signs of actual <a href="/wiki/Sepsis" title="Sepsis">sepsis</a> are seen (in appendicitis, sepsis and bacteremia usually only occurs at some point after rupture, once peritonitis has begun), or if there is reasonable suspicion that the appendix has ruptured (e.g., on imaging) or if the onset of peritonitis- which will lead to full sepsis if not quickly treated- is suspected; otherwise, a single dose of prophylactic intravenous antibiotics is given immediately before surgery.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/General_anaesthesia" title="General anaesthesia">General anaesthesia</a> is induced, with <a href="/wiki/Endotracheal_tube" class="mw-redirect" title="Endotracheal tube">endotracheal intubation</a> and full <a href="/wiki/Muscle_relaxant" title="Muscle relaxant">muscle relaxation</a>, and the patient is positioned <a href="/wiki/Supine_position" title="Supine position">supine</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Abdomen" title="Abdomen">abdomen</a> is prepared and draped and is examined under anesthesia.</li>
<li>If a mass is present, the incision is made over the mass. Otherwise, the incision is made over <a href="/wiki/McBurney%27s_point" title="McBurney&#39;s point">McBurney's point</a> (one-third of the way from the <a href="/wiki/Anterior_superior_iliac_spine" title="Anterior superior iliac spine">anterior superior iliac spine</a>  to the <a href="/wiki/Navel" title="Navel">umbilicus</a>), which represents the most common position of the base of the appendix.</li>
<li>The various layers of the <a href="/wiki/Abdominal_wall" title="Abdominal wall">abdominal wall</a> are opened. In order to preserve the integrity of abdominal wall, the <a href="/wiki/External_oblique" class="mw-redirect" title="External oblique">external oblique</a> <a href="/wiki/Aponeurosis" title="Aponeurosis">aponeurosis</a> is split along the line of its fibers, as is the <a href="/wiki/Internal_oblique" class="mw-redirect" title="Internal oblique">internal oblique</a> muscle. As the two run at right angles to each other, this reduces the risk of later <a href="/wiki/Incisional_hernia" title="Incisional hernia">incisional hernia</a>.</li>
<li>On entering the <a href="/wiki/Peritoneum" title="Peritoneum">peritoneum</a>, the appendix is identified, mobilized, and then <a href="/wiki/Ligature_(medicine)" title="Ligature (medicine)">ligated</a> and divided at its base.</li>
<li>Some surgeons choose to bury the stump of the appendix by inverting it so it points into the <a href="/wiki/Caecum" class="mw-redirect" title="Caecum">caecum</a>.</li>
<li>Each layer of the abdominal wall is then closed in turn.</li>
<li>The skin may be closed with staples or stitches.</li>
<li>The wound is dressed.</li>
<li>The patient is brought to the recovery room.</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Incisions">Incisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Appendectomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Incisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These incisions are placed for appendectomy:
</p>
<ol><li>McBurney's incision, also known as grid iron incision</li>
<li>Lanz incision</li>
<li>Rutherford Morison incision</li>
<li>Paramedian incision</li></ol>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Appendectomy_plus_10_days.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Appendectomy_plus_10_days.png/220px-Appendectomy_plus_10_days.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Appendectomy_plus_10_days.png/330px-Appendectomy_plus_10_days.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Appendectomy_plus_10_days.png/440px-Appendectomy_plus_10_days.png 2x" data-file-width="2420" data-file-height="2040" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Appendectomy_plus_10_days.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wound healing - ten days after a laparoscopic appendectomy</div></div></div>
<p>Over the past decade, the outcomes of laparoscopic appendectomies have compared favorably to those for open appendectomies because of decreased pain, fewer postoperative complications, shorter hospitalization, earlier mobilization, earlier return to work, and better <a href="/wiki/Cosmesis" title="Cosmesis">cosmesis</a>; however, despite these advantages, efforts are still being made to decrease abdominal incision and visible scars after laparoscopy. Recent research has led to the development of natural orifice transluminal endoscopic surgery (NOTES); however, numerous difficulties need to be overcome before a wider clinical application of NOTES is adopted, including complications such as the opening of hollow viscera, failed sutures, a lack of fully developed instrumentation, and the necessity of reliable cost-benefit analyses.<sup id="cite_ref-journalofmas.com_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journalofmas.com-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-journalofmas.com_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journalofmas.com-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many surgeons have attempted to reduce incisional morbidity and improve cosmetic outcomes in laparoscopic appendicectomy by using fewer and smaller ports. Kollmar<i> et al.</i> described moving laparoscopic incisions to hide them in the natural camouflages like the suprapubic hairline to improve cosmesis. Additionally, reports in the literature indicate that minilaparoscopic appendectomy using 2– or 3-mm or even smaller instruments along with one 12-mm port minimizes pain and improves cosmesis. More recently, studies by Ates <i>et al.</i> and Roberts <i>et al.</i> have described variants of an <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/intracorporeal" class="extiw" title="wikt:intracorporeal">intracorporeal</a> sling-based single-port laparoscopic appendectomy with good clinical results.<sup id="cite_ref-journalofmas.com_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journalofmas.com-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Also, a trend is increasing towards single-incision laparoscopic surgery (SILS), using a special multiport umbilical trocar. With SILS, a more conventional view of the field of surgery is seen compared to NOTES. The equipment used for SILS is familiar to surgeons already doing laparoscopic surgery. Most importantly, it is easy to convert SILS to conventional laparoscopy by adding a few trocars; this conversion to conventional laparoscopy is called 'port rescue'. SILS has been shown to be feasible, reasonably safe, and cosmetically advantageous, compared to standard laparoscopy; however, this newer technique involves specialized instruments and is more difficult to learn because of a loss of triangulation, clashing of instruments, crossing of instruments (cross triangulation), and a lack of maneuverability.<sup id="cite_ref-journalofmas.com_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journalofmas.com-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  The additional problem of decreased exposure and the added financial burden of procuring special articulating or curved coaxial instruments exist. SILS is still evolving, being used successfully in many centres, but with some way to go before it becomes mainstream. This limits its widespread use, especially in rural or peripheral centres with limited resources.<sup id="cite_ref-journalofmas.com_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-journalofmas.com-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pregnancy">Pregnancy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Appendectomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pregnancy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>If appendicitis develops in a <a href="/wiki/Pregnancy" title="Pregnancy">pregnant</a> woman, an appendectomy is usually performed and should not harm the <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">fetus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The risk of premature delivery is about 10% <sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The risk of fetal death in the perioperative period after an appendectomy for early acute appendicitis is 3 to 5%. The risk of fetal death is 20% in perforated appendicitis.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recovery">Recovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Appendectomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Recovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SCAR.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/SCAR.jpg/220px-SCAR.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/SCAR.jpg/330px-SCAR.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/SCAR.jpg/440px-SCAR.jpg 2x" data-file-width="604" data-file-height="453" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SCAR.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Scar and bruise 2 days after operation</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2011-08-03_Cicatriz_posterior_apendicectom%C3%ADa_paciente_masculino.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/2011-08-03_Cicatriz_posterior_apendicectom%C3%ADa_paciente_masculino.jpg/220px-2011-08-03_Cicatriz_posterior_apendicectom%C3%ADa_paciente_masculino.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/2011-08-03_Cicatriz_posterior_apendicectom%C3%ADa_paciente_masculino.jpg/330px-2011-08-03_Cicatriz_posterior_apendicectom%C3%ADa_paciente_masculino.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/2011-08-03_Cicatriz_posterior_apendicectom%C3%ADa_paciente_masculino.jpg/440px-2011-08-03_Cicatriz_posterior_apendicectom%C3%ADa_paciente_masculino.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2560" data-file-height="1920" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2011-08-03_Cicatriz_posterior_apendicectom%C3%ADa_paciente_masculino.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Scar 10 days after operation</div></div></div>
<p>A study from 2010 found that the average hospital stay for people with appendicitis in the United States was 1.8 days. For people with a perforated (ruptured) appendix, the average length of stay was 5.2 days.<sup id="cite_ref-hcup-us.ahrq.gov_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hcup-us.ahrq.gov-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Recovery time from the operation varies from person to person. Some take up to three weeks before being completely active; for others, it can be a matter of days. In the case of a laparoscopic operation, the patient has three stapled scars of about an inch (2.5&#160;cm) in length, between the navel and pubic hair line. When an open appendectomy has been performed, the patient has a 2– to 3-inch (5–7.5&#160;cm) scar, which will initially be heavily bruised.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Frequency">Frequency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Appendectomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Frequency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>About 327,000 appendectomies were performed during U.S. hospital stays in 2011, a rate of 10.5 procedures per 10,000 population. Appendectomies accounted for 2.1% of all operating-room procedures in 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Appendectomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Appendectomy_at_the_French_Hospital_in_Tiflis_(Dartigues_1919).JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Appendectomy_at_the_French_Hospital_in_Tiflis_%28Dartigues_1919%29.JPG/220px-Appendectomy_at_the_French_Hospital_in_Tiflis_%28Dartigues_1919%29.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="199" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Appendectomy_at_the_French_Hospital_in_Tiflis_%28Dartigues_1919%29.JPG/330px-Appendectomy_at_the_French_Hospital_in_Tiflis_%28Dartigues_1919%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Appendectomy_at_the_French_Hospital_in_Tiflis_%28Dartigues_1919%29.JPG/440px-Appendectomy_at_the_French_Hospital_in_Tiflis_%28Dartigues_1919%29.JPG 2x" data-file-width="868" data-file-height="786" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Appendectomy_at_the_French_Hospital_in_Tiflis_(Dartigues_1919).JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An appendectomy at the French Hospital in <a href="/wiki/Tbilisi,_Georgia" class="mw-redirect" title="Tbilisi, Georgia">Tbilisi, Georgia</a>, 1919</div></div></div>
<p>The first recorded successful appendectomy was on December 6, 1735, at <a href="/wiki/St_George%27s_University_of_London" class="mw-redirect" title="St George&#39;s University of London">St. George's Hospital</a> in London, when French surgeon <a href="/wiki/Claudius_Amyand_(surgeon)" title="Claudius Amyand (surgeon)">Claudius Amyand</a> described the presence of a perforated appendix within the <a href="/wiki/Inguinal_hernia" title="Inguinal hernia">inguinal hernial</a> sac of an 11-year-old boy.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The organ had apparently been perforated by a pin the boy had swallowed. The patient, Hanvil Andersen, made a recovery and was discharged a month later.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Harry Hancock performed the first <a href="/wiki/Abdominal_surgery" title="Abdominal surgery">abdominal surgery</a> for appendicitis in 1848, but he did not remove the appendix.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In 1889 in New York City, <a href="/wiki/Charles_McBurney_(surgeon)" title="Charles McBurney (surgeon)">Charles McBurney</a> described the presentation and pathogenesis of appendicitis accurately and developed the teaching that an early appendectomy was the best treatment to avoid perforation and <a href="/wiki/Peritonitis" title="Peritonitis">peritonitis</a>.
</p><p>Some cases of autoappendectomies have occurred. One was attempted by <a href="/wiki/Evan_O%27Neill_Kane" title="Evan O&#39;Neill Kane">Evan O'Neill Kane</a> in 1921, but the operation was completed by his assistants. Another was <a href="/wiki/Leonid_Rogozov" title="Leonid Rogozov">Leonid Rogozov</a>, who had to perform the operation on himself as he was the only doctor on a remote Antarctic base.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On September 13, 1980, <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Semm" title="Kurt Semm">Kurt Semm</a> performed the first laparoscopic appendectomy opening up the path for a much wider application of minimally invasive surgery.<sup id="cite_ref-litynski_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-litynski-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cost">Cost</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Appendectomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cost">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Appendectomy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While appendectomy is a standard surgical procedure, its cost has been found to vary considerably in the United States. A 2012 study  analyzed 2009 data from nearly 20,000 adult patients treated for appendicitis in California hospitals. Researchers examined “only uncomplicated episodes of acute appendicitis” that involved “visits for patients 18 to 59 years old with hospitalization that lasted fewer than four days with routine discharges to home.” The lowest charge for removal of an appendix was $1,529 and the highest $182,955, more than 120 times greater. The median charge was $33,611.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> While the study was limited to California, the researchers indicated that the results were applicable anywhere in the United States. Many, but not all, patients are covered by some sort of medical insurance.<sup id="cite_ref-fl120424_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fl120424-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A study by the <a href="/wiki/Agency_for_Healthcare_Research_and_Quality" title="Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality">Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality</a> found that in 2010, the average cost for a stay in the United States involving appendicitis was $7,800. For stays where the appendix had ruptured, the average cost was $12,800. The majority of patients seen in the hospital were covered by private insurance.<sup id="cite_ref-hcup-us.ahrq.gov_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hcup-us.ahrq.gov-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
