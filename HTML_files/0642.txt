	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Narrowboat</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For the 1944 book by L. T. C. Rolt, see <a href="/wiki/Narrow_Boat_(book)" title="Narrow Boat (book)">Narrow Boat (book)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">

</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bugsworth_058069.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Bugsworth_058069.jpg/220px-Bugsworth_058069.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Bugsworth_058069.jpg/330px-Bugsworth_058069.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Bugsworth_058069.jpg/440px-Bugsworth_058069.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bugsworth_058069.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Modern narrowboats for leisure cruising,<br /> <a href="/wiki/Bugsworth_Basin" title="Bugsworth Basin">Bugsworth Basin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buxworth" title="Buxworth">Buxworth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a>, England</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>narrowboat</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Boat" title="Boat">boat</a> (or <a href="/wiki/Barge" title="Barge">barge</a>) of a particular design, made to fit the narrow <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_British_canal_system" title="History of the British canal system">canal locks of the United Kingdom</a>. The canal system were the UK's "motorways" of the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>, but with the advent of the railways commercial canal traffic gradually diminished and the last regular long-distance traffic disappeared in 1970. However, some commercial traffic continued into the 1980s and beyond.
</p><p>To enter a narrow lock, a narrowboat must be under 7 feet (2.13&#160;m) wide, so most narrowboats are just 6&#160;feet 10&#160;inches (2.08&#160;m) wide). A narrowboat's maximum length is 72 feet (21.95&#160;m). Anything wider or longer will be unable to navigate most of the British canal network. Some locks are shorter than 72', so to access the entire canal network the maximum length is 57 feet (17.37&#160;m)
</p><p>The first narrow boats played a key part in the economic changes of the British <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>.  They were wooden boats drawn by a horse walking on the canal <a href="/wiki/Towpath" title="Towpath">towpath</a> led by a crew member. Horses were gradually replaced with steam and then diesel engines. By the end of the 19th century it was common practice to paint roses and castles on narrow boats and their fixtures and fittings. This tradition has continued into the 21st century, but not all narrowboats have such decorations.
</p><p>Modern narrowboats are used for holidays, weekend breaks, touring, or as permanent or part-time residences. Usually, they have steel hulls and a steel superstructure. The hull's flat base is usually 10mm thick, the hull sides 6mm or 8mm, the cabin sides 6mm, and the roof 4mm or 6mm. The numbers of boats have been rising, with the number of licensed boats (not all of them narrowboats) on canals and rivers managed by the <a href="/wiki/Canal_%26_River_Trust" title="Canal &amp; River Trust">Canal &amp; River Trust</a> (CRT) estimated at about 27,000 in 2006 and over 30,000 in 2014. Although a small number of steel narrowboats dispense with the need for a rear steering deck entirely, by imitating some river cruisers in providing wheel steering from a central cockpit, most narrowboats' steering is by a <a href="/wiki/Tiller" title="Tiller">tiller</a> on the stern. There are three major configurations for the stern: <a href="#Traditional_stern">traditional stern</a>, <a href="#Cruiser_stern">cruiser stern</a> and <a href="#Semi-traditional_stern">semi-traditional stern</a>.
</p><p>Narrowboats are "Category D" boats which are intended only for navigating rivers, canals and small lakes; but some intrepid boaters have crossed the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> in a narrowboat.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wide-beam_boats">Wide-beam boats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narrowboat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Wide-beam boats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The narrow canals of England are primarily found in the Midlands, but it was soon realised that wider canal boats could carry more cargoes, so later canals in the north and south of England  had 14-foot wide locks. (There is no wide link between north and south; a possible link at Northampton being stymied by a flight of narrow locks). English widebeam canal boats are of similar appearance to narrowboats, but <a href="/wiki/Dutch_barge" title="Dutch barge">Dutch barges</a> are more popular as they are better suited for those wishing to navigate beyond the UK waterways to the European system. 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narrowboat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The narrowboat (one word) definition in the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> is:<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>A British canal boat of traditional long, narrow design, steered with a tiller; spec. one not exceeding 7 feet (approx. 2.1 metres) in width or 72 feet (approx. 21.9 metres) in length
</p></blockquote>
<p>Earlier quotations listed in the Oxford English Dictionary use the term "narrow boat", with the most recent, a quotation from an advertisement in <i>Canal Boat &amp; Inland Waterways</i> in 1998, uses "narrowboat".
</p><p>The single word "narrowboat" has been adopted by authorities such as the <a href="/wiki/Canal_and_River_Trust" class="mw-redirect" title="Canal and River Trust">Canal and River Trust</a>, Scottish Canals and the magazine <i>Waterways World</i> to refer to all boats built in the style and tradition of commercial boats that were able to fit in the narrow canal locks.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Although some narrow boats were built to a design based on river barges and many conform to the strict definition of the term, it is incorrect to refer to a narrowboat (or narrow boat) as a <a href="/wiki/Barge" title="Barge">barge</a>. In the context of the British inland waterways, a barge is usually a much wider, cargo-carrying boat or a modern boat modelled on one, certainly more than 7 feet (2.13&#160;m) wide.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Another historic term for a narrow boat is a <a href="/wiki/Long_boat" class="mw-redirect" title="Long boat">long boat</a>,  this name was used in the Midlands and especially on the River Severn and connecting waterways to Birmingham.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Usage has not quite settled down as regards (a) boats based on narrowboat design, but too wide for narrow canals; or (b) boats the same width as narrowboats but based on other types of boat.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Narrowboats may have <a href="/wiki/Ship_prefix" title="Ship prefix">ship prefix</a> <i>NB</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Size">Size</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narrowboat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Size">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The key distinguishing feature of a narrowboat is its width, which must be less than 7 feet (2.13&#160;m) wide to navigate British narrow canals. Some old boats are very close to this limit (often built 7&#160;feet <span class="frac nowrap">1<span class="visualhide">&#160;</span><sup>1</sup>&#8260;<sub>2</sub></span>&#160;inches or 2.17 metres or slightly wider), and can have trouble using certain narrow <a href="/wiki/Canal_lock" class="mw-redirect" title="Canal lock">locks</a> whose width has been reduced over time because of <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">subsidence</a>. Modern boats are usually produced to a maximum of 6&#160;feet 10&#160;inches (2.08&#160;m) wide to guarantee easy passage throughout the complete system.
</p><p>Because of their slenderness, some narrowboats seem very long.  The maximum length is about 72 feet (21.95&#160;m), which matches the length of the longest locks on the system. Modern narrowboats tend to be shorter, to permit cruising anywhere on the connected network of British canals — including on canals built for wider, but shorter, boats.  The shortest lock on the main network is <a href="/wiki/Salterhebble" title="Salterhebble">Salterhebble</a> Middle Lock on the <a href="/wiki/Calder_and_Hebble_Navigation" title="Calder and Hebble Navigation">Calder and Hebble Navigation</a>, at about 56 feet (17.07&#160;m) long. However, the C&amp;H is a wide canal, so the lock is about 14&#160;feet 2&#160;inches (4.32&#160;m) wide. This makes the largest "go-anywhere-on-the-network" narrowboat slightly longer (about 58 feet or 17.68 metres) than the straight length of the lock, because it can (with a certain amount of "shoehorning") lie diagonally. Some locks on isolated waterways are as short as 40 feet (12.19&#160;m). Where it was possible to avoid going through locks, narrow boats were sometimes built a little larger.<sup id="cite_ref-wareB&amp;B_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wareB&amp;B-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Wharf boats or more usually 'Amptons, operated on the Wolverhampton level of the <a href="/wiki/Water_levels_of_the_Birmingham_Canal_Navigations" title="Water levels of the Birmingham Canal Navigations">Birmingham Canal Navigations</a> and were up to 89 feet in length and 7 foot 10.5 inches wide.<sup id="cite_ref-wareB&amp;B_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wareB&amp;B-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hire fleets on British canals usually consist of narrow boats in varied lengths from 30 feet (9.14&#160;m) upwards, to allow parties of different numbers or varying budgets to be able to hire a boat and get afloat.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Finowkanal-treidel.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Finowkanal-treidel.jpg/220px-Finowkanal-treidel.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="179" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Finowkanal-treidel.jpg/330px-Finowkanal-treidel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Finowkanal-treidel.jpg/440px-Finowkanal-treidel.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="651" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Finowkanal-treidel.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A widebeam barge being drawn by horses on a towpath in 1880s Germany.</div></div></div>
<p>The first narrow boats played a key part in the economic changes of the British <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>.  They were wooden boats drawn by a horse walking on the canal <a href="/wiki/Towpath" title="Towpath">towpath</a> led by a crew member, often a child. Narrow boats were chiefly designed for carrying cargo, though some <a href="/wiki/Packet_trade" title="Packet trade">packet boats</a>, carried passengers, luggage, mail and parcels.
</p><p>Boatmen's families originally lived ashore, but in the 1830s as canals started to suffer competition from the burgeoning <a href="/wiki/Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway">railway</a> system, families (especially those of independent single boat owner/skippers) began to live on board, partly because they could no longer afford rents, partly to provide extra hands to work the boats harder, faster and further, partly to keep families together. As late as 1858, a <i><a href="/wiki/Household_Words" title="Household Words">Household Words</a></i> article states that "the Grand Junction Canal company did not allow the boatmen's families on board." The crew of the non-stopping ("<a href="/wiki/Fly-boat" class="mw-redirect" title="Fly-boat">fly</a>") boat in the article (skipper, two crew and a "youth") is said to be typical.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Working_canal_boats.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Working_canal_boats.jpg/220px-Working_canal_boats.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Working_canal_boats.jpg/330px-Working_canal_boats.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Working_canal_boats.jpg/440px-Working_canal_boats.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="668" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Working_canal_boats.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Historic working narrow boats on the <a href="/wiki/Macclesfield_Canal" title="Macclesfield Canal">Macclesfield Canal</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a>, England. The leading boat, <i>Forget Me Not</i>, is hauling the un-powered butty <i>Lilith</i>. This became a familiar operating pattern once motors began to replace horses.</div></div></div>
<p>The rear portion of the boat became the "boatman's cabin", familiar from picture postcards and museums, famous for its space-saving ingenuity and interior made attractive by a warm stove, a steaming kettle, gleaming brass, fancy lace, painted housewares and decorated plates. Such descriptions rarely consider the actual comfort of a (sometimes large) family, working brutally hard and long days, sleeping in one tiny cabin. However many shore-bound workers endured harder indoor trades in less healthy conditions and in worse accommodation, where the family was separated for long hours rather than being together all day. The lifestyle afloat, by definition itinerant, made it impossible for children to attend school. Most boat people were effectively illiterate and ostracised by those living "on the bank", who considered themselves superior.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>As steam and diesel progressively replaced the tow-horse in the early years of the 20th century, it became possible to move even more cargo with fewer hands by towing a second, un-powered boat, referred to as a "butty", "buttyboat" or "butty boat". Although there was no longer a horse to maintain, the butty had to be steered while being towed. So that the butty boatman could lengthen or shorten towline as needed, the towline wasn't tied-off on the bow, instead travelled over the buttyboat through permanent running blocks on stands or retractable middle masts and managed in the stern.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  On a wide canal, such as the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Union_Canal" title="Grand Union Canal">Grand Union Canal</a>, the pair could be roped side-to-side ("breasted up") and handled as a unit through working locks.
</p><p>Cargo-carrying by narrow boat diminished from 1945 and the last regular long-distance traffic disappeared in 1970. However, some traffic continued into the 1980s and beyond. Two million tonnes of aggregate were carried on the Grand Union (River Soar) between 1976 and 1996, latterly using wide beam barges. Aggregate continues to be carried between Denham and West Drayton on the (wide) Grand Union Canal and on the tidal estuary of Bow Creek (which is the eventual outflow of the Lee &amp; Stort Navigation).
</p><p>A few people are doing their best in the 21st century to keep the tradition of canal-borne cargo-carrying alive, mostly by "one-off" deliveries rather than regular runs, or by selling goods such as coal to other boaters. Enthusiasts remain dedicated to restoring the remaining old narrow boats, often as members of the Historic Narrow Boat Owners Club.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> There are many replicas, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Hadar_(narrowboat)" title="Hadar (narrowboat)">Hadar</a></i>, ornately painted with traditional designs, usually of roses and castles. Boats not horse-drawn may have a refurbished, slow-revving, vintage <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engine</a>. There are some steam-driven narrow boats such as the ex-<a href="/wiki/Fellows_Morton_and_Clayton" title="Fellows Morton and Clayton">Fellows Morton and Clayton</a> steamer <a href="/wiki/President_(narrowboat)" title="President (narrowboat)"><i>President</i></a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Painted_decoration">Painted decoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narrowboat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Painted decoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Narrowboat_decoration.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Narrowboat_decoration.jpg/220px-Narrowboat_decoration.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Narrowboat_decoration.jpg/330px-Narrowboat_decoration.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Narrowboat_decoration.jpg/440px-Narrowboat_decoration.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="2048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Narrowboat_decoration.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Decoration on a traditional English narrowboat: roses on the water can (top) and castles on the open doors to the cabin</div></div></div>
<p>By the end of the 19th century it was common practice to paint roses and castles on narrow boats and their fixtures and fittings. Common sites include the doors to the cabin, the water can or barrel and the side of the boat along with ornate lettering giving the boat's name and owner. This tradition did not happen in <i>all</i> regions, the <a href="/wiki/Chesterfield_Canal" title="Chesterfield Canal">Chesterfield Canal</a> being one waterway where narrow boats never bore such decorations.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The origin of the roses and castles found on canal boats is unclear. The first written reference to them appears to be in an 1858 edition of the magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Household_Words" title="Household Words">Household Words</a></i> in one of a series of articles titled "On the Canal", showing that the art form must have existed by this date. For some time, a popular suggestion was that it had some form of <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romani</a> origin; however, there does not appear to be a significant link between the Romani and boating communities. Other suggestions include transfer of styles from the clock-making industry (in particular the decoration on the face), the <a href="/wiki/Japanning" title="Japanning">japanning</a> industry or the pottery industry. There is certainly a similarity in style and a geographical overlap, but no solid proof of a link. There are similar styles of folk art in <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>, Germany, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>.
</p><p>In the 18th century, similar <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Dutch</a> <a href="/wiki/Hindeloopen" title="Hindeloopen">Hindeloopen</a> paintwork would only have been a sailing barge journey away from the Thames. There is also an article in the Midland Daily Telegraph of 22 July 1914 that credits the practice of painting of water cans, at least, to a Mr Arthur Atkins.
</p><p>While the practice declined as commercial use of the canals dwindled, it has seen something of a revival in recent times with the emergence of leisure boating. Narrowboat decoration with roses and castle themes are a common sight on today's canals, although these may utilise cheaper printed vinyl transfers in place of the traditional craft of hand-painted designs.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_narrowboats">Modern narrowboats</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narrowboat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Modern narrowboats">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Modern_Narrow_Boats.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Modern_Narrow_Boats.jpg/220px-Modern_Narrow_Boats.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Modern_Narrow_Boats.jpg/330px-Modern_Narrow_Boats.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Modern_Narrow_Boats.jpg/440px-Modern_Narrow_Boats.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2848" data-file-height="2136" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Modern_Narrow_Boats.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Modern narrowboats on the <a href="/wiki/Kennet_and_Avon_Canal" title="Kennet and Avon Canal">Kennet and Avon Canal</a></div></div></div>
<p>The number of licensed boats on canals and rivers managed by the <a href="/wiki/Canal_%26_River_Trust" title="Canal &amp; River Trust">Canal &amp; River Trust</a> (CRT), a charitable trust, formerly British Waterways, was estimated at about 27,000 in 2006. By 2014 this number had risen to over 30,000. There were perhaps another 5,000 unlicensed boats kept in private moorings or on other waterways in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Most boats on CRT waterways are steel (or occasionally, aluminium) cruisers popularly referred to as narrowboats.
</p><p>Modern leisure narrowboats are used for holidays, weekend breaks, touring, as permanent or part-time residences. Usually, they have steel hulls and a steel superstructure, but when they were first being developed for leisure use in the 1970s glass reinforced plastic (fibre-glass) or timber was often used above gunwale height. Newer narrowboats, say post 1990, are usually powered by modern <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">diesel engines</a> and may be fitted inside to a high standard.  There will be at least 6 feet (1.8&#160;m) internal headroom and often or usually similar domestic facilities as land homes: central heating, flush toilets, shower or even bath, four-ring hobs, oven, grill, microwave oven, and refrigerator; some may have satellite television and mobile broadband, using <a href="/wiki/4G" title="4G">4G</a> technology. Externally, their resemblance to traditional boats can vary from a faithful imitation (false "rivets", and copies of traditional paintwork) through "interpretation" (clean lines and simplified paintwork) through to a free-style approach which does not try to pretend in any way that this is a traditional boat.
</p><p>They are owned by individuals, shared by a group of friends (or by a more formally organised syndicate), rented out by holiday firms, or used as cruising hotels. A few boats are lived on permanently: either based in one place (though long-term moorings for residential narrowboats are currently very difficult to find) or <a href="/wiki/Continuous_cruiser" title="Continuous cruiser">continuously moving around the network</a> (perhaps with a fixed location for the coldest months, when many stretches of canal are closed by repair works or "stoppages").
</p><p>A support infrastructure has developed to provide services to the leisure boats, with some narrowboats being used as platforms to provide services such as engine maintenance and boat surveys; while some others are used as fuel tenders, that provide <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">diesel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solid_fuel" title="Solid fuel">solid fuel</a> (coal and wood) and <a href="/wiki/Calor_Gas" title="Calor Gas">Calor Gas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narrowboat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On almost all narrowboats steering is by tiller, as was the case on all working narrow boats.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The steerer stands at the stern of the boat, aft of the hatchway and/or rear doors at the top of the steps up from the cabin. The steering area comes in three basic types, each meeting different needs of maximising internal space; having a more traditional appearance; having a big enough rear deck for everyone to enjoy summer weather or long evenings; or protection outside in bad weather. Each type has its advocates. However, the boundaries are not fixed, and some boats blur the categories as new designers try out different arrangements and combinations.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_stern">Traditional stern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narrowboat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Traditional stern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Narrow_boat_sterns.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Narrow_boat_sterns.jpg/220px-Narrow_boat_sterns.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="166" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Narrow_boat_sterns.jpg/330px-Narrow_boat_sterns.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Narrow_boat_sterns.jpg/440px-Narrow_boat_sterns.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1632" data-file-height="1232" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Narrow_boat_sterns.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Traditional-stern narrowboats at Saul, <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a></div></div></div>
<p>Many modern canal boats retain the traditional layout of a small open, unguarded "counter" or deck behind the rear doors from which the crew can step onto land. It is possible to steer from the counter, but this is not very safe, with the propeller churning below only one missed step away. The "tiller extension" allows the steerer to stand in safety on the top step, forward of the rear doors. (On a working boat, this step would have been over the top of the coal box). On cold days, the steerer can even close the rear doors behind themselves, and be in relative comfort, their lower body in the warmth of the cabin, and only their upper body emerging from the hatchway and exposed to the elements. In good weather, many trad-stern steerers sit up on the hatchway edge, a high vantage point giving good all-round visibility. On trad boats, the bow "well-deck" forms the main outside viewing area, because the traditional stern is not large enough for anyone other than the steerer to stand on safely. Internally, trads may have an engine room forward of a traditional "boatman's cabin",<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> or an enclosed engine tucked away out of sight and the increased living space this brings.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Cruiser_stern">Cruiser stern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narrowboat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cruiser stern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Narrowboats-at-tardebigge.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Narrowboats-at-tardebigge.jpg/220px-Narrowboats-at-tardebigge.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="87" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Narrowboats-at-tardebigge.jpg/330px-Narrowboats-at-tardebigge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Narrowboats-at-tardebigge.jpg/440px-Narrowboats-at-tardebigge.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1844" data-file-height="732" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Narrowboats-at-tardebigge.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cruiser-stern narrowboat at <a href="/wiki/Tardebigge" title="Tardebigge">Tardebigge</a></div></div></div>
<p>The name for this style arises from the large open rear deck resembling that of the large rear cockpits common on <a href="/wiki/Glass-fibre" class="mw-redirect" title="Glass-fibre">glass-fibre</a> (<a href="/wiki/Glass-reinforced_plastic" class="mw-redirect" title="Glass-reinforced plastic">glass-reinforced plastic</a> or GRP) river <i>cruisers</i> which in turn derives from elliptical sterns used on cruisers and larger warships in the 20th century. At the stern, a "cruiser" narrowboat looks very different from traditional boats: the hatch and rear doors are considerably further forward than on a "trad", creating a large open deck between counter and rear doors, protected by railing (perhaps with built-in seating) around back and sides. The large rear deck provides a good <a href="/wiki/Al_fresco_dining" title="Al fresco dining">al fresco dining</a> area or social space, allowing people to congregate on deck in good weather and the summer holiday season.
</p><p>In winter (or less than perfect weather of summer) the steerer may be unprotected from the elements. The lack of an enclosed engine room means that engine heat does not contribute to keeping the boat warm and there may be "wasted" space above the deck area.  A "cruiser" stern allows the engine to be located under the deck, rather than in the body of the boat. Although this may make access to the engine more of a nuisance (due to weather considerations) the whole deck can usually be lifted off in whole or in sections, allowing the operative to stand inside the engine bay, the cruiser stern has a major advantage that the engine is located entirely outside the living space. In this configuration also, it is common to find that the engine bay contains batteries, isolator switching, fuel tanks and seldom-used kit, spares and equipment.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Semi-traditional_stern">Semi-traditional stern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narrowboat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Semi-traditional stern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A <i>semi-traditional stern</i> is a compromise to gain some of the "social" benefits of a cruiser stern, while retaining a more traditional design and providing some protection for the steerer in bad weather or in cooler seasons. As with the cruiser stern, the deck is extended back from the hatch and rear doors, but in this case most of the deck is protected at the sides by walls which extend back from the cabin sides – giving a more sheltered area for the steerer and companions, usually with lockers to sit on. The engine is located under the deck, much like a cruiser, again allowing a separation between the cabin and the engine bay, with the steps down to the cabin being located past the false sides of the "semi-trad" social area. 
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="With_a_butty_stern">With a butty stern <span id="Narrowboats_with_a_Butty_stern"></span></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narrowboat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: With a butty stern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A butty boat is an unpowered boat traditionally with a larger rudder with (usually) a wooden tiller (known as an <i>elum</i>, a corruption of <i><a href="/wiki/Tiller" title="Tiller">helm</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>) as the steering does not benefit from the force of water generated by the propeller. The tiller is usually removed and reversed in the rudder-post socket to get it out of the way when moored. A few butty boats have been converted into powered narrowboats like NB <i>Sirius</i>. The term butty is derived from a dialect word meaning companion.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Centre_cockpit">Centre cockpit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narrowboat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Centre cockpit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>While the vast majority of narrowboats have tiller steering at the stern, a small number of steel narrowboats dispense with the need for a rear steering deck entirely, by imitating some river cruisers in providing wheel steering from a central cockpit.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_organisations">National organisations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narrowboat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: National organisations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
