	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Pennines</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Pennine_Alps" title="Pennine Alps">Pennine Alps</a>.</div>

</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pennines_location_map.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Pennines_location_map.png/260px-Pennines_location_map.png" decoding="async" width="260" height="260" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Pennines_location_map.png/390px-Pennines_location_map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Pennines_location_map.png/520px-Pennines_location_map.png 2x" data-file-width="2000" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pennines_location_map.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Northern England and adjoining areas, showing the general extent of the Pennines</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clougha_heather.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Clougha_heather.jpg/260px-Clougha_heather.jpg" decoding="async" width="260" height="195" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Clougha_heather.jpg/390px-Clougha_heather.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Clougha_heather.jpg/520px-Clougha_heather.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clougha_heather.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Scenery in the <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Bowland" title="Forest of Bowland">Forest of Bowland</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Pennines</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;p&#39; in &#39;pie&#39;">p</span><span title="/ɛ/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">ɛ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="&#39;z&#39; in &#39;zoom&#39;">z</span></span>/</a></span></span>), also known as the <b>Pennine Chain</b> or <b>Pennine Hills</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> are a range of <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountains</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hill" title="Hill">hills</a> in England separating <a href="/wiki/North_West_England" title="North West England">North West England</a> from <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_East_England" title="North East England">North East England</a>.
</p><p>Often described as the "<a href="/wiki/Vertebral_column" title="Vertebral column">backbone</a> of England",<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> the Pennine Hills form a more-or-less continuous range in most of <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">Northern England</a>. The range stretches northwards from the <a href="/wiki/Peak_District" title="Peak District">Peak District</a> in the northern <a href="/wiki/Midlands" title="Midlands">Midlands</a>, through the <a href="/wiki/South_Pennines" title="South Pennines">South Pennines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Dales" title="Yorkshire Dales">Yorkshire Dales</a> and <a href="/wiki/North_Pennines" title="North Pennines">North Pennines</a> up to the <a href="/wiki/River_Tyne" title="River Tyne">Tyne</a> Gap, which separates the range from the <a href="/wiki/Cheviot_Hills" title="Cheviot Hills">Cheviot Hills</a>. Some definitions of the Pennines also include the Cheviot Hills across the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Scottish_border" title="Anglo-Scottish border">Anglo-Scottish border</a> while excluding the southern Peak District. South of the <a href="/wiki/Aire_Gap" title="Aire Gap">Aire Gap</a> is a western <a href="/wiki/Spur_(mountain)" class="mw-redirect" title="Spur (mountain)">spur</a> into east Lancashire, comprising the <a href="/wiki/Rossendale_Valley" title="Rossendale Valley">Rossendale Fells</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Pennine_Moors" title="West Pennine Moors">West Pennine Moors</a><sup id="cite_ref-Britain&#39;s_Structure_and_Scenery_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Britain&#39;s_Structure_and_Scenery-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Bowland" title="Forest of Bowland">Bowland Fells</a> in North <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Great_Britain_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Great_Britain-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Howgill_Fells" title="Howgill Fells">Howgill Fells</a><sup id="cite_ref-Great_Mountain_Days_in_the_Pennines:_Cicerone_Press_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Great_Mountain_Days_in_the_Pennines:_Cicerone_Press-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Orton_Fells" title="Orton Fells">Orton Fells</a><sup id="cite_ref-A_Nature_Conservation_Review_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A_Nature_Conservation_Review-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a> are sometimes considered to be Pennine spurs to the west of the range. The Pennines are an important water catchment area with numerous <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoirs</a> in the head streams of the river valleys.
</p><p>The region is widely considered to be one of the most scenic areas of the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/North_Pennines_AONB" class="mw-redirect" title="North Pennines AONB">North Pennines</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nidderdale" title="Nidderdale">Nidderdale</a> are designated <a href="/wiki/Areas_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" class="mw-redirect" title="Areas of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Areas of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> (AONB) within the range, as are <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Bowland" title="Forest of Bowland">Bowland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pendle_Hill" title="Pendle Hill">Pendle Hill</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Parts of the Pennines are incorporated into the <a href="/wiki/Peak_District" title="Peak District">Peak District National Park</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Dales_National_Park" title="Yorkshire Dales National Park">Yorkshire Dales National Park</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Britain's oldest <a href="/wiki/Long-distance_trail" title="Long-distance trail">long-distance footpath</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Pennine_Way" title="Pennine Way">Pennine Way</a>, runs along most of the Pennine Chain and is 268&#160;miles (429&#160;km) long.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><span id="Etymology"></span><span id="Toponymy"></span>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pennines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stanage_Edge.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Stanage_Edge.jpg/220px-Stanage_Edge.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Stanage_Edge.jpg/330px-Stanage_Edge.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Stanage_Edge.jpg/440px-Stanage_Edge.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2400" data-file-height="1730" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stanage_Edge.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Stanage_Edge" title="Stanage Edge">Stanage Edge</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Peak_District" title="Peak District">Peak District</a></div></div></div>
<p>Various etymologies have proposed treating "Pennine" as though it were a native <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">Brittonic</a>/Modern <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> name related to <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pen#Welsh" class="extiw" title="wikt:pen">pen-</a></i> ("head") .<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It did not become a common name until the 18th century and almost certainly derives from modern comparisons with the <a href="/wiki/Apennine_Mountains" title="Apennine Mountains">Apennine Mountains</a>, which run down the middle of <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> in a similar fashion.<sup id="cite_ref-Redmonds_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Redmonds-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following an 1853 article by <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Hussey" title="Arthur Hussey">Arthur Hussey</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> it has become a common belief that the name derives from a passage in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Description_of_Britain" title="The Description of Britain">The Description of Britain</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">De Situ Britanniæ</i>),<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> an infamous <a href="/wiki/Literary_forgery" title="Literary forgery">historical forgery</a> concocted by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Bertram" title="Charles Bertram">Charles Bertram</a> in the 1740s and accepted as genuine until the 1840s. In 2004, George Redmonds reassessed this, finding that numerous respected writers passed over the origin of the mountains' name in silence even in works dedicated to the topological etymology of <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Redmonds_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Redmonds-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> He found that the derivation from Bertram was widely believed and considered uncomfortable.<sup id="cite_ref-Redmonds_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Redmonds-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In fact, he found repeated comparisons going back at least as early as <a href="/wiki/William_Camden" title="William Camden">Camden</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> many of whose placenames and ideas Bertram incorporated into his work. Bertram was responsible (at most) with popularizing the name against other contenders such as <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Defoe" title="Daniel Defoe">Daniel Defoe</a>'s "English Andes".<sup id="cite_ref-Redmonds_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Redmonds-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> His own form of the name was the "Pennine Alps" (<i><i lang="la" title="Latin language text">Alpes Peninos</i></i>), which today is used for <a href="/wiki/Pennine_Alps" title="Pennine Alps">a western section</a> of the continental <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a>. Those mountains (the area around the <a href="/wiki/Great_St_Bernard_Pass" title="Great St Bernard Pass">St. Bernard Pass</a>) derive their name from the Latin <i><a href="/wiki/Alpes_P%C5%93nin%C3%A6" class="mw-redirect" title="Alpes Pœninæ">Alpes Pœninæ</a></i> whose name has been variously derived from the <a href="/wiki/Hannibal%27s_crossing_of_the_Alps" title="Hannibal&#39;s crossing of the Alps">Carthaginians</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> a local god,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Common_Celtic" class="mw-redirect" title="Common Celtic">Celtic</a> <i>peninus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The St. Bernard Pass was the pass used in the <a href="/wiki/Campaign_history_of_the_Roman_military#Celtic_invasion_of_Italia_.28390.E2.80.93387_BC.29" title="Campaign history of the Roman military">invasions of Italy</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Gauls" title="Gauls">Gallic</a> <a href="/wiki/Boii" title="Boii">Boii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lingones" title="Lingones">Lingones</a> in 390&#160;<span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">BC</span></span>. The etymology of the <a href="/wiki/Apennines" class="mw-redirect" title="Apennines">Apennines</a> themselves—whose name first referred to their northern extremity and then later spread southward—is also disputed but is usually taken to derive from some form of <a href="/wiki/Common_Celtic" class="mw-redirect" title="Common Celtic">Celtic</a> <i>pen</i> or <i>ben</i> ("mountain, head").<sup id="cite_ref-lewis_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lewis-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Various towns and geographical features within the Pennines retain <a href="/wiki/Britons_(Celtic_people)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (Celtic people)">Celtic</a> names, including <a href="/wiki/Penrith,_Cumbria" title="Penrith, Cumbria">Penrith</a>, the fell <a href="/wiki/Pen-y-ghent" title="Pen-y-ghent">Pen-y-ghent</a>, the <a href="/wiki/River_Eden,_Cumbria" title="River Eden, Cumbria">River Eden</a>, and the area of <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a>. More commonly, local names result from later <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">Norse</a> settlements. In Yorkshire and Cumbria, many words of <a href="/wiki/Norse_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Norse language">Norse</a> origin, not commonly used in standard English, are part of everyday speech: for example, <i><a href="/wiki/Gill_(stream)" class="mw-redirect" title="Gill (stream)">gill/ghyll</a></i> (narrow steep valley), <i><a href="/wiki/Beck_(stream)" class="mw-redirect" title="Beck (stream)">beck</a></i> (brook or stream), <i><a href="/wiki/Fell" title="Fell">fell</a></i> (hill), and <i><a href="/wiki/Dale_(origin)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dale (origin)">dale</a></i> (valley).<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pennines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Croasdale_Bowland.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Croasdale_Bowland.jpg/220px-Croasdale_Bowland.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Croasdale_Bowland.jpg/330px-Croasdale_Bowland.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Croasdale_Bowland.jpg/440px-Croasdale_Bowland.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Croasdale_Bowland.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bowland_Forest_High" title="Bowland Forest High">Croasdale Fell</a>, Forest of Bowland.</div></div></div>
<p>The northern Pennine range is bordered by the <a href="/wiki/Foothill" class="mw-redirect" title="Foothill">foothills</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Lake_District" title="Lake District">Lake District</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Highland" title="Highland">uplands</a> of the Howgill Fells, Orton Fells and Cheviot Hills. The West Pennine Moors, Rossendale Valley<sup id="cite_ref-Britain&#39;s_Structure_and_Scenery_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Britain&#39;s_Structure_and_Scenery-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and Forest of Bowland<sup id="cite_ref-Great_Britain_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Great_Britain-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> are western spurs of the range, the former two of which are included as part of the South Pennines. The Howgill Fells<sup id="cite_ref-Great_Mountain_Days_in_the_Pennines:_Cicerone_Press_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Great_Mountain_Days_in_the_Pennines:_Cicerone_Press-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and Orton Fells<sup id="cite_ref-A_Nature_Conservation_Review_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-A_Nature_Conservation_Review-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> are sometimes considered to be part of the Pennines, with both of them lying inside the Yorkshire Dales National Park.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The Pennines are fringed by extensive <a href="/wiki/Upland_and_lowland" title="Upland and lowland">lowlands</a> including the <a href="/wiki/River_Eden,_Cumbria" title="River Eden, Cumbria">Eden Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Lancashire_Coastal_Plain" title="West Lancashire Coastal Plain">West Lancashire Coastal Plain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheshire_Plain" title="Cheshire Plain">Cheshire Plain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vale_of_York" title="Vale of York">Vale of York</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Midlands" title="Midlands">Midland Plains</a>.
</p><p>Most of the Pennine landscape is characterised by upland areas of high <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moorland</a> indented by more fertile river valleys, although the landscape varies in different areas. The Peak District consists of hilly plateaus, uplands and valleys, divided into the <a href="/wiki/Dark_Peak" title="Dark Peak">Dark Peak</a> with moorlands and gritstone edges, and the <a href="/wiki/White_Peak" title="White Peak">White Peak</a> with limestone gorges.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The South Pennines is an area of hilly landscape and moorlands with narrow valleys between the Peak District, Forest of Bowland and Yorkshire Dales.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Bowland is dominated by a central upland landform of deeply incised gritstone <a href="/wiki/Fell" title="Fell">fells</a> covered with tracts of heather-covered <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> moorland, <a href="/wiki/Blanket_bog" title="Blanket bog">blanket bog</a> and steep-sided wooded valleys linking the upland and lowland landscapes.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> The landscape is higher and more mountainous in the Yorkshire Dales and North Pennines. The Yorkshire Dales are characterised by moorlands, valleys, hills, fells and mountains<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> while the North Pennines consist of high upland plateaus, moorlands, fells, edges and valleys with most of the area containing flat topped hills while the higher peaks are in the western half.
</p><p>Although the Pennines mostly cover the area between the Tyne Gap and the Peak District, the presence of the Pennine Way affects the northern and southern extents of the defined area. The Cheviot Hills, separated by the Tyne Gap and the <a href="/wiki/Whin_Sill" title="Whin Sill">Whin Sill</a>, along which run the <a href="/wiki/A69_road" title="A69 road">A69</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hadrian%27s_Wall" title="Hadrian&#39;s Wall">Hadrian's Wall</a>, are not part of the Pennines but, perhaps because the Pennine Way crosses them, they are often treated as such. As a result, the northern end of the Pennines may be considered to be either at the Tyne Gap or the Cheviot Hills across the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Scottish_border" title="Anglo-Scottish border">Anglo-Scottish border</a>. Conversely the southern end of the Pennines is commonly said to be in the <a href="/wiki/High_Peak,_Derbyshire" title="High Peak, Derbyshire">High Peak</a> of <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a> at <a href="/wiki/Edale" title="Edale">Edale</a>, the start of the Pennine Way, excluding the southern Peak District. However, the range and its foothills continue across the Peak District as far south as <a href="/wiki/Stoke-on-Trent" title="Stoke-on-Trent">Stoke-on-Trent</a> in northern Staffordshire<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>  and <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a> in southern Derbyshire.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elevation">Elevation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pennines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Elevation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Rising less than 3,000 feet (900&#160;m), the Pennines are often referred to as fells, with the majority of mountainous terrain in the north. The highest is <a href="/wiki/Cross_Fell" title="Cross Fell">Cross Fell</a> in eastern Cumbria, at 2,930 feet (893&#160;m), while other principal peaks at the North Pennines include <a href="/wiki/Great_Dun_Fell" title="Great Dun Fell">Great Dun Fell</a> 2,782&#160;ft (848&#160;m), <a href="/wiki/Mickle_Fell" title="Mickle Fell">Mickle Fell</a> 2,585&#160;ft (788&#160;m), and <a href="/wiki/Burnhope_Seat" title="Burnhope Seat">Burnhope Seat</a> 2,451&#160;ft (747&#160;m). Principal peaks at the Yorkshire Dales include <a href="/wiki/Whernside" title="Whernside">Whernside</a> 2,415&#160;ft (736&#160;m), <a href="/wiki/Ingleborough" title="Ingleborough">Ingleborough</a> 2,372&#160;ft (723&#160;m), <a href="/wiki/High_Seat_(Yorkshire_Dales)" title="High Seat (Yorkshire Dales)">High Seat</a> 2,328&#160;ft (710&#160;m), <a href="/wiki/Wild_Boar_Fell" title="Wild Boar Fell">Wild Boar Fell</a> 2,324&#160;ft (708&#160;m) and <a href="/wiki/Pen-y-ghent" title="Pen-y-ghent">Pen-y-ghent</a> 2,274&#160;ft (693&#160;m). Principal peaks at the Forest of Bowland include <a href="/wiki/Ward%27s_Stone" title="Ward&#39;s Stone">Ward's Stone</a> 1,841&#160;ft (561&#160;m), <a href="/wiki/Fair_Snape_Fell" title="Fair Snape Fell">Fair Snape Fell</a> 1,710&#160;ft (521&#160;m), and <a href="/wiki/Hawthornthwaite_Fell" title="Hawthornthwaite Fell">Hawthornthwaite Fell</a> 1,572&#160;ft (479&#160;m). Although terrain is lower towards the south, principal peaks at the South Pennines and Peak District include <a href="/wiki/Kinder_Scout" title="Kinder Scout">Kinder Scout</a> 2,087&#160;ft (636&#160;m), <a href="/wiki/Bleaklow" title="Bleaklow">Bleaklow</a> 2,077&#160;ft (633&#160;m), <a href="/wiki/Black_Chew_Head" title="Black Chew Head">Black Chew Head</a> 1,778&#160;ft (542&#160;m), <a href="/wiki/Rombalds_Moor" title="Rombalds Moor">Rombalds Moor</a> 1,319&#160;ft (402&#160;m) and <a href="/wiki/Winter_Hill_(North_West_England)" title="Winter Hill (North West England)">Winter Hill</a> 1,496&#160;ft (456&#160;m).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drainage">Drainage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pennines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Drainage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kinder_Downfall_in_spate.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Kinder_Downfall_in_spate.jpg/220px-Kinder_Downfall_in_spate.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Kinder_Downfall_in_spate.jpg/330px-Kinder_Downfall_in_spate.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Kinder_Downfall_in_spate.jpg/440px-Kinder_Downfall_in_spate.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kinder_Downfall_in_spate.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/River_Kinder#Kinder_Downfall" title="River Kinder">Kinder Downfall</a>, a waterfall on Kinder Scout, Dark Peak</div></div></div>
<p>For much of their length the Pennines form the main <a href="/wiki/Water_divide" class="mw-redirect" title="Water divide">watershed</a> in northern England, dividing east and west. The rivers <a href="/wiki/River_Eden,_Cumbria" title="River Eden, Cumbria">Eden</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Ribble" title="River Ribble">Ribble</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Dane" title="River Dane">Dane</a> and tributaries of the <a href="/wiki/River_Mersey" title="River Mersey">Mersey</a> (including the <a href="/wiki/River_Irwell" title="River Irwell">Irwell</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Tame,_Greater_Manchester" title="River Tame, Greater Manchester">Tame</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Goyt" title="River Goyt">Goyt</a>) flow westwards towards the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a>. On the eastern side of the watershed, the rivers <a href="/wiki/River_Tyne,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="River Tyne, England">Tyne</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Tees" title="River Tees">Tees</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Wear" title="River Wear">Wear</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Swale" title="River Swale">Swale</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Ure" title="River Ure">Ure</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Nidd" title="River Nidd">Nidd</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Wharfe" title="River Wharfe">Wharfe</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Aire" title="River Aire">Aire</a>, <a href="/wiki/River_Calder,_West_Yorkshire" title="River Calder, West Yorkshire">Calder</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Don,_South_Yorkshire" class="mw-redirect" title="River Don, South Yorkshire">Don</a> rise in the region and flow eastwards to the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>. The <a href="/wiki/River_Trent" title="River Trent">River Trent</a>, however, rises on the western side of the Pennines before flowing around the southern end of the range and up the eastern side; together with its tributaries (principally the <a href="/wiki/River_Dove,_Central_England" title="River Dove, Central England">Dove</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Derwent,_Derbyshire" title="River Derwent, Derbyshire">Derwent</a>) it thus drains both east and west sides of the southern end to the North Sea.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pennines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Pennine climate is generally temperate like that of the rest of England, but the hills have more precipitation, stronger winds and colder weather than the surrounding areas. Higher elevations have a <a href="/wiki/Tundra_climate" class="mw-redirect" title="Tundra climate">tundra climate</a>.
More snow falls on the Pennines than on surrounding lowland areas due to the elevation and distance from the coast; unlike lowland areas of England, the Pennines can have quite severe winters.
</p><p>The northwest is amongst the wettest regions of England and much of the rain falls on the Pennines. The eastern side is drier than the west—the <a href="/wiki/Rain_shadow" title="Rain shadow">rain shadow</a> shields northeast England from rainfall that would otherwise fall there.
</p><p>Precipitation is important for the area's biodiversity and human population. Many towns and cities are located along rivers flowing from the hills and in northwest England the lack of natural aquifers is compensated for by reservoirs.
</p><p>Water has carved out gorges, caves and limestone landscapes in the Yorkshire Dales and Peak District. In some areas precipitation has contributed to poor soils, resulting in part in <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moorland</a> landscapes that characterize much of the range. In other areas where the soil has not been degraded, it has resulted in lush vegetation.
</p><p>For the purpose of growing plants, the Pennines are in <a href="/wiki/Hardiness_zone" title="Hardiness zone">hardiness zones</a> 7 and 8, as defined by the <a href="/wiki/USDA" class="mw-redirect" title="USDA">USDA</a>. Zone 8 is common throughout most of the UK, and zone 7 is the UK's coldest hardiness zone. The Pennines, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Highlands" title="Scottish Highlands">Scottish Highlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Southern_Uplands" title="Southern Uplands">Southern Uplands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Snowdonia" title="Snowdonia">Snowdonia</a> are the only areas of the UK in zone 7.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geology">Geology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pennines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Thor%27s_cave.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Thor%27s_cave.jpg/170px-Thor%27s_cave.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="207" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Thor%27s_cave.jpg/255px-Thor%27s_cave.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Thor%27s_cave.jpg/340px-Thor%27s_cave.jpg 2x" data-file-width="493" data-file-height="600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Thor%27s_cave.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Limestone scenery: <a href="/wiki/Thor%27s_Cave" title="Thor&#39;s Cave">Thor's Cave</a>, Staffordshire, from the Manifold Way. Limestone is common in the White Peak and Yorkshire Dales, making those areas distinct from other parts of the Pennines.</div></div></div>
<p>The Pennines have been carved from a series of geological structures whose overall form is a broad <a href="/wiki/Anticline" title="Anticline">anticline</a> whose axis extends in a north–south direction. The North Pennines are coincident with the <a href="/wiki/Alston_Block" title="Alston Block">Alston Block</a>, whilst the Yorkshire Dales are coincident with the <a href="/wiki/Askrigg_Block" title="Askrigg Block">Askrigg Block</a>. In the south the Peak District is essentially a flat-topped dome.
Each of these structures consists of <a href="/wiki/Carboniferous" title="Carboniferous">Carboniferous</a> <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> overlain with <a href="/wiki/Millstone_Grit" title="Millstone Grit">Millstone Grit</a>. The limestone is exposed at the surface in the North Pennines and Yorkshire Dales to the north of the range and the Peak District to the south. In the Yorkshire Dales and the <a href="/wiki/White_Peak" title="White Peak">White Peak</a> the limestone exposure has led to the formation of large cave systems and watercourses. 
</p><p>In the Dales the caves or potholes are known as "pots" in the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_dialect_and_accent" class="mw-redirect" title="Yorkshire dialect and accent">Yorkshire dialect</a>. They include some of the largest caves in England at <a href="/wiki/Gaping_Gill" title="Gaping Gill">Gaping Gill</a>, more than 350&#160;ft (107&#160;m) deep and <a href="/wiki/Rowten_Pot" title="Rowten Pot">Rowten Pot</a>, 365&#160;ft (111&#160;m) deep. <a href="/wiki/Titan_(cave)" title="Titan (cave)">Titan</a> in the Peak District, the deepest shaft known in Britain, is connected to <a href="/wiki/Peak_Cavern" title="Peak Cavern">Peak Cavern</a> in <a href="/wiki/Castleton,_Derbyshire" title="Castleton, Derbyshire">Castleton, Derbyshire</a>, the largest cave entrance in the country. Erosion of the limestone has led to some unusual geological formations, such as the <a href="/wiki/Limestone_pavement" title="Limestone pavement">limestone pavements</a> at <a href="/wiki/Malham_Cove" title="Malham Cove">Malham Cove</a>. Between the northern and southern areas of exposed limestone (between <a href="/wiki/Skipton" title="Skipton">Skipton</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dark_Peak" title="Dark Peak">Dark Peak</a>) lies a narrow belt of exposed gritstone. Here the shales and sandstones of the Millstone Grit form high hills occupied by a <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moorland</a> of <a href="/wiki/Bracken" title="Bracken">bracken</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a>, heather and coarse grasses;<sup id="cite_ref-DB_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DB-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> the higher ground is uncultivable and barely fit for pasture.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pennines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Harkerside_Moor.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Harkerside_Moor.jpg/220px-Harkerside_Moor.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Harkerside_Moor.jpg/330px-Harkerside_Moor.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Harkerside_Moor.jpg/440px-Harkerside_Moor.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="427" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Harkerside_Moor.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A prehistoric settlement on Harkerside Moor in <a href="/wiki/Swaledale" title="Swaledale">Swaledale</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The area contains many <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> settlements, and evidence of <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> settlement (including many <a href="/wiki/Stone_circles" class="mw-redirect" title="Stone circles">stone circles</a> or <a href="/wiki/Henges" class="mw-redirect" title="Henges">henges</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Long_Meg_and_Her_Daughters" title="Long Meg and Her Daughters">Long Meg and Her Daughters</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Pennines were controlled by the tribal federation of the <a href="/wiki/Brigantes" title="Brigantes">Brigantes</a>, made up of mainly small tribes who inhabited the area and cooperated on defence and external affairs. The Brigantes evolved an early form of kingdom. During <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman times</a>, the Brigantes were dominated by the Romans who exploited the Pennines for their natural resources including the wild animals found there.
</p><p>The Pennines were a major obstacle for <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon">Anglo-Saxon</a> expansion westwards, although it appears the Anglo-Saxons travelled through the valleys. During the <a href="/wiki/Dark_Ages_(historiography)" title="Dark Ages (historiography)">Dark Ages</a> the Pennines were controlled by Celtic and Anglo-Saxon kingdoms. It is believed that the north Pennines were under the control of the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Rheged" title="Rheged">Rheged</a>.
</p><p>During Norse times the Pennines were settled by <a href="/wiki/History_of_Denmark" title="History of Denmark">Viking Danes</a> in the east and <a href="/wiki/History_of_Norway" title="History of Norway">Norwegian Vikings</a> in the west. The Vikings influenced place names, culture and genetics. When England was unified the Pennines were incorporated into it.
Their mixture of Celtic, Anglo-Saxon and Viking heritage resembled much of the rest of <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">northern England</a> and its culture developed alongside its lowland neighbours in northwest and northeast England. The Pennines were not a distinct political <a href="/wiki/Polity" title="Polity">polity</a>, but were divided between neighbouring counties in northeast and northwest England; a major part was in the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demography">Demography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pennines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Demography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Pennine region is sparsely populated by English standards.  Larger population centres are in the foothills and lowlands fringing the southern Pennine range, such as <a href="/wiki/Barnsley" title="Barnsley">Barnsley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chesterfield" title="Chesterfield">Chesterfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Halifax,_West_Yorkshire" title="Halifax, West Yorkshire">Halifax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Macclesfield" title="Macclesfield">Macclesfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oldham" title="Oldham">Oldham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rochdale" title="Rochdale">Rochdale</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Stockport" title="Stockport">Stockport</a> but most of the northern Pennine range is thinly populated.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The cities of <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stoke-on-Trent" title="Stoke-on-Trent">Stoke-on-Trent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a> also fringe the range.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pennines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main economic activities in the Pennines include <a href="/wiki/Sheep_farm" class="mw-redirect" title="Sheep farm">sheep farming</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quarry" title="Quarry">quarrying</a>, finance and <a href="/wiki/Tourism" title="Tourism">tourism</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Peak_District" title="Peak District">Peak District</a>, tourism is the major local employment for park residents (24%), with manufacturing industries (19%) and <a href="/wiki/Peak_District#Quarrying" title="Peak District">quarrying</a> (12%) also being important while 12% are employed in agriculture.<sup id="cite_ref-Place_called_home_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Place_called_home-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Limestone is the most important mineral quarried, mainly for roads and cement, while other extracted materials include shale for cement and <a href="/wiki/Gritstone" title="Gritstone">gritstone</a> for building stone.<sup id="cite_ref-Mineral_factsheet_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mineral_factsheet-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> The springs at Buxton and Ashbourne are exploited to produce bottled <a href="/wiki/Mineral_water" title="Mineral water">mineral water</a> and there are approximately 2,700 farms in the National Park.<sup id="cite_ref-Waugh_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Waugh-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/South_Pennines" title="South Pennines">South Pennines</a> are predominantly industrial, with the main industries including textiles, quarrying and mining,<sup id="cite_ref-South_Pennines_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-South_Pennines-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> while other economic activities within the South Pennines include tourism and farming.<sup id="cite_ref-£1.2m_fund_available_to_South_Pennines_farm_businesses_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-£1.2m_fund_available_to_South_Pennines_farm_businesses-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Bowland" title="Forest of Bowland">Forest of Bowland</a> is mostly rural, the main economic activities in the area include farming<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> and tourism.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Dales" title="Yorkshire Dales">Yorkshire Dales</a>, tourism accounts for £350 million of expenditure every year while employment is mostly dominated by farming, accommodation and food sectors. There are also significant challenges for managing tourism, farming and other developments within the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Dales_National_Park" title="Yorkshire Dales National Park">National Park</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Yorkshire_Dales_-_Economy_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yorkshire_Dales_-_Economy-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> The main economic activities in the <a href="/wiki/North_Pennines" title="North Pennines">North Pennines</a> include tourism, farming, timber and small-scale quarrying, due to the rural landscape.<sup id="cite_ref-North_Pennines_-_Economy_and_business_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-North_Pennines_-_Economy_and_business-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pennines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gaps_through_Pennine_Mountains_UK_topographic_map.gif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Gaps_through_Pennine_Mountains_UK_topographic_map.gif/220px-Gaps_through_Pennine_Mountains_UK_topographic_map.gif" decoding="async" width="220" height="258" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Gaps_through_Pennine_Mountains_UK_topographic_map.gif/330px-Gaps_through_Pennine_Mountains_UK_topographic_map.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/36/Gaps_through_Pennine_Mountains_UK_topographic_map.gif 2x" data-file-width="440" data-file-height="515" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gaps_through_Pennine_Mountains_UK_topographic_map.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Pennines are traversed by several passes, mostly aligned with major rivers.</div></div></div>
<p>Gaps that allow west–east communication across the Pennines include the Tyne Gap between the Pennines and the Cheviots, through which the <a href="/wiki/A69_road" title="A69 road">A69 road</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tyne_Valley_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Tyne Valley Line">Tyne Valley railway</a> link <a href="/wiki/Carlisle,_Cumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Carlisle, Cumbria">Carlisle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a>. The <a href="/wiki/A66_road" title="A66 road">A66 road</a>, its summit at 1,450 feet (440&#160;m), follows the course of a <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman</a> road from <a href="/wiki/Scotch_Corner" title="Scotch Corner">Scotch Corner</a> to <a href="/wiki/Penrith,_Cumbria" title="Penrith, Cumbria">Penrith</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Stainmore" title="Stainmore">Stainmore</a> Gap between the <a href="/wiki/River_Eden,_Cumbria" title="River Eden, Cumbria">Eden Valley</a> in Cumbria and <a href="/wiki/Teesdale" title="Teesdale">Teesdale</a> in County Durham. The <a href="/wiki/Aire_Gap" title="Aire Gap">Aire Gap</a> links Lancashire and Yorkshire via the valleys of the <a href="/wiki/River_Aire" title="River Aire">Aire</a> and <a href="/wiki/River_Ribble" title="River Ribble">Ribble</a>. Other high-level roads include <a href="/wiki/Buttertubs_Pass" title="Buttertubs Pass">Buttertubs Pass</a>, named from <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">limestone</a> <a href="/wiki/Pothole" title="Pothole">potholes</a> near its 1,729-foot (527&#160;m) summit, between <a href="/wiki/Hawes" title="Hawes">Hawes</a> in <a href="/wiki/Wensleydale" title="Wensleydale">Wensleydale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Swaledale" title="Swaledale">Swaledale</a> and the <a href="/wiki/A684_road" title="A684 road">A684 road</a> from <a href="/wiki/Sedbergh" title="Sedbergh">Sedbergh</a> to Hawes via <a href="/wiki/Garsdale_Head" title="Garsdale Head">Garsdale Head</a> which reaches 1,100 feet (340&#160;m).<sup id="cite_ref-TPCrossings_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TPCrossings-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Further south the <a href="/wiki/A58_road" title="A58 road">A58 road</a> traverses the <a href="/wiki/Calder_Valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Calder Valley">Calder Valley</a> between <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester" title="Greater Manchester">Greater Manchester</a> reaching 1,282 feet (391&#160;m) between Littleborough and <a href="/wiki/Ripponden" title="Ripponden">Ripponden</a>, while the <a href="/wiki/A646_road" class="mw-redirect" title="A646 road">A646 road</a> along the Calder Valley between <a href="/wiki/Burnley" title="Burnley">Burnley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Halifax,_West_Yorkshire" title="Halifax, West Yorkshire">Halifax</a> reaches 764 feet (233&#160;m) following valley floors. In the Peak District the <a href="/wiki/A628_road" title="A628 road">A628</a> Woodhead road links the <a href="/wiki/M67_motorway" title="M67 motorway">M67 motorway</a> in Greater Manchester with the <a href="/wiki/M1_motorway" title="M1 motorway">M1 motorway</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_Yorkshire" title="South Yorkshire">South Yorkshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holme_Moss" title="Holme Moss">Holme Moss</a> is crossed by the <a href="/wiki/A6024_road" class="mw-redirect" title="A6024 road">A6024 road</a>, whose highest point is near <a href="/wiki/Holme_Moss_transmitting_station" title="Holme Moss transmitting station">Holme Moss transmitting station</a> between <a href="/wiki/Longdendale" title="Longdendale">Longdendale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holmfirth" title="Holmfirth">Holmfirth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TPCrossings_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TPCrossings-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Pennines are traversed by the <a href="/wiki/M62_motorway" title="M62 motorway">M62 motorway</a>, the highest motorway in England at 1,221 feet (372&#160;m) on <a href="/wiki/Windy_Hill_(Pennines)" title="Windy Hill (Pennines)">Windy Hill</a> near Junction 23.<sup id="cite_ref-TPCrossings_47-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TPCrossings-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Three trans-Pennine canals built during the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> cross the range:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_Narrow_Canal" title="Huddersfield Narrow Canal">Huddersfield Narrow Canal</a> connects <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a> in the east with <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> in the west. When it reaches <a href="/wiki/Marsden,_West_Yorkshire" title="Marsden, West Yorkshire">Marsden</a>, it passes underneath the hills through the <a href="/wiki/Standedge_Tunnels" title="Standedge Tunnels">Standedge Tunnel</a> to <a href="/wiki/Diggle,_Greater_Manchester" title="Diggle, Greater Manchester">Diggle</a>. Fortnightly during the summer season, one can pass through the tunnel on a public narrowboat.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Rochdale_Canal" title="Rochdale Canal">Rochdale Canal</a> crosses the Pennines via <a href="/wiki/Rochdale" title="Rochdale">Rochdale</a>, connecting the market town of <a href="/wiki/Sowerby_Bridge" title="Sowerby Bridge">Sowerby Bridge</a> with Manchester.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Leeds_and_Liverpool_Canal" title="Leeds and Liverpool Canal">Leeds and Liverpool Canal</a>, the longest and most northerly of the three, crosses the Pennines via <a href="/wiki/Skipton" title="Skipton">Skipton</a>, Burnley, <a href="/wiki/Chorley" title="Chorley">Chorley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wigan" title="Wigan">Wigan</a> connecting <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> in the east with <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> in the west.</li></ul>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Class_76_locomotives_76033_and_76031_at_Woodhead_on_24th_March_1981.jpeg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Class_76_locomotives_76033_and_76031_at_Woodhead_on_24th_March_1981.jpeg/220px-Class_76_locomotives_76033_and_76031_at_Woodhead_on_24th_March_1981.jpeg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Class_76_locomotives_76033_and_76031_at_Woodhead_on_24th_March_1981.jpeg/330px-Class_76_locomotives_76033_and_76031_at_Woodhead_on_24th_March_1981.jpeg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Class_76_locomotives_76033_and_76031_at_Woodhead_on_24th_March_1981.jpeg/440px-Class_76_locomotives_76033_and_76031_at_Woodhead_on_24th_March_1981.jpeg 2x" data-file-width="1444" data-file-height="960" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Class_76_locomotives_76033_and_76031_at_Woodhead_on_24th_March_1981.jpeg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A train in <a href="/wiki/British_Rail" title="British Rail">British Rail</a> blue about to enter the western portal of Woodhead 3, shortly before closure in 1981</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Woodhead_tunnels_western_portals_2048400.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Woodhead_tunnels_western_portals_2048400.jpg/220px-Woodhead_tunnels_western_portals_2048400.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Woodhead_tunnels_western_portals_2048400.jpg/330px-Woodhead_tunnels_western_portals_2048400.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Woodhead_tunnels_western_portals_2048400.jpg/440px-Woodhead_tunnels_western_portals_2048400.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="683" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Woodhead_tunnels_western_portals_2048400.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Photograph from 1953, showing the western portals of Woodhead 1&amp;2 in the background, with Woodhead 3 under construction in the foreground</div></div></div>
<p>The first of the earlier twin tunnels (<a href="/wiki/Woodhead_Tunnel" title="Woodhead Tunnel">Woodhead 1 and 2</a>) was completed by the <a href="/wiki/Sheffield,_Ashton-Under-Lyne_and_Manchester_Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Sheffield, Ashton-Under-Lyne and Manchester Railway">Sheffield, Ashton-Under-Lyne and Manchester Railway</a> in 1845, engineered by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Vignoles" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles Vignoles">Charles Vignoles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Locke" title="Joseph Locke">Joseph Locke</a>. At the time of its completion in 1845, Woodhead 1 was one of the world's longest railway tunnels at a length of 3&#160;miles 13&#160;yards (4,840&#160;m); it was the first of several trans-Pennine tunnels including the <a href="/wiki/Standedge_Tunnels" title="Standedge Tunnels">Standedge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Totley_Tunnel" title="Totley Tunnel">Totley</a> tunnels, which are only slightly longer.
</p><p>The first two tunnels were replaced by Woodhead 3, which was longer than the other two at 3 miles 66 yards (4860m). It was bored purposely for the overhead electrification of the route and completed in 1953. The tunnel was opened by the then transport minister <a href="/wiki/Alan_Lennox-Boyd,_1st_Viscount_Boyd_of_Merton" title="Alan Lennox-Boyd, 1st Viscount Boyd of Merton">Alan Lennox-Boyd</a> on 3 June 1954.<sup id="cite_ref-RB_W_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RB_W-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> It was designed by Sir <a href="/wiki/William_Halcrow" title="William Halcrow">William Halcrow</a> &amp; Partners.  The line was closed in 1981.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/London_and_North_Western_Railway" title="London and North Western Railway">London and North Western Railway</a> acquired the Huddersfield and Manchester Railway in 1847 and built a single-line tunnel parallel to the canal tunnel at Standedge with a length of 3 miles, 57 yards (4803 m). Today rail services along the <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Huddersfield Line">Huddersfield line</a> between <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_railway_station" title="Huddersfield railway station">Huddersfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Victoria_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchester Victoria railway station">Victoria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Piccadilly_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchester Piccadilly railway station">Piccadilly</a> stations in <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> are operated by <a href="/wiki/TransPennine_Express" title="TransPennine Express">TransPennine Express</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northern_(train_operating_company)" title="Northern (train operating company)">Northern</a>. Between 1869 and 1876 the <a href="/wiki/Midland_Railway" title="Midland Railway">Midland Railway</a> built the <a href="/wiki/Settle-Carlisle_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Settle-Carlisle Line">Settle-Carlisle Line</a> through remote, scenic regions of the Pennines from near <a href="/wiki/Settle,_North_Yorkshire" title="Settle, North Yorkshire">Settle</a> to <a href="/wiki/Carlisle,_Cumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Carlisle, Cumbria">Carlisle</a> passing <a href="/wiki/Appleby-in-Westmorland" title="Appleby-in-Westmorland">Appleby-in-Westmorland</a> and a number of other settlements, some a distance from their stations. The line has survived, despite difficult times<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
and is operated by Northern Rail.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Trans_Pennine_Trail" title="Trans Pennine Trail">Trans Pennine Trail</a>, a long-distance route for cyclists, horse riders and walkers, runs west–east alongside rivers and canals, along disused railway tracks and through historic towns and cities from <a href="/wiki/Southport" title="Southport">Southport</a> to <a href="/wiki/Hornsea" title="Hornsea">Hornsea</a> (207 miles/333&#160;km).<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> It crosses the north–south <a href="/wiki/Pennine_Way" title="Pennine Way">Pennine Way</a> (268 miles/431&#160;km) at <a href="/wiki/Crowden-in-Longdendale" class="mw-redirect" title="Crowden-in-Longdendale">Crowden-in-Longdendale</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="National_Parks_and_AONBs">National Parks and AONBs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pennines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: National Parks and AONBs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:192px;"><a href="/wiki/File:National_Parks_in_England_and_Wales.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/National_Parks_in_England_and_Wales.png/190px-National_Parks_in_England_and_Wales.png" decoding="async" width="190" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/National_Parks_in_England_and_Wales.png/285px-National_Parks_in_England_and_Wales.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e5/National_Parks_in_England_and_Wales.png 2x" data-file-width="377" data-file-height="447" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:National_Parks_in_England_and_Wales.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The National Parks of England and Wales; two include areas of the Pennines, those marked as 7 and 1</div></div></div>
<p>Considerable areas of Pennine landscape are protected as <a href="/wiki/National_parks_of_England_and_Wales" title="National parks of England and Wales">UK national parks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Areas_of_Outstanding_Natural_Beauty" class="mw-redirect" title="Areas of Outstanding Natural Beauty">Areas of Outstanding Natural Beauty</a> (AONBs). Areas of Outstanding Natural Beauty are afforded much the same protection as national parks. The two national parks within the Pennines are the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Dales" title="Yorkshire Dales">Yorkshire Dales National Park</a> (7) and the <a href="/wiki/Peak_District" title="Peak District">Peak District National Park</a> (1).
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:AONBSUK.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/AONBSUK.png/220px-AONBSUK.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/AONBSUK.png/330px-AONBSUK.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/AONBSUK.png/440px-AONBSUK.png 2x" data-file-width="886" data-file-height="915" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:AONBSUK.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>England, Wales and Northern Ireland AONBs. The Pennines host three, with a large one protecting the North Pennines.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/North_Pennines" title="North Pennines">North Pennines</a> AONB just north of the Yorkshire Dales rivals the national park in size and includes some of the Pennines' highest peaks and some of its most isolated and sparsely populated areas while  <a href="/wiki/Nidderdale" title="Nidderdale">Nidderdale</a> is an AONB east of the Yorkshire Dales National Park, and the <a href="/wiki/Forest_of_Bowland" title="Forest of Bowland">Bowland Fells</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Pendle_Hill" title="Pendle Hill">Pendle Hill</a>, is an AONB west of the Yorkshire Dales.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pennines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The language used in pre-Roman and Roman times was <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">Common Brittonic</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">Early Middle Ages</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cumbric_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Cumbric language">Cumbric language</a> developed. Little evidence of Cumbric remains, so it is difficult to ascertain whether or not it was distinct from <a href="/wiki/Old_Welsh_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Welsh language">Old Welsh</a>. The extent of the region in which Cumbric was spoken is also unknown.
</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxon</a> times the area was settled by Anglian peoples of <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">Mercia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Northumbria">Northumbria</a>, rather than the <a href="/wiki/Saxon_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Saxon people">Saxon people</a> of <a href="/wiki/Southern_England" title="Southern England">Southern England</a>. <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic speech</a> remained in most areas of the Pennines longer than it did in the surrounding areas of England. Eventually, the Celtic tongue of the Pennines was replaced by early <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> as Anglo-Saxons and Vikings settled the area and assimilated the Celts.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Norse</a> times, <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> settlers brought their languages of Old Norse, <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Old Danish</a> (mainly in the Yorkshire Dales and parts of the Peak District) and <a href="/wiki/Old_Norwegian" title="Old Norwegian">Old Norwegian</a> (mainly in the western Pennines).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> With the eventual consolidation of England by the Saxon <a href="/wiki/Wessex" title="Wessex">kingdom of Wessex</a>, the pure Norse speech died out in England, though it survived in the Pennines longer than in most areas<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. However, the fusion of Norse and Old English was important in the formation of <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">Middle English</a> and hence Modern English, and many individual <a href="/wiki/List_of_English_words_of_Old_Norse_origin" title="List of English words of Old Norse origin">words of Norse descent</a> remain in use in local dialects, such as <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_accent_and_dialect" class="mw-redirect" title="Yorkshire accent and dialect">that of Yorkshire</a>, and in local place names.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Folklore_and_customs">Folklore and customs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pennines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Folklore and customs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The folklore and customs are mostly based on <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">Celtic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon">Anglo-Saxon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> customs and folklore.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Many customs and stories have their origin in Christianised pagan traditions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In the Peak District, a notable custom is <a href="/wiki/Well_dressing" title="Well dressing">well dressing</a>, which has its origin in pagan traditions that became Christianised.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Flora">Flora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pennines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Flora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Flora in the Pennines is adapted to <a href="/wiki/Moorland" title="Moorland">moorland</a> and subarctic landscapes and climates. The flora found there can be found in other areas of moorland in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern Europe</a> and some species are also found in areas of <a href="/wiki/Tundra" title="Tundra">tundra</a>.
</p><p>In the Pennine millstone grit areas above an altitude of 900 feet (270&#160;m) the topsoil is so acidic, <a href="/wiki/PH" title="PH">pH</a> 2 to 4, that it can grow only <a href="/wiki/Bracken" title="Bracken">bracken</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ericaceae" title="Ericaceae">heather</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Sphagnum" title="Sphagnum">sphagnum</a>, and coarse grasses<sup id="cite_ref-DB_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DB-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> such as <a href="/wiki/Cottongrass" class="mw-redirect" title="Cottongrass">cottongrass</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Molinia_caerulea" title="Molinia caerulea">purple moor grass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Juncus" title="Juncus">heath rush</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kelsall_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kelsall-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As the <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">Ice age</a> <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">glacial sheets</a> retreated c.&#160;11,500 BC trees returned and archaeological <a href="/wiki/Palynology" title="Palynology">palynology</a> can identify their species.
The first trees to settle were willow, birch and juniper, followed later by alder and pine. By 6500 BC temperatures were warmer and woodlands covered 90% of the dales with mostly pine, elm, lime and oak. On the limestone soils the oak was slower to colonize and pine and birch predominated.
Around 3000 BC a noticeable decline in tree pollen indicates that neolithic farmers were clearing woodland to increase grazing for domestic livestock, and studies at <a href="/wiki/Linton,_North_Yorkshire" title="Linton, North Yorkshire">Linton Mires</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eshton" title="Eshton">Eshton Tarn</a> find an increase in grassland species.<sup id="cite_ref-landcape_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-landcape-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On poorly drained impermeable areas of millstone grit, shale or clays the topsoil gets waterlogged in winter and spring. Here tree suppression combined with the heavier rainfall results in <a href="/wiki/Blanket_bog" title="Blanket bog">blanket bog</a> up to 7&#160;ft (2&#160;m) thick. The erosion of peat still exposes stumps of ancient trees.<sup id="cite_ref-landcape_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-landcape-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>In digging it away they frequently find vast fir trees, perfectly sound, and some oaks&#160;...</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite><a href="/wiki/Arthur_Young_(writer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Arthur Young (writer)">Arthur Young</a>, <i>A Six Months' Tour of the North of England</i> (1771)<sup id="cite_ref-young_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-young-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fauna">Fauna</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pennines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Fauna">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Two_grouse_%22picked%22_after_the_previous_day%27s_shoot._-_geograph.org.uk_-_547403.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Two_grouse_%22picked%22_after_the_previous_day%27s_shoot._-_geograph.org.uk_-_547403.jpg/220px-Two_grouse_%22picked%22_after_the_previous_day%27s_shoot._-_geograph.org.uk_-_547403.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Two_grouse_%22picked%22_after_the_previous_day%27s_shoot._-_geograph.org.uk_-_547403.jpg/330px-Two_grouse_%22picked%22_after_the_previous_day%27s_shoot._-_geograph.org.uk_-_547403.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Two_grouse_%22picked%22_after_the_previous_day%27s_shoot._-_geograph.org.uk_-_547403.jpg/440px-Two_grouse_%22picked%22_after_the_previous_day%27s_shoot._-_geograph.org.uk_-_547403.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="426" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Two_grouse_%22picked%22_after_the_previous_day%27s_shoot._-_geograph.org.uk_-_547403.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Shooting of <a href="/wiki/Red_grouse" title="Red grouse">red grouse</a> is an economically important activity in the Pennines.</div></div></div>
<p>Fauna in the Pennines is similar to the rest of <a href="/wiki/Fauna_of_England" title="Fauna of England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fauna_of_Wales" class="mw-redirect" title="Fauna of Wales">Wales</a>, but the area hosts some specialised species. Deer are found throughout the Pennines and some species of animals that are rare elsewhere in England can be found here. <a href="/wiki/Arctic_hare" title="Arctic hare">Arctic hares</a>, which were common in Britain during the Ice Age and retreated to the cooler, more tundra-like uplands once the climate warmed up, were introduced to the <a href="/wiki/Dark_Peak" title="Dark Peak">Dark Peak</a> area of the <a href="/wiki/Peak_District" title="Peak District">Peak District</a> in the 19th century.
</p><p>Large areas of heather moorland in the Pennines are managed for <a href="/wiki/Driven_grouse_shooting" title="Driven grouse shooting">driven shooting</a> of wild <a href="/wiki/Red_grouse" title="Red grouse">red grouse</a>. The related and declining <a href="/wiki/Black_grouse" title="Black grouse">black grouse</a> is still found in northern parts of the Pennines. Other birds whose English breeding strongholds are in the Pennines include <a href="/wiki/European_golden_plover" title="European golden plover">golden plover</a>, <a href="/wiki/Common_snipe" title="Common snipe">snipe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eurasian_curlew" title="Eurasian curlew">curlew</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dunlin" title="Dunlin">dunlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merlin_(bird)" title="Merlin (bird)">merlin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Short-eared_owl" title="Short-eared owl">short-eared owl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ring_ouzel" title="Ring ouzel">ring ouzel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Twite" title="Twite">twite</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-BTO93Atlas_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BTO93Atlas-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> though many of these are at the southern limit of their distributions and are more common in Scotland.
</p>
