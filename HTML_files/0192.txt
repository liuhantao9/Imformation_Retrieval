<p><b>Pleonasm</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;p&#39; in &#39;pie&#39;">p</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/iː/: &#39;ee&#39; in &#39;fleece&#39;">iː</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/æ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;bad&#39;">æ</span><span title="&#39;z&#39; in &#39;zoom&#39;">z</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span></span>/</a></span></span>; from&#32;<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>&#32;<i> </i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%80%CE%BB%CE%B5%CE%BF%CE%BD%CE%B1%CF%83%CE%BC%CF%8C%CF%82" class="extiw" title="wikt:πλεονασμός">πλεονασμός</a><i>, <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek language text">pleonasmós</i></i>, from&#32;<i> </i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%80%CE%BB%CE%AD%CE%BF%CE%BD" class="extiw" title="wikt:πλέον">πλέον</a><i>, <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek language text">pleon</i></i>, meaning 'more; too much') is the use of more <a href="/wiki/Word" title="Word">words</a> or <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">parts of words</a> than are necessary or sufficient for clear expression: for example <i>black darkness</i> or <i>burning fire</i>. Such <a href="/wiki/Redundancy_(linguistics)" title="Redundancy (linguistics)">redundancy</a> is, by traditional <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetorical</a> criteria, a manifestation of <a href="/wiki/Tautology_(language)" title="Tautology (language)">tautology</a>, and might be considered a fault of style.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Pleonasm may also be used for emphasis or because the phrase has become established in a certain form. Tautology and pleonasm are not consistently differentiated in the literature.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pleonasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most often, <i>pleonasm</i> is understood to mean a word or phrase which is useless, <a href="/wiki/Clich%C3%A9" title="Cliché">clichéd</a>, or repetitive, but a pleonasm can also be simply an unremarkable use of <a href="/wiki/Idiom_(language_structure)" title="Idiom (language structure)">idiom</a>. It can aid in achieving a specific linguistic effect, be it social, poetic, or literary. In particular, pleonasm sometimes serves the same function as rhetorical repetition—it can be used to reinforce an idea, contention, or question, rendering writing clearer and easier to understand. Further, pleonasm can serve as a <a href="/wiki/Redundancy_check" class="mw-redirect" title="Redundancy check">redundancy check</a>: If a word is unknown, misunderstood, or misheard, or the medium of communication is poor—a wireless telephone connection or sloppy handwriting—pleonastic phrases can help ensure that the entire meaning gets across even if some of the words get lost.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Idiomatic_expressions">Idiomatic expressions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pleonasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Idiomatic expressions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some pleonastic phrases are part of a language's <a href="/wiki/Idiom_(language_structure)" title="Idiom (language structure)">idiom</a>, like "tuna fish" and "safe haven" in <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>. They are so common that their use is unremarkable and often even unnoticeable for native speakers, although in many cases the redundancy can be dropped with no loss of meaning.
</p><p>When expressing possibility, English speakers often use potentially pleonastic expressions such as <i>It may be possible</i> or <i>maybe it's possible</i>, where both terms (verb <i>may</i>&#160;or adverb <i>maybe</i> along with the adjective <i>possible</i>) have the same meaning under certain constructions. Many speakers of English use such expressions for possibility in general, such that most instances of such expressions by those speakers are in fact pleonastic. Others, however, use this expression only to indicate a distinction between <a href="/wiki/Ontological" class="mw-redirect" title="Ontological">ontological</a> possibility and <a href="/wiki/Epistemic" class="mw-redirect" title="Epistemic">epistemic</a> possibility, as in "Both the ontological possibility of X under current conditions and the ontological impossibility of X under current conditions are epistemically possible" (in <a href="/wiki/Propositional_logic" class="mw-redirect" title="Propositional logic">logical</a> terms, "I am not aware of any facts inconsistent with the truth of proposition X, but I am likewise not aware of any facts inconsistent with the truth of the negation of X"). The habitual use of the double construction to indicate possibility <i>per se</i> is far less widespread among speakers of most other languages (except in Spanish; see examples); rather, almost all speakers of those languages use one term in a single expression:
</p>
<ul><li>French: <i>Il est possible</i> or <i>il peut arriver</i>.</li>
<li>Romanian: <i>Este posibil</i> or <i>se poate întâmpla</i>.</li>
<li>Typical Spanish pleonasms
<ul><li><i>Voy a subir arriba</i> – I am going to go up upstairs, "<i>arriba</i>" not being necessary.</li>
<li><i>Entra para adentro</i> – Go in inside, "<i>para adentro</i>" not being necessary.</li></ul></li>
<li>Turkish has many pleonastic constructs because certain verbs necessitate objects:
<ul><li><i>yemek yemek</i> – to eat food.</li>
<li><i>yazı yazmak</i> – to write writing.</li>
<li><i>dışarı çıkmak</i> – to go out outside.</li>
<li><i>içeri girmek</i> – to go in inside.</li>
<li><i>oyun oynamak</i> – to play game.</li></ul></li></ul>
<p>In a <a href="/wiki/Verb_framing" title="Verb framing">satellite-framed</a> language like English, <a href="/wiki/Verb_phrase" title="Verb phrase">verb phrases</a> containing <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_particle" title="Grammatical particle">particles</a> that denote direction of motion are so frequent that even when such a particle is pleonastic, it seems natural to include it (e.g. "enter into").
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_and_scholarly_use">Professional and scholarly use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pleonasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Professional and scholarly use">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some pleonastic phrases, when used in professional or scholarly writing, may reflect a standardized usage that has evolved or a meaning familiar to specialists but not necessarily to those outside that discipline. Such examples as "null and void", "terms and conditions", "each and every" are <a href="/wiki/Legal_doublet" title="Legal doublet">legal doublets</a> that are part of <a href="/wiki/Legal_English" title="Legal English">legally operative language</a> that is often drafted into legal documents. A classic example of such usage was that by the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor" title="Lord Chancellor">Lord Chancellor</a> at the time (1864), <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bethell,_1st_Baron_Westbury" title="Richard Bethell, 1st Baron Westbury">Lord Westbury</a>, in the English case of <i><i lang="la" title="Latin language text">ex parte</i> Gorely</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> when he described a phrase in an Act as "redundant and pleonastic". Although this type of usage may be favored in certain contexts, it may also be disfavored when used gratuitously to portray false erudition, obfuscate, or otherwise introduce verbiage. This is especially so in disciplines where imprecision may introduce ambiguities (such as the natural sciences).<sup id="cite_ref-Partridge_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Partridge-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stylistic_preference">Stylistic preference</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pleonasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Stylistic preference">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition, pleonasms can serve purposes external to meaning. For example, a speaker who is too terse is often interpreted as lacking ease or grace, because, in oral and sign language, sentences are spontaneously created without the benefit of editing. The restriction on the ability to plan often creates much redundancy. In written language, removing words not strictly necessary sometimes makes writing seem stilted or awkward, especially if the words are cut from an idiomatic expression.
</p><p>On the other hand, as is the case with any literary or rhetorical effect, excessive use of pleonasm weakens writing and speech; words distract from the content. Writers wanting to conceal a thought or a purpose obscure their meaning with verbiage. <a href="/wiki/William_Strunk_Jr" class="mw-redirect" title="William Strunk Jr">William Strunk Jr</a>. advocated concision in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Elements_of_Style" title="The Elements of Style">The Elements of Style</a></i> (1918):
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts. This requires not that the writer make all his sentences short, or that he avoid all detail and treat his subjects only in outline, but that every word tell.
</p></blockquote>
<p>Yet, one has only to look at <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">Baroque</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mannerist" class="mw-redirect" title="Mannerist">Mannerist</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Victorian_literature" title="Victorian literature">Victorian</a> sources for different opinions.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_uses">Literary uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pleonasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Literary uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>"This was the most unkindest cut of all." —<a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar_(play)" title="Julius Caesar (play)">Julius Caesar</a></i> (Act 3, Scene 2,183).</li>

<p>There are various kinds of pleonasm, including <b>bilingual tautological expressions</b>, <b>syntactic pleonasm</b>, <b>semantic pleonasm</b> and <b>morphological pleonasm</b>:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bilingual_tautological_expressions">Bilingual tautological expressions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pleonasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Bilingual tautological expressions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A bilingual tautological expression is a phrase that consists of two words that mean the same thing in two different languages.<sup id="cite_ref-language_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-language-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>138</span></sup> An example of a bilingual tautological expression is the <a href="/wiki/Yiddish" title="Yiddish">Yiddish</a> expression מים אחרונים וואַסער <i>mayim akhroynem vaser</i>. It literally means "water last water" and refers "water for washing the hands after meal, grace water’.<sup id="cite_ref-language_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-language-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>138</span></sup> Its first element, <i>mayim</i>, derives from the <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">Hebrew</a> מים ['majim] "water". Its second element, <i>vaser</i>, derives from the <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> <i>Wasser</i> "water".
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Ghil%27ad_Zuckermann" title="Ghil&#39;ad Zuckermann">Ghil'ad Zuckermann</a>, Yiddish abounds with both bilingual tautological compounds and bilingual tautological first names.<sup id="cite_ref-language_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-language-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>138</span></sup>
</p><p>The following are examples of bilingual tautological compounds in Yiddish:
</p>
<ul><li>פֿינצטער חושך <i>fíntster khóyshekh</i> "very dark", literally "dark darkness", traceable back to the German word <i>finster</i> "dark" and the Hebrew word חושך <i>ħōshekh</i> "darkness".<sup id="cite_ref-language_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-language-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>138</span></sup></li>
<li>חמור-אייזל <i>khameréyzļ</i> "womanizer", literally "donkey-donkey", traceable back to the Hebrew word חמור [ħă'mōr] "donkey" and the German word <i>Esel</i> "donkey".<sup id="cite_ref-language_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-language-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>138</span></sup></li></ul>
<p>The following are examples of bilingual tautological first names (<a href="/wiki/Anthroponyms" class="mw-redirect" title="Anthroponyms">anthroponyms</a>) in Yiddish:
</p>
<ul><li>דוב-בער <i>Dov-Ber</i>, literally "bear-bear", traceable back to the Hebrew word דב <i>dov</i> "bear" and the German word <i>Bär</i> "bear".<sup id="cite_ref-language_5-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-language-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>138</span></sup></li>
<li>צבי-הירש <i>Tsvi-Hirsh</i>, literally "deer-deer", traceable back to the Hebrew word צבי <i>tsvi</i> "deer" and the German word <i>Hirsch</i> "deer".<sup id="cite_ref-language_5-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-language-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>138</span></sup></li>
<li>זאב-וואָלף <i>Ze'ev-Volf</i>, literally "wolf-wolf", traceable back to the Hebrew word זאב <i>ze'ev</i> "wolf" and the German word <i>Wolf</i> "wolf".<sup id="cite_ref-language_5-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-language-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>138</span></sup></li>
<li>אריה-לייב <i>Arye-Leyb</i>, literally "lion-lion", traceable back to the Hebrew word אריה <i>arye</i> "lion" and the German word <i>Löwe</i> "lion".<sup id="cite_ref-language_5-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-language-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>138</span></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Syntactic_pleonasm">Syntactic pleonasm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pleonasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Syntactic pleonasm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">Syntactic</a> pleonasm occurs when the <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">grammar</a> of a language makes certain <a href="/wiki/Function_word" title="Function word">function words</a> optional. For example, consider the following <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> sentences:
</p>
<ul><li>"I know you're coming."</li>
<li>"I know that you're coming."</li></ul>
<p>In this construction, the <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjunction" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammatical conjunction">conjunction</a> <i>that</i> is optional when joining a sentence to a <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">verb</a> phrase with <i>know</i>. Both sentences are grammatically correct, but the word <i>that</i> is pleonastic in this case. By contrast, when a sentence is in spoken form and the verb involved is one of assertion, the use of <i>that</i> makes clear that the present speaker is making an indirect rather than a direct quotation, such that he is not imputing particular words to the person he describes as having made an assertion; the demonstrative adjective <i>that</i> also does not fit such an example. Also, some writers may use "that" for technical clarity reasons.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In some languages, such as French, the word is not optional and should therefore not be considered pleonastic.
</p><p>The same phenomenon occurs in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> with subject pronouns. Since Spanish is a <a href="/wiki/Null-subject_language" title="Null-subject language">null-subject language</a>, which allows subject pronouns to be deleted when understood, the following sentences mean the same:
</p>
<ul><li>"<i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">Yo te amo.</i>"</li>
<li>"<i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">Te amo.</i>"</li></ul>
<p>In this case, the pronoun <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">yo</i> ('I') is grammatically optional; both sentences mean "I love you" (however, they may not have the same tone or <i>intention</i>—this depends on <a href="/wiki/Pragmatics" title="Pragmatics">pragmatics</a> rather than grammar). Such differing but <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntactically</a> equivalent constructions, in many languages, may also indicate a difference in <a href="/wiki/Register_(sociolinguistics)" title="Register (sociolinguistics)">register</a>.
</p><p>The process of deleting pronouns is called <i><a href="/wiki/Pro-drop_language" title="Pro-drop language">pro-dropping</a></i>, and it also happens in many other languages, such as <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Scandinavian languages">Scandinavian languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swahili_language" title="Swahili language">Swahili</a>, some <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic languages</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Lao_language" title="Lao language">Lao language</a>.
</p><p>In contrast, formal English requires an overt subject in each clause. A sentence may not need a subject to have valid meaning, but to satisfy the syntactic requirement for an explicit subject a pleonastic (or <a href="/wiki/Dummy_pronoun" title="Dummy pronoun">dummy pronoun</a>) is used; only the first sentence in the following pair is acceptable English:
</p>
<ul><li>"It's raining."</li>
<li>"Is raining."</li></ul>
<p>In this example the pleonastic "it" fills the subject function, however, it does not contribute any meaning to the sentence. The second sentence, which omits the pleonastic <b>it</b> is marked as ungrammatical although no meaning is lost by the omission.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Elements such as "it" or "there," serving as empty subject markers, are also called (syntactic) <a href="/wiki/Syntactic_expletive" title="Syntactic expletive">expletives</a>, and also dummy pronouns. Compare:
</p>
<ul><li>"There is rain."</li>
<li>"Today is rain."</li></ul>
<p>The pleonastic <i lang="fr" title="French language text">ne</i> (<i lang="fr" title="French language text">ne pléonastique</i>), expressing uncertainty in formal <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, works as follows:
</p>
<ul><li>"<i lang="fr" title="French language text">Je crains qu'il ne pleuve.</i>"<br />('I fear it may rain.')</li>
<li>"<i lang="fr" title="French language text">Ces idées sont plus difficiles à comprendre que je ne pensais.</i>"<br />('These ideas are harder to understand than I thought.')</li></ul>
<p>Two more striking examples of French pleonastic construction are the word <i lang="fr" title="French language text">aujourd'hui</i> translated as 'today', but originally meaning "on the day of today", and the phrase <i lang="fr" title="French language text">Qu'est-ce que c'est?</i> meaning 'What's that?' or 'What is it?', while literally it means "What is it that it is?".
</p><p>There are examples of the pleonastic, or dummy, negative in English, such as the construction, heard in the New England region of the United States, in which the phrase "So don't I" is intended to have the same positive meaning as "So do I."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When <a href="/wiki/Robert_South" title="Robert South">Robert South</a> said, "It is a pleonasm, a figure usual in <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Scripture</a>, by a multiplicity of expressions to signify one notable thing",<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> he was observing the <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Hebrew" title="Biblical Hebrew">Biblical Hebrew</a> poetic propensity to repeat thoughts in different words, since written Biblical Hebrew was a comparatively early form of written language and was written using oral patterning, which has many pleonasms. In particular, very many verses of the <a href="/wiki/Psalms" title="Psalms">Psalms</a> are split into two halves, each of which says much the same thing in different words. The complex rules and forms of written language as distinct from spoken language were not as well-developed as they are today when the books making up the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a> were written.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> See also <a href="/wiki/Parallelism_(rhetoric)" title="Parallelism (rhetoric)">parallelism (rhetoric)</a>.
</p><p>This same pleonastic style remains very common in modern poetry and songwriting (e.g., "Anne, with her father&#160;/ is out in the boat&#160;/ riding the water&#160;/ riding the waves&#160;/ on the sea", from <a href="/wiki/Peter_Gabriel" title="Peter Gabriel">Peter Gabriel</a>'s "Mercy Street").
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_syntactic_pleonasm">Types of syntactic pleonasm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pleonasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Types of syntactic pleonasm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li><i>Overinflection</i>: Many languages with <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">inflection</a>, as a result of convention, tend to inflect more words in a given phrase than actually needed in order to express a single grammatical property. Take for example the German, <i>Die alten Frauen sprechen</i>. Even though the plural form of the noun<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2019)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> tells us the grammatical number of the noun phrase, the German language still dictates that the definite article, attributive adjective, and the verb must all also express the plural. Not all languages are quite as redundant however, and will permit inflection for number when there is an obvious numerical marker, as is the case with Hungarian, which does have a plural proper, but would express <i>two flowers</i> as <i>two flower</i>. (The same is the case in <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">Celtic languages</a>, where numerical markers precede singular nouns.) The main contrast between Hungarian and other tongues such as German or even English (to a lesser extent) is that in either of the latter, expressing plurality when already evident is not optional, but mandatory; making the neglect of these rules result in an ungrammatical sentence. As well as for number, our aforementioned German phrase also overinflects for grammatical case.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Double_negative" title="Double negative">Multiple negation</a></i>: In some languages, repeated <a href="/wiki/Negation_(linguistics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Negation (linguistics)">negation</a> may be used for emphasis, as in the English sentence, "There ai<b>n't nothing</b> wrong with that". While a literal interpretation of this sentence would be "There is not nothing wrong with that," i.e. "There is something wrong with that," the intended meaning is, in fact, the opposite: "There is nothing wrong with that" or "There isn't anything wrong with that." The repeated negation is used pleonastically for emphasis. However, this is not always the case. In the sentence "I do<b>n't not</b> like it," the repeated negative may be used to convey ambivalence ("I neither like nor dislike it") or even affirmation ("I <i>do</i> like it"). (Rhetorically, this becomes the device of <a href="/wiki/Litotes" title="Litotes">litotes</a>; it can be difficult to distinguish litotes from pleonastic double negation, a feature which may be used for <a href="/wiki/Irony" title="Irony">ironic</a> effect.) Although the use of "double negatives" for emphatic purposes is sometimes discouraged in standard English, it is mandatory in other languages like Spanish or French. For example, the Spanish phrase <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">No es nada</i> ('It is nothing') contains both a negated verb ("no es") and another negative, the word for nothing ("nada").</li>
<li><i>Multiple affirmations</i>: In English, repeated affirmation can be used to add emphasis to an affirmative statement, just as repeated negation can add emphasis to a negative one. A sentence like <i>I <b>do</b> love you</i>, with a stronger intonation on the <i>do</i>, uses double affirmation. This is because all languages, by default, automatically express their sentences in the affirmative<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and must then alter the sentence in one way or another to express the opposite. Therefore, the sentence <i>I love you</i> is <i>already</i> affirmative, and adding the extra <i>do</i> only adds emphasis and does not change the meaning of the statement.</li>
<li><i>Double possession</i>: The <a href="/wiki/English_possessive#Double_genitive" title="English possessive">double genitive</a> of English, as with <i>a friend of mine</i>, although seemingly pleonastic, and therefore has been stigmatized, has a long history of use by careful writers and has been analyzed as either a <a href="/wiki/Partitive" title="Partitive">partitive</a> genitive or an <a href="/wiki/Appositive" class="mw-redirect" title="Appositive">appositive</a> genitive.</li>
<li><i>Multiple quality gradation</i>: In English, different <a href="/wiki/Degrees_of_comparison" class="mw-redirect" title="Degrees of comparison">degrees of comparison</a> (<a href="/wiki/Comparative" title="Comparative">comparatives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Superlative" class="mw-redirect" title="Superlative">superlatives</a>) are created through a morphological change to an adjective (e.g. "prettier", "fastest") or a syntactic construction (e.g. "more complex", "most impressive"). It is thus possible to combine both forms for additional emphasis: "more bigger" or "bestest". This may be considered ungrammatical, but is common in informal speech for some English speakers. "The most unkindest cut of all" is from Shakespeare's <i>Julius Caesar</i>. Musical notation has a repeated Italian superlative in <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fortississimo" class="extiw" title="wikt:fortississimo">fortississimo</a></i> and <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pianississimo" class="extiw" title="wikt:pianississimo">pianississimo</a></i>.</li></ul>
<dl><dd>Not all uses of constructions such as "more bigger" are pleonastic, however. Some speakers who use such utterances do so in an attempt, albeit a grammatically unconventional one, to create a non-pleonastic construction: A person who says "X is more bigger than Y" may, in the context of a conversation featuring a previous comparison of some object Z with Y, mean "The degree by which X exceeds Y in size is greater than the degree by which Z exceeds Y in size". This usage amounts to the treatment of "bigger than Y" as a single grammatical unit, namely an adjective itself admitting of degrees, such that "X is more bigger than Y" is equivalent to "X is more bigger-than-Y than Z is." Another common way to express this is: "X is even bigger than Z."</dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Semantic_pleonasm">Semantic pleonasm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pleonasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Semantic pleonasm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Semantic pleonasm is a question more of <a href="/wiki/Style_(sociolinguistics)" title="Style (sociolinguistics)">style</a> and <a href="/wiki/Usage_(language)" title="Usage (language)">usage</a> than of grammar.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Linguists usually call this <i>redundancy</i> to avoid confusion with syntactic pleonasm, a more important phenomenon for <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_linguistics" title="Theoretical linguistics">theoretical linguistics</a>. It usually takes one of two forms: Overlap or prolixity.
</p><p><b>Overlap</b>: One word's semantic component is subsumed by the other:
</p>
<ul><li>"Receive a <b>free gift</b> with every purchase."</li>
<li>"I ate a <b>tuna fish</b> sandwich."</li>
<li>"The plumber fixed our <b>hot</b> water <b>heater</b>." (This pleonasm was famously attacked by American comedian <a href="/wiki/George_Carlin" title="George Carlin">George Carlin</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> but is not truly redundant; a device that increases the temperature of cold water to room temperature would also be a water heater.)</li>
<li><i>The <b>Big</b> Friendly <b>Giant</b></i> (title of a children's book by <a href="/wiki/Roald_Dahl" title="Roald Dahl">Roald Dahl</a>), a giant is inherently big</li></ul>
<p><b>Prolixity</b>: A phrase may have words which add nothing, or nothing logical or relevant, to the meaning.
</p>
<ul><li>"I'm going <b>down</b> south."<br />(South is not really "down", it is just drawn that way on maps by convention.)</li>
<li>"You can't seem to face <b>up to</b> the facts."</li>
<li>"He <b>entered into</b> the room."</li>
<li>"Every <b>mother's</b> child" (as in 'The Christmas Song' by Nat King Cole', also known as 'Chestnuts roasting...').<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> (Being a child, or a human at all, generally implies being the child of/to a mother. So the redundancy here is used to broaden the context of the child's curiosity regarding the sleigh of Santa Claus, including the concept of maternity. The full line goes: "And every mother's child is gonna spy, to see if reindeer really know how to fly". One can furthermore argue that the word "mother" is included for the purpose of lyrical flow, adding two syllables, which make the line sound complete, as "every child" would be too short to fit the lyrical/rhyme scheme. )</li>
<li>"What therefore God hath joined <b>together</b>, let no man put asunder."</li>
<li>"He raised <b>up</b> his hands in a gesture of surrender."</li>
<li>"Where are you <b>at</b>?"</li>
<li>"Located" or similar before a <a href="/wiki/Preposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Preposition">preposition</a>: "the store is <b>located</b> on Main St." The preposition contains the idea of locatedness and does not need a servant.</li>
<li>"The house <b>itself</b>" for "the house", and similar: unnecessary re-specifiers.</li>
<li>"<b>Actual</b> fact": fact.</li>
<li>"On a daily basis": daily.</li>
<li>"This <b>particular</b> item": this item.</li>
<li>"Different" or "separate" after numbers: for example:
<ul><li>"Four <b>different</b> <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a>" are merely "four species", as two non-different species are together one same species.</li>
<li>"Nine <b>separate</b> cars": cars are always separate. (However, in "a discount if you buy ten different items", "different" has meaning, because if the ten items include two packets of frozen peas of the same weight and brand, those ten items are not all different.)</li></ul></li>
<li>"Despite the fact that": although</li></ul>
<p>An expression like "tuna fish", however, might elicit one of many possible responses, such as:
</p>
<ol><li>It will simply be accepted as <b>synonymous</b> with "tuna".</li>
<li>It will be perceived as <b>redundant</b> (and thus perhaps silly, illogical, ignorant, inefficient, dialectal, odd, and/or intentionally humorous).</li>
<li>It will imply a <b>distinction</b>. A reader of "tuna fish" could properly wonder: "Is there a kind of tuna which is not a fish? There is, after all, a dolphin mammal and a <a href="/wiki/Mahi-mahi" title="Mahi-mahi">dolphin fish</a>." This assumption turns out to be correct, as a "tuna" can also mean a <a href="/wiki/Opuntia" title="Opuntia">prickly pear</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MW_Tuna_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MW_Tuna-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Further, "tuna fish" is sometimes used to refer to the flesh of the animal as opposed to the animal itself (similar to the distinction between <i>beef</i> and <i>cattle</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-MW_Tuna_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MW_Tuna-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>It will be perceived as a verbal clarification, since the word "tuna" is quite short, and may, for example, be misheard as "tune" followed by an <a href="/wiki/Aspiration_(phonetics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Aspiration (phonetics)">aspiration</a>, or (in dialects that <a href="/wiki/Arhotic" class="mw-redirect" title="Arhotic">drop the final <i>-r</i> sound</a>) as "tuner".</li></ol>
<p>Careful speakers, and writers, too, are aware of pleonasms, especially with cases such as "tuna fish", which is normally used only in some dialects of <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a>, and would sound strange in other variants of the language, and even odder in translation into other languages.
</p><p>Similar situations are:
</p>
<ul><li>"Ink pen" instead of merely "pen" in the southern United States, where "pen" and "pin" are pronounced similarly.</li>
<li>"Extra accessories" which must be ordered separately for a new camera, as distinct from the accessories provided with the camera as sold.</li></ul>
<p>Not all constructions that are typically pleonasms are so in all cases, nor are all constructions derived from pleonasms themselves pleonastic:
</p>
<ul><li>"Put that glass <b>over there</b> on the table."<br /> This could, depending on room layout, mean "Put that glass on the table across the room, not the table right in front of you"; if the room were laid out like that, most English speakers would intuitively understand that the distant, not immediate table was the one being referred to; however, if there were only one table in the room, the phrase would indeed be pleonastic. Also, it could mean, "Put that glass on that certain spot on the table"; thus, in this case, it is <i>not</i> pleonastic.</li>
<li>"I'm going <b>way down</b> South."<br /> This may imply "I'm going much farther south than you might think if I didn't stress the southerliness of my destination"; but such phrasing is also sometimes—and sometimes jokingly—used pleonastically when simply "south" would do; it depends upon the context, the intent of the speaker/writer, and ultimately even on the expectations of the listener/reader.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphemic_pleonasm">Morphemic pleonasm</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pleonasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Morphemic pleonasm">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="box-Original_research plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Original_research" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/40px-Ambox_important.svg.png" decoding="async" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/60px-Ambox_important.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/80px-Ambox_important.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="40" data-file-height="40" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>possibly contains <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">original research</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pleonasm&amp;action=edit">improve it</a> by <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verifying</a> the claims made and adding <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#Inline_citations" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">inline citations</a>. Statements consisting only of original research should be removed.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">January 2016</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">Morphemes</a>, not just words, can enter the realm of pleonasm: Some word-parts are simply optional in various languages and dialects. A familiar example to American English speakers would be the allegedly optional "-al-", probably most commonly seen in "publically" vs. "publicly"—both spellings are considered correct/acceptable in American English, and both pronounced the same, in this dialect, rendering the "publically" spelling pleonastic in US English; in other dialects it is "required", while it is quite conceivable that in another generation or so of American English it will be "forbidden". This treatment of words ending in "-ic", "-ac", etc., is quite inconsistent in US English—compare "maniacally" or "forensically" with "stoicly" or "heroicly"; "forensicly" doesn't look "right" in any dialect, but "heroically" looks internally redundant to many Americans. (Likewise, there are thousands of mostly American Google search results for "eroticly", some in reputable publications, but it does not even appear in the 23-volume, 23,000-page, 500,000-definition <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> (<i>OED</i>), the largest in the world; and even American dictionaries give the correct spelling as "erotically".) In a more modern pair of words, <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Electrical_and_Electronics_Engineers" title="Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers">Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers</a> dictionaries say that "electric" and "electrical" mean the same thing. However, the usual adverb form is "electrically". (For example, "The glass rod is electrically charged by rubbing it with silk".)
</p><p>Some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2016)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> (mostly US-based) <a href="/wiki/Prescriptive_grammar" class="mw-redirect" title="Prescriptive grammar">prescriptive grammar</a> pundits would say that the "-ly" not "-ally" form is "correct" in any case in which there is no "-ical" variant of the basic word, and vice versa; i.e. "maniacally", not "maniacly", is correct because "maniacal" is a word, while "publicly", not "publically", must be correct because "publical" is (arguably) not a real word (it does not appear in the <i>OED</i>). This logic is in doubt, since most if not all "-ical" constructions arguably are "real" words and most have certainly occurred more than once in "reputable" publications, and are also immediately understood by any educated reader of English even if they "look funny" to some, or do not appear in popular dictionaries. Additionally, there are numerous examples of words that have very widely accepted extended forms that have skipped one or more intermediary forms, e.g. "disestablishmentarian" in the absence of "disestablishmentary" (which does not appear in the <i>OED</i>). At any rate, while some US editors might consider "-ally" vs. "-ly" to be pleonastic in some cases, the majority of other English speakers would not, and many "-ally" words are not pleonastic to anyone, even in American English.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The most common definitely pleonastic morphological usage in English is "<a href="/wiki/Irregardless" title="Irregardless">irregardless</a>", which is very widely criticized as being a non-word. The standard usage is "regardless", which is already negative; adding the additional negative <i>ir-</i> is interpreted by some as logically reversing the meaning to "with regard to/for", which is certainly not what the speaker intended to convey. (According to most dictionaries that include it, "irregardless" appears to derive from confusion between "regardless" and "irrespective", which have overlapping meanings.)
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Morphemic_pleonasm_in_Modern_Standard_Chinese">Morphemic pleonasm in Modern Standard Chinese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pleonasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Morphemic pleonasm in Modern Standard Chinese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>There are several instances in Chinese vocabulary where pleonasms and <a href="/wiki/Cognate_object" title="Cognate object">cognate objects</a> are present. Their presence usually indicate the plural form of the noun or the noun in formal context.
</p>
<ul><li><span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">书 – 书籍</span> ('book(s)' – in general)</li>
<li><span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">纸 – 纸张</span> ('paper, tissue, pieces of paper' – formal)</li></ul>
<p>In some instances, the pleonasmic form of the verb is used with the intention as an emphasis to one meaning of the verb, isolating them from their idiomatic and figurative uses. But over time, the pseudo-object, which sometimes repeats the verb, is almost inherently coupled with the it.
</p><p>For example, the word <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">睡</span> ('to sleep') is an intransitive verb, but may express different meaning when coupled with objects of prepositions as in "to sleep with". However, in Mandarin, <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">睡</span> is usually coupled with a pseudo-character <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">觉</span>, yet it is not entirely a cognate object, to express the act of resting.
</p>
<ul><li><span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">我要睡</span> ('I want sleep'). Although such usage of <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">睡</span> is not found among native speakers of Mandarin and may sound awkward, this expression is grammatically correct and it is clear that <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">睡</span> means 'to sleep/to rest' in this context.</li>
<li><span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">我要睡觉</span> ('I want to sleep') and <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">我要睡了</span> ('I'm going to sleep'). In this context, <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">睡觉</span> ('to sleep') is a complete verb and native speakers often express themselves this way. Adding this particle clears any suspicion from using it with any direct object shown in the next example:</li>
<li><span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">我要睡她</span> ('I want to have sex with her') and <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">我要和她睡</span> ('I want to sleep with her'). When the verb follows an animate direct object <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">她</span> the meaning changes dramatically. The first instance is mainly seen in colloquial speech. Note that the object of preposition of "to have sex with" is the equivalent of the direct object of <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">睡</span> in Mandarin.</li></ul>
<p>One can also find a way around this verb, using another one which does not is used to express idiomatic expressions nor necessitate a pleonasm, because it only has one meaning:
</p>
<ul><li><span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">我要就寝</span> ('I want "to dorm<span style="padding-right:.15em;">"</span>')</li></ul>
<p>Nevertheless, <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">就寝</span> is a verb used in high-<a href="/wiki/Register_(sociolinguistics)" title="Register (sociolinguistics)">register</a> diction, just like English verbs with Latin roots.
</p><p>There is no relationship found between Chinese and English regarding verbs that can take pleonasms and cognate objects. Although the verb <i>to sleep</i> may take a cognate object as in "sleep a restful sleep", it is a pure coincidence, since verbs of this form are more common in Chinese than in English; and when the English verb is used without the cognate objects, its diction is natural and its meaning is clear in every level of diction, as in "I want to sleep" and "I want to have a rest".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subtler_redundancies">Subtler redundancies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pleonasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Subtler redundancies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In some cases, the redundancy in meaning occurs at the syntactic level above the word, such as at the phrase level:
</p>
<dl><dd>"It's <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9j%C3%A0_vu" title="Déjà vu">déjà vu</a> all over again."</dd>
<dd>"I never make predictions, especially about the future."</dd></dl>
<p>The redundancy of these two well-known statements is deliberate, for <a href="/wiki/Humor" class="mw-redirect" title="Humor">humorous</a> effect. (See <a href="/wiki/Yogi_Berra#Yogi-isms" title="Yogi Berra">Yogi Berra#Yogi-isms</a>.) But one does hear educated people say "my predictions about the future of politics" for "my predictions about politics", which are equivalent in meaning. While predictions are necessarily about the future (at least in relation to the time the prediction was made), the nature of this future can be subtle (e.g., "I predict that he died a week ago"—the prediction is about future discovery or proof of the date of death, not about the death itself). Generally "the future" is assumed, making most constructions of this sort pleonastic. The latter humorous quote above about not making predictions – by <a href="/wiki/Yogi_Berra" title="Yogi Berra">Yogi Berra</a> – is not really a pleonasm, but rather an <a href="/wiki/Irony" title="Irony">ironic</a> <a href="/wiki/Play_on_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Play on words">play on words</a>.
</p><p>But "It's <i>déjà vu</i> all over again." could mean that there was earlier another <i>déjà vu</i> of the same event or idea, which has now arisen for a third time.
</p><p>Redundancy, and "useless" or "nonsensical" words (or phrases, or morphemes), can also be inherited by one language from the influence of another and are not pleonasms in the more critical sense but actual changes in grammatical construction considered to be required for "proper" usage in the language or dialect in question. <a href="/wiki/Irish_English" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish English">Irish English</a>, for example, is prone to a number of constructions that non-Irish speakers find strange and sometimes directly confusing or silly:
</p>
<ul><li>"I'<b>m after</b> put<b>ting</b> it on the table."<br />('I [have] put it on the table.') This example further shows that the effect, whether pleonastic or only pseudo-pleonastic, can apply to words and word-parts, and multi-word phrases, given that the fullest rendition would be "I <b>am after</b> put<b>ting</b> it on the table".</li>
<li>"Have a look at <b>your</b> man there."<br />('Have a look at that man there.') An example of word substitution, rather than addition, that seems illogical outside the dialect. This common possessive-seeming construction often confuses the non-Irish enough that they do not at first understand what is meant. Even "<b>Have a </b>look at that man<b> there</b>" is arguably further doubly redundant, in that a shorter "Look at that man" version would convey essentially the same meaning.</li>
<li>"She's my wife<b> so she is</b>."<br />('She's my wife.') Duplicate subject and verb, post-complement, used to emphasize a simple factual statement or assertion.</li></ul>
<p>All of these constructions originate from the application of <a href="/wiki/Irish_Gaelic" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Gaelic">Irish Gaelic</a> grammatical rules to the English dialect spoken, in varying particular forms, throughout the island.
</p><p>Seemingly "useless" additions and substitutions must be contrasted with similar constructions that are used for stress, humor, or other intentional purposes, such as:
</p>
<ul><li>"I abso<b>-fuckin'-</b>lutely agree!"<br />(<a href="/wiki/Tmesis" title="Tmesis">tmesis</a>, for stress)</li>
<li>"Topless<b>-shmopless</b>—nudity doesn't distract me."<br />(<a href="/wiki/Shm-reduplication" title="Shm-reduplication">shm-reduplication</a>, for humor)</li></ul>
<p>The latter of these is a result of Yiddish influences on modern English, especially <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a> US English.
</p><p>Sometimes editors and grammatical stylists will use "pleonasm" to describe simple wordiness. This phenomenon is also called <a href="/wiki/Prolixity" class="mw-redirect" title="Prolixity">prolixity</a> or <a href="/wiki/Logorrhea_(rhetoric)" class="mw-redirect" title="Logorrhea (rhetoric)">logorrhea</a>. Compare:
</p>
<ul><li>"The sound of the <a href="/wiki/Loud_music" title="Loud music">loud music</a> drowned out the sound of the burglary."</li>
<li>"The loud music drowned out the sound of the burglary."</li></ul>
<p>or even:
</p>
<ul><li>"The music drowned out the burglary."</li></ul>
<p>The reader or hearer does not have to be told that loud music has a sound, and in a newspaper headline or other abbreviated prose can even be counted upon to infer that "burglary" is a proxy for "sound of the burglary" and that the music necessarily must have been loud to drown it out, unless the burglary was relatively quiet (this is not a trivial issue, as it may affect the legal culpability of the person who played the music); the word "loud" may imply that the music should have been played quietly if at all. Many are critical of the excessively abbreviated constructions of "<a href="/wiki/Headline" title="Headline">headline</a>-itis" or "<a href="/wiki/News_style" title="News style">newsspeak</a>", so "loud [music]" and "sound of the [burglary]" in the above example should probably not be properly regarded as pleonastic or otherwise genuinely redundant, but simply as informative and clarifying.
</p><p>Prolixity is also used to obfuscate, confuse, or euphemize and is not necessarily redundant or pleonastic in such constructions, though it often is. "Post-traumatic stress disorder" (<a href="/wiki/Shell_shock" title="Shell shock">shell shock</a>) and "pre-owned vehicle" (<a href="/wiki/Used_car" title="Used car">used car</a>) are both <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tumid" class="extiw" title="wikt:tumid">tumid</a> euphemisms but are not redundant. Redundant forms, however, are especially common in business, political, and academic language that is intended to sound impressive (or to be vague so as to make it hard to determine what is actually being promised, or otherwise misleading). For example: "This quarter, we are presently focusing with determination on an all-new, innovative integrated methodology and framework for rapid expansion of customer-oriented external programs designed and developed to bring the company's consumer-first paradigm into the marketplace as quickly as possible."
</p><p>In contrast to redundancy, an <a href="/wiki/Oxymoron" title="Oxymoron">oxymoron</a> results when two seemingly contradictory words are adjoined.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_forms">Other forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pleonasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Other forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Redundancies sometimes take the form of foreign words whose meaning is repeated in the context:
</p>
<ul><li>"We went to <b>the El Restaurante restaurant</b>."</li>
<li>"<b>The<i> La Brea </i>tar</b> pits are fascinating."</li>
<li>"Roast beef served <b>with <i>au</i> <i>jus</i> sauce</b>."</li>
<li>"<b>Please</b> R.<b>S.V.P.</b>"</li>
<li>"The Schwarz<b>wald</b> <b>Forest</b> is deep and dark."</li>
<li>"The Drakens<b>berg</b> <b>Mountains</b> are in South Africa."</li>
<li>Libre<b>Office</b> <b>office</b> suite.</li>
<li><b>The <i>hoi polloi.</i></b></li>
<li>I'd like to hav<b></b>e a <b><i>chai</i> tea</b>.</li></ul>
<p>These sentences use phrases which mean, respectively, "the the restaurant restaurant", "the the tar tar", "with in juice sauce" and so on. However, many times these redundancies are necessary—especially when the foreign words make up a proper noun as opposed to a common one. For example, "We went to Il Ristorante" is acceptable provided the audience can infer that it is a restaurant (if they understand Italian and English it might likely, if spoken rather than written, be misinterpreted as a generic reference and not a proper noun, leading the hearer to ask "Which ristorante do you mean?" Such confusions are common in richly bilingual areas like <a href="/wiki/Montreal" title="Montreal">Montreal</a> or the <a href="/wiki/American_Southwest" class="mw-redirect" title="American Southwest">American Southwest</a> when people mix phrases from two languages at once). But avoiding the redundancy of the Spanish phrase in the second example would only leave an awkward alternative: "La Brea pits are fascinating".
</p><p>Most find it best to not even drop articles when using proper nouns made from foreign languages:
</p>
<ul><li>"The movie is playing at <b>the El</b> Capitan theater."</li></ul>
<p>This is also similar to the treatment of definite and indefinite articles in titles of books, films, etc. where the article can—some would say <i>must</i>—be present where it would otherwise be "forbidden":
</p>
<ul><li>"Stephen King<b>'s</b> <i><b>The</b> Shining</i> is scary."<br />(Normally, the article would be left off following a possessive.)</li>
<li>"I'm having <b>an <i>An</i></b> <i>American Werewolf in London</i> movie night at my place."<br />(Seemingly doubled article, which would be taken for a <a href="/wiki/Stuttering" title="Stuttering">stutter</a> or typographical error in other contexts.)</li></ul>
<p>Some cross-linguistic redundancies, especially in placenames, occur because a word in one language became the title of a place in another (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" class="mw-redirect" title="Sahara Desert">Sahara Desert</a>—"Sahara" is an English approximation of the word for "deserts" in Arabic). "The <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Angels" title="Los Angeles Angels">Los Angeles Angels</a>" professional baseball team is literally "the The Angels Angels." A supposed extreme example is <a href="/wiki/Torpenhow_Hill" title="Torpenhow Hill">Torpenhow Hill</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a>, if etymologized as meaning "hill" in the language of each of the cultures that have lived in the area during recorded history, could be translated as "Hillhillhill Hill". See the <a href="/wiki/List_of_tautological_place_names" title="List of tautological place names">List of tautological place names</a> for many more examples.
</p><p>Acronyms can also form the basis for redundancies; this is known humorously as <a href="/wiki/RAS_syndrome" title="RAS syndrome">RAS syndrome</a> (for Redundant Acronym Syndrome Syndrome):
</p>
<ul><li>"I forgot my <b>PIN number</b> for the <b>ATM machine</b>" is actually: "I forgot my Personal Identification Number number for the Automated Teller Machine machine."</li>
<li>"I upgraded the <b>RAM memory</b> of my computer."</li>
<li>"She is infected with the <b>HIV virus</b>."</li>
<li>"I have installed a <b>CMS system</b> on my server."</li>
<li>"Next year, I will hike the <b>PCT Trail</b>."</li></ul>
<p>In all the examples listed above, the word after the acronym repeats a word represented in the acronym—respectively, "Personal Identification Number number", "Automated Teller Machine machine", "Random Access Memory memory", "Human Immunodeficiency Virus virus", "Content Management System system". (See <a href="/wiki/RAS_syndrome" title="RAS syndrome">RAS syndrome</a> for many more examples.) The expansion of an acronym like PIN or HIV may be well known to English speakers, but the acronyms themselves have come to be treated as words, so little thought is given to what their expansion is (and "PIN" is also pronounced the same as the word "pin"; disambiguation is probably the source of "PIN number"; "SIN number" for "Social Insurance Number number" &#32;&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>&#93; is a similar common phrase in Canada.) But redundant acronyms are more common with technical (e.g. computer) terms where well-informed speakers recognize the redundancy and consider it silly or ignorant, but mainstream users might not, since they may not be aware or certain of the full expansion of an acronym like "RAM".
</p><p>Some redundancies are simply typographical. For instance, when a short inflexional word like "the" occurs at the end of a line, it is very common to accidentally repeat it at the beginning of the following line, and a large number of readers would not even notice it.
</p><p>Carefully constructed expressions, especially in poetry and political language, but also some general usages in everyday speech, may appear to be redundant but are not. This is most common with cognate objects (a verb's object that is cognate with the verb):
</p>
<ul><li>"She <b>slept</b> a deep <b>sleep</b>."</li></ul>
<p>Or, a classic example from Latin:
</p>
<ul><li><i><a href="/wiki/Mutatis_mutandis" title="Mutatis mutandis">mutatis mutandis</a></i> = "with change made to what needs to be changed" (an <a href="/wiki/Ablative_absolute" class="mw-redirect" title="Ablative absolute">ablative absolute</a> construction)</li></ul>
<p>The words need not be etymologically related, but simply conceptually, to be considered an example of cognate object:
</p>
<ul><li>"We <b>wept</b> <b>tears</b> of joy."</li></ul>
<p>Such constructions are not actually redundant (unlike "She slept a sleep" or "We wept tears") because the object's modifiers provide additional information. A rarer, more constructed form is <a href="/wiki/Polyptoton" title="Polyptoton">polyptoton</a>, the stylistic repetition of the same word or words derived from the same root:
</p>
<ul><li>"...[T]he only thing we have to <b>fear</b> is <b>fear</b> itself."—<a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>, "<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.bartleby.com/124/pres49.html">First Inaugural Address</a>", March 1933.</li>
<li>"With eager <b>feeding</b>[,] <b>food</b> doth choke the <b>feeder</b>."—<a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Richard_II_(play)" title="Richard II (play)">Richard II</a></i>, II, i, 37.</li></ul>
<p>As with cognate objects, these constructions are not redundant because the repeated words or derivatives cannot be removed without removing meaning or even destroying the sentence, though in most cases they could be replaced with non-related synonyms at the cost of style (e.g., compare "The only thing we have to fear is terror".)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Semantic_pleonasm_and_context">Semantic pleonasm and context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pleonasm&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Semantic pleonasm and context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In many cases of semantic pleonasm, the status of a word as pleonastic depends on context. The relevant context can be as local as a neighboring word, or as global as the extent of a speaker's knowledge. In fact, many examples of redundant expressions are not inherently redundant, but can be redundant if used one way, and are not redundant if used another way. The "up" in "climb up" is not always redundant, as in the example "He climbed up and then fell down the mountain." Many other examples of pleonasm are redundant only if the speaker's knowledge is taken into account. For example, most English speakers would agree that "tuna fish" is redundant because tuna is a kind of fish. However, given the knowledge that "tuna" can also refer a kind of edible prickly pear,<sup id="cite_ref-MW_Tuna_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MW_Tuna-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> the "fish" in "tuna fish" can be seen as non-pleonastic, but rather a disambiguator between the fish and the prickly pear.
</p><p>Conversely, to English speakers who do not know Spanish, there is nothing redundant about "the La Brea tar pits" because the name "La Brea" is opaque: the speaker does not know that it is Spanish for "the tar". Similarly, even though <a href="/wiki/Scuba_diving" title="Scuba diving">scuba</a> stands for "self-contained underwater breathing apparatus", a phrase like "the scuba gear" would probably not be considered pleonastic because "scuba" has been <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">reanalyzed</a> into English as a simple word, and not an acronym suggesting the pleonastic word sequence "apparatus gear". (Most do not even know that it is an acronym and do not spell it SCUBA or S.C.U.B.A. Similar examples are <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a>.)
</p>
