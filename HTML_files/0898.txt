<p><b>Patrick Joseph Kennedy</b> (January 14, 1858 – May 18, 1929) was an American businessman and politician. Kennedy was a major figure in the Democratic Party in Boston. He was the father of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Kennedy_Sr." title="Joseph P. Kennedy Sr.">Joseph P. Kennedy Sr.</a> and the paternal grandfather of President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>.
</p><p>Kennedy was the only surviving male in the family after two outbreaks of cholera killed his father and brother. He started work at fourteen as a stevedore in the docks. Kennedy owned three saloons and a whisky import house, and eventually had major interests in coal and banking. He moved successfully into politics, as a sociable man able to mix comfortably with both the Catholic and the Protestant elite, and he served in the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_House_of_Representatives" title="Massachusetts House of Representatives">Massachusetts House of Representatives</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Senate" title="Massachusetts Senate">Massachusetts Senate</a>. His involvement with the behind-the-scenes machinations made Boston politics notorious.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P._J._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pj_kennedy.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Pj_kennedy.jpg/170px-Pj_kennedy.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="232" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7c/Pj_kennedy.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="192" data-file-height="262" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pj_kennedy.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Young P. J. Kennedy around the mid-to-late 1870s</div></div></div>
<p>Kennedy was the youngest of five children born to Irish Catholic immigrants Patrick Kennedy (1823–1858) and Bridget Murphy (1824–1888), who were both from <a href="/wiki/New_Ross" title="New Ross">New Ross</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Wexford" title="County Wexford">County Wexford</a>, and married in <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a> on September 26, 1849. The couple's elder son, John, had died of <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">cholera</a> in infancy two years before Kennedy was born. Ten months after Kennedy's birth, his father Patrick also succumbed to the infectious cholera epidemic that infested the family's <a href="/wiki/East_Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="East Boston, Massachusetts">East Boston neighborhood</a>. As the only surviving male, Kennedy was the first family member to receive a formal education. His mother Bridget had purchased an East Boston stationery and notions store where she had worked. The business took off and expanded into a grocery and liquor store.
</p><p>At the age of fourteen, Kennedy left school to work with his mother and three older sisters, Mary, Joanna, and Margaret, as a <a href="/wiki/Stevedore" title="Stevedore">stevedore</a> on the Boston Docks. In the 1880s, with money he had saved from his modest earnings, he launched a business career by buying a saloon in Haymarket Square downtown. In time, he bought a second establishment by the East Boston docks. Next, to capitalize on the social drinking of upper-class Boston, Kennedy purchased a third bar in an upscale East Boston hotel, the Maverick House. Before he was thirty, his growing prosperity allowed him to buy a whiskey-importing business.
</p><p>By the time of his death in 1929, Kennedy held an interest in a coal company and a substantial amount of stock in a bank, the Columbia Trust Company. His wealth afforded his family of one son and two daughters an attractive home on Jeffries Point in East Boston.<sup id="cite_ref-dallek_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dallek-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_career">Political career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P._J._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Political career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:76px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Massachusetts_Senator_Patrick_J_Kennedy.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c6/Massachusetts_Senator_Patrick_J_Kennedy.png" decoding="async" width="74" height="104" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="74" data-file-height="104" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Massachusetts_Senator_Patrick_J_Kennedy.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>P. J. Kennedy in 1893 as a Massachusetts State Senator</div></div></div>
<p>Kennedy was "always ready to help less fortunate fellow Irishmen with a little cash and some sensible advice." He enjoyed the approval and respect of most folks in East Boston, living on the hill of a mixed Boston neighborhood of upscale Irish and Protestant elite. Beginning in 1884, he converted his popularity into five consecutive one-year terms in the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_House_of_Representatives" title="Massachusetts House of Representatives">Massachusetts House of Representatives</a>, followed by three two-year terms in the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Senate" title="Massachusetts Senate">Massachusetts Senate</a>. Establishing himself as one of Boston's principal Democratic leaders, he was invited to give one of the seconding speeches for <a href="/wiki/Grover_Cleveland" title="Grover Cleveland">Grover Cleveland</a> at the party's 1888 national convention in St. Louis. However, he found campaigning, speech making, and legislative maneuvering, to be less appealing than the behind-the-scenes machinations that characterized so much of Boston politics in the late-nineteenth and early-twentieth centuries. After leaving the Senate in 1895, Kennedy spent his political career as an appointed elections commissioner, an appointed fire commissioner, as the backroom boss of Boston's Ward Two, and as a member of his party's unofficial Board of Strategy.<sup id="cite_ref-dallek_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dallek-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_children">Marriage and children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P._J._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Marriage and children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P._J._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his later years, Kennedy developed degenerative liver disease. In April 1929, he was admitted to <a href="/wiki/Beth_Israel_Deaconess_Medical_Center" title="Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center">Deaconess Hospital</a> to receive treatment.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He died there on May 18 at the age of 71. His funeral was held at St. John the Evangelist Church in <a href="/wiki/Winthrop,_Massachusetts" title="Winthrop, Massachusetts">Winthrop, Massachusetts</a> on May 21. The <i><a href="/wiki/Boston_Globe" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Globe">Boston Globe</a></i> reported that hundreds of mourners lined the streets to watch Kennedy's funeral procession and businesses in East Boston closed to honor him.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy is buried in Holy Cross Cemetery in <a href="/wiki/Malden,_Massachusetts" title="Malden, Massachusetts">Malden, Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=P._J._Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1914, P.J. Kennedy's son Joseph married <a href="/wiki/Rose_Kennedy" title="Rose Kennedy">Rose Fitzgerald</a> (1890–1995), the eldest daughter of Boston Mayor <a href="/wiki/John_F._Fitzgerald" title="John F. Fitzgerald">John F. Fitzgerald</a> (1863–1950). Joe and Rose had nine children, including World War II casualty <a href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Kennedy_Jr." title="Joseph P. Kennedy Jr.">Joseph P. Kennedy Jr.</a> (1915–1944), President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> (1917–1963), Senator <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a> (1925–1968), and longtime Senator <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Ted Kennedy</a> (1932–2009).
</p><p>The Patrick J. Kennedy School, named for him, is a public grammar school located in East Boston.
