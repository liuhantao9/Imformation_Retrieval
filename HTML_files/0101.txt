	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Spanish naming customs</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Historical traditions for naming children practiced in Spain</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about naming customs in Spain. For naming customs in Hispanic America, see <a href="/wiki/Naming_customs_of_Hispanic_America" title="Naming customs of Hispanic America">Naming customs of Hispanic America</a>.</div>
<p><b>Spanish naming customs</b> are historical traditions for naming children practised in <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>. According to these customs, a person's name consists of a <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> (simple or composite) followed by two family names (<a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surnames</a>). Historically, the first surname was the father's first surname, and the second the mother's first surname. In recent years, the order of the surnames in a family is decided when registering the first child, but the traditional order is still largely the choice.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Often, the practice is to use one given name and the first surname only most of the time (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Unamuno" title="Miguel de Unamuno">Miguel de Unamuno</a>), the complete name being typically reserved for legal, formal, and documentary matters; however, both surnames are sometimes systematically used when the first surname is very common (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Federico_Garc%C3%ADa_Lorca" title="Federico García Lorca">Federico García Lorca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Ruiz_Picasso" class="mw-redirect" title="Pablo Ruiz Picasso">Pablo Ruiz Picasso</a> or <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Luis_Rodr%C3%ADguez_Zapatero" title="José Luis Rodríguez Zapatero">José Luis Rodríguez Zapatero</a>) so as to get a more customized name.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> In these cases, it is even common to use only the second surname, as in "Lorca", "Picasso" or "Zapatero". This does not affect alphabetization: discussions of "Lorca", the Spanish poet, must be alphabetized in an index under "García Lorca", never "Lorca".
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_system_in_Spain">Naming system in Spain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Naming system in Spain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Currently in Spain, people bear a single or composite <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> (<i>nombre</i> in Spanish) and two <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">surnames</a> (<i>apellidos</i> in Spanish).
</p><p>A composite given name comprises two (or more) single names; for example <i>Juan Pablo</i> is considered not to be a first and a second forename, but a single composite forename.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The two surnames refer to each of the parental families. Traditionally, a person's first surname is the father's first surname (<i>apellido paterno</i>), while their second surname is the mother's first surname (<i>apellido materno</i>). For example, if a man named <i>Eduardo Fernández Garrido</i> marries a woman named <i>María Dolores Martínez Ruiz</i> and they have a child named <i>José</i>, there are several legal options, but their child would most usually be known as <i>José Fernández Martínez</i>.
</p><p>Spanish <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a> law has allowed surname transposition since 1999,<sup id="cite_ref-Orden_de_apellidos_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Orden_de_apellidos-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> subject to the condition that every sibling must bear the same surname order recorded in the <i>Registro Civil</i> (<a href="/wiki/Civil_registry" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil registry">civil registry</a>), but there have been legal exceptions. 
</p><p>Since 2013, if the parents of a child were unable to agree on the order of surnames, an official would decide which is to come first,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> with the paternal name being the default option. The only requirement is that every son and daughter must have the same order of the surnames, so they cannot change it separately.
</p><p>Since June 2017, adopting the paternal name first is no longer the standard method, and parents are required to sign an agreement wherein the name order is expressed explicitly.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-elmundo2_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elmundo2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The law also grants a person the option, upon reaching adulthood, of reversing the order of their surnames. However, this legislation only applies to Spanish citizens; people of other nationalities are issued the surname indicated by the laws of their original country.<sup id="cite_ref-elmundo2_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elmundo2-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Each surname can also be composite, with the parts usually linked by the conjunction <i>y</i> or <i>e</i> (and), by the preposition <i>de</i> (of), or by a hyphen. For example, a person's name might be <i>Juan Pablo Fernández de Calderón García-Iglesias</i>, consisting of a forename (<i>Juan Pablo</i>), a paternal surname (<i>Fernández de Calderón</i>), and a maternal surname (<i>García-Iglesias</i>).
</p><p>There are times when it is impossible, by inspection of a name, to correctly analyse it. For example, the writer <a href="/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A0_Juan_Arb%C3%B3" title="Sebastià Juan Arbó">Sebastià Juan Arbó</a> was alphabetised by the <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">Library of Congress</a> for many years under "<i>Arbó</i>", assuming that <i>Sebastià</i> and <i>Juan</i> were both given names. However, "Juan" was actually his first surname. Resolving questions like this, which typically involve very common names ("<i>Juan</i>" is rarely a surname), often requires the consultation of the person involved or legal documents pertaining to them.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forms_of_address">Forms of address</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Forms of address">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A man named <i>José Antonio Gómez Iglesias</i> would normally be addressed as either <i>señor Gómez</i> or <i>señor Gómez Iglesias</i> instead of <i>señor Iglesias</i>, because <i>Gómez</i> is his first surname. Furthermore, Mr. Gómez might be informally addressed as
</p>
<ol><li><i>José Antonio</i></li>
<li><i>José</i></li>
<li><i>Pepe</i> (nickname for José)</li>
<li><i>Antonio</i></li>
<li><i>Toño</i> (nickname for Antonio)</li>
<li><i>Joselito, Josito, Joselillo, Josico</i> or <i>Joselín</i> (diminutives of José)</li>
<li><i>Antoñito, Toñín</i>, <i>Toñito, Ñoño</i> or <i>Nono</i> (diminutives of Antonio)</li>
<li><i>Joseán</i> (<a href="/wiki/Apocope" title="Apocope">apocopation</a>).</li></ol>
<p>Very formally, he could be addressed with an honorific such as <i><a href="/wiki/Don_(honorific)" title="Don (honorific)">don</a> José Antonio</i> or <i>don José</i>.
</p><p>It is not unusual, when the first surname is very common, like <i>García</i> in the example above, for a person to be referred to formally using both family names, or casually by their second surname only. For example, <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Luis_Rodr%C3%ADguez_Zapatero" title="José Luis Rodríguez Zapatero">José Luis Rodríguez Zapatero</a> (elected President of the Spanish Government in the 2004 and 2008 general elections) is often called simply Zapatero, the name he inherited from his mother's family since Rodríguez is a common surname and may be ambiguous. The same occurs with another former Spanish Socialist leader, <a href="/wiki/Alfredo_P%C3%A9rez_Rubalcaba" title="Alfredo Pérez Rubalcaba">Alfredo Pérez Rubalcaba</a>, with the poet and dramatist <a href="/wiki/Federico_Garc%C3%ADa_Lorca" title="Federico García Lorca">Federico García Lorca</a>, and with the painter <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Ruiz Picasso</a>. As these people's paternal names are very common, they are often called with their maternal names (Rubalcaba, Lorca, Picasso). It would nonetheless be a mistake to index José Luis Rodríguez Zapatero under Z as "Zapatero", or Federico García Lorca under L as "Lorca".
</p><p><span id="Hyphenation"></span>In an English-speaking environment, Spanish-named people sometimes <a href="/wiki/Hyphen" title="Hyphen">hyphenate</a> their surnames to avoid Anglophone confusion or to fill in forms with only one space provided for last name, thus: <i>Mr. José Antonio Gómez-Iglesias</i>. A practical option to spare an explanation is using a single surname composed of two separate words.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Forenames">Forenames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Forenames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Parents choose their child's <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>, which must be recorded in the <i>Registro Civil</i> (Civil Registry) to establish his or her legal identity.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> With few restrictions, parents can now choose any name; common sources of names are the parents' taste, honouring a relative, the <a href="/wiki/General_Roman_Calendar" title="General Roman Calendar">General Roman Calendar</a> <i>nomina</i> (nominal register), and traditional Spanish names. Legislation in <a href="/wiki/Spain_under_Franco" class="mw-redirect" title="Spain under Franco">Spain under Franco</a> legally limited cultural naming customs to only <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> (Jesus, Mary, saints) and typical Spanish names (Álvaro, Jimena, etc.)<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Reliable source for this alleged legal limitation needed (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. Although the first part of a composite forename generally reflects the gender of the child, the second personal name need not (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mar%C3%ADa_Aznar" title="José María Aznar">José María Aznar</a>). At present, the only naming limitation is the dignity of the child, who cannot be given an insulting name. Similar limitations applied against diminutive, familiar, and colloquial variants not recognized as names proper, and "those that lead to confusion regarding sex";<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
however, current law<sup id="cite_ref-&#39;&#39;Identidad_de_género&#39;&#39;_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-&#39;&#39;Identidad_de_género&#39;&#39;-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> allows registration of diminutive names.<sup id="cite_ref-Pepe_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pepe-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span id="Mar.C3.ADa_and_Jos.C3.A9"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="María_and_José">María and José</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: María and José">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Girls are often named <i>María</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> honouring the <a href="/wiki/Mary_(mother_of_Jesus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mary (mother of Jesus)">Virgin Mary</a>, by appending either a shrine, place, or religious-concept suffix-name to <i>María</i>. In daily life, such women omit the "Mary of the ..." nominal prefix, and use the suffix portion of their composite names as their public, rather than legal, <a href="/wiki/Personally_identifiable_information" class="mw-redirect" title="Personally identifiable information">identity</a>. Hence, women with <a href="/wiki/Blessed_Virgin_Mary_(Roman_Catholic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Blessed Virgin Mary (Roman Catholic)">Marian</a> names such as <i>María de los Ángeles</i> (Mary of the Angels), <i>María del Pilar</i> (<a href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of_the_Pillar" title="Our Lady of the Pillar">Mary of the Pillar</a>), and <i>María de la Luz</i> (Mary of the Light), are normally addressed as <i>Ángeles</i> (Angels), <i>Pilar</i> (Pillar), and <i>Luz</i> (Light); however, each might be addressed as <i>María</i>. Nicknames such as <i>Maricarmen</i> for <i>María del Carmen</i>, <i>Marisol</i> for "María (de la) Soledad" ("Our Lady of Solitude", the Virgin Mary), <i>Dolores</i> or <i>Lola</i> for <i>María de los Dolores</i> ("Our Lady of Sorrows"), <i>Mercedes</i> or <i>Merche</i> for <i>María de las Mercedes</i> ("Our Lady of Mercy"), etc. are often used. Also, parents can simply name a girl <i>María</i>, or <i>Mari</i> without a suffix portion.
</p><p>It is not unusual for a boy's formal name to include <i>María</i>, preceded by a masculine name, e.g. <i>José María Aznar</i> (Joseph Mary Aznar) or <a href="/wiki/Juan_Mar%C3%ADa_Vicencio_de_Ripperd%C3%A1" title="Juan María Vicencio de Ripperdá">Juan María Vicencio de Ripperdá</a> (John Mary Vicencio de Ripperdá). Equivalently, a girl can be formally named <i>María José</i> (Mary Joseph), e.g. skier <a href="/wiki/Mar%C3%ADa_Jos%C3%A9_Rienda" title="María José Rienda">María José Rienda</a>, and informally named <i>Marijose</i>, <i>Mariajo</i>, <i>Majo</i>, <i>Ajo</i>, <i>Josefa</i>, <i>Josefina</i>, <i>Fina</i>, <i>Pepa</i>, <i>Pepi</i>, <i>Chepi</i>, <i>Pepita</i>, <i>Marisé</i> or even <i>José</i> in honor of St. Joseph. <i>María</i> as a masculine name is often abbreviated in writing as <i>M.</i> (José M. Aznar), <i>Ma.</i> (José Ma. Aznar), or <i>M.ª</i> (<a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mar%C3%ADa_Morelos" title="José María Morelos">José M.ª Morelos</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> It is unusual for any names other than the religiously significant <i>María</i> and <i>José</i> to be used in this way except for the name <i>Jesús</i> that is also very common and can be used as "<i>Jesús</i>" or "<i>Jesús María</i>" for a boy and "<i>María Jesús</i>" for a girl, and can be abbreviated as "<i>Sus</i>", "<i>Chus</i>" and other nicknames.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Registered_names">Registered names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Registered names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <i>Registro Civil</i> (Civil Registry) officially records a child's identity as composed of a <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">forename</a> (simple or composite) and the two <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">surnames</a>; however, a child can be religiously <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptized</a> with several forenames, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Felipe_Juan_Froil%C3%A1n_de_Marichalar_y_Borb%C3%B3n" class="mw-redirect" title="Felipe Juan Froilán de Marichalar y Borbón">Felipe Juan Froilán de Todos los Santos</a>. Until the 1960s, it was customary to baptize children with three forenames: the first was the main and the only one used by the child; if parents agreed, one of the other two was the name of the day's saint. Nowadays, baptizing with three or more forenames is usually a <a href="/wiki/Royal_family" title="Royal family">royal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">noble</a> family practice.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage">Marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Spain, upon marrying, one does not change one's surname.  In some instances, such as high society meetings, the partner's surname can be added after the person's surnames using the preposition <i>de</i> (of). An example would be a <i>Leocadia Blanco Álvarez</i>, married to a <i>Pedro Pérez Montilla</i>, may be addressed as <i>Leocadia Blanco de Pérez</i> or as <i>Leocadia Blanco Álvarez de Pérez</i>. This format is not used in everyday settings and has no legal value.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Spanish_surnames_by_province_of_residence.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Spanish_surnames_by_province_of_residence.png/400px-Spanish_surnames_by_province_of_residence.png" decoding="async" width="400" height="278" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Spanish_surnames_by_province_of_residence.png/600px-Spanish_surnames_by_province_of_residence.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Spanish_surnames_by_province_of_residence.png/800px-Spanish_surnames_by_province_of_residence.png 2x" data-file-width="1705" data-file-height="1185" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Spanish_surnames_by_province_of_residence.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Surname distribution: the most common surnames in Spain, by <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Spain" title="Provinces of Spain">province</a> of residence.</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Generational_transmission">Generational transmission</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Generational transmission">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the generational transmission of surnames, the paternal surname's precedence eventually eliminates the maternal surnames from the family <a href="/wiki/Lineal_descendant" title="Lineal descendant">lineage</a>. Contemporary law (1999) allows the maternal surname to be given precedence, but most people<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> observe the traditional paternal–maternal surname order. Therefore, the daughter and son of <i>Ángela López Sáenz</i> and <i>Tomás Portillo Blanco</i> are usually called <i>Laura Portillo López</i> and <i>Pedro Portillo López</i> but could also be called <i>Laura López Portillo</i> and <i>Pedro López Portillo</i>. The two surnames of all siblings must be in the same order when recorded in the <i>Registro Civil</i>.
</p><p>Patrilineal surname transmission was not always the <a href="/wiki/Norm_(sociology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Norm (sociology)">norm</a> in Spanish-speaking societies. Prior to the mid-eighteenth century,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> when the current paternal-maternal surname combination norm was adopted, Hispanophone societies often practiced matrilineal surname transmission, giving children the maternal surname and occasionally giving children a grandparent's surname (borne by neither parent) for prestige – being perceived as <a href="/wiki/Gentry" title="Gentry">gentry</a> – and profit, flattering the <a href="/wiki/Matriarchy" title="Matriarchy">matriarch</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Patriarchy" title="Patriarchy">patriarch</a> in hope of <a href="/wiki/Inheritance" title="Inheritance">inheriting</a> land. Spanish naming customs include the <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthographic</a> option of conjoining the surnames with the <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjunction" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammatical conjunction">conjunction</a> particle <i><a href="#The_particle_&quot;y&quot;_(and)">y</a></i>, or <i>e</i> before a name starting with 'I', 'Hi' or 'Y', (both meaning "and") (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Ortega_y_Gasset" title="José Ortega y Gasset">José Ortega y Gasset</a></i>, <i>Tomás Portillo y Blanco</i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Dato_e_Iradier" class="mw-redirect" title="Eduardo Dato e Iradier">Eduardo Dato e Iradier</a></i>), following an antiquated <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy_(class)" title="Aristocracy (class)">aristocratic</a> usage.
</p><p>Not every surname is a single word; such conjoining usage is common with doubled surnames (maternal-paternal), ancestral composite surnames <a href="/wiki/Inheritance" title="Inheritance">bequeathed</a> to the following generations – especially when the paternal surname is socially undistinguished. <i><a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mar%C3%ADa_%C3%81lvarez_del_Manzano" title="José María Álvarez del Manzano">José María Álvarez del Manzano y López del Hierro</a></i> is an example, his name comprising the composite single name <i>José María</i> and two composite surnames, <i>Álvarez del Manzano</i> and <i>López del Hierro</i>. Other examples derive from church place-names such as San José. When a person bears doubled surnames, the means of disambiguation is to insert <i>y</i> between the paternal and maternal surnames.
</p><p>In case of <a href="/wiki/Legitimacy_(family_law)" title="Legitimacy (family law)">illegitimacy</a> – when the child's father either is unknown or refuses to recognize his child legally – the child bears both of the mother's surnames, which may be interchanged.<sup id="cite_ref-Single-parent_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Single-parent-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Occasionally, a person with a common paternal surname and an uncommon maternal surname becomes widely known by the maternal surname. Some examples include the artist <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">Pablo Ruiz Picasso</a>, the poet <a href="/wiki/Federico_Garc%C3%ADa_Lorca" title="Federico García Lorca">Federico García Lorca</a>, and the politician <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Luis_Rodr%C3%ADguez_Zapatero" title="José Luis Rodríguez Zapatero">José Luis Rodríguez Zapatero</a>. With a similar effect, the foreign paternal surname of the Uruguayan writer <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Galeano" title="Eduardo Galeano">Eduardo Hughes Galeano</a> (his father was British) is usually omitted. (As a boy, however, he occasionally signed his name as <i>Eduardo Gius</i>, using a Hispanicised approximation of the English pronunciation of "Hughes".) Such use of the second last name by itself is colloquial, however, and may not be applied in legal contexts.
</p><p>Also rarely, a person may become widely known by both surnames, with an example being a tennis player <a href="/wiki/Arantxa_S%C3%A1nchez_Vicario" title="Arantxa Sánchez Vicario">Arantxa Sánchez Vicario</a> – whereas her older brothers <a href="/wiki/Emilio_S%C3%A1nchez" title="Emilio Sánchez">Emilio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Javier_S%C3%A1nchez" title="Javier Sánchez">Javier</a>, also professional tennis players, are mainly known only by the paternal surname of Sánchez in everyday life, although they would <i>formally</i> be addressed as <i>Sánchez Vicario</i>.
</p>
<h4><span id="Navarrese_and_.C3.81lavan_surnames"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Navarrese_and_Álavan_surnames">Navarrese and Álavan surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Navarrese and Álavan surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Where <a href="/wiki/Basque_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Basque culture">Basque</a> and Romance cultures have linguistically long coexisted, the surnames denote the father's name and the (family) <a href="/wiki/Family" title="Family">house</a> or town/village. Thus the Romance <a href="/wiki/Patronym" class="mw-redirect" title="Patronym">patronymic</a> and the place-name are conjoined with the prepositional particle <i>de</i> ("from"+"provenance"). For example, in the name <i>José Ignacio López de Arriortúa</i>, the composite surname <i>López de Arriortúa</i> is a single surname, despite <i>Arriortúa</i> being the original family name. This can lead to confusion because the Spanish <i>López</i> and the Basque <i>Arriortúa</i> are discrete surnames in Spanish and Basque respectively. This pattern was also in use in other Basque districts, but was phased out in most of the Basque-speaking areas and only remained in place across lands of heavy Romance influence, i.e. some central areas of <a href="/wiki/Navarre" title="Navarre">Navarre</a> and most of <a href="/wiki/%C3%81lava" title="Álava">Álava</a>. To a lesser extent, this pattern has been also present in Castile, where <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">Basque</a>-<a href="/wiki/Castilian_Spanish" title="Castilian Spanish">Castilian</a> bilingualism was common in northern and eastern areas up to the 13th century.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nominal_conjunctions">Nominal conjunctions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Nominal conjunctions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="The_particle_.22de.22_.28of.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_particle_&quot;de&quot;_(of)">The particle "de" (of)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The particle &quot;de&quot; (of)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Spanish_nobility" title="Spanish nobility">Spanish nobility</a></div>
<p>In Spanish, the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_prepositions" title="Spanish prepositions">preposition</a> particle <i>de</i> ("of") is used as a <a href="/wiki/Spanish_grammar" title="Spanish grammar">conjunction</a> in two surname spelling styles, and to disambiguate a surname. The first style is in <a href="/wiki/Patronymy" class="mw-redirect" title="Patronymy">patronymic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toponymic_surname" title="Toponymic surname">toponymic surname</a> spelling formulæ,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Gonzalo Fernández de Córdoba">Gonzalo Fernández de Córdoba</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Pedro_L%C3%B3pez_de_Ayala" class="mw-redirect" title="Pedro López de Ayala">Pedro López de Ayala</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Vasco_N%C3%BA%C3%B1ez_de_Balboa" title="Vasco Núñez de Balboa">Vasco Núñez de Balboa</a></i>, as in many <i><a href="/wiki/Conquistador" title="Conquistador">conquistador</a></i> names.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The spellings of surnames containing the <a href="/wiki/Preposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Preposition">prepositional</a> particle <i>de</i> are written in lower-case when they follow the name, thus <i>José Manuel de la Rúa</i> ("of the street") and <i>Cunegunda de la Torre</i> ("of the tower"), otherwise the upper-case spellings <i>doctor De la Rúa</i> and <i>señora De la Torre</i> are used.
</p>
<dl><dt>Without a patronymic</dt>
<dd><a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_I_of_Spain" title="Juan Carlos I of Spain">Juan Carlos de Borbón</a>. Unlike in French, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_orthography" title="Spanish orthography">Spanish orthography</a> does not require a contraction when a vowel begins the surname, but <i>de el</i> ("of the") becomes <i>del</i>, e.g. <i>Carlos Arturo del Monte</i> (Charles Arthur of the Mountain).</dd></dl>
<dl><dt>The patronymic exception</dt>
<dd>The current (1958) Spanish name law, <i>Artículo 195 del Reglamento del Registro Civil</i> (Article 195 of the Civil Registry Regulations) does not allow a person to prefix <i>de</i> to their surname, except as the clarifying addition of <i>de</i> to a surname (<i>apellido</i>) that might be misunderstood as a forename (<i>nombre</i>);<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> thus, a child would be registered as <i>Pedro de Miguel Jiménez</i>, to avoid the surname <i>Miguel</i> being mistaken as the second part of a composite name, as <i>Pedro Miguel</i>.</dd></dl>
<p>Bearing the <i>de</i> particle does not necessarily denote a noble family, especially in eastern <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">Castile</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alava" class="mw-redirect" title="Alava">Alava</a>, and western Navarre, the <i>de</i> usually applied to the place-name (town or village) from which the person and his or her ancestors originated. This differs from another practice established in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, i.e. the usage of <i>de</i> following the one's own name as a way of denoting the bearer's noble heritage to avoid the misperception that he or she is either a Jew or a Moor. In that time, many people, regardless of their true origins,  used the particle, e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Cervantes" title="Miguel de Cervantes">Miguel de Cervantes</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lope_de_Vega" title="Lope de Vega">Lope de Vega</a></i>, etc.; moreover, following that fashion a high noble such as <i>Francisco Sandoval Rojas</i> called himself <i>Francisco de Sandoval y Rojas</i>. During the eighteenth century, the Spanish nobility fully embraced the French custom of using <i>de</i> as a nobility identifier, however, commoners also bore the <i>de</i> particle, which made the <i>de</i> usages unclear; thus, nobility was emphasised with the surname's lineage.
</p>
<h3><span id="The_particle_.22y.22_.28and.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_particle_&quot;y&quot;_(and)">The particle "y" (and)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: The particle &quot;y&quot; (and)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the sixteenth century,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the Spanish adopted the copulative <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjunction" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammatical conjunction">conjunction</a> <i>y</i> ("and") to distinguish a person's surnames; thus the Andalusian Baroque writer <a href="/wiki/Luis_de_G%C3%B3ngora" title="Luis de Góngora">Luis de Góngora y Argote</a> (1561–1627), the Aragonese painter <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Goya" title="Francisco Goya">Francisco José de Goya y Lucientes</a> (1746–1828), the Andalusian artist <a href="/wiki/Picasso" class="mw-redirect" title="Picasso">Pablo Diego Ruiz y Picasso</a> (1881–1973), and the Madrilenian liberal philosopher <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Ortega_y_Gasset" title="José Ortega y Gasset">José Ortega y Gasset</a> (1883–1955). In Hispanic America, this spelling convention was common to clergymen (e.g. Salvadoran Bishop <a href="/wiki/%C3%93scar_Arnulfo_Romero_y_Gald%C3%A1mez" class="mw-redirect" title="Óscar Arnulfo Romero y Galdámez">Óscar Arnulfo Romero y Galdámez</a>), and sanctioned by the <i>Ley de Registro Civil</i> (Civil Registry Law) of 1870, requiring birth certificates indicating the paternal and maternal surnames conjoined with <i>y</i> – thus, <i>Felipe González y Márquez</i> and <i>José María Aznar y López</i> are the respective true names of the Spanish politicians <a href="/wiki/Felipe_Gonz%C3%A1lez" title="Felipe González">Felipe González Márquez</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mar%C3%ADa_Aznar" title="José María Aznar">José María Aznar López</a>; however, unlike in <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">Catalan</a>, the Spanish usage is infrequent. In the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, <i>y</i> and its associated usages are retained only in formal state documents such as police records, but is otherwise dropped in favour of a more American-influenced naming order.
</p><p>The conjunction <i>y</i> avoids denominational confusion when the paternal surname might appear to be a (first) name: without it, the physiologist <a href="/wiki/Santiago_Ram%C3%B3n_y_Cajal" title="Santiago Ramón y Cajal">Santiago Ramón y Cajal</a> might appear to be named <i>Santiago Ramón</i> (composite) and surnamed <i>Cajal</i>, likewise the jurist <i>Francisco Tomás y Valiente</i>, and the cleric <i>Vicente Enrique y Tarancón</i>. Without the conjunction, the <a href="/wiki/Football_(soccer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Football (soccer)">footballer</a> <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Mart%C3%ADn_V%C3%A1zquez" title="Rafael Martín Vázquez">Rafael Martín Vázquez</a>, when referred to by his surnames <i>Martín Vázquez</i> mistakenly appears to be forenamed <i>Martín</i> rather than <i>Rafael</i>, whilst, to his annoyance, the linguist <a href="/wiki/Fernando_L%C3%A1zaro_Carreter" title="Fernando Lázaro Carreter">Fernando Lázaro Carreter</a> occasionally was addressed as <i>Don Lázaro</i>, rather than as <i>Don Fernando</i> (Lázaro can be either forename or surname).
</p><p>Moreover, when the maternal surname begins with an <i>i</i> vowel sound, written with either the vowel <i>I</i> (<i>Ibarra</i>), the vowel <i>Y</i> (<i>Ybarra</i> archaic spelling) or the combination <i>Hi</i> + consonant (<i>Higueras</i>), Spanish <a href="/wiki/Euphony" class="mw-redirect" title="Euphony">euphony</a> substitutes <i>e</i> in place of <i>y</i>, thus the example of the Spanish statesman <i><a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Dato_e_Iradier" class="mw-redirect" title="Eduardo Dato e Iradier">Eduardo Dato e Iradier</a></i> (1856–1921).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Denotations">Denotations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Denotations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To communicate a person's <a href="/wiki/Identity_(social_science)" title="Identity (social science)">social identity</a>, Spanish naming customs provide <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">orthographic</a> means, such as suffix-letter abbreviations, <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> spellings, and place names, which denote and connote the person's place in <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">society</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Identity_and_descent">Identity and descent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Identity and descent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>h.</b> (son of): A man named like his father, might append the lower-case suffix <b>h.</b> (denoting <i>hijo</i>, son) to his surname, thus distinguishing himself, <i>Juan Gómez Marcos, h.</i>, from his father, <i>Juan Gómez Marcos</i>; the English analogue is "Jr." (<i>junior</i>).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="The_suffix_-ez">The suffix -ez</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: The suffix -ez">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Visigoth" class="mw-redirect" title="Visigoth">Visigothic</a> invasion of the Iberian peninsula, the local population adopted to a large extent a <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> naming system: the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <a href="/wiki/Genitive" class="mw-redirect" title="Genitive">genitive</a> suffix <i>-icī</i>, meaning <i>son of</i> would be attached to the name of a man's father.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> This suffix gradually evolved into different forms, depending on the local language: <i>-ez</i>, <i>-es</i>, <i>-is</i>, <i>-iz</i>, etc.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>As such, the son of Sancho would be called:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: Sánchez</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">Catalan</a>: Santxis</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">Galician</a>: Sanches</li></ul>
<p>And the son of Jimeno/Ximeno/Ximen would be called:
</p>
<ul><li>Spanish: Jiménez</li>
<li>Catalan: Ximenis</li>
<li>Portuguese and Galician: Ximenes</li></ul>
<p>Apart from natural spelling variations (such as using Giménez or Ximénez), language contact has brought a number of crossed versions, showing characteristics from multiple languages. It is possible to find the Catalan politician <a href="/wiki/Jordi_S%C3%A0nchez" class="mw-redirect" title="Jordi Sànchez">Jordi Sànchez</a> (who has a Spanish surname spelled the Catalan way) or the journalist <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vicen%C3%A7_Sanchis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Vicenç Sanchis (page does not exist)">Vicenç Sanchis</a> (who has a Catalan surname spelled the Spanish way).
</p><p>Not every similar surname is patronymic. Occasionally the suffix was used to denote a professional or geographical origin, such as in the surnames <i>Claves</i> (son of keys, that is son of a locksmith) or <i>Torres</i> (son of a tower, that is from the tower).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Furthermore, due to the letters <i>z</i> and <i>s</i> being pronounced alike in Latin American dialects of Spanish many non-patronymic surnames with an <i>-es</i> have come to be written with an <i>-ez</i>. In Hispano-American <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, the <i>-ez</i> spellings of <i>Chávez</i> (<a href="/wiki/Hugo_Ch%C3%A1vez" title="Hugo Chávez">Hugo Chávez</a>), <i>Cortez</i> (<a href="/wiki/Alberto_Cortez" title="Alberto Cortez">Alberto Cortez</a>) and <i>Valdez</i> (<a href="/wiki/Nelson_Valdez" class="mw-redirect" title="Nelson Valdez">Nelson Valdez</a>) are not patronymic surnames,  but simply variant spellings of the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian</a> Spanish spelling with <i>-es</i>, as in the names of <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Chaves_Gonz%C3%A1lez" title="Manuel Chaves González">Manuel Chaves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hern%C3%A1n_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Hernán Cortés">Hernán Cortés</a> and <a href="/wiki/V%C3%ADctor_Vald%C3%A9s" title="Víctor Valdés">Víctor Valdés</a>.  For more on the <i>-z</i> surnames in Spanish see <a href="/wiki/Influences_on_the_Spanish_language" title="Influences on the Spanish language">Influences on the Spanish language</a>.
</p><p>A number of the most common surnames with this suffix are:

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foundlings">Foundlings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Foundlings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Anonymous <a href="/wiki/Child_abandonment" title="Child abandonment">foundlings</a> were a naming problem for civil registrars, but such anonymous children were often named <a href="/wiki/Place_name_origins" title="Place name origins">toponymically</a>, after the town where they were found. Because most foundlings were reared in church orphanages, they were often given the surnames <i>Iglesia</i> or <i>Iglesias</i> (church[es]) and <i>Cruz</i> (cross). <i>Blanco</i> (connoting "blank" here, rather than the more usual "white") was another option. A toponymical first surname might be followed by <i>Iglesia</i> or <i>Cruz</i> as the second surname.
</p><p>Foundlings often were <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surnamed</a> <i>Expósito</i>/<i>Expósita</i> (Lat. <i>exposĭtus</i>, "exposed", connoting "foundling"), which marked them, and their descendants,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> as of low <a href="/wiki/Caste" title="Caste">caste</a> and <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">social class</a>, people without social pedigree.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  In the <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">Catalan language</a>, the surname <i>Deulofeu</i> ("made by God") was often given to foundlings. In 1921, Spanish law allowed the surname <i>Expósito</i> to be changed without charge.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Aragón, anonymous children used to receive the family surname <i>Gracia</i> ("grace") or <i>de Gracia</i> as well, because they were thought to survive by the <a href="/wiki/Grace_(Christianity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Grace (Christianity)">grace</a> of God.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign_citizens">Foreign citizens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Foreign citizens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Spain, legal and illegal foreign immigrants retain use of their cultural naming customs,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> yet upon becoming Spanish <a href="/wiki/Citizenship" title="Citizenship">citizens</a>, they are legally obliged to assume Spanish-style names (a name and two surnames).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  If the <a href="/wiki/Nationality" title="Nationality">naturalised</a> person is from a one-surname culture, the actual surname is duplicated;<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> therefore, the English name "George Albert Duran" becomes the Spanish name "George Albert Duran Duran", yet the law optionally allows him to adopt his mother's <a href="/wiki/Maiden_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Maiden name">maiden name</a> (her surname) as his maternal (second) surname. Formally, Spanish naming customs conflate his name "George" and his middle-name "Albert" to the composite name "George Albert", and his sole surname, "Duran", is duplicated as his paternal and maternal surnames.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Flamenco_artists">Flamenco artists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Flamenco artists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Historically, <a href="/wiki/Flamenco" title="Flamenco">flamenco</a> artists seldom used their proper names. According to the flamenco guitarist <a href="/wiki/Juan_Serrano_(Flamenco)" class="mw-redirect" title="Juan Serrano (Flamenco)">Juan Serrano</a>, this was because flamenco was considered disreputable and they did not want to embarrass their families:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>We have to start with the history of the gypsies in Spain. They gained a bad reputation because of the minor crimes they had to commit to survive. They did not have any kind of jobs, they had to do something to live, and of course this created hostility. And Flamenco was the music of the Gypsies, so many high society people did not accept it – they said Flamenco was in the hands of criminals, bandits, et cetera. And the girls, that maybe liked dancing or singing, their parents said, "Oh no, you want to be a prostitute!".</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite>Juan Serrano, interview in <i>Guitar International</i>, Nov 1987</cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>This tradition has persisted to the present day, even though Flamenco is now legitimate.  Sometimes the artistic name consists of the home town appended to the first name (<a href="/wiki/Manolo_Sanl%C3%BAcar" title="Manolo Sanlúcar">Manolo Sanlúcar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ram%C3%B3n_de_Algeciras" title="Ramón de Algeciras">Ramón de Algeciras</a>); but many, perhaps most, of such names are more eccentric: Pepe de la Matrona (because his mother was a midwife); <a href="/w/index.php?title=Perico_del_Lunar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Perico del Lunar (page does not exist)">Perico del Lunar</a> (because he had a mole); <a href="/wiki/Tomatito" title="Tomatito">Tomatito</a> (son of a father known as <i>Tomate</i> (tomato) because of his red face); <a href="/wiki/Sabicas" title="Sabicas">Sabicas</a> (because of his childhood passion for green beans, from <i>niño de las habicas</i>); <a href="/wiki/Paco_de_Luc%C3%ADa" title="Paco de Lucía">Paco de Lucía</a>, born Francisco ("<a href="/wiki/Paco" title="Paco">Paco</a>") Gustavo Sánchez Gomes, was known from infancy after his Portuguese mother, Lucía Gomes (de Lucía = [son] of Lucía). And many more.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_hypocoristics_and_nicknames">Spanish hypocoristics and nicknames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Spanish hypocoristics and nicknames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many Spanish names can be shortened into <a href="/wiki/Hypocoristic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypocoristic">hypocoristic</a>, affectionate "<a href="/wiki/Baby_talk" title="Baby talk">child-talk</a>" forms using a <a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">diminutive</a> suffix, especially <b>-ito</b> and <b>-cito</b> (masculine) and <b>-ita</b> and <b>-cita</b> (feminine). Sometimes longer than the person's name, a <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nickname</a> is usually derived via <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistic</a> rules.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> However, in contrast to English use, hypocoristic names in Spanish are only used to address a person in a very familiar environment – the only exception being when the hypocoristic is an artistic name (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Nacho_Duato" title="Nacho Duato">Nacho Duato</a> born Juan Ignacio Duato). The common English practice of using a nickname in the press or media, or even on business cards (such as <a href="/wiki/Bill_Gates" title="Bill Gates">Bill Gates</a> instead of William Gates), is not accepted in Spanish, being considered excessively colloquial. <h2><span id="Spain.27s_other_languages"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Spain's_other_languages">Spain's other languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Spain&#039;s other languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The official recognition of Spain's <a href="/wiki/Regional_language" title="Regional language">other written languages</a> – <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">Catalan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">Basque</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">Galician</a> – legally allowed the autonomous communities to re-establish their vernacular <a href="/wiki/Identity_(social_science)" title="Identity (social science)">social identity</a>, including the legal use of personal names in the local languages and written traditions – banned since 1938<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> – sometimes via the re-spelling of names from Castilian Spanish to their original languages.
</p>

<p>The Basque-speaking territories (the <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">Basque Autonomous Community</a> and <a href="/wiki/Navarre" title="Navarre">Navarre</a>) usually follow Spanish naming customs (given names + two family names, the two family names being usually the father's and the mother's).
</p><p>The given names are officially in one language (Basque or Spanish) but often people use a translated or shortened version. A bilingual Basque-Spanish speaker will not necessarily bear a Basque name, and a monolingual Spanish speaker can use a Basque name or a Basque hypocoristic of an official Spanish name; e.g. a <i>Francisco</i> (official Spanish name) may be known as <i>Patxi</i> (Basque hypocoristic).
</p><p>Some Basque-language names and surnames are foreign transliterations into the Basque tongue, e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Ander" title="Ander">Ander</a></i> (English: "<a href="/wiki/Andrew" title="Andrew">Andrew</a>"; Spanish: <i>Andrés</i>), <i>Mikel</i> (English: "Michael"; Spanish: <i>Miguel</i>), or <i>Ane</i> (English: "<a href="/wiki/Anne" title="Anne">Anne</a>"; Spanish: <i>Ana</i>). In some cases, the name's original-language denotation is translated to Basque, e.g., <i>Zutoia</i> and <i>Zedarri</i> denote the Spanish <i><a href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of_the_Pillar" title="Our Lady of the Pillar">Pilar</a></i> (English: "Pillar"). Moreover, some originally Basque names, such as <i>Xabier</i> and <i>Eneko</i> (English "<a href="/wiki/Xavier_(given_name)" title="Xavier (given name)">Xavier</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Inigo" title="Inigo">Inigo</a>") have been transliterated into Spanish (<i><a href="/wiki/Javier_(name)" title="Javier (name)">Javier</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%8D%C3%B1igo" class="mw-redirect" title="Íñigo">Íñigo</a></i>).
</p><p>Recently, Basque names without a direct equivalent in other languages have become popular, e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Aitor" title="Aitor">Aitor</a></i> (a legendary patriarch), <i>Hodei</i> ("cloud"), <i><a href="/wiki/Iker" title="Iker">Iker</a></i> ("to investigate"), and <i><a href="/wiki/Amaia" class="mw-redirect" title="Amaia">Amaia</a></i> ("the end"). Some Basque names without a direct Spanish meaning, are unique to the Basque language, for instance, <i>Eneko</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Garikoitz" class="mw-redirect" title="Garikoitz">Garikoitz</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Urtzi" title="Urtzi">Urtzi</a></i>. Basque names, rather than Spanish names, are preponderant<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> in the Basque Country, countering the Spanish-name imposition of the Franco régime requiring people being given only Spanish names at birth. After Franco's death and the restoration of democracy in Spain, many Basque adults changed their Spanish names to the Basque equivalent, e.g. from <i>Miguel</i> to <i>Mikel</i>.
</p><p>A source for modern Basque names is <a href="/wiki/Sabino_Arana" title="Sabino Arana">Sabino Arana</a>'s <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deun-Ixendegi_Euzkotarra&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Deun-Ixendegi Euzkotarra (page does not exist)">Deun-Ixendegi Euzkotarra</a></i> ("Basque saint-name collection", published in 1910). Instead of the traditional Basque adaptations of Romance names, he proposed others he made up and that in his opinion were truer to the originals and adapted better to the Basque phonology. For example, <a href="/wiki/Luis_Arana" title="Luis Arana">his brother Luis</a> became <i>Koldobika</i>, from Frankish <i>Hlodwig</i>. The traditionals <i>Peru</i> (from Spanish "<a href="/wiki/Pedro" title="Pedro">Pedro</a>"), <i>Pello</i> or <i>Piarres</i> (from French "<a href="/wiki/Pierre" title="Pierre">Pierre</a>"), all meaning "<a href="/wiki/Peter_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Peter (name)">Peter</a>", became <i>Kepa</i> from Aramaic כיפא (Kepha). He believed that the suffix <i>-[n]e</i> was inherently feminine, and new names like <i>Nekane</i> ("pain"+<i>ne</i>,"<a href="/wiki/Dolores_(given_name)" title="Dolores (given name)">Dolores</a>") or <i>Garbiñe</i> ("clean"+<i>ne</i>, "<a href="/wiki/Immaculate_Conception" title="Immaculate Conception">Immaculate [Conception]</a>") are frequent among Basque females.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Basque_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="Basque surname">Basque surnames</a> usually denote the patronymic house of the bearer; e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Etxebarria" title="Etxebarria">Etxebarria</a></i> – "the new house", from <i>etxe</i> (house) + <i>barri</i> (new) + <i>a</i> (the), denotes "related to a so-named farmhouse"; in the same way, <i>Garaikoetxea</i> – "the house in the heights", <i>garai</i> ("height") + <i>etxe</i> ("house") + <i>a</i> (the). Sometimes, surnames denote not the house itself but a characteristic of the place, e.g. <i>Saratxaga</i> – "willow-place", from <i>saratze</i> ("willow") + <i>-aga</i> ("place of"); <i>Loyola</i>, from <i>loi</i> ("mud") + <i>ola</i> ("iron smithery"); <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arriortua&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Arriortua (page does not exist)">Arriortua</a></i> – "stone orchard", from <i>harri</i> ("stone") + <i>ortua</i> ("orchard"). Before the 20th century all Basque men were considered <a href="/wiki/Hidalgo_(Spanish_nobility)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hidalgo (Spanish nobility)">nobles</a> (indeed, some Basque surnames, e.g. <i>Irujo</i> or <i>Medoza</i>, were related to some of the oldest Spanish noble families), and many of them used their status to emigrate with privileges to other regions of the Spanish Empire, especially the Americas, due to which some Basque surnames became common to the Spanish-American world; e.g. <i>Mendoza</i> – "cold mountain", from <i>mendi</i> ("mountain" + <i>hotza</i> ("cold"); <i><a href="/wiki/Salazar_(surname)" title="Salazar (surname)">Salazar</a></i> – "Old hall", from <i>sala</i> ("hall") + <i>zahar</i> ("old"). Until 1978, Spanish was the single official language of the Spanish civil registries and Basque surnames had to be registered according to the Spanish phonetical rules (for example, the Spanish "ch" sound merges the Basque "ts", "tx", and "tz", and someone whose surname in <a href="/wiki/Standard_Basque" title="Standard Basque">Standard Basque</a> would be "Krutxaga" would have to write it as "Cruchaga", letter "k" also not being used in Spanish). Although the democratic restoration ended this policy, allowing surnames to be officially changed into their Basque phonology, there still are many people who hold Spanish-written Basque surnames, even in the same family: a father born before 1978 would be surnamed "Echepare" and his children, "Etxepare". This policy even changed the usual pronunciation of some Basque surnames. For instance, in Basque, the letter "z" maintained a sibilant "s"-like sound, while Spanish changed it; thus, a surname such as <i>"Zabala"</i> should be properly read similar to "sabala" (<small>Basque pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Basque" title="Help:IPA/Basque">[s̻abala]</a></span>), although in Spanish, because the "z" denotes a "th" sound (<span class="IPA nowrap" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">&#91;<a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_fricative" title="Voiceless dental fricative">θ</a>&#93;</span>), it would be read as "Tha-bala" (<small>Spanish pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">[θaˈβala]</a></span>). However, since the letter "z" exists in Spanish, the registries did not force the Zabalas to transliterate their surname.
</p><p>In the Basque provinces of <a href="/wiki/Biscay" title="Biscay">Biscay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gipuzkoa" title="Gipuzkoa">Gipuzkoa</a>, it was uncommon to take a surname from the place (town or village) where one resided, unless one was a foundling; in general, people bearing surnames such as <i>Bilbao</i> (after the Basque city of <a href="/wiki/Bilbao" title="Bilbao">Bilbao</a>) are descendants of foundlings. However, in the Basque province of <a href="/wiki/Alava" class="mw-redirect" title="Alava">Alava</a> and, to a lesser extent, in <a href="/wiki/Navarre" title="Navarre">Navarre</a>, it was common to add one's birth village to the surname using the Spanish particle <i>de</i> to denote a <i>toponymic</i>, particularly when the surname was a common one; for instance, someone whose surname was <i>Lopez</i> and whose family was originally from the valley of <i>Ayala</i> could employ <i>Lopez de Ayala</i> as a surname. This latter practice is also common in <a href="/wiki/Castile_(historical_region)" title="Castile (historical region)">Castile</a>.
</p><p>Basque compound surnames are relatively common, and were created with two discrete surnames, e.g. <i>Elorduizapaterietxe</i> – <i>Elordui</i> + <i>Zapaterietxe</i>, a practice denoting family allegiances or the equal importance of both families. This custom sometimes conduced to incredibly long surnames, for compound surnames could be used to create others; for example, the longest surname recorded in Spain is Basque, <i>Burionagonatotoricagageazcoechea</i>, formed by <i>Buriona</i>+ <i>Gonatar</i> + <i>Totorika</i> + <i>Beazcoetxea</i>.
</p><p>Finally, the nationalist leader Sabino Arana pioneered a naming custom of transposing the name-surname order to what he thought was the proper Basque language syntax order; e.g. the woman named <i>Miren Zabala</i> would be referred to as <i>Zabalatar Miren</i> – the surname first, plus the <i>-tar</i> suffix denoting "from a place", and then the name. Thus, <i>Zabalatar Miren</i> means "Miren, of the Zabala family". The change in the order is effected because in the Basque tongue, declined words (such as <i>Zabalatar</i>) that apply to a noun are uttered before the noun itself; another example of this would be his pen name, <i>Arana ta Goiritar Sabin</i>. This Basque naming custom was used in nationalist literature, not in formal, official documents wherein the Castilian naming convention is observed.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Catalan_names">Catalan names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Catalan names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Category:Catalan-language_surnames" title="Category:Catalan-language surnames">Category:Catalan-language surnames</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">Catalan</a>-speaking territories also abide by the Spanish naming customs, yet usually the discrete surnames are joined with the word <i>i</i> ("and"), instead of the Spanish <i>y</i>, and this practice is very common in formal contexts. For example, the former <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Generalitat_de_Catalunya" class="mw-redirect" title="President of the Generalitat de Catalunya">president of the Generalitat de Catalunya</a> (Government of Catalonia) is formally called <i>El Molt Honorable Senyor <a href="/wiki/Carles_Puigdemont_i_Casamaj%C3%B3" class="mw-redirect" title="Carles Puigdemont i Casamajó">Carles Puigdemont i Casamajó</a></i>. Furthermore, the national language policy enumerated in article 19.1 of Law 1/1998 stipulates that "the citizens of Catalonia have the right to use the proper regulation of their Catalan names and surnames and to introduce the conjunction between surnames".
</p><p>The correction, translation, and surname-change are regulated by the <i>Registro Civil</i> (Civil Registry) with the Decree 138/2007 of 26 June, modifying the Decree 208/1998 of 30 July, which regulates the accreditation of the <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">linguistic</a> correctness of names. The attributes and functions of Decree 138/2007 of 26 July regulate the issuance of language-correction certificates for <a href="/wiki/Translation" title="Translation">translated</a> Catalan names, by the <i> <a href="/wiki/Institut_d%27Estudis_Catalans" class="mw-redirect" title="Institut d&#39;Estudis Catalans">Institut d'Estudis Catalans</a></i> (Institute of Catalan Studies) in Barcelona. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Galician_names">Galician names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Galician names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">Galician</a>-speaking areas also abide by the Spanish naming customs. Main differences are the usage of Galician given names and surnames.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Galician_surnames">Galician surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Galician surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Category:Galician-language_surnames" title="Category:Galician-language surnames">Category:Galician-language surnames</a></div>
<p>Most Galician surnames have their origin in local <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">toponymies</a>, being these either Galician regions (<b>Salnés</b> &lt; <a href="/wiki/Saln%C3%A9s" class="mw-redirect" title="Salnés">Salnés</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carnota" title="Carnota">Carnota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Berganti%C3%B1os" title="Bergantiños">Bergantiños</a>), towns (<a href="/wiki/Ferrol,_Galicia" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferrol, Galicia">Ferrol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Noia" title="Noia">Noia</a>), parishes or villages (as <a href="/wiki/Andrade" title="Andrade">Andrade</a>). Just like elsewhere, many surnames were also generated from jobs or professions (<b>Carpinteiro</b> 'carpenter', <b>Cabaleiro</b> 'Knight', <b>Ferreiro</b> 'Smith', <b>Besteiro</b> 'Crossbowman'), physical characteristics (<b>Gago</b> 'Twangy', <b>Tato</b> 'Stutterer', <b>Couceiro</b> 'Tall and thin', <b>Bugallo</b> 'fat', <b>Pardo</b> 'Swarthy'), or origin of the person (<b>Franco</b> and <b>Francés</b> 'French', <b>Portugués</b> 'Portuguese').
</p><p>Although many Galician surnames have been historically adapted into Spanish phonetics and orthography, they are still clearly recognizable as Galician words: <b>Freijedo</b>, Spanish adaptation of <i>freixedo</i> 'place with ash-trees'; <b>Seijo</b> from <i>seixo</i> 'stone'; <b>Doval</b> from <i>do Val</i> 'of the Valley'; <b>Rejenjo</b> from <i>Reguengo</i>, Galician evolution of local Latin-Germanic word <i>Regalingo</i> 'Royal property'.
</p><p>Specially relevant are the Galician surnames originated from medieval <a href="/wiki/Patronymics" class="mw-redirect" title="Patronymics">patronymics</a>, present in local documentation since the 9th century, and popularized from the 12th century on. Although many of them have been historically adapted into Spanish orthography,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> phonetics and traditions, many are still characteristically Galician; most common ones are:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Alonso</b> (medieval form <i>Afonso</i>, from the latinicised Germanic name <i>Adefonsus</i>): Spanish 'Alfonso', 'Alonso'.</li>
<li><b>Álvarez</b> (from médieval Alvares, from the Germanic name <i>Halvar(d)</i>, latinicised as <i>Alvarus</i>).</li>
<li><b>Ares</b> (from the name <i>Arias' or the town of <a href="/wiki/Ares" title="Ares">Ares</a>): Spanish 'Arias'.</i></li>
<li><b>Bermúdez</b> (medieval form <i>Vermues</i>, from the latinicised Germanic name <i>Veremodus</i> + suffix -ici-).</li>
<li><b>Bernárdez</b> (from the Frankish name <i>Bernard</i> + suffix -ici-).</li>
<li><b>Vieitez, Vieites</b> (from the name <i>Bieito</i>, from Latin <i>Benedictus</i> + suffix -ici-): Spanish 'Benítez'.</li>
<li><b>Diz, Díaz</b> (from the name Didacus + suffix -ici-): Spanish 'Díaz'.</li>
<li><b>Domínguez</b> (medieval form <i>Domingues</i>, derived of the name <i>Domingo</i>, from Dominicus, + suffix -ici-).</li>
<li><b>Enríquez</b> (medieval form <i>Anrriques</i>, from the Frankish name <i>Henric</i> + suffiz -ici-).</li>

<p>Some of them (namely Páez, Méndez, Vázquez) are characteristically Galician due to the drop of intervocalic -l-, -d-, -g- and -n-, but the most present surnames in Galicia could also be of Spanish origin (although <a href="/wiki/Lugo" title="Lugo">Lugo</a> is the only province in Spain with a majority of people surnamed <i>López</i>).
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ceuta_and_Melilla">Ceuta and Melilla</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Ceuta and Melilla">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As the provincial <i>Surname distribution</i> map (above) indicates, <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_(name)" title="Muhammad (name)">Mohamed</a> is an often-occurring <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> in the autonomous Mediterranean North African cities of <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">Ceuta</a> and <a href="/wiki/Melilla" title="Melilla">Melilla</a> (respectively registered 10,410 and 7,982 occurrences),<sup id="cite_ref-INE_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-INE-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Hispanophone</a> Muslims use the Spanish "Mohamed" spelling for "<a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>". As such, it is often a component of <a href="/wiki/Arabic_name" title="Arabic name">Arabic names</a> for men; hence, many Ceutan and Melillan Muslims share surnames despite not sharing a common ancestry. Furthermore, <i>Mohamed</i> (Muhammad) is the most popular name for new-born boys,<sup id="cite_ref-INEExcel_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-INEExcel-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> thus it is not unusual to encounter a man named <i>Mohamed Mohamed Mohamed</i>: the first occurrence is the given name, the second occurrence is the paternal surname, and the third occurrence is the maternal surname.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indexing">Indexing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Indexing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In English, the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Manual_of_Style" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago Manual of Style">Chicago Manual of Style</a> recommends that Spanish and Hispanophone names be <a href="/wiki/Subject_indexing" title="Subject indexing">indexed</a> by the family name. When there are two family names, the indexing is done under the father's family name; this would be the first element of the surname if the father's and mother's or husband's family names are joined by a <i>y</i>. Depending upon the person involved, the particle <i>de</i> may be treated as a part of a family name or it may be separated from a family name. The indexing of Hispanophone names differs from that of Portuguese or <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_name" title="Portuguese name">Lusophone names</a>, where the final element of the name is indexed because the Portuguese custom is for the father's surname to follow, rather than precede, the mother's. The effect is that the father's surname is the one indexed both for Spanish and Portuguese names.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
