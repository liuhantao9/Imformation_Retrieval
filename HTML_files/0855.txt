<p>The <b>John F. Kennedy Eternal Flame</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Presidential_memorials_in_the_United_States" title="Presidential memorials in the United States">presidential memorial</a> at the gravesite of <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>, 35th <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">president</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Arlington_National_Cemetery" title="Arlington National Cemetery">Arlington National Cemetery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a>. This permanent site replaced a temporary grave and <a href="/wiki/Eternal_flame" title="Eternal flame">eternal flame</a> used at the time of President Kennedy's <a href="/wiki/State_funeral_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="State funeral of John F. Kennedy">state funeral</a> on November 25, 1963, three days after <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="Assassination of John F. Kennedy">his assassination</a>. The site was designed by architect <a href="/wiki/John_Carl_Warnecke" title="John Carl Warnecke">John Carl Warnecke</a>, a long-time friend of the president.<sup id="cite_ref-WPObit_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WPObit-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Obit_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Obit-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The permanent John F. Kennedy Eternal Flame grave site was consecrated and opened to the public on March 15, 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-LevyMoved_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LevyMoved-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Original_grave_site">Original grave site</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Eternal_Flame&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Original grave site">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>President Kennedy was <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="Assassination of John F. Kennedy">assassinated</a> on November 22, 1963 in <a href="/wiki/Dallas" title="Dallas">Dallas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">Texas</a>, while there on a campaign trip. His body was brought back to Washington D.C. soon after his death. <a href="/wiki/List_of_dignitaries_at_the_state_funeral_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="List of dignitaries at the state funeral of John F. Kennedy">Dignitaries from 92 countries</a> attended his <a href="/wiki/State_funeral_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="State funeral of John F. Kennedy">state funeral</a> three days later, on November 25.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JFK_Funeral_and_temporary_grave_November_25_1963.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/JFK_Funeral_and_temporary_grave_November_25_1963.jpg/210px-JFK_Funeral_and_temporary_grave_November_25_1963.jpg" decoding="async" width="210" height="216" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/JFK_Funeral_and_temporary_grave_November_25_1963.jpg/315px-JFK_Funeral_and_temporary_grave_November_25_1963.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/JFK_Funeral_and_temporary_grave_November_25_1963.jpg/420px-JFK_Funeral_and_temporary_grave_November_25_1963.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2885" data-file-height="2971" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JFK_Funeral_and_temporary_grave_November_25_1963.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Jacqueline_Kennedy_Onassis" title="Jacqueline Kennedy Onassis">Jacqueline Kennedy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a> walk away from the president's casket after lighting the Eternal Flame, November 25, 1963</div></div></div>
<p>Initial press reports indicated that President Kennedy would be buried at <a href="/wiki/Holyhood_Cemetery" title="Holyhood Cemetery">Holyhood Cemetery</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brookline,_Massachusetts" title="Brookline, Massachusetts">Brookline, Massachusetts</a>, where his son <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Bouvier_Kennedy" title="Patrick Bouvier Kennedy">Patrick Bouvier Kennedy</a> (who had died on August 9, 1963, two days after his premature birth) was buried.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> But the site for the president's grave was quickly changed to the hillside just below <a href="/wiki/Arlington_House,_The_Robert_E._Lee_Memorial" title="Arlington House, The Robert E. Lee Memorial">Arlington House</a> in <a href="/wiki/Arlington_National_Cemetery" title="Arlington National Cemetery">Arlington National Cemetery</a>. The site was chosen because Kennedy and his friend, architect John Carl Warnecke, happened to visit the site in March 1963 and the President had admired the peaceful atmosphere of the location.<sup id="cite_ref-Hamblin_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hamblin-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AIA334_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIA334-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The initial suggestion to bury Kennedy at Arlington appears to have been made by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">Secretary of Defense</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_McNamara" title="Robert McNamara">Robert McNamara</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hilty_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hilty-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Johnston_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnston-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/First_Lady_of_the_United_States" title="First Lady of the United States">First Lady</a> <a href="/wiki/Jacqueline_Kennedy_Onassis" title="Jacqueline Kennedy Onassis">Jacqueline Kennedy</a> agreed to the change.<sup id="cite_ref-Hilty_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hilty-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Although Kennedy's sisters and many of his long-time associates from <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> were opposed to burial at Arlington, his brother, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Attorney_General" title="United States Attorney General">Attorney General</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a> visited the site with McNamara on Saturday, November 23, and concluded that Jacqueline Kennedy's wishes should be honored.<sup id="cite_ref-Hilty_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hilty-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Johnston_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnston-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On Sunday, November 24, 1963, Jacqueline Kennedy requested an <a href="/wiki/Eternal_flame" title="Eternal flame">eternal flame</a> for her husband's grave.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bugliosi502_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bugliosi502-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> According to several published accounts, she drew inspiration from a number of sources. One was the eternal flame at the <a href="/wiki/Tomb_of_the_Unknown_Soldier" title="Tomb of the Unknown Soldier">Tomb of the Unknown Soldier</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Arc_de_Triomphe" title="Arc de Triomphe">Arc de Triomphe</a> in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a>, which she and her husband had seen during a visit to <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> in 1961.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> She also took inspiration from the novel <i>The Candle in the Wind</i> (the fourth book from the collection <i><a href="/wiki/The_Once_and_Future_King" title="The Once and Future King">The Once and Future King</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/T._H._White" title="T. H. White">T. H. White</a>), which was part of the inspiration for the 1960 <a href="/wiki/Musical_theatre" title="Musical theatre">stage musical</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Camelot_(musical)" title="Camelot (musical)">Camelot</a></i> (the <a href="/wiki/Cast_recording" title="Cast recording">cast recording</a> was a favorite of the Kennedys).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Matthews_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Matthews-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Her brother-in-law, <a href="/wiki/Sargent_Shriver" title="Sargent Shriver">Sargent Shriver</a>, counseled against an eternal flame, worried that it might appear ostentatious or that it would compete with other such memorials at Arlington National Cemetery;<sup id="cite_ref-Matthews_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Matthews-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> but she remained adamant.<sup id="cite_ref-Matthews_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Matthews-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The president's funeral was set for Monday, November 25, which left very little time to manufacture and install an eternal flame. Overnight, <a href="/wiki/Colonel_(United_States)" title="Colonel (United States)">Colonel</a> Clayton B. Lyle and a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Corps_of_Engineers" title="United States Army Corps of Engineers">United States Army Corps of Engineers</a> team built the eternal flame: A <a href="/wiki/Propane" title="Propane">propane</a> gas-fueled <a href="/wiki/Tiki_culture" title="Tiki culture">tiki</a> <a href="/wiki/Torch" title="Torch">torch</a> was procured from the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington Gas">Washington Gas and Light Company</a>, tested, and slightly modified for emplacement.<sup id="cite_ref-Bugliosi502_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bugliosi502-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Stockland_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stockland-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-JackAssignsPlot_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JackAssignsPlot-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Pearson_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The Corps also installed a gas line to a propane tank 200 yards (180&#160;m) away to feed the torch.<sup id="cite_ref-Stockland_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stockland-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> A mound of <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreens</a> was placed around the base of the flame to cover the tubing and torch mechanism, and the head of the grave dug in front of the flame.
</p><p>The grave was set in a plot of grass roughly 5 yards (4.6&#160;m) on each side.<sup id="cite_ref-Fence_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fence-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> The site was about halfway up the hill on which Arlington House stands.<sup id="cite_ref-Fence_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fence-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wainwright_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wainwright-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The grave was placed so that it had a view of the <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Memorial" title="Lincoln Memorial">Lincoln Memorial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington_Monument" title="Washington Monument">Washington Monument</a>, and was aligned with them.<sup id="cite_ref-Johnston_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Johnston-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Jacqueline Kennedy lit a taper from a candle held by a nearby soldier, and then brought the eternal flame to life at the end of the burial service.<sup id="cite_ref-Bzdek_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bzdek-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The president's brothers, Robert and <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Ted</a>, symbolically lit the flame after her.<sup id="cite_ref-Hilty_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hilty-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Bzdek_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bzdek-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;b&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On the evening of November 26, the site was surrounded by a white picket fence.<sup id="cite_ref-Fence_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fence-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Wainwright_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wainwright-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The fencing covered an expanded area 30 feet (9.1&#160;m) long by 20 feet (6.1&#160;m) wide.<sup id="cite_ref-Wainwright_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wainwright-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The enlarged site was due to Jacqueline Kennedy's desire to have her deceased children, Patrick and Arabella (a stillborn daughter born in 1956), reinterred next to their father.<sup id="cite_ref-JackAssignsPlot_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JackAssignsPlot-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> She had read that in 1865, President <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a> had been buried next to his deceased son, [[William Wallace Lincoln|Willie], and she recalled her husband's desire to be buried with his family.<sup id="cite_ref-Pottker_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pottker-34">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> A small white cross was placed at the head of Arabella's grave, and a small white headstone placed at the head of Patrick's.<sup id="cite_ref-Wainwright_29-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wainwright-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the funeral, flowers were laid on the hillside above the gravesite.<sup id="cite_ref-Fence_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fence-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> After the erection of the fence, flowers were placed inside the enclosure, leaning against the uphill side of the fence.<sup id="cite_ref-Fence_21-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fence-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> A canvas-covered circular wooden walkway was built from Sheridan Drive to the grave site to give members of the public access to the grave.<sup id="cite_ref-Wainwright_29-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wainwright-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_a_permanent_gravesite">Development of a permanent gravesite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Eternal_Flame&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Development of a permanent gravesite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Robert_E._Lee%27s_onetime_home,_Arlington_House,_looms_above_the_Kennedy_Family_gravesite_at_Arlington_National_Cemetery_LCCN2011633148.tif" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Robert_E._Lee%27s_onetime_home%2C_Arlington_House%2C_looms_above_the_Kennedy_Family_gravesite_at_Arlington_National_Cemetery_LCCN2011633148.tif/lossy-page1-210px-Robert_E._Lee%27s_onetime_home%2C_Arlington_House%2C_looms_above_the_Kennedy_Family_gravesite_at_Arlington_National_Cemetery_LCCN2011633148.tif.jpg" decoding="async" width="210" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Robert_E._Lee%27s_onetime_home%2C_Arlington_House%2C_looms_above_the_Kennedy_Family_gravesite_at_Arlington_National_Cemetery_LCCN2011633148.tif/lossy-page1-315px-Robert_E._Lee%27s_onetime_home%2C_Arlington_House%2C_looms_above_the_Kennedy_Family_gravesite_at_Arlington_National_Cemetery_LCCN2011633148.tif.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Robert_E._Lee%27s_onetime_home%2C_Arlington_House%2C_looms_above_the_Kennedy_Family_gravesite_at_Arlington_National_Cemetery_LCCN2011633148.tif/lossy-page1-420px-Robert_E._Lee%27s_onetime_home%2C_Arlington_House%2C_looms_above_the_Kennedy_Family_gravesite_at_Arlington_National_Cemetery_LCCN2011633148.tif.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6947" data-file-height="5432" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Robert_E._Lee%27s_onetime_home,_Arlington_House,_looms_above_the_Kennedy_Family_gravesite_at_Arlington_National_Cemetery_LCCN2011633148.tif" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View of <a href="/wiki/Arlington_House,_The_Robert_E._Lee_Memorial" title="Arlington House, The Robert E. Lee Memorial">Arlington House</a> from the Kennedy grave site</div></div></div>
<p>John Carl Warnecke, a friend of the Kennedys, visited the grave with Jacqueline Kennedy and Robert F. Kennedy on November 28, to discuss themes and plans for a permanent memorial.<sup id="cite_ref-JackAssignsPlot_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JackAssignsPlot-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Clopton_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clopton-35">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> The following day, Warnecke was chosen by now former-first lady to design the president's tomb.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Clopton_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clopton-35">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Warnecke immediately concluded that the permanent grave must be simple and must incorporate the eternal flame.<sup id="cite_ref-JackAssignsPlot_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JackAssignsPlot-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Clopton_35-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clopton-35">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> A few days later, Warnecke agreed that, although it was not required, he would submit the design for the permanent Kennedy grave site to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Commission_of_Fine_Arts" title="United States Commission of Fine Arts">U.S. Commission on Fine Arts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-JackAssignsPlot_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JackAssignsPlot-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Initially, there was some concern that an eternal flame might not be approved by the cemetery. The Army Corps of Engineers was studying the installation of a permanent flame just a week after Kennedy's burial.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> But the Army was also considering removing the flame, as no such memorials were permitted in Arlington National Cemetery.<sup id="cite_ref-Tacitly_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tacitly-38">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> On December 3, 1963, the Army concluded that the Kennedy plot was not part of the official burial section of Arlington National Cemetery, and agreed to continue to allow an eternal flame.<sup id="cite_ref-Tacitly_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tacitly-38">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The U.S. government formally set aside a 3 acres (1.2&#160;ha) site surrounding the President's grave on December 5, 1963.<sup id="cite_ref-JackAssignsPlot_18-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JackAssignsPlot-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The grave design process was placed under tight secrecy.<sup id="cite_ref-AtOdds_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AtOdds-39">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-KennedyTomb_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KennedyTomb-40">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonTomb_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonTomb-41">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> An extensive research project was conducted in which hundreds of famous tombs (such as the <a href="/wiki/Mausoleum_of_Halicarnassus" class="mw-redirect" title="Mausoleum of Halicarnassus">Mausoleum of Halicarnassus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grant%27s_Tomb" title="Grant&#39;s Tomb">Grant's Tomb</a>) as well as all existing presidential burial sites were documented and images of them collected.<sup id="cite_ref-EckardtCombines_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EckardtCombines-42">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Warnecke discussed design concepts with more than 40 architects, sculptors, painters, <a href="/wiki/Landscape_architect" title="Landscape architect">landscape architects</a>, stonemasons, calligraphers, and <a href="/wiki/Liturgy" title="Liturgy">liturgical</a> experts<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CongressGets_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CongressGets-43">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>—including the sculptor <a href="/wiki/Isamu_Noguchi" title="Isamu Noguchi">Isamu Noguchi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Architectural_model" title="Architectural model">architectural model</a> maker Theodore Conrad, and the U.S. Commission on Fine Arts.<sup id="cite_ref-AtOdds_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AtOdds-39">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-KennedyTomb_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KennedyTomb-40">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Noguchi counseled Warnecke to add a large sculptural cross to the site and to eliminate the eternal flame (which he felt was <a href="/wiki/Kitsch" title="Kitsch">kitschy</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-AtOdds_39-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AtOdds-39">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Warnecke consulted with Jacqueline Kennedy about the design of the grave many times over the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-AIA334_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIA334-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Hundreds of architectural drawings and models were produced to explore design ideas.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CongressGets_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CongressGets-43">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> On April 6, 1964, Warnecke sent a memorandum to her in which he outlined his desire to retain the eternal flame as the centerpiece of the burial site and to keep the site's design as simple as possible.<sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonOutlined_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonOutlined-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> In the course of the research and conceptualization effort, Warnecke considered the appropriateness of structures or memorials at the site (such as crosses, shafts, <a href="/wiki/Pavilion#Free-standing_structure" title="Pavilion">pavilions</a>, etc.), the history of Arlington National Cemetery, the vista, and how to handle ceremonies at the site.<sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonOutlined_44-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonOutlined-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> By August 1964, Warnecke and his assistants had written a 76-page research report which concluded that the gravesite was not a memorial nor monument, but a grave.<sup id="cite_ref-EckardtCombines_42-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EckardtCombines-42">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonOutlined_44-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonOutlined-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> "This particular hillside, this flame, this man and this point in history must be synthesized in one statement that has distinctive character of its own. We must avoid adding elements that in later decades might become superficial and detract from the deeds of the man," Warnecke wrote<sup id="cite_ref-EckardtCombines_42-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EckardtCombines-42">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> This conclusion drove the final design. The walkways and elliptical overlook were conceptualized very early in the design process.<sup id="cite_ref-EckardtCombines_42-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EckardtCombines-42">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Landscape architects <a href="/wiki/Hideo_Sasaki" title="Hideo Sasaki">Hideo Sasaki</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Halprin" title="Lawrence Halprin">Lawrence Halprin</a> helped design the approaches and setting, but not the grave site.<sup id="cite_ref-EckardtCombines_42-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EckardtCombines-42">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> For some time in the spring and summer of 1964, the design process appeared to slow as Warnecke and his associates struggled to design the grave site. But in the summer of 1964 <a href="/wiki/Sargent_Shriver" title="Sargent Shriver">Sargent Shriver</a>, President Kennedy's brother-in-law, forcefully told Warnecke that "There must be something there when we get there."<sup id="cite_ref-EckardtCombines_42-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EckardtCombines-42">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> This spurred the design efforts forward. In the late summer and early fall, Warnecke considered massive headstones, a <a href="/wiki/Sarcophagus" title="Sarcophagus">sarcophagus</a>, a sunken <a href="/wiki/Tomb" title="Tomb">tomb</a>, a raised tomb, and sculpture to mark the graves.<sup id="cite_ref-EckardtCombines_42-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EckardtCombines-42">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Very late in the design process, two <a href="/wiki/Abstract_art" title="Abstract art">abstract</a> sculptures were designed but ultimately rejected.<sup id="cite_ref-EckardtCombines_42-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EckardtCombines-42">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Lincoln_Memorial_and_Washington_Memorial_from_Kennedy_grave_in_Arlington_National_Cemetery.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Lincoln_Memorial_and_Washington_Memorial_from_Kennedy_grave_in_Arlington_National_Cemetery.jpg/210px-Lincoln_Memorial_and_Washington_Memorial_from_Kennedy_grave_in_Arlington_National_Cemetery.jpg" decoding="async" width="210" height="204" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Lincoln_Memorial_and_Washington_Memorial_from_Kennedy_grave_in_Arlington_National_Cemetery.jpg/315px-Lincoln_Memorial_and_Washington_Memorial_from_Kennedy_grave_in_Arlington_National_Cemetery.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Lincoln_Memorial_and_Washington_Memorial_from_Kennedy_grave_in_Arlington_National_Cemetery.jpg/420px-Lincoln_Memorial_and_Washington_Memorial_from_Kennedy_grave_in_Arlington_National_Cemetery.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2931" data-file-height="2848" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Lincoln_Memorial_and_Washington_Memorial_from_Kennedy_grave_in_Arlington_National_Cemetery.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>View from the Kennedy grave site across the <a href="/wiki/Arlington_Memorial_Bridge" title="Arlington Memorial Bridge">Arlington Memorial Bridge</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Memorial" title="Lincoln Memorial">Lincoln Memorial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington_Monument" title="Washington Monument">Washington Monument</a></div></div></div>
<p>The final design was unveiled publicly at the <a href="/wiki/National_Gallery_of_Art" title="National Gallery of Art">National Gallery of Art</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, on November 13, 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">Secretary of Defense</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_McNamara" title="Robert McNamara">Robert McNamara</a> unveiled the design, with the president's brother, Robert F. Kennedy, and sister, <a href="/wiki/Eunice_Kennedy_Shriver" title="Eunice Kennedy Shriver">Eunice Kennedy Shriver</a>, in attendance.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The final design had won the approval of the Kennedy family, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a>, the U.S. Commission on Fine Arts, and the <a href="/wiki/National_Capital_Planning_Commission" title="National Capital Planning Commission">National Capital Planning Commission</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonTomb_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonTomb-41">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Two overarching design concerns guided the design of the site. First, Warnecke intended the grave itself to reflect the early <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">New England</a> tradition of a simple <a href="/wiki/Headstone" title="Headstone">headstone</a> set flat in the ground surrounded by grass.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Second, the site was designed to reflect President Kennedy's Catholic faith.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> As originally envisioned, a circular <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">granite</a> walkway was envisioned which would create two approaches to the grave site.<sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonOutlined_44-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonOutlined-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HuxtableDilemma_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HuxtableDilemma-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The walkways were intended to overcome the steep 45-degree incline of the hill up to the burial plot.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonTomb_41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonTomb-41">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> President Kennedy was buried so that his grave faced northeast toward the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Monument" title="Washington Monument">Washington Monument</a>. The entrance to the circular walkway was from the southeast, which created a southern, shorter leg of the circular walkway.  Warnecke intended for this shorter walkway to be used by family members and dignitaries who were making private visits to the grave, while the longer walkway would not only separate the public from these <a href="/wiki/Very_Important_Person" class="mw-redirect" title="Very Important Person">VIPs</a> but also accommodate the long lines of people wishing to pay their respects.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> A small elliptical plaza (120 feet (37&#160;m) long and 50 feet (15&#160;m) wide) made of <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">marble</a> was set at the top of and inside the circle.<sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonOutlined_44-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonOutlined-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HuxtableDilemma_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HuxtableDilemma-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonFirstStones_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonFirstStones-46">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The northeastern side of the elliptical plaza would be enclosed by a low wall inscribed with quotes from Kennedy's speeches.<sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonOutlined_44-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonOutlined-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HuxtableDilemma_45-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HuxtableDilemma-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Marble steps would lead up from the plaza to a rectangular terrace 66 feet (20&#160;m) long and 42 feet (13&#160;m) wide.<sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonOutlined_44-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonOutlined-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HuxtableDilemma_45-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HuxtableDilemma-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonFirstStones_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonFirstStones-46">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Flowering <a href="/wiki/Magnolia" title="Magnolia">magnolia</a> trees would be planted on either side of the steps up to the terrace.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Centered in the terrace would be a rectangular plot of grass 30 feet (9.1&#160;m) long and 18 feet (5.5&#160;m) wide, raised slightly above the ground level, which would accommodate the graves.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonOutlined_44-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonOutlined-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HuxtableDilemma_45-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HuxtableDilemma-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonFirstStones_46-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonFirstStones-46">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Flat black slate grave markers (3 feet (0.91&#160;m) by 4.53 feet (1.38&#160;m)) would mark each grave, listing the name and date of birth and death in raised lettering.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The headstones would be set flush with the earth.<sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonOutlined_44-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonOutlined-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HuxtableDilemma_45-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HuxtableDilemma-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> A 7.5 feet (2.3&#160;m) high and 36 feet (11&#160;m) long retaining wall, inscribed with the <a href="/wiki/Seal_of_the_President_of_the_United_States" title="Seal of the President of the United States">presidential seal</a>, formed the rear of the burial site.<sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonOutlined_44-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonOutlined-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HuxtableDilemma_45-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HuxtableDilemma-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ChangesMade_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ChangesMade-47">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> The walkways, elliptical plaza, and terrace were designed to accommodate more than 50,000 visitors per day.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The eternal flame itself would be placed in the center of the grassy plot in a flat, triangular bronze sculpture intended to resemble a <a href="/wiki/Votive_candle" title="Votive candle">votive candle</a> or <a href="/wiki/Brazier" title="Brazier">brazier</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonOutlined_44-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonOutlined-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HuxtableDilemma_45-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HuxtableDilemma-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Rachel_Lambert_Mellon" title="Rachel Lambert Mellon">Rachel Lambert Mellon</a> was employed to landscape the approaches with flowering trees (magnolia, <a href="/wiki/Cherry_blossom" title="Cherry blossom">cherry</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Crataegus_monogyna" title="Crataegus monogyna">hawthorn</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-EckardtCritical_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EckardtCritical-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> At the time of the design's unveiling, the quotations for the low wall had not yet been selected by Mrs. Kennedy.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The original design won near-universal praise.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction_of_the_new_gravesite">Construction of the new gravesite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Eternal_Flame&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Construction of the new gravesite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JFK_grave_site_under_construction_1965.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/JFK_grave_site_under_construction_1965.jpg/210px-JFK_grave_site_under_construction_1965.jpg" decoding="async" width="210" height="138" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/JFK_grave_site_under_construction_1965.jpg/315px-JFK_grave_site_under_construction_1965.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/JFK_grave_site_under_construction_1965.jpg/420px-JFK_grave_site_under_construction_1965.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6813" data-file-height="4486" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JFK_grave_site_under_construction_1965.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Permanent Kennedy grave site under construction in October 1965; the temporary grave is just beyond the picket fence, where the crowd has gathered.</div></div></div>
<p>The plan was for work to begin in the fall of 1965 and be completed by the fall of 1966.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The design required that the bodies of President Kennedy and his children be moved downhill about 20 feet (6.1&#160;m).<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonOutlined_44-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonOutlined-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HuxtableDilemma_45-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HuxtableDilemma-45">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonFirstStones_46-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonFirstStones-46">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> A 150-year-old oak tree, which was off-center in the circular pathway, was to be retained.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The total cost of the tomb was estimated at $2 million.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonOutlined_44-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonOutlined-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The Kennedy family offered to pay for the entire cost, but the U.S. government refused and asked them to pay only the $200,000-$300,000 cost of the grave itself.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonOutlined_44-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonOutlined-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> Most of the cost was attributed to the need to reinforce and strengthen the site to accommodate the weight of such large crowds.<sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonOutlined_44-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonOutlined-44">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> The U.S. Department of Defense formally hired Warnecke to design the approaches (although this was a <i><a href="/wiki/List_of_French_words_and_phrases_used_by_English_speakers#F" class="mw-redirect" title="List of French words and phrases used by English speakers">fait accompli</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Work on the John F. Kennedy burial site continued over the next two and a half years. The Washington Gas and Light Company offered to build, maintain, and supply gas to the eternal flame at no expense.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> The final burner was a specially designed torch created by the <a href="/wiki/Gas_Technology_Institute" title="Gas Technology Institute">Institute of Gas Technology</a> with an electrical ignition which kept the flame lit in wind or rain and which fed the gas oxygen to create the correct color.<sup id="cite_ref-Arlington_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arlington-52">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> A debate broke out between providers of bottled propane gas and line-fed natural gas as to which source of fuel should be used to supply the eternal flame. The debate was so vigorous that it broke out in public in March 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-Pearson_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pearson-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The cost of construction of the approaches, elliptical plaza, walls were estimated at $1.77 million in February 1965.<sup id="cite_ref-CongressGets_43-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CongressGets-43">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> The cost of construction of the actual grave site was estimated at $309,000.<sup id="cite_ref-CongressGets_43-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CongressGets-43">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Fifteen firms were invited to bid on the construction contract and nine did so.<sup id="cite_ref-Dewar_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dewar-53">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> A $1.4 million contract for construction was awarded to Aberthaw Construction in mid-July 1965.<sup id="cite_ref-Dewar_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dewar-53">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> The Army Corps of Engineers consulted with the Kennedy family before letting the award.<sup id="cite_ref-Dewar_53-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dewar-53">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> A second contract for structural design consulting in the amount of $71,026 went to <a href="/wiki/Ammann_%26_Whitney" title="Ammann &amp; Whitney">Ammann &amp; Whitney</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Arlington_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Arlington-52">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> At this time, contracts for the quotation inscriptions, the marble base for the flame, the bronze brazier, and the slate markers had yet to be let.<sup id="cite_ref-Dewar_53-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dewar-53">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> The white marble for the plaza, terrace, and steps came from <a href="/wiki/Proctor,_Vermont" title="Proctor, Vermont">Proctor, Vermont</a>, and the granite for the approaches came from <a href="/wiki/Deer_Isle,_Maine" title="Deer Isle, Maine">Deer Isle, Maine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Dewar_53-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dewar-53">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Hunter_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter-54">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Prior to construction, several design changes were made to the Kennedy grave site. The retaining wall behind the grave was removed, and the hill landscaped to allow an unobstructed view of Arlington House.<sup id="cite_ref-ChangesMade_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ChangesMade-47">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EckardtCritical_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EckardtCritical-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Concerned that the grass on the burial plot would wither in Washington's hot summers, in the fall of 1966 the decision was made to replace the grass with rough-hewn reddish-gold granite <a href="/wiki/Fieldstone" title="Fieldstone">fieldstone</a> set in a <a href="/wiki/Flagstone" title="Flagstone">flagstone</a> pattern.<sup id="cite_ref-ChangesMade_47-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ChangesMade-47">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EckardtCritical_48-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EckardtCritical-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The fieldstones used had been taken more than 150 years ago from a quarry on <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cod" title="Cape Cod">Cape Cod</a> near where President Kennedy used to spend his summers.<sup id="cite_ref-ChangesMade_47-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ChangesMade-47">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Semple_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Semple-55">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The burial plot, originally designed to be raised a substantial height above the surrounding terrace, was lowered so that it was just three to four inches higher.<sup id="cite_ref-EckardtCritical_48-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EckardtCritical-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The bronze brazier shape for the eternal flame was also replaced. Instead, a 5 feet (1.5&#160;m) wide beige circular fieldstone (found on Cape Cod in 1965) was set nearly flush with the earth and used as a bracket for the flame.<sup id="cite_ref-ChangesMade_47-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ChangesMade-47">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EckardtCritical_48-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EckardtCritical-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kennedy_Grave_Site_-_November_2005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Kennedy_Grave_Site_-_November_2005.jpg/210px-Kennedy_Grave_Site_-_November_2005.jpg" decoding="async" width="210" height="120" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Kennedy_Grave_Site_-_November_2005.jpg/315px-Kennedy_Grave_Site_-_November_2005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Kennedy_Grave_Site_-_November_2005.jpg/420px-Kennedy_Grave_Site_-_November_2005.jpg 2x" data-file-width="523" data-file-height="299" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kennedy_Grave_Site_-_November_2005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Aerial view of the John F. Kennedy grave site and Eternal Flame at Arlington National Cemetery in Arlington, Virginia, in November 2005.</div></div></div>
<p>Construction of the approaches required <a href="/wiki/Grade_(slope)" title="Grade (slope)">regrading</a> the hill. Crews were forced to work with <a href="/wiki/Pickaxe" title="Pickaxe">picks</a> and shovels around the oak tree to protect its roots while engaged in regrading work.<sup id="cite_ref-NewGravesite_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NewGravesite-56">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> The tree's roots were reinforced with concrete to provide stability to the plant, and a "breathing system" incorporated into the concrete to allow the roots to still secure nourishment.<sup id="cite_ref-NewGravesite_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NewGravesite-56">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Twenty tons of steel were used to build the terrace, and 280 tons of concrete poured to build the grave vaults and the eternal flame site.<sup id="cite_ref-NewGravesite_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NewGravesite-56">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> The first fieldstones for the graves were placed April 11, 1966.<sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonFirstStones_46-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonFirstStones-46">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> At the same time, the ground was prepared for the emplacement of the granite blocks which would form the low memorial wall on the downslope side of the elliptical plaza.<sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonFirstStones_46-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonFirstStones-46">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Jacqueline Kennedy, with assistance of Kennedy <a href="/wiki/Speechwriter" title="Speechwriter">speechwriter</a> <a href="/wiki/Ted_Sorensen" title="Ted Sorensen">Ted Sorensen</a>, selected the inscriptions for the wall by November 1965, all of which came from <a href="/wiki/Inaugural_address_of_John_F._Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Inaugural address of John F. Kennedy">Kennedy's inaugural 1961 address</a> (although some were shortened for artistic reasons).<sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonFirstStones_46-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonFirstStones-46">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NewGravesite_56-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NewGravesite-56">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Benson_(artisan)" title="John Benson (artisan)">John E. Benson</a> inscribed the quotations onto the seven granite blocks.<sup id="cite_ref-RobertsonFirstStones_46-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RobertsonFirstStones-46">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EckardtCritical_48-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EckardtCritical-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The lettering is in <a href="/wiki/Roman_square_capitals" title="Roman square capitals">Roman majuscule</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-OKeefeVisible_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OKeefeVisible-57">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> In November 1965, the contractors estimated that the site would be finished by July 1966.<sup id="cite_ref-Hunter_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter-54">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The government announced that the bodies of the President and his two children would be reburied in a private ceremony at night after cemetery had closed on the day before the site was opened to the public.<sup id="cite_ref-Hunter_54-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hunter-54">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NewGravesite_56-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NewGravesite-56">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> For a time in the fall of 1966, the Army considered <a href="/wiki/Gas-discharge_lamp#High-intensity_discharge_lamps" title="Gas-discharge lamp">floodlighting</a> the site to permit night-time ceremonies, but this plan was quickly discarded.<sup id="cite_ref-ReburialFalse_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReburialFalse-58">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> In mid-October 1966, design changes and construction delays had forced the opening of the new burial site to early 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-ReburialTrue_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReburialTrue-59">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consecration_of_the_new_grave">Consecration of the new grave</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Eternal_Flame&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Consecration of the new grave">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The permanent John F. Kennedy grave site opened with little announcement or fanfare on March 15, 1967. A few days before, the eternal flame had been moved from its temporary location to the new site.<sup id="cite_ref-Bodies_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bodies-60">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> The reburial of the bodies occurred on the evening of March 14, after Arlington National Cemetery had closed.<sup id="cite_ref-LevyMoved_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LevyMoved-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Earth over the existing grave was removed, and a small crane was used to lift the burial vault (which remained unopened) from the old grave and place it in the new one.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESelect_Committee_on_Assassinations197932_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESelect_Committee_on_Assassinations197932-61">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;c&#93;</a></sup> The event was unannounced.<sup id="cite_ref-Bodies_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bodies-60">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> The transfer was witnessed by U.S. senators Robert and Ted Kennedy, and Cardinal Richard Cushing of Boston.<sup id="cite_ref-Semple_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Semple-55">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Exhumation began at 6:19 PM and was complete at 9:00 PM.<sup id="cite_ref-Semple_55-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Semple-55">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Consecration" title="Consecration">Consecration</a> of the new burial site occurred at 7:00 AM on March 15, 1967, in a driving rain.<sup id="cite_ref-LevyMoved_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LevyMoved-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The ceremony, which took 20 minutes, was attended by President <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a>, Jacqueline Kennedy, and several members of the Kennedy family.<sup id="cite_ref-LevyMoved_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LevyMoved-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Semple_55-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Semple-55">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Cardinal Cushing presided over the consecration.<sup id="cite_ref-Semple_55-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Semple-55">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> The final cost of the entire project was $2.2 million.<sup id="cite_ref-LevyMoved_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LevyMoved-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Landscaping around the permanent site was not complete at the time of its consecration, and continued for several more weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-EckardtCritical_48-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EckardtCritical-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation_of_the_site">Operation of the site</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Eternal_Flame&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Operation of the site">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One spontaneous act of respect at the site was curbed almost immediately. Jacqueline Kennedy had requested that a member of the <a href="/wiki/Special_Forces_(United_States_Army)" class="mw-redirect" title="Special Forces (United States Army)">U.S. Army Special Forces</a> (the Green Berets) be part of the military honor squad at President Kennedy's burial service.<sup id="cite_ref-Hamblin_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hamblin-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> She specifically asked that the Special Forces soldier wear a green beret rather than formal Army headgear.<sup id="cite_ref-Hamblin_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hamblin-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> After the funeral, the six military personnel in the honor guard (Army, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">Navy</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Air_Force" title="United States Air Force">Air Force</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">Marine</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Coast_Guard" title="United States Coast Guard">Coast Guard</a>, and Special Forces) had spontaneously removed their hats and laid them on the evergreen boughs around the eternal flame.<sup id="cite_ref-EckardtCritical_48-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EckardtCritical-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Heyman_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heyman-63">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> Also laid on the greenery were the insignia of a <a href="/wiki/Military_Police_Corps_(United_States_Army)" class="mw-redirect" title="Military Police Corps (United States Army)">U.S. Army military policeman</a> and the shoulder braid from a soldier in the <a href="/wiki/3rd_US_Infantry_Regiment_(The_Old_Guard)" class="mw-redirect" title="3rd US Infantry Regiment (The Old Guard)">3rd US Infantry Regiment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EckardtCritical_48-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EckardtCritical-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Heyman_63-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heyman-63">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> The presence of the headgear was widely criticized after the dedication of the permanent grave site,<sup id="cite_ref-EckardtCritical_48-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EckardtCritical-48">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> and the U.S. Army (which administers Arlington National Cemetery) ordered all such memorabilia removed from the grave in April 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alterations_caused_by_new_burials_at_the_grave_site">Alterations caused by new burials at the grave site</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Eternal_Flame&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Alterations caused by new burials at the grave site">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Four_graves.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Four_graves.jpg/180px-Four_graves.jpg" decoding="async" width="180" height="120" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Four_graves.jpg/270px-Four_graves.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Four_graves.jpg/360px-Four_graves.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2050" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Four_graves.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Grave site as it was reconfigured after Jacqueline Kennedy's death; this image shows all four graves at the site.</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Arlington_Oak_sapling_-_official_replacement_tree_-_Arlington_National_Cemetery_-_2012-05-19.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Arlington_Oak_sapling_-_official_replacement_tree_-_Arlington_National_Cemetery_-_2012-05-19.jpg/180px-Arlington_Oak_sapling_-_official_replacement_tree_-_Arlington_National_Cemetery_-_2012-05-19.jpg" decoding="async" width="180" height="280" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Arlington_Oak_sapling_-_official_replacement_tree_-_Arlington_National_Cemetery_-_2012-05-19.jpg/270px-Arlington_Oak_sapling_-_official_replacement_tree_-_Arlington_National_Cemetery_-_2012-05-19.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Arlington_Oak_sapling_-_official_replacement_tree_-_Arlington_National_Cemetery_-_2012-05-19.jpg/360px-Arlington_Oak_sapling_-_official_replacement_tree_-_Arlington_National_Cemetery_-_2012-05-19.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1287" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Arlington_Oak_sapling_-_official_replacement_tree_-_Arlington_National_Cemetery_-_2012-05-19.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Replacement sapling of the Arlington Oak, planted in April 2012 at the Kennedy grave site.</div></div></div>
<p>Robert F. Kennedy was <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy_assassination" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert F. Kennedy assassination">assassinated</a> on June 6, 1968 in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles, California</a>. An expansion to the John F. Kennedy grave site was dedicated in 1971 to accommodate <a href="/wiki/Grave_of_Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Grave of Robert F. Kennedy">Robert Kennedy's grave</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Etched_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Etched-66">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Robert F. Kennedy's resting place is only about 50 feet (15&#160;m) southwest from the terrace at the John F. Kennedy site.<sup id="cite_ref-Reed_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reed-67">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> Robert Kennedy is buried on the upslope side of the walkway, his burial vault marked by a white cross and a slate headstone set flush with the earth.<sup id="cite_ref-Reed_67-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reed-67">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>  Opposite his grave is a granite plaza designed by architect <a href="/wiki/I._M._Pei" title="I. M. Pei">I. M. Pei</a> and dedicated on December 6, 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-Etched_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Etched-66">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> A low granite wall similar to the one at the John F. Kennedy terrace contains quotations from famous Robert F. Kennedy speeches, and a small reflecting pool.<sup id="cite_ref-Reed_67-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reed-67">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> As with his brother, Robert Kennedy's first grave was a temporary one, about 10 feet (3.0&#160;m) upslope from its current location.<sup id="cite_ref-Reed_67-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reed-67">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Kennedy grave site's approaches were altered at the time the Robert F. Kennedy memorial was built. Previously, the approach consisted of a series of long steps. But several individuals in wheelchairs appealed to Senator Edward M. Kennedy, and the steps were replaced by long ramps in June 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Jacqueline Kennedy Onassis was buried at the site alongside her husband following her death from cancer in May 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> Senator Edward M. Kennedy was buried about 100 feet (30&#160;m) south of Robert Kennedy's memorial between two maple trees shortly after his death on August 25, 2009, from brain cancer.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Arlington_Oak">The Arlington Oak</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Eternal_Flame&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Arlington Oak">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 220-year-old "Arlington Oak", which stood off-center within the Kennedy memorial gravesite area was uprooted and killed on August 27, 2011, during <a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irene_(2011)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hurricane Irene (2011)">Hurricane Irene</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-OldOak_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OldOak-72">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> The  gravesite was closed to the public for two days to remove the tree and stump, but reopened on August 30.<sup id="cite_ref-OldOak_72-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OldOak-72">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On <a href="/wiki/Arbor_Day" title="Arbor Day">Arbor Day</a>, April 27, 2012, a sapling grown from an acorn of the Arlington Oak was planted at the same site. Two other Arlington Oak saplings were planted nearby, while a fourth was planted in Section 26 near <a href="/wiki/Tanner_Amphitheater" title="Tanner Amphitheater">Tanner Amphitheater</a> and a fifth in Section 36 near Custis Walk.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maintenance">Maintenance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Eternal_Flame&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Maintenance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Workers_transfer_eternal_flame_at_JFK_gravesite_-_Arlington_National_Cemetery_-_2013-04-29.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Workers_transfer_eternal_flame_at_JFK_gravesite_-_Arlington_National_Cemetery_-_2013-04-29.JPG/180px-Workers_transfer_eternal_flame_at_JFK_gravesite_-_Arlington_National_Cemetery_-_2013-04-29.JPG" decoding="async" width="180" height="120" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Workers_transfer_eternal_flame_at_JFK_gravesite_-_Arlington_National_Cemetery_-_2013-04-29.JPG/270px-Workers_transfer_eternal_flame_at_JFK_gravesite_-_Arlington_National_Cemetery_-_2013-04-29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Workers_transfer_eternal_flame_at_JFK_gravesite_-_Arlington_National_Cemetery_-_2013-04-29.JPG/360px-Workers_transfer_eternal_flame_at_JFK_gravesite_-_Arlington_National_Cemetery_-_2013-04-29.JPG 2x" data-file-width="5000" data-file-height="3333" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Workers_transfer_eternal_flame_at_JFK_gravesite_-_Arlington_National_Cemetery_-_2013-04-29.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Transferring the eternal flame to a temporary location on April 29, 2013, for repair and upgrades to the permanent site.</div></div></div>
<p>Maintenance of the eternal flame is an ongoing issue. Arlington National Cemetery experts said in 2001 that it cost about $200 a month to keep the flame burning.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> As of 2010, Fenwal Controls, a gas equipment manufacturer based in Boston, was the contractor responsible for maintaining the flame.<sup id="cite_ref-Fenwal_75-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fenwal-75">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> The eternal flame's original, custom-manufactured ignition system was contained in a weather-proof box buried a few feet from the grave site.<sup id="cite_ref-Fenwal_75-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fenwal-75">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> The system controlled the flow of gas and oxygen to the flame and kept it lit via a high-voltage cable and 20,000-volt <a href="/wiki/Ignition_system" title="Ignition system">spark ignition</a> electrode near the gas burner.<sup id="cite_ref-Fenwal_75-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fenwal-75">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> No longer constantly sparking to keep the flame lit, the system monitored the flame and activated the ignition system only when the flame was extinguished.<sup id="cite_ref-Fenwal_75-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fenwal-75">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> The electrode was specially designed for outdoor use.<sup id="cite_ref-Fenwal_75-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fenwal-75">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> Oversight of the flame's maintenance was provided by the Army Corps of Engineers, which contracted with Fenwal Controls to ensure the eternal flame was maintained and upgraded as needed.<sup id="cite_ref-Fenwal_75-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fenwal-75">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> According to Arlington National Cemetery historian Tom Sherlock, when the gas burner required maintenance the flame was shut off.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> But maintenance needs were so minimal that no living Fenwal Controls employee had seen the interior of the ignition system.<sup id="cite_ref-Fenwal_75-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fenwal-75">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> An on-site visit was planned by Fenwal Controls for early 2010 to inspect the eternal flame and conduct a review of the ignition and burner systems.<sup id="cite_ref-Fenwal_75-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fenwal-75">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In late 2012, the eternal flame began malfunctioning. The causes of the malfunction were not known, but the automatic ignition system began clicking audibly. On January 31, 2013, Meltech Corporation of <a href="/wiki/Landover,_Maryland" title="Landover, Maryland">Landover, Maryland</a>, was awarded a $350,000 contract to upgrade the eternal flame. The work required that the permanent flame be extinguished, and a temporary flame displayed to one side. Army Corps of Engineers officials said that this was the first time a temporary flame was used since the permanent flame was installed. A solid white fence surrounded the permanent flame during the renovations, although the headstones of Kennedy and his family were not obscured.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> The flame was transferred to the upgraded permanent eternal flame on May 17, 2013, and the temporary flame extinguished. The refurbishment replaced the original 1967 burner with one that did not require a separate oxygen supply, laid new gas lines, relocated gas pressure regulators, installed automated controls to provide a more energy-efficient flame, and laid new electrical cables and replaced aging electrical conduits.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2010, the carved Kennedy quotations in the low stone wall of the plaza in front of the grave site underwent a major restoration.  Power cleaning of the site and weather had made the letters difficult to read.  The <a href="/wiki/Knights_of_Columbus" title="Knights of Columbus">Knights of Columbus</a>, a Roman Catholic fraternal service organization to which Kennedy belonged, donated $6,000 to have the letters darkened and more deeply incised in time for the 50th anniversary of <a href="/wiki/Inauguration_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="Inauguration of John F. Kennedy">Kennedy's inauguration</a>. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gordon_Ponsford&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gordon Ponsford (page does not exist)">Gordon Ponsford</a>, a sculptor who restored several major monuments at Arlington National Cemetery, performed the work.<sup id="cite_ref-OKeefeVisible_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OKeefeVisible-57">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accidental_extinguishments">Accidental extinguishments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Eternal_Flame&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Accidental extinguishments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The eternal flame has been extinguished a few times by accident.  On December 10, 1963, a group of Catholic schoolchildren were sprinkling the temporary flame with <a href="/wiki/Holy_water" title="Holy water">holy water</a>. The cap came off the bottle and water poured onto the flame, putting it out. A cemetery official quickly relit the flame by hand.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> In August 1967, an exceptionally heavy rain extinguished the permanent flame. A nearby electrical <a href="/wiki/Transformer" title="Transformer">transformer</a> flooded as well, depriving the spark igniter of power and preventing the flame from being relit. After the rain ended, a cemetery official relit the flame by hand. Two of the gravesite's flagstones had to be removed to access the transformer and repair it.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Emigrant_Flame">The Emigrant Flame</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Eternal_Flame&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The Emigrant Flame">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2013, the eternal flame was shared for the first time in its history.<sup id="cite_ref-ByrneFlame_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ByrneFlame-81">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> On June 18, a U.S. Army honor guard accompanied Irish <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_State_at_the_Department_of_Defence" title="Minister of State at the Department of Defence">Minister of State</a> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Kehoe" title="Paul Kehoe">Paul Kehoe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Teachta_D%C3%A1la" title="Teachta Dála">T.D.</a>, in a wreath-laying ceremony at the Kennedy grave site. An <a href="/wiki/Irish_Army" title="Irish Army">Irish Army</a> officer, accompanied by an Irish Army honor guard, lit a lamp in a metal burner<sup id="cite_ref-ByrneFlame_81-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ByrneFlame-81">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> from the eternal flame.<sup id="cite_ref-WalshFlame_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WalshFlame-82">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-APflame_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APflame-83">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> (The lamp and burner were created by the Bullfinch company, which also designed the torches for the <a href="/wiki/2012_Summer_Olympics_torch_relay" title="2012 Summer Olympics torch relay">2012 Summer Olympics torch relay</a>.)<sup id="cite_ref-ByrneFlame_81-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ByrneFlame-81">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> The "spark" traveled back to <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a> aboard a special <a href="/wiki/Aer_Lingus" title="Aer Lingus">Aer Lingus</a> flight, accompanied by Kehoe, Irish Army personnel, and a delegation from the <a href="/wiki/New_Ross" title="New Ross">New Ross</a> Town Council.<sup id="cite_ref-WalshFlame_82-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WalshFlame-82">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The "spark" arrived at <a href="/wiki/Dublin_Airport" title="Dublin Airport">Dublin Airport</a> on June 20, where Kehoe transferred the flame to Colonel Brendan Delaney. Delaney transferred the flame to officers of the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Service_(Ireland)" class="mw-redirect" title="Naval Service (Ireland)">Irish Naval Service</a>. The flame was taken by the Naval vessel <a href="/wiki/L%C3%89_Orla_(P41)" title="LÉ Orla (P41)">LÉ&#160;<i>Orla</i>&#160;<span class="nowrap">(P41)</span></a>,<sup id="cite_ref-RTEflame_84-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RTEflame-84">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> which traversed the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a> and sailed up the <a href="/wiki/River_Barrow" title="River Barrow">River Barrow</a> to New Ross<sup id="cite_ref-WalshFlame_82-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WalshFlame-82">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> (the town which John F. Kennedy's great-grandfather emigrated from in 1848).<sup id="cite_ref-ByrneFlame_81-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ByrneFlame-81">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> On June 22, several Irish <a href="/wiki/Special_Olympics" title="Special Olympics">Special Olympians</a> carried the flame from the <i>Orla</i> to a ceremony at the Kennedy Homestead. <a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a> <a href="/wiki/Enda_Kenny" title="Enda Kenny">Enda Kenny</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Kennedy_Smith" title="Jean Kennedy Smith">Jean Kennedy Smith</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Kennedy" title="Caroline Kennedy">Caroline Kennedy</a> used the burner to jointly light an "Emigrant Flame" in an iron globe to mark the 50th anniversary of President Kennedy's trip to Ireland. The Emigrant Flame is now the centerpiece of the expanded homestead visitor center.<sup id="cite_ref-APflame_83-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-APflame-83">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> Four days later, the flame went out, but was reignited with fire from the miner's lamps that were lit at Arlington.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_influence">Cultural influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_F._Kennedy_Eternal_Flame&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Cultural influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Immediately after Kennedy's burial, the eternal flame was being visited by 50,000 people per day.<sup id="cite_ref-VonEckardtClassic_9-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VonEckardtClassic-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> More than 16 million people visited the site in its first three years.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> In 1971, the grave attracted more than 7 million people.<sup id="cite_ref-Carlson_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-87">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:212px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stamp_US_1964_5c_Kennedy.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Stamp_US_1964_5c_Kennedy.jpg/210px-Stamp_US_1964_5c_Kennedy.jpg" decoding="async" width="210" height="135" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Stamp_US_1964_5c_Kennedy.jpg/315px-Stamp_US_1964_5c_Kennedy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Stamp_US_1964_5c_Kennedy.jpg/420px-Stamp_US_1964_5c_Kennedy.jpg 2x" data-file-width="493" data-file-height="317" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stamp_US_1964_5c_Kennedy.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1964 5-cent U.S. postal stamp depicting the eternal flame.</div></div></div>
<p>The presence of the grave also boosted attendance at Arlington National Cemetery. The president's 1963 funeral had been televised live, with 93 percent of all American homes watching.<sup id="cite_ref-Television_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Television-88">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> Satellites beamed the proceedings to another 23 countries, where another 600 million viewers watched.<sup id="cite_ref-Television_88-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Television-88">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> The television coverage transformed Arlington National Cemetery from a quiet veterans' cemetery into one of the Washington area's most popular tourist attractions.<sup id="cite_ref-Carlson_87-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-87">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> Average yearly attendance rose from 1 million people in 1962 to 9 million in the first six months of 1964.<sup id="cite_ref-Carlson_87-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-87">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1964, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Post_Office_Department" title="United States Post Office Department">United States Post Office Department</a> used an image of the eternal flame on an <a href="/wiki/Five_cents_John_Kennedy" title="Five cents John Kennedy">official postage stamp</a> issued to commemorate the assassinated president.<sup id="cite_ref-Lidman_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lidman-89">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> The stamp also used the words "And the glow from that fire can truly light the world"—an excerpt from Kennedy's inaugural address.<sup id="cite_ref-Lidman_89-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lidman-89">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Kennedy eternal flame has also attracted some unwanted attention as well. The leader of a group protesting segregation in housing was nearly arrested at the grave site in August 1967 after attempting to lead a group of protesters in the singing of "<a href="/wiki/America_the_Beautiful" title="America the Beautiful">America the Beautiful</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> A mentally ill individual attempted to throw red paint on the grave in August 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> A 23-year-old Army veteran committed suicide at the grave in 1972 by plunging a knife into his chest.<sup id="cite_ref-Carlson_87-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-87">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> The cross and the headstone marking Robert F. Kennedy's grave were stolen in 1981 and never recovered.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> In December 1982, an intoxicated <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">Salvadoran</a> immigrant broke into the cemetery at night and knelt before the eternal flame. He experienced a fatal heart attack, and fell into the flame.<sup id="cite_ref-Carlson_87-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carlson-87">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> In 1997, thieves pried loose one of the paving stones from the terrace in front of the eternal flame and attempted to make off with it. They gave up after realizing the 500-pound (230&#160;kg) stone was too heavy to move.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
