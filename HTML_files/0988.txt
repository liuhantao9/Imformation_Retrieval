<p><b>Chelsea</b> is a <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a> in <a href="/wiki/Suffolk_County,_Massachusetts" title="Suffolk County, Massachusetts">Suffolk County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, United States, directly across the <a href="/wiki/Mystic_River" title="Mystic River">Mystic River</a> from the city of <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>. As of 2018, Chelsea had an estimated population of 40,160.<sup id="cite_ref-Census_2010_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Census_2010-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is also the <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_cities_by_population_density" title="List of United States cities by population density">second most densely populated city in Massachusetts</a> behind <a href="/wiki/Somerville,_Massachusetts" title="Somerville, Massachusetts">Somerville</a>. With a total area of just 2.21 square miles,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Chelsea is the smallest city in Massachusetts in terms of total area.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chelsea is a diverse, working-class community that contains a high level of industrial activity. It is one of only three Massachusetts cities in which the majority of the population identifies as Hispanic or Latino, alongside <a href="/wiki/Lawrence,_Massachusetts" title="Lawrence, Massachusetts">Lawrence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holyoke,_Massachusetts" title="Holyoke, Massachusetts">Holyoke</a>. After flirting with bankruptcy in the 1990s, the once-struggling industrial city has reversed a prolonged decline and in recent years has enjoyed sustained economic growth. Thanks to its relative affordability and close proximity to Boston, Chelsea has added more than 1,200 homes since 2005, mostly loft-style apartments and condominiums suitable for small families or young professionals. There has also been significant office, retail and restaurant development throughout the city.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_settlement">Early settlement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early settlement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The area of Chelsea was first called <i>Winnisimmet</i> (meaning "good spring nearby") by the <a href="/wiki/Massachusett" title="Massachusett">Massachusett</a> tribe, which once lived there. It was settled in 1624 by <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Maverick_(colonist)" title="Samuel Maverick (colonist)">Samuel Maverick</a>, whose <a href="/wiki/Palisade" title="Palisade">palisaded</a> <a href="/wiki/Trading_post" title="Trading post">trading post</a> is considered the first permanent settlement by <a href="/wiki/Boston_Harbor" title="Boston Harbor">Boston Harbor</a>. In 1635, Maverick sold all of Winnisimmet, except for his house and farm, to <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bellingham" title="Richard Bellingham">Richard Bellingham</a>. The community remained part of <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston</a> until it was set off and incorporated in 1739, when it was named after <a href="/wiki/Chelsea,_London" title="Chelsea, London">Chelsea</a>, a neighborhood in <a href="/wiki/London,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="London, England">London, England</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_the_Revolutionary_War">Role in the Revolutionary War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Role in the Revolutionary War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1775, the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Chelsea_Creek" title="Battle of Chelsea Creek">Battle of Chelsea Creek</a> was fought in the area, the second battle of the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">Revolution</a>, at which American forces made one of their first captures of a <a href="/wiki/British_people" title="British people">British</a> ship. Part of <a href="/wiki/George_Washington" title="George Washington">George Washington</a>'s army was stationed in Chelsea during the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Boston" title="Siege of Boston">Siege of Boston</a>.
</p><p>On February 22, 1841, part of Chelsea was annexed by <a href="/wiki/Saugus,_Massachusetts" title="Saugus, Massachusetts">Saugus, Massachusetts</a>. On March 19, 1846, North Chelsea, which consists of present-day <a href="/wiki/Revere,_Massachusetts" title="Revere, Massachusetts">Revere</a> and <a href="/wiki/Winthrop,_Massachusetts" title="Winthrop, Massachusetts">Winthrop</a>, was established as a separate town.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Reincorporated as a city in 1857, Chelsea developed as an industrial center and by mid-century had become a powerhouse in wooden sailing ship construction. As the century wore on, steam power began to overtake the age of the sail and industry in the town began to shift toward manufacturing. Factories making <a href="/wiki/Rubber" class="mw-redirect" title="Rubber">rubber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elasticity_(physics)" title="Elasticity (physics)">elastic</a> goods, <a href="/wiki/Boot" title="Boot">boots</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shoemaking" title="Shoemaking">shoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stove" title="Stove">stoves</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Adhesive" title="Adhesive">adhesives</a> began to appear along the banks of Boston Harbor. It became home to the <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_Naval_Hospital" class="mw-redirect" title="Chelsea Naval Hospital">Chelsea Naval Hospital</a> designed by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Parris" title="Alexander Parris">Alexander Parris</a> and home for soldiers.<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke2004_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke2004-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="A_changing_population">A changing population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: A changing population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to local historical records, Nathan Morse, the first Jewish resident of Chelsea, arrived in 1864, and by 1890 there were only 82 Jews living in the city. However, Chelsea was a major destination for the "great wave" of Russian and Eastern European immigrants, especially <a href="/wiki/Russian_Jews" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian Jews">Russian Jews</a>, who came to the United States after 1890. By 1910 the number of Jews had grown to 11,225, nearly one third of the entire population of the city. In the 1930s there were about 20,000 Jewish residents in Chelsea out of a total population of almost 46,000. Given the area of the city, Chelsea may well have had the most Jewish residents per square mile of any city outside of <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Square_After_Great_Fire_of_1908,_Chelsea,_MA.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Square_After_Great_Fire_of_1908%2C_Chelsea%2C_MA.jpg/220px-Square_After_Great_Fire_of_1908%2C_Chelsea%2C_MA.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Square_After_Great_Fire_of_1908%2C_Chelsea%2C_MA.jpg/330px-Square_After_Great_Fire_of_1908%2C_Chelsea%2C_MA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Square_After_Great_Fire_of_1908%2C_Chelsea%2C_MA.jpg/440px-Square_After_Great_Fire_of_1908%2C_Chelsea%2C_MA.jpg 2x" data-file-width="616" data-file-height="429" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Square_After_Great_Fire_of_1908,_Chelsea,_MA.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chelsea Square after the Great Fire of 1908</div></div></div>
<p>On April 12, 1908, nearly half the city was destroyed in the <a href="/wiki/Great_Chelsea_Fire_of_1908" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Chelsea Fire of 1908">first of two great fires</a> that would devastate Chelsea in the 20th century. The fire left 18,000 people, 56 percent of the population, homeless. Despite the magnitude of the destruction, it would only take the city about two and a half years to rebuild and five years to surpass the extent of 1908's infrastructure. The city was also laid out differently after the fire, with wider streets and more access for emergency vehicles. Many of the city's residents left and never returned, which opened the door for many immigrants living in Boston to “move up” to Chelsea. To immigrants living in crowded tenements in Boston's <a href="/wiki/West_End,_Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="West End, Boston, Massachusetts">West End</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_Boston" title="East Boston">East</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_End,_Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="South End, Boston, Massachusetts">South Ends</a>, Chelsea was the next stop on their path of economic upward mobility.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By 1919 Chelsea's population had reached the record level of 52,662, with foreign-born residents comprising 46 percent of the population. Fully transitioned from a suburb to an industrial city, the waterfront flourished, with shipbuilding, lumberyards, metalworks and paint companies lined Marginal Street.<sup id="cite_ref-Clarke2004_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clarke2004-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the 1930s the first exodus of Jews from Chelsea to the suburbs began. As the community prospered and grew, many wanted to seek new opportunities in the more affluent communities of <a href="/wiki/Newton,_Massachusetts" title="Newton, Massachusetts">Newton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brookline,_Massachusetts" title="Brookline, Massachusetts">Brookline</a>. By the 1950s the Jewish population had decreased to about 8,000 and more people began to establish roots in the seaside towns of <a href="/wiki/Swampscott,_Massachusetts" title="Swampscott, Massachusetts">Swampscott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marblehead,_Massachusetts" title="Marblehead, Massachusetts">Marblehead, Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After World War II, Chelsea began a long, slow decline; between 1940 and 1980, the population declined by 38 percent. Chelsea, however, lost more population than other urban areas after the 1950s because of the elevated expressway built to connect the <a href="/wiki/North_Shore_(Massachusetts)" title="North Shore (Massachusetts)">North Shore</a> to Boston, via the <a href="/wiki/Tobin_Bridge" title="Tobin Bridge">Mystic River Bridge</a> (later renamed for Boston Mayor <a href="/wiki/Maurice_J._Tobin" title="Maurice J. Tobin">Maurice J. Tobin</a>). Hundreds of homes were lost to make way for the expressway as it cut the city in half. The resulting out-migration took with it many small, local businesses. Historic homes were abandoned, along with industrial buildings, <a href="/wiki/Brownfields" class="mw-redirect" title="Brownfields">brownfields</a>, salt piles and gas storage tanks dotting the cityscape.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chelsea_Fire.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Chelsea_Fire.jpg/220px-Chelsea_Fire.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="143" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Chelsea_Fire.jpg/330px-Chelsea_Fire.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Chelsea_Fire.jpg/440px-Chelsea_Fire.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1062" data-file-height="691" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chelsea_Fire.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chelsea Fire 1973</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chelsea_Fire_1973_(2).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Chelsea_Fire_1973_%282%29.jpg/220px-Chelsea_Fire_1973_%282%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="151" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Chelsea_Fire_1973_%282%29.jpg/330px-Chelsea_Fire_1973_%282%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Chelsea_Fire_1973_%282%29.jpg/440px-Chelsea_Fire_1973_%282%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1006" data-file-height="691" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chelsea_Fire_1973_(2).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chelsea Fire 1973 - Rag Shop District</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Chelsea_Fire_1973.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Chelsea_Fire_1973.jpg/220px-Chelsea_Fire_1973.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="144" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Chelsea_Fire_1973.jpg/330px-Chelsea_Fire_1973.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Chelsea_Fire_1973.jpg/440px-Chelsea_Fire_1973.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1056" data-file-height="690" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Chelsea_Fire_1973.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chelsea, MA</div></div></div>
<p>In 1973, disaster struck again when the <a href="/wiki/Second_Great_Chelsea_Fire" class="mw-redirect" title="Second Great Chelsea Fire">Second Great Chelsea Fire</a> burned 18 <a href="/wiki/City_block" title="City block">city blocks</a>, leaving nearly a fifth of the city in ashes. Both fires originated in Chelsea's “rag shop district,” cluttered streets filled with junk shops hawking scraps, metal, and combustible items. Wood-frame buildings and three- to six-family houses were built tightly together, and quickly caught fire.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A major shift took place in the early 1970s as Chelsea became a center for Latino immigration. Racial conflict and tension became a regular part of life in Chelsea. Although the Hispanic population continued to grow, the city did not hire its first Spanish-speaking police officer until 1992. By the early 1990s, Chelsea was both the poorest and most dangerous city in Massachusetts.
</p><p>By 1990, Chelsea had collapsed economically and socially. Crime was rampant, not least among the police and local government officials. The population drain made way for more immigrants, but depleted the city's tax base. The cost of running the city and maintaining its infrastructure did not decrease correspondingly so, in 1991, the city suffered fiscal collapse.
</p><p>The Commonwealth of Massachusetts enacted special legislation to place Chelsea into <a href="/wiki/Receivership" title="Receivership">receivership</a>. For the first time since the Great Depression, a Massachusetts city surrendered home rule and allowed a state-appointed receiver to control all aspects of city government. Governor <a href="/wiki/William_Weld" class="mw-redirect" title="William Weld">William Weld</a> named James Carlin as the first receiver followed by Lewis "Harry" Spence. City Hall was eviscerated, the police and fire departments reorganized, management of the public schools given to <a href="/wiki/Boston_University" title="Boston University">Boston University</a>, and indictments handed down. <a href="/wiki/List_of_mayors_of_Chelsea,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="List of mayors of Chelsea, Massachusetts">Mayor</a> John “Butchie” Brennan and two former mayors were found guilty of federal crimes.
</p><p>Fortunately, Chelsea had no long-term debt publicly held; thus, a solution to its problems could be explored in isolation of <a href="/wiki/Creditor" title="Creditor">creditors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_revitalization">Contemporary revitalization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Contemporary revitalization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By the summer of 1995, when the state returned City Hall to the people of Chelsea, a new government had been born, brought to life by a panel of citizens charged with drafting a new <a href="/wiki/City_charter" class="mw-redirect" title="City charter">city charter</a>. The new charter eliminated the position of mayor, converting management of the city from a <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">mayor</a> to a <a href="/wiki/Council%E2%80%93manager_government" title="Council–manager government">council–manager government</a> system, where a <a href="/wiki/City_manager" title="City manager">city manager</a> is selected by <a href="/wiki/City_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="City Council">City Council</a> members. As such, municipal government focused on improving the quality of services provided to residents and businesses, while establishing financial policies that have significantly improved the city's financial condition. With their leaders more accountable and efficient, Chelsea reversed its long decline and entered a period of population growth and economic development.
</p><p>In 1998, the City Council's focused efforts earned Chelsea the distinction of being named one of 10 <a href="/wiki/All-America_City_Award" title="All-America City Award">All-America City Award</a> winners by the <a href="/wiki/National_Civic_League" title="National Civic League">National Civic League</a> in recognition of its grass-roots approach to solving problems. Since earning that honor, Chelsea has claimed other victories. The city weathered a recession in the early 2000s and emerged with its finances intact, prompting Chelsea's <a href="/wiki/Bond_rating" class="mw-redirect" title="Bond rating">bond rating</a> to improve.
</p><p>Chelsea's skyline has been transformed by the construction of the <a href="/wiki/Wyndham_Hotels_%26_Resorts" class="mw-redirect" title="Wyndham Hotels &amp; Resorts">Wyndham</a> Chelsea, built on a lot where junk cars once sat abandoned in the wake of the fire of 1973. Opened in 2001, the hotel chain was the first company to make a substantial investment in Chelsea after receivership. The city's two main shopping centers, the Mystic Mall and Chelsea Commons, have recently undergone major renovations as well.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On July 28, 2014, a tornado touched down in northern Chelsea and made its way to neighboring <a href="/wiki/Revere,_Massachusetts" title="Revere, Massachusetts">Revere</a>. While there was relatively little damage in Chelsea, it caused major damage in Revere.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Located on a small <a href="/wiki/Peninsula" title="Peninsula">peninsula</a> in <a href="/wiki/Boston_Harbor" title="Boston Harbor">Boston Harbor</a> covering a mere 2.21 square miles (6&#160;km<sup>2</sup>), Chelsea is the smallest city by area in Massachusetts. Chelsea is bordered on three sides by water. The <a href="/wiki/Mystic_River" title="Mystic River">Mystic River</a> borders Chelsea to the southwest, the <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_Creek" title="Chelsea Creek">Chelsea Creek</a> and Mill Creek and the Island End River border it to the west.
</p><p>The topography of Chelsea consists primarily of coastal lowlands, punctuated by four <a href="/wiki/Drumlins" class="mw-redirect" title="Drumlins">drumlins</a> formed during the last Ice Age. These drumlins are located in the southwest (Admirals Hill), southeast (Mount Bellingham), northeast (Powderhorn Hill) and northwest (Mount Washington). A smaller drumlin (Mill Hill) is located on the east side of Chelsea, adjacent to Mill Creek. This sloped and hilly landscape helps to divide the city into discernible neighborhoods, each with its own character, thereby giving the city a manageable sense of scale and orientation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neighborhoods_and_districts">Neighborhoods and districts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Neighborhoods and districts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite its small size, there are several distinct neighborhoods in Chelsea:
</p><p><b>Admirals Hill</b>: Admirals Hill sits atop a point of land between the Mystic River and Island End River. Containing the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Hospital_Boston_Historic_District" title="Naval Hospital Boston Historic District">Naval Hospital Historic District</a>, the area is mostly residential development enjoying expansive views. On the south slope of the hill is the site of the historic <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_Naval_Hospital" class="mw-redirect" title="Chelsea Naval Hospital">Chelsea Naval Hospital</a>, with several brick and granite structures that have been converted to other uses. Between the Naval Hospital and the shoreline is the Mary O'Malley Park, the largest public park in Chelsea.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Admiral%27s_Hill_Naval_Hospital.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/09/Admiral%27s_Hill_Naval_Hospital.jpg/220px-Admiral%27s_Hill_Naval_Hospital.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="158" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/09/Admiral%27s_Hill_Naval_Hospital.jpg/330px-Admiral%27s_Hill_Naval_Hospital.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/0/09/Admiral%27s_Hill_Naval_Hospital.jpg 2x" data-file-width="372" data-file-height="268" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Admiral%27s_Hill_Naval_Hospital.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Admiral's Hill Naval Hospital</div></div></div>
<p><b>Addison-Orange</b>: Adjacent to the north side of downtown, the Addison-Orange neighborhood is residential, flat and densely populated. Washington Avenue runs through this neighborhood.
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Bellingham_Square_Historic_District" title="Bellingham Square Historic District">Bellingham Square</a></b>: This <a href="/wiki/Historic_district" title="Historic district">historic district</a> became the center of commerce and government after the 1908 fire. The cohesiveness of design is the result of community planning after the Great Fire of 1908. The district includes City Hall, modeled after Old Independence Hall in <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">Philadelphia</a>, the Public Library and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoenix_Charter_Academy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Phoenix Charter Academy (page does not exist)">Phoenix Charter Academy</a>'s campus.
</p><p><b>Box District</b>: Just over a block from City Hall, this once blighted neighborhood gets its name from various box manufacturing companies that operated in the area as early as 1903, when the Russell Box Company began operations at the foot of Gerrish Avenue. Abandoned in the 1960s, the area fell into disrepair until it was rezoned for residential use in the 2000s. It is now the fastest-growing part of Chelsea and has enjoyed a building boom since 2005, with town homes and multifamily housing complexes proliferating in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The Kayem Foods headquarters is located here, off Marginal Street.
</p><p><b>Carter Park—Wyndham Area</b>: The neighborhood around Carter Park is a small enclave of mostly single-family <a href="/wiki/Queen_Anne_style_architecture" title="Queen Anne style architecture">Queen Anne style</a> homes surrounded by heavy commercial and highly trafficked areas. Route 1 looms above the southeastern edge of this tree-lined neighborhood, and <a href="/wiki/Revere_Beach_Parkway" title="Revere Beach Parkway">Revere Beach Parkway</a> winds along the northern edge. The Chelsea High School, Floramos, Wyndham Hotel, Boston's <a href="/wiki/FBI" class="mw-redirect" title="FBI">FBI</a> regional field office, MGH Healthcare Center, and Mystic Mall (Market Basket, TJ Maxx, Home Goods, Five Guys) are located in this area. The historic <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_Clock_Company" title="Chelsea Clock Company">Chelsea Clock Company</a> used to be located in this area until 2015.
</p><p><b>Chelsea Square</b>: This <a href="/wiki/Historic_district" title="Historic district">historic district</a> located in the downtown area contains the finest and most intact grouping of mid 19th and early 20th century commercial architecture in the city. Area includes a waterfront district (South Broadway neighborhood) with brick row houses dating to the mid to late 19th century. Third Street is also in the area, and becomes the industrial Everett Avenue. The Chelsea Police Department is located here.
</p><p><b>Chelsea Commons</b>: Formerly known as Parkway Plaza, Chelsea Commons sits on a low flat area near the end of Mill Creek, part of which was on a former landfill and clay pit. The plaza consists of big-box retail, fast-food restaurants, and two large apartment buildings. It is bordered by a strip of wetlands on both sides. Webster Ave, Mill Creek Riverwalk, Creekside Common, and Beth Israel Deaconess HealthCare.
</p><p><b>Mill Hill</b>: This largely residential area consists mostly of two- and three-story wood frame <a href="/wiki/Single-family_detached_home" title="Single-family detached home">detached buildings</a>. Covering the smallest of the city's drumlins, the Mill Hill neighborhood sits on a small neck of land bounded by Chelsea Creek and Mill Creek. This neighborhood is on the Revere line. Eastern Avenue goes through his neighborhood.
</p><p><b>Prattville</b>: Is the northwestern section of the city. It also borders Revere, and Everett to the west. Pizza Lovers, Washington Park, Voke Park, and The Newbridge Cafe are located in this area. Metro Credit Union and <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald&#39;s">McDonald's</a> have a large presence there as well. Next to these chains is another, smaller Chelsea Firefighter station. Garfield and Washington Avenues are in Prattville. Route 1 is on the east side of Prattville, and Route 16 is on the south side.
</p><p><b>Soldiers Home</b>: The Soldiers Home neighborhood covers the steep slopes and the peak of Powderhorn Hill. This residential area contains some examples of <a href="/wiki/Queen_Anne_style_architecture" title="Queen Anne style architecture">Queen Anne style</a> architecture. Soldiers Home is one of the least dense neighborhoods in the city. At the peak sits the Soldiers Home, a large structure that dominates much of this area. However, there are some smaller associated brick structures in the area as well as Malone Park.
</p><p><b>Waterfront District</b>: For the first time, Chelsea is breaking through the heavy industry piled up along the Chelsea Creek, and reconnecting with its waterfront. Established to promote water-oriented industrial uses at Forbes Industrial Park and the lower Chelsea Creek waterfront, its use remains primarily industrial. Most of the waterfront from the Tobin Bridge to the mouth of Mill Creek is a Designated Port Area (DPA), and Chelsea has in the last decade embraced it. The city is encouraging industry to move in, but only if they help finance a new park on the waterfront.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_States_Census" title="2010 United States Census">2010 United States Census</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-GR2_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> there were 35,177 people, 11,888 households, and 7,614 families residing in the city. The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 16,036.8 people per square mile (6,184.7/km<sup>2</sup>), placing it among the highest in population density among U.S. cities.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> There were 12,337 housing units at an average density of 5,639.9 per square mile (2,175.0/km<sup>2</sup>). The racial makeup of the city was 47.8% <a href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> 8.5% Black or <a href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="African American (U.S. Census)">African American</a>, 3.1% <a href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 1.1% <a href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 0.09% <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 33.6% from <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 5.9% were <a href="/wiki/Multiracial" title="Multiracial">multiracial</a>. In addition, 62.1% of residents identified as <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> (of any race), which includes 18.2% <a href="/wiki/Salvadorans" title="Salvadorans">Salvadoran</a>, 12.7% <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Puerto Rican people">Puerto Rican</a>, 8.4% <a href="/wiki/Hondurans" title="Hondurans">Honduran</a>, 7.3% <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemalan</a>, 2.8% <a href="/wiki/Mexican_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Mexican people">Mexican</a>,  2.2% <a href="/wiki/Dominican_people_(Dominican_Republic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dominican people (Dominican Republic)">Dominican</a>, 0.5% <a href="/wiki/Cubans" title="Cubans">Cuban</a>, 0.5% <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rican" class="mw-redirect" title="Costa Rican">Costa Rican</a>, 0.4% <a href="/wiki/Nicaraguan" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicaraguan">Nicaraguan</a>, 0.4% <a href="/wiki/Panamanian" class="mw-redirect" title="Panamanian">Panamanian</a>, 1.4% other Central American countries, 2.5% other South American countries, 5.3% other Hispanic/Latino.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There were 11,888 households out of which 36.4% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 36.9% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 20.1% had a female householder with no husband present, and 36% were non-families. Of all households 28.8% were made up of individuals and 10.8% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older. The average household size was 2.87 and the average family size was 3.5.
</p><p>The population was spread out with 27.3 under the age of 18, 10.6% from 18 to 24, 34.6% from 25 to 44, 16.3% from 45 to 64, and 11.2% who were 65 years of age or older. The median age was 31 years. For every 100 females, there were 100.9 males. For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 99.7 males.
</p><p>The median income for a household in the city was $30,161, and the median income for a family was $32,130. Males had a median income of $27,280 versus $26,010 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the city was $14,628. About 20.6% of families and 23.3% of the population were below the <a href="/wiki/Poverty_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 28.8% of those under age 18 and 20.9% of those age 65 or over.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Foreign-born_population">Foreign-born population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Foreign-born population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, 38% of Chelsea residents had been born outside of the United States. This is the highest percentage of foreign-born residents in the Commonwealth of Massachusetts.<sup id="cite_ref-SacchetiStretchsubp1_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SacchetiStretchsubp1-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Within recent years, the city has made a sustained effort to work with both immigrant communities and persons of minority faiths. Its “Interfaith Alliance” brings members of the Jewish, Christian, and Muslim communities together to promote inclusiveness, diversity, and tolerance. And its continued loyalty to its 2007 Sanctuary City Resolution aims to support all foreign born residents regardless of their country of origin or immigration status.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Economic_development">Economic development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Economic development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Under City Manager Ambrosino, Chelsea has implemented several innovative data analysis and tracking programs. Many of these programs are led and administered in conjunction with fellows from the Harvard Kennedy School's Innovation Field Lab. According to Chelsea's 2017 “State of the City” report, "this partnership allows the city to benefit from the questions and suggestions of [domestic and] international graduate students."
</p><p>These data analysis programs have played a particularly helpful role in the city's efforts to “revitalize” the downtown area around Bellingham Square via the "Re-Imagining Broadway" initiative. In 2016, the City Council approved a $5.2 million grant for infrastructure improvements in the district. The project has been supported by a newly hired Downtown Coordinator and aims to engage residents and local businesses in a collective effort to advance the economic prosperity and quality of life in the district.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Capital_Improvement_Plan">Capital Improvement Plan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Capital Improvement Plan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>As summarized by the "State of the City" report<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (April 2019)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, Chelsea's Capital Improvement Plan will invest in "park development, building improvements, water and sewer upgrades, and neighborhood street/sidewalks improvements. Other specific investments in new graffiti removal and snow removal equipment will enhance the quality of life for residents." The expenditures for Fiscal Year 2018 and Fiscal Year 2018–2022 can be found to the right. Total expenditures are divided between utility enhancement, equipment acquisition, parks and open space, public buildings and facilities, public safety, and surface enhancement. For Fiscal Year 2018, almost 64% of the budget will be dedicated to surface enhancements, which includes citywide sidewalks, marginal street pre-engineering, Shurtleff Street roadway and sidewalks, casino mitigation/transportation, citywide traffic calming, Congress Avenue road and sidewalks, Downtown Broadway engineering and construction, and Highland Street Greenway Phase II.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#What_information_to_include" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="A complete citation is needed (April 2019)">full citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Residential_development">Residential development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Residential development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Housing Composition: According to the 2011–2015 Community Survey, there are a total of 12,940 households in Chelsea, 27.9% of which are owner-occupied and 72.1% of which are renter-occupied. Although Chelsea has been known as the "City of Renters", there has been a push for home ownership. This has been pushed in particular by the Chelsea Restoration Corporation, which offers educational housing workshops and works in partnership with other state, municipal, and private partners to "rehabilitate properties and increase the stock of affordable housing."
</p><p>Heating: Over half (55.4%) of the housing units in Chelsea use utility gas, 29.8% use electricity, 12.7% use fuel oil, kerosene, etc. and smaller portion (2.1%) of housing units use bottled, tank, or LP gas, as well as other fuel or no fuel at all. With these statistics in mind, Chelsea has started several initiatives towards renewable energy and sustainability. One includes a partnership with SolSmart, a team of individuals dedicated to implementation of Solar energy, by making solar panels accessible through zoning laws, offering affordable solar options and providing education and resources for those who are interested in these efforts.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Reimagine_Broadway">Reimagine Broadway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Reimagine Broadway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Reimagine Broadway was a six-month long planning effort that began in 2017 to transform downtown Chelsea, with the guidance of the Chelsea City Council, City Manager Ambrosino, Planning and Developing Downtown Coordinator Mimi Graney and several others. This effort ranges from supporting small business owners to re-designing the streetscape. The goals of this project were to "Enhance how public space is used and accessed downtown, support existing businesses and encourage new growth, beautify the area and create a consistent, vibrant look, improve overall safety for all users, establish a circulation pattern that works for cars, buses, pedestrians, transit riders, and bicyclists."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrastructure">Infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Roads">Roads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Roads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_1_in_Massachusetts" title="U.S. Route 1 in Massachusetts">Route 1 North Expressway</a> is a limited access highway that cuts City of Chelsea in half. The <a href="/wiki/Tobin_Bridge" title="Tobin Bridge">Tobin Bridge</a>, a major regional transportation artery, carries Route 1 from Chelsea across the <a href="/wiki/Mystic_River" title="Mystic River">Mystic River</a> to <a href="/wiki/Charlestown,_Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Charlestown, Boston, Massachusetts">Charlestown</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Bus">Bus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Bus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Chelsea is served by the <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Bay_Transit_Authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Massachusetts Bay Transit Authority">Massachusetts Bay Transit Authority</a>. The <a href="/wiki/MBTA_Commuter_Rail" title="MBTA Commuter Rail">MBTA Commuter Rail</a> provides service from <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>'s <a href="/wiki/North_Station" title="North Station">North Station</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Bellingham_Square_station" title="Bellingham Square station">Chelsea</a> station on its <a href="/wiki/Newburyport/Rockport_Line" title="Newburyport/Rockport Line">Newburyport/Rockport Line</a>.
</p><p>Additionally, Chelsea is served by many <a href="/wiki/MBTA_bus" class="mw-redirect" title="MBTA bus">MBTA bus</a> routes providing local service to East Boston, <a href="/wiki/Revere,_Massachusetts" title="Revere, Massachusetts">Revere</a>, <a href="/wiki/Everett,_Massachusetts" title="Everett, Massachusetts">Everett</a> and other nearby cities. Chelsea does not have a link to the MBTA <a href="/wiki/Rapid_transit" title="Rapid transit">subway</a> or <a href="/wiki/Light_rail" title="Light rail">light rail</a> systems.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Silver_Line_Gateway_Extension">Silver Line Gateway Extension</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Silver Line Gateway Extension">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Silver_Line_(MBTA)#Extension_to_Chelsea" title="Silver Line (MBTA)">Silver Line (MBTA) §&#160;Extension to Chelsea</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Silver_Line_Chelsea_proposal_map.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Silver_Line_Chelsea_proposal_map.svg/220px-Silver_Line_Chelsea_proposal_map.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="288" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Silver_Line_Chelsea_proposal_map.svg/330px-Silver_Line_Chelsea_proposal_map.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Silver_Line_Chelsea_proposal_map.svg/440px-Silver_Line_Chelsea_proposal_map.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="583" data-file-height="763" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Silver_Line_Chelsea_proposal_map.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Map of the planned Silver Line Gateway route to Mystic Mall in Chelsea</div></div></div>
<p>Governor <a href="/wiki/Deval_Patrick" title="Deval Patrick">Deval Patrick</a> announced in October 2013 that <a href="/wiki/MassDOT" class="mw-redirect" title="MassDOT">MassDOT</a> would collaborate with the <a href="/wiki/MBTA" class="mw-redirect" title="MBTA">MBTA</a> to extend the <a href="/wiki/Silver_Line_(MBTA)" title="Silver Line (MBTA)">Silver Line</a> <a href="/wiki/Bus_rapid_transit" title="Bus rapid transit">bus rapid transit</a> line to Chelsea via East Boston by 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The new SL3 route begins at <a href="/wiki/South_Station" title="South Station">South Station</a> and runs through the Waterfront Tunnel, along with the SL1 and SL2 routes, to <a href="/wiki/Silver_Line_Way_(MBTA_station)" class="mw-redirect" title="Silver Line Way (MBTA station)">Silver Line Way</a>, continuing with the SL1 through the <a href="/wiki/Ted_Williams_Tunnel" title="Ted Williams Tunnel">Ted Williams Tunnel</a>. The new route diverges to meet the <a href="/wiki/Blue_Line_(MBTA)" title="Blue Line (MBTA)">Blue Line</a> at <a href="/wiki/Airport_(MBTA_station)" class="mw-redirect" title="Airport (MBTA station)">Airport Station</a>, and follows the Coughlin Bypass Road (a half-mile commercial-use-only road which opened in 2012)<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> to the <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_Street_Bridge" title="Chelsea Street Bridge">Chelsea Street Bridge</a>. The Silver Line stops at the four stations in Chelsea: Eastern Avenue, Box District, Downtown Chelsea, and Mystic Mall. A new $20 million Chelsea commuter rail station and "transit hub" was constructed at the Mystic Mall terminus of the new Silver Line route, so that trains no longer block Sixth Street.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The new Silver Line and commuter rail stations is fully handicapped accessible.<sup id="cite_ref-stateannouncement_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stateannouncement-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On March 12, 2018, the MBTA announced that construction of the first phase was completed, and that Silver Line service to Chelsea would begin on April 21.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Additionally, a multi-use 0.75-mile (1.21&#160;km) shared path 0.75-mile (1.21&#160;km) <a href="/wiki/Linear_park" title="Linear park">linear park</a> runs parallel to the <a href="/wiki/Silver_Line_(MBTA)" title="Silver Line (MBTA)">Silver Line</a> <a href="/wiki/Bus_rapid_transit" title="Bus rapid transit">bus rapid transit</a> busway utilizing the Boston &amp; Albany Railroad's <a href="/wiki/Grand_Junction_Railroad" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Junction Railroad">Grand Junction Branch</a> right-of-way. Located within the Box District neighborhood, the path connects Downtown Chelsea and Eastern Avenue stations.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chelsea_Public_Schools" title="Chelsea Public Schools">Chelsea Public Schools</a> has four elementary schools, three middle schools, and one high school, <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_High_School_(Massachusetts)" title="Chelsea High School (Massachusetts)">Chelsea High School</a>. The Chelsea school system has historically been towards the bottom of the state's test score rankings. It is plagued by high turnover among students. A very high percentage of students move in or out over the course of the year, and the dropout rate is high. In 1988, the school board made the unprecedented move of delegating its authority for control of the school district to <a href="/wiki/Boston_University" title="Boston University">Boston University</a>. In June 2008, a partnership with BU ended, and the schools returned to full local control.
</p><p>Chelsea has only one private school remaining, St. Rose School, as the others have closed. St. Rose is located on Broadway and, as of 2012, has an enrollment of about 220 students.  In addition, there are two public <a href="/wiki/Charter_schools" class="mw-redirect" title="Charter schools">charter schools</a>, the Excel Academy and Phoenix Charter Academy. <a href="/wiki/Bunker_Hill_Community_College" title="Bunker Hill Community College">Bunker Hill Community College</a> and the <a href="/wiki/For-profit" class="mw-redirect" title="For-profit">for-profit</a> <a href="/wiki/Everest_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Everest Institute">Everest Institute</a> have satellite locations of their schools in Chelsea.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="School_committee">School committee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: School committee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Chelsea School Committee is composed of nine elected members, eight of whom represent a <a href="/wiki/District" title="District">district</a> within Chelsea and one of whom serves the committee At-Large. The 2016-2017 School Committee members include Shawn O'Reagan (Delegate to the Chelsea City Council), Rosemarie Carlisle (Vice-chairman), Jeanette Velez (Chairperson), Richard Maronski, Diana Maldonado, Robert J. Pereira (Delegate to the Shore Collaborative), Ana Hernandez, Kelly Garcia, and Yessenia Alfaro-Alvarez.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As stated by the "Rules and Regulations of the Chelsea School Committee", the responsibilities of these elected officials include: hiring a Superintendent, developing policy that will then be used as a guide by the <a href="/wiki/Superintendent_of_Schools" class="mw-redirect" title="Superintendent of Schools">Superintendent of Schools</a>, assessing the effectiveness of these policies, determining a budget to support policy implementation, and encouraging community engagement to keep the local community informed about the state of schools, as well as a manner of identifying the needs of the public.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Community_development">Community development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Community development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl><dt>ROCA</dt></dl>
<p>Chelsea houses several non-profit organizations. Among them are ROCA, which provides services to disadvantaged youth and immigrant families, respectively.  ROCA's goal is to reduce incarceration rates and transform the lives of young individuals in and around Chelsea.
</p>
<dl><dt>Chelsea Collaborative</dt></dl>
<p>Similarly, according to the Chelsea Collaborative's website, it is committed to the "social, environmental and economic health of the community and its people." These non-profit organizations continue to positively impact and engage the community within Chelsea.
</p>
<dl><dt>CAPIC</dt></dl>
<p>The Community Action Programs Inter-City, Inc. (CAPIC) is a non-profit organization, which serves the cities of Revere, Winthrop and Chelsea. CAPIC's goals are to identify the "needs, problems, and concerns of those in poverty" and enable them to be self-sufficient. CAPIC is an umbrella organization which houses many programs that range from early childhood education to Head Start to day-to-day resources through their tax preparation program.
</p>
<dl><dt>MGH Chelsea HealthCare Center</dt></dl>
<p>After the 2016 Community Health Needs Assessment Report and Implementation Plan, the Mass General Hospital's (MGH) Center for Community Health Improvement (CCHI) determined a need to address factors that lead to adolescent substance use and mental health within Chelsea and surrounding cities
</p>
<dl><dt>TND - Chelsea Thrives</dt></dl>
<p>The Neighborhood Developers' (TND) mission is to promote healthy homes, financial mobility and engage neighbors through offering affordable home options, access to a Financial Opportunity Center and coordinated community events, such as neighborhood walks. With the collaboration of 25 different private and public sector institutions, TND has been a partner in the goal of reducing crime over a ten-year span.
</p>
<dl><dt>Hub</dt></dl>
<p>As stated on the Chelsea Police Department's website, the Hub is a collaborative initiative between the Police department and several community organizations to help remove risks, such as, but not limited to drug addiction, domestic violence, or human trafficking from individuals and families. Their goal is to create a safe and healthy environment, where every individual has the opportunity to thrive.
</p>
<dl><dt>Healthy Chelsea</dt></dl>
<p>In 2009, after identifying the needs for "obesity prevention and promotion of healthy living," a coalition of 75 individuals that serve the local government, state government, community healthcare providers and businesses dedicated themselves to addressing Chelsea's high obesity rate.
</p><p>The coalition works closely with Chelsea Public Schools to implement healthier eating habits and advocate for a dedicated time spent on physical activity. The coalition also formed a collaboration with Chelsea's Planning and Development Department to improve infrastructure through park renovations and installations, traffic calming measures, and road and sidewalk redesigns. Healthy Chelsea works alongside the Board of Health to integrate a regulation for artificial trans-fat free for prepared foods served in businesses within Chelsea.
</p>
<dl><dt>GreenRoots</dt></dl>
<p>GreenRoots, Inc. is an independent community-based organization based at 227 Marginal Street, Suite 1, Chelsea, MA, which focuses on environmental justice and public health within the city of Chelsea and surrounding communities. Before becoming an independent organization in 2016, it was a grassroots community committee under the Chelsea Collaborative, Inc. (otherwise known as the Collaborative), which was founded in 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> GreenRoots' accomplishments include enacting the Youth Pass, creating additional community gardens, investing $3 million to reduce air pollutants at the New England Produce Center that were contributing to hotspots, stopping the transport of flammable ethanol, and restoring the Mill Creek area in Chelsea and Revere.
</p>
<dl><dt>Chelsea Homeowner's Association</dt></dl>
<p>The Chelsea Homeowner’s Association is an active local group in the city that promotes the need for increased home ownership, cleaner streets, safer areas, and encouraging business growth. Chelsea currently has the lowest home ownership in all of Massachusetts and the group is committed to reversing this course as well as welcoming all people to Chelsea regardless of race, gender, religion, and income to buy in the city.
</p>

<p>The city of Chelsea is protected by the career professional firefighters of the City of Chelsea Fire Department, operating from three fire stations across the city, each shift commanded by a Deputy Chief. Chelsea Fire operates an apparatus fleet of four engines, two ladders, two special operations units, a maintenance unit, a foam-tender unit, and several other special, support, and reserve units. Chelsea Fire responds to ~11,000 emergency calls annually.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> As of April 2019, the Chief of Department was Leonard A. Albanese, who accepted the job in 2016, after serving as Fire Chief of the North Providence Fire Department.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> He succeeded Acting Chief Robert Houghton,<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> who had taken on that role while former Chief of Department Robert Better was on medical leave; Better retired in January 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chelsea Engine 2 responded to over 4,000 emergency calls annually, with 4,269 in 2011, placing it among the busiest engine companies in the United States, and at 120% of the maximum acceptable threshold for engine company call volume, as set by the Commission on Fire Accreditation International.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Chelsea_Clock_Company">Chelsea Clock Company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chelsea,_Massachusetts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Chelsea Clock Company">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Founded in 1897, the <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_Clock_Company" title="Chelsea Clock Company">Chelsea Clock Company</a> is one of the oldest, largest, and few remaining American <a href="/wiki/Clock" title="Clock">clock</a> manufacturing companies in existence. For over a century, Chelsea's <a href="/wiki/Clockmakers" class="mw-redirect" title="Clockmakers">clockmakers</a> have been designing and handcrafting distinguished, high quality clocks for customers in the corporate, consumer, government and marine markets. In 2015, the Chelsea Clock Company moved to a smaller building a few blocks away from the original location.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Today, the company continues to build and repair clocks from its new headquarters. The old building is slated for demolition to make way for a new apartment building.
</p>
