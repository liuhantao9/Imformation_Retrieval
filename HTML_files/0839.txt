<p>The <b>American University speech</b>, titled "<b>A Strategy of Peace</b>", was a <a href="/wiki/Commencement_address" class="mw-redirect" title="Commencement address">commencement address</a> delivered by <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">United States President</a> <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> at the <a href="/wiki/American_University" title="American University">American University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, on Monday, June 10, 1963.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Delivered at the height of his rhetorical powers and widely considered one of his most powerful speeches,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy not only outlined a plan to curb nuclear arms, but also "laid out a hopeful, yet realistic route for world peace at a time when the U.S. and Soviet Union faced the potential for an escalating nuclear arms race."<sup id="cite_ref-20150804MSNBC.COM_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20150804MSNBC.COM-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In the speech, Kennedy announced his agreement to negotiations "toward early agreement on a comprehensive test ban treaty" (which resulted in the <a href="/wiki/Partial_Test_Ban_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Partial Test Ban Treaty">Nuclear Test-Ban Treaty</a>) and also announced, for the purpose of showing "good faith and solemn convictions", his decision to unilaterally suspend all U.S. atmospheric testing of nuclear weapons as long as all other nations would do the same. Noteworthy are his comments that the United States was seeking a goal of "complete disarmament" of nuclear weapons and his vow that America "will never start a war". The speech was unusual in its peaceful outreach to the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> at the height of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, and is remembered as one of Kennedy's finest and most important speeches.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_University_speech&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Missile_Crisis" title="Cuban Missile Crisis">Cuban Missile Crisis</a> in October 1962, Kennedy was determined to construct a better relationship with the Soviet Union to discourage another threat of nuclear war. He believed that Soviet Premier <a href="/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev" title="Nikita Khrushchev">Nikita Khrushchev</a> was also interested in renewing U.S.–Soviet relations. On November 19, 1962, Khrushchev had submitted a report to the Central Committee of the Communist Party that implicitly called for a halt in foreign intervention to concentrate on the economy.  One month later, Khrushchev wrote Kennedy a letter stating "the time has come now to put an end once and for all to nuclear tests."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy greeted this response with enthusiasm and suggested that technical discussions for nuclear inspections begin between representatives of the two governments.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> However, Kennedy faced opposition for any test ban from Republican leaders and his own State Department. After several months the opposition in the Senate lessened and gave the Kennedy Administration the opportunity to pursue the ban with the Soviet Union. In May 1963, the president informed his National Security Advisor <a href="/wiki/McGeorge_Bundy" title="McGeorge Bundy">McGeorge Bundy</a> that he wished to deliver a major address on peace. According to Special Assistant <a href="/wiki/Ted_Sorensen" title="Ted Sorensen">Ted Sorensen</a> the speech was kept confidential in fear that the unprecedented tone would "set off alarm bells in more bellicose quarters in Washington" and allow political attacks against Kennedy in advance of the speech.<sup id="cite_ref-edge_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edge-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In the days before the speech, Kennedy was committed to addressing the U.S. Conference of Mayors in Honolulu and asked Sorensen to construct the initial draft with input from several members of Kennedy's staff. The speech was reviewed and edited by Kennedy and Sorensen on the return flight from Honolulu days before the address. Historian and Special Assistant <a href="/wiki/Arthur_M._Schlesinger,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Arthur M. Schlesinger, Jr.">Arthur M. Schlesinger, Jr.</a> observed in his diary, "from the viewpoint of orderly administration, this was a bad way to prepare a major statement on foreign policy. But the State Department could never in a thousand years have produced this speech."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_University_speech&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sorensen had been Kennedy's aide since the 1953 Massachusetts Senatorial election, and eventually served as his primary campaign speechwriter and as Special Counsel during and after the 1960 Presidential election.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> By 1963 he had written drafts for nearly every speech Kennedy delivered in office, including the <a href="/wiki/Inaugural_address_of_John_F._Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Inaugural address of John F. Kennedy">inaugural address</a>, the Cuban Missile Crisis speech, and the <a href="/wiki/Ich_bin_ein_Berliner" title="Ich bin ein Berliner">Ich bin ein Berliner</a> speech. Common elements of the Kennedy-Sorensen speeches were alliteration, repetition and chiasmus as well as historical references and quotations.<sup id="cite_ref-edge_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edge-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Although Kennedy often interposed off-the-cuff ad-libs to his speeches, he did not deviate from the final draft of the address. Anca Gata described Ted Sorensen as "the chief architect of the speech in language, style, composition, and rhetoric. One of the most original issues in the speech was the reintroduction of the Russian people to the Americans as a great culture with important achievements in science and space, and as promoting economic and industrial growth on their own."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The content of the speech was unapologetically "dovish" in its pursuit of peace. Kennedy noted that almost uniquely among the "major world powers" the United States and Russia had never been at war with each other. He also acknowledged the massive human casualties that Russia suffered during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and declared that no nation had "ever suffered more than the Soviet Union in the Second World War," a fact that had gone largely unheralded in the West due to the onset of the Cold War. Kennedy sought to draw similarities between the United States and the Soviet Union several times and called for a "reexamination" of American attitudes towards Russia. He warned that adopting a course towards nuclear confrontation would be "evidence only of the bankruptcy of our policy—or of a collective death-wish for the world."
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Sachs" title="Jeffrey Sachs">Jeffrey Sachs</a>, American economist and director of the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Earth Institute">Earth Institute</a> at <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">Columbia University</a>, was deeply moved by the speech, "not only for its eloquence and content, but also for its relevance to today's global challenges. For in it Kennedy tells us about transforming our deepest aspirations—in this case for peace—into practical realities. He almost presents a method, a dream-and-do combination that soars with high vision and yet walk on earth with practical results."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESachs2013xiv_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESachs2013xiv-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> In reviewing the history and context of Kennedy's speech at American University, Sachs' esteem for Kennedy grew further, concluding, "I have come to believe that Kennedy's quest for peace is not only the greatest achievement of his presidency, but also one of the greatest acts of world leadership in the modern era."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESachs2013xv_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESachs2013xv-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_University_speech&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Soviet_response">Soviet response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_University_speech&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Soviet response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kennedy's speech was made available, in its entirety, in the Soviet press<sup id="cite_ref-20150804MSNBC.COM_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20150804MSNBC.COM-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> so that the people in the Soviet Union could read it without hindrance. Additionally, the speech could be heard in the Soviet Union without censorship because jamming measures against the western broadcast agencies such as Voice of America didn't take place upon rebroadcast of Kennedy's speech. Khrushchev was deeply moved and impressed by Kennedy's speech, telling Undersecretary of State Averell Harriman that it was "the greatest speech by any American President since Roosevelt."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After 12 days of negotiations and less than two months after the president's speech the <a href="/wiki/Partial_Nuclear_Test_Ban_Treaty" title="Partial Nuclear Test Ban Treaty">Partial Nuclear Test Ban Treaty</a> was completed.<sup id="cite_ref-20150804MSNBC.COM_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20150804MSNBC.COM-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Partial Nuclear Test Ban Treaty was signed by the governments of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> (represented by <a href="/wiki/Dean_Rusk" title="Dean Rusk">Dean Rusk</a>), named the "Original Parties", at <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">Moscow</a> on August 5, 1963. US ratification occurred by the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Senate" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Senate">U.S. Senate</a> on September 24, 1963 by a vote of 80-19<sup id="cite_ref-20150804MSNBC.COM_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20150804MSNBC.COM-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and the treaty was signed into law by Kennedy on October 7, 1963. The treaty went into effect on October 10, 1963.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_reactions">Other reactions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_University_speech&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other reactions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The speech was met with little response in the United States; after one week, only 896 letters were sent to the White House concerning its content (in contrast to over 28,000 related to a bill affecting the price of freight). The response from Republicans in <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a> was mostly dismissive in nature.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETalbot2009206_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETalbot2009206-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Senator <a href="/wiki/Barry_Goldwater" title="Barry Goldwater">Barry Goldwater</a> accused Kennedy of taking a "soft stance" on the Soviet Union.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGoduti_Jr.2009210_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoduti_Jr.2009210-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=American_University_speech&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_McNamara" title="Robert McNamara">Robert McNamara</a>, Kennedy's <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">Secretary of Defense</a>, declared at a 2003 memorial event at the <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_Presidential_Library_and_Museum" title="John F. Kennedy Presidential Library and Museum">John F. Kennedy Presidential Library and Museum</a> that the speech was "one of the great documents of the 20th century." He later commented that it "laid out exactly what Kennedy's intentions were."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETalbot2009206_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETalbot2009206-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Ted Sorensen considered the address Kennedy's most important speech.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESorensen19882_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESorensen19882-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
