<p>The <b>double hyphen</b> (＝ or ゠) is a <a href="/wiki/Punctuation" title="Punctuation">punctuation mark</a> that consists of two parallel <a href="/wiki/Hyphen" title="Hyphen">hyphens</a>. It was a development of the earlier <span class="nowrap"><b>double oblique hyphen</b></span> (⸗) which developed from a <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">Central European</a> variant of the <a href="/wiki/Slash_(punctuation)#History" title="Slash (punctuation)">virgule slash</a>, originally a form of <a href="/wiki/Comma_(punctuation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Comma (punctuation)">scratch comma</a>. In order to avoid its being confused with the <a href="/wiki/Equals_sign" title="Equals sign">equals sign</a> (=), the double hyphen is often given as double oblique hyphen in modern typography. The double hyphen is also not to be confused with two consecutive hyphens (-<span style="padding-left: 0.1em;">-</span>), which are often used to represent an <a href="/wiki/Em_dash" class="mw-redirect" title="Em dash">em dash</a> (&#8212;) or <a href="/wiki/Dash" title="Dash">en dash</a> (–); that practice arose due to the limitations of typewriter character sets which did not have both hyphen and dash.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double_hyphen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The double hyphen is used for several different purposes throughout the world:
</p>
<ul><li>Some <a href="/wiki/Typeface" title="Typeface">typefaces</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Fraktur_(typeface)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fraktur (typeface)">Fraktur</a> faces, use the double hyphen as a <a href="/wiki/Glyph" title="Glyph">glyphic</a> variant of the single hyphen. (With Fraktur faces, such a double hyphen usually is oblique.)</li>
<li>It may be also used for artistic or commercial purposes to achieve a distinctive visual effect. For example, the name of <a href="/wiki/Waldorf%3DAstoria_Hotel" class="mw-redirect" title="Waldorf=Astoria Hotel">The Waldorf=Astoria</a> hotel was officially written with a double hyphen from 1949 to 2009.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Merriam-Webster" title="Merriam-Webster">Merriam-Webster</a> dictionaries if a word is divided at the end of the line, and the division point happens to be a hyphen, it is replaced with a double hyphen to graphically indicate that the divided word is normally hyphenated, for example <i>cross⸗<br />country</i>.</li>
<li>In several dictionaries published in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, all such compound words are linked with double hyphens, whether at the end of the line or not, and the normal use of the single hyphen for non-compound words is retained. An example from the first or second page of such dictionaries is <i><a href="/wiki/Aaron%27s_rod" title="Aaron&#39;s rod">Aaron's⸗rod</a></i>. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/Century_Dictionary" title="Century Dictionary">Century Dictionary</a> and <a href="/wiki/Funk_%26_Wagnalls" title="Funk &amp; Wagnalls">Funk &amp; Wagnalls</a> New Standard Dictionary of the English Language.</li>
<li>It is used as a distinct punctuation mark by <a href="/wiki/Coptic_language" title="Coptic language">Coptic language</a> and ancient Near Eastern language scholars.</li>
<li>It is used as a distinct punctuation mark in <a href="/wiki/Ojibwe_language" title="Ojibwe language">Ojibwe language</a> publications in the Fiero Roman orthography, as a hyphen is used to separate compound preverb units regardless of their line position, while a double hyphen is used to divide a word at the end of a line and can be treated as a <a href="/wiki/Soft_hyphen" title="Soft hyphen">soft hyphen</a>. However, due to lack of availability of a double hyphen in most fonts, an equal sign is often used as a substitute.</li>
<li>In Ojibwe, <a href="/wiki/Cree_language" title="Cree language">Cree</a> and other languages using <a href="/wiki/Unified_Canadian_syllabics" class="mw-redirect" title="Unified Canadian syllabics">Unified Canadian syllabics</a>, as final <i>c</i> ( ᐨ )—encoded as <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047074">.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}</style><span class="smallcaps" style="font-size: smaller;">CANADIAN SYLLABICS FINAL SHORT HORIZONTAL STROKE</span> (U+1428)—resembles a hyphen, a double hyphen (᐀) named <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047074"/><span class="smallcaps" style="font-size: smaller;">CANADIAN SYLLABICS HYPHEN</span> (U+1400) is used to distinguish the punctuation from the syllabics letter.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>, the double hyphen<span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">ダブルハイフン</span><span style="display:none">,</span>&#32;<i>daburu haifun</i>)</span> in rare cases replaces an English en dash or hyphen when writing foreign words in <a href="/wiki/Katakana" title="Katakana">katakana</a> due to their potential confusion with the <a href="/wiki/Ch%C5%8Don" class="mw-redirect" title="Chōon">prolonged sound mark</a> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">ー</span>). It may be used to separate a person's given and family names, such as transcribing the name of <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a> as: ガリレオ＝ガリレイ.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Middle_dot" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle dot">middle dot</a> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">・</span>) is however much more commonly used for these purposes. (For foreign names that include both spaces and hyphens, both the middle dot and double hyphen may appear together as in <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Zeta-Jones" title="Catherine Zeta-Jones">Catherine Zeta-Jones</a>: キャサリン・ゼタ゠ジョーンズ.) The double hyphen is part of the <a href="/wiki/JIS_X_0213" title="JIS X 0213">JIS X 0213</a> standard, but is not included in more commonly used character encodings, such as <a href="/wiki/Shift-JIS" class="mw-redirect" title="Shift-JIS">Shift-JIS</a> and <a href="/wiki/EUC-JP" class="mw-redirect" title="EUC-JP">EUC-JP</a>. For this reason, the equals sign is frequently used in its place.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unicode">Unicode</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Double_hyphen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Unicode">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When the double hyphen is used as a functionally equivalent graphical variant of the single hyphen, it is represented in <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">Unicode</a> as a normal hyphen.
</p><p>When used as a punctuation mark distinct from the single hyphen, the double hyphen is represented in Unicode by several different points.
