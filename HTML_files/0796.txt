<p><b>James Michael Curley</b> (November 20, 1874 – November 12, 1958) was an American <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democratic Party</a> <a href="/wiki/Politician" title="Politician">politician</a> from <a href="/wiki/Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston, Massachusetts">Boston, Massachusetts</a>.  One of the most colorful figures in Massachusetts politics in the first half of the 20th century, Curley served four terms as Democratic <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_Boston,_Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect" title="Mayor of Boston, Massachusetts">Mayor of Boston, Massachusetts</a>, including part of one while in prison.  He also served a single term as <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Massachusetts" title="Governor of Massachusetts">Governor of Massachusetts</a>, characterized by one biographer as "a disaster mitigated only by moments of farce",<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> for its free spending and corruption.
</p><p>Curley was immensely popular with working-class Roman Catholic <a href="/wiki/Irish_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish American">Irish Americans</a> in Boston, among whom he grew up and became active in <a href="/wiki/Ward_(politics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ward (politics)">ward</a> politics. During the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression_in_the_United_States" title="Great Depression in the United States">Great Depression</a>, he enlarged <a href="/wiki/Boston_City_Hospital" title="Boston City Hospital">Boston City Hospital</a>, expanded the city's public transit system (now the <a href="/wiki/MBTA" class="mw-redirect" title="MBTA">MBTA</a>), funded projects to improve the roads and bridges, and improved the neighborhoods with beaches and bathhouses, playgrounds and parks, public schools and libraries, all the while collecting graft and raising taxes. He became a leading and at times divisive force in the state's Democratic Party, contesting for power with its <a href="/wiki/White_Anglo-Saxon_Protestant" title="White Anglo-Saxon Protestant">White Anglo-Saxon Protestant</a> leadership at the local and state levels, and with Boston's ward bosses.  He served two terms in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a>, and was regularly a candidate for a variety of local and state offices for half a century.  He was twice convicted of crimes, and notably served time for a felony conviction related to earlier corruption during his last term as mayor.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Michael_Curley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Michael_Curley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>James Michael Curley was born in Boston's <a href="/wiki/Roxbury,_Boston" title="Roxbury, Boston">Roxbury</a> neighborhood in 1874.  Curley's father Michael left <a href="/wiki/Oughterard" title="Oughterard">Oughterard</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/County_Galway" title="County Galway">County Galway</a>, Ireland, at age 14, and settled in Roxbury, where he met Curley's mother, Sarah Clancy, also from County Galway.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  Roxbury, originally an independent city, was annexed to Boston in 1868, and Michael Curley worked as a day laborer and foot soldier for ward boss P. James "Pea-Jacket" Maguire.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Michael Curley died in 1884, when James was ten.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  James and his brother John worked to supplement the meager family income, while James took classes at the local public school.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>  His mother is likely responsible for instilling in him the strain of generosity that would make up a significant part of his public personality.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  He left school at fifteen, beginning a series of jobs, including factory work and delivery jobs, the latter of which exposed him to large parts of the growing city.  He sought to pass the civil service exam to become a fire fighter, but was too young to take the job.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>  Curley's mother continually intervened to turn him away from his father's unsavory associates while working at a job scrubbing floors in offices and churches all over Boston.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The combination of his mother instilling good hard working values, while he watched his mother's back-breaking work and struggle against a backdrop of semi-criminal political graft in ward politics, influenced Curley's attitude toward the poor and the utility of political organizing for the rest of his life.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_rise">Political rise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Michael_Curley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Political rise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As Curley came of age, Boston's politics was one of growing Irish power, a trend that was opposed by existing Yankee Protestant powers. Curley involved himself in both the local Roman Catholic church, and in the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Order_of_Hibernians" title="Ancient Order of Hibernians">Ancient Order of Hibernians</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Fraternal_benefit_society" class="mw-redirect" title="Fraternal benefit society">fraternal benefit society</a> that assisted Irish immigrants and poor, and acquired a reputation as a hustler who was willing to help others get ahead. His entrance into politics included the traditional practice of <a href="/wiki/Ward_(country_subdivision)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ward (country subdivision)">ward</a> politics such as knocking on doors, drumming up votes, and taking complaints. He first ran for the city common council in 1897 and 1898, failing to make the cutoff for a Democratic nomination in ward caucuses each year. Curley claimed because he was at the time outside the <a href="/wiki/Machine_politics" class="mw-redirect" title="Machine politics">political machine</a>, he was denied victory by corrupt counting of the votes.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Curley was successful in 1899, by joining the machine faction controlled by Charles I. Quirk.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> He won election to the state legislature in 1901, and rose in the Democratic organization of Ward 17 to become its chairman. He established the Tammany Club (named in a nod to the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> <a href="/wiki/Tammany_Hall" title="Tammany Hall">Tammany Hall</a> political club) as a platform for his personal political activities, including speechmaking and assisting needy constituents.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Curley would recount stories of the ward's poor and needy lining up outside the club to ask for assistance in securing work or assistance.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  In his first two years on the common council, Curley placed roughly 700 people into what were essentially patronage positions.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  His reputation as an urban populist earned him the unofficial title, "Mayor of the Poor".<sup id="cite_ref-nath_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nath-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> According to the <i>Harvard Crimson</i>:
</p>
<blockquote><p>In his debut, Curley swept the city with a wave of reform that left his critics gasping. He built schools, playgrounds and beaches; he hired new doctors for the city hospital; he extended the transit systems and pulled down old elevated lines, making thousands of jobs. When the banks in Boston refused to lend him money for this spending spree, he bolted traditions and borrowed from banks all over the country.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Curley's first public notoriety came when he was elected to Boston's board of aldermen in 1904 while in prison on a fraud conviction. Curley and an associate, Thomas Curley (no relation), took the civil service exams for postmen in place of two men in their district to help them get the jobs with the federal government. Though the incident gave him a dark reputation in Boston's non-Irish circles, it aided his image among the <a href="/wiki/Irish_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish American">Irish American</a> working class and poor because they saw him as a man willing to stick his neck out to help those in need. During that election, his campaign slogan was, "he did it for a friend."<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  He would also quickly gain a reputation for taking <a href="/wiki/Kickback_(bribery)" title="Kickback (bribery)">kickbacks</a> in exchange for his support.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:James_Michael_Curley_in_1912.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/James_Michael_Curley_in_1912.png/220px-James_Michael_Curley_in_1912.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="286" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/James_Michael_Curley_in_1912.png/330px-James_Michael_Curley_in_1912.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e6/James_Michael_Curley_in_1912.png 2x" data-file-width="375" data-file-height="488" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:James_Michael_Curley_in_1912.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>James Michael Curley in his first term as a Member of Congress in 1912.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1910, while a member of Boston's board of aldermen, Curley decided to run for the 10th District U.S. congressional seat then occupied by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_F._O%27Connell" title="Joseph F. O&#39;Connell">Joseph F. O'Connell</a>.  He made this run even though he actually wanted to be mayor, because <a href="/wiki/John_F._Fitzgerald" title="John F. Fitzgerald">John "Honey Fitz" Fitzgerald</a> wanted to reclaim that seat.  In exchange, Fitzgerald promised not to run for reelection after a single four-year term.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In the previous election for the seat, O'Connell won by a four-vote margin over his Republican opponent,<sup id="cite_ref-The_New_York_Times_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_New_York_Times-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> ex-City Clerk <a href="/wiki/J._Mitchel_Galvin" title="J. Mitchel Galvin">J. Mitchel Galvin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In a three-way primary among O'Connell, Curley, and O'Connell's predecessor <a href="/wiki/William_S._McNary" title="William S. McNary">William S. McNary</a>, Curley defeated O'Connell<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and McNary. After winning the nomination of the Democratic Party Curley went on to win the general election by a substantial plurality over Galvin, who was again the Republican nominee.<sup id="cite_ref-The_New_York_Times_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_New_York_Times-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mayor_of_Boston">Mayor of Boston</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Michael_Curley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mayor of Boston">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Curley's election as Mayor of Boston in <a href="/wiki/Boston_mayoral_election,_1914" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston mayoral election, 1914">January 1914</a> marked his consolidation of control over the city's politics, which he would retain until 1950, serving four separate terms (1914–1918, 1922–1926, 1930–1934 and 1946–1950),<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> and always having influence even when he wasn't in that office.  His first election was marked by a battle against Honey Fitz, who had opted to run for reelection despite his earlier promise not to.  Curley secured Fitzgerald's exit from the race by threatening to expose a dalliance the older man had had with a cigarette girl in a Boston gambling den.  Curley's role in this was abetted by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_H._Coakley" title="Daniel H. Coakley">Daniel H. Coakley</a>, a lawyer of dubious morals whose specialties included extortion and buying off prosecutors to bury criminal charges against his clients.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Curley's first term included his family moving into a luxurious house in <a href="/wiki/Jamaica_Plain,_Boston" class="mw-redirect" title="Jamaica Plain, Boston">Jamaica Plain</a>, which was plainly beyond the means of a typical civil servant's salary.  Begun in 1915, the twenty-plus room mansion was apparently built by contractors seeking favors from Curley, the work frequently recorded as "no charge".  Curley's finances were regularly investigated by the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Finance_Commission" title="Boston Finance Commission">Boston Finance Commission</a>, a body dominated by hostile Protestant Republicans, but he eluded legal charges in part through the intervention of Coakley.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>  He was also able to effectively muzzle investigations by the local press by threatening <a href="/wiki/Libel" class="mw-redirect" title="Libel">libel</a> charges against offending media.  In one notable incident, he also physically assaulted the publisher of the <i>Boston Telegraph</i> for publishing unflattering articles.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In his first term Curley embarked on a series of public improvements, a practice he continued in his later terms as mayor.  Projects included the development of recreational facilities throughout the poorer parts of the city, expansion of its public transit systems, and an enlargement of <a href="/wiki/Boston_City_Hospital" title="Boston City Hospital">Boston City Hospital</a>.  He accomplished this with little regard to city finances, raising property taxes and securing loans from city banks, sometimes by threatening city inspectional actions against bank facilities.  He deliberately tweaked the sensibilities of the Protestant "good government" advocates, suggesting that the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Public_Garden" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston Public Garden">Boston Public Garden</a> be sold off, and that the <a href="/wiki/Shirley-Eustis_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Shirley-Eustis House">Shirley-Eustis House</a> be razed for failing to meet modern codes.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Curley's attempt at reelection was foiled by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Lomasney" title="Martin Lomasney">Martin Lomasney</a>, the boss of Boston's <a href="/wiki/West_End,_Boston" title="West End, Boston">West End</a>.  Lomasney, a longtime opposition figure to Curley in the city, orchestrated the entry of an Irish-American candidate into the <a href="/wiki/Boston_mayoral_election,_1917" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston mayoral election, 1917">1917 mayoral race</a>, who successfully siphoned enough votes away from Curley to hand victory to <a href="/wiki/Andrew_J._Peters" class="mw-redirect" title="Andrew J. Peters">Andrew J. Peters</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>  In 1918, the state legislature acted to curb the ability of Curley to entrench himself in the mayoralty by enacting legislating forbidding Boston mayors from holding consecutive terms.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Governor_of_Massachusetts">Governor of Massachusetts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Michael_Curley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Governor of Massachusetts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:James_Michael_Curley_in_1922.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/James_Michael_Curley_in_1922.png/220px-James_Michael_Curley_in_1922.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="310" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/James_Michael_Curley_in_1922.png/330px-James_Michael_Curley_in_1922.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0d/James_Michael_Curley_in_1922.png 2x" data-file-width="411" data-file-height="580" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:James_Michael_Curley_in_1922.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>James Michael Curley in his second term as Mayor of Boston, in 1922.</div></div></div>
<p>When Curley was denied by a place in the Massachusetts <a href="/wiki/Delegate_(American_politics)" title="Delegate (American politics)">delegation</a> to the <a href="/wiki/1932_Democratic_National_Convention" title="1932 Democratic National Convention">1932 Democratic National Convention</a> by Governor <a href="/wiki/Joseph_B._Ely" title="Joseph B. Ely">Joseph B. Ely</a>, Curley engineered his selection as a delegate from <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> (under the alias of Alcalde Jaime Curleo). Some say his support was instrumental in winning the <a href="/wiki/Presidential_nomination" class="mw-redirect" title="Presidential nomination">presidential nomination</a> for <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a>, but he broke with Roosevelt after the president refused to appoint him <a href="/wiki/United_States_Ambassador_to_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Ambassador to Ireland">Ambassador to Ireland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1924, when he was Mayor of Boston, Curley ran for <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Massachusetts" title="Governor of Massachusetts">Governor of Massachusetts</a>, but was defeated by then Republican Lieutenant Governor <a href="/wiki/Alvan_T._Fuller" title="Alvan T. Fuller">Alvan T. Fuller</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> In 1934, Curley tried again.  This time he defeated Republican Lieutenant Governor <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_G._Bacon" title="Gaspar G. Bacon">Gaspar G. Bacon</a>, an opponent of Roosevelt's New Deal, by more than 100,000.<sup id="cite_ref-ONeill88_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ONeill88-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Curley's single term as governor was described by one commentator as "ludicrous part of the time, shocking most of the time, and tawdry all of the time."<sup id="cite_ref-ONeill88_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ONeill88-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>  It began with a shoving match with outgoing Governor <a href="/wiki/Joseph_B._Ely" title="Joseph B. Ely">Joseph B. Ely</a>, and descended into bare-knuckle politics.  Curley expended significant political capital seeking to defang the Boston Finance Commission, which was closing in on the financial malfeasance of his mayoral terms.  Committee members were accused of failing to do their jobs and impeached, and investigators were fired.  Curley was eventually able to install a more pliant commission membership, under which its attention turned instead to his political opponents.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>  The negative press surrounding these actions ensured a loss of public popularity, as did his failure to significantly address widespread unemployment.  His administration embarked on one major public works project, the <a href="/wiki/Quabbin_Reservoir" title="Quabbin Reservoir">Quabbin Reservoir</a>, whose construction contracts were issued in signature Curley style.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Rather than seeking reelection as governor, Curley opted to run for the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a> in 1936. He lost against a moderate Republican, <a href="/wiki/Henry_Cabot_Lodge,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Cabot Lodge, Jr.">Henry Cabot Lodge, Jr.</a>, in a general election that was otherwise a Democratic landslide.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>  He was twice defeated, in <a href="/wiki/Boston_mayoral_election,_1937" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston mayoral election, 1937">November 1937</a> and <a href="/wiki/Boston_mayoral_election,_1941" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston mayoral election, 1941">November 1941</a>, for the Boston mayoralty by one of his closest former political confidants, <a href="/wiki/Maurice_J._Tobin" title="Maurice J. Tobin">Maurice J. Tobin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>  In 1938 he made another try for the governorship, defeating incumbent Democrat <a href="/wiki/Charles_F._Hurley" title="Charles F. Hurley">Charles F. Hurley</a> in a bruising party primary fight.  He lost the general election to moderate Republican <a href="/wiki/Leverett_Saltonstall" title="Leverett Saltonstall">Leverett Saltonstall</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>  Curley would take his revenge against Tobin later, supporting Republican <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Bradford" title="Robert F. Bradford">Robert F. Bradford</a> when Tobin sought reelection as governor in <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_gubernatorial_election,_1946" class="mw-redirect" title="Massachusetts gubernatorial election, 1946">1946</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After leaving the office of governor, Curley squandered a substantial sum of his money in unsuccessful investments in <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">Nevada</a> gold mines; then he lost a civil suit brought by the <a href="/wiki/Suffolk_County,_Massachusetts" title="Suffolk County, Massachusetts">Suffolk County</a> prosecutor that forced him to forfeit to the city of Boston the $40,000 he received from General Equipment Company for "fixing" a damage claim settlement.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_Congressional_term">Second Congressional term</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Michael_Curley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Second Congressional term">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1942, Curley managed to revive his faltering career by returning to Congress, serving from 1943 to 1947, this time in the <span data-sort-value="Massachusetts11&#160;!"><a href="/wiki/Massachusetts%27s_11th_congressional_district" title="Massachusetts&#39;s 11th congressional district">11th district</a></span>. He defeated his liberal opponent <a href="/wiki/Thomas_H._Eliot" title="Thomas H. Eliot">Thomas H. Eliot</a>, a former <a href="/wiki/New_Deal" title="New Deal">New Deal</a> attorney with an exemplary voting record on behalf of the Roosevelt administration, in the Democratic primary. Eliot was the son of a Unitarian minister and grandson of Harvard president Charles Eliot. Curley campaigned largely on appeals to working class anger toward the <a href="/wiki/White_Anglo-Saxon_Protestant" title="White Anglo-Saxon Protestant">White Anglo-Saxon Protestant</a> <a href="/wiki/Yankee" title="Yankee">Yankee</a> upper class. Ultimately, Curley saw an opening in breaking the lock of ethnic politics in the state with the spectre of growing Communist influence. In a quote from a campaign speech which has famously entered Boston political lore, Curley raised the specter of Communist leanings in his opponent saying, "There is more Americanism in one half of Jim Curley's ass than in that pink body of Tom Eliot." Thus, despite his long-proven corrupting influence and antagonism toward the state's native Yankee population, Curley managed to win over substantial numbers of them, winning the election easily.
</p><p>Curley appears to have been paid off by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph P. Kennedy">Joseph P. Kennedy</a> (who supposedly agreed to pay off some of Curley's debts, and may have helped fund his 1949 run for reelection as mayor) to leave the Congressional seat open in the 1946 election, so that Kennedy's son <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John</a> could run for it without significant Democratic opposition.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Last_term_as_mayor">Last term as mayor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Michael_Curley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Last term as mayor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a result of the extensive corruption in city politics, numerous investigations were conducted against Curley's machine. After several campaigns involving bribery, Curley finally faced felony <a href="/wiki/Indictment" title="Indictment">indictments</a> brought by federal prosecutors. Nonetheless, Curley's popularity with the Irish American community in Boston remained so high that even in the face of this indictment he was re-elected on the slogan "Curley Gets Things Done", winning an unprecedented fourth term as mayor of Boston in <a href="/wiki/Boston_mayoral_election,_1945" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston mayoral election, 1945">November 1945</a>. A second indictment by a federal grand jury, for <a href="/wiki/Mail_fraud" class="mw-redirect" title="Mail fraud">mail fraud</a>, did not harm his campaign, and Curley won the election with 45% of the vote.<sup id="cite_ref-boston_irish_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boston_irish-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>  In June 1947, he was sentenced to 6–18 months on the mail fraud conviction and spent five months at the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Correctional_Institution,_Danbury" title="Federal Correctional Institution, Danbury">Federal Correctional Institution</a> in <a href="/wiki/Danbury,_Connecticut" title="Danbury, Connecticut">Danbury, Connecticut</a>.  His sentence was commuted by <a href="/wiki/Harry_S._Truman" title="Harry S. Truman">President Truman</a>, under pressure from the Massachusetts congressional delegation. City Clerk <a href="/wiki/John_B._Hynes" class="mw-redirect" title="John B. Hynes">John B. Hynes</a> served as acting mayor during his absence. Truman commuted his sentence in 1947, and gave him a full pardon in 1950, for both his 1904 and 1947 convictions.
</p><p>A crowd of thousands greeted Curley upon his return to Boston, with a brass band playing "<a href="/wiki/Hail_to_the_Chief" title="Hail to the Chief">Hail to the Chief</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-boston_irish_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boston_irish-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> In a fit of hubris after his first day back in office, Curley told reporters, "I have accomplished more in one day than has been done in the five months of my absence."<sup id="cite_ref-boston_irish_49-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boston_irish-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Curley's bid for reelection in <a href="/wiki/Boston_mayoral_election,_1949" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston mayoral election, 1949">November 1949</a> was squelched by Hynes, who took Curley's public comments as a personal affront,<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> and marshaled support to defeat him.  While Curley argued Hynes lacked experience, Hynes responded that the city could not "afford the city bosses anymore", and tapped into widespread dissatisfaction with the city's high tax rate to defeat Curley in the primary.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>  During the lame duck period, Curley granted a large number of tax abatements, further hampering the city's finances, and granted exorbitant city contracts to cronies.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hynes was again victorious in a <a href="/wiki/Boston_mayoral_election,_1951" class="mw-redirect" title="Boston mayoral election, 1951">November 1951</a> rematch,<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> and Curley's career in elective politics was over.  He was financially supported by a state-granted pension ushered through the legislature by <a href="/wiki/Tip_O%27Neill" title="Tip O&#39;Neill">Thomas P. "Tip" O'Neill</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>  Curley continued to support other candidates and remained active within the Democratic Party after his defeats.  His death in Boston in 1958 was followed by one of the largest funerals in the city's history.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>James had two brothers: John J. (1872–1944) and Michael (born 1879), who died at 2½.  Curley married twice, first to Mary Emelda Herlihy (1884–1930) in 1906 and then to Gertrude Casey Dennis, widowed mother of two boys, George and Richard. This marriage, on January 7, 1937, was on his last day as governor. George died at the age of 74 while Richard still lives in Massachusetts. Richard has 5 children and 1 grandchild.
</p><p>Curley's personal life was unusually tragic. He outlived his first wife Mary Emelda (<a href="/wiki/N%C3%A9e" class="mw-redirect" title="Née">née</a> Herlihy) and seven of his nine children. His wife died in 1930 after a long battle with cancer. Twin sons John and Joseph died in infancy. Daughter Dorothea died of pneumonia as a teenager. His namesake, James Jr., who was being groomed as Curley's political successor, died in 1931 at age 21 following an operation to remove a gallstone. Son Paul, who was an <a href="/wiki/Alcoholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Alcoholic">alcoholic</a>, died while Curley ran for mayor in 1945. His remaining daughter Mary died of a stroke in February 1950 and when her brother <a href="/wiki/Leo_Curley" title="Leo Curley">Leo</a> was called to the scene, he became so distraught that he, too, suffered a cerebral hemorrhage and died the same day, at age 35. Two remaining sons, George (1919–1983) and Francis X. (1923–1992), a Jesuit priest, outlived Curley.
</p><p>Curley is honored with two statues at <a href="/wiki/Faneuil_Hall" title="Faneuil Hall">Faneuil Hall</a>, across from Boston's new <a href="/wiki/Boston_City_Hall" title="Boston City Hall">City Hall</a>. One shows him seated on a park bench, the other shows him standing, as if giving a speech, a campaign button on his lapel. A few feet away was a bar named for one of his symbols, The Purple Shamrock.
</p><p>His house, known in his time as "the house with the shamrock shutters," located at 350 Jamaicaway, is now a city historical site. His former summer home in <a href="/wiki/Scituate,_Massachusetts" title="Scituate, Massachusetts">Scituate</a> also has shamrock shutters.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Michael_Curley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Curley is considered the inspiration for the protagonist <a href="/wiki/Frank_Skeffington" class="mw-redirect" title="Frank Skeffington">Frank Skeffington</a> in the 1956 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_Hurrah" title="The Last Hurrah">The Last Hurrah</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Edwin_O%27Connor" title="Edwin O&#39;Connor">Edwin O'Connor</a>, on which director <a href="/wiki/John_Ford_(director)" class="mw-redirect" title="John Ford (director)">John Ford</a> based his <a href="/wiki/The_Last_Hurrah_(1958_film)" title="The Last Hurrah (1958 film)">film with the same title</a>. Curley, initially considering legal action, changed his mind, and upon meeting O'Connor, he told him he enjoyed the book, the passage he enjoyed most being: "The part where I die."<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> He did successfully sue the film's producers.<sup id="cite_ref-burke200611_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-burke200611-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Curley was the inspiration for the song "<a href="/wiki/The_Rascal_King_(song)" title="The Rascal King (song)">The Rascal King</a>" on the album <i><a href="/wiki/Let%27s_Face_It" title="Let&#39;s Face It">Let's Face It</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/The_Mighty_Mighty_Bosstones" title="The Mighty Mighty Bosstones">The Mighty Mighty Bosstones</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mtv_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mtv-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>The Curley family still holds Massachusetts auto registration number 5. It is owned by his step-son Richard.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In a tweak at the state's <a href="/wiki/White_Anglo-Saxon_Protestant" title="White Anglo-Saxon Protestant">WASP</a> elite's rupture with its own constituency and origins, Curley appeared at the Harvard University commencement ceremony in 1935 in his role as governor wearing silk stockings, knee britches, a powdered wig, and a three-cornered hat with flowing plume. When University marshals objected to his costume, the story goes, Curley whipped out a copy of the Statutes of the Massachusetts Bay Colony which prescribed proper dress for the occasion and claimed that he was the only person at the ceremony properly dressed, thereby endearing him to many working and middle class Yankees.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>A paper by Harvard economists <a href="/wiki/Andrei_Shleifer" title="Andrei Shleifer">Andrei Shleifer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Edward_Glaeser" title="Edward Glaeser">Edward Glaeser</a>, 'The Curley Effect: The Economics of Shaping the Electorate', describes the strategy used by Curley and other political leaders of increasing their political base by using distortionary economic policies to cause groups which tend to oppose them to emigrate as 'The Curley Effect'.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In the final <a href="/wiki/Southern_Victory_Series" class="mw-redirect" title="Southern Victory Series">Southern Victory Series</a> novel <i><a href="/wiki/Settling_Accounts:_In_at_the_Death" title="Settling Accounts: In at the Death">Settling Accounts: In at the Death</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Harry_Turtledove" title="Harry Turtledove">Harry Turtledove</a>, Jim Curley is a candidate for <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">Vice President of the United States</a>.</li></ul>
