	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Warren Commission</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>The <b>President's Commission on the Assassination of President Kennedy</b>, known unofficially as the <b>Warren Commission</b>, was established by <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a> <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a> through <a href="/wiki/Executive_order_(United_States)" class="mw-redirect" title="Executive order (United States)">Executive Order</a> <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Executive_Order_11130" class="extiw" title="s:Executive Order 11130">11130</a> on November 29, 1963<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> to investigate the <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_John_F._Kennedy" title="Assassination of John F. Kennedy">assassination of United States President John F. Kennedy</a> that had taken place on November 22, 1963.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The U.S. Congress passed Senate Joint Resolution 137 authorizing the Presidential appointed Commission to report on the assassination of President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>, mandating the attendance and testimony of witnesses and the production of evidence.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Its 888-page final report was presented to President Johnson on September 24, 1964<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and made public three days later.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> It concluded that President Kennedy was assassinated by <a href="/wiki/Lee_Harvey_Oswald" title="Lee Harvey Oswald">Lee Harvey Oswald</a> and that Oswald acted entirely alone.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It also concluded that <a href="/wiki/Jack_Ruby" title="Jack Ruby">Jack Ruby</a> acted alone when he killed Oswald two days later.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The Commission's findings have proven controversial and have been both challenged and supported by later studies.
</p><p>The Commission took its unofficial name—the Warren Commission—from its chairman, <a href="/wiki/Chief_Justice_of_the_United_States" title="Chief Justice of the United States">Chief Justice</a> <a href="/wiki/Earl_Warren" title="Earl Warren">Earl Warren</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> According to published transcripts of Johnson's presidential phone conversations, some major officials were opposed to forming such a commission and several commission members took part only reluctantly. One of their chief reservations was that a commission would ultimately create more controversy than consensus.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">Formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren_Commission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Katzenbach" title="Nicholas Katzenbach">Nicholas Katzenbach</a> has been named as providing advice after the assassination of John F. Kennedy that led to the creation of the Warren Commission.<sup id="cite_ref-Savage_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Savage-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> On November 28 he sent a memo to Johnson's <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> aide <a href="/wiki/Bill_Moyers" title="Bill Moyers">Bill Moyers</a> recommending the formation of a <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Commission_(United_States)" title="Presidential Commission (United States)">Presidential Commission</a> to investigate the assassination.<sup id="cite_ref-Savage_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Savage-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Politico;_May_9,_2012_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Politico;_May_9,_2012-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> To combat <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy_assassination_conspiracy_theories" title="John F. Kennedy assassination conspiracy theories">speculation of a conspiracy</a>, Katzenbach said that the results of the FBI's investigation should be made public.<sup id="cite_ref-Savage_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Savage-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Politico;_May_9,_2012_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Politico;_May_9,_2012-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> He wrote, in part: "The public must be satisfied that Oswald was the assassin; that he did not have confederates who are still at large".<sup id="cite_ref-Politico;_May_9,_2012_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Politico;_May_9,_2012-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Four days after Katzenbach's memo, Johnson appointed some of the nation's most prominent figures, including the <a href="/wiki/Chief_Justice_of_the_United_States" title="Chief Justice of the United States">Chief Justice of the United States</a>, to the Commission.<sup id="cite_ref-Savage_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Savage-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Politico;_May_9,_2012_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Politico;_May_9,_2012-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meetings">Meetings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren_Commission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Meetings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Warren Commission met formally for the first time on December 5, 1963 on the second floor of the <a href="/wiki/National_Archives_Building" title="National Archives Building">National Archives Building</a> in Washington, D.C.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The Commission conducted its business primarily in closed sessions, but these were not <i>secret</i> sessions.
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Two misconceptions about the Warren Commission hearing need to be clarified...hearings were closed to the public unless the witness appearing before the Commission requested an open hearing. No witness except one...requested an open hearing... Second, although the hearings (except one) were conducted in private, they were not secret. In a secret hearing, the witness is instructed not to disclose his testimony to any third party, and the hearing testimony is not published for public consumption. The witnesses who appeared before the Commission were free to repeat what they said to anyone they pleased, and <i>all</i> of their testimony was subsequently published in the first fifteen volumes put out by the Warren Commission.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conclusions_of_the_report">Conclusions of the report</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren_Commission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conclusions of the report">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The report concluded:
</p>
<ol><li>The shots which killed President Kennedy and wounded Governor Connally were fired from the sixth floor window at the southeast corner of the Texas School Book Depository.</li>
<li>President Kennedy was first struck by a bullet which entered at the back of his neck and exited through the lower front portion of his neck, causing a wound which would not necessarily have been lethal. The President was struck a second time by a bullet which entered the right-rear portion of his head, causing a massive and fatal wound.</li>
<li>Governor Connally was struck by a bullet which entered on the right side of his back and traveled downward through the right side of his chest, exiting below his right nipple. This bullet then passed through his right wrist and entered his left thigh where it caused a superficial wound.</li>
<li>There is no credible evidence that the shots were fired from the Triple Underpass, ahead of the motorcade, or from any other location.</li>
<li>The weight of the evidence indicates that there were three shots fired.</li>
<li>Although it is not necessary to any essential findings of the Commission to determine just which shot hit Governor Connally, there is very persuasive evidence from the experts to indicate that the same bullet which pierced the President's throat also caused Governor Connally's wounds. However, Governor Connally's testimony and certain other factors have given rise to some difference of opinion as to this probability but there is no question in the mind of any member of the Commission that all the shots which caused the President's and Governor Connally's wounds were fired from the sixth floor window of the Texas School Book Depository.</li>
<li>The shots which killed President Kennedy and wounded Governor Connally were fired by Lee Harvey Oswald.</li>
<li>Oswald killed Dallas Police Patrolman J. D. Tippit approximately 45 minutes after the assassination.</li>
<li>Ruby entered the basement of the Dallas Police Department and killed Lee Harvey Oswald and there  is no evidence to support the rumor that Ruby may have been assisted by any members of the Dallas Police Department.</li>
<li>The Commission has found no evidence that either Lee Harvey Oswald or Jack Ruby was part of any conspiracy, domestic or foreign, to assassinate President Kennedy.</li>
<li>The Commission has found no evidence of conspiracy, subversion, or disloyalty to the U.S. Government by any Federal, State, or local official.</li>
<li>The Commission could not make any definitive determination of Oswald's motives.</li>
<li>the Commission believes that recommendations for improvements in Presidential protection are compelled by the facts disclosed in this investigation.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death_of_Lee_Harvey_Oswald">Death of Lee Harvey Oswald</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren_Commission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Death of Lee Harvey Oswald">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In response to Jack Ruby's shooting of Lee Harvey Oswald, the Warren Commission declared that the news media must share responsibility with the Dallas police department for "the breakdown of law enforcement" that led to Oswald's death. In addition to the police department's "inadequacy of coordination," the Warren Commission noted that "these additional deficiencies [in security] were related directly to the decision to admit newsmen to the basement."
</p><p>The commission concluded that the pressure of press, radio, and television for information about Oswald's prison transfer resulted in lax security standards for admission to the basement, allowing Ruby to enter and subsequently shoot Oswald, noting that "the acceptance of inadequate press credentials posed a clear avenue for a one-man assault." Oswald's death was said to have been a direct result of "the failure of the police to remove Oswald secretly or control the crowd in the basement."
</p><p>The consequence of Oswald's death, according to the Commission, was that "it was no longer possible to arrive at the complete story of the assassination of John F. Kennedy through normal judicial procedures during the trial of the alleged assassin." While the Commission noted that the prime responsibility was that of the police department, it also recommended the adoption of a new "code of conduct" for news professionals regarding the collecting and presenting of information to the public that would ensure "there [would] be no interference with pending criminal investigations, court proceedings, or the right of individuals to a fair trial."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Secret_Service">Secret Service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren_Commission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Secret Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The findings prompted the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secret_Service" title="United States Secret Service">Secret Service</a> to make numerous modifications to its security procedures.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commission_records">Commission records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren_Commission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Commission records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 1964, two months after the publication of its 888-page report, the Commission published twenty-six volumes of supporting documents, including the testimony or depositions of 552 witnesses and more than 3,100 exhibits.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> All of the commission's records were then transferred on November 23 to the <a href="/wiki/National_Archives_and_Records_Administration" title="National Archives and Records Administration">National Archives</a>. The unpublished portion of those records was initially sealed for 75 years (to 2039) under a general National Archives policy that applied to all federal investigations by the executive branch of government,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> a period "intended to serve as protection for innocent persons who could otherwise be damaged because of their relationship with participants in the case."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The 75-year rule no longer exists, supplanted by the <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_Information_Act_(United_States)" title="Freedom of Information Act (United States)">Freedom of Information Act</a> of 1966 and the <a href="/wiki/President_John_F._Kennedy_Assassination_Records_Collection_Act_of_1992" title="President John F. Kennedy Assassination Records Collection Act of 1992">JFK Records Act of 1992</a>. By 1992, 98 percent of the Warren Commission records had been released to the public.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Six years later, at the conclusion of the <a href="/wiki/Assassination_Records_Review_Board" class="mw-redirect" title="Assassination Records Review Board">Assassination Records Review Board</a>'s work, all Warren Commission records, except those records that contained <a href="/wiki/Tax_return_(United_States)" title="Tax return (United States)">tax return</a> information, were available to the public with <a href="/wiki/Sanitization_(classified_information)" title="Sanitization (classified information)">redactions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> The remaining Kennedy assassination related documents were partly released to the public on October 26, 2017,<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> twenty-five years after the passage of the JFK Records Act. President Donald Trump, as directed by the FBI and the CIA,<sup id="cite_ref-businessinsider.com_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-businessinsider.com-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> took action on that date to withhold certain remaining files, delaying the release until April 26, 2018,<sup id="cite_ref-businessinsider.com_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-businessinsider.com-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> then on April 26, 2018 took action to further withhold the records "until 2021".<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="CIA_.22Benign_Cover-up.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="CIA_&quot;Benign_Cover-up&quot;">CIA "Benign Cover-up"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren_Commission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: CIA &quot;Benign Cover-up&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>CIA Director <a href="/wiki/John_A._McCone" title="John A. McCone">McCone</a> was "complicit" in a <a href="/wiki/Central_Intelligence_Agency" title="Central Intelligence Agency">Central Intelligence Agency</a> "benign cover-up" by withholding information from the Warren Commission, according to a report by the CIA Chief Historian David Robarge released to the public in 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-politico.com_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-politico.com-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> According to this CIA report, CIA officers had been instructed to give only "passive, reactive, and selective" assistance to the commission, in order to keep the commission focused on "what the Agency believed at the time was the 'best truth' — that Lee Harvey Oswald, for as yet undetermined motives, had acted alone in killing John Kennedy." The CIA was also covering up evidence that the CIA may have been in communication with Oswald before 1963, according to the CIA findings.<sup id="cite_ref-politico.com_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-politico.com-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Also withheld were earlier CIA plots, involving CIA links with the Mafia, to assassinate Cuban president <a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a>, which might have been considered to provide a motive to assassinate Kennedy. The report concluded "In the long term, the decision of John McCone and Agency leaders in 1964 not to disclose information about CIA's anti-Castro schemes might have done more to undermine the credibility of the commission than anything else that happened while it was conducting its investigation."<sup id="cite_ref-politico-20151006_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-politico-20151006-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cia-2013_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cia-2013-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Skepticism">Skepticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren_Commission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Skepticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WC_Vol18_CE903_SBT_alignement.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/WC_Vol18_CE903_SBT_alignement.jpg/220px-WC_Vol18_CE903_SBT_alignement.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/WC_Vol18_CE903_SBT_alignement.jpg/330px-WC_Vol18_CE903_SBT_alignement.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/WC_Vol18_CE903_SBT_alignement.jpg/440px-WC_Vol18_CE903_SBT_alignement.jpg 2x" data-file-width="537" data-file-height="378" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WC_Vol18_CE903_SBT_alignement.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Arlen_Specter" title="Arlen Specter">Arlen Specter</a> reproducing the assumed alignment of the <a href="/wiki/Single-bullet_theory" title="Single-bullet theory">single-bullet theory</a></div></div></div>
<p>Four of the seven members of the commission, Boggs, Cooper, McCloy, and Russell, had serious doubts regarding the conclusions of the commission that the President and Governor Connally were both wounded by the "magic bullet" and regarding the view that Oswald had acted alone.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ARRB-ES_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ARRB-ES-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> In the years following the release of its report and 26 investigatory evidence volumes in 1964, the Warren Commission has been frequently criticized for some of its methods, important omissions, and conclusions.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1992, following popular political pressure in the wake of the film <i><a href="/wiki/JFK_(film)" title="JFK (film)">JFK</a></i>, the <a href="/wiki/Assassination_Records_Review_Board" class="mw-redirect" title="Assassination Records Review Board">Assassination Records Review Board</a> (ARRB) was created by the JFK Records Act to collect and preserve the documents relating to the assassination. In a footnote in its final report, the ARRB wrote: "Doubts about the Warren Commission's findings were not restricted to ordinary Americans. Well before 1978, President Johnson, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Robert Kennedy">Robert Kennedy</a>, and four of the seven members of the Warren Commission all articulated, if sometimes off the record, some level of skepticism about the Commission's basic findings."<sup id="cite_ref-ARRB-ES_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ARRB-ES-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_investigations">Other investigations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warren_Commission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other investigations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Three other U.S. government investigations have agreed with the Warren Commission's conclusion that two shots struck JFK from the rear: the 1968 panel set by Attorney General <a href="/wiki/Ramsey_Clark" title="Ramsey Clark">Ramsey Clark</a>, the 1975 <a href="/wiki/United_States_President%27s_Commission_on_CIA_activities_within_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="United States President&#39;s Commission on CIA activities within the United States">Rockefeller Commission</a>, and the 1978-79 <a href="/wiki/House_Select_Committee_on_Assassinations" class="mw-redirect" title="House Select Committee on Assassinations">House Select Committee on Assassinations</a> (HSCA), which reexamined the evidence with the help of the largest forensics panel. The HSCA involved Congressional hearings and ultimately concluded that Oswald assassinated Kennedy, probably as the result of a conspiracy.  The HSCA concluded that Oswald fired shots number one, two, and four, and that an unknown assassin fired shot number three (but missed) from near the corner of a picket fence that was above and to President Kennedy's right front on the <a href="/wiki/Dealey_Plaza" title="Dealey Plaza">Dealey Plaza</a> <a href="/wiki/Grassy_knoll" class="mw-redirect" title="Grassy knoll">grassy knoll</a>. However, this conclusion has also been criticized, especially for its reliance upon disputed acoustic evidence. The HSCA Final Report in 1979 did agree with the Warren Report's conclusion in 1964 that two bullets caused all of President Kennedy's and Governor Connally's injuries, and that both bullets were fired by Oswald from the sixth floor of the Texas School Book Depository.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As part of its investigation, the HSCA also evaluated the performance of the Warren Commission, which included interviews and public testimony from the two surviving Commission members (Ford and McCloy) and various Commission legal counsel staff. The Committee concluded in their final report that the Commission was reasonably thorough and acted in good faith, but failed to adequately address the possibility of conspiracy.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In his September 1978 testimony to the HSCA, President Ford defended the Warren Commission's investigation as thorough.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawrence_Journal_Daily_World;_September_21,_1978_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawrence_Journal_Daily_World;_September_21,_1978-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Ford stated that knowledge of the assassination plots against Castro may have affected the scope of the Commission's investigation but expressed doubt that it would have altered its finding that Oswald acted alone in assassinating Kennedy.<sup id="cite_ref-Lawrence_Journal_Daily_World;_September_21,_1978_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lawrence_Journal_Daily_World;_September_21,_1978-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
