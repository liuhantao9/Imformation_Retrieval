scope="row">Type</th><td class="category">Settlement</td></tr></tbody></table><p><i><b>Jodensavanne</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>, "<a href="/wiki/Jew" class="mw-redirect" title="Jew">Jewish</a> <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">Savanna</a>") was an agricultural community in <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> established by Jews fleeing persecution in Spain. It was located in <a href="/wiki/Para_District" title="Para District">Para District</a>, about 50&#160;km (31&#160;mi) south of the capital <a href="/wiki/Paramaribo" title="Paramaribo">Paramaribo</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/Suriname_River" title="Suriname River">Suriname River</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">Sugarcane</a> <a href="/wiki/Sugar_plantations_in_the_Caribbean" title="Sugar plantations in the Caribbean">plantations</a> were established and slaves used. The colony faced an attack and heavy levies imposed by a French captain, competition with sugar beets, disease and revolts from indigenous people and slaves. The community eventually relocated to the capital of <a href="/wiki/Paramaribo" title="Paramaribo">Paramaribo</a>. Clearing of grave-sites and maintenance of the synagogue ruins has taken place in the 21st century.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jodensavanne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jodensavanne_1830.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Jodensavanne_1830.jpg/180px-Jodensavanne_1830.jpg" decoding="async" width="180" height="118" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Jodensavanne_1830.jpg/270px-Jodensavanne_1830.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Jodensavanne_1830.jpg/360px-Jodensavanne_1830.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1884" data-file-height="1233" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jodensavanne_1830.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Jodensavanne around 1830</div></div></div>
<p>In 1639, the English government allowed <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">Sephardi Jews</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> to settle the area. They came first to the old capital <a href="/wiki/Torarica" title="Torarica">Torarica</a>.On April 8, 1651, a petition was sent to the King of England by Benjamin de Caseras, Henry de Caseras, and Jacob Fraso for the permission to live and trade in the territories of <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> and Barbados, marking a solid date for the origins of a Jewish community being established within the territory. In the year 1652, a new group of approximately twelve hundred that migrated under the leadership of Lord Willoughby (<a href="/wiki/Francis_Willoughby,_5th_Baron_Willoughby_of_Parham" title="Francis Willoughby, 5th Baron Willoughby of Parham">Francis Willoughby, 5th Baron Willoughby of Parham</a>) settled in the area now known as Jodensavanne. A third group came 1664, after their expulsion from <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a> and then <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/David_Cohen_Nassy" title="David Cohen Nassy">David Cohen Nassy</a>. In 1670, according to the Essai Historique, approximately two hundred or so Jewish peoples had left Suriname, and in 1677, a year before the Carib assault on Jodensavanne, ten Jewish families had left with their slaves.
</p><p>The Jewish community was granted some autonomy<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and developed a <a href="/wiki/Sugar-cane" class="mw-redirect" title="Sugar-cane">sugar-cane</a> <a href="/wiki/Plantation_economy" title="Plantation economy">plantation economy</a>. European settlements including in the Jodensavanne were attacked by Carib (<a href="/wiki/Kalina_people" title="Kalina people">Kalina people</a>) in the latter part of 1678 and slaves also revolted.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The congregation <i>Beracha ve Shalom</i> ("Blessings and Peace") was founded, with the first wooden synagogue in the community (the <a href="/wiki/Oldest_synagogues_in_the_world#South_America_and_Caribbean" class="mw-redirect" title="Oldest synagogues in the world">3rd synagogue in South America</a>) built between 1665 and 1671 and a second, made of imported brick, was constructed in 1685. Before the construction of the <i>Beracha ve Shalom</i>, there had been no synagogue of major architectural significance in the Americas.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The centennial celebration of the Synagogue, celebrated in October, was said to have an attendance of more than 1500 persons.
</p><p>Jodensavanne declined during the mid-18th century, and most of its population moved to <a href="/wiki/Paramaribo" title="Paramaribo">Paramaribo</a>. The population was approximated to be around twenty-two, excluding slaves, in 1790, and dropped to less than ten by the early 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The colony continued to survive until it was destroyed in 1832 by a slave revolt and resulting fire. The location served as a camp for political prisoners in the Second World War.
</p><p>Historian <a href="/wiki/Natalie_Zemon_Davis" title="Natalie Zemon Davis">Natalie Zemon Davis</a> is working on a history of 18th century Jodensavanne, focusing on <a href="/wiki/David_Cohen_Nassy" title="David Cohen Nassy">David Isaac Cohen Nassy</a> (born 1747) and relations between blacks and whites in the Jewish community.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> An article titled 'Regaining Jerusalem' was published in 2016 by Davis, detailing a celebration of <a href="/wiki/Passover" title="Passover">Passover</a> within Jodensavanne.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Gravestone_-_Jodensavanne_(Jewish_Savanna)_(23831044676).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Gravestone_-_Jodensavanne_%28Jewish_Savanna%29_%2823831044676%29.jpg/220px-Gravestone_-_Jodensavanne_%28Jewish_Savanna%29_%2823831044676%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Gravestone_-_Jodensavanne_%28Jewish_Savanna%29_%2823831044676%29.jpg/330px-Gravestone_-_Jodensavanne_%28Jewish_Savanna%29_%2823831044676%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Gravestone_-_Jodensavanne_%28Jewish_Savanna%29_%2823831044676%29.jpg/440px-Gravestone_-_Jodensavanne_%28Jewish_Savanna%29_%2823831044676%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3860" data-file-height="2573" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Gravestone_-_Jodensavanne_(Jewish_Savanna)_(23831044676).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A recovered gravestone from one of the cemeteries located in Jodensavanne, inscribed in Hebrew</div></div></div>
<p>As of the current day, all that remains at the site of Jodensavanne are the remnants of the <i>Berache ve Shalom</i> Synagogue, alongside three cemeteries, of which the headstones are primarily inscribed with Hebrew and Portuguese.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Archaeological Institute of the Americas, in partnership with the University of Suriname, engaged in a project labeled as the 'Interactive Dig Jodensavanne', of which conservation efforts and record-keeping projects have been active since 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jodensavanne&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The population at Jodensavanne has been approximated in two ideals. Foremost, as held by sources such as the Essai Historique,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> would assert the population to be held at approximately one thousand in 1677; in contrast, historians such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Harry_Friedenwald&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Harry Friedenwald (page does not exist)">Harry Friedenwald</a> have published on the lack of strong Jewish presence on the island, an assertion that would imply a less populated Jodensavanne than most assertions.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Algemene_kaart_van_Suriname,_Alexander_de_Lavaux,_Hendrik_de_Leth,_Directeurs_van_de_soci%C3%ABteit_van_Suriname,_1737_-_1757_-_Rijksmuseum.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Algemene_kaart_van_Suriname%2C_Alexander_de_Lavaux%2C_Hendrik_de_Leth%2C_Directeurs_van_de_soci%C3%ABteit_van_Suriname%2C_1737_-_1757_-_Rijksmuseum.jpg/220px-Algemene_kaart_van_Suriname%2C_Alexander_de_Lavaux%2C_Hendrik_de_Leth%2C_Directeurs_van_de_soci%C3%ABteit_van_Suriname%2C_1737_-_1757_-_Rijksmuseum.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Algemene_kaart_van_Suriname%2C_Alexander_de_Lavaux%2C_Hendrik_de_Leth%2C_Directeurs_van_de_soci%C3%ABteit_van_Suriname%2C_1737_-_1757_-_Rijksmuseum.jpg/330px-Algemene_kaart_van_Suriname%2C_Alexander_de_Lavaux%2C_Hendrik_de_Leth%2C_Directeurs_van_de_soci%C3%ABteit_van_Suriname%2C_1737_-_1757_-_Rijksmuseum.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Algemene_kaart_van_Suriname%2C_Alexander_de_Lavaux%2C_Hendrik_de_Leth%2C_Directeurs_van_de_soci%C3%ABteit_van_Suriname%2C_1737_-_1757_-_Rijksmuseum.jpg/440px-Algemene_kaart_van_Suriname%2C_Alexander_de_Lavaux%2C_Hendrik_de_Leth%2C_Directeurs_van_de_soci%C3%ABteit_van_Suriname%2C_1737_-_1757_-_Rijksmuseum.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5977" data-file-height="4007" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Algemene_kaart_van_Suriname,_Alexander_de_Lavaux,_Hendrik_de_Leth,_Directeurs_van_de_soci%C3%ABteit_van_Suriname,_1737_-_1757_-_Rijksmuseum.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A map detailing and listing the plantations found alongside the Surinam river, the location of Jodensavanne provided.</div></div></div>
<p>There were around 70 existing plantations along the <a href="/wiki/Suriname_River" title="Suriname River">Suriname River</a> in 1750, most of which bearing 'Jewish' names, such as the thousand acre properties owned by one Solomon Meza.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> These plantations were marked and identified through an 18th-century map titled, "<i>Algemeene Kaart van de Colonie of Provintie van Surinam</i>", drawn by engineer Alexander de Lavaux, a Berlin native who served in Prussian forces.
</p><p>There were several cemeteries located within Jodensavanne, of which the most heavily used, and first existing cemetery is known as the Cassipora Cemetery. Named in due part to the Cassipora Creek that stems from the Surinam river, it is expected to hold approximately two hundred tombstones, the earliest of which being from the early 17th century, and the most recent believed to have been constructed in 1840. The headstones here are primarily inscribed with Hebrew, Portuguese, and Dutch, and there exist several <a href="/wiki/Ohel_(grave)" title="Ohel (grave)">ohelim</a> in the area as well, an indication of the Jewish community structures within the settlement.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
