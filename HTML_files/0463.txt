	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Luddite</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Organization of English workers in the 19th century protesting adoption of textile machinery</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Ludites" class="mw-redirect" title="Ludites">Ludites</a>.</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For the EP by Grotus, see <a href="/wiki/Luddite_(EP)" title="Luddite (EP)">Luddite (EP)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">

</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Luddite.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Luddite.jpg/250px-Luddite.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="375" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Luddite.jpg/375px-Luddite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Luddite.jpg/500px-Luddite.jpg 2x" data-file-width="940" data-file-height="1411" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Luddite.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>The Leader of the Luddites</i>, engraving of 1812</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>Luddites</b> were a secret oath-based organization<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> of English textile workers in the 19th century, a radical faction which destroyed textile machinery as a form of protest. The group was protesting against the use of machinery in a "fraudulent and deceitful manner" to get around standard labour practices.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  Luddites feared that the time spent learning the skills of their craft would go to waste, as machines would replace their role in the industry.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Over time, however, the term has come to mean one opposed to industrialisation, automation, computerisation, or new technologies in general.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The Luddite movement began in <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a> in England and culminated in a region-wide rebellion that lasted from 1811 to 1816. Mill and factory owners took to shooting protesters and eventually the movement was suppressed with legal and military force.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luddite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name Luddite (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/ʌ/: &#39;u&#39; in &#39;cut&#39;">ʌ</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/./: syllable break">.</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is of uncertain origin. The movement was said to be named after <a href="/wiki/Ned_Ludd" title="Ned Ludd">Ned Ludd</a>, an apprentice who allegedly smashed two <a href="/wiki/Stocking_frame" title="Stocking frame">stocking frames</a> in 1779 and whose name had become emblematic of machine destroyers. Ned Ludd, however, was completely fictional and used as a way to shock and provoke the government.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Palmer_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Palmer-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Chambers_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chambers-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The name developed into the imaginary General Ludd or King Ludd, who was reputed to live in <a href="/wiki/Sherwood_Forest" title="Sherwood Forest">Sherwood Forest</a> like <a href="/wiki/Robin_Hood" title="Robin Hood">Robin Hood</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_precedents">Historical precedents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luddite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical precedents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The lower classes of the 18th century were not openly disloyal to the king or government, generally speaking,<sup id="cite_ref-Wearmouth1945_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wearmouth1945-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and violent action was rare because punishments were harsh. The majority of individuals were primarily concerned with meeting their own daily needs.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Working conditions were harsh in the English textile mills at the time but efficient enough to threaten the livelihoods of skilled artisans. The new inventions produced faster and cheaper labour because machines were operated by less-skilled, low-wage labourers, and the Luddite goal was to gain a better bargaining position with their employers.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kevin Binfield asserts that organized action by <a href="/wiki/Stockinger_(occupation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stockinger (occupation)">stockingers</a> had occurred at various times since 1675, and he suggests that the movements of the early 19th century should be viewed in the context of the hardships suffered by the working class during the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>, rather than as an absolute aversion to machinery.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-thomis_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thomis-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Irregular rises in food prices provoked the <a href="/wiki/Keelmen" title="Keelmen">Keelmen</a> to riot in the <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Tyne" title="Port of Tyne">port of Tyne</a> in 1710<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and tin miners to steal from granaries at <a href="/wiki/Falmouth,_Cornwall" title="Falmouth, Cornwall">Falmouth</a> in 1727. There was a rebellion in <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Durham,_England" title="Durham, England">Durham</a> in 1740, and an assault on Quaker corn dealers in 1756. Skilled artisans in the cloth, building, shipbuilding, printing, and cutlery trades organized friendly societies to peacefully insure themselves against unemployment, sickness, and intrusion of foreign labour into their trades, as was common among guilds.<sup id="cite_ref-Charles_Wilson_1965_p._344-5_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charles_Wilson_1965_p._344-5-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;b&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Malcolm L. Thomis argued in his 1970 history <i>The Luddites</i> that machine-breaking was one of a very few tactics that workers could use to increase pressure on employers, to undermine lower-paid competing workers, and to create solidarity among workers. "These attacks on machines did not imply any necessary hostility to machinery as such; machinery was just a conveniently exposed target against which an attack could be made."<sup id="cite_ref-thomis_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thomis-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> An agricultural variant of Luddism occurred during the widespread <a href="/wiki/Swing_Riots" title="Swing Riots">Swing Riots</a> of 1830 in southern and eastern England, centering on breaking threshing machines.<sup id="cite_ref-harrison249_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-harrison249-21">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_of_the_movement">Birth of the movement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luddite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Birth of the movement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl><dd><i>See also <a href="/wiki/Barth%C3%A9lemy_Thimonnier" title="Barthélemy Thimonnier">Barthélemy Thimonnier</a>, whose sewing machines were destroyed by tailors who believed that their jobs were threatened</i></dd></dl>
<p><a href="/wiki/Loom#Handloom" title="Loom">Handloom</a> weavers burned mills and pieces of factory machinery. Textile workers destroyed industrial equipment during the late 18th century,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> prompting acts such as the <a href="/wiki/Protection_of_Stocking_Frames,_etc._Act_1788" title="Protection of Stocking Frames, etc. Act 1788">Protection of Stocking Frames, etc. Act 1788</a>.
</p><p>The Luddite movement emerged during the harsh economic climate of the <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>, which saw a rise of difficult working conditions in the new textile factories. Luddites objected primarily to the rising popularity of automated textile equipment, threatening the jobs and livelihoods of skilled workers as this technology allowed them to be replaced by cheaper and less skilled workers.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-22">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>  The movement began in <a href="/wiki/Arnold,_Nottinghamshire" title="Arnold, Nottinghamshire">Arnold</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a> on 11 March 1811 and spread rapidly throughout England over the following two years.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Conniff_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conniff-24">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The Luddites met at night on the moors surrounding industrial towns to practice drills and maneuvers. Their main areas of operation began in <a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire" title="Nottinghamshire">Nottinghamshire</a> in November 1811, followed by the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a> in early 1812 then <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a> by March 1813. They smashed <a href="/wiki/Stocking_frame" title="Stocking frame">stocking frames</a> and cropping frames among others. There does not seem to have been any political motivation behind the Luddite riots and there was no national organization; the men were merely attacking what they saw as the reason for the decline in their livelihoods.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Luddites battled the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> at Burton's Mill in <a href="/wiki/Middleton,_Greater_Manchester" title="Middleton, Greater Manchester">Middleton</a> and at <a href="/wiki/Westhoughton_Mill" title="Westhoughton Mill">Westhoughton Mill</a>, both in Lancashire.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The Luddites and their supporters anonymously sent death threats to, and possibly attacked, magistrates and food merchants. Activists smashed Heathcote's lacemaking machine in Loughborough in 1816.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESale1995188_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESale1995188-27">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> He and other industrialists had secret chambers constructed in their buildings that could be used as hiding places during an attack.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1817, an unemployed Nottingham <a href="/wiki/Stockinger_(occupation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stockinger (occupation)">stockinger</a> and probably ex-Luddite, named <a href="/wiki/Jeremiah_Brandreth" title="Jeremiah Brandreth">Jeremiah Brandreth</a> led the <a href="/wiki/Pentrich_Rising" class="mw-redirect" title="Pentrich Rising">Pentrich Rising</a>. While this was a general uprising unrelated to machinery, it can be viewed as the last major Luddite act.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_response">Government response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luddite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Government response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:FrameBreaking-1812.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/FrameBreaking-1812.jpg/220px-FrameBreaking-1812.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="192" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/FrameBreaking-1812.jpg/330px-FrameBreaking-1812.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/FrameBreaking-1812.jpg/440px-FrameBreaking-1812.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="1045" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:FrameBreaking-1812.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Later interpretation of machine breaking (1812), showing two men superimposed on an 1844 engraving from the Penny magazine which shows a post 1820s <a href="/wiki/Jacquard_loom" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacquard loom">Jacquard loom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;c&#93;</a></sup> Machine-breaking was criminalised by the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">Parliament of the United Kingdom</a> as early as 1721, the penalty being <a href="/wiki/Penal_transportation" title="Penal transportation">penal transportation</a>, but as a result of continued opposition to mechanisation the <a href="/wiki/Frame_Breaking_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Frame Breaking Act">Frame Breaking Act 1812</a> made the <a href="/wiki/Death_penalty" class="mw-redirect" title="Death penalty">death penalty</a> available: see "<a href="/wiki/Criminal_damage_in_English_law" title="Criminal damage in English law">criminal damage in English law</a>".</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> clashed with the Luddites on several occasions. At one time there were more British soldiers fighting the Luddites than there were fighting <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_I_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon I of France">Napoleon</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">Iberian Peninsula</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;d&#93;</a></sup> Three Luddites, led by George Mellor, ambushed and assassinated mill owner William Horsfall of Ottiwells Mill in <a href="/wiki/Marsden,_West_Yorkshire" title="Marsden, West Yorkshire">Marsden, West Yorkshire</a> at <a href="/wiki/Crosland_Moor" title="Crosland Moor">Crosland Moor</a> in <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>. Horsfall had remarked that he would "Ride up to his saddle in Luddite blood".<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Mellor fired the fatal shot to Horsfall's groin, and all three men were arrested.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lord_Byron" title="Lord Byron">Lord Byron</a> denounced what he considered to be the plight of the working class, the government's inane policies and ruthless repression in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> on 27 February 1812: "I have been in some of the most oppressed provinces of <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">Turkey</a>; but never, under the most despotic of infidel governments, did I behold such squalid wretchedness as I have seen since my return, in the very heart of a Christian country".<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/British_government" class="mw-redirect" title="British government">British government</a> sought to suppress the Luddite movement with a mass trial at <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> in January 1813, following the attack on Cartwrights mill at Rawfolds near Cleckheaton. The government charged over 60 men, including Mellor and his companions, with various crimes in connection with Luddite activities. While some of those charged were actual Luddites, many had no connection to the movement. Although the proceedings were legitimate jury trials, many were abandoned due to lack of evidence and 30 men were acquitted. These trials were certainly intended to act as <a href="/wiki/Show_trials" class="mw-redirect" title="Show trials">show trials</a> to deter other Luddites from continuing their activities. The harsh sentences of those found guilty, which included <a href="/wiki/Execution" class="mw-redirect" title="Execution">execution</a> and <a href="/wiki/Penal_transportation" title="Penal transportation">penal transportation</a>, quickly ended the movement.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
Parliament made "machine breaking" (i.e. industrial <a href="/wiki/Sabotage" title="Sabotage">sabotage</a>) a <a href="/wiki/Capital_crime" class="mw-redirect" title="Capital crime">capital crime</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Destruction_of_Stocking_Frames,_etc._Act_1812" title="Destruction of Stocking Frames, etc. Act 1812">Frame Breaking Act</a> of 1812.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/George_Gordon_Byron,_6th_Baron_Byron" class="mw-redirect" title="George Gordon Byron, 6th Baron Byron">Lord Byron</a> opposed this legislation, becoming one of the few prominent defenders of the Luddites after the treatment of the defendants at the York trials.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Ironically, Lord Byron's only legitimate daughter <a href="/wiki/Ada_Lovelace" title="Ada Lovelace">Ada Lovelace</a> would become the first computer programmer by combining the technology of the <a href="/wiki/Analytical_Engine" title="Analytical Engine">Analytical Engine</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Jacquard_loom" class="mw-redirect" title="Jacquard loom">Jacquard loom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luddite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="In_retrospect"></span>
</p>
<table class="box-Cleanup plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-Cleanup" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png" decoding="async" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/60px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/80px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="48" data-file-height="48" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article may <b>require <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Cleanup" title="Wikipedia:Cleanup">cleanup</a></b> to meet Wikipedia's <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style">quality standards</a>. The specific problem is: <b>Needs to be related to Luddite protests and timeframe</b><span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Luddite&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> if you can.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">August 2019</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>In the 19th century, occupations that arose from the growth of trade and shipping in ports, also in "domestic" manufacturers, were notorious for precarious employment prospects. Underemployment was chronic during this period,<sup id="cite_ref-Charles_Wilson_1965_p._344-5_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charles_Wilson_1965_p._344-5-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and it was common practice to retain a larger workforce than was typically necessary for insurance against labour shortages in boom times.<sup id="cite_ref-Charles_Wilson_1965_p._344-5_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charles_Wilson_1965_p._344-5-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Moreover, the organization of manufacture by merchant-capitalists in the textile industry was inherently unstable. While the financiers' capital was still largely invested in raw material, it was easy to increase commitment where trade was good and almost as easy to cut back when times were bad. Merchant-capitalists lacked the incentive of later factory owners, whose capital was invested in building and plants, to maintain a steady rate of production and return on fixed capital. The combination of seasonal variations in wage rates and violent short-term fluctuations springing from harvests and war produced periodic outbreaks of violence.<sup id="cite_ref-Charles_Wilson_1965_p._344-5_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Charles_Wilson_1965_p._344-5-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_usage">Modern usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Luddite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Modern usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1956, a speech<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (August 2019)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> said that "organized workers were by no means wedded to a Luddite Philosophy."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESale1995205_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESale1995205-39">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> More recently, the term <a href="/wiki/Neo-Luddism" title="Neo-Luddism">Neo-Luddism</a> has emerged to describe opposition to many forms of technology.<sup id="cite_ref-jonesagainstechnology_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jonesagainstechnology-40">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> According to a manifesto drawn up by the Second Luddite Congress (April 1996; Barnesville, Ohio), Neo-Luddism is "a leaderless movement of passive resistance to consumerism and the increasingly bizarre and frightening technologies of the Computer Age."<sup id="cite_ref-Sale_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sale-41">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "<a href="/wiki/Luddite_fallacy" class="mw-redirect" title="Luddite fallacy">Luddite fallacy</a>" is used by economists in reference to the fear that <a href="/wiki/Technological_unemployment" title="Technological unemployment">technological unemployment</a> inevitably generates <a href="/wiki/Structural_unemployment" title="Structural unemployment">structural unemployment</a> and is consequently <a href="/wiki/Macroeconomics" title="Macroeconomics">macroeconomically</a> injurious. If a technological innovation results in a reduction of necessary labour inputs in a given sector, then the industry-wide cost of production falls, which lowers the competitive price and increases the equilibrium supply point which, theoretically, will require an increase in aggregate labour inputs.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
