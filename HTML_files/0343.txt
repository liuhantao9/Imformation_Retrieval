<p>The <b>Courantyne/Corentyne/Corantijn River</b> is a river in northern <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> in  <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>.  It is the longest <a href="/wiki/List_of_rivers_of_Suriname" title="List of rivers of Suriname">river</a> in the country. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Course">Course</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courantyne_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Course">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The river runs through the <a href="/wiki/Guianan_moist_forests" title="Guianan moist forests">Guianan moist forests</a> ecoregion.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
It originates in the <a href="/wiki/Acarai_Mountains" title="Acarai Mountains">Acarai Mountains</a> and flows northward via the Boven (Upper) Courantyne which is the source river for approximately 724&#160;km (450&#160;mi) between <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> and Suriname, emptying into the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">Atlantic Ocean</a> near <a href="/wiki/Corriverton" title="Corriverton">Corriverton</a>, Guyana and <a href="/wiki/Nieuw_Nickerie" title="Nieuw Nickerie">Nieuw Nickerie</a>, Suriname. A ferry service operates between these two towns.
</p><p>Small ocean-going vessels are able to navigate the river for about 120&#160;km, to <a href="/wiki/Apura,_Suriname" class="mw-redirect" title="Apura, Suriname">Apura, Suriname</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Waterfalls">Waterfalls</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courantyne_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Waterfalls">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Wonotobo Falls, Frederik Willem IV (Anora) Falls, and the <a href="/wiki/King_George_VI_Falls" title="King George VI Falls">King George VI Falls</a> are on the Courantyne River. Other falls include the <a href="/wiki/Barrington_Brown_Falls" title="Barrington Brown Falls">Barrington Brown Falls</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Drios_Falls" title="Drios Falls">Drios Falls</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Maopityan_Falls" title="Maopityan Falls">Maopityan Falls</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Territorial_dispute">Territorial dispute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courantyne_River&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Territorial dispute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Between the upper reaches of the Courantyne, the Upper Courantyne, the Coeroeni and the Koetari rivers lay the controversial <a href="/wiki/Tigri_Area" title="Tigri Area">Tigri Area</a> claimed by both Suriname and Guyana. The Guyanese–Surinamese border is the Guyanese river bank (the west bank of the river), Suriname regarded the left bank of the Courantyne as a border, but Guyana disputes this and viewed the center of the river as a frontier, based on the <a href="/wiki/Thalweg_Doctrine" class="mw-redirect" title="Thalweg Doctrine">Thalweg Doctrine</a>. This conflict, which has been fueled since the colonial era, was solved in 2007 by a verdict by the Hague Arbitration Court, which settles the border between Guyana and Suriname on the left bank of the river, and the river water body belonging to Suriname. The tribunal that defined the maritime boundary between Suriname and Guyana in 2007 confirmed Surinamese sovereignty over the full width of the Courantyne River.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Suriname has control over all ship traffic from the mouth of the Courantyne.
</p>
