<p>A <b>Balinese name</b> is part of a system of identification used by the <a href="/wiki/Balinese_people" title="Balinese people">Balinese people</a> and in the western parts of the neighboring island of <a href="/wiki/Lombok" title="Lombok">Lombok</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>. A Balinese name will have three parts: a title, a birth order name and a personal name.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Balinese people do not use a family name.
</p><p>Both boys and girls receive <a href="/wiki/Birth_order" title="Birth order">birth order name</a> from a small typical group of names for each birth order position. These names may vary due to <a href="/wiki/Caste" title="Caste">caste</a>, regional customs and variations in the Balinese language between the north and the south of the island. Balinese people use the birth order name to refer to one another.
</p><p>As most Balinese are <a href="/wiki/Hinduism_in_Indonesia" title="Hinduism in Indonesia">Hindus</a>, many names will be of <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> origin. Some people have traditional Balinese names. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Birth_order_name">Birth order name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Birth order name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A birth order name is chosen from a few typical names according to the position of the child in the birth order of siblings. The people of Bali use the birth order name to refer to one another. 
</p><p>The first born are named <i>Wayan</i>, <i>Putu</i>, <i>Gede</i> or for a girl, <i>Ni Luh</i>. <i>Wayan</i> is a Balinese name meaning "eldest".
</p><p>Second born children are named <i>Made</i>, <i>Kadek</i>, or <i>Nengah</i>. <i>Made</i> and <i>Nengah</i> mean <i>madya</i> or "middle". <i>Kadek</i> means "little brother" or "little sister".
</p><p>The third born is given the name <i>Nyoman</i> or <i>Komang</i>. These names may be shortened to "Man" and "Mang" respectively.
</p><p>Fourth born children are named <i>Ketut</i>. Ketut is often shortened to "Tut".
</p><p>If there is a fifth child in the family, she is often called <i>Wayan Balik</i> (meaning "Wayan again").<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_according_to_caste">Names according to caste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Names according to caste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The naming system allows a person to recognise another's caste. A person's caste, unlike in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, is relatively unimportant to the Balinese people. The idea of caste flowed into Balinese culture as close links with Hindu-Buddhist Java evolved. The inclusion of the caste may also have been due to <a href="/wiki/Airlangga" title="Airlangga">Airlangga</a> (991 – 1049), a half Balinese <a href="/wiki/Raja" title="Raja">raja</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Kediri_Kingdom" title="Kediri Kingdom">Kediri Kingdom</a>.
</p><p>The naming system of the peasant farmers of Bali may have preceded the idea of the caste. The farmers included indigenous Balinese and very early Hindu-Buddhist missionaries and their followers. The farmers represented a caste level that in India, would be called <i>Sudra</i>. This "farmer caste" also used birth order names, perhaps to indicate inheritance.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sudra">Sudra</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sudra">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beyond the birth order name, there are no special names to denote people from the <a href="/wiki/Shudra" title="Shudra">Sudra</a> caste. Those of the Sudra caste add an "I" (male) and "Ni" (female) in front of their names. For example, <a href="/wiki/I_Made_Mangku_Pastika" title="I Made Mangku Pastika">I Made Mangku Pastika</a>, is the governor of Bali.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wesya">Wesya</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Wesya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Vaishya" title="Vaishya">Wesya</a> is the trader and farmer caste. The Wesya once added "Ngakan", "Kompyang", "Sang", or "Si" before their name. However, most no longer do so due to assimilation into the Sudra. An example is Ngakan Gede Sugiarta Garjitha, a major general.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ksatria">Ksatria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ksatria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Ksatria caste are the ruling and military elite in Hindu society. Some typical names of people of the <a href="/wiki/Kshatriya" title="Kshatriya">Ksatria</a> caste include: <i>I Gusti Ngurah</i> (male), <i>I Gusti Ayu</i> (female), <i>Anak Agung</i> (male), <i>Anak Agung Ayu</i> or <i>Anak Agung Istri</i> (female), <i>Tjokorda</i> which is sometimes abbreviated as <i>Tjok</i> (male), <i>Tjokorda Istri</i> (female), <i>Ida I Dewa</i>, <i>Dewa Agung</i> or <i>I Dewa</i> (male), and <i>I Dewa Ayu</i>, and <i>Desak</i> (female).
</p><p>The name <i>Gusti</i> literally means "leader" as members of the Ksatria were often families promoted from the farmer caste. The Ksatria often use birth order names. Sometimes the Kastria borrow the whole order of the farmer caste names, so it is possible to find a name like <i>I Gusti Ketut Rajendra</i>, indicating a male of the Wesya caste, fourth born, whose personal name is <i>Rajendra</i>.
</p><p>The word <i>Agung</i> means "great", or "prominent". The word <i>Tjokorda</i> is a conjunction of the Sanskrit words <i>Tjoka</i> and <i>Dewa</i>. It literally means "the foot of the Gods", and is awarded to the highest members of the aristocracy. 
</p><p>Another typical name might be <i>Anak Agung Rai</i>, meaning a Ksatria, whose personal name means "the great one". It is more difficult to differentiate sexes by name alone among the Ksatria people, though personal names often tell, like <i>Putra</i>, or "prince", for a boy, and <i>Putri</i>, or "princess", for a girl.
</p><p>An example is <a href="/wiki/Sri_Aji_Kresna_Kepakisan" title="Sri Aji Kresna Kepakisan">Sri Aji Kresna Kepakisan</a> (reign 1352 CE – 1380 CE}}. Other examples are <a href="/wiki/I_Gusti_Ngurah_Rai" title="I Gusti Ngurah Rai">I Gusti Ngurah Rai</a>, military commander and national hero, <a href="/wiki/Ide_Anak_Agung_Gde_Agung" title="Ide Anak Agung Gde Agung">Ide Anak Agung Gde Agung</a>, former Indonesian Minister of Foreign Affairs, <a href="/wiki/Tjokorda_Gde_Raka_Soekawati" title="Tjokorda Gde Raka Soekawati">Tjokorda Gde Raka Soekawati</a>, president of the <a href="/wiki/State_of_East_Indonesia" title="State of East Indonesia">State of East Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dewa_Made_Beratha" title="Dewa Made Beratha">Dewa Made Beratha</a>, a former <a href="/wiki/List_of_Governors_of_Bali" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Governors of Bali">Governor of Bali</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Brahmana">Brahmana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Brahmana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Brahmin caste are academics, intellectuals, economists, aristocrats and lawyers. Names for the <a href="/wiki/Brahmin" title="Brahmin">Brahmana</a> caste include <i>Ida Bagus</i> (male), <i>Ida Ayu</i> (female). A typical name might be <i>Ida Ayu Ngurah</i>, meaning "Brahman woman, Beautiful highness whose personal name is Ngurah," (in English, "gift from heaven"). Other examples are <a href="/wiki/Oka_Rusmini" title="Oka Rusmini">Ida Ayu Oka Rusmini</a>, novelist and <a href="/wiki/Ida_Bagus_Oka" title="Ida Bagus Oka">Ida Bagus Oka</a>, a former governor of Bali.
</p><p>Inter-caste marriages occur. Those who marry someone from a higher caste will adopt the name <i>Jero</i> ("come in") in front of their name.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender">Gender</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Balinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Gender">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A name may have a prefix to indicate gender, <i>I</i> for males and <i>Ni</i> for females. Typical names are. for example, <i>I Wayan Pedjeng</i> (first-born male whose personal name is "moon") or <i>Ni Ketut Sulastri</i> (fourth-born female whose personal name is "fine light"). Unlike Javanese names, Balinese names of Sanskrit origin do not experience vowel change from <i>a</i> to <i>o</i> (as in Javanese <i>Susilo</i>, from <i>Susila</i>).
</p>
