<p><b>Sir William Henry Broadbent, 1st Baronet</b> <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Knight_Commander_of_the_Royal_Victorian_Order" class="mw-redirect" title="Knight Commander of the Royal Victorian Order">KCVO</a>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">FRS</a>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_College_of_Physicians" class="mw-redirect" title="Fellow of the Royal College of Physicians">FRCP</a></span> (23 January 1835 – 10 July 1907<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>) was an English <a href="/wiki/Neurologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Neurologist">neurologist</a> who was a leading British authority in the field of <a href="/wiki/Cardiology" title="Cardiology">cardiology</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/Neurology" title="Neurology">neurology</a>. He also performed research involving diseases such as <a href="/wiki/Tuberculosis" title="Tuberculosis">tuberculosis</a> and cancer. In 1881 he was elected President of the <a href="/wiki/London_Medical_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="London Medical Society">London Medical Society</a><sup id="cite_ref-times_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and in 1887 President of the <a href="/wiki/Clinical_Society_of_London" title="Clinical Society of London">Clinical Society of London</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> 
Broadbent was a Physician-Extraordinary to <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> and Physician-in-Ordinary to <a href="/wiki/King_Edward_VII" class="mw-redirect" title="King Edward VII">King Edward VII</a> and the <a href="/wiki/George_V" title="George V">Prince of Wales</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">Early life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Broadbent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Broadbent was born at Longwood Edge in <a href="/wiki/Lindley,_West_Yorkshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Lindley, West Yorkshire">Lindley</a>, now part of <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>, West Yorkshire. He was the eldest of seven children born to John Broadbent, a wool manufacturer and prominent <a href="/wiki/Wesleyanism" class="mw-redirect" title="Wesleyanism">Wesleyan</a>, and Esther (<i>née</i> Butterworth). His younger brother was Colonel John Edward Broadbent <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Companion_of_the_Order_of_the_Bath" class="mw-redirect" title="Companion of the Order of the Bath">CB</a>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_Peace#United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Justice of the Peace">JP</a></span>.<sup id="cite_ref-burke_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-burke-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He was educated at <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_New_College" title="Huddersfield New College">Huddersfield College</a> before he decided to study medicine. He was apprenticed to a doctor in Manchester before studying medicine at <a href="/wiki/Owens_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Owens College">Owens College</a>, and the Royal School of Medicine in <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>. He studied in Paris in 1857 and returned in 1858 to pass the M.B. examination at Manchester.<sup id="cite_ref-rcp_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rcp-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Broadbent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1859, Broadbent took up a junior post at <a href="/wiki/St_Mary%27s_Hospital,_London" title="St Mary&#39;s Hospital, London">St Mary's Hospital, London</a>, with which he was associated for much of his career (1859–1896). The next year he was elected physician to the <a href="/wiki/London_Fever_Hospital" title="London Fever Hospital">London Fever Hospital</a>. In 1865, he was promoted to physician in charge of patients at St. Mary's, and full physician in 1871.<sup id="cite_ref-rcp_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rcp-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-times_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was through his work at St. Mary's that Broadbent earned his reputation as an expert pathologist and outstanding clinical teacher. His areas of expertise included neurology and cardiology, as well as cancer and typhoid.<sup id="cite_ref-rcp_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rcp-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The months of November 1891 to October 1892 were critically important to Broadbent and his career, and gave him a narrow involvement in a notorious series of crimes.  In November to December 1891 Broadbent was involved in saving the life of Prince George (the future King <a href="/wiki/George_V_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="George V of the United Kingdom">George V</a>) from typhoid fever.  At about the same time that he was involved in this he was sent a mysterious letter accusing him of murdering a prostitute named Matilda Clover the previous October with poison.  This letter demanded a huge blackmail amount to avoid ruin.  Broadbent wisely sent the letter to Scotland Yard.  In January 1892, he was sent for by the Royal Family in an attempt to save <a href="/wiki/Albert_Victor,_Duke_of_Clarence_and_Avondale" class="mw-redirect" title="Albert Victor, Duke of Clarence and Avondale">Albert Victor, Duke of Clarence and Avondale</a> the older son of the Prince and Princess of Wales and "heir presumptive" to the throne of England.  The Prince, known as "Prince Eddy" to the public (and nicknamed "Collars and Cuffs" due to some wardrobe choices he made) had caught a virulent strain of influenza.  Broadbent tried everything he could to save the Duke, but on 14 January 1892 the Duke died.  However, the Prince of Wales sent Broadbent a letter thanking him for his endeavours, that preserved for him and his wife one of their sons.
</p><p>Then, in the late spring, Broadbent was informed of a police investigation into the deaths of a series of prostitutes in the Stepney and Lambeth areas of London that began in October 1891 and continued until April 1892.  One of the victims was Matilda Clover, who had been classified as having died of natural causes, but the letter from the blackmailer to Broadbent gave details showing the woman had been poisoned.  An arrest was made in June 1892 of Dr. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Neill_Cream" title="Thomas Neill Cream">Thomas Neill Cream</a> for blackmailing another physician named Harper.  Subsequently this was changed to a charge of murder for the poisoning of Matilda Clover.  The trial of the defendant occurred in October 1892, with the charges including the attempted blackmail of Broadbent, and Broadbent's appearance as a witness. Cream was found guilty and sentenced to death, being hanged in November 1892.
</p><p>Broadbent was a Physician-Extraordinary to <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> and Physician-in-Ordinary to <a href="/wiki/King_Edward_VII" class="mw-redirect" title="King Edward VII">King Edward VII</a> and the <a href="/wiki/George_V" title="George V">Prince of Wales</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rcp_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rcp-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honours">Honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Broadbent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Broadbent received honorary doctorates from the universities of <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_St_Andrews" title="University of St Andrews">St. Andrews</a>, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Leeds" title="University of Leeds">Leeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/McGill_University" title="McGill University">McGill</a> and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">Toronto</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-times_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Broadbent was created a <a href="/wiki/Baronet" title="Baronet">baronet</a>, of Brook Street and Longwood, in 1893. He was appointed a <a href="/wiki/Royal_Victorian_Order" title="Royal Victorian Order">Knight Commander of the Royal Victorian Order</a> in March 1901.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Broadbent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Broadbent married Eliza Harpin in 1863, and had three sons and three daughters:<sup id="cite_ref-burke_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-burke-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Mary Ethel (1864 – 10 March 1954)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sir_John_Broadbent,_2nd_Baronet" title="Sir John Broadbent, 2nd Baronet">John Francis Harpin</a> (16 October 1865 – 27 January 1946)</li>
<li>William Herbert (10 April 1867 – 17 May 1867)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Walter_Broadbent" title="Walter Broadbent">Walter</a> (4 August 1868 – 17 October 1951)</li>
<li>Gertrude (1870 – 2 August 1905)</li>
<li>Eliza Madeleine (1872 – 17 December 1949)</li></ul>
<p>His sons John and Walter also became physicians.<sup id="cite_ref-rcp_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rcp-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He died at his home in London in 1907, following an eighth-month illness that began with pneumonia. His eldest son succeeded him in the baronetcy.<sup id="cite_ref-times_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-times-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Associated_eponyms">Associated eponyms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=William_Broadbent&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Associated eponyms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li><i>Broadbent apoplexy</i>: A type of stroke caused by a <a href="/wiki/Cerebrum" title="Cerebrum">cerebral</a> haemorrhage into the <a href="/wiki/Ventricular_system" title="Ventricular system">ventricular system</a>.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Broadbent_sign" title="Broadbent sign">Broadbent sign</a></i>: Recession of the intercostal spaces (near the eleventh and twelfth ribs on the left side of the back) as a sign of adherent <a href="/wiki/Pericardium" title="Pericardium">pericardium</a> (this sign was first described by his son, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Broadbent" title="Walter Broadbent">Walter Broadbent</a>, in 1895).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Broadbent_inverted_sign" title="Broadbent inverted sign">Broadbent inverted sign</a></i>: Pulsations synchronising with ventricular <a href="/wiki/Systole_(medicine)" class="mw-redirect" title="Systole (medicine)">systole</a> on the posterior lateral wall of the chest in gross <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dilatation" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:dilatation">dilatation</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Left_atrium" class="mw-redirect" title="Left atrium">left atrium</a>.</li>
<li><i>Broadbent law</i>: Medical law that states "lesions of the upper segment of the motor tract cause less marked <a href="/wiki/Paralysis" title="Paralysis">paralysis</a> of muscles that habitually produce bilateral movements than of those that commonly act independently of the opposite side".</li></ul>
