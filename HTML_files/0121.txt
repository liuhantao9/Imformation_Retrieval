	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Pietas</i></h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For the Christian image, see <a href="/wiki/Piet%C3%A0" title="Pietà">Pietà</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:332px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Antoninus_Pius_Coin_pieta.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Antoninus_Pius_Coin_pieta.jpg/330px-Antoninus_Pius_Coin_pieta.jpg" decoding="async" width="330" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Antoninus_Pius_Coin_pieta.jpg/495px-Antoninus_Pius_Coin_pieta.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/47/Antoninus_Pius_Coin_pieta.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="247" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Antoninus_Pius_Coin_pieta.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Pietas</i>, as a virtue of the emperor <a href="/wiki/Antoninus_Pius" title="Antoninus Pius">Antoninus Pius</a>, represented by a woman offering a sacrifice on the reverse of this <a href="/wiki/Sestertius" title="Sestertius">sestertius</a></div></div></div>
<p><i><b>Pietas</b></i>, translated variously as "duty", "religiosity"<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> or "religious behavior",<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> "loyalty",<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> "devotion", or "<a href="/wiki/Filial_piety" title="Filial piety">filial piety</a>" (English "piety" derives from the Latin), was one of the chief <a href="/wiki/Virtue" title="Virtue">virtues</a> among the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Roman" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Roman">ancient Romans</a>. It was the distinguishing virtue of the <a href="/wiki/Founding_of_Rome" title="Founding of Rome">founding</a> hero <a href="/wiki/Aeneas" title="Aeneas">Aeneas</a>, who is often given the <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">adjectival</a> epithet <i>pius</i> ("religious") throughout <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>'s epic <i><a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">Aeneid</a></i>. The sacred nature of <i>pietas</i> was embodied by the divine personification Pietas, a goddess often pictured on Roman coins. The Greek equivalent is <i><a href="/wiki/Eusebeia" title="Eusebeia">eusebeia</a></i> (εὐσέβεια).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a> defined <i>pietas</i> as the virtue "which admonishes us to do our duty to our country or our parents or other blood relations."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The man who possessed <i>pietas</i> "performed all his duties towards the deity and his fellow human beings fully and in every respect," as the 19th-century classical scholar <a href="/wiki/Georg_Wissowa" title="Georg Wissowa">Georg Wissowa</a> described it.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Cicero suggests people should have awareness of our own honor, we must always attempt to raise the honor of others by our dignified praise, such praise, admiration and honored actions must be beyond all our own desires, as Cicero said, we must choose our actions and words with respect to our friends, colleagues, family or blood relations. Cicero describes youth in the pursuit of honour: “How they yearn for praise! What labours will they not undertake to stand fast among their peers! How will they remember those who have shown them kindness and how eager to repay it!”.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_virtue">As virtue</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pietas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: As virtue">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Pietas erga parentes</i> ("<i>pietas</i> toward one's parents") was one of the most important aspects of demonstrating virtue. <i>Pius</i> as a <i><a href="/wiki/Cognomen" title="Cognomen">cognomen</a></i> originated as way to mark a person as especially "pious" in this sense: announcing one's personal <i>pietas</i> through official nomenclature seems to have been an innovation of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">late Republic</a>, when <a href="/wiki/Quintus_Caecilius_Metellus_Pius" title="Quintus Caecilius Metellus Pius">Quintus Caecilius Metellus Pius</a> claimed it for his efforts to have his father, <a href="/wiki/Quintus_Caecilius_Metellus_Numidicus" title="Quintus Caecilius Metellus Numidicus">Numidicus</a>, recalled from exile.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <i>Pietas</i> extended also toward "parents" in the sense of "ancestors," and was one of the basic principles of <a href="/wiki/Mos_maiorum" title="Mos maiorum">Roman tradition</a>, as expressed by the care of the dead.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Pietas</i> as a virtue resided within a person, in contrast to a virtue or gift such as <i><a href="/wiki/Victoria_(mythology)" title="Victoria (mythology)">Victoria</a>,</i> which was given by the gods. <i>Pietas,</i> however, allowed a person to recognize the divine source of benefits conferred.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first recorded use of <i>pietas</i> in English occurs in Anselm Bayly’s <i>The Alliance of Music, Poetry, and Oratory</i>, published in 1789.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Religious_Rituals_in_Ancient_Rome" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious Rituals in Ancient Rome">Religious Rituals in Ancient Rome</a></div>
<p>"A Roman with the virtue of pietas did not leave his religious duties at the door of the temple, but carried them with him everywhere, following the will of the gods in his business transactions and everyday life."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pietas held importance in international relations and diplomacy, where the credibility of a commander was dependent on his cessation of all self-gain and to commit to the cause, without action of treachery. "Due  to  this  reliance  on credibility,  the  reputation  of  individual  commanders  and  the  Roman  state  itself  held  a practical  role in negotiations  and  discussions.". The commanders belief in fides must be one of credibility by continuity of action, consistency in dealing with neighbours will be applied to the current parties. Ensure respect in existing contracts, means the pledges and oaths will be held, Rome will continue to do what is right and thus continue diplomatic strategies. Ending conflict was slim if perfidy was the norm of commander in the negotiation.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Iconography">Iconography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pietas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Iconography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Herennia1.1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Herennia1.1.jpg/250px-Herennia1.1.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="121" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Herennia1.1.jpg/375px-Herennia1.1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Herennia1.1.jpg/500px-Herennia1.1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="550" data-file-height="266" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Herennia1.1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Denarius of Herennius, depicting Pietas and an act of <i>pietas</i>.</div></div></div>
<p>Pietas was represented on coin by cult objects, but also as a woman conducting a sacrifice by means of fire at an altar.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In the imagery of sacrifice, <a href="/wiki/Libation" title="Libation">libation</a> was the fundamental act that came to symbolize <i>pietas</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Pietas</i> is first represented on Roman coins on <i><a href="/wiki/Denarius" title="Denarius">denarii</a></i> issued by <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Herennius" class="mw-redirect" title="Marcus Herennius">Marcus Herennius</a> in 108 or 107 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Pietas appears on the obverse as a divine <a href="/wiki/Personification" title="Personification">personification</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Bust_(sculpture)" title="Bust (sculpture)">bust</a> form; the quality of <i>pietas</i> is represented by a son carrying his father on his back; the symbolism of which would be echoed in <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">Virgil</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Aeneid" title="Aeneid">Aeneid</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Aeneas" title="Aeneas">Aeneas</a> carrying his father <a href="/wiki/Anchises" title="Anchises">Anchises</a> out of the burning <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">Troy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Pietas is among the virtues that appear frequently on Imperial coins, including those issued under <a href="/wiki/Hadrian" title="Hadrian">Hadrian</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One of the symbols of <i>pietas</i> was the stork, described by <a href="/wiki/Petronius" title="Petronius">Petronius</a> as <i>pietaticultrix</i>, "cultivator of <i>pietas</i>." The stork represented filial piety in particular, as the Romans believed that it demonstrated family loyalty by returning to the same nest every year, and that it took care of its parents in old age. As such, a stork appears next to Pietas on  <a href="/wiki/File:Quintus_Caecilius_Metellus_Pius.jpg" title="File:Quintus Caecilius Metellus Pius.jpg">a coin issued by Metellus Pius</a> (on whose <i>cognomen</i> see <a href="#As_virtue">above</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_goddess">As goddess</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pietas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: As goddess">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bronze-Flavia_Maximiana_Theodora-trier_RIC_65.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Bronze-Flavia_Maximiana_Theodora-trier_RIC_65.jpg/250px-Bronze-Flavia_Maximiana_Theodora-trier_RIC_65.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="123" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Bronze-Flavia_Maximiana_Theodora-trier_RIC_65.jpg/375px-Bronze-Flavia_Maximiana_Theodora-trier_RIC_65.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e4/Bronze-Flavia_Maximiana_Theodora-trier_RIC_65.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="246" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bronze-Flavia_Maximiana_Theodora-trier_RIC_65.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Flavia_Maximiana_Theodora" title="Flavia Maximiana Theodora">Flavia Maximiana Theodora</a> on the obverse, on the reverse Pietas holding infant to her breast.</div></div></div>
<p>Pietas was the divine presence in everyday life that cautioned humans not to intrude on the realm of the gods.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Violations of <i>pietas</i> required a <i><a href="/wiki/Piaculum" class="mw-redirect" title="Piaculum">piaculum</a></i>, expiatory rites.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A temple to Pietas was vowed <i>(<a href="/wiki/Votum" title="Votum">votum</a>)</i> by <a href="/wiki/Manius_Acilius_Glabrio_(consul_191_BC)" title="Manius Acilius Glabrio (consul 191 BC)">Manius Acilius Glabrio</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Thermopylae_(191_BC)" title="Battle of Thermopylae (191 BC)">Battle of Thermopylae in 191 BC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to a miraculous legend <i>(<a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_ancient_Roman_religion#miraculum" title="Glossary of ancient Roman religion">miraculum</a>),</i><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> a poor woman who was starving in prison was saved when her daughter gave her breast milk (compare <a href="/wiki/Roman_Charity" title="Roman Charity">Roman Charity</a>). Caught in the act, the daughter was not punished, but recognized for her <i>pietas</i>. Mother and daughter were set free, and given public support for the rest of their lives. The site was regarded as sacred to the goddess Pietas <i>(consecratus deae)</i> because she had chosen to manifest her presence there.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The story exemplified <i>pietas erga parentes</i>, the proper devotion one ought to show to one's parents.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Imperial_women_portrayed_as_Pietas">Imperial women portrayed as Pietas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pietas&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Imperial women portrayed as Pietas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pietas was often depicted as goddess on the reverse of Roman Imperial coins, with women of the imperial family on the obverse,<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> as an appropriate virtue to be attributed to them. Women of the Imperial family might be portrayed in art in the goddess's guise.
</p>
