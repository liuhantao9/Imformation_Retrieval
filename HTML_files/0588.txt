<p><b>OpenStreetMap</b> (<b>OSM</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Virtual_community" title="Virtual community">collaborative project</a> to create a <a href="/wiki/Free_content" title="Free content">free</a> editable <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">map</a> of the world. The <a href="/wiki/Geodata" class="mw-redirect" title="Geodata">geodata</a> underlying the map is considered the primary output of the project. The creation and growth of OSM has been motivated by restrictions on use or availability of map data across much of the world, and the advent of inexpensive portable <a href="/wiki/Satellite_navigation" title="Satellite navigation">satellite navigation</a> devices.<sup id="cite_ref-GPS-inspires-DIY_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GPS-inspires-DIY-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> OSM is considered a prominent example of <a href="/wiki/Volunteered_geographic_information" title="Volunteered geographic information">volunteered geographic information</a>.
</p><p>Created by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Coast" title="Steve Coast">Steve Coast</a> in the UK in 2004, it was inspired by the success of Wikipedia<sup id="cite_ref-tc10_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tc10-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and the predominance of <a href="/wiki/Vendor_lock-in" title="Vendor lock-in">proprietary</a> map data in the UK and elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Since then, it has grown to over two million registered users,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> who can collect data using manual survey, <a href="/wiki/GPS" class="mw-redirect" title="GPS">GPS</a> devices, <a href="/wiki/Aerial_photography" title="Aerial photography">aerial photography</a>, and other free sources. This <a href="/wiki/Crowdsourcing" title="Crowdsourcing">crowdsourced</a> data is then made available under the <a href="/wiki/Open_Database_License" title="Open Database License">Open Database License</a>. The site is supported by the <a href="/wiki/OpenStreetMap_Foundation" title="OpenStreetMap Foundation">OpenStreetMap Foundation</a>, a non-profit organisation registered in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>.
</p><p>The data from OSM can be used in various ways including production of paper maps and electronic maps (similar to <a href="/wiki/Google_Maps" title="Google Maps">Google Maps</a>, for example), <a href="/wiki/Geocoding" title="Geocoding">geocoding</a> of address and place names, and route planning. Prominent users include <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a>, <a href="/wiki/Craigslist" title="Craigslist">Craigslist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seznam" class="mw-redirect" title="Seznam">Seznam</a>, <a href="/wiki/OsmAnd" title="OsmAnd">OsmAnd</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geocaching" title="Geocaching">Geocaching</a>, <a href="/wiki/MapQuest" title="MapQuest">MapQuest</a> Open, <a href="/wiki/JMP_(statistical_software)" title="JMP (statistical software)">JMP statistical software</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Foursquare" class="mw-redirect" title="Foursquare">Foursquare</a>. Many users of GPS devices use OSM data to replace the built-in map data on their devices.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> OpenStreetMap data has been favourably compared with proprietary datasources,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> although in 2009 data quality varied across the world.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenStreetMap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Steve_Coast_-_OSM_im_Rheinland_(0604).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Steve_Coast_-_OSM_im_Rheinland_%280604%29.jpg/220px-Steve_Coast_-_OSM_im_Rheinland_%280604%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="196" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Steve_Coast_-_OSM_im_Rheinland_%280604%29.jpg/330px-Steve_Coast_-_OSM_im_Rheinland_%280604%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Steve_Coast_-_OSM_im_Rheinland_%280604%29.jpg/440px-Steve_Coast_-_OSM_im_Rheinland_%280604%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1500" data-file-height="1339" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Steve_Coast_-_OSM_im_Rheinland_(0604).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The founder of OSM, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Coast" title="Steve Coast">Steve Coast</a>, in 2009</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Steve_Coast" title="Steve Coast">Steve Coast</a> founded the project in 2004, initially focusing on mapping the United Kingdom. In the UK and elsewhere, government-run and tax-funded projects like the <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey" title="Ordnance Survey">Ordnance Survey</a> created massive datasets but failed to freely and widely distribute them. The first contribution, made in the British city of <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> in 2005,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> was thought to be a road by the Directions Mag.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2006, the <a href="/wiki/OpenStreetMap_Foundation" title="OpenStreetMap Foundation">OpenStreetMap Foundation</a> was established to encourage the growth, development and distribution of free <a href="/wiki/Geospatial" class="mw-redirect" title="Geospatial">geospatial</a> data and provide geospatial data for anybody to use and share. In December 2006, <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a> confirmed that OpenStreetMap could use its <a href="/wiki/Aerial_photography" title="Aerial photography">aerial photography</a> as a backdrop for map production.<sup id="cite_ref-ogd-Coast1_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ogd-Coast1-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 2007, <a href="/wiki/Automotive_Navigation_Data" title="Automotive Navigation Data">Automotive Navigation Data</a> (AND) donated a complete road data set for the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trunk_road" title="Trunk road">trunk road</a> data for <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and China to the project<sup id="cite_ref-ogd-Coast3_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ogd-Coast3-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> and by July 2007, when the first OSM international The State of the Map conference was held, there were 9,000 registered users. Sponsors of the event included <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a>, Yahoo! and <a href="/wiki/Multimap.com" title="Multimap.com">Multimap</a>. In October 2007, OpenStreetMap completed the import of a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">US Census</a> <a href="/wiki/Topologically_Integrated_Geographic_Encoding_and_Referencing" title="Topologically Integrated Geographic Encoding and Referencing">TIGER</a> road dataset.<sup id="cite_ref-Boeing2017CEUS_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Boeing2017CEUS-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-linux_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-linux-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In December 2007, <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">Oxford University</a> became the first major organisation to use OpenStreetMap data on their main website.<sup id="cite_ref-geothought_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-geothought-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ways to import and export data have continued to grow – by 2008, the project developed tools to export OpenStreetMap data to power portable GPS units, replacing their existing proprietary and out-of-date maps.<sup id="cite_ref-systemed_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-systemed-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In March, two founders<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="of what? (May 2016)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> announced that they have received venture capital funding of €2.4<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>million for CloudMade, a commercial company that uses OpenStreetMap data.<sup id="cite_ref-CloudMade2_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CloudMade2-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In November 2010, <a href="/wiki/Bing_(search_engine)" title="Bing (search engine)">Bing</a> changed their licence to allow use of their satellite imagery for making maps.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2012, the launch of pricing for <a href="/wiki/Google_Maps" title="Google Maps">Google Maps</a> led several prominent websites to switch from their service to OpenStreetMap and other competitors.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Chief among these were <a href="/wiki/Foursquare" class="mw-redirect" title="Foursquare">Foursquare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Craigslist" title="Craigslist">Craigslist</a>, which adopted OpenStreetMap, and <a href="/wiki/Apple,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Apple, Inc.">Apple</a>, which ended a contract with Google and launched a self-built mapping platform using <a href="/wiki/TomTom" title="TomTom">TomTom</a> and OpenStreetMap data.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Map_production">Map production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenStreetMap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Map production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Adding_data_to_OSM_after_mapping_Brighton_Pier.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Adding_data_to_OSM_after_mapping_Brighton_Pier.jpg/220px-Adding_data_to_OSM_after_mapping_Brighton_Pier.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Adding_data_to_OSM_after_mapping_Brighton_Pier.jpg/330px-Adding_data_to_OSM_after_mapping_Brighton_Pier.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Adding_data_to_OSM_after_mapping_Brighton_Pier.jpg/440px-Adding_data_to_OSM_after_mapping_Brighton_Pier.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="678" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Adding_data_to_OSM_after_mapping_Brighton_Pier.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Editing with <a href="/wiki/JOSM" title="JOSM">JOSM</a> after a ground survey</div></div></div>
<p>Map data is collected from scratch by volunteers performing systematic ground surveys using tools such as a handheld GPS unit, a notebook, digital camera, or a voice recorder. The data is then entered into the OpenStreetMap database. <a href="/wiki/Mapathon" title="Mapathon">Mapathon</a> competition events are also held by OpenStreetMap team and by non-profit organisations and local governments to map a particular area.
</p><p>The availability of aerial photography and other data from commercial and government sources has added important sources of data for manual editing and automated imports. Special processes are in place to handle automated imports and avoid legal and technical problems.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Software_for_editing_maps">Software for editing maps</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenStreetMap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Software for editing maps">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:StreetComplete_-_House_number_quest.png" class="image"><img alt="A map with different colored icons on it, currently a quest about a house number" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/StreetComplete_-_House_number_quest.png/220px-StreetComplete_-_House_number_quest.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="391" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/StreetComplete_-_House_number_quest.png/330px-StreetComplete_-_House_number_quest.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/StreetComplete_-_House_number_quest.png/440px-StreetComplete_-_House_number_quest.png 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="1280" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:StreetComplete_-_House_number_quest.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>StreetComplete asking user a question, with answer filled in. After tapping "OK" this answer will be added to an OpenStreetMap database.</div></div></div>
<p>Editing of maps can be done using the default web browser editor called <a href="/wiki/ID_(software)" title="ID (software)">iD</a>, an <a href="/wiki/HTML5" title="HTML5">HTML5</a> application using <a href="/wiki/D3.js" title="D3.js">D3.js</a> and written by <a href="/wiki/Mapbox" title="Mapbox">Mapbox</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> which was originally financed by the <a href="/wiki/Knight_Foundation" class="mw-redirect" title="Knight Foundation">Knight Foundation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The earlier Flash-based application <a href="/wiki/Potlatch_(software)" title="Potlatch (software)">Potlatch</a> is retained for intermediate-level users. <a href="/wiki/JOSM" title="JOSM">JOSM</a> and Merkaartor are more powerful desktop editing applications that are better suited for advanced users.
</p><p>Vespucci is the first full-featured editor for <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a>; it was released in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> StreetComplete is a new, easy Android app launched in 2016,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> which allows users without any OpenStreetMap knowledge to answer simple quests for existing data in OpenStreetMap, and thus contribute data.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Maps.me" title="Maps.me">Maps.me</a> is a mobile application (which runs on both Android and iOS) offering offline maps which also includes a limited OSM data editor.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> <a href="//wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/Go_Map!!" class="extiw" title="openstreetmap:Go Map!!">Go Map!!</a> is an iOS app that lets users create and edit information in OpenStreetMap. <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.pushpinosm.org">Pushpin</a> is another iOS app that lets you add POI on the go.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Contributors">Contributors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenStreetMap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Contributors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The project has a geographically diverse user-base, due to emphasis of local knowledge and <a href="/wiki/Ground_truth" title="Ground truth">ground truth</a> in the process of data collection. Many early contributors were <a href="/wiki/Bicycle" title="Bicycle">cyclists</a> who survey with and for bicyclists, charting <a href="/wiki/Bikeway" class="mw-redirect" title="Bikeway">cycleroutes</a> and navigable trails.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Others are <a href="/wiki/Geographic_Information_System" class="mw-redirect" title="Geographic Information System">GIS</a> professionals who contribute data with <a href="/wiki/Esri" title="Esri">Esri</a> tools.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Contributors are predominately men, with only 3–5% being women.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By August 2008, shortly after the second The State of the Map conference was held, there were over 50,000 registered contributors; by March 2009, there were 100,000 and by the end of 2009 the figure was nearly 200,000. In April 2012, OpenStreetMap cleared 600,000 registered contributors.<sup id="cite_ref-osm-wiki-stats_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-osm-wiki-stats-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> On 6 January 2013, OpenStreetMap reached one million registered users.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Around 30% of users have contributed at least one point to the OpenStreetMap database.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surveys_and_personal_knowledge">Surveys and personal knowledge</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenStreetMap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Surveys and personal knowledge">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Motion_X_GPS_and_OSM.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Motion_X_GPS_and_OSM.jpg/220px-Motion_X_GPS_and_OSM.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Motion_X_GPS_and_OSM.jpg/330px-Motion_X_GPS_and_OSM.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Motion_X_GPS_and_OSM.jpg/440px-Motion_X_GPS_and_OSM.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="768" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Motion_X_GPS_and_OSM.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Surveying routes with a <a href="/wiki/GPS_receiver" class="mw-redirect" title="GPS receiver">GPS receiver</a></div></div></div>
<p>Ground surveys are performed by a mapper, <a href="/wiki/Pedestrian" title="Pedestrian">on foot</a>, bicycle, or in a <a href="/wiki/Car" title="Car">car</a>, motorcycle, or <a href="/wiki/Boat" title="Boat">boat</a>. Map data are usually collected using a GPS unit, although this is not strictly necessary if an area has already been traced from satellite imagery.
</p><p>Once the data has been collected, it is entered into the database by uploading it onto the project's website together with appropriate attribute data. As collecting and uploading data may be separated from editing objects, contribution to the project is possible without using a GPS unit.
</p><p>Some committed contributors adopt the task of mapping whole towns and cities, or organising mapping parties to gather the support of others to complete a map area. A large number of less active users contribute corrections and small additions to the map.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Street-level_image_data">Street-level image data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenStreetMap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Street-level image data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In addition to several different sets of satellite image backgrounds available to OSM editors, data from several street-level image platforms are available as map data photo overlays: <a href="/wiki/Bing_Maps#Streetside" title="Bing Maps">Bing Streetside</a> 360º image tracks, and the open and crowdsourced <a href="/wiki/Mapillary" title="Mapillary">Mapillary</a> and <a href="/wiki/OpenStreetCam" title="OpenStreetCam">OpenStreetCam</a> platforms, generally smartphone and other windshield-mounted camera images. Additionally, a Mapillary traffic sign data layer can be enabled; it is the product of user-submitted images.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_data">Government data</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenStreetMap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Government data">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some government agencies have released official data on appropriate licences. This includes the United States, where works of the federal government are placed under <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the United States, OSM uses <a href="/wiki/Landsat_7" title="Landsat 7">Landsat 7</a> satellite imagery, Prototype Global Shorelines from <a href="/wiki/NOAA" class="mw-redirect" title="NOAA">NOAA</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Topologically_Integrated_Geographic_Encoding_and_Referencing" title="Topologically Integrated Geographic Encoding and Referencing">TIGER</a> from the Census. In the UK, some <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey" title="Ordnance Survey">Ordnance Survey</a> OpenData is imported, while <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Canada" title="Natural Resources Canada">Natural Resources Canada</a>'s CanVec vector data and <a href="/wiki/GeoBase_(geospatial_data)" title="GeoBase (geospatial data)">GeoBase</a> provide landcover and streets.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/List_of_countries%27_copyright_length" class="mw-redirect" title="List of countries&#39; copyright length">Out-of-copyright</a> maps can be good sources of information about features that do not change frequently. Copyright periods vary, but in the UK <a href="/wiki/Crown_copyright" title="Crown copyright">Crown copyright</a> expires after 50 years and hence Ordnance Survey maps until the 1960s can legally be used. A complete set of UK 1&#160;inch/mile maps from the late 1940s and early 1950s has been collected, scanned, and is available online as a resource for contributors.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Route_planning">Route planning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenStreetMap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Route planning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In February 2015, OpenStreetMap added route planning functionality to the map on its official website. The routing uses external services, namely <a href="/wiki/OSRM" class="mw-redirect" title="OSRM">OSRM</a>, <a href="/wiki/GraphHopper" title="GraphHopper">GraphHopper</a> and <a href="/wiki/MapQuest" title="MapQuest">MapQuest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are other routing providers and applications listed in the official Routing wiki.
</p>

<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">Web browser</a></dt>
<dd>Data provided by the OpenStreetMap project can be viewed in a <a href="/wiki/Web_browser" title="Web browser">web browser</a> with <a href="/wiki/JavaScript" title="JavaScript">JavaScript</a> support via <a href="/wiki/Hypertext_Transfer_Protocol" title="Hypertext Transfer Protocol">Hypertext Transfer Protocol</a> (HTTP) on its official website. The basic map views offered are: Standard, Cycle map, Transport map and Humanitarian. Finer map display and category options are available using <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.openstreetbrowser.org/">OpenStreetBrowser</a></dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/OsmAnd" title="OsmAnd">OsmAnd</a></dt>
<dd>OsmAnd is <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free software</a> for <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> and <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> mobile devices that can use offline vector data from OSM. It also supports layering OSM vector data with prerendered raster map tiles from OpenStreetMap and other sources.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Maps.me" title="Maps.me">Maps.me</a></dt>
<dd>Maps.me is <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free software</a> for <a href="/wiki/Android_(operating_system)" title="Android (operating system)">Android</a> and <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> mobile devices that provides offline maps based on OSM data.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/GNOME_Maps" class="mw-redirect" title="GNOME Maps">GNOME Maps</a></dt>
<dd>GNOME Maps is a graphical front-end written in JavaScript and introduced in <a href="/wiki/GNOME" title="GNOME">GNOME</a> 3.10. It provides a mechanism to find the user's location with the help of <a href="/w/index.php?title=GeoClue&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="GeoClue (page does not exist)">GeoClue</a>, finds directions via GraphHopper and it can deliver a list as answer to queries.</dd>
<dt><a href="/wiki/Marble_(software)" title="Marble (software)">Marble</a></dt>
<dd>Marble is a <a href="/wiki/KDE" title="KDE">KDE</a> <a href="/wiki/Virtual_globe" title="Virtual globe">virtual globe</a> application which received support for OpenStreetMap.</dd>
<dt>FoxtrotGPS</dt>
<dd>FoxtrotGPS is a <a href="/wiki/GTK%2B" class="mw-redirect" title="GTK+">GTK+</a>-based map viewer, that is especially suited to touch input.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> It is available in the <a href="/wiki/SHR_(operating_system)" title="SHR (operating system)">SHR</a> or <a href="/wiki/Debian" title="Debian">Debian</a> repositories.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>The web site OpenStreetMap.org provides a <a href="/wiki/Tiled_web_map" title="Tiled web map">slippy map</a> interface based on the <a href="/wiki/Leaflet_(software)" title="Leaflet (software)">Leaflet</a> JavaScript library (and formerly built on <a href="/wiki/OpenLayers" title="OpenLayers">OpenLayers</a>), displaying map tiles rendered by the Mapnik rendering engine, and tiles from other sources including OpenCycleMap.org.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Custom maps can also be generated from OSM data through various software including Jawg Maps, <a href="/wiki/Mapnik" title="Mapnik">Mapnik</a>, Mapbox Studio, <a href="/wiki/Mapzen" title="Mapzen">Mapzen</a>'s Tangrams.
</p><p>OpenStreetMap maintains lists of online and offline routing engines available, such as the <a href="/wiki/Open_Source_Routing_Machine" title="Open Source Routing Machine">Open Source Routing Machine</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> OSM data is popular with routing researchers, and is also available to open-source projects and companies to build routing applications (or for any other purpose).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Humanitarian_aid">Humanitarian aid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenStreetMap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Humanitarian aid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Crisis_mapping" title="Crisis mapping">Crisis mapping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smart_mob" title="Smart mob">Smart mob</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ushahidi" title="Ushahidi">Ushahidi</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Openstreetmap_Philippines_GPS_Map_by_Schadow1_Expeditions.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Openstreetmap_Philippines_GPS_Map_by_Schadow1_Expeditions.jpg/220px-Openstreetmap_Philippines_GPS_Map_by_Schadow1_Expeditions.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="295" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Openstreetmap_Philippines_GPS_Map_by_Schadow1_Expeditions.jpg/330px-Openstreetmap_Philippines_GPS_Map_by_Schadow1_Expeditions.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Openstreetmap_Philippines_GPS_Map_by_Schadow1_Expeditions.jpg/440px-Openstreetmap_Philippines_GPS_Map_by_Schadow1_Expeditions.jpg 2x" data-file-width="516" data-file-height="691" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Openstreetmap_Philippines_GPS_Map_by_Schadow1_Expeditions.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>OpenStreetMap Philippines GPS map, an end-product of over a thousand crisis mappers that contributed almost 5 million map updates during the 2013 Haiyan humanitarian activation.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2010_Haiti_earthquake" title="2010 Haiti earthquake">2010 Haiti earthquake</a> has established a model for non-governmental organisations (NGOs) to collaborate with international organisations. OpenStreetMap and Crisis Commons volunteers<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> using available satellite imagery<sup id="cite_ref-forbes_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forbes-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> to map the roads, buildings and refugee camps of <a href="/wiki/Port-au-Prince" title="Port-au-Prince">Port-au-Prince</a> in just two days, building "the most complete digital map of Haiti's roads".<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The resulting data and maps have been used by several organisations providing relief aid, such as the <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> the European Commission Joint Research Centre,<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Office_for_the_Coordination_of_Humanitarian_Affairs" class="mw-redirect" title="Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs">Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-pbatty_video_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pbatty_video-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/UNOSAT" title="UNOSAT">UNOSAT</a><sup id="cite_ref-pbatty_video_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pbatty_video-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> and others.<sup id="cite_ref-osm-wiki-haiti_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-osm-wiki-haiti-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>NGOs, like the <a href="/wiki/HOT-OSM" class="mw-redirect" title="HOT-OSM">Humanitarian OpenStreetMap Team</a> and others, have worked with donors like <a href="/wiki/United_States_Agency_for_International_Development" title="United States Agency for International Development">United States Agency for International Development</a> (USAID) to map other parts of Haiti and parts of many other countries, both to create map data for places that were blank, and to engage and build capacity of local people.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After Haiti, the OpenStreetMap community continued mapping to support humanitarian organisations for various crises and disasters. After the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Mali_conflict" title="Northern Mali conflict">Northern Mali conflict</a> (January 2013),<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Typhoon_Haiyan" title="Typhoon Haiyan">Typhoon Haiyan</a> in the Philippines (November 2013),<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Ebola_virus_epidemic_in_West_Africa" class="mw-redirect" title="Ebola virus epidemic in West Africa">Ebola virus epidemic in West Africa</a> (March 2014),<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> the OpenStreetMap community has shown it can play a significant role in supporting humanitarian organisations.
</p><p>The Humanitarian OpenStreetMap Team acts as an interface between the OpenStreetMap community and the humanitarian organisations.
</p><p>Along with post-disaster work, the Humanitarian OpenStreetMap Team has worked to build better risk models and grow the local OpenStreetMap communities in multiple countries including <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">Uganda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">Senegal</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">Democratic Republic of the Congo</a> in partnership with the <a href="/wiki/International_Red_Cross_and_Red_Crescent_Movement" title="International Red Cross and Red Crescent Movement">Red Cross</a>, <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9decins_Sans_Fronti%C3%A8res" title="Médecins Sans Frontières">Médecins Sans Frontières</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> and other humanitarian groups.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_research">Scientific research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenStreetMap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Scientific research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>OpenStreetMap data was used in scientific studies. For example, road data was used for research of remaining roadless areas.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_aspects">Legal aspects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenStreetMap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Legal aspects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Licensing_terms">Licensing terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenStreetMap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Licensing terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>OpenStreetMap data was originally published under the <a href="/wiki/Creative_Commons" title="Creative Commons">Creative Commons</a> Attribution-ShareAlike licence (CC BY-SA) with the intention of promoting free use and redistribution of the data. In September 2012, the licence was changed to the <a href="/wiki/Open_Database_License" title="Open Database License">Open Database Licence</a> (ODbL) published by Open Data Commons (ODC) in order to more specifically define its bearing on data rather than representation.<sup id="cite_ref-ogd-Fairhurst_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ogd-Fairhurst-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As part of this relicensing process, some of the map data was removed from the public distribution. This included all data contributed by members that did not agree to the new licensing terms, as well as all subsequent edits to those affected objects. It also included any data contributed based on input data that was not compatible with the new terms. Estimates suggested that over 97% of data would be retained globally, however certain regions would be affected more than others, such as in Australia where 24 to 84% of objects would be retained, depending on the type of object.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> Ultimately, more than 99% of the data was retained, with Australia and Poland being the countries most severely affected by the change.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>All data added to the project needs to have a licence compatible with the Open Database Licence. This can include out-of-copyright information, public domain or other licences. Contributors agree to a <a href="//wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/Copyright" class="extiw" title="openstreetmap:Copyright">set of terms</a> which require compatibility with the current licence. This may involve examining licences for government data to establish whether it is compatible.
</p><p>Software used in the production and presentation of OpenStreetMap data is available from many different projects and each may have its own licensing. The application&#160;– what users access to edit maps and view changelogs, is powered by <a href="/wiki/Ruby_on_Rails" title="Ruby on Rails">Ruby on Rails</a>. The application also uses <a href="/wiki/PostgreSQL" title="PostgreSQL">PostgreSQL</a> for storage of user data and edit metadata. The default map is rendered by <a href="/wiki/Mapnik" title="Mapnik">Mapnik</a>, stored in <a href="/wiki/PostGIS" title="PostGIS">PostGIS</a>, and powered by an <a href="/wiki/Apache_HTTP_Server" title="Apache HTTP Server">Apache</a> module called <i>mod_tile</i>. Certain parts of the software, such as the map editor <a href="/wiki/Potlatch_(software)" title="Potlatch (software)">Potlatch2</a>, have been made available as <a href="/wiki/Public_domain" title="Public domain">public domain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-osm-wiki-legalfaq_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-osm-wiki-legalfaq-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_data_contributions">Commercial data contributions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenStreetMap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Commercial data contributions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some OpenStreetMap data is supplied by companies that choose to freely license either actual street data or satellite imagery sources from which OSM contributors can trace roads and features.
</p><p>Notably, <a href="/wiki/Automotive_Navigation_Data" title="Automotive Navigation Data">Automotive Navigation Data</a> provided a complete road data set for Netherlands and details of trunk roads in China and India. In December 2006, <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">Yahoo!</a> confirmed that OpenStreetMap was able to make use of their vertical aerial imagery and this photography was available within the editing software as an overlay. Contributors could create their vector based maps as a derived work, released with a <a href="/wiki/Free_content_license" class="mw-redirect" title="Free content license">free</a> and <a href="/wiki/Open_content_license" class="mw-redirect" title="Open content license">open</a> licence,<sup id="cite_ref-ogd-Coast1_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ogd-Coast1-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> until the shutdown of the Yahoo! Maps API on 13 September 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-yahoo-maps-close_81-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yahoo-maps-close-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> In November 2010, <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a> announced that the OpenStreetMap community could use Bing vertical aerial imagery as a backdrop in its editors.<sup id="cite_ref-ogd-Coast2_82-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ogd-Coast2-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup> For a period from 2009 to 2011, <a href="/wiki/NearMap" class="mw-redirect" title="NearMap">NearMap Pty Ltd</a> made their high-resolution PhotoMaps (of major Australian cities, plus some rural Australian areas) available for deriving OpenStreetMap data under a <a href="/wiki/CC_BY-SA" class="mw-redirect" title="CC BY-SA">CC BY-SA</a> licence.<sup id="cite_ref-nearmap_83-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nearmap-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In June 2018, the Microsoft Bing team announced a major contribution of 125 million U.S. building footprints to the project – four times the number contributed by users and government data imports.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenStreetMap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While OpenStreetMap aims to be a central data source, its map rendering and aesthetics are meant to be only one of many options, some which highlight different elements of the map or emphasise design and performance.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_format">Data format</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenStreetMap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Data format">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>OpenStreetMap uses a <a href="/wiki/Topology" title="Topology">topological</a> <a href="/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics)" title="Graph (discrete mathematics)">data structure</a>, with four core elements (also known as <i>data primitives</i>):
</p>
<ul><li><i>Nodes</i> are points with a geographic position, stored as coordinates (pairs of a <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a>) according to <a href="/wiki/WGS_84" class="mw-redirect" title="WGS 84">WGS 84</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> Outside of their usage in ways, they are used to represent map features without a size, such as <a href="/wiki/Point_of_interest" title="Point of interest">points of interest</a> or mountain peaks.</li>
<li><i>Ways</i> are ordered lists of <i>nodes</i>, representing a <a href="/wiki/Polyline" class="mw-redirect" title="Polyline">polyline</a>, or possibly a <a href="/wiki/Polygon" title="Polygon">polygon</a> if they form a closed loop. They are used both for representing linear features such as streets and rivers, and areas, like forests, parks, parking areas and lakes.</li>
<li><i>Relations</i> are ordered lists of nodes, ways and relations (together called "members"), where each member can optionally have a "role" (a string). Relations are used for representing the relationship of existing nodes and ways. Examples include turn restrictions on roads, routes that span several existing ways (for instance, a long-distance motorway), and areas with holes.</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Tag_(metadata)" title="Tag (metadata)">Tags</a></i> are key-value pairs (both arbitrary strings). They are used to store <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">metadata</a> about the map objects (such as their type, their name and their physical properties). Tags are not free-standing, but are always attached to an object: to a node, a way or a relation. A recommended <a href="/wiki/Ontology_(computer_science)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ontology (computer science)">ontology</a> of map features (the meaning of <i>tags</i>) is <a href="//wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/Map_features" class="extiw" title="openstreetmap:Map features">maintained on a wiki</a>. New tagging schemes can always be proposed by a <a href="//wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/Proposal_process" class="extiw" title="openstreetmap:Proposal process">popular vote of a written proposal</a> in OpenStreetMap wiki, however, there is no requirement to follow this process. There are over 89 million different kinds of tags in use as of June 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Data_storage">Data storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenStreetMap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Data storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The OSM data primitives are stored and processed in different formats.
</p><p>The main copy of the OSM data is stored in OSM's main database. The main database is a <a href="/wiki/PostgreSQL" title="PostgreSQL">PostgreSQL</a> database with <a href="/wiki/PostGIS" title="PostGIS">PostGIS</a> extension, which has one table for each data primitive, with individual objects stored as rows.<sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> All edits happen in this database, and all other formats are created from it.
</p><p>For data transfer, several database dumps are created, which are available for download. The complete dump is called <a href="//wiki.openstreetmap.org/wiki/planet.osm" class="extiw" title="openstreetmap:planet.osm">planet.osm</a>. These dumps exist in two formats, one using <a href="/wiki/XML" title="XML">XML</a> and one using the <a href="/wiki/Protocol_Buffers" title="Protocol Buffers">Protocol Buffer Binary Format</a> (PBF).
</p><p>The LinkedGeoData<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> data uses the <a href="/wiki/GeoSPARQL" class="mw-redirect" title="GeoSPARQL">GeoSPARQL</a> and <a href="/wiki/Well-known_text" class="mw-redirect" title="Well-known text">well-known text</a> (WKT) <a href="/wiki/Resource_Description_Framework" title="Resource Description Framework">RDF</a> vocabularies to represent OpenStreetMap data.
It is a work of the Agile Knowledge Engineering and Semantic Web (AKSW) research group at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Leipzig" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Leipzig">University of Leipzig</a>, a group mostly known for <a href="/wiki/DBpedia" title="DBpedia">DBpedia</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_services">Popular services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=OpenStreetMap&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Popular services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Moovit_Navigate_3.0.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Moovit_Navigate_3.0.png/170px-Moovit_Navigate_3.0.png" decoding="async" width="170" height="331" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Moovit_Navigate_3.0.png/255px-Moovit_Navigate_3.0.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Moovit_Navigate_3.0.png/340px-Moovit_Navigate_3.0.png 2x" data-file-width="491" data-file-height="956" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Moovit_Navigate_3.0.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Moovit Navigate</div></div></div>
<p>A variety of popular services incorporate some sort of <a href="/wiki/Geolocation" title="Geolocation">geolocation</a> or map-based component. Notable services using OSM for this include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple Inc.</a> unexpectedly created an OpenStreetMap-based map for <a href="/wiki/IPhoto#iOS_version" title="IPhoto">iPhoto for iOS</a> on 7&#160;March&#160;2012<span style="display:none">&#160;(<span class="bday dtstart published updated">2012-03-07</span>)</span>, and launched the maps without properly citing the data source – though this was corrected in 1.0.1. OpenStreetMap is one of the many cited sources for Apple's custom maps in <a href="/wiki/IOS" title="IOS">iOS</a> 6, though the majority of map data is provided by <a href="/wiki/TomTom" title="TomTom">TomTom</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">Facebook</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Flickr" title="Flickr">Flickr</a> uses OpenStreetMap data for various cities around the world, including <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">Baghdad</a>, Beijing, <a href="/wiki/Kabul" title="Kabul">Kabul</a>, Santiago, Sydney and Tokyo.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-OSM-in-Japan_93-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OSM-in-Japan-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> In 2012, the maps switched to use <a href="/wiki/Nokia" title="Nokia">Nokia</a> data primarily, with OSM being used in areas where the commercial provider lacked performance.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/MapQuest" title="MapQuest">MapQuest</a> announced a service based on OpenStreetMap in 2010, which eventually became MapQuest Open.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/MapBox" class="mw-redirect" title="MapBox">MapBox</a></li>
<li><span id="Nominatim"></span> Nominatim<sup id="cite_ref-OSM-Nominatim_96-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OSM-Nominatim-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Nominatim-OSM_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nominatim-OSM-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup> (from the Latin, 'by name') is a tool to search OSM data by name and address (geocoding) and then to generate synthetic addresses of OSM points (reverse geocoding).</li>
<li>On 29 February 2012, <a href="/wiki/Foursquare" class="mw-redirect" title="Foursquare">Foursquare</a> started using OpenStreetMap via <a href="/wiki/Mapbox" title="Mapbox">Mapbox</a>'s rendering and infrastructure.<sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Craigslist" title="Craigslist">Craigslist</a> switched to OpenStreetMap in 2012, rendering their own tiles based on the data.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2015, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mapworks&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mapworks (page does not exist)">Mapworks</a> incorporated the OSM Data set for rendering under a vector publication method. This allows basic GIS analysis capabilities to be performed at web clients supporting <a href="/wiki/HTML5" title="HTML5">HTML5</a>.</li>
<li>In September 2009, <a href="/wiki/Hasbro" title="Hasbro">Hasbro</a>, the toy company behind the real estate-themed <a href="/wiki/Board_game" title="Board game">board game</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Monopoly_(game)" title="Monopoly (game)">Monopoly</a></i>, launched <i><a href="/wiki/Monopoly_City_Streets" class="mw-redirect" title="Monopoly City Streets">Monopoly City Streets</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Massively_multiplayer_online_game" title="Massively multiplayer online game">massively multiplayer online game</a> (MMORPG) which allowed players to "buy" streets all over the world. The game used map tiles from Google Maps and the Google Maps API to display the game board, but the underlying street data was obtained from OpenStreetMap.<sup id="cite_ref-pcworld_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pcworld-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup> The online game was a limited time offering, its servers were shut down in the end of January 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC-Monopoly_101-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-Monopoly-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Moovit" title="Moovit">Moovit</a> uses maps based on OpenStreetMap in their free mobile application for public transit navigation.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/TCDD_Ta%C5%9F%C4%B1mac%C4%B1l%C4%B1k" title="TCDD Taşımacılık">TCDD Taşımacılık</a> uses OpenStreetMap as a location map on passenger seats on <a href="/wiki/YHT" class="mw-redirect" title="YHT">YHTs</a>.</li>
<li>Wikipedia uses OpenStreetMap data to render custom maps used by the articles. Many languages are included in the WIWOSM project (Wikipedia Where in OSM) which aims to show OSM objects on a slippy map, directly visible on the article page.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wikimedia" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikimedia">Wikimedia</a> projects uses OpenStreetMap as a <a href="/wiki/Locator_map" title="Locator map">locator map</a> for cities and travel points of interest.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dublin" title="Dublin">Dublin</a>-based indie games developer Ballardia launched <i><a href="/wiki/World_of_the_Living_Dead" title="World of the Living Dead">World of the Living Dead: Resurrection</a></i> in October 2013,<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup> which has incorporated OpenStreetMap into its game engine, along with census information to create a browser-based game mapping over 14,000 square kilometres of greater <a href="/wiki/Greater_Los_Angeles_Area" class="mw-redirect" title="Greater Los Angeles Area">Los Angeles</a> and survival strategy gameplay. Its previous incarnation had used Google Maps,<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup> which had proven incapable of supporting high volumes of players, so during 2013 they shut down the Google Maps version and ported the game to OSM.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Geotab" title="Geotab">Geotab</a> uses OpenStreetMap data in their Vehicle Tracking Software platform, MyGeotab.<sup id="cite_ref-Geotab_107-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Geotab-107">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Strava" title="Strava">Strava</a> switched to OpenStreetMap rendered and hosted by Mapbox from Google Maps in July 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-Stava_108-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stava-108">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Tableau_Software" title="Tableau Software">Tableau</a> has integrated OSM for all their mapping needs. It has been integrated in all of their products.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Niantic_(company)" title="Niantic (company)">Niantic</a> switched to OSM based maps from Google Maps on 1 December 2017 for their games <a href="/wiki/Ingress_(video_game)" title="Ingress (video game)">Ingress</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Go" title="Pokémon Go">Pokémon Go</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Snapchat" title="Snapchat">Snapchat's</a> June 2017 update introduced its Snap Map with data from Mapbox, OpenStreetMap, and DigitalGlobe.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Webots" title="Webots">Webots</a> uses OpenStreetMap data to create virtual environment for autonomous vehicle simulations.<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">&#91;112&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
