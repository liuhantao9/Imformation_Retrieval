	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Moravian Church</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p>The <b>Moravian Church</b>, formally named the <b><i lang="la" title="Latin language text">Unitas Fratrum</i></b> (<a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "Unity of the Brethren"),<sup id="cite_ref-history_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a> known as <i lang="de" title="German language text">[Herrnhuter] Brüdergemeine</i><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> (meaning 'Brethren's Congregation [from <a href="/wiki/Herrnhut" title="Herrnhut">Herrnhut</a>]', the place of the Church's renewal in the 18th century), is one of the oldest <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> <a href="/wiki/Christian_denomination" title="Christian denomination">denominations</a> in the world, with its heritage dating back to the <a href="/wiki/Bohemian_Reformation" title="Bohemian Reformation">Bohemian Reformation</a> in the 15th century and the <a href="/wiki/Unity_of_the_Brethren" title="Unity of the Brethren">Unity of the Brethren</a> (Czech: <i><a href="/wiki/Jednota_bratrsk%C3%A1" class="mw-redirect" title="Jednota bratrská">Jednota bratrská</a></i>) established in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bohemia" title="Kingdom of Bohemia">Kingdom of Bohemia</a>.
</p><p>The name by which the denomination is commonly known comes from the original exiles who fled to <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a> in 1722 from <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a> to escape <a href="/wiki/Religious_persecution" title="Religious persecution">religious persecution</a>, but its heritage began in 1457 in <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Lands_of_the_Bohemian_Crown" title="Lands of the Bohemian Crown">crown lands</a> Moravia and <a href="/wiki/Silesia" title="Silesia">Silesia</a>, then forming an autonomous kingdom within the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>. The modern <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">Unitas Fratrum</i>, with about one million members worldwide,<sup id="cite_ref-stats_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stats-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> continues to draw on traditions established during the 18th century. The Moravians continue their tradition of <a href="/wiki/Missionary_work" class="mw-redirect" title="Missionary work">missionary work</a>, such as in the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, as is reflected in their broad global distribution. They place high value on <a href="/wiki/Ecumenism" title="Ecumenism">ecumenism</a>, personal <a href="/wiki/Piety" title="Piety">piety</a>, missions, and music.
</p><p>The Moravian Church's <a href="/wiki/Emblem" title="Emblem">emblem</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Lamb_of_God" title="Lamb of God">Lamb of God</a> (<i lang="la" title="Latin language text">Agnus Dei</i>) with the flag of victory, surrounded by the Latin inscription "<i lang="la" title="Latin language text">Vicit agnus noster, eum sequamur</i>" ('Our Lamb has conquered, let us follow Him').
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moravian_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Moravian_Church" title="History of the Moravian Church">History of the Moravian Church</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jan_Hus_and_the_Bohemian_Reformation">Jan Hus and the Bohemian Reformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moravian_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Jan Hus and the Bohemian Reformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Jan_Hus" title="Jan Hus">Jan Hus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hussite_Wars" title="Hussite Wars">Hussite Wars</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bohemian_Reformation" title="Bohemian Reformation">Bohemian Reformation</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Jan_Hus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Jan_Hus.jpg/220px-Jan_Hus.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b6/Jan_Hus.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="309" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Jan_Hus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Jan_Hus" title="Jan Hus">Jan Hus</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hussite" class="mw-redirect" title="Hussite">Hussite</a> movement that was to become the Moravian Church was started by <a href="/wiki/Jan_Hus" title="Jan Hus">Jan Hus</a> (English: <span lang="en">John Huss</span>) in early 15th century <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>, in what is today the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-history_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-history-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Hus objected to some of the practices and doctrines of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>; specifically, he wanted the <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">liturgy</a> to be celebrated in <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">Czech</a>, lay people to receive <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)#Communion_rite" title="Mass (liturgy)">communion</a> in both kinds (bread <i>and</i> wine – that is, in Latin, <a href="/wiki/Utraquist" class="mw-redirect" title="Utraquist"><i>communio sub utraque specie</i></a>), married priests, and eliminating <a href="/wiki/Indulgences" class="mw-redirect" title="Indulgences">indulgences</a> and the idea of <a href="/wiki/Purgatory" title="Purgatory">Purgatory</a>. Since these actions predate the Protestant Reformation by a century, some historians claim the Moravian Church was the first <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> church.<sup id="cite_ref-Rican1992_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rican1992-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Crews2008_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crews2008-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Vaclav_Brozik_-_Hus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>Jan Hus-Council of Constance</i> by <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A1clav_Bro%C5%BE%C3%ADk" title="Václav Brožík">Václav Brožík</a></div></div></div>
<p>The movement gained support in the <a href="/wiki/Lands_of_the_Bohemian_Crown" title="Lands of the Bohemian Crown">Crown of Bohemia</a>. However, Hus was summoned to attend the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Constance" title="Council of Constance">Council of Constance</a>, which decided that he was a heretic and released him to the secular authority, which sentenced him to be burned at the stake on 6 July 1415.  From 1419 to 1437 were a series of <a href="/wiki/Hussite_Wars" title="Hussite Wars">Hussite Wars</a> initially between various Catholic rulers and the Hussites, and then the political situation continued into a Hussite civil war between the more compromising <a href="/wiki/Utraquism" title="Utraquism">Utraquists</a> and the radical <a href="/wiki/Taborite" class="mw-redirect" title="Taborite">Taborites</a>. In 1434, an army of Utraquists and Catholics defeated the Taborites at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lipany" title="Battle of Lipany">Battle of Lipany</a>.  The Utraquists signed the <a href="/wiki/Compacts_of_Basel" title="Compacts of Basel">Compacts of Basel</a> on 5 July 1436.
</p><p>Within fifty years of Hus' death, a contingent of his followers had become independently organised as the "Bohemian Brethren" (<i>Čeští bratři</i>) or <a href="/wiki/Unity_of_the_Brethren" title="Unity of the Brethren">Unity of the Brethren</a> (<i>Jednota bratrská</i>), which was founded in <a href="/wiki/Kunvald" title="Kunvald">Kunvald</a>, Bohemia, in 1457. A brother known as Gregory the Patriarch was very influential in forming the group, as well as the teachings of <a href="/wiki/Peter_Chelcicky" class="mw-redirect" title="Peter Chelcicky">Peter Chelcicky</a>. This group held to a strict obedience to the Sermon on the Mount, which included non-swearing of oaths, non-resistance, and not accumulating wealth. Because of this, they considered themselves separate from the majority Hussites that did not hold those teachings. They received episcopal ordination through the <a href="/wiki/Waldensians" title="Waldensians">Waldensians</a> in 1467.<sup id="cite_ref-Rican1992_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rican1992-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>36 ff</span></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Crews2008_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crews2008-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>107 ff</span></sup> These were some of the earliest <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestants</a>, rebelling against Rome some fifty years before <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rican1992_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rican1992-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Crews2008_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Crews2008-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> By the middle of the 16th century as many as 90 per cent of the inhabitants of the <a href="/wiki/Lands_of_the_Bohemian_Crown" title="Lands of the Bohemian Crown">Bohemian Crown</a> were <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nabozenstvi_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nabozenstvi-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The majority of the nobility was Protestant, and the schools and printing-shops established by the Moravian Church were flourishing.
</p><p>Protestantism had a strong influence in the education of the population. Even in the middle of the 16th century there was not a single town without a Protestant school in the <a href="/wiki/Lands_of_the_Bohemian_Crown" title="Lands of the Bohemian Crown">Bohemian crown lands</a>, and many had more than one, mostly with two to six teachers each. In <a href="/wiki/Jihlava" title="Jihlava">Jihlava</a>, a principal Protestant center in Moravia, there were five major schools: two German, one Czech, one for girls and one teaching in Latin, which was at the level of a <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(school)" title="Gymnasium (school)">high/grammar school</a>, lecturing on Latin, Greek and Hebrew, Rhetorics, Dialectics, fundamentals of Philosophy and fine arts, as well as religion according to the <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_Confession" title="Augsburg Confession">Lutheran Augustana</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-elvert_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elvert-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Counter-Reformation">Counter-Reformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moravian_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Counter-Reformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With the <a href="/wiki/Charles_University#Protestant_academy_.281419.E2.80.931622.29" title="Charles University">University of Prague</a> also firmly in hands of Protestants, the local Catholic church was unable to compete in the field of education. Therefore, the Jesuits were invited, with the backing of the Catholic Habsburg rulers, to come to the <a href="/wiki/Lands_of_the_Bohemian_Crown" title="Lands of the Bohemian Crown">Bohemian Crown</a> and establish a number of Catholic educational institutions. One of these is the <a href="/wiki/Palack%C3%BD_University,_Olomouc" class="mw-redirect" title="Palacký University, Olomouc">university</a> in the Moravian capital of <a href="/wiki/Olomouc" title="Olomouc">Olomouc</a>. In 1582 they forced closure of local Protestant schools.
</p><p>In 1617, <a href="/wiki/Matthias,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Matthias, Holy Roman Emperor">Emperor Matthias</a> had his fiercely Catholic brother <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Ferdinand II, Holy Roman Emperor">Ferdinand of Styria</a> elected King of Bohemia, but in 1618 <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> Bohemian noblemen, who feared losing their religious freedom,<sup id="cite_ref-Polisensky_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Polisensky-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> started the <a href="/wiki/Bohemian_Revolt" title="Bohemian Revolt">Bohemian Revolt</a>. The Revolt started by the unplanned second <a href="/wiki/Defenestrations_of_Prague" title="Defenestrations of Prague">Defenestrations of Prague</a> and was defeated in 1620 in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_White_Mountain" title="Battle of White Mountain">Battle of White Mountain</a> near Prague. As consequence the local Protestant noblemen were either executed or expelled from the country while the Habsburgs placed Catholic (and mostly German speaking) nobility in their place. The war, plague, and subsequent disruption led to a decline in the population from over 3 million to some 800,000 people. By 1622 the entire education system was in the hands of Jesuits and all Protestant schools were closed.
</p><p>The Brethren were forced to operate underground and eventually dispersed across <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">Northern Europe</a> as far as the <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">Low Countries</a>, where their <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">Bishop</a> <a href="/wiki/John_Amos_Comenius" title="John Amos Comenius">John Amos Comenius</a> attempted to direct a resurgence. The largest remaining communities of the Brethren were located in <a href="/wiki/Leszno" title="Leszno">Leszno</a> (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <i lang="de">Lissa</i>) in <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a>, which had historically strong ties with the Czechs, and small, isolated groups in <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a>. These latter are referred to as "the Hidden Seed" which John Amos Comenius had prayed would preserve the evangelical faith in the land of the fathers.
</p><p>In addition to the Renewed Unitas Fratrum or Moravian Church, which preserves the Unitas Fratrum's three orders of <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">episcopal</a> ordination, The <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Church_of_Czech_Brethren" title="Evangelical Church of Czech Brethren">Evangelical Church of Czech Brethren</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Czechoslovak_Hussite_Church" title="Czechoslovak Hussite Church">Czechoslovak Hussite Church</a> also continue the Hussite tradition in the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovak</a> Republics today, although they only account for <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_the_Czech_Republic" title="Religion in the Czech Republic">0.8% of the Czech population</a> (which is 79.4% non-religious, and 10.4% Catholic).
</p>
<h3><span id="Herrnhuter_Br.C3.BCdergemeine.2C_18th-century_renewal"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Herrnhuter_Brüdergemeine,_18th-century_renewal">Herrnhuter Brüdergemeine, 18th-century renewal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moravian_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Herrnhuter Brüdergemeine, 18th-century renewal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Haidt_Zizendorf.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/12/Haidt_Zizendorf.JPG/220px-Haidt_Zizendorf.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="183" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/12/Haidt_Zizendorf.JPG/330px-Haidt_Zizendorf.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/12/Haidt_Zizendorf.JPG/440px-Haidt_Zizendorf.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1320" data-file-height="1100" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Haidt_Zizendorf.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Nicolaus_Zinzendorf" title="Nicolaus Zinzendorf">Nicolaus Zinzendorf</a> preaching to people from many nations</div></div></div>
<p>In 1722, a small group of Bohemian Brethren (the "Hidden Seed") who had been living in northern <a href="/wiki/Moravia" title="Moravia">Moravia</a> as an illegal underground remnant surviving in Catholic setting of the Habsburg Empire for nearly 100 years, arrived at the <a href="/wiki/Berthelsdorf" title="Berthelsdorf">Berthelsdorf</a> estate of <a href="/wiki/Nicolaus_Ludwig_Zinzendorf" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicolaus Ludwig Zinzendorf">Nikolaus Ludwig von Zinzendorf</a>, a nobleman who had been brought up in the traditions of <a href="/wiki/Lutheran" class="mw-redirect" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a> <a href="/wiki/Pietism" title="Pietism">Pietism</a>. Out of a personal commitment to helping the poor and needy, he agreed to a request from their leader (<a href="/wiki/Christian_David" title="Christian David">Christian David</a>, an itinerant carpenter) that they be allowed to settle on his lands in <a href="/wiki/Upper_Lusatia" title="Upper Lusatia">Upper Lusatia</a>, which is in present-day <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a> in the eastern part of modern-day <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>. The Margraviates of Upper and Lower Lusatia were governed in personal union by the Saxon rulers and enjoyed great autonomy, especially in religious questions.
</p><p>The refugees established a new village called <a href="/wiki/Herrnhut" title="Herrnhut">Herrnhut</a>, about 2 miles (3&#160;km) from Berthelsdorf. The town initially grew steadily, but major religious disagreements emerged and by 1727 the community was divided into factions. Count Zinzendorf worked to bring about unity in the town and the <i>Brotherly Agreement</i> was adopted by the community on 12 May 1727. This is considered the beginning of the renewal. Then, on 13 August 1727 the community underwent a dramatic transformation when the inhabitants of Herrnhut "learned to love one another", following an experience that they attributed to a visitation of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit" title="Holy Spirit">Holy Spirit</a>, similar to that recorded in the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> on the day of <a href="/wiki/Pentecost" title="Pentecost">Pentecost</a>.
</p><p>Herrnhut grew rapidly following this transforming revival and became the centre of a major movement for Christian renewal and mission during the 18th century. The <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">episcopal</a> <a href="/wiki/Ordination" title="Ordination">ordination</a> of the Ancient Unitas Fratrum was transferred in 1735 to the Renewed Unitas Fratrum by the Unity's two surviving bishops, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Ernst_Jablonski" title="Daniel Ernst Jablonski">Daniel Ernst Jablonski</a> and Christian Sitkovius. The carpenter <a href="/wiki/David_Nitschmann_der_Bischof" title="David Nitschmann der Bischof">David Nitschmann</a> and, later, Count von Zinzendorf, were the first two bishops of the Renewed Unity.  Moravian historians identify the main achievements of this period as:
</p>
<ol><li>Setting up a watch of <a href="/wiki/Continual_prayer" title="Continual prayer">continuous prayer</a> that ran uninterrupted, 24 hours a day, for 100 years.</li>
<li>Originating the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Watchwords" title="Daily Watchwords">Daily Watchwords</a></i>.</li>
<li>Establishing more than 30 settlements internationally on the Herrnhut model, which emphasized prayer and worship, and a form of communal living in which simplicity of lifestyle and generosity with wealth were held to be important spiritual attributes. The purpose of these communities was to assist the members resident there in the sanctification of their lives, to provide a meeting place for Christians from different confessional backgrounds, to provide Christian training for their own children and the children of their friends and supporters and to provide support for the Moravian Mission work throughout the world. As a result, although personal property was held, divisions between social groups and extremes of wealth and poverty were largely eliminated.</li>
<li>Being the first Protestant church body to begin missionary work; and</li>
<li>Forming many hundreds of small renewal groups operating within the existing churches of Europe, known as "diaspora societies". These groups encouraged personal prayer and worship, Bible study, confession of sins and mutual accountability.</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Missions">Missions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moravian_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Missions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Group_associated_with_the_Moravian_Church_by_Johann_Valentin_Haidt.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Group_associated_with_the_Moravian_Church_by_Johann_Valentin_Haidt.jpg/220px-Group_associated_with_the_Moravian_Church_by_Johann_Valentin_Haidt.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Group_associated_with_the_Moravian_Church_by_Johann_Valentin_Haidt.jpg/330px-Group_associated_with_the_Moravian_Church_by_Johann_Valentin_Haidt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/Group_associated_with_the_Moravian_Church_by_Johann_Valentin_Haidt.jpg/440px-Group_associated_with_the_Moravian_Church_by_Johann_Valentin_Haidt.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2400" data-file-height="2013" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Group_associated_with_the_Moravian_Church_by_Johann_Valentin_Haidt.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Portrait of a group of Moravian Church members with King <a href="/wiki/George_II_of_Great_Britain" title="George II of Great Britain">George II of Great Britain</a>, attributed to <a href="/wiki/Johann_Valentin_Haidt" class="mw-redirect" title="Johann Valentin Haidt">Johann Valentin Haidt</a>, circa 1752–1754</div></div></div>
<p>Along with the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Danish_Mission_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Danish Mission College">Royal Danish Mission College</a>, the Moravian missionaries were the first large-scale <a href="/wiki/Mission_(Christian)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mission (Christian)">Protestant missionary movement</a>. They sent out the first missionaries when there were only 300 inhabitants in Herrnhut. Within 30 years, the church sent hundreds of Christian missionaries to many parts of the world, including the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> (see <a href="/wiki/Christian_Munsee" title="Christian Munsee">Christian Munsee</a>), the <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">Arctic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Far_East" title="Far East">Far East</a>. They were the first to send lay people (rather than clergy) as missionaries, the first Protestant denomination to minister to slaves, and the first Protestant presence in many countries.
</p><p>Owing to Zinzendorf's personal contacts with their royalty, the first Moravian missions were directed to the <a href="/wiki/Denmark-Norway" class="mw-redirect" title="Denmark-Norway">Dano-Norwegian Empire</a>. While attending the coronation of <a href="/wiki/Christian_VI_of_Denmark" title="Christian VI of Denmark">Christian VI of Denmark</a>, Zinzendorf was profoundly struck by two <a href="/wiki/Kalaallit_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Kalaallit people">Inuit</a> converts of <a href="/wiki/Hans_Egede" title="Hans Egede">Hans Egede</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Kangeq" title="Kangeq">mission in Greenland</a> and also by an African from the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The first Moravian mission was established on the Caribbean island of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Thomas,_U.S._Virgin_Islands" title="Saint Thomas, U.S. Virgin Islands">St Thomas</a> in 1732 by a potter named <a href="/wiki/Johann_Leonhard_Dober" title="Johann Leonhard Dober">Johann Leonhard Dober</a> and a carpenter named <a href="/wiki/David_Nitschmann_der_Bischof" title="David Nitschmann der Bischof">David Nitschmann</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-S&amp;H_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S&amp;H-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>7</span></sup> who later became the first bishop of the Renewed Unity in 1735. <a href="/wiki/Matthias_Stach" class="mw-redirect" title="Matthias Stach">Matthaeus Stach</a> and two others founded the first <a href="/wiki/Moravian_mission_in_Greenland" class="mw-redirect" title="Moravian mission in Greenland">Moravian mission in Greenland</a> in 1733 at <a href="/wiki/Neu-Herrnhut" class="mw-redirect" title="Neu-Herrnhut">Neu-Herrnhut</a> on <a href="/wiki/Baal%27s_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Baal&#39;s River">Baal's River</a>, which became the nucleus of the modern capital <a href="/wiki/Nuuk" title="Nuuk">Nuuk</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bishop_John_Ettwein.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Bishop_John_Ettwein.png/170px-Bishop_John_Ettwein.png" decoding="async" width="170" height="211" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Bishop_John_Ettwein.png/255px-Bishop_John_Ettwein.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Bishop_John_Ettwein.png/340px-Bishop_John_Ettwein.png 2x" data-file-width="603" data-file-height="750" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bishop_John_Ettwein.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>In 1772, <a href="/wiki/John_Ettwein" class="mw-redirect" title="John Ettwein">John Ettwein</a><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and his group of some 200 <a href="/wiki/Lenape" title="Lenape">Lenape</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mohican" class="mw-redirect" title="Mohican">Mohican</a> <a href="/wiki/Christians" title="Christians">Christians</a> traveled west along <a href="/wiki/Great_Shamokin_Path" title="Great Shamokin Path">The Great Shamokin Path</a> from their village of Friedenshütten (<i>Cabins of Peace</i>) near modern <a href="/wiki/Wyalusing,_Pennsylvania" title="Wyalusing, Pennsylvania">Wyalusing</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Susquehanna_River" title="Susquehanna River">North Branch Susquehanna River</a> to their new village of <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://trailofhopeblog.com/2011/04/878/">Friedensstadt (<i>City of Peace</i>)</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Beaver_River_(Pennsylvania)" title="Beaver River (Pennsylvania)">Beaver River</a> in southwestern <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Moravians founded missions with <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">Algonquian</a>-speaking <a href="/wiki/Mohican" class="mw-redirect" title="Mohican">Mohican</a> in the British <a href="/wiki/Colony_of_New_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Colony of New York">colony of New York</a> in <a href="/wiki/British_North_America" title="British North America">British North America</a>. For instance, they founded one in 1740 at the Mohican village of <i><a href="/wiki/Shekomeko" class="mw-redirect" title="Shekomeko">Shekomeko</a></i> in present-day <a href="/wiki/Dutchess_County,_New_York" title="Dutchess County, New York">Dutchess County, New York</a>. The converted Mohican people formed the first native Christian congregation in the present-day United States of America. Because of local hostility to the Mohican, the Moravian support of the Mohican led to rumors of their being secret <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Jesuits</a>, trying to ally the Mohican with France in the ongoing <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_Wars" title="French and Indian Wars">French and Indian Wars</a>.
</p><p>Although supporters defended their work, at the end of 1744, the colonial government based at <a href="/wiki/Poughkeepsie,_New_York" title="Poughkeepsie, New York">Poughkeepsie</a> expelled the Moravians from New York.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1741, <a href="/wiki/David_Nitschmann_der_Bischof" title="David Nitschmann der Bischof">David Nitschmann</a> and <a href="/wiki/Count_Zinzendorf" class="mw-redirect" title="Count Zinzendorf">Count Zinzendorf</a> led a small community to found a mission in the colony of <a href="/wiki/Pennsylvania" title="Pennsylvania">Pennsylvania</a>. The mission was established on <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Eve" title="Christmas Eve">Christmas Eve</a>, and was named Bethlehem, after the <a href="/wiki/Bethlehem" title="Bethlehem">Biblical town in Judea</a>. There, they ministered to the Algonquian <a href="/wiki/Lenape_Nation" class="mw-redirect" title="Lenape Nation">Lenape</a>. <a href="/wiki/Bethlehem,_Pennsylvania" title="Bethlehem, Pennsylvania">Bethlehem, Pennsylvania</a> is today the seventh largest city in Pennsylvania. Later, colonies were also founded in North Carolina, where Moravians led by Bishop August Gottlieb Spangenberg purchased 98,985 acres (400.58 km2) from <a href="/wiki/John_Carteret,_2nd_Earl_Granville" title="John Carteret, 2nd Earl Granville">John Carteret, 2nd Earl Granville</a>. This large tract of land was named <i>die <a href="/wiki/Wachau" title="Wachau">Wachau</a></i>, or <a href="/wiki/Wachovia,_North_Carolina" class="mw-redirect" title="Wachovia, North Carolina">Wachovia</a>, after one of Zinzendorf's ancestral estates on the <a href="/wiki/Danube_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Danube River">Danube River</a> in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Austria" title="Lower Austria">Lower Austria</a>. Other early settlements included Bethabara (1753), Bethania (1759) and Salem (now referred to as <a href="/wiki/Old_Salem" title="Old Salem">Old Salem</a> in <a href="/wiki/Winston-Salem" class="mw-redirect" title="Winston-Salem">Winston-Salem</a> North Carolina) (1766).
</p><p>In 1801 the Moravians established Springplace mission to the <a href="/wiki/Cherokee_Nation" title="Cherokee Nation">Cherokee Nation</a> in what is now <a href="/wiki/Murray_County,_Georgia" title="Murray County, Georgia">Murray County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>.  Coinciding with the <a href="/wiki/Trail_of_Tears" title="Trail of Tears">forced removal</a> of the Cherokees to Oklahoma, this mission was replaced in 1842 by New Springplace in <a href="/wiki/Oaks,_Oklahoma" title="Oaks, Oklahoma">Oaks, Oklahoma</a>. Due to <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">Civil War</a>-related violence, New Springplace closed in 1862, and resumed during the 1870s. Finally, in 1898, the Moravian Church discontinued their missionary engagement with the Cherokees, and New Springplace, now the Oaks Indian Mission, was transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America" class="mw-redirect" title="Danish Evangelical Lutheran Church in America">Danish Evangelical Lutheran Church</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-EOHC-Oaks_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EOHC-Oaks-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The start of far-flung missionary work necessitated the establishment of independently administered provinces. So, from about 1732,<sup id="cite_ref-S&amp;H_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-S&amp;H-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>7</span></sup> the history of the church becomes the history of its provinces.
</p><p>Eventually, the Moravian missions in <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> were transferred to the local Presbyterian and Lutheran Churches respectively.
</p><p>The first mission station in present-day <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> was established by the Moravian Georg Schmidt at <a href="/wiki/Genadendal" title="Genadendal">Genadendal</a> in 1738.  The mission at <a href="/wiki/Wupperthal" title="Wupperthal">Wupperthal</a>, established by the <a href="/wiki/Rhenish_Missionary_Society" title="Rhenish Missionary Society">Rhenish Missionary Society</a> was eventually transferred to the Moravian Church.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Present">Present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moravian_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Mary_Greenwood_on_gravestones_born_1752.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Mary_Greenwood_on_gravestones_born_1752.jpg/220px-Mary_Greenwood_on_gravestones_born_1752.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Mary_Greenwood_on_gravestones_born_1752.jpg/330px-Mary_Greenwood_on_gravestones_born_1752.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Mary_Greenwood_on_gravestones_born_1752.jpg/440px-Mary_Greenwood_on_gravestones_born_1752.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3474" data-file-height="2314" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Mary_Greenwood_on_gravestones_born_1752.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mary Greenwoord was buried in <a href="/wiki/Gracehill" title="Gracehill">Gracehill</a> in <a href="/wiki/County_Antrim" title="County Antrim">County Antrim</a> in 1752. Her gravestone is identical in style to hundreds of others irrespective of their gender or former status</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Friedensthal_Moravian.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e5/Friedensthal_Moravian.jpg/220px-Friedensthal_Moravian.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e5/Friedensthal_Moravian.jpg/330px-Friedensthal_Moravian.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/e/e5/Friedensthal_Moravian.jpg/440px-Friedensthal_Moravian.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2288" data-file-height="1712" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Friedensthal_Moravian.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Friedensthal Moravian Church <a href="/wiki/Christiansted,_United_States_Virgin_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Christiansted, United States Virgin Islands">Christiansted, St Croix, USVI</a> founded in 1755</div></div></div>
<p>The modern Moravian Church, with about 750,000 members worldwide,<sup id="cite_ref-stats_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stats-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> continues to draw on traditions established during the 18th-century renewal. In many places it observes the convention of the <a href="/wiki/Lovefeast" class="mw-redirect" title="Lovefeast">lovefeast</a>, originally started in 1727. It uses older and traditional music in worship. Brass music, congregational singing and choral music continue to be very important in Moravian congregations. In addition, in some older congregations, Moravians are buried in a traditional <a href="/wiki/God%27s_Acre" title="God&#39;s Acre">God's Acre</a>, a graveyard with only flat gravestones, signifying the equality of the dead before God and organized by sex, age and marital status rather than family.
</p><p>The Moravians continue their long tradition of missionary work, for example in the Caribbean, where the Jamaican Moravian Church has begun work in Cuba and in Africa where the Moravian Church in Tanzania has missions in the Democratic Republic of Congo and Uganda. This is reflected in their broad global distribution. The Moravians in Germany, whose central settlement remains at <a href="/wiki/Herrnhut" title="Herrnhut">Herrnhut</a>, are highly active in education and social work. The American Moravian Church sponsors the <a href="/wiki/Moravian_College" title="Moravian College">Moravian College</a> and Seminary. The largest concentration of Moravians today is in <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">Tanzania</a>.
</p><p>The motto of the Moravian Church is: "In essentials, unity; in nonessentials, liberty; and in all things, love".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some Moravian scholars point to a different formula as a guide to constructive debate about faith. This formula was first advanced by <a href="/wiki/Luke_of_Prague" title="Luke of Prague">Luke of Prague</a> (1460–1528), one of the bishops of the ancient Unitas Fratrum. Luke taught that one must distinguish between things that are essential, ministerial or incidental to salvation. The essentials are God's work of creation, redemption and sanctification, as well as the response of the believer through faith, hope and love. Things ministerial are such items as the Bible, church, sacraments, doctrine and priesthood. These mediate the sacred and should thus be treated with respect, but they are not considered essential. Finally, incidentals include things such as vestments or names of services that may reasonably vary from place to place
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moravian_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Provinces">Provinces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moravian_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Provinces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For its global work, the Church is organised into Unity Provinces, Mission Provinces and Mission Areas and four regions of Africa, Caribbean and Latin America, Northern America, and Europe. The categorisation is based on the level of independence of the Province. Unity Province implies a total level of independence, Mission Province implies a partial level of supervision from a Unity Province, and Mission Area implies full supervision by a Unity Province. (The links below connect to articles about the history of the Church in specific provinces after 1732, where written.)
</p><p>In the Czech Republic and Honduras occurred splits within the churches after charismatic revivals; non-charismatic minorities formed own bodies but both sides remained connected to the international church. The minority communities are listed as "mission provinces".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

</p><p>Each Province is governed by a <a href="/wiki/Synod" title="Synod">synod</a>, made up of representatives from each congregation plus <i>ex officio</i> members.
</p><p>The Synod elects the Provincial Board (aka Provincial Elders' Conference or PEC<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>) to be responsible for the work of the Province and its international links between Synods.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Districts">Districts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moravian_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Districts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many, but not all, of the Provinces are divided into Districts.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Congregations">Congregations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moravian_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Congregations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Neudietendorf_Bruederkirche-Innen1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Neudietendorf_Bruederkirche-Innen1.jpg/220px-Neudietendorf_Bruederkirche-Innen1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Neudietendorf_Bruederkirche-Innen1.jpg/330px-Neudietendorf_Bruederkirche-Innen1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Neudietendorf_Bruederkirche-Innen1.jpg/440px-Neudietendorf_Bruederkirche-Innen1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3055" data-file-height="2029" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Neudietendorf_Bruederkirche-Innen1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A Moravian church in <a href="/wiki/Neudietendorf" title="Neudietendorf">Neudietendorf</a> in <a href="/wiki/Thuringia" title="Thuringia">Thuringia</a> in Germany</div></div></div>
<p>Each congregation belongs to a district and has spiritual and financial responsibilities for work in its own area as well as provincially. The Congregation Council (all the members of a congregation) usually meets twice a year and annually elects the Joint Board of Elders and Trustees that acts as an executive.
</p><p>In some provinces two or more congregations may be grouped into circuits, under the care of one minister.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unity_Synod">Unity Synod</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moravian_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Unity Synod">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Unity Synod meets every seven years and is attended by delegates from the different Unity Provinces and affiliated Provinces.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unity_Board">Unity Board</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moravian_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Unity Board">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Unity Board is made up of one member from each Provincial Board, and acts as an executive committee between Unity Synods. It meets three times between Synods but much of its work is done by correspondence and postal voting.
</p><p>The <b>President</b> of the Unity Board (who is elected by the Board for two years and not allowed to serve for more than two terms) works from his/her own Provincial office. The <b>Unity Business Administrator</b> is an officer appointed by the Unity Board to administer the day-to-day affairs of the Unity through the office of the Unity.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orders_of_Ministry">Orders of Ministry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moravian_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Orders of Ministry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ordained ministry in the Moravian Church emphasizes the pastoral role. A candidate for ministry who has been approved by their home province and has completed the prescribed course of study (usually a Master of Divinity degree in the US and Europe) may be ordained a Deacon upon acceptance of a call. Deacons may serve in a pastoral office and administer sacraments. A deacon is normally supervised by a presbyter who serves as mentor. After several years of satisfactory service, the Deacon may be consecrated as a Presbyter. Presbyters function in the local congregation in the same manner as deacons, but may also
serve to mentor deacons and may be assigned to other leadership roles in a particular province.
</p><p>An Acolyte is a layperson who has received approval to assist the pastor in a specific local congregation. The acolyte may assist in the serving of Holy Communion but may not consecrate the elements.
</p><p>The highest order of ministry is that of a bishop. Bishops are elected by Provincial Synods usually through ecclesiastical ballot without nomination. In the Moravian Church, bishops do not have an administrative role but rather serve as spiritual leaders and pastors to the pastors. Bishops serve the worldwide Unity.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The Moravian Church is reputed to have received the <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_succession" title="Apostolic succession">apostolic succession</a> through the Waldensian Church, but the historicity of this is disputed.  In Berlin, 1735, the Renewed Unity, i.e. the Moravian Brethren in Herrnhut, received the historic episcopal ordination from the two surviving bishops of the Ancient Unity: Bishop <a href="/wiki/John_Amos_Comenius" title="John Amos Comenius">John Amos Comenius'</a> grandson <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Ernst_Jablonski" title="Daniel Ernst Jablonski">Daniel Ernst Jablonski</a> and Christian Sitkovius. This bishop's consecration continues today.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Beliefs">Beliefs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moravian_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Beliefs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In her <i>Book of Order</i><sup id="cite_ref-UBoO_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UBoO-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-UBoO-Online_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UBoO-Online-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> the several provinces of the Moravian Unity accept:
</p>
<ul><li>The three <a href="/wiki/Ecumenical_Creeds" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecumenical Creeds">Ecumenical Creeds</a>: <a href="/wiki/Apostles%27_Creed" title="Apostles&#39; Creed">Apostles'</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicene_Creed" title="Nicene Creed">Nicene</a> and <a href="/wiki/Athanasian_Creed" title="Athanasian Creed">Athanasian</a></li>
<li>The first 21 articles of the Unaltered <a href="/wiki/Augsburg_Confession" title="Augsburg Confession">Augsburg Confession</a></li>
<li>The Confession of the Unity of the Bohemian Brethren of 1535</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Barmen_Declaration" title="Barmen Declaration">Barmen Declaration</a> of 1934</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Small_Catechism" class="mw-redirect" title="Small Catechism">Small Catechism</a> of <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">Martin Luther</a></li>
<li>The Synod of Berne of 1532</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Thirty-Nine_Articles" class="mw-redirect" title="The Thirty-Nine Articles">The Thirty-Nine Articles</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a></li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg_Catechism" title="Heidelberg Catechism">Heidelberg Catechism</a></li></ul>
<p>According to the Ground of the Unity<sup id="cite_ref-UBoO_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UBoO-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-UBoO-Online_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UBoO-Online-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> of 1957, fundamental beliefs include but are not limited to:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Holy_Trinity" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Trinity">Holy Trinity</a>: the Father, the Son or Logos/Word, and the Holy Spirit.</li>
<li>The Fatherhood of God</li>
<li>God's Love for fallen humanity</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Incarnation" title="Incarnation">Incarnation</a> of God in the God/Man <a href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesus Christ">Jesus Christ</a></li>
<li>Jesus' sacrificial death for the sinful rebellion of humanity</li>
<li>Jesus' <a href="/wiki/Resurrection" title="Resurrection">Resurrection</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ascension_of_Jesus_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Ascension of Jesus Christ">Ascension</a> and Exaltation to the Right Hand of the Father</li>
<li>Jesus' sending of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Spirit" title="Holy Spirit">Holy Spirit</a> to strengthen, sustain and empower believers</li>
<li>Jesus' eventual return, in majesty, <a href="/wiki/Last_Judgment" title="Last Judgment">to judge the living and the dead</a></li>
<li>The Kingdom of Christ shall never end</li>
<li>There is one <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">Baptism</a> for the forgiveness of sins.  Rebaptism is not allowed.</li>
<li>Infants are baptized most commonly, but all forms of baptism are accepted (infant or adult; pouring, sprinkling or immersion).</li>
<li>Moravian doctrine teaches that the Body and Blood of Christ are present in <a href="/wiki/Holy_Communion" class="mw-redirect" title="Holy Communion">Holy Communion</a>. Without seeking to explain the "Mode" or the "How" of the Presence of Jesus' Body and Blood in the Eucharist, they teach a <a href="/wiki/Sacramental_union" title="Sacramental union">sacramental union</a> whereby with the Bread and the Wine the Body and Blood are also received.  Individual believers are allowed to believe in other interpretations, however. Cf. the "Easter Morning Litany" of the Moravian Church, a statement of faith, in the Moravian Book of Worship, p.&#160;85.</li></ul>
<p>These tenets of classical Christianity are not unique to the Moravian Church. The emphasis in both the Ancient Unity and the Renewed Unity is on Christian living and the fellowship of believers as a true witness to a vibrant Christian Faith.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spirit_of_the_Moravian_Church">Spirit of the Moravian Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moravian_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Spirit of the Moravian Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An account of the ethos of the Moravian Church is given by one of its British bishops, Clarence H. Shawe.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In a lecture series delivered at the Moravian Theological Seminary in Bethlehem, Pennsylvania, Shawe described the Spirit of the Moravian Church as having five characteristics: simplicity, happiness, unintrusiveness, fellowship, and the ideal of service.
</p><p><i><b>Simplicity</b></i> is a focus on the essentials of faith and a lack of interest in the niceties of doctrinal definition. Shawe quotes Zinzendorf's remark that "The Apostles say: 'We believe we have salvation through the grace of Jesus Christ....' If I can only teach a person that catechism I have made him a divinity scholar for all time" (Shawe, 1977, p.&#160;9). From this simplicity flow secondary qualities of genuineness and practicality.
</p><p><i><b>Happiness</b></i> is the natural and spontaneous response to God's free and gracious gift of salvation. Again Shawe quotes Zinzendorf: "There is a difference between a genuine Pietist and a genuine Moravian. The Pietist has his sin in the foreground and looks at the wounds of Jesus; the Moravian has the wounds in the forefront and looks from them upon his sin. The Pietist in his timidity is comforted by the wounds; the Moravian in his happiness is shamed by his sin" (p.&#160;13).
</p><p><i><b>Unintrusiveness</b></i> is based on the Moravian belief that God positively wills the existence of a variety of churches to cater for different spiritual needs. There is no need to win converts from other churches. The source of Christian unity is not legal form but everyone's heart-relationship with the Saviour.
</p><p><i><b>Fellowship</b></i> is based on this heart-relationship. Shawe says: "The Moravian ideal has been to gather together kindred hearts.... Where there are 'Christian hearts in love united', there fellowship is possible in spite of differences of intellect and intelligence, of thought, opinion, taste and outlook. ... Fellowship [in Zinzendorf's time] meant not only a bridging of theological differences but also of social differences; the artisan and aristocrat were brought together as brothers and sat as equal members on the same committee" (pp.&#160;21,22).
</p><p><i><b>The ideal of service</b></i> entails happily having the attitude of a servant. This shows itself partly in faithful service in various roles within congregations but more importantly in service of the world "by the extension of the Kingdom of God". Historically, this has been evident in educational and especially missionary work. Shawe remarks that "none could give themselves more freely to the spread of the gospel than those Moravian emigrants who, by settling in Herrnhut [i.e., on Zinzendorf's estate], had gained release from suppression and persecution" (p.&#160;26).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecumenical_relations">Ecumenical relations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Moravian_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Ecumenical relations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Full_communion" title="Full communion">Full communion</a></div>
<p>The Moravian Church provinces are members individually of the <a href="/wiki/World_Council_of_Churches" title="World Council of Churches">World Council of Churches</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_World_Federation" title="Lutheran World Federation">Lutheran World Federation</a>. Most provinces are also members of their national councils of churches, such as the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Church_in_Germany" title="Evangelical Church in Germany">Evangelische Kirche in Deutschland (EKD)</a> in Germany and the <a href="/wiki/National_Council_of_Churches" title="National Council of Churches">National Council of Churches</a> of Christ in the US, the all African Council of Churches, the Caribbean Council of Churches, the Jamaica Council of Churches. The American Southern Province was instrumental in the founding the North Carolina Council of Churches. The British Province is of the <a href="/wiki/Churches_Together_in_Britain_and_Ireland" title="Churches Together in Britain and Ireland">Churches Together in Britain and Ireland</a> (formerly the <a href="/wiki/British_Council_of_Churches" class="mw-redirect" title="British Council of Churches">British Council of Churches</a>) and has an interim Communion agreement with the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>. The two North American provinces are in full communion with the <a href="/wiki/Evangelical_Lutheran_Church_in_America" title="Evangelical Lutheran Church in America">Evangelical Lutheran Church in America</a> (ELCA). The Northern Province of the Moravian Church voted June 18, 2010 to enter into full communion with the <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_(United_States)" title="Episcopal Church (United States)">Episcopal Church</a>. The Moravian Church's Southern Province also voted to enter into full communion with the Episcopal Church during its synod in September 2010. Each province can independently enter into full communion relationships.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> In the 1980s there were discussions in England by which an agreement was created that would have created full communion between the Moravians, <a href="/wiki/Presbyterians" class="mw-redirect" title="Presbyterians">Presbyterians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Methodists" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodists">Methodists</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>. The Presbyterians and Methodists would have accepted the <a href="/wiki/Historic_Episcopate" class="mw-redirect" title="Historic Episcopate">Historic Episcopate</a>, but since the Moravians already had this, they would have changed nothing. This agreement fell through because the <a href="/wiki/General_Synod_of_the_Church_of_England" title="General Synod of the Church of England">General Synod of the Church of England</a> did not give approval.
</p><p>One aspect of Moravian history and mission is the so-called "Diaspora" work in Germany and Eastern Europe, seeking to deepen and encourage the Christian life among members of the territorial churches, particularly in <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_states" title="Baltic states">Baltic states</a>, but also throughout the German-speaking lands. Count Zinzendorf's ideal was a fellowship of all Christians, regardless of denominational names, and the Moravian Brethren sought in the Diaspora not to convert people to the Moravian Church but to awaken the hearts of believers and make them better members of the churches to which they already belonged. At first the object of suspicion, in the course of time the Moravian Diaspora workers became valued co-workers in eastern Europe. This Diaspora work suffered almost total destruction in World War II, but is still carried on within the territorial churches of Germany. With the independence of <a href="/wiki/Estonia" title="Estonia">Estonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a>, it was revealed that much of the Diaspora Work there had been kept alive in spite of domination by the former <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> and had even borne fruit.
</p>
