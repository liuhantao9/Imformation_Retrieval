<p><b>Hindu communities</b> are found in several countries of South America, but they are strongest in <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>.
There are about 550,000 <a href="/wiki/Hindu" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindu">Hindus</a> in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, chiefly the descendants of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indian</a> indentured labourers in the <a href="/wiki/Guianas" class="mw-redirect" title="Guianas">Guianas</a>. There are about 270,000 Hindus in <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>, 150,000 in <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>, and some others in <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>. In Guyana, Hindus form 35% of the population.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism_in_Argentina">Hinduism in Argentina</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hinduism_in_South_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Hinduism in Argentina">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Argentina has 2,030 <a href="/wiki/Non-resident_Indian_and_person_of_Indian_origin" title="Non-resident Indian and person of Indian origin">persons of Indian origin</a> (PIOs) and 1,300 non-resident Indians (NRIs). Some of them still refer to <a href="/wiki/Ayurveda" title="Ayurveda">ayurveda</a>, practice yoga, enjoy Indian classical music and speak the Hindi language.
</p><p>They have established an Indian Association <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://moia.gov.in/showsublink.asp?sublinkid=184">[2]</a> in the northern <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Argentina" title="Provinces of Argentina">provinces</a> and organise social and cultural events to celebrate Indian festivals. Unfortunately, there is little interaction between them and those who have settled down in other parts of the extensive country.
</p><p>A large number of the Indian diaspora living in <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a> are businessmen, doctors, financial
or business executives and employees of multinational corporations. Most of them have retained
their Indian citizenship.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://indiandiaspora.nic.in/diasporapdf/chapter19.pdf">[3]</a>
</p><p>Many <a href="/wiki/Hindus" title="Hindus">Hindus</a> are <a href="/wiki/Indo-Caribbeans" title="Indo-Caribbeans">Indo-Caribbeans</a> from <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism_in_Brazil">Hinduism in Brazil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hinduism_in_South_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hinduism in Brazil">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hinduism_in_Brazil" title="Hinduism in Brazil">Hinduism in Brazil</a></div>
<p>According to the 2011 census, there are about 9500 Hindus in brazil constituting 0.01 of the population of brazil.
Most of the Brazilian Hindus are ethnic East <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indians</a>. However, there are new converts due to the missionary effects of <a href="/wiki/International_Society_for_Krishna_Consciousness" title="International Society for Krishna Consciousness">Hare Krishnas</a>. 
</p><p>A small number of <a href="/wiki/Sindhis" title="Sindhis">Sindhis</a> that arrived from Suriname and Central America in 1960 to set up shop as traders in the city of <a href="/wiki/Manaus" title="Manaus">Manaus</a> is considered to be the first wave of immigration. The second wave consisted of university professors who arrived in the 1960s and also in the 1970s.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism_in_Chile">Hinduism in Chile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hinduism_in_South_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hinduism in Chile">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A few Indians had gone to <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> in the 1920s. The others migrated there about 30 years ago<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2017)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> &#8212; not
only from India, but also from <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">Nigeria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> and
<a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>.
</p><p>The Hindu Community in Chile comprises more than 1400 members.Among these ,400 people (90 families) lives in the Capital city Santiago.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most of the Hindus in Chile are Sindhis.There is a Hindu Temple  in <a href="/wiki/Punta_Arenas" title="Punta Arenas">Punta Arenas</a> which provide services in both Sindhi and Spanish.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Besides Punta Arenas, the Indian business community is also present in <a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Chile" class="mw-redirect" title="Santiago de Chile">Santiago</a>, the capital of Chile, and <a href="/wiki/Iquique" title="Iquique">Iquique</a>. The activities of businessmen in Santiago are mainly confined to imports and retail stores.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism_in_Colombia">Hinduism in Colombia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hinduism_in_South_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hinduism in Colombia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hinduism_in_Colombia" title="Hinduism in Colombia">Hinduism in Colombia</a></div>
<p>Hinduism in <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a> was mainly introduced with the arrival of <a href="/wiki/Indian_people" title="Indian people">Indians</a>, especially <a href="/wiki/Indo-Caribbeans" title="Indo-Caribbeans">Indian</a> migrant workers from <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>. There are <a href="/wiki/ISKCON" class="mw-redirect" title="ISKCON">ISKCON</a> (<i>The Hare-Krishnas</i>) centers in the capital, <a href="/wiki/Bogota" class="mw-redirect" title="Bogota">Bogota</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism_in_French_Guiana">Hinduism in French Guiana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hinduism_in_South_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hinduism in French Guiana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hinduism_in_French_Guiana" title="Hinduism in French Guiana">Hinduism in French Guiana</a></div>
<p>Most of the Hindus in French Guiana are of Surinamese origin. According to the 2000 census 1.6% of the total population (3,200 out of 202,000) were Hindu. <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.worldstatesmen.org/Fr_Guiana.html">[4]</a>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism_in_Guyana">Hinduism in Guyana</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hinduism_in_South_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hinduism in Guyana">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Religion_in_Guyana#Hinduism" title="Religion in Guyana">Religion in Guyana §&#160;Hinduism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hinduism_in_Guyana" title="Hinduism in Guyana">Hinduism in Guyana</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Central_Vaidik_Mandir,_Georgetown,_Guyana..jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Central_Vaidik_Mandir%2C_Georgetown%2C_Guyana..jpg/200px-Central_Vaidik_Mandir%2C_Georgetown%2C_Guyana..jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Central_Vaidik_Mandir%2C_Georgetown%2C_Guyana..jpg/300px-Central_Vaidik_Mandir%2C_Georgetown%2C_Guyana..jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Central_Vaidik_Mandir%2C_Georgetown%2C_Guyana..jpg/400px-Central_Vaidik_Mandir%2C_Georgetown%2C_Guyana..jpg 2x" data-file-width="3456" data-file-height="2304" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Central_Vaidik_Mandir,_Georgetown,_Guyana..jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Central Vaidik Mandir in Georgetown</div></div></div>
<p>About 84% of the East Indian immigrants were <a href="/wiki/Hindu" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindu">Hindu</a>. During the indenture period, the East Indian caste system broke down. Hinduism was redefined, and caste-distinguishing practices were eliminated. Christian missionaries attempted to convert East Indians during the indenture period, beginning in 1852, but met with little success. The missionaries blamed the Brahmins for their failure: the Brahmins began administering spiritual rites to all Hindus regardless of caste once the Christian missionaries started proselytizing in the villages, hastening the breakdown of the caste system. After the 1930s, Hindu conversions to Christianity slowed because the status of Hinduism improved and the discrimination against Hindus  diminished.
</p><p>In areas where there are large percentage of Indo Guyanese residing together — <a href="/wiki/Mandirs" class="mw-redirect" title="Mandirs">Mandirs</a> of various sizes can be found, according to the population. All main Hindu occasions are observed — <a href="/wiki/Basant_Panchami" class="mw-redirect" title="Basant Panchami">Basant Panchami</a> in January to <a href="/wiki/Gita_Jayanti" title="Gita Jayanti">Gita Jayanti</a> in December.
</p><p>Since the late 1940s, reform movements caught the attention of many Guyanese Hindus. The most important, the <a href="/wiki/Arya_Samaj" title="Arya Samaj">Arya Samaj</a> movement, arrived in Guyana in 1910. Arya Samaj doctrine rejects the idea of caste and the exclusive role of Brahmins as religious leaders. The movement preaches monotheism and opposition to the use of images in worship as well as many traditional Hindu rituals. The <a href="/wiki/Arya_Samaj" title="Arya Samaj">Arya Samajis</a> often did not get along with the <a href="/wiki/San%C4%81tan%C4%AB" title="Sanātanī">Sanātanīs</a> (orthodox <a href="/wiki/Hindus" title="Hindus">Hindus</a>), but today many have put those differences aside. Approximately between 216,000 and 230,000 identified themselves as Hindus in the 2002 census.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism_in_Paraguay">Hinduism in Paraguay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hinduism_in_South_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hinduism in Paraguay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the 2002 census, it was estimated that about 551 Hindus live in <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">Paraguay</a>. They make up 0.01% of Paraguay's population. Paraguay's ambassador to India, Mr Pappalardo, gave Punjab farmers a high opportunity to invest the country.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Most of the Hindus live in <a href="/wiki/Asunci%C3%B3n" title="Asunción">Asunción</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism_in_Peru">Hinduism in Peru</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hinduism_in_South_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hinduism in Peru">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The first ‘Indian Indians’ to have arrived in <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a> were businessmen who had gone there in the early 1960s. Later on, the community grew in number marginally until the early 80s, after which many of its members left due to the severe local economic crises and the prevailing terrorism. Those with relatives in other Latin countries joined them.
</p><p>In the recent past, the size of the community has remained stable. There is a small remnant of the original ‘native Indians’ in this country who still maintain their traditional culture and religious beliefs.
</p><p>Most members of the local Indian community are Sindhis. They are reasonably well-off, but very few can be regarded as prosperous. Their general level of education is low. Most of them speak only their mother tongue and Spanish, with a smattering of English.
</p><p>There is also here a small number of professionals from other parts of India. Residence permits are not difficult to obtain in Peru. But citizenship is more complicated and only a small number of Indians have obtained it – not more than 10 out of a total number of almost forty persons. While a few cultural activities are organized by the more enterprising PIOs, in general they maintain a low profile. Considering the vast distance that separates the community from India, its interest in its country of origin is limited to major events, mainly derived from occasional browsing on the internet. But being invariably first generation migrants, many of them do occasionally visit India.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism_in_Suriname">Hinduism in Suriname</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hinduism_in_South_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hinduism in Suriname">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Arya_Dewaker,_exterior5.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Arya_Dewaker%2C_exterior5.jpg/220px-Arya_Dewaker%2C_exterior5.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Arya_Dewaker%2C_exterior5.jpg/330px-Arya_Dewaker%2C_exterior5.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Arya_Dewaker%2C_exterior5.jpg/440px-Arya_Dewaker%2C_exterior5.jpg 2x" data-file-width="709" data-file-height="399" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Arya_Dewaker,_exterior5.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Arya Dewaker Hindu temple in Paramaribo.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hinduism_in_Suriname" title="Hinduism in Suriname">Hinduism in Suriname</a></div>
<p>The story of Hinduism in Suriname is broadly parallel to that in <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a>. <a href="/wiki/Indian_indenture_system" title="Indian indenture system">Indian indentured labourers</a> were sent to colonial <a href="/wiki/Dutch_colonisation_of_the_Guianas" title="Dutch colonisation of the Guianas">Dutch Guiana</a> by special arrangement between the Dutch and British.  
The difference is that the Netherlands' more liberal policy toward Hinduism allowed the culture to develop stronger. Examples are the lack of a rigid caste system and the almost universal reading of <a href="/wiki/Gita" class="mw-redirect" title="Gita">Gita</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ramayan" class="mw-redirect" title="Ramayan">Ramayan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>According to the 2012 census of Suriname, Hindus constitute 22.3% of the population.<sup id="cite_ref-CENSUS2012_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CENSUS2012-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  Hindus are mostly concentrated in Northern coastal regions of Suriname: <a href="/wiki/Nickerie_District" title="Nickerie District">Nickerie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wanica_District" title="Wanica District">Wanica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saramacca_District" title="Saramacca District">Saramacca</a>, where they constitute the largest religious group.  There are several <a href="/wiki/Hindu" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindu">hindu</a> temples in Suriname.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism_in_Uruguay">Hinduism in Uruguay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hinduism_in_South_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Hinduism in Uruguay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are a few Yoga organizations in <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">Uruguay</a>, which spread Indian thought
and philosophy-prominent among them are, Sivapremananda Ashram of the Divine
Society. A portion of the beach in <a href="/wiki/Montevideo" title="Montevideo">Montevideo</a> has been named after Mahatma
Gandhi and a bust of Gandhiji installed in one of the parks along the beach. There
is a school named after him in Montevideo, a street and another school named after
Republic of India.<sup id="cite_ref-meaindia.nic.in_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-meaindia.nic.in-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> There is a small Indian community in Uruguay consisting of 300 members approx.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism_in_Venezuela">Hinduism in Venezuela</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hinduism_in_South_America&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Hinduism in Venezuela">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the oil-related high-income years of the 1970s, there were around 400 NRIs in this country. There are also several thousand of <a href="/wiki/Indo-Caribbeans" title="Indo-Caribbeans">Indo-Caribbeans</a> from <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> in the cities of <a href="/wiki/Ciudad_Guayana" title="Ciudad Guayana">Ciudad Guayana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Caracas" title="Caracas">Caracas</a>.
</p><p>The Indian community consisted of personnel from the petroleum and petrochemical sectors, as
well as a large number of traders. Many of them had taken their families with them to Venezuela,
whether from India or elsewhere. Most of the traders belonged to the <a href="/wiki/Sindhi_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Sindhi people">Sindhi</a> community but
there were also some persons from Gujarat, Punjab and the southern Indian States.
</p><p>When the oil boom ended in 1982, followed by devaluation of the local currency, many of the NRIs decided to seek their fortunes elsewhere. Currently, the Diaspora has been whittled down to half its former size. There are now only about 45 Indian families in Venezuela who are mainly engaged in retail trade. There are also a small number of experts in high tech. industries such as telecommunications.
</p><p>All of them have adapted themselves very well to their country of residence and are generally
held in high regard by the local people on account of their hard work, expertise and non-political
nature.
</p><p>The Venezuelan Constitution guarantees equal rights without discrimination to all expatriate
personnel. This has facilitated the Indian community’s life.
</p><p>Another interesting feature is that many local persons are interested in Indian religions and spirituality.
</p><p>Some members of the Indian community also attend their functions. Most of the NRIs are well educated. However, given their small numbers, they have not formed themselves into an active representative body. But they remain in touch with one another and with the Indian Embassy in Caracas. Even though they have little time to engage in numerous cultural activities, they do
get together to celebrate Indian festivals like <a href="/wiki/Diwali" title="Diwali">Diwali</a>.
</p><p>On the whole, the Indian community in Venezuela is quite prosperous and has a per capita income
that is above the national average that is itself as high as US$8,300 in terms of PPP. They take an active part in mobilising donations to help in alleviating distress at times of national calamities in India.
</p><p><b>Source</b>
