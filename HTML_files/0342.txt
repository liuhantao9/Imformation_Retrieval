<p>The <b>Maroni</b> or <b>Marowijne</b> (French: <i lang="fr">Maroni</i>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i lang="nl">Marowijne</i>, <a href="/wiki/Sranan_Tongo" title="Sranan Tongo">Sranan Tongo</a>: <i>Marwina-Liba</i>) is a river in South America that forms the border between <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Course">Course</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroni_(river)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Course">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Maroni runs through the <a href="/wiki/Guianan_moist_forests" title="Guianan moist forests">Guianan moist forests</a> ecoregion.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
It originates in the <a href="/wiki/Tumuk_Humak_Mountains" title="Tumuk Humak Mountains">Tumuk Humak Mountains</a> and forms the (disputed) border between <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> (<a href="/wiki/Region_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Region of France">region</a> of French Guiana) and Suriname. In its upper reaches, it is also known as the <a href="/wiki/Lawa_River_(South_America)" title="Lawa River (South America)">Lawa River</a>, and close to its source it is known as the Marouini River.
</p><p>There are two nature preserves located in the estuary region on the Surinamese side of the river, near the village of <a href="/wiki/Galibi,_Suriname" title="Galibi, Suriname">Galibi</a>. They provide protection for the birds and the <a href="/wiki/Leatherback_Sea_Turtle" class="mw-redirect" title="Leatherback Sea Turtle">Leatherback Sea Turtles</a> that hatch there.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Territorial_dispute">Territorial dispute</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroni_(river)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Territorial dispute">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Borders_of_Suriname" title="Borders of Suriname">Borders of Suriname</a></div>
<p>In 1860, the question was posed from the French side, which of the two tributary rivers was the headwater, and thus the border. A joint French-Dutch commission was appointed to review the issue. The Dutch side of the commission consisted of J.H. Baron van Heerdt tot Eversberg, J.F.A. Cateau van Rosevelt and August Kappler. Luits Vidal, Ronmy, Boudet and Dr. Rech composed the French side. In 1861 measurements were taken, which produced the following result: the Lawa had a <a href="/wiki/Discharge_(hydrology)" title="Discharge (hydrology)">discharge</a> of 35,960 m<sup>3</sup>/minute at a width of 436&#160;m; the Tapanahony had a discharge of 20,291 m<sup>3</sup>/minute at a width of 285&#160; m. Thus, the Lawa River was the <a href="/wiki/Headwater" class="mw-redirect" title="Headwater">headwater</a> of the Maroni River.
</p><p>There were no problems with this decision until 1885. However, the discovery of gold in the area between the Lawa and the Tapanahony created a new border conflict. On 29 November 1888, France and the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> reached an agreement that the conflict should be subject to arbitration. Czar <a href="/wiki/Alexander_III_of_Russia" title="Alexander III of Russia">Alexander III of Russia</a>, acting as the arbitrator, decided that the Lawa was the headwater of the Maroni, and thus should be considered the border.
</p><p>However, this decision created another issue as to which river is the source of the Lawa. The Netherlands considered the Marowini (the eastern tributary river) to be the source of the Lawa; the French considered the <a href="/wiki/Litani_River_(South_America)" class="mw-redirect" title="Litani River (South America)">Litani</a>, located further to the west, to be the source of the Lawa. This issue has still not been resolved.
</p><p>The <i>Litani</i> originates in the Tumuk Humak Mountains at approximately 2½°&#160;N 55°&#160;W; along its path it is fed by <i>Koele Koelebreek</i>, the <i>Lokereek</i>, the <i>Mapaonikreek</i> and the <i>Oelemari</i>.
</p><p>The <i>Marowini</i> also has its source in the Tumuk Humak Mountains, at approximately 2°&#160;N, 54°&#160;W; it also absorbs the <i>Koelebreek</i>, among others.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Numbers">Numbers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroni_(river)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Numbers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Maroni basin is 68,700&#160;km²; above the island <i><a href="/wiki/Langatabbetje" class="mw-redirect" title="Langatabbetje">Langatabbetje</a></i> (110&#160;km away from the mouth of the river) this area is 63,700&#160;km². Between 1952 and 1973, the average discharge near <i>Langatabbetje</i> was about 1,700&#160;m³/second. The minimum was 95&#160;m³/second, the maximum 6,550&#160;m³/second. The estuary is approximately 90&#160;km long; the average tidal range in the estuary is 2&#160;m; in the dry season, the salt reaches about 40&#160;km upstream to the town of <a href="/wiki/Albina,_Suriname" title="Albina, Suriname">Albina</a>.
</p><p>The estimated potential hydro power for Suriname is between 1150 and 1250 megawatts.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">Exploration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maroni_(river)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Exploration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Maroni is the most extensively studied of all the rivers in Suriname. In the 16th century, there were already ships exploring the estuary, by <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Keymis" class="mw-redirect" title="Lawrence Keymis">Lawrence Keymis</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Masham&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Thomas Masham (page does not exist)">Thomas Masham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de_Berrio" title="Antonio de Berrio">Antonio de Berrio</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adriaen_Cabeliau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Adriaen Cabeliau (page does not exist)">Adriaen Cabeliau</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and in the 17th century, by Harcourt, Fisher and De Vries. In the 18th century more extensive excursions took place, by Mentell, Patris, Le Blond and Heneman and in the 19th century by Zegelaar, <a href="/wiki/Jules_Crevaux" title="Jules Crevaux">Jules Crevaux</a>, Coudreau, Ten Kate, Joost and others.
</p><p>Of importance in the 20th century were the Gonini and Tapanahony expeditions, and the Tumuk Humak and Southern Border Expeditions. From these arose the research of the <i>Geologisch Mijnbouwkundige Dienst</i> (GMD) and the <i>Centraal Bureau voor Luchtkartering</i> (CBL).
</p>
