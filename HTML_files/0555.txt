<p>The <b>Native American name controversy</b> is an ongoing discussion about the changing terminology used by the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">indigenous peoples of the Americas</a> to describe themselves, as well as how they prefer to be referred to by others.  Preferred terms vary primarily by region and age. As indigenous people and communities are diverse, there is no consensus on naming, aside from the fact that most people prefer to be referred to by their specific <a href="/wiki/Nation" title="Nation">nation</a>.
</p><p>When discussing broad groups of peoples, naming may be based on shared language, region, or historical relationship, such as  "<a href="/wiki/Algonquin_language" title="Algonquin language">Algonquin</a>-speaking peoples", "<a href="/wiki/Pueblo" title="Pueblo">Pueblo</a>-dwelling peoples", "<a href="/wiki/Plains_Indians" title="Plains Indians">Plains Indians</a>" or "<a href="/wiki/Sioux" title="Sioux">LDN</a> peoples" (<a href="/wiki/Lakota_people" title="Lakota people">Lakota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dakota_people" title="Dakota people">Dakota</a> and <a href="/wiki/Assiniboine" title="Assiniboine">Nakota</a> peoples).
</p><p>Many <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Exonyms" class="mw-redirect" title="Exonyms">exonyms</a> have been used to refer to the indigenous peoples of <a href="/wiki/The_Americas" class="mw-redirect" title="The Americas">the Americas</a>, who were resident within their own countries when European colonists arrived in the 15th and 16th centuries. Some of these names were based on <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, or other European language terminology used by earlier explorers and colonists; some resulted from the colonists' attempt to translate <a href="/wiki/Endonyms" class="mw-redirect" title="Endonyms">endonyms</a> from the native language into their own; and some were pejorative terms arising out of prejudice and fear, during periods of <a href="/wiki/American_Indian_Wars" title="American Indian Wars">conflict</a> between the cultures involved.
</p><p>In the 20th and 21st centuries, indigenous peoples in the Americas have been more vocal about the ways they wish to be referred to, pressing for the elimination of terms widely considered to be obsolete, inaccurate, or <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racist</a>. During the latter half of the 20th century and the rise of the <a href="/wiki/Indian_rights_movement" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian rights movement">Indian rights movement</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_government" class="mw-redirect" title="United States government">United States government</a> responded by proposing the use of the term "Native American", to recognize the primacy of indigenous peoples' tenure in the nation. The term has met with only partial acceptance. Other naming conventions have been proposed and used, but none are accepted by all indigenous groups. Typically, each name has a particular audience and political or cultural connotation, and regional usage varies.
</p><p>In Canada, while <a href="/wiki/Status_Indian" class="mw-redirect" title="Status Indian">Status Indian</a> remains a legal designation due to the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Act" title="Indian Act">Indian Act</a>, the term "Indian" is generally considered offensive when used by non-Natives with the term <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a> being preferred for peoples covered by the Indian Act and Indigenous peoples preferred for Native peoples generally or when talking about <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> and <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_in_Canada" title="Métis in Canada">Métis</a> who do not fall under the "First Nations" category.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Salient_issues_affecting_the_debate">Salient issues affecting the debate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Salient issues affecting the debate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>Historical, traditional use of a term (example: "Indian" is a name which many <a href="/wiki/American_Indian_elder" title="American Indian elder">elders</a> have known all their lives, and their families may continue to use the familiar term)</li>
<li>Rejection of a word perceived as <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/quaint" class="extiw" title="wikt:quaint">quaint</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pejorative" title="Pejorative">pejorative</a> (example: "<a href="/wiki/Eskimo#Nomenclature" title="Eskimo">Eskimo</a>")</li>
<li>Rejection of names used by outsiders and not chosen by the tribe itself, or indigenous people at large (example: "<a href="/wiki/Nez_Perce_people" title="Nez Perce people">Nez Perce</a>" is a French phrase; "Native American" was coined by the US government)</li>
<li>Perception that a name is inherently racist, or has over time acquired racist overtones (example: "Redskin")</li>
<li>Rejection of names assigned by an occupying and oppressive colonial government or expedition</li>
<li>Belief that a name is too inclusive or not inclusive enough of all indigenous people, so does not effectively represent the intended group. Examples: "Aboriginal", still used in Canada, has become associated with <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_Australians" title="Aboriginal Australians">Aboriginal Australians</a>, given its wide use on that continent; the United Nations uses "Indigenous" to refer to all tribal peoples around the world (as their representatives chose to be identified); "Native American" in general use has not applied to indigenous peoples within Canada or Mexico</li>
<li>Reluctance to be referred to by a collective, racial name, rather than simply their traditional name for themselves</li>
<li>Belief that a universal or collective name suggests, inaccurately, that the indigenous cultures referred to are homogeneous, monolithic bodies, rather than the widely varied separate nations that they actually are</li>
<li>Understanding that "Indians" cannot be used to describe global indigenous cultures when it already is used for <a href="/wiki/Indian_people" title="Indian people">people from India</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_in_the_United_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Métis in the United States">Métis in the United States</a></div>
<h3><span id=".22Indian.22_and_.22American_Indian.22_.28since_1492.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Indian&quot;_and_&quot;American_Indian&quot;_(since_1492)">"Indian" and "American Indian" (since 1492)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: &quot;Indian&quot; and &quot;American Indian&quot; (since 1492)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1683_Mortier_Map_of_North_America,_the_West_Indies,_and_the_Atlantic_Ocean_-_Geographicus_-_Atlantique-mortier-1693.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/1683_Mortier_Map_of_North_America%2C_the_West_Indies%2C_and_the_Atlantic_Ocean_-_Geographicus_-_Atlantique-mortier-1693.jpg/250px-1683_Mortier_Map_of_North_America%2C_the_West_Indies%2C_and_the_Atlantic_Ocean_-_Geographicus_-_Atlantique-mortier-1693.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="199" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/1683_Mortier_Map_of_North_America%2C_the_West_Indies%2C_and_the_Atlantic_Ocean_-_Geographicus_-_Atlantique-mortier-1693.jpg/375px-1683_Mortier_Map_of_North_America%2C_the_West_Indies%2C_and_the_Atlantic_Ocean_-_Geographicus_-_Atlantique-mortier-1693.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/1683_Mortier_Map_of_North_America%2C_the_West_Indies%2C_and_the_Atlantic_Ocean_-_Geographicus_-_Atlantique-mortier-1693.jpg/500px-1683_Mortier_Map_of_North_America%2C_the_West_Indies%2C_and_the_Atlantic_Ocean_-_Geographicus_-_Atlantique-mortier-1693.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5000" data-file-height="3982" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1683_Mortier_Map_of_North_America,_the_West_Indies,_and_the_Atlantic_Ocean_-_Geographicus_-_Atlantique-mortier-1693.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1693 nautical chart of the Atlantic Ocean marked with "<i>Route d'Europe aux Indes Occidentales</i>" or "West Indies"</div></div></div>
<p>Europeans at the time of <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">Christopher Columbus</a>'s voyage often referred to all of South and East Asia as "India" or "the Indias/<a href="/wiki/Indies" class="mw-redirect" title="Indies">Indies</a>", sometimes dividing the area into "<a href="/wiki/Greater_India" title="Greater India">Greater India</a>", "Middle India", and "Lesser India".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Erdapfel" title="Erdapfel">oldest surviving terrestrial globe</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Behaim" title="Martin Behaim">Martin Behaim</a> in 1492 (before Columbus' voyage), labels the entire Asian subcontinent region as "India".<sup id="cite_ref-Encarta_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Encarta-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Columbus carried a passport in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> from the Spanish monarchs that dispatched him <i>ad partes Indie</i><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> ("toward the regions of India") on their behalf. When he landed in the <a href="/wiki/Antilles" title="Antilles">Antilles</a>, Columbus referred to the resident peoples he encountered there as "Indians" reflecting his purported belief that he had reached the Indian Ocean.<sup id="cite_ref-STRAIGHTDOPE_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-STRAIGHTDOPE-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The name stuck; for centuries the native people of the Americas were collectively called "Indians" in various European languages. This misnomer was perpetuated in place naming; the islands of the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> were named, and are still known as, the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a>.
</p><p>As European colonists began to move into the Americas in the 16th and 17th centuries, and have more sustained contact with the resident peoples, it became clear to them that the residents were not a homogeneous group sharing a unified culture and government, but discrete societies with their own distinct languages and social systems. Early historical accounts show that some colonists attempted to learn and record the <a href="/wiki/Exonym_and_endonym" title="Exonym and endonym">autonyms</a> of these individual groups, but the use of the general term "Indian" persisted.
</p><p>In 1968, the <a href="/wiki/American_Indian_Movement" title="American Indian Movement">American Indian Movement</a> was founded. In 1977, a delegation from the <a href="/wiki/International_Indian_Treaty_Council" title="International Indian Treaty Council">International Indian Treaty Council</a>, an arm of AIM, elected to collectively identify as "American Indian", at the United Nations <a href="/wiki/Conference_on_Indians_in_the_Americas" title="Conference on Indians in the Americas">Conference on Indians in the Americas</a> at Geneva, Switzerland. Some activists and public figures of indigenous descent, such as Russell Means, prefer "American Indian" to the more recently adopted "Native American".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_etymology">Alternative etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Alternative etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the late 20th century, some American public figures suggested that the origin of the term was not from a confusion with India, but from the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> expression <i>En Dios</i>, meaning "in God", or a similar one in Italian. Proponents of this idea include the American Indian activist <a href="/wiki/Russell_Means" title="Russell Means">Russell Means</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-I_AM_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-I_AM-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and the comedian <a href="/wiki/George_Carlin" title="George Carlin">George Carlin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WORDMYTHS_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WORDMYTHS-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In his book <i>The Wind Is My Mother</i>, the <a href="/wiki/Muscogee_(Creek)" class="mw-redirect" title="Muscogee (Creek)">Muscogee</a> writer Bear Heart (<i>Nokus Feke Ematha Tustanaki</i>) wrote: "When Columbus found the natives here, they were gentle people who accepted him, so Columbus wrote in his journal, 'These are people of God' ("<i>una gente in Dios</i>"). Later the 's' was dropped and <i>Indio</i> became <i>Indian</i>."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> However, as the writer David Wilton noted in his book <i>Word Myths: Debunking Linguistic Urban Legends</i>, this phrase does not appear in any of Columbus' writing. Wilton also says that since Greek and Roman times, more than a millennium before the voyages of Columbus, many European languages used variations of the term "Indian" to describe the peoples of the Indian subcontinent.<sup id="cite_ref-WORDMYTHS_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WORDMYTHS-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Objections_.28since_the_1970s.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Objections_(since_the_1970s)">Objections (since the 1970s)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Objections (since the 1970s)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Objections to the usage of "Indian" and "American Indian" include the fact that "Indian" arose from a historical error, and thus does not accurately reflect the derivation of the people to whom it refers; and some feel that the term has absorbed negative and demeaning connotations through its historical usage that render it objectionable in context. Additionally, "American Indian" is often understood to mean only the peoples of the mainland body of the United States, which excludes other <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans in the United States</a> who are considered <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">indigenous peoples of the Americas</a>; including the <a href="/wiki/Haida_people" title="Haida people">Haida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tlingit_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Tlingit people">Tlingit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Athabaskan_languages" title="Athabaskan languages">Athabascan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yupik_peoples" title="Yupik peoples">Yup'ik</a> (<a href="/wiki/Siberian_Yupik_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Siberian Yupik people">Yuits</a>/<a href="/wiki/Alutiiq_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Alutiiq people">Alutiiq</a>/<a href="/wiki/Central_Alaskan_Yup%27ik_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Central Alaskan Yup&#39;ik people">Cup'ik</a>), <a href="/wiki/Inupiat_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Inupiat people">Iñupiat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aleut_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Aleut people">Aleut</a> (i.e., the groups whose traditional languages are <a href="/wiki/Eskimo%E2%80%93Aleut_languages" title="Eskimo–Aleut languages">Eskimo–Aleut languages</a>), <a href="/wiki/Marshallese_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Marshallese people">Marshallese</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Samoan_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Samoan people">Samoan</a>; who are referred to collectively as either <a href="/wiki/Alaskan_Native" class="mw-redirect" title="Alaskan Native">Alaskan Natives</a>, <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Native_Hawaiians" title="Native Hawaiians">Native Hawaiians</a> or <a href="/wiki/Siberian" class="mw-redirect" title="Siberian">Siberians</a>.
</p><p>Supporters of the terms "Indian" and "American Indian" argue that they have been in use for such a long period of time that many people have become accustomed to them and no longer consider them <a href="/wiki/Exonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Exonym">exonyms</a>. Both terms are still widely used today. "American Indian" appears often in treaties between the United States and the indigenous peoples with whom they have been negotiating since the colonial period, and many <a href="/wiki/United_States_federal_law" class="mw-redirect" title="United States federal law">federal</a>, state and local laws also use it.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Native_American.22_.28since_the_1960s.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Native_American&quot;_(since_the_1960s)">"Native American" (since the 1960s)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: &quot;Native American&quot; (since the 1960s)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> cites usage of the uncapitalized term <i>native American</i> in several publications reaching as far back as 1737,<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_English_Dictionary_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_English_Dictionary-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> but it is unclear whether these texts refer to indigenous peoples or simply to <a href="/wiki/Birthright_citizenship_in_the_United_States" title="Birthright citizenship in the United States">persons born on American soil</a>. During the 1850s, a group of <a href="/wiki/White_Anglo-Saxon_Protestant" title="White Anglo-Saxon Protestant">Anglo-Saxon Protestant Americans</a> used the capitalized term <i>Native Americans</i> to differentiate themselves from recent <a href="/wiki/Irish_Americans" title="Irish Americans">Irish</a> and <a href="/wiki/German_Americans" title="German Americans">German</a> immigrants, both of whom were predominantly Catholic. The group later formed the "<a href="/wiki/Know-Nothings" class="mw-redirect" title="Know-Nothings">Know-Nothings</a>", a 19th-century political party that <a href="/wiki/Opposition_to_immigration#United_States" title="Opposition to immigration">opposed immigration to the United States</a>, a policy known as <a href="/wiki/Nativism_(politics)#United_States" title="Nativism (politics)">nativism</a>. The Know-Nothings also called themselves the "Native American Party" and were referred to in the press with the capitalized term.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1918, leaders of the <a href="/wiki/Native_American_Church#History_of_the_peyote_religion" title="Native American Church">Peyote Religion</a> incorporated as the <a href="/wiki/Native_American_Church" title="Native American Church">Native American Church</a> of Oklahoma.<sup id="cite_ref-weston_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-weston-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In 1956, <a href="/wiki/Aldous_Huxley" title="Aldous Huxley">Aldous Huxley</a> wrote a letter in which he thanks his correspondent for "your most interesting letter about the Native American churchmen".<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_English_Dictionary_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford_English_Dictionary-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The use of <i>Native American</i> or <i>native American</i> to refer to peoples indigenous to the Americas came into widespread, common use during the <a href="/wiki/Civil_rights_movement" title="Civil rights movement">civil rights era</a> of the 1960s and 1970s. This term was considered to represent historical fact more accurately (i.e., "Native" cultures predated European colonization), while activists also believed it was free of negative historical connotations that had come to be associated with previous terms.
</p><p>Between 1982 and 1993, most American <a href="/wiki/Manual_of_style" class="mw-redirect" title="Manual of style">manuals of style</a> came to agree that "color terms" referring to ethnic groups should be capitalized as proper names, as well as <i>Native American</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Other objections to <i>Native American</i>—whether capitalized or not—include a concern that it is often understood to exclude American groups outside the continental US (e.g., Alaska, Hawaii, and Puerto Rico), and indigenous groups in South America, Mexico and Canada. The word <i>American</i> is sometimes questioned because the peoples referred to resided in the Americas before they were so named.
</p><p>As of 1995, according to the <a href="/wiki/US_Census_Bureau" class="mw-redirect" title="US Census Bureau">US Census Bureau</a>, 50% of people who identified as Indigenous preferred the term <i>American Indian</i>, 37% preferred <i>Native American</i>, and the remainder preferred other terms or had no preference.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Indigenous.22_.281980s.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Indigenous&quot;_(1980s)">"Indigenous" (1980s)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: &quot;Indigenous&quot; (1980s)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_American_Heritage_Dictionary" class="mw-redirect" title="The American Heritage Dictionary">The American Heritage Dictionary</a></i>, "<i>indigenous</i> specifies that something or someone is native rather than coming or being brought in from elsewhere: an indigenous crop; the <a href="/wiki/Ainu_people" title="Ainu people">Ainu</a>, a people indigenous to the northernmost islands of Japan."<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The United Nations <a href="/wiki/World_Summit_on_Sustainable_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="World Summit on Sustainable Development">World Summit on Sustainable Development</a> used the term "indigenous peoples" for the first time in its official political declaration in 2002. Prior to this date, the term was considered to be "still under debate" for usage in official UN documents.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term is less favored among some Canadian First Nations peoples; the French equivalent <i lang="fr" title="French language text">indigène</i> has historically been used in a derogatory sense toward them.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Aboriginal.22_and_.22Aborigine.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Aboriginal&quot;_and_&quot;Aborigine&quot;">"Aboriginal" and "Aborigine"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: &quot;Aboriginal&quot; and &quot;Aborigine&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The English adjective "aboriginal" and the noun "aborigine" comes from a Latin phrase meaning "from the origin;" the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Romans</a> used it to refer <a href="/wiki/Aborigines_(mythology)" title="Aborigines (mythology)">a contemporary group</a>, one of many <a href="/wiki/List_of_ancient_peoples_of_Italy" title="List of ancient peoples of Italy">ancient peoples in Italy</a>. Until about 1910, these terms were used in English to refer to various indigenous peoples. Today throughout most of the English-speaking world, it is most commonly understood to refer to the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australians" title="Indigenous Australians">Indigenous Australians</a>, with the notable exception of Canada, where the term "aboriginal" refers to <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_Canadians" class="mw-redirect" title="Aboriginal Canadians">Aboriginal Canadians</a> (but usually "aborigine" does not) (see below).
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Alaska_Native.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Alaska_Native&quot;">"Alaska Native"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: &quot;Alaska Native&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Alaska_Native" class="mw-redirect" title="Alaska Native">Alaska Native</a>" refers to the indigenous peoples in <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">Alaska</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Aleut_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Aleut people">Aleut</a>, <a href="/wiki/Athabaskan_languages" title="Athabaskan languages">Athabascan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alutiiq_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Alutiiq people">Alutiiq</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_Alaskan_Yup%27ik_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Central Alaskan Yup&#39;ik people">Cup'ik</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haida_people" title="Haida people">Haida</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inupiat_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Inupiat people">Iñupiat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tlingit_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Tlingit people">Tlingit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yupik_peoples" title="Yupik peoples">Yup'ik</a> peoples. The term predominates because of its legal use in the <a href="/wiki/Alaska_Native_Claims_Settlement_Act" title="Alaska Native Claims Settlement Act">Alaska Native Claims Settlement Act</a>, and includes all the above-named peoples.
</p>
<h4><span id=".22Eskimo.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Eskimo&quot;">"Eskimo"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: &quot;Eskimo&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Eskimo#Terminology" title="Eskimo">Eskimo §&#160;Terminology</a></div>
<p>The term <i><a href="/wiki/Eskimo" title="Eskimo">Eskimo</a></i> was once common, but is now sometimes perceived as derogatory, especially in Canada. In Alaska, however, the term <i>Eskimo</i> is commonly used, because it includes both Yupik and Inupiat, while <i>Inuit</i> is not accepted as a collective term or even specifically used for <i>Inupiat</i> (who technically are <i>Inuit</i>). No universal term other than Eskimo, inclusive of all Inuit and Yupik people, exists for the Inuit and Yupik peoples.<sup id="cite_ref-kaplan_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kaplan-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The primary reason that <i>Eskimo</i> is considered derogatory is the questionable but widespread perception<sup id="cite_ref-aueo_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aueo-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mailhot_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mailhot-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-creeml_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-creeml-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-igoddard_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-igoddard-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> that in <a href="/wiki/Algonkian_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Algonkian languages">Algonkian languages</a> it means "eaters of raw meat".<sup id="cite_ref-natlang_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-natlang-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bartlebyeskimo_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bartlebyeskimo-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> One <a href="/wiki/Cree" title="Cree">Cree</a> speaker suggested the original word that became corrupted to Eskimo might indeed have been <i>askamiciw</i> (which means "he eats it raw"), and the Inuit are referred to in some Cree texts as <i>askipiw</i> (which means "eats something raw").<sup id="cite_ref-natlang_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-natlang-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> It is generally held in Canada and Greenland that the term <i>Eskimo</i> is pejorative.<sup id="cite_ref-kaplan_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kaplan-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bartlebyeskimo_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bartlebyeskimo-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-stern1_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stern1-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ostg1_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ostg1-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id=".22Inuit.22_.28since_1977.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Inuit&quot;_(since_1977)">"Inuit" (since 1977)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: &quot;Inuit&quot; (since 1977)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Inuit#Nomenclature" title="Inuit">Inuit §&#160;Nomenclature</a></div>
<p>The people of the Canadian Arctic are currently officially known as the Inuit, which means 'the people', or singularly, Inuk, which means 'the person'.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Inuit_Circumpolar_Conference" class="mw-redirect" title="Inuit Circumpolar Conference">Inuit Circumpolar Conference</a> meeting in <a href="/wiki/Barrow,_Alaska" class="mw-redirect" title="Barrow, Alaska">Barrow, Alaska</a> officially adopted "Inuit" as a designation for all Eskimos, regardless of their local usages, in 1977.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> However, the Inuit Circumpolar Council, as it is known today, uses both "Inuit" and "Eskimo" in its official documents.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Amerind.22_or_.22Amerindian.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Amerind&quot;_or_&quot;Amerindian&quot;">"Amerind" or "Amerindian"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: &quot;Amerind&quot; or &quot;Amerindian&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The term "Amerind"/"Amerindian" is a <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> of "American Indian",<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> though it can also be parsed as a blend of "American" and "Indigenous". It was coined in 1902 by the <a href="/wiki/American_Anthropological_Association" title="American Anthropological Association">American Anthropological Association</a>. Usage in English occurs primarily in anthropological and linguistic contexts as well as in up-to-date news sources<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>. In French, the term "Amérindien" is used to describe the peoples residing in the Americas prior to European contact.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id=".22Canadian_Indians.22_.281700s.E2.80.93late_20th_century.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Canadian_Indians&quot;_(1700s–late_20th_century)">"Canadian Indians" (1700s–late 20th century)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: &quot;Canadian Indians&quot; (1700s–late 20th century)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Canadian <a href="/wiki/Indian_Act" title="Indian Act">Indian Act</a>, in defining the rights of people of recognized First Nations, refers to them as "Indians". The responsible federal government department was the Department of Indian Affairs and Northern Development, now <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_and_Northern_Affairs_Canada" title="Indigenous and Northern Affairs Canada">Indigenous and Northern Affairs Canada</a>, headed by the <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Indigenous_and_Northern_Affairs" class="mw-redirect" title="Minister of Indigenous and Northern Affairs">Minister of Indigenous and Northern Affairs</a>. The Act officially recognizes people commonly known as "Status Indians", although "Registered Indian" is the official term for those on the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Register" title="Indian Register">Indian Register</a>. Lands set aside for the use of First Nations are officially known as <a href="/wiki/Indian_reserve" title="Indian reserve">Indian reserves</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mandel,_Michael_1994_pp._354-356_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mandel,_Michael_1994_pp._354-356-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> The word "band" persists in <a href="/wiki/Band_government" title="Band government">band government</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Aboriginal_peoples.22_.28since_1900.29_and_.22Indigenous_peoples.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Aboriginal_peoples&quot;_(since_1900)_and_&quot;Indigenous_peoples&quot;">"Aboriginal peoples" (since 1900) and "Indigenous peoples"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: &quot;Aboriginal peoples&quot; (since 1900) and &quot;Indigenous peoples&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Canada, the term "<a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_peoples_in_Canada" class="mw-redirect" title="Aboriginal peoples in Canada">Aboriginal peoples in Canada</a>" is used for all indigenous peoples within the country, including the <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> and <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_people_(Canada)" class="mw-redirect" title="Métis people (Canada)">Métis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Mandel,_Michael_1994_pp._354-356_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mandel,_Michael_1994_pp._354-356-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> More recently, the term Indigenous peoples has been used more frequently and in 2015 the federal government department responsible for First Nations, Metis, and Inuit issues changed its name from <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_Affairs_and_Northern_Development_Canada" class="mw-redirect" title="Aboriginal Affairs and Northern Development Canada">Aboriginal Affairs and Northern Development Canada</a> to <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_and_Northern_Affairs_Canada" title="Indigenous and Northern Affairs Canada">Indigenous and Northern Affairs Canada</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id=".22First_Nations.22_.28since_the_1980s.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;First_Nations&quot;_(since_the_1980s)">"First Nations" (since the 1980s)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: &quot;First Nations&quot; (since the 1980s)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a></div>
<p>"First Nations" came into common usage in the 1980s to replace the term "Indian band".<sup id="cite_ref-gibson5_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gibson5-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Elder Sol Sanderson says that he coined the term in the early 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Others state that the term came into common usage in the 1970s to avoid using the word “Indian”, which some people considered offensive. Apparently, no legal definition of the term exists. Some Aboriginal peoples in Canada have also adopted the term “First Nation” to replace the word “band” in the name of their community.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>"First Nations" (most often used in the plural) has come into general use for the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">Indigenous peoples</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> located in what is now Canada, and their descendants, excluding the <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> and <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_people_(Canada)" class="mw-redirect" title="Métis people (Canada)">Métis</a>, who have distinct identities.<sup id="cite_ref-Rights_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rights-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The singular commonly used is "First Nations person" (when gender-specific, "First Nations man" or "First Nations woman").
</p><p>Some tribal governments of Canada also use the term "First Nations" to refer to any indigenous, tribal or nomadic society, using the term for such diverse groups as the <a href="/wiki/Romani_people" title="Romani people">Romani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sami_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Sami people">Saami</a>, <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Māori</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hmong_people" title="Hmong people">Hmong</a>, and the Australian Aborigines.<sup id="cite_ref-Rights_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rights-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the Canadian government has formally adopted use of the term "First Nations" and "Aboriginal peoples", the federal ministerial portfolio in charge of their affairs is named the Department of Indian Affairs and Northern Development and under the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Identity_Program" title="Federal Identity Program">Federal Identity Program</a> is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_Affairs_and_Northern_Development_Canada" class="mw-redirect" title="Aboriginal Affairs and Northern Development Canada">Aboriginal Affairs and Northern Development Canada</a> (AANDC), and the historical term "<a href="/wiki/Indian_Reserve" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian Reserve">Indian Reserve</a>" is still a legal land description. Some First Nations peoples also use "Indian Band" in their official names.
</p>
<h4><span id=".22First_Peoples.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;First_Peoples&quot;">"First Peoples"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: &quot;First Peoples&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>"First Peoples" is a broad term that includes First Nations, Inuit, <a href="/wiki/Inuvialuit" title="Inuvialuit">Inuvialuit</a>, and Métis (equivalent to "Aboriginal" or "indigenous" peoples)—and could be extended outside the Canadian context to comprise all descendants of pre-Columbian ethnic groups in the Americas, including (self-identified) ethnic groups whose ancestry is only partially of pre-Columbian groups (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Mestizo" title="Mestizo">Mestizo</a>). Due to its similarity with the term "First Nations", the two terms are sometimes used interchangeably.
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Native_Canadians.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Native_Canadians&quot;">"Native Canadians"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: &quot;Native Canadians&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>"Native" or "Native Canadian" is an ambiguous term, but people frequently use it in conversation or informal writing. A great majority use this term for describing aboriginal peoples, including aboriginal people themselves.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canadian_French_nomenclature">Canadian French nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Canadian French nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Canadian_French" title="Canadian French">Canadian French</a>, the terms are <i>première(s) nation(s)</i> for "First Nations" and <i>autochtone</i> for "Aboriginal" (used both as a noun and adjective).
</p><p>The term <i>indien</i> or <i>indienne</i> is used in the legislation, although the preferred term is now <i>amérindien</i>. The term <i>indigène</i> is not used as it is seen as having negative connotations because of its similarity to the French equivalent of <i>indigent</i> ("poor"). It has also acquired further negative associations in French, due to the <i><a href="/wiki/Indig%C3%A9nat" title="Indigénat">indigénat</a></i> code enforced in French colonial Africa, 1887–1947. The old French term <i>sauvage</i> ("wild") is no longer used either, as it is considered racist.
</p>
<h3><span id=".22Inuit.22_.28since_1977.29_2"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Inuit&quot;_(since_1977)_2">"Inuit" (since 1977)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: &quot;Inuit&quot; (since 1977)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Inuit#Nomenclature" title="Inuit">Inuit §&#160;Nomenclature</a></div>
<p>As a result of the Inuit Circumpolar Conference meeting in <a href="/wiki/Barrow,_Alaska" class="mw-redirect" title="Barrow, Alaska">Barrow, Alaska</a>, in 1977, the Canadian government usage has replaced the (locally) defunct term Eskimo with <i>Inuit</i> (<i>Inuk</i> in singular). The preferred term in Canada's Central Arctic is <i>Inuinnaq</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-translate_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-translate-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> and in the eastern Canadian Arctic <i><a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a></i>. The language is often called <i><a href="/wiki/Inuktitut" title="Inuktitut">Inuktitut</a></i>, though other local designations are also used.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional">Regional</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Regional">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h4><span id=".22Anishinaabe.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Anishinaabe&quot;">"Anishinaabe"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: &quot;Anishinaabe&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Algonquin_language" title="Algonquin language">Algonquin</a> <a href="/wiki/Ethnonym" title="Ethnonym">autonym</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Anishinaabe" title="Anishinaabe">Anishinaabe</a></i>, or <i>Anishinabe</i>, is used as a cross-tribal term in Algonquian-majority areas, such as Anishnabe Health, Anishnabe Education, and Training Circle. The term is also used among historically Anishinaabe peoples in the <a href="/wiki/Upper_Midwest" title="Upper Midwest">Upper Midwest</a> region of the United States.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinook_Jargon_nomenclature">Chinook Jargon nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Chinook Jargon nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chinook_Jargon" title="Chinook Jargon">Chinook Jargon</a>, the old trade language of the Pacific Northwest, uses <i>siwash</i> (an adaptation of the French <i>sauvage</i>) for "Indian", "Native American", or "First Nations", either as adjective or noun. While normally meaning a male native, it is used in certain combinations, such as <i>siwash cosho</i> ("a seal", literally "Indian pig" or "Indian pork").
</p><p>Many native communities perceive the terms <i>sauvage</i> and <i>siwash</i> negatively, but others use it freely. They consider use by non-natives to be derogatory. In the creolized form of Chinook Jargon spoken at the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Ronde_Community" title="Grand Ronde Community">Grand Ronde</a> Agency in Oregon, a distinction is made between <i>siwash</i> and <i>sawash</i>. The accent in the latter is on the second syllable, resembling the French original, and is used in Grand Ronde Jargon meaning "anything native or Indian"; by contrast, they consider <i>siwash</i> to be defamatory.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The Chinook Jargon term for a native woman is <i>klootchman</i>, an originally <a href="/wiki/Nuu-chah-nulth_language" title="Nuu-chah-nulth language">Nootka</a> word adopted in regional English to mean a native woman or, as in the Jargon, all women and also anything female. It originated as a compound of Nootka <i>łūts</i> 'female' with the English suffix <i>-man</i>. <i>Hyas klootchman tyee</i> means "queen", <i>klootchman cosho</i>, "sow"; and <i>klootchman tenas</i> or <i>tenas klootchman</i> means "girl" or "little girl". Generally <i>klootchman</i> in regional English simply means a native woman and has not acquired the derisive sense of <i>siwash</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Squaw" title="Squaw">squaw</a></i>. The short form <i>klootch</i>—encountered only in English-Chinook hybrid phrasings—is always derisive, especially in forms such as <i>blue-eyed klootch</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_America">Latin America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Latin America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, the preferred expression used by both the Government and the media is "Indigenous peoples" (<i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">pueblos indígenas</i> in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>).
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, the preferred expression for the population is also Indigenous peoples (<i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">pueblos indígenas</i> and <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">pueblos originarios</i> in <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>, and <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">povos indígenas</i> in <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>). In Spanish Latin America, "Indians" (<i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">indios</i>) is increasingly no longer used – today, to refer to indigenous people from a rural area, one would most likely say <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">campesino originario</i> or <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">indígena campesino</i> if they were to use language that is largely not seen as inaccurate, controversial, pejorative or offensive.
</p><p>In Brazil the most usual expression is by far <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">índio</i>, with <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">indígena</i> sounding a little more formal; the Portuguese demonym for the country of India is <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">indiano</i>. <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">Indios</i> is still in common use, including among people of Indigenous identity. In Mexico, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, and several other countries, these names are normally applied only to the ethnic groups that have maintained their identity and, to a some extent, their original way of life.
</p><p>Less common terms for Indigenous peoples of the Americas include <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">amerindio</i> (in Spanish) and <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">ameríndio</i> (in Portuguese).
</p><p>In most of Latin America there are also large segments of the population with mixed Indigenous and non-Indigenous ancestry, who are largely integrated into mainstream society, and by and large no longer identify themselves with their Indigenous ancestral groups unless they coexist with their ancestral Indigenous nation. Names for such groups include <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">mestizo</i>, <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">zambo</i> and <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">pardo</i> in Spanish, for people with European/Middle Eastern, Native and European/Middle Eastern and African admixed ancestry, respectively, besides <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">castizo</i> for people who are over three quarters European with some Native assimilation, and <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">caboclo</i> (current) or <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">mameluco</i> (dated), <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">cafuzo</i>, <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">juçara</i>, <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ainoko">ainoko</a></i>/<i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">ainoco</i>and <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">sarará</i> in Portuguese, for people of European and Native, Native and African, European/Middle Eastern, Native and African, East Asian (most particularly <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Brazilian" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese Brazilian">Japanese</a>), and European and the latter being mostly European/Middle Eastern and African (with fair hair and skin, but black facial features and/or hair texture) admixed ancestry, respectively, with the first three necessarily involving a degree of Indigenous ancestry. <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">Ainokos</i> and <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">sararás</i> might have some level of Native DNA assimilation. <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">Ainoko</i> is sometimes replaced by another Japanese term known as <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">hafu</i> or <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">eurasiano</i>, involving all Asians.
</p><p>In some Spanish-speaking countries, there are also <a href="/wiki/Ladino_people" title="Ladino people">Ladinos</a> who do not have significant European ancestry, but have adopted the culture of the dominant non-Indigenous population. In Brazil, however, assimilated Indigenous people are called <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">caboclos</i> (itself a subset of <a href="/wiki/Pardo" title="Pardo">pardos</a>, or brown people), the same term used for people of European and Amerindian mix who do not have at the same time a white-passing phenotype and a mainstream Brazilian cultural identity – which also means that <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">caboclos</i> are not necessarily <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text"><a href="/wiki/Mesti%C3%A7o" title="Mestiço">mestiços</a></i> (Portuguese for "mixed-race" in general).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International">International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: International">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id=".22Indigenous_peoples.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Indigenous_peoples&quot;">"Indigenous peoples"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: &quot;Indigenous peoples&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the late 20th century the term "Indigenous peoples" evolved into a political term that refers to ethnic groups with historical ties to groups that existed in a territory prior to <a href="/wiki/Colonization" title="Colonization">colonization</a> or formation of a <a href="/wiki/Nation_state" title="Nation state">nation state</a>. In the Americas, the term "Indigenous peoples of the Americas" was adopted, and the term is tailored to specific geographic or political regions, such as "<a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_Panama" title="Indigenous peoples of Panama">Indigenous peoples of Panama</a>".  "'Indigenous peoples' ... is a term that internationalizes the experiences, the issues and the struggles of some of the world's colonized peoples", writes <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Māori</a> educator <a href="/wiki/Linda_Tuhiwai_Smith" title="Linda Tuhiwai Smith">Linda Tuhiwai Smith</a>. "The final 's' in 'indigenous peoples' ... [is] a way of recognizing that there are real differences between different indigenous peoples."<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turtle_Islander">Turtle Islander</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Turtle Islander">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Another, less commonly used term is in reference to the continent: <a href="/wiki/Turtle_Island_(North_America)" title="Turtle Island (North America)">Turtle Island</a>. Though officially named <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, a number of histories from various Turtle Island countries make reference to the continent existing atop a turtle's back. Though not present across all nations and countries, this symbolism and icon has spread to become nearly pan-Indigenous. As Europeans, Asians and Africans have terms that allude to their home continents, "Turtle Islander"<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> is an attempt to do just that.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversial_terminology">Controversial terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Controversial terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Indian.2FInjun"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Indian/Injun">Indian/Injun</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Indian/Injun">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In some situations, the term "<a href="/wiki/Indian_princess_(Native_American)" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian princess (Native American)">Indian princess</a>" is considered demeaning to Native American women as the concept of a "princess" is not a part of Native culture; dressing up with fake Native regalia as in a <a href="/wiki/Halloween" title="Halloween">Halloween</a> "Indian Princess" costume is considered particularly offensive.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> However, this should not be confused with the more recent practice of tribes, <a href="/wiki/Powwow" class="mw-redirect" title="Powwow">powwow</a> organizations, colleges, and other indigenous groups choosing young women with leadership and talent to represent their groups as tribal "princesses."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>"Injun" is an originally 17th century mispronunciation of "Indian", generally considered offensive today, used to mock or impersonate Native Americans' or early settlers' supposed heavily accented English (e.g., "Honest Injun", "Injun time").<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> The word and related terms have been defined as derogatory by indigenous people and are not widely used.
</p>
<h3><span id="Redskin.2FRed_Indian"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Redskin/Red_Indian">Redskin/Red Indian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Redskin/Red Indian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Redskin" title="Redskin">Redskin</a></div>
<p>Some Europeans have historically called <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a> "Red Indians". The term was largely used in the 18th and 19th Centuries, partially based on the <a href="/wiki/Color_terminology_for_race" title="Color terminology for race">color metaphors for race</a> which colonists and settlers historically used in North America and Europe, and also to distinguish Native Americans from the <a href="/wiki/Indian_people" title="Indian people">Indian people</a> of <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>. 
</p><p>The term "Redskins" is now seen, by Native Americans in particular, as pejorative and often highly offensive,<sup id="cite_ref-odr_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-odr-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mwr_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mwr-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-American_Heritage_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-American_Heritage-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> as it is the term that was used for body parts used as "proof of kill" when Native Americans were hunted for bounty by colonists on the frontier.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> There is an American <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">National Football League</a> team named the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Redskins" title="Washington Redskins">Washington Redskins</a>, and "Redskin" is the name of the mascot at the <a href="/wiki/Red_Mesa_High_School" title="Red Mesa High School">Red Mesa High School</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Navajo_Reservation" class="mw-redirect" title="Navajo Reservation">Navajo Reservation</a> in <a href="/wiki/Teec_Nos_Pos" class="mw-redirect" title="Teec Nos Pos">Teec Nos Pos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Native Americans have been <a href="/wiki/Native_American_mascot_controversy" title="Native American mascot controversy">protesting the use of these names</a> by non-Natives since the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Congress_of_American_Indians" title="National Congress of American Indians">National Congress of American Indians</a> (NCAI) maintains that names like <a href="/wiki/Redskin" title="Redskin">Redskins</a> perpetuate negative stereotypes of Native American people, "Often citing a long held myth by non-Native people that 'Indian' mascots 'honor Native people', American sports businesses such as the <a href="/wiki/National_Football_League" title="National Football League">NFL</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Washington_Redskins" title="Washington Redskins">Washington 'Redskins'</a>... continue to profit from harmful stereotypes originated during a time when white superiority and segregation were commonplace."<sup id="cite_ref-NCAI.2013_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NCAI.2013-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "Red Indians" was also more specifically used by Europeans to refer to the <a href="/wiki/Beothuk" title="Beothuk">Beothuk</a>, a people living on Newfoundland, who used <a href="/wiki/Red_ochre" class="mw-redirect" title="Red ochre">red ochre</a> in spring to paint not only their bodies, but also their houses, canoes, weapons, household appliances and musical instruments.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Savage">Savage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Savage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Noble_savage" title="Noble savage">Noble savage</a> and <a href="/wiki/Barbarian" title="Barbarian">Barbarian</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">Anthropologists</a> once used <i>savage</i> as a blanket term to refer to indigenous peoples worldwide (for example, <a href="/wiki/Bronis%C5%82aw_Malinowski" title="Bronisław Malinowski">Bronisław Malinowski</a> titled his 1929 study <i>The Sexual Life of Savages</i>).  At the beginning of the nineteenth century, representatives of the relatively new United States government often used the term in official records when referring to Indian nations (e.g., Justice Baldwin's concurring opinion in <i><a href="/wiki/Cherokee_Nation_v._Georgia" title="Cherokee Nation v. Georgia">Cherokee Nation v. Georgia</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>). This was related to their association of non-Christian people as savages. Early anthropologist <a href="/wiki/Lewis_H._Morgan" title="Lewis H. Morgan">Lewis H. Morgan</a> posited in <i><a href="/wiki/Ancient_Society" title="Ancient Society">Ancient Society</a></i> (1877) a three-part <a href="/wiki/Sociocultural_evolution" title="Sociocultural evolution">evolution of societies</a> from, in his terms, savagery through barbarism to civilization.
European <a href="/wiki/Christians" title="Christians">Christians</a> once broadly used the word "heathens" to refer to Native Americans, a pejorative Christian term that refers to people who do not worship the Christian god.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Squaw">Squaw</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Native_American_name_controversy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Squaw">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Squaw" title="Squaw">Squaw</a></div>
<p>The English word "<a href="/wiki/Squaw" title="Squaw">squaw</a>", when used to refer to Indigenous women, is considered highly offensive, derogatory, <a href="/wiki/Misogyny" title="Misogyny">misogynist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Vowel-1_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vowel-1-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NMAI_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NMAI-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BNews1_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNews1-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AIM-SC_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIM-SC-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>  Although there has been some controversy on the topic, it is almost always grouped with other words that carry a colonial implication of <a href="/wiki/Exoticism" title="Exoticism">exotic</a> inferiority based on race, such as "negress" or "Jewess".<sup id="cite_ref-AICRJ_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AICRJ-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> There is a movement to remove the name "squaw" from geographic place names across the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-BNews1_61-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BNews1-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> There is a minority counter-movement among a small number of academics to "reclaim" what they claim is the possible original meaning of the word, as an <a href="/wiki/Ingroups_and_outgroups" title="Ingroups and outgroups">in-group term</a>, which could still be offensive if used outside of that <a href="/wiki/Speech_community" title="Speech community">speech community</a>. But even this usage only be relevant to the original, Algonquian-language <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">phonemes</a> of the word — the small parts that make up larger, historical forms — not the English form currently used as a slur. Any effort at "reclamation" would not apply to the much larger <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> community of women who are affected by this slur, as Algonquian-speakers only make up a small minority of those affected by it.<sup id="cite_ref-AIM-SC_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AIM-SC-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Goddard_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goddard-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
