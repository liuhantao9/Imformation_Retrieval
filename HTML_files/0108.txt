<p><i><b>The Rainbow Children</b></i> is the twenty-fourth <a href="/wiki/Studio_album" class="mw-redirect" title="Studio album">studio album</a> by American recording artist <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince</a>. It was released on November 20, 2001 by <a href="/wiki/NPG_Records" title="NPG Records">NPG Records</a> and Redline Entertainment. It was also released through Prince's website earlier in the year. It is the first album released outside of the <a href="/wiki/NPG_Music_Club" title="NPG Music Club">NPG Music Club</a> to be released under the name of Prince again, as he had reverted to his previous stage name from his symbolic moniker a year earlier.
</p><p>This <a href="/wiki/Concept_album" title="Concept album">concept album</a> illustrates common Prince themes of <a href="/wiki/Spirituality" title="Spirituality">spirituality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Human_sexuality" title="Human sexuality">human sexuality</a>, as well as love and <a href="/wiki/Racism" title="Racism">racism</a>, through the fictitious story of a <a href="/wiki/Social_movement" title="Social movement">social movement</a> toward a <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther_King,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Martin Luther King, Jr.">Martin Luther King, Jr.</a>-inspired utopian society. The album seems to allude to his recent conversion to the <a href="/wiki/Jehovah%27s_Witnesses" title="Jehovah&#39;s Witnesses">Jehovah's Witnesses</a> religion, but <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_religion#Atenism" title="Ancient Egyptian religion">Egyptian monotheism</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_Age" title="New Age">New Age</a> concepts such as the <a href="/wiki/Akashic_records" title="Akashic records">Akashic records</a> are used as <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphors</a> as well. <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">Jazzier</a> than any of his previous efforts, it was met with mixed reactions. Some fans saw the album as a musical and spiritual evolution for Prince.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Musically, <i>The Rainbow Children</i> marked a shift back towards a more "organic" sound for Prince. Unlike its predecessors, the album featured live drums, and made ample use of horns. Many songs were performed live during Prince's 2002 <a href="/wiki/One_Nite_Alone..._Tour" title="One Nite Alone... Tour">One Nite Alone... Tour</a>, which became an instant success with fans and critics alike.
</p><p><i>The Rainbow Children</i> was released through the <a href="/wiki/Independent_music" title="Independent music">independent</a> <a href="/wiki/Record_distributor" class="mw-redirect" title="Record distributor">distributor</a> Redline Entertainment, and was released with minimal promotion per Prince's wishes, as he wanted to focus more on the music and less on the sales factor of the release. It has sold 158,000 copies in US stores as of summer 2007, and an estimated 560,000 copies worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The album also had a dedicated promotional website that offered the tracks "She Loves Me 4 Me" and "Mellow" as free <a href="/wiki/MP3" title="MP3">MP3</a> downloads.
</p><p>The album cover features Cbabi Bayoc's "The Reine Keis Quintet". Prince favored the painting of a women's band, as he was backed by an all-female ensemble.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
