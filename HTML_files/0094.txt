<p><b>Sir Basil Henry Liddell Hart</b> (31 October 1895 – 29 January 1970), commonly known throughout most of his career as <b>Captain B. H. Liddell Hart</b>, was a British soldier, <a href="/wiki/Military_historian" class="mw-redirect" title="Military historian">military historian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Military_theorist" class="mw-redirect" title="Military theorist">military theorist</a>.  In the 1920s and later he wrote a series of military histories that proved influential among strategists. He argued that frontal assault was a strategy that was bound to fail at great cost in lives, as happened in 1914–1918. He instead recommended the "<a href="/wiki/Indirect_approach" title="Indirect approach">indirect approach</a>" and reliance on fast-moving armoured formations.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._H._Liddell_Hart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The son of a Methodist minister, Liddell Hart received his formal academic education at <a href="/wiki/St_Paul%27s_School_(London)" class="mw-redirect" title="St Paul&#39;s School (London)">St Paul's School</a> in London and at <a href="/wiki/Corpus_Christi_College,_Cambridge" title="Corpus Christi College, Cambridge">Corpus Christi College, Cambridge</a> (where he was a student of <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_G._Butler" title="Geoffrey G. Butler">Geoffrey Butler</a>). His mother's side of the family, the Liddells, came from <a href="/wiki/Liddesdale" title="Liddesdale">Liddesdale</a>, on the Scottish side of the border with England, and were associated with the <a href="/wiki/London_and_South_Western_Railway" title="London and South Western Railway">London and South Western Railway</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The Harts were farmers from Gloucestershire and Herefordshire.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> As a child Hart was fascinated by aviation.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_I">World War I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._H._Liddell_Hart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: World War I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the outbreak of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> in August 1914 Liddell Hart volunteered for the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a>, where he became an officer in the <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Own_Yorkshire_Light_Infantry" title="King&#39;s Own Yorkshire Light Infantry">King's Own Yorkshire Light Infantry</a> and served with the regiment on the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_I)" title="Western Front (World War I)">Western Front</a>. Liddell Hart's front line experience was relatively brief, confined to two short spells in the autumn and winter of 1915, being sent home from the front after suffering concussive injuries from a shell burst. He was promoted to the rank of captain. He returned to the front for a third time in 1916, in time to participate in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Somme" title="Battle of the Somme">Battle of the Somme</a>. He was hit three times without serious injury before being badly gassed and sent out of the line on 19 July 1916.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> His battalion was nearly wiped out on the <a href="/wiki/First_day_on_the_Somme" title="First day on the Somme">first day of the offensive</a> on 1 July, a part of the 60,000 casualties suffered in the heaviest single day's loss in British history. The experiences he suffered on the Western Front profoundly affected him for the rest of his life.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Transferred to be Adjutant to Volunteer units in <a href="/wiki/Stroud" title="Stroud">Stroud</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, he was in charge of training new units.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> During this time he wrote several booklets on infantry drill and training, which came to the attention of <a href="/wiki/General_(United_Kingdom)" title="General (United Kingdom)">General</a> <a href="/wiki/Ivor_Maxse" title="Ivor Maxse">Sir Ivor Maxse</a>, commander of the <a href="/wiki/18th_(Eastern)_Division" title="18th (Eastern) Division">18th (Eastern) Division</a>. After the war he transferred to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Army_Educational_Corps" title="Royal Army Educational Corps">Royal Army Educational Corps</a>, where he prepared a new edition of the Infantry Training Manual. In this manual Liddell Hart strove to instil the lessons of 1918, and carried on a correspondence with Maxse, a commanding officer during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Hamel" title="Battle of Hamel">Battle of Hamel</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Amiens_(1918)" title="Battle of Amiens (1918)">Battle of Amiens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In April 1918 Liddell Hart married Jessie Stone, the daughter of J. J. Stone – who had been his assistant adjutant at <a href="/wiki/Stroud" title="Stroud">Stroud</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> – and their son <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Liddell_Hart" title="Adrian Liddell Hart">Adrian</a> was born in 1922.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Journalist_and_military_historian">Journalist and military historian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._H._Liddell_Hart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Journalist and military historian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Liddell Hart was placed on <a href="/wiki/Half-pay" title="Half-pay">half-pay</a> from 1924.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> He later retired from the Army in 1927. Two mild <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">heart attacks</a> in 1921 and 1922, probably the long-term effects of his gassing, precluded his further advancement in the downsized post-war army. He spent the rest of his career as a theorist and writer. In 1924 he became a <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">lawn tennis</a> correspondent and assistant military correspondent for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Morning_Post" title="The Morning Post">The Morning Post</a></i> covering <a href="/wiki/Wimbledon_Championships" class="mw-redirect" title="Wimbledon Championships">Wimbledon</a> and in 1926 publishing a collection of his tennis writings as <i>The Lawn Tennis Masters Unveiled</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> He worked as the military correspondent of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">The Daily Telegraph</a></i> from 1925 to 1935, and of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> from 1935 to 1939.
</p><p>In the mid to late twenties Liddell Hart wrote a series of histories of major military figures, through which he advanced his ideas that the frontal assault was a strategy that was bound to fail at great cost in lives. He argued that the tremendous losses Britain suffered in the Great War were due to her commanding officers not appreciating this fact of history. He believed the British decision in 1914 to directly intervene on the Continent with a great army was a mistake. He claimed that historically "the British way in warfare" was to leave Continental land battles to her allies, intervening only through naval power, with the army fighting the enemy away from its principal front in a "limited liability".<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In his early writings on mechanised warfare Liddell Hart had proposed that infantry be carried along with the fast-moving armoured formations. He described them as "tank marines" like the soldiers the Royal Navy carried with their ships. He proposed they be carried along in their own tracked vehicles and dismount to help take better-defended positions that otherwise would hold up the armoured units. This contrasted with <a href="/wiki/J.F.C._Fuller" class="mw-redirect" title="J.F.C. Fuller">J.F.C. Fuller</a>'s ideas of a tank army, which put heavy emphasis on massed armoured formations. Liddell Hart foresaw the need for a combined arms force with mobile infantry and artillery, which was similar but not identical to the make-up of the panzer divisions that <a href="/wiki/Heinz_Guderian" title="Heinz Guderian">Guderian</a> developed in Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to Liddell Hart's memoirs, in a series of articles for <i>The Times</i> from November 1935 to November 1936, he had argued that Britain's role in the next European war could be entrusted to the air force. He further theorised that Britain could pursue to defeat her enemies while avoiding the high casualties and limited influence that Britain could impart by placing a large conscript army on the Continent.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  These ideas influenced Chamberlain, then Chancellor, who argued in discussions of the Defence Policy and Requirements Committee for a strong air force rather than a large army that would fight on continental Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As Prime Minister in 1937, Chamberlain placed Liddell Hart in a position of influence behind British grand strategy of the late thirties.<sup id="cite_ref-Barnett,_p._502_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barnett,_p._502-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> In May he prepared schemes for the reorganisation of the British Army for defence of the <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British Empire</a> and delivered them to Sir <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Inskip" class="mw-redirect" title="Thomas Inskip">Thomas Inskip</a>, the Minister for the Co-Ordination of Defence. In June he gained an introduction to the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_War" title="Secretary of State for War">Secretary of State for War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Hore-Belisha" title="Leslie Hore-Belisha">Leslie Hore-Belisha</a>. Through July 1938 the two had an unofficial, close advisory relationship. Liddell Hart provided Hore-Belisha with ideas that he would argue for in Cabinet or committees.<sup id="cite_ref-Barnett,_p._502_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barnett,_p._502-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> On 20 October 1937, Chamberlain wrote to Hore-Belisha: "I have been reading in <i>Europe in Arms</i> by Liddell Hart. If you have not already done so you might find it interesting to glance at this, especially the chapter on the “Role of the British Army”". Hore-Belisha wrote in reply: "I immediately read the “Role of the British Army” in Liddell Hart's book. I am impressed by his general theories".<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With the German <a href="/wiki/Invasion_of_Poland" title="Invasion of Poland">invasion of Poland</a> in September 1939 the War Cabinet reversed the Chamberlain policy advanced by Liddell Hart. With Europe on the brink of war and Germany threatening an invasion of Poland, the cabinet chose instead to advocate a British and Imperial army of 55 divisions, for intervention on the Continent to come to the aid of Poland, Norway and France.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war">Post-war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._H._Liddell_Hart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Post-war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the war, Liddell Hart was responsible for extensive interviews and debriefs for several high-ranking German generals, who were held by the Allies as prisoners-of-war. Liddell Hart provided commentary on their outlook. The work was published as <i>The Other Side of the Hill</i> (UK Edition, 1948) and <i>The German Generals Talk</i> (condensed US Edition, 1948).
</p><p>A few years later Liddell Hart had the opportunity to review the notes that <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Rommel" title="Erwin Rommel">Erwin Rommel</a> had kept during the war. Rommel had kept these notes with the intention of writing of his experiences after the war; the Rommel family had previously published these notes in German as <i>War without Hate</i> in 1950. Some of the notes had been destroyed by Rommel and the rest (including Rommel's letters to his wife) had been confiscated by the American authorities. With Liddell Hart's help, these were later returned to Rommel's widow. Liddell Hart then edited and condensed the book and helped integrate the new material. The writings, along with notes and commentary by former general <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Bayerlein" title="Fritz Bayerlein">Fritz Bayerlein</a> and Liddell Hart were published in 1953 as <i>The Rommel Papers</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMajor2008_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMajor2008-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> (See below for Liddell Hart's role in the Rommel myth.)
</p><p>In 1954, Liddell Hart published his most influential work, <i>Strategy</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;n 1&#93;</a></sup> This was followed by a second expanded edition in 1967. It was a book largely devoted to a historical study of the indirect approach, and in what ways various battles and campaigns could be analyzed using this concept. Still relevant at the turn of the century, it was a factor in the development of the British <a href="/wiki/Maneuver_warfare" title="Maneuver warfare">maneuver warfare</a> doctrine.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Queen made Liddell Hart a <a href="/wiki/Knight_Bachelor" title="Knight Bachelor">Knight Bachelor</a> in the <a href="/wiki/New_Year_Honours" title="New Year Honours">New Year Honours</a> of 1966.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> As of 2009, Liddell Hart's personal papers and library form the central collection in the <a href="/wiki/Liddell_Hart_Centre_for_Military_Archives" title="Liddell Hart Centre for Military Archives">Liddell Hart Centre for Military Archives</a> at <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_London" title="King&#39;s College London">King's College London</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Key_ideas_and_observations">Key ideas and observations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._H._Liddell_Hart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Key ideas and observations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tbody><tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 20px; font-family: &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“
</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">[N]ot of one period but of its whole course, points to the fact that all decisive campaigns the dislocation of the enemy’s psychological and physical balance has been the vital prelude to his overthrow.”
</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 20px; font-family: &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”
</td></tr>
<tr>
<td colspan="3" class="cquotecite" style="border: none; padding-right: 4%; font-size: smaller; line-height:normal; text-align: right;"><cite>—&#8202;B. H. Liddell Hart<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup></cite>
</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Liddell Hart was an advocate of the notion that it was easier to succeed in war when an indirect approach was used.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> To attack where the opponent expects, as Liddell Hart explained, made the task of winning harder. "To move along the line of natural expectation consolidates the opponent's balance and thus increases his resisting power." This is in contrast to an indirect approach, in which physical or psychological surprise is a component: "The indirectness is usually physical and always psychological. In strategy, the longest way round is often the shortest way home."
</p><p>Liddell Hart would illustrate the notion with historical examples. For example, Liddell Hart considered the battle of <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Leuctra" title="Battle of Leuctra">Leuctra</a>, won by the general Epaminondas, an example of an indirect approach.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Rather than weighting his army on the right wing, as was standard at the time, <a href="/wiki/Epaminondas" title="Epaminondas">Epaminondas</a> weighted his left wing, held back his right wing, and routed the Spartan army. A more modern example would be the landings of the Allies at Normandy on June 6, 1944, as the Germans were expecting a landing in the vicinity of Pas de Calais.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> By contrast, an example of a direct attack, in Liddell Hart's eyes, was the attack by Union forces at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Fredericksburg" title="Battle of Fredericksburg">battle of Fredericksburg</a> in 1862.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Even more impressive in Liddell Hart's eyes was the further campaign by Epaminondas, <a href="/wiki/Epaminondas#First_Invasion_of_the_Peloponnese_(370_BC)" title="Epaminondas">his invasion of the Peloponnese</a> in which in winter and in separate columns, invaded Spartan controlled territory.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The general was unable to draw the Spartans into combat, and so settled on freeing helots. He then built two city states as a break against Spartan power, and thus the campaign was successful. By breaking the Spartan economic base, he won a campaign without ever fighting a battle.
</p><p>When analyzing the campaigns of <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a>, Liddell Hart noted that the general's approaches were less subtle and more brute force as his forces became larger, and that when his forces were lesser, he was more apt to be creative in his battles.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Constant victory it seems, dulled his skills as a soldier.
</p><p>According to Reid, Liddell Hart's indirect approach has seven key themes.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Summarized, they are:
</p>
<ul><li>The dislocation of the enemy’s balance should be the prelude to defeat, not utter destruction.</li>
<li>Negotiate an end to unprofitable wars.</li>
<li>The methods of the indirect approach are better suited to a democracy.</li>
<li>Military power relies on economic endurance. Defeating an enemy by beating him economically incurs no risk.</li>
<li>Implicitly, war is an activity between states.</li>
<li>Liddell Hart's notion of "rational pacifism".<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Victory often emerges as the result of an enemy defeating itself.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._H._Liddell_Hart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>At the height of his popularity, <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> called Liddell Hart "the Captain who teaches Generals" and was using his writings to attack the Eisenhower administration, which he said was too dependent on nuclear arms.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> His influence extended to armies outside England and the US as well. Baumgarten says of Liddell Hart's influence in the Australian Army: "The indirect approach was also one of the key influences on the development of <a href="/wiki/Maneuver_warfare" title="Maneuver warfare">manoeuvre theory</a>, a dominant element in Army thinking throughout the 1990s."<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>. Retired Pakistani General Shaafat Shah called Liddell Hart's book <i>Strategy: the Indirect Approach</i> "A seminal work of military history and theory".<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>  In the book <i>Science, Strategy and War</i>, Netherlander Frans Osinga mentions, while speaking of <a href="/wiki/John_Boyd_(military_strategist)" title="John Boyd (military strategist)">John Boyd</a>, "In his recently published study of modern strategic theory, Colin Gray ranked Boyd among the outstanding general theorists of strategy of the 20th century, along with the likes of <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Brodie_(military_strategist)" title="Bernard Brodie (military strategist)">Bernard Brodie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Luttwak" title="Edward Luttwak">Edward Luttwak</a>, Basil Liddell Hart and <a href="/wiki/J.C._Wylie" title="J.C. Wylie">John Wylie</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> His biographer, Alex Danchev, noted that his books were still being translated all around the world, some of them seventy years after they had been written.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Controversies">Controversies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._H._Liddell_Hart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Controversies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_Panzerwaffe">Influence on Panzerwaffe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._H._Liddell_Hart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Influence on Panzerwaffe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Following the Second World War Liddell Hart pointed out that the German <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a> adopted theories developed from those of <a href="/wiki/J._F._C._Fuller" title="J. F. C. Fuller">J. F. C. Fuller</a> and from his own, and that it used them against the Allies in <a href="/wiki/Blitzkrieg" title="Blitzkrieg">Blitzkrieg</a> warfare.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Some scholars, such as the political scientist <a href="/wiki/John_Mearsheimer" title="John Mearsheimer">John Mearsheimer</a>, have questioned the extent of the influence which the British officers, and in particular Liddell Hart, had in the development of the method of war practised by the <a href="/wiki/Panzerwaffe" title="Panzerwaffe">Panzerwaffe</a> in 1939–1941. During the post-war debriefs of the former Wehrmacht generals, Liddell Hart attempted to tease out his influence on their war practices. Following these interviews, many of the generals said that Liddell Hart had been an influence on their strategies, something that had not been claimed previously nor has any contemporary, pre-war, documentation been found to support their assertions. Liddell Hart thus put "words in the mouths of German Generals" with the aim, according to Mearsheimer, to "resurrect a lost reputation".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELuvaas1990_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELuvaas1990-51">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Shimon Naveh, the founder and former head of the <a href="/wiki/Israel_Defense_Forces" title="Israel Defense Forces">Israel Defense Forces</a>' Operational Theory Research Institute, stated that after World War II Liddell Hart "created" the idea of Blitzkrieg as a military doctrine: "It was the opposite of a doctrine. Blitzkrieg consisted of an avalanche of actions that were sorted out less by design and more by success."<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Naveh stated that, 
</p>
<blockquote><p>by manipulation and contrivance, Liddell Hart distorted the actual circumstances of the Blitzkrieg formation and obscured its origins. Through his indoctrinated idealization of an ostentatious concept he reinforced the myth of Blitzkrieg. By imposing, retrospectively, his own perceptions of mobile warfare upon the shallow concept of Blitzkrieg, he created a theoretical imbroglio that has taken 40 years to unravel.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Naveh stated that in his letters to German generals <a href="/wiki/Erich_von_Manstein" title="Erich von Manstein">Erich von Manstein</a> and Guderian, as well as to relatives and associates of Rommel,  Liddell Hart "imposed his own fabricated version of Blitzkrieg on the latter and compelled him to proclaim it as original formula".<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Naveh pointed out that the edition of Guderian's memoirs published in Germany differed from the one published in the United Kingdom. Guderian neglected to mention the influence of the English theorists such as Fuller and Liddell Hart in the German-language versions. One example of the influence of these men on Guderian was the report on the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cambrai_(1917)" title="Battle of Cambrai (1917)">Battle of Cambrai</a> published by Fuller in 1920, who at the time served as a staff officer at the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Tank_Corps" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Tank Corps">Royal Tank Corps</a>. Liddell Hart alleged that his findings and theories on armoured warfare were read and later taken in by Guderian, which thus helped to formulate the basis of operations that would become known as Blitzkrieg warfare. These tactics involved deep penetration of the armoured formations supported behind enemy lines by bomb-carrying aircraft.  Dive bombers were the principal agents of delivery of high explosives in support of the forward units.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Though the German version of the Guderian memoirs mentions Liddell Hart, it did not ascribe to him his role in developing the theories behind armoured warfare. An explanation for the difference between the two translations can be found in the correspondence between the two men. In one letter to Guderian, Liddell Hart reminded the German general that he should provide him the credit he was due, offering "You might care to insert a remark that I emphasise the use of armoured forces for long-range operations against the opposing Army's communications, and also the proposed type of armoured division combining Panzer and Panzer-infantry units – and that these points particularly impressed you."<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Richard M.Swain comments that while some arguments against Liddell Hart's thinking are deserved, Liddell Hart the man himself was not a knave and Mearsheimer's attempt of character assassination is unwarranted.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Jay Luvass comments that Liddell Hart and Fuller did actually anticipate the role of the armoured forces in blitzkrieg. Luvass opines that Liddell Hart did overestimate (in a sincere way) his influence on German generals, but the fact that many military leaders in Germany and other countries (including generals lịke  <a href="/wiki/Yigal_Allon" title="Yigal Allon">Yigal  Allon</a>  and  <a href="/wiki/Andre_Beaufre" class="mw-redirect" title="Andre Beaufre">Andre  Beaufre</a>) knew about his theories and considered his opinions as worth thinking about is true. According to Luvass, von Mellenthin recounted that Rommel mentioned Liddell Hart many times and had a good opinion about him - although, in Luvass's opinion, this would not make him a pupil. Luvass also sees Liddell Hart as a scholar who needed public recognition and influence, but also a naturally generous person whose efforts in building connection to other people should not be assigned motives without evidence.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> Joseph Forbes dismisses the claim that Liddell Hart,  Guderian and Rommel's friends and relatives were in a conspiracy to misrepresent Liddell Hart's influence as baseless insinuations, considering that: Liddell Hart's chapter on Guderian quotes Guderian as having faith in the theories of Hobart and not of Liddell Hart; the fact Desmond Young once recommended Liddell Hart to Manfred Rommel as a person who might help to publish his father's memoirs should not be used as proof that there was a conspiracy to give undue recognition to Liddell Hart; the whole book <i>The German Generals talk</i> contains one statement about Liddell Hart's influence (According to Forbes, Mearsheimer relies less on the actual text  than on Frank Mahin's review to make the claim that Hart fills the book with fabricated comments by Germans to exaggerate his role).<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>    
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_in_Rommel_myth">Role in Rommel myth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._H._Liddell_Hart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Role in Rommel myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Rommel_myth" title="Rommel myth">Rommel myth</a></div>
<p>Liddell Hart was instrumental in the creation of the "<a href="/wiki/Rommel_myth" title="Rommel myth">Rommel myth</a>", a view that the German field marshal <a href="/wiki/Erwin_Rommel" title="Erwin Rommel">Erwin Rommel</a> was an apolitical, brilliant commander and a victim of the <a href="/wiki/Third_Reich" class="mw-redirect" title="Third Reich">Third Reich</a> due to his (now disputed) participation in the <a href="/wiki/20_July_plot" title="20 July plot">20 July plot</a> against <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>. The myth was created with Rommel's participation as a component of <a href="/wiki/Nazi_propaganda" class="mw-redirect" title="Nazi propaganda">Nazi propaganda</a> to praise the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a> and instil optimism in the German public. Starting in 1941, it was picked up and disseminated in the West by the British press as they sought to explain its continued inability to defeat the Axis forces in North Africa. Following the war, the Western Allies, and particularly the British, depicted Rommel as the "good German" and "our friend Rommel". His reputation for conducting a clean war was used in the interest of the <a href="/wiki/Wiederbewaffnung" title="Wiederbewaffnung">West German rearmament</a> and reconciliation between the former enemies – Britain and the United States on one side and the new Federal Republic on the other.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESearle20148-27_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESearle20148-27-60">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECaddick-Adams2012471-478_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECaddick-Adams2012471-478-61">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMajor2008520-535_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMajor2008520-535-62">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the outbreak of the Korean War in 1950, it became clear to the Americans and the British that a German army would have to be revived to help face off against the Soviet Union. Many former German officers were convinced, however, that no future German army would be possible without the rehabilitation of the <a href="/wiki/Wehrmacht" title="Wehrmacht">Wehrmacht</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESmelserDavies200872–73_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESmelserDavies200872–73-63">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Thus, in the atmosphere of the <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a>, Rommel's former enemies, especially the British, played a key role in the manufacture and propagation of the myth.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECaddick-Adams2012471–472_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECaddick-Adams2012471–472-64">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> The German rearmament was highly dependent on the image boosting that the Wehrmacht needed. Liddell Hart, an early proponent of these two interconnected initiatives, provided the first widely available source on Rommel in his 1948 book on Hitler's generals. He devoted a chapter to Rommel, portraying him as an outsider to the Nazi regime. Additions to the chapter published in 1951 concluded with laudatory comments about Rommel's "gifts and performance" that "qualified him for a place in the role of the 'Great Captains' of history".<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESearle20148,_27_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESearle20148,_27-65">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>1953 saw the publication of Rommel's writings of the war period as <i>The Rommel Papers</i>, edited by Liddell Hart, the former Wehrmacht officer <a href="/wiki/Fritz_Bayerlein" title="Fritz Bayerlein">Fritz Bayerlein</a>, and Rommel's widow and son, with an introduction by Liddell Hart. The historian <a href="/wiki/Mark_Connelly" title="Mark Connelly">Mark Connelly</a> argues that <i>The Rommel Papers</i> was one of the two foundational works that lead to a "Rommel renaissance", the other being Desmond Young's biography <i><a href="/wiki/Rommel:_The_Desert_Fox" title="Rommel: The Desert Fox">Rommel: The Desert Fox</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEConnelly2014163-163_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConnelly2014163-163-66">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;n 2&#93;</a></sup> The book contributed to the perception of Rommel as a brilliant commander; in an introduction, Liddell Hart drew comparisons between Rommel and <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_of_Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Lawrence of Arabia">Lawrence of Arabia</a>, "two masters of desert warfare", according to Liddell Hart.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMajor2008526_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMajor2008526-68">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Liddell Hart's work on the book was also self-serving: he had coaxed Rommel's widow into adding material that suggested that Rommel was influenced by Liddell Hart's theories on mechanised warfare, making Rommel his "pupil" and giving Liddell Hart credit for Rommel's dramatic successes in 1940.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMearsheimer1988199–200_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMearsheimer1988199–200-69">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> (The controversy was described by the political scientist <a href="/wiki/John_Mearsheimer" title="John Mearsheimer">John Mearsheimer</a> in his work <i>The Weight of History</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMearsheimer1988199–200_69-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMearsheimer1988199–200-69">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> A review of Mearsheimer's work, published by the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Studies_Institute" title="Strategic Studies Institute">Strategic Studies Institute</a>, points out that Mearsheimer "correctly takes 'The Captain' [Liddell Hart] to task for [...] manipulating history".)<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELuvaas1990_51-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELuvaas1990-51">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to Connelly, Young and Liddell Hart laid the foundation for the Anglo-American myth, which consisted of three themes: Rommel's ambivalence towards Nazism; his military genius; and the emphasis of the chivalrous nature of the fighting in North Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEConnelly2014163-163_66-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConnelly2014163-163-66">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Their works lent support to the image of the "<a href="/wiki/Clean_Wehrmacht" class="mw-redirect" title="Clean Wehrmacht">clean Wehrmacht</a>" and were generally not questioned, since they came from British authors, rather than German revisionists.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECaddick-Adams2012483_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECaddick-Adams2012483-70">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;n 3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="MI5_controversy">MI5 controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=B._H._Liddell_Hart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: MI5 controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 4 September 2006, <a href="/wiki/MI5" title="MI5">MI5</a> files were released which showed that in early 1944 MI5 had suspicions that plans for the <a href="/wiki/D-Day" class="mw-redirect" title="D-Day">D-Day</a> invasion had been leaked. Liddell Hart had prepared a treatise titled <i>Some Reflections on the Problems of Invading the Continent</i> which he circulated amongst political and military figures. It is possible that in his treatise Liddell Hart had correctly deduced a number of aspects of the upcoming Allied invasion, including the location of the landings. MI5 suspected that Liddell Hart had received plans of the invasion from General <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Alfred_Pile" title="Frederick Alfred Pile">Sir Alfred "Tim" Pile</a> who was in command of Britain's anti-aircraft defences. MI5 placed him under surveillance, intercepting his telephone calls and letters. The investigation showed no suggestion that Liddell Hart was involved in any subversive activity. No case was ever brought against Pile. Liddell Hart stated his work was merely speculative. It would appear that Liddell Hart had simply perceived the same problems and arrived at similar conclusions as the Allied general staff.<sup id="cite_ref-Files_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Files-73">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
