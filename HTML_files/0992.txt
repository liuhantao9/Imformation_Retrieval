	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Operation Aphrodite</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>Aphrodite</b> and <b>Anvil</b> were the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> <a href="/wiki/Code_name" title="Code name">code names</a> of <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army_Air_Forces" title="United States Army Air Forces">United States Army Air Forces</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> operations to use <a href="/wiki/B-17_Flying_Fortress_variants" class="mw-redirect" title="B-17 Flying Fortress variants">B-17</a> and <a href="/wiki/PB4Y" class="mw-redirect" title="PB4Y">PB4Y</a> bombers as <a href="/wiki/Precision-guided_munition" title="Precision-guided munition">precision-guided munitions</a> against bunkers and other hardened/reinforced enemy facilities, such as those targeted during <a href="/wiki/Operation_Crossbow" title="Operation Crossbow">Operation Crossbow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The plan called for B-17 aircraft that had been taken out of operational service (various nicknames existed such as "robot", "baby", "drone" or "weary Willy"<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>) to be loaded to capacity with <a href="/wiki/Explosive_material" class="mw-redirect" title="Explosive material">explosives</a>, and flown by radio control into bomb-resistant fortifications such as German <a href="/wiki/U-boat" title="U-boat">U-boat</a> pens and <a href="/wiki/V-weapon" class="mw-redirect" title="V-weapon">V-weapon</a> sites.
</p><p>It was hoped that it would match the British success with <a href="/wiki/Tallboy_bomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Tallboy bomb">Tallboy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grand_Slam_bomb" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Slam bomb">Grand Slam</a> ground penetration bombs but the project was dangerous, expensive and unsuccessful. Of 14 missions flown, none resulted in the successful destruction of a target. Many aircraft lost control and crashed or were shot down by flak, and many pilots were killed. However, a handful of aircraft scored near misses. One notable pilot death was that of Lt&#160;<a href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Kennedy,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph P. Kennedy, Jr.">Joseph P. Kennedy, Jr.</a>, USNR, the elder brother of future US President <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a>.
</p><p>The program effectively ceased on January 27, 1945 when General <a href="/wiki/Carl_Andrew_Spaatz" class="mw-redirect" title="Carl Andrew Spaatz">Spaatz</a> sent an urgent message to Doolittle: "Aphrodite babies must not be launched against the enemy until further orders".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposal">Proposal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Aphrodite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Proposal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By late 1943, General <a href="/wiki/Henry_H._Arnold" title="Henry H. Arnold">Henry H. Arnold</a> had directed Brigadier General <a href="/wiki/Grandison_Gardner" title="Grandison Gardner">Grandison Gardner</a>'s electronic engineers at <a href="/wiki/Eglin_Field" class="mw-redirect" title="Eglin Field">Eglin Field</a>, Florida, to outfit war-weary bombers with automatic pilots so that they could be remotely controlled.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The plan was first proposed to Major General <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Doolittle" title="Jimmy Doolittle">Jimmy Doolittle</a> some time in 1944. Doolittle approved the plan for Operation Aphrodite on June 26, and assigned the <a href="/wiki/3d_Air_Division" class="mw-redirect" title="3d Air Division">3rd Bombardment Division</a> with preparing and flying the drone aircraft, which was to be designated BQ-7.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In the U.S. Navy's similar project, Operation Anvil, the drone was designated BQ-8.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Final assignment of responsibility was given to the <a href="/wiki/562d_Flying_Training_Squadron" class="mw-redirect" title="562d Flying Training Squadron">562nd Bomb Squadron</a> at <a href="/wiki/RAF_Honington" title="RAF Honington">RAF Honington</a> in <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a>. Similarly, on July 6, 1944, the U.S. Navy Special Attack Unit (SAU-1) was formed under ComAirLant, with Commander James A. Smith, Officer in Charge, for transfer without delay to Commander Fleet Air Wing 7 in Europe to attack German V-1 and <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket_facilities_of_World_War_II" title="V-2 rocket facilities of World War II">V-2 sites</a> with <a href="/wiki/B-24_Liberator#U.S._Navy_nomenclature_and_sub-variants" class="mw-redirect" title="B-24 Liberator">PB4Y-1s</a> converted to assault drones.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Procedure">Procedure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Operation_Aphrodite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Procedure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:B-17.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/B-17.jpg/260px-B-17.jpg" decoding="async" width="260" height="199" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/B-17.jpg/390px-B-17.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/B-17.jpg/520px-B-17.jpg 2x" data-file-width="860" data-file-height="659" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:B-17.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>After completing 80 <a href="/wiki/91st_Bomb_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="91st Bomb Group">323rd BS</a> missions, Aphrodite B-17F (<i>The Careful Virgin</i>) was used against Mimoyecques but impacted short of target by controller error.</div></div></div>
<p>Old <a href="/wiki/Boeing_B-17_Flying_Fortress" title="Boeing B-17 Flying Fortress">Boeing B-17 Flying Fortress</a> bombers were stripped of all normal combat armament and all other non-essential gear (armor, guns, bomb racks, <a href="/wiki/Transceiver" title="Transceiver">transceiver</a>, seats, etc.), relieving about 12,000&#160;lb (5,400&#160;kg) of weight. To allow easier exit when the pilot and co-pilot were to parachute out, the <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_canopy" title="Aircraft canopy">canopy</a>  was removed.  <a href="/wiki/Azon" title="Azon">Azon</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Radio_control" title="Radio control">radio remote-control</a> equipment was added, with two <a href="/wiki/Television_camera" class="mw-redirect" title="Television camera">television cameras</a> fitted in the cockpit to allow a view of both the ground and the main instrumentation panel to be transmitted back to an accompanying CQ-17 'mothership'.
</p><p>The drone was loaded with explosives weighing more than twice that of a B-17's normal bomb payload. The British <a href="/wiki/Torpex" title="Torpex">Torpex</a> used for the purpose was itself 50% more powerful than TNT.
</p><p>A relatively remote location in <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a>, <a href="/wiki/RAF_Fersfield" title="RAF Fersfield">RAF Fersfield</a>, was the launch site. Initially, <a href="/wiki/RAF_Woodbridge" title="RAF Woodbridge">RAF Woodbridge</a> had been selected for its long runway, but the possibility of a damaged aircraft that diverted to Woodbridge for landings colliding with a loaded drone caused concerns. The remote control system was insufficient for safe <a href="/wiki/Takeoff" title="Takeoff">takeoff</a>, so each drone was taken aloft by a volunteer pilot and a volunteer flight engineer to an altitude of 2,000&#160;ft (600 m) for transfer of control to the CQ-17 operators. After successful turnover of control of the drone, the two-man crew would arm the payload and parachute out of the <a href="/wiki/Cockpit" title="Cockpit">cockpit</a>. The 'mothership' would then direct the missile to the target.
</p><p>
When the training program was complete, the 562nd Squadron had ten drones and four "motherships".</p><div style="clear:left;"></div>
