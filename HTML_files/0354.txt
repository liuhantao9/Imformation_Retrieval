	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">World Heritage Site</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">place listed by the UNESCO as of special cultural or natural significance</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:World_Heritage_Logo_global.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/World_Heritage_Logo_global.svg/220px-World_Heritage_Logo_global.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/World_Heritage_Logo_global.svg/330px-World_Heritage_Logo_global.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/World_Heritage_Logo_global.svg/440px-World_Heritage_Logo_global.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="160" data-file-height="160" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:World_Heritage_Logo_global.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Logo" title="Logo">Logo</a> of the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization (UNESCO)</a> <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Committee" title="World Heritage Committee">World Heritage Committee</a></div></div></div>
<p>A <b>World Heritage Site</b> is a landmark or area which is chosen by the <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organisation  (UNESCO)</a> as having cultural, historical, scientific or other form of significance, and is legally protected by international treaties. The sites are judged important to the collective interests of humanity.
</p><p>To be selected, a World Heritage Site must be an already-classified landmark, unique in some respect as a geographically and historically identifiable place having special cultural or physical significance (such as an ancient ruin or  historical structure, building, city, complex, desert, forest, island, lake, monument, mountain, or wilderness area<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>). It may signify a remarkable accomplishment of humanity, and serve as evidence of our intellectual history on the planet.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The sites are intended for practical conservation for posterity, which otherwise would be subject to risk from human or animal trespassing, unmonitored/uncontrolled/unrestricted access, or threat from local administrative negligence. Sites are demarcated by UNESCO as protected zones.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lists_of_World_Heritage_Sites" title="Lists of World Heritage Sites">The list</a> is maintained by the international World Heritage Program administered by the UNESCO <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Committee" title="World Heritage Committee">World Heritage Committee</a>, composed of 21 "states parties" that are elected by their General Assembly.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The programme catalogues, names, and conserves sites of outstanding <a href="/wiki/Cultural_heritage" title="Cultural heritage">cultural</a> or <a href="/wiki/Natural_heritage" title="Natural heritage">natural</a> importance to the common culture and heritage of <a href="/wiki/World_population" title="World population">humanity</a>. Under certain conditions, listed sites can obtain funds from the World Heritage Fund. The program began with the <i>Convention Concerning the Protection of the World's Cultural and Natural Heritage</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> which was adopted by the General Conference of UNESCO on 16 November 1972. Since then, 193 state parties have ratified the convention, making it one of the most widely recognized international agreements and the world's most popular cultural program.
</p><p>As of July 2019, a total of 1,121 World Heritage Sites (869 cultural, 213 natural, and 39 mixed properties) exist across <a href="/wiki/Table_of_World_Heritage_Sites_by_country" class="mw-redirect" title="Table of World Heritage Sites by country">167 countries</a>. <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_Sites_in_China" title="List of World Heritage Sites in China">China</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_Sites_in_Italy" title="List of World Heritage Sites in Italy">Italy</a>, both with 55 sites, have the most of any country, followed by <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_Sites_in_Spain" title="List of World Heritage Sites in Spain">Spain</a> (48), <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_Sites_in_Germany" title="List of World Heritage Sites in Germany">Germany</a> (46), <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_Sites_in_France" title="List of World Heritage Sites in France">France</a> (45), <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_Sites_in_India" title="List of World Heritage Sites in India">India</a> (38), and <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_Sites_in_Mexico" title="List of World Heritage Sites in Mexico">Mexico</a> (35).<sup id="cite_ref-:0_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Heritage_Site&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Committee" title="World Heritage Committee">World Heritage Committee</a></div>
<table class="infobox vevent" style="width:22em"><caption>Convention concerning the Protection of the World's Cultural and Natural Heritage</caption><tbody><tr><th scope="row">Signed</th><td>16 November 1972</td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Location</th><td class="location">Paris, France</td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Effective</th><td>17 December 1975</td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Condition</th><td>20 ratifications</td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Ratifiers</th><td class="plainlist">193 <small>(189 <a href="/wiki/Member_states_of_the_United_Nations" title="Member states of the United Nations">UN member states</a> plus the <a href="/wiki/Cook_Islands" title="Cook Islands">Cook Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niue" title="Niue">Niue</a>, and <a href="/wiki/State_of_Palestine" title="State of Palestine">Palestine</a>)</small></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Depositary</th><td>Director-General of the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Educational,_Scientific_and_Cultural_Organization" class="mw-redirect" title="United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization">United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization</a></td></tr><tr><th scope="row">Languages</th><td class="hlist">Arabic, English, French, Russian, and Spanish</td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>In 1954, the government of <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">Egypt</a> decided to build the new <a href="/wiki/Aswan_Dam" title="Aswan Dam">Aswan High Dam</a>, whose resulting future reservoir would eventually <a href="/wiki/Flood" title="Flood">inundate</a> a large stretch of the <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">Nile</a> valley containing cultural treasures of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">ancient Egypt</a> and ancient <a href="/wiki/Nubia" title="Nubia">Nubia</a>. In 1959, the governments of Egypt and <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">Sudan</a> requested UNESCO to assist their countries to protect and rescue the endangered monuments and sites. In 1960, the Director-General of UNESCO launched an appeal to the member states for an International Campaign to Save the Monuments of Nubia.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> This appeal resulted in the excavation and recording of hundreds of sites, the recovery of thousands of objects, as well as the salvage and relocation to higher ground of a number of important temples, the most famous of which are the temple complexes of <a href="/wiki/Abu_Simbel_temples" title="Abu Simbel temples">Abu Simbel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Philae" title="Philae">Philae</a>. The campaign, which ended in 1980, was considered a success. As tokens of its gratitude to countries which especially contributed to the campaign's success, Egypt donated four temples: the <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Dendur" title="Temple of Dendur">Temple of Dendur</a> was moved to the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art" title="Metropolitan Museum of Art">Metropolitan Museum of Art</a> in New York City, the <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Debod" title="Temple of Debod">Temple of Debod</a> was moved to the <a href="/wiki/Parque_del_Oeste" title="Parque del Oeste">Parque del Oeste</a> in Madrid, the <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Taffeh" title="Temple of Taffeh">Temple of Taffeh</a> was moved to the <a href="/wiki/Rijksmuseum_van_Oudheden" title="Rijksmuseum van Oudheden">Rijksmuseum van Oudheden</a> in the Netherlands, and the <a href="/wiki/Temple_of_Ellesyia" title="Temple of Ellesyia">Temple of Ellesyia</a> to <a href="/wiki/Museo_Egizio" title="Museo Egizio">Museo Egizio</a> in Turin.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The project cost $80&#160;million, about $40&#160;million of which was collected from 50 countries.<sup id="cite_ref-WHC-history_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHC-history-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The project's success led to other safeguarding campaigns: saving <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">Venice</a> and its <a href="/wiki/Venetian_Lagoon" title="Venetian Lagoon">lagoon</a> in Italy, the ruins of <a href="/wiki/Mohenjo-daro" title="Mohenjo-daro">Mohenjo-daro</a> in Pakistan, and the <a href="/wiki/Borobodur" class="mw-redirect" title="Borobodur">Borobodur</a> Temple Compounds in Indonesia. UNESCO then initiated, with the <a href="/wiki/International_Council_on_Monuments_and_Sites" title="International Council on Monuments and Sites">International Council on Monuments and Sites</a>, a draft convention to protect cultural heritage.<sup id="cite_ref-WHC-history_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHC-history-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Convention_and_background">Convention and background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Heritage_Site&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Convention and background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The United States initiated the idea of cultural conservation with nature conservation. The <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> conference in 1965 called for a "World Heritage Trust" to preserve "the world's superb natural and scenic areas and historic sites for the present and the future of the entire world citizenry". The <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">International Union for Conservation of Nature</a> developed similar proposals in 1968, and they were presented in 1972 to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Conference_on_the_Human_Environment" title="United Nations Conference on the Human Environment">United Nations Conference on the Human Environment</a> in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>. Under the World Heritage Committee, signatory countries are required to produce and submit periodic <a href="/wiki/Data_reporting" title="Data reporting">data reporting</a> providing the World Heritage Committee with an overview of each participating nation's implementation of the World Heritage Convention and a "snapshot" of current conditions at World Heritage properties.
</p><p>Based on the draft convention that UNESCO had initiated, a single text was eventually agreed on by all parties, and the "Convention Concerning the Protection of the World Cultural and Natural Heritage" was adopted by the General Conference of UNESCO on 16 November 1972.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Convention came into force on 17 December 1975. As of May 2017, it has been ratified by 193 states parties,<sup id="cite_ref-unesco_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-unesco-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> including 189 <a href="/wiki/UN_member_states" class="mw-redirect" title="UN member states">UN member states</a> plus the <a href="/wiki/Cook_Islands" title="Cook Islands">Cook Islands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a>, <a href="/wiki/Niue" title="Niue">Niue</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/State_of_Palestine" title="State of Palestine">State of Palestine</a>. Only four UN member states have not ratified the Convention: <a href="/wiki/Liechtenstein" title="Liechtenstein">Liechtenstein</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nauru" title="Nauru">Nauru</a>, <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">Somalia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tuvalu" title="Tuvalu">Tuvalu</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomination_process">Nomination process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Heritage_Site&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nomination process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A country must first list its significant cultural and natural sites; the result is called the Tentative List. A country may not nominate sites that have not been first included on the Tentative List. Next, it can place sites selected from that list into a Nomination File.
</p><p>The Nomination File is evaluated by the <a href="/wiki/International_Council_on_Monuments_and_Sites" title="International Council on Monuments and Sites">International Council on Monuments and Sites</a> and the <a href="/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature" title="International Union for Conservation of Nature">World Conservation Union</a>. These bodies then make their recommendations to the World Heritage Committee. The Committee meets once per year to determine whether or not to inscribe each nominated property on the World Heritage List and sometimes defers or refers the decision to request more information from the country which nominated the site. There are ten selection criteria – a site must meet at least one of them to be included on the list.<sup id="cite_ref-WHS_Guidelines_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHS_Guidelines-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Selection_criteria">Selection criteria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Heritage_Site&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Selection criteria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Up to 2004, there were six criteria for cultural heritage and four criteria for natural heritage. In 2005, this was modified so that there is now only one set of ten criteria. Nominated sites must be of "outstanding universal value" and meet at least one of the ten criteria.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> These criteria have been modified or/amended several times since their creation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural">Cultural</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Heritage_Site&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cultural">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Taj_Mahal_2012.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Site#252: The Taj Mahal, an example of a World Heritage Site." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Taj_Mahal_2012.jpg/220px-Taj_Mahal_2012.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="127" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Taj_Mahal_2012.jpg/330px-Taj_Mahal_2012.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Taj_Mahal_2012.jpg/440px-Taj_Mahal_2012.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4014" data-file-height="2309" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Taj_Mahal_2012.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Site#252: <a href="/wiki/Taj_Mahal" title="Taj Mahal">Taj Mahal</a>, an example of a cultural heritage site.</div></div></div>
<ol type="i" start="1">
<li>"represents a masterpiece of human creative genius and cultural significance"</li>
<li>"exhibits an important interchange of human values, over a span of time, or within a cultural area of the world, on developments in architecture or technology, monumental arts, town-planning, or landscape design"</li>
<li>"to bear a unique or at least exceptional testimony to a cultural tradition or to a civilization which is living or which has disappeared"</li>
<li>"is an outstanding example of a type of building, architectural, or technological ensemble or landscape which illustrates a significant stage in human history"</li>
<li>"is an outstanding example of a traditional  human settlement, land-use, or sea-use which is representative of a culture, or human interaction with the environment especially when it has become vulnerable under the impact of irreversible change"</li>
<li>"is directly or tangibly associated with events or living traditions, with ideas, or with beliefs, with artistic and literary works of outstanding universal significance"</li><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</ol>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_status">Legal status</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Heritage_Site&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legal status">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>UNESCO designation as a World Heritage Site provides <i><a href="/wiki/Prima_facie" title="Prima facie">prima facie</a></i> evidence that such culturally sensitive sites are legally protected pursuant to the Law of War, under the <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Convention" class="mw-redirect" title="Geneva Convention">Geneva Convention</a>, its articles, protocols and customs, together with other treaties including the <a href="/wiki/Hague_Convention_for_the_Protection_of_Cultural_Property_in_the_Event_of_Armed_Conflict" title="Hague Convention for the Protection of Cultural Property in the Event of Armed Conflict">Hague Convention for the Protection of Cultural Property in the Event of Armed Conflict</a> and international law.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (December 2018)">which?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The Geneva Convention and the Hague Convention for the Protection of Cultural Property AREN&#39;T considered international law? (December 2018)">ambiguous</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Thus, the Geneva Convention treaty promulgates:
</p><p>"Article 53. PROTECTION OF CULTURAL OBJECTS AND OF PLACES OF WORSHIP. Without prejudice to the provisions of the Hague Convention for the Protection of Cultural Property in the Event of Armed Conflict of 14 May 1954,' and of other relevant international instruments, it is prohibited:<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><br />
</p>
<dl><dd>(a) To commit any acts of hostility directed against the historic monuments, works of art or places of worship which constitute the cultural or spiritual heritage of peoples;</dd>
<dd>(b) To use such objects in support of the military effort;</dd>
<dd>(c) To make such objects the object of reprisals."</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extensions_and_other_modifications">Extensions and other modifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Heritage_Site&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Extensions and other modifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A country may request to extend or reduce the boundaries, modify the official name, or change the selection criteria of one of its already listed sites. Any proposal for a significant boundary change or modify the site's selection criteria must be submitted as if it were a new nomination, including first placing it on the Tentative List and then onto the Nomination File.<sup id="cite_ref-WHS_Guidelines_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHS_Guidelines-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A request for a minor boundary change, one that does not have a significantly impact on the extent of the property or affect its "outstanding universal value", is also evaluated by the advisory bodies before being sent to the Committee. Such proposals can be rejected by either the advisory bodies or the Committee if they judge it to be a significant change instead of a minor one.<sup id="cite_ref-WHS_Guidelines_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHS_Guidelines-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Proposals to change the site's official name is sent directly to the Committee.<sup id="cite_ref-WHS_Guidelines_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHS_Guidelines-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Endangerment">Endangerment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Heritage_Site&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Endangerment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_in_Danger" title="List of World Heritage in Danger">List of World Heritage in Danger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Former_UNESCO_World_Heritage_Sites" title="Former UNESCO World Heritage Sites">Former UNESCO World Heritage Sites</a></div>
<p>A site may be added to the <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_in_Danger" title="List of World Heritage in Danger">List of World Heritage in Danger</a> if there are conditions that threaten the characteristics for which the landmark or area was inscribed on the World Heritage List. Such problems may involve armed conflict and war, natural disasters, pollution, poaching, or uncontrolled urbanization or human development. This danger list is intended to increase international awareness of the threats and to encourage counteractive measures. Threats to a site can be either proven imminent threats or potential dangers that could have adverse effects on a site.<sup id="cite_ref-UNESCO-WHinDanger_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-WHinDanger-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The state of conservation for each site on the danger list is reviewed on a yearly basis, after which the committee may request additional measures, delete the property from the list if the threats have ceased or consider deletion from both the List of World Heritage in Danger and the World Heritage List.<sup id="cite_ref-WHS_Guidelines_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHS_Guidelines-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Only two sites have ever been <a href="/wiki/Former_UNESCO_World_Heritage_sites" class="mw-redirect" title="Former UNESCO World Heritage sites">delisted</a>: the <a href="/wiki/Arabian_Oryx_Sanctuary" title="Arabian Oryx Sanctuary">Arabian Oryx Sanctuary</a> in Oman and the <a href="/wiki/Dresden_Elbe_Valley" title="Dresden Elbe Valley">Dresden Elbe Valley</a> in Germany. The Arabian Oryx Sanctuary was directly delisted in 2007, instead of first being put on the danger list, after the Omani government decided to reduce the protected area's size by 90 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The Dresden Elbe Valley was first placed on the danger list in 2006 when the World Heritage Committee decided that plans to construct the <a href="/wiki/Waldschl%C3%B6sschen_Bridge" title="Waldschlösschen Bridge">Waldschlösschen Bridge</a> would significantly alter the valley's landscape. In response, Dresden City Council attempted to stop the bridge's construction, but after several court decisions allowed the building of the bridge to proceed, the valley was removed from the World Heritage List in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The first global assessment to quantitatively measure threats to Natural World Heritage sites found<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> that 63 percent of sites have been damaged by increasing human pressures<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> including encroaching roads, agriculture infrastructure and settlements over the last two decades. These activities endanger Natural World Heritage sites and could compromise their unique values. Of the Natural World Heritage sites that contain forest, 91 percent of those experienced some loss since the year 2000. Many Natural World Heritage sites are more threatened than previously thought and require immediate conservation action.
</p>

<p>The World Heritage Committee has divided the world into five geographic zones which it calls regions: Africa, Arab states, Asia and the Pacific, Europe and North America, and Latin America and the Caribbean.
</p><p>Russia and the <a href="/wiki/Caucasus" title="Caucasus">Caucasus</a> states are classified as European, while Mexico and the Caribbean are classified as belonging to the Latin America and Caribbean zone. The UNESCO geographic zones also give greater emphasis on administrative, rather than geographic associations. Hence, <a href="/wiki/Gough_Island" title="Gough Island">Gough Island</a>, located in the South Atlantic, is part of the Europe and North America region because the government of the United Kingdom nominated the site.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Consequences">Consequences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=World_Heritage_Site&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Consequences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Despite the successes of World Heritage listing in promoting conservation, the UNESCO administered project has attracted criticism from some for perceived under-representation of heritage sites outside Europe, disputed decisions on site selection and adverse impact of mass tourism on sites unable to manage rapid growth in visitor numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A sizable <a href="/wiki/Lobbying" title="Lobbying">lobbying industry</a> has grown around the awards because World Heritage listing has the potential to significantly increase tourism revenue from sites selected. Site listing bids are often lengthy and costly, putting poorer countries at a disadvantage. Eritrea's efforts to promote <a href="/wiki/Asmara" title="Asmara">Asmara</a> is one example.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, the Australian government was reported to have successfully lobbied for <a href="/wiki/Great_Barrier_Reef" title="Great Barrier Reef">Great Barrier Reef</a> conservation efforts to be removed from a UNESCO report titled 'World Heritage and Tourism in a Changing Climate'. The Australian government's actions were in response to their concern about the negative impact that an 'at risk' label could have on tourism revenue at a previously designated UNESCO World Heritage site.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A number of listed World Heritage locations such as <a href="/wiki/George_Town,_Penang" title="George Town, Penang">George Town, Penang</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Casco_Viejo,_Panama" title="Casco Viejo, Panama">Casco Viejo, Panama</a>, have struggled to strike the balance between the economic benefits of catering to greatly increased visitor numbers and preserving the original culture and local communities that drew the recognition.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
