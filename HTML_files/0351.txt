	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Post-nominal letters</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Postnominal" redirects here. For adjectives that follow their noun, see <a href="/wiki/Postnominal_adjective" class="mw-redirect" title="Postnominal adjective">Postnominal adjective</a>.</div>
<p><b>Post-nominal letters</b>, also called <b>post-nominal initials</b>, <b>post-nominal titles</b> or <b>designatory letters</b>, are letters placed after a person's name to indicate that the individual holds a position, academic degree, accreditation, office, military decoration, or honour, or is a member of a religious institute or fraternity. An individual may use several different sets of post-nominal letters, but in some contexts it may be customary to limit the number of sets to one or just a few. The order in which post-nominals are listed after a name is based on rules of precedence and what is appropriate for a given situation. Post-nominal  letters are one of the main types of <a href="/wiki/Suffix_(name)" title="Suffix (name)">name suffix</a>. In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Pre-nominal_letters" title="Pre-nominal letters">pre-nominal letters</a> precede the name rather than following it.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List">List</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: List">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_post-nominal_letters" title="List of post-nominal letters">List of post-nominal letters</a></div>
<p>Different awards and post-nominal letters are in use in the English-speaking countries.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Listing_order">Listing order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Listing order">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The order in which post-nominal letters are listed after a person's name is dictated by standard practice, which may vary by region and context.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Order_of_post-nominals_in_the_United_States">Order of post-nominals in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Order of post-nominals in the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_professional_designations_in_the_United_States" title="List of professional designations in the United States">List of professional designations in the United States</a></div>
<p>In the United States, standard protocol is to list post-nominal letters in the following order:<sup id="cite_ref-US_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Religious institutions</li>
<li>Theological degrees</li>
<li>Military decorations</li>
<li>Academic degrees</li>
<li>Honorary degrees, honors, decorations</li>
<li>Professional licenses, certifications and affiliations</li>
<li>Retired uniformed service.</li></ol>
<p>Active duty services personnel do not use any post-nominals other than, if applicable, Staff Corps affiliation (Navy only) followed by a comma and then their branch of service. Names are bracketed by the appropriate pre-nominal and post-nominal, e.g. LCDR John Q Public, MC, USN.<sup id="cite_ref-US_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Order_of_post-nominals_in_the_UK">Order of post-nominals in the UK</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Order of post-nominals in the UK">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_post-nominal_letters_(United_Kingdom)" title="List of post-nominal letters (United Kingdom)">List of post-nominal letters (United Kingdom)</a></div>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_usage_in_the_UK">Civil usage in the UK</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Civil usage in the UK">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>In the United Kingdom various sources have issued guidance on the ordering of styles and titles for British citizens, including the Ministry of Justice, <a href="/wiki/Debrett%27s" title="Debrett&#39;s">Debrett's</a> and <a href="/wiki/A_%26_C_Black" title="A &amp; C Black">A &amp; C Black</a>'s <i>Titles and Forms of Address</i>; these are generally in close agreement, with the exception of the position of MP, etc., in the listing:<sup id="cite_ref-MoJ_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoJ-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Debrett&#39;s_post_nominals_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Debrett&#39;s_post_nominals-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Baronet" title="Baronet">Bt/Bart</a> or <a href="/wiki/Esquire" title="Esquire">Esq</a>;
<ul><li>In the UK, "Esq." may be used to refer to any gentleman in place of the pre-nominal Mr or Dr;<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Orders,_decorations,_and_medals_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Orders, decorations, and medals of the United Kingdom">British Orders and decorations</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Officer_of_the_Most_Excellent_Order_of_the_British_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Officer of the Most Excellent Order of the British Empire">OBE</a>; in descending order of precedence);</li>
<li>Crown Appointments, i.e.:<div><ol style="list-style-type:lower-alpha"><li><a href="/wiki/Privy_Counsellor_(United_Kingdom)" class="mw-redirect" title="Privy Counsellor (United Kingdom)">Privy Counsellor</a> (PC), <a href="/wiki/Personal_aide-de-camp" title="Personal aide-de-camp">Aide-de-Camp to the Queen</a> (ADC(P)), <a href="/wiki/Physician_to_the_Queen" title="Physician to the Queen">Physician to the Queen</a> (QHP), <a href="/wiki/Medical_Household" title="Medical Household">Honorary Surgeon to the Queen</a> (QHS), <a href="/wiki/Medical_Household" title="Medical Household">Honorary Dental Surgeon to the Queen</a> (QHDS), <a href="/wiki/Medical_Household" title="Medical Household">Honorary Nursing Sister to the Queen</a> (QHNS), and <a href="/wiki/Honorary_Chaplain_to_the_Queen" title="Honorary Chaplain to the Queen">Honorary Chaplain to the Queen</a> (QHC)</li><li><a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Counsel" title="Queen&#39;s Counsel">Queen's Counsel</a> (QC), <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_Peace" class="mw-redirect" title="Justice of the Peace">Justice of the Peace</a> (JP) and <a href="/wiki/Deputy_Lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Deputy Lieutenant">Deputy Lieutenant</a> (DL); (according to the Ministry of Justice) Member of Parliament or of a devolved assembly (MP, MSP, AM, MLA);</li></ol></div></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Academic_degree" title="Academic degree">University degrees</a>:
<ul><li>According to Debrett's, DD, MD and MS degrees are always given; other doctorates, other medical degrees, and other divinity degrees are sometimes given; and other degrees are seldom shown, with BA and MA never used socially (although formal lists may include them);<sup id="cite_ref-Debrett&#39;s_degrees_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Debrett&#39;s_degrees-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li>&#160; <div><ol style="list-style-type:lower-alpha"><li><a href="/wiki/Religious_institute" title="Religious institute">Religious institutes</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Society_of_St_Francis" class="mw-redirect" title="Society of St Francis">SSF</a>),</li><li>Medical qualifications (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Royal_College_of_Physicians#Membership_and_fellowship" title="Royal College of Physicians">FRCP</a>);</li></ol></div></li>
<li>&#160; <div><ol style="list-style-type:lower-alpha"><li>Fellowship of <a href="/wiki/Learned_society" title="Learned society">learned societies</a> (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">FRS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Geographical_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Geographical Society">FRGS</a>),</li><li>Royal Academicians and associates, (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Arts#Membership" title="Royal Academy of Arts">RA,  ARA</a>),</li><li>Fellowships, Membership, etc. of professional institutions, associations, etc. (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Institution_of_Civil_Engineers" class="mw-redirect" title="Fellow of the Institution of Civil Engineers">FICE</a>) – chartered and other professional statuses should be shown before the designatory letters for the relevant professional body (e.g. CEng FMIET; EngTech TMIET),<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li><li>According to Debrett's: <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Writers_to_Her_Majesty%27s_Signet" title="Society of Writers to Her Majesty&#39;s Signet">Writers to the Signet</a> (WS);</li></ol></div></li>
<li>According to Debrett's and Black's: Member of Parliament (MP), etc.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Cleanup" title="Wikipedia:Cleanup"><span title="Wikipedia:Cleanup">what does the etc encompass?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> (Black's also includes Writers to the Signet here);</li>
<li>Membership of the Armed Forces (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Royal_Air_Force" title="Royal Air Force">RAF</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">RN</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volunteer_Reserves_(United_Kingdom)" title="Volunteer Reserves (United Kingdom)">VR</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Marines" title="Royal Marines">RM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Military_Police" title="Royal Military Police">RMP</a>) (not included by Black's).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>
<p>In addition, British citizens who have received honours from <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">Commonwealth</a> countries are usually given permission from the Queen to use the postnominals for that honour.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_usage_in_the_UK">Academic usage in the UK</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Academic usage in the UK">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oxford" title="University of Oxford">Oxford University</a> Style Guide and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Nottingham" title="University of Nottingham">University of Nottingham</a> Style Guide give the alternative ordering:<sup id="cite_ref-OxStyle_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OxStyle-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Civil Honours</li>
<li>Military Honours</li>
<li>QC</li>
<li>Degrees in the order:
<ol><li>Bachelor's</li>
<li>Master's</li>
<li>Doctorates</li>
<li>Postdoctoral</li></ol></li>
<li>Diplomas</li>
<li>Certificates</li>
<li>Membership of academic or professional bodies</li></ol>
<p>This differs from the civil ordering in that it omits appointments except for QC, includes diplomas and certificates in addition to degrees, merges medical qualifications, fellowships of learned societies, royal academicians, and membership of professional bodies into a single item, and omits membership of the armed forces.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Loughborough_University" title="Loughborough University">Loughborough University</a> gives a very similar ordering, but with "Appointments (e.g MP, QC)" replacing item 3 (QC) and "Higher Education awards (in ascending order, commencing with undergraduate)" replacing items 4–6 (Degrees, Diplomas and Certificates). This restores the
Appointments section from the civil list omitted by Oxford and Nottingham, although the other differences remain.<sup id="cite_ref-Lboro_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lboro-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nottingham_Trent_University" title="Nottingham Trent University">Nottingham Trent University</a> gives essentially the same ordering as Oxford and Nottingham, but without specifying the order in which degrees should be given.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Nottingham Trent, Oxford and Loughborough recommend degree abbreviations be given in mixed case without stops between the letters (e.g. BA, not B.A.; PhD, not Ph.D.), as does <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">Cambridge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Imperial_College_London" title="Imperial College London">Imperial College London</a>, however, uses all small caps for post-nominals (e.g. <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047074">.mw-parser-output .smallcaps{font-variant:small-caps}</style><span class="smallcaps">phd</span>, not PhD).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Where all degrees are shown, as in university calendars, most universities will give these in ascending order.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> However, advice on the precise ordering varies:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford University">Oxford University</a> Calendar Style Guide places degrees in the order: bachelor's degrees (including postgraduate bachelor's degrees such as the Oxford <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Civil_Law" title="Bachelor of Civil Law">BCL</a>) and other first degrees; master's degrees (including those that are first degrees, such as <a href="/wiki/MPhys" class="mw-redirect" title="MPhys">MPhys</a>); doctorates; higher doctorates in order of academic precedence. Degrees at the same level are ordered alphabetically by awarding institution and multiple degrees from the same institution are grouped, with position determined by the lowest degree in the grouping. Certificates and diplomas are listed after degrees (no mention is made of foundation degrees). Oxford recommends giving institution names separated by a space from the degree, not enclosed in parentheses, and that degrees from the same institution be separated by spaces only, with commas between degrees from different institutions.<sup id="cite_ref-OxCal_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OxCal-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Loughborough_University" title="Loughborough University">Loughborough University</a> advises listing all higher education awards in ascending order starting from undergraduate, so MPhys would come before BCL and postgraduate certificates and diplomas would come between undergraduate and postgraduate degrees. The examples given do not include institution names.<sup id="cite_ref-Lboro_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lboro-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:102px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Zirkel_of_Corps_Altsachsen_Dresden.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Zirkel_of_Corps_Altsachsen_Dresden.png/100px-Zirkel_of_Corps_Altsachsen_Dresden.png" decoding="async" width="100" height="86" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Zirkel_of_Corps_Altsachsen_Dresden.png/150px-Zirkel_of_Corps_Altsachsen_Dresden.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e6/Zirkel_of_Corps_Altsachsen_Dresden.png 2x" data-file-width="200" data-file-height="172" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Zirkel_of_Corps_Altsachsen_Dresden.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <b>Zirkel</b> of a <a href="/wiki/German_Student_Corps" title="German Student Corps">German Student Corps</a>. This symbol captures the letters "v, c, f, A", as  post-nominal for that fraternity.</div></div></div>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_European_fraternities">In European fraternities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In European fraternities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Going back to the mid 17th century, today's classical European fraternities such as the <a href="/wiki/German_Student_Corps" title="German Student Corps">German Student Corps</a> are using post-nominal symbols and letters to allow their members to indicate their fraternity membership and honorary positions held in their signature. The German word for the symbol is "Zirkel", literally "circle", referring to the hand-written symbol representing the fraternity which is commonly composed by combining letters from an acronym such as "<b>v</b>ivat, <b>c</b>rescat, <b>f</b>loreat" (Latin: grow, bloom, prosper) followed by the first letter of the fraternity. The word "Zirkel" became a synonym in the late Middle Ages representing the entire group of close brothers. An example was Schiller's use of the sentence "Schließt den heil'gen Zirkel dichter" (literally: closer draw the holy circle [of brothers]) in the original version of the Ode to the Joy.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Australia">In Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_post-nominal_letters_(Australia)" title="List of post-nominal letters (Australia)">List of post-nominal letters (Australia)</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Sydney" title="University of Sydney">University of Sydney</a> Style Guide and the Australian Government Style Manual give the ordering:<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>National and Royal honours</li>
<li>Degrees before diplomas, in order of conferral</li>
<li>Fellowships then memberships of professional bodies and learned societies</li>
<li>Parliamentary designations</li></ol>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Technology_Sydney" title="University of Technology Sydney">University of Technology Sydney</a> adds QC between honours and degrees and specifies that honours should be in order of precedence.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="In_Canada">In Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: In Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_post-nominal_letters_in_Canada" title="List of post-nominal letters in Canada">List of post-nominal letters in Canada</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Canada" title="Government of Canada">Canadian government's</a> <i>The Canadian Style</i> specifies that no more than two sets of post-nominal letters should normally be given, unless all are to be given either for information or for reasons of protocol, and that these should be the two highest of different types. The types and the order in which they are given are:<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Distinctions conferred directly by the Crown</li>
<li>University degrees</li>
<li>Memberships of societies and other distinctions</li></ol>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etiquette_for_higher_educational_qualifications">Etiquette for higher educational qualifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Etiquette for higher educational qualifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Higher_education_qualifications_in_the_US">Higher education qualifications in the US</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Higher education qualifications in the US">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In academia and research, all degrees may be listed. In general, however, it is normal to only list those relevant to the circumstance. For example, if Jane Doe had a BS, MS, and PhD in computer science as well as an MBA, then if working in management in a retail company she would write "Jane Doe, MBA", but if working in an IT company she might write "Jane Doe, PhD", and if working in academia she could write "Jane Doe, BS, MS, MBA, PhD".<sup id="cite_ref-US_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-US-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/The_Gregg_Reference_Manual" title="The Gregg Reference Manual">The Gregg Reference Manual</a> recommends placing periods between the letters of post-nominals (e.g., <i>B.S.</i>, <i>Ph.D.</i>); however, <a href="/wiki/The_Chicago_Manual_of_Style" title="The Chicago Manual of Style">The Chicago Manual of Style</a> recommends writing degrees without periods (e.g., <i>BS</i>, <i>PhD</i>). If post-nominals are given, the full name should be used, without Dr., Mr., Ms., Mrs., or Miss. Other prefixes (e.g., <i>Professor</i>) may be used.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Higher_education_qualifications_in_the_UK">Higher education qualifications in the UK</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Higher education qualifications in the UK">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the UK, it is usual to list only doctorates, degrees in medicine, and degrees in divinity.<sup id="cite_ref-Debrett&#39;s_degrees_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Debrett&#39;s_degrees-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In particular, when a person has letters indicating Crown honours or decorations, only the principal degree would normally be given.<sup id="cite_ref-Debrett&#39;s_post_nominals_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Debrett&#39;s_post_nominals-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The University of Oxford Style Guide advises writers: "Remember that you do not need to list all awards, degrees, memberships etc held by an individual – only those items relevant to your writing."<sup id="cite_ref-OxStyle_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OxStyle-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In an academic context, or in formal lists, all degrees may be listed in ascending order of academic status, which may not be the same as the order in which they were obtained (although see notes on medical qualifications, below). The Oxford style is to list qualifications by their title starting with bachelor's degrees, then master's degrees, then doctorates. Postgraduate Certificates and Diplomas are listed after doctorates, but before professional qualifications,<sup id="cite_ref-OxCal_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OxCal-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> with a similar ordering being used by other universities.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> In this style, foundation degrees and other sub-bachelor qualifications are not shown. An alternative style is to give all higher education qualifications, starting from undergraduate, ordered by their level rather than their title. In this style, one might list a <a href="/wiki/Certificate_of_higher_education" class="mw-redirect" title="Certificate of higher education">Certificate</a> or <a href="/wiki/Diploma_of_higher_education" class="mw-redirect" title="Diploma of higher education">Diploma of Higher Education</a> first, then <a href="/wiki/Foundation_degree" title="Foundation degree">foundation degrees</a>, first degrees at bachelor level, first degrees at master level (integrated master's degrees and first degrees in medicine), postgraduate degrees at master level (including postgraduate bachelor's degrees such the Oxford BCL), and doctorates. In this style, postgraduate certificates and diplomas could be shown either before postgraduate degrees at master's level (as in the table given by Loughborough University) or before first degrees at master's level (reflecting their position in the <a href="/wiki/Frameworks_for_Higher_Education_Qualifications_of_UK_Degree-Awarding_Bodies" class="mw-redirect" title="Frameworks for Higher Education Qualifications of UK Degree-Awarding Bodies">Frameworks for Higher Education Qualifications of UK Degree-Awarding Bodies</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Lboro_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lboro-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Strictly speaking, both the Debrett's and Ministry of Justice lists only allow for the inclusion of degrees, not non-degree academic awards.
</p><p>For someone with a substantive doctorate, it is usual either to give "Dr" as the title (without a stop as per normal British usage) or to list their degrees post-nominally, e.g. "Dr John Smith" or "John Smith, PhD" but not "Dr John Smith, PhD". Postnominals may be used with other titles, e.g. "Mr John Smith, PhD", "Sir John Smith, PhD", or "The Rev John Smith, PhD".<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the case of a BA from Oxford, Cambridge or Dublin who proceeds to be an <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts_(Oxbridge_and_Dublin)" class="mw-redirect" title="Master of Arts (Oxbridge and Dublin)">MA of those universities</a> (which is taken without further study), the MA replaces the BA and thus only the MA should be listed.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Oxford has said that there is no risk of confusion between their MA and "earned" MAs as the Oxford MA is denoted "MA (Oxon)" rather than simply MA.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> However, Debrett's has advised using just "MA" to describe a Cambridge Master of Arts.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Graduates from British and Irish universities sometimes add the name of the university that awarded their degree after the post-nominals for their degree, either in parentheses or not, depending on preferred style. University names are often abbreviated and sometimes given in Latin, e.g."BA, MA (Dunelm), PhD (Ebor)";<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> a list of abbreviations used for university names can be found at <a href="/wiki/Universities_in_the_United_Kingdom#Post-nominal_abbreviations" title="Universities in the United Kingdom">Universities in the United Kingdom#Post-nominal abbreviations</a>. Where the same degree has been granted by more than one university, this can be shown by placing the names or abbreviations in a single bracket after the degree name, e.g. "<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047074"/><span class="smallcaps">Sir <a href="/wiki/Edward_Elgar" title="Edward Elgar">Edward Elgar</a></span>, Mus.D. (Oxon., Cantab., Dunelm. et Yale, U.S.A.), LL.D. (Leeds, Aberdeen, and W. University, Pennsylvania.)".<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Honorary_degree" title="Honorary degree">Honorary degrees</a>, if shown, can be indicated either by "Hon" before the post-nominals for the degree or "hc" (for <i>honoris causa</i>) after the post-nominals, e.g. "Professor <a href="/wiki/Evelyn_Ebsworth" title="Evelyn Ebsworth">Evelyn Algernon Valentine Ebsworth</a> CBE, PhD, MA, ScD, <b>DCL hc</b>, FRSC, <a href="/wiki/FRSE" class="mw-redirect" title="FRSE">FRSE</a>" (emphasis added);<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> "Professor <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hawking" title="Stephen Hawking">Stephen Hawking</a> <b>Hon.ScD</b>, CH, CBE, FRS" (emphasis added).<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The Oxford University Calendar Style Guide recommends not giving honorary degrees in post-nominals.<sup id="cite_ref-OxCal_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OxCal-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etiquette_for_medical_qualifications">Etiquette for medical qualifications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Etiquette for medical qualifications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Medical_qualifications_in_the_UK">Medical qualifications in the UK</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Medical qualifications in the UK">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In contrast to the style for academic qualifications, medical qualifications are listed in <i>descending</i> order, i.e.: doctorates, master's degrees, bachelor's degrees, postgraduate diplomas, and qualifying diplomas. Letters indicating doctorates, master's degrees and fellowships of royal colleges are always given, while bachelor's degrees, memberships and qualifying diplomas are only shown for people with no higher qualifications. In all but formal lists, only three medical qualifications are normally given.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Where someone holds qualifications in multiple fields, they are normally given in the order: medicine, surgery (except for MRCS, which is considered a qualifying diploma), obstetrics, gynaecology and other specialities. These are followed by qualifying diplomas and other diplomas.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Note that the academic style guides do not have a separate section for medical qualifications, so if following one of these guides, medical degrees should be listed with other degrees, medical diplomas with other diplomas, and fellowships and memberships of royal colleges with other fellowships and memberships of professional bodies.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etiquette_for_fellowships_or_memberships">Etiquette for fellowships or memberships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Etiquette for fellowships or memberships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="Learned_societies.2C_royal_academies_and_professional_institutions_in_the_UK"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Learned_societies,_royal_academies_and_professional_institutions_in_the_UK">Learned societies, royal academies and professional institutions in the UK</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Learned societies, royal academies and professional institutions in the UK">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the UK there is, according to <a href="/wiki/Debrett%27s" title="Debrett&#39;s">Debrett's</a>, no defined order of precedence for placing designatory letters for <a href="/wiki/Fellowship" class="mw-redirect" title="Fellowship">fellowships</a> of <a href="/wiki/Learned_societies" class="mw-redirect" title="Learned societies">learned societies</a> and memberships of <a href="/wiki/Professional_bodies" class="mw-redirect" title="Professional bodies">professional bodies</a> within their respective groups. Debrett's suggests that "In practice, where one society is indisputably of greater importance than another the letters are usually placed in that order. Alternatively, the fellowship of the junior society may be omitted. If such precedence cannot be determined, the letters may be placed in order of conferment.  Where this is not known, they may be placed in alphabetical order."<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Earlier guidance that "Strictly speaking, they should be arranged according to date of foundation or incorporation of the societies concerned"<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> has now been removed.
</p><p>Only postnominals indicating honorific fellowships (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">FRS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_British_Academy" title="Fellow of the British Academy">FBA</a>) are normally used socially. For professional bodies it is usual to list those most relevant to a person's profession first, or those most relevant to the particular circumstances. It is common to omit fellowships (except honorific fellowships) and memberships that are not relevant in a given situation.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Debrett's notes that although <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academicians" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal Academicians">Royal Academicians</a> are listed after fellows of learned societies (and before members of professional bodies), they do not yield to them in precedence, "In practice the two lists do not coincide."<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It should also be noted that the distinction between a learned society and a professional body is not well defined. Many organisations (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Chemistry" title="Royal Society of Chemistry">Royal Society of Chemistry</a>) claim to be both learned societies and professional bodies.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> However, it is clear from both the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Justice_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Justice (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Justice</a> and Debrett's that only <i>fellowships</i> of learned societies are listed, while <i>fellowships and memberships</i> may be listed for professional bodies.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Example_farm plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/40px-Ambox_important.svg.png" decoding="async" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/60px-Ambox_important.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/b4/Ambox_important.svg/80px-Ambox_important.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="40" data-file-height="40" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>may contain <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:What_Wikipedia_is_not#Wikipedia_is_not_an_indiscriminate_collection_of_information" title="Wikipedia:What Wikipedia is not">indiscriminate</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Embedded_lists#List_size" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Embedded lists">excessive</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view#Due_and_undue_weight" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view">irrelevant</a> examples</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Post-nominal_letters&amp;action=edit">improve the article</a> by adding more descriptive text and removing <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Example_cruft" title="Wikipedia:Example cruft">less pertinent examples</a>. See Wikipedia's <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Writing_better_articles" title="Wikipedia:Writing better articles">guide to writing better articles</a> for further suggestions.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">April 2019</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Examples of post-nominal letters:
</p>
<ul><li>A Knight Commander of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">Order of the British Empire</a> is authorised to use the post-nominal KBE, e.g. "Sir <a href="/wiki/Terry_Wogan" title="Terry Wogan">Terry Wogan</a> KBE DL" (DL indicating he was <a href="/wiki/Deputy_Lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Deputy Lieutenant">Deputy Lieutenant</a> of <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a>).</li>
<li>A Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">Royal Society</a> uses the post-nominal FRS and a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Edinburgh" title="Royal Society of Edinburgh">Royal Society of Edinburgh</a> FRSE, e.g. "Professor Malcolm Longair CBE, FRS, FRSE" (CBE indicating he is also a Commander of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">Order of the British Empire</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Doctor of Philosophy</a> may use the post-nominal PhD or DPhil (according to the usage of the awarding institution), or the pre-nominals Dr or Dr., e.g. <a href="/wiki/Indiana_Jones" title="Indiana Jones">Indiana Jones</a> could be styled "Dr. Henry Walton Jones, Jr." or "Henry Walton Jones, Jr., PhD", but not "Dr. Henry Walton Jones, Jr., PhD".</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Postgraduate_Diploma" class="mw-redirect" title="Postgraduate Diploma">Postgraduate Diploma</a> is indicated by the post-nominals PgDip. Someone with a BA, MA and postgraduate diploma could write "BA PgDip MA"<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> or (following the Oxford Calendar's style) "BA MA PgDip".</li>
<li>Commonly seen postnominals for religious orders include OFM for the <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan">Franciscans</a> (<a href="/wiki/Order_of_Friars_Minor" title="Order of Friars Minor">Order of Friars Minor</a>), SJ for the <a href="/wiki/Jesuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesuit">Jesuits</a> (<a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">Society of Jesus</a>), e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Pope_Francis" title="Pope Francis">Jorge Bergoglio</a>, SJ,<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> and OP for the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominicans</a> (<a href="/wiki/Order_of_Preachers" class="mw-redirect" title="Order of Preachers">Order of Preachers</a>); most other <a href="/wiki/Religious_institute_(Catholic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Religious institute (Catholic)">Catholic religious institutes</a> have specific post-nominal letters.</li>
<li>A BA graduate of the <a href="/wiki/Open_University" title="Open University">Open University</a> could indicate the university from which they got their degree using "BA (Open)" or "BA Open".</li>
<li>Someone with a BSc from Cardiff, an MSc from London and a PhD from Cardiff could write "BSc PhD Card, MSc Lond", or "BSc Card, MSc Lond, PhD Card"; alternatively they could choose to omit the lower degrees and simply write "PhD Card" ("London" may be substituted for "Lond" and "Cardiff" for "Card", and institution names could be enclosed in parentheses). If not indicating the awarding universities, they would write "BSc MSc PhD", or simply "PhD".</li>
<li>A member of the British parliament may use the postnominals "MP", e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Caroline_Lucas" title="Caroline Lucas">Caroline Lucas</a> MP".<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Note the post-nominals MP may not be used once someone ceases to be a Member of Parliament, including after Parliament has been dissolved for an election.</li>
<li>A peer who is a member of the Privy Council may use the postnominals "PC"; for non-peers, the pre-nominal "Right Honourable", abbreviated "Rt Hon", identifies them as members.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Thus: "The Rt Hon <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">David Cameron</a> MP",<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> but "The Rt Hon <a href="/wiki/Charles_Grey,_2nd_Earl_Grey" title="Charles Grey, 2nd Earl Grey">Earl Grey</a> KG PC" (KG indicating he was also a <a href="/wiki/Knight_of_the_Garter" class="mw-redirect" title="Knight of the Garter">Knight of the Garter</a>).</li>
<li>Chartered status is shown before the relevant professional membership, e.g. "Prof. Dame <a href="/wiki/Carole_Jordan" title="Carole Jordan">Carole Jordan</a> DBE FRS CPhys FInstP", where DBE indicates Dame Commander of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">Order of the British Empire</a>, FRS Fellow of the Royal Society, CPhys <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Physicist" title="Chartered Physicist">Chartered Physicist</a>, and FInstP Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Physics" title="Institute of Physics">Institute of Physics</a>, the awarding body for CPhys.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In the UK, someone who earned a BA, MA and BSc in that order would normally write "BA, BSc, MA", but in Australia they would normally write "BA, MA, BSc".</li>
<li>"Reverend Canon Mark Tanner MA (Oxon), BA (Hons), MTh"<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> demonstrates: 1) the use of '(Oxon)' to indicate an <a href="/wiki/Oxbridge_MA" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxbridge MA">Oxbridge MA</a> (to avoid confusion with an earned MA); 2) the use of '(Hons)' to indicate an <a href="/wiki/Honours_degree" title="Honours degree">honours degree</a>; 3) ordering of degrees by date gained.</li></ul>
