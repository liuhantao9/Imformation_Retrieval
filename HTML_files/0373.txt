<p>The <b>history of the <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">Jews</a> in <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a></b> starts at least in the 17th century, when in 1639, the English government allowed <a href="/wiki/Spanish_and_Portuguese_Jews" title="Spanish and Portuguese Jews">Spanish and Portuguese Jews</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, Portugal and <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a> to settle the region, coming to the old capital <a href="/wiki/Torarica" title="Torarica">Torarica</a>. In 1652, a new group that migrated under the leadership of <a href="/wiki/Francis_Willoughby,_5th_Baron_Willoughby_of_Parham" title="Francis Willoughby, 5th Baron Willoughby of Parham">Francis Willoughby, 5th Baron Willoughby of Parham</a> came to <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> <sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and settled in the <a href="/wiki/Jodensavanne" title="Jodensavanne">Jodensavanne</a> area. A third group came 1664, after their expulsion from <a href="/wiki/Recife" title="Recife">Recife</a> and then <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/David_Cohen_Nassy" title="David Cohen Nassy">David Cohen Nassy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> According to the <i>Encyclopedia of Latin America</i>, "Suriname was one of the most important centers of the Jewish population in the Western Hemisphere, and Jews there were planters and slaveholders."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:262px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Synagoge-Keizerstraat-Suriname.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Synagoge-Keizerstraat-Suriname.jpg/260px-Synagoge-Keizerstraat-Suriname.jpg" decoding="async" width="260" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Synagoge-Keizerstraat-Suriname.jpg/390px-Synagoge-Keizerstraat-Suriname.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Synagoge-Keizerstraat-Suriname.jpg/520px-Synagoge-Keizerstraat-Suriname.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2544" data-file-height="1496" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Synagoge-Keizerstraat-Suriname.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Neveh_Shalom_Synagogue" title="Neveh Shalom Synagogue">Neveh Shalom Synagogue</a> (right)</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Plantation_economy" title="Plantation economy">plantation economy</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Jodensavanne" title="Jodensavanne">Jodensavanne</a>—an area of <a href="/wiki/Savannah" class="mw-redirect" title="Savannah">savannah</a> that was settled and planted with <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">sugarcane</a>—relied on <a href="/wiki/Slave_labour" class="mw-redirect" title="Slave labour">slave labour</a>. The community declined in the wake of the French <a href="/wiki/Cassard_expedition" title="Cassard expedition">Cassard expedition</a> in 1712 and the levies he instituted, competition from <a href="/wiki/Beet_sugar" class="mw-redirect" title="Beet sugar">beet sugar</a>, and a revolt by <a href="/wiki/Maroon_(people)" title="Maroon (people)">Maroons</a>. Settlers relocated to the capital of <a href="/wiki/Paramaribo" title="Paramaribo">Paramaribo</a>. They returned to the synagogue in the Jodensavanne to celebrate the holidays until 10 September 1832, when a fire destroyed the village and synagogue. The savanna area was subsequently overtaken with jungle regrowth.<sup id="cite_ref-j_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-j-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Jews_in_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although in the present-day the term Creole (as used in the context of Suriname) means a synonym of Afro-Surinamese, its original meaning carried a negative connotation to mean that a white European person had forgotten how to be a "good Jew" (or proper Englishman, etc.) due to having adopted some characteristics that European communities considered "native".<sup id="cite_ref-vink_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vink-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Jews did interact with other groups in Suriname and the illegitimate children of Jewish males with Afro-Surinamese women were raised as Jews and given Jewish names.
</p><p>Some Jewish family names have endured and are now considered Afro-Surinamese family names and the names of the <a href="/wiki/Saramaka" title="Saramaka">Saramaka</a> clan of <a href="/wiki/Maroon_(people)" title="Maroon (people)">Maroons</a> refer to the Jewish plantation owners their ancestors escaped from. In the cemeteries of <a href="/wiki/Paramaribo" title="Paramaribo">Paramaribo</a> Jewish tombstones appear alongside <a href="/wiki/Creole_peoples" title="Creole peoples">creole</a> ones.<sup id="cite_ref-vink_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vink-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Identity can be used to exclude persons from a community, but it can also be used to force people to be part of community against their will. During the 17th and 18th centuries forced inclusion was commonplace in both the Portuguese and High German Jewish communities and the rigid identity boundaries were often supported by legislation. Though the cultural identity of Portuguese Jews was defined as being a white colonial elite, this identity existed alongside an aggressive policy to include poor and Jews of color.<sup id="cite_ref-vink_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vink-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Synagogues">Synagogues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Jews_in_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Synagogues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Three synagogues were built in Suriname: Beracha Ve Shalom in 1685, in the Jodensavanne; <a href="/wiki/Neveh_Shalom_Synagogue" title="Neveh Shalom Synagogue">Neveh Shalom Synagogue</a> in 1719, built by <a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi" class="mw-redirect" title="Ashkenazi">Ashkenazi</a> Jews in the new capital of <a href="/wiki/Paramaribo" title="Paramaribo">Paramaribo</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Zedek_ve_Shalom" class="mw-redirect" title="Zedek ve Shalom">Zedek ve Shalom</a> in 1735, built by <a href="/wiki/Sephardic" class="mw-redirect" title="Sephardic">Sephardic</a> Jews.<sup id="cite_ref-j_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-j-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> There was also a Darkhe Yesharim for Judeo African creoles.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Depopulation">Depopulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Jews_in_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Depopulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most Jews left Suriname when it was granted independence in 1975 and others left during the <a href="/wiki/Suriname_Guerrilla_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Suriname Guerrilla War">civil war of the 1980s</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-j_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-j-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> About 130 Jewish community members remained in a combined Sephardic and Ashkenazic congregation at Neve Shalom (which includes community hall and <a href="/wiki/Mikveh" title="Mikveh">mikveh</a>). The second synagogue was rented for use as a computer service shop, its furniture and art loaned to the <a href="/wiki/Israel_Museum" title="Israel Museum">Israel Museum</a> in Jerusalem.
</p><p>In the 1990s, the jungle growth in the Jodensavanne was cleared, 450 graves uncovered and the ruins of the synagogue maintained.<sup id="cite_ref-j_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-j-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In a 21st-century census, 181 entered "Judaism" as their religion, out of a total population of 560,000.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
