<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Space_policy_process">Space policy process</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_policy_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Space policy process">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>United States space policy is drafted by the <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States#Executive_branch" title="Federal government of the United States">Executive branch</a> at the direction of the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a>, and submitted for approval and establishment of funding to  the legislative process of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Space_advocacy" title="Space advocacy">Space advocacy</a> organizations may provide advice to the government and lobby for space goals.  These include advocacy groups such as the <a href="/wiki/Space_Science_Institute" title="Space Science Institute">Space Science Institute</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_Space_Society" title="National Space Society">National Space Society</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Space_Generation_Advisory_Council" title="Space Generation Advisory Council">Space Generation Advisory Council</a>, the last of which among other things runs the annual <a href="/wiki/Yuri%27s_Night" title="Yuri&#39;s Night">Yuri's Night</a> event; <a href="/wiki/Learned_societies" class="mw-redirect" title="Learned societies">learned societies</a> such as the <a href="/wiki/American_Astronomical_Society" title="American Astronomical Society">American Astronomical Society</a> and the <a href="/wiki/American_Astronautical_Society" title="American Astronautical Society">American Astronautical Society</a>; and policy organizations such as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academies" class="mw-redirect" title="United States National Academies">National Academies</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drafting">Drafting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_policy_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Drafting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In drafting space policy, the President consults with the <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">National Aeronautics and Space Administration</a> (NASA), responsible for civilian and scientific space programs, and with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a>, responsible for military space activities, which include communications, reconnaissance, intelligence, mapping, and missile defense.<sup id="cite_ref-Goldman,_pp._91–97_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldman,_pp._91–97-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The President is legally responsible for deciding which space activities fall under the civilian and military areas.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The President also consults with the <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Security_Council" title="United States National Security Council">National Security Council</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Science_and_Technology_Policy" title="Office of Science and Technology Policy">Office of Science and Technology Policy</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Management_and_Budget" title="Office of Management and Budget">Office of Management and Budget</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 1958 <a href="/wiki/National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Act" title="National Aeronautics and Space Act">National Aeronautics and Space Act</a>, which created NASA, created a <a href="/wiki/National_Space_Council#1958_-_1973_National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Council_(NASC)" title="National Space Council">National Aeronautics and Space Council</a> chaired by the President to help advise him, which included the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_State" title="United States Secretary of State">Secretary of State</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States_Secretary_of_Defense" title="United States Secretary of Defense">Secretary of Defense</a>, NASA Administrator, Chairman of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Atomic_Energy_Commission" title="United States Atomic Energy Commission">Atomic Energy Commission</a>, plus up to one member of the federal government, and up to three private individuals "eminent in science, engineering, technology, education, administration, or public affairs" appointed by the President.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Before taking office as President, <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> persuaded Congress to amend the Act to allow him to set the precedent of delegating chairmanship of this council to his Vice President (<a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a>). The Council was discontinued in 1973 during the presidency of <a href="/wiki/Richard_M._Nixon" class="mw-redirect" title="Richard M. Nixon">Richard M. Nixon</a>. In 1989, President <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">George H. W. Bush</a> re-established a differently constituted <a href="/wiki/National_Space_Council#1989_-_1993_National_Space_Council" title="National Space Council">National Space Council</a> by <a href="/wiki/Executive_order" title="Executive order">executive order</a>, which was discontinued in 1993 by President <a href="/wiki/Bill_Clinton" title="Bill Clinton">Bill Clinton</a>. President <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> reestablished the Council by executive order in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-Trump_NSC_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trump_NSC-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>International aspects of US space policy may involve diplomatic negotiation with other countries, such as the 1967 <a href="/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty" title="Outer Space Treaty">Outer Space Treaty</a>. In these cases, the President negotiates and signs the treaty on behalf of the United States according to his <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Constitution">constitutional</a> authority, then presents it to the Congress for ratification.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation">Legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_policy_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Once a request is submitted, the Congress exercises due diligence to approve the policy and authorize a budgetary expenditure for its implementation. In support of this, civilian policies are reviewed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_Science_Subcommittee_on_Space_and_Aeronautics" title="United States House Science Subcommittee on Space and Aeronautics">House Subcommittee on Space and Aeronautics</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Commerce_Subcommittee_on_Science_and_Space" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Senate Commerce Subcommittee on Science and Space">Senate Subcommittee on Science and Space</a>. These committees may exercise oversight of NASA's implementation of established space policies, monitoring progress of large space programs such as the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a>, and in special cases such as serious space accidents like the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_1" title="Apollo 1">Apollo 1</a> fire, where Congress oversees NASA's investigation of the accident.
</p><p>Military policies are reviewed and overseen by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_Armed_Services_Subcommittee_on_Strategic_Forces" title="United States House Armed Services Subcommittee on Strategic Forces">House Subcommittee on Strategic Forces</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Armed_Services_Subcommittee_on_Strategic_Forces" title="United States Senate Armed Services Subcommittee on Strategic Forces">Senate Subcommittee on Strategic Forces</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_Permanent_Select_Committee_on_Intelligence" title="United States House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence">House Permanent Select Committee on Intelligence</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Select_Committee_on_Intelligence" title="United States Senate Select Committee on Intelligence">Senate Select Committee on Intelligence</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate_Committee_on_Foreign_Relations" title="United States Senate Committee on Foreign Relations">Senate Foreign Relations Committee</a> conducts hearings on proposed space treaties, and the various appropriations committees have power over the budgets for space-related agencies.  Space policy efforts are supported by Congressional agencies such as the <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Research_Service" title="Congressional Research Service">Congressional Research Service</a> and, until it was disbanded in 1995, the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Technology_Assessment" title="Office of Technology Assessment">Office of Technology Assessment</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Budget_Office" title="Congressional Budget Office">Congressional Budget Office</a> and <a href="/wiki/Government_Accountability_Office" title="Government Accountability Office">Government Accountability Office</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Congress' final space policy product is, in the case of domestic policy a bill explicitly stating the policy objectives and the budget appropriation for their implementation to be submitted to the President for signature into law, or else a ratified treaty with other nations.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Implementation">Implementation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_policy_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Implementation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Civilian space activities have traditionally been implemented exclusively by NASA, but the nation is transitioning into a model where more activities are implemented by private companies under NASA's advisement and launch site support. In addition, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Commerce" title="United States Department of Commerce">Department of Commerce</a>'s <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration</a> operates various services with space components, such as the <a href="/wiki/Landsat_program" title="Landsat program">Landsat program</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Goldman,_pp._91–97_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Goldman,_pp._91–97-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Military space activities are implemented by the <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Space_Command" title="Air Force Space Command">Air Force Space Command</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naval_Space_Command" title="Naval Space Command">Naval Space Command</a>, and Army <a href="/wiki/Space_and_Missile_Defense_Command" class="mw-redirect" title="Space and Missile Defense Command">Space and Missile Defense Command</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Licensing">Licensing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_policy_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Licensing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Any activities "which are intended to conduct in the United States a launch of a launch vehicle, operation of a launch or re-entry site, re-entry of a re-entry vehicle"  needs a license to operate in outer space. This license needs to by applied for by "any citizen of or entity organized under the laws of the United States, as well as other entities, as defined by space-related regulations, which are intended to conduct in the United States… should obtain a license form the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_Transportation" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of Transportation">Secretary of Transportation</a>" compliance is monitored by the <a href="/wiki/FAA" class="mw-redirect" title="FAA">FAA</a>, <a href="/wiki/FCC" class="mw-redirect" title="FCC">FCC</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_Commerce" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of Commerce">Secretary of Commerce</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Funding for space programs occurs through the <a href="/wiki/United_States_budget_process" title="United States budget process">federal budget process</a>, where it is mainly considered to be part of <a href="/wiki/Science_policy_of_the_United_States" title="Science policy of the United States">the nation's science policy</a>.  In the <a href="/wiki/Obama_administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Obama administration">Obama administration</a>'s budget request for <a href="/wiki/Fiscal_year" title="Fiscal year">fiscal year</a> 2011, NASA would receive $11.0 billion, out of a total research and development budget of $148.1 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-aaasbudget_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aaasbudget-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  Other space activities are funded out of the research and development budget of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">Department of Defense</a>, and from the budgets of the other regulatory agencies involved with space issues.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_law">International law</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_policy_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: International law">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The United States is a party to four of the five <a href="/wiki/Space_law" title="Space law">space law</a> treaties ratified by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Committee_on_the_Peaceful_Uses_of_Outer_Space" title="United Nations Committee on the Peaceful Uses of Outer Space">United Nations Committee on the Peaceful Uses of Outer Space</a>.  The United States has ratified the <a href="/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty" title="Outer Space Treaty">Outer Space Treaty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rescue_Agreement" title="Rescue Agreement">Rescue Agreement</a>, <a href="/wiki/Space_Liability_Convention" title="Space Liability Convention">Space Liability Convention</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Registration_Convention" title="Registration Convention">Registration Convention</a>, but not the <a href="/wiki/Moon_Treaty" title="Moon Treaty">Moon Treaty</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The five treaties and agreements of international space law cover "non-appropriation of outer space by any one country, arms control, the freedom of exploration, liability for damage caused by space objects, the safety and rescue of spacecraft and astronauts, the prevention of harmful interference with space activities and the environment, the notification and registration of space activities, scientific investigation and the exploitation of natural resources in outer space and the settlement of disputes."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> adopted five declarations and legal principles which encourage exercising the international laws, as well as unified communication between countries. The five declarations and principles are:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>The Declaration of Legal Principles Governing the Activities of States in the Exploration and Uses of Outer Space</i> (1963)</dd>
<dd>All space exploration will be done with good intentions and is equally open to all States that comply with international law. No one nation may claim ownership of outer space or any celestial body. Activities carried out in space must abide by the international law and the nations undergoing these said activities must accept responsibility for the governmental or non-governmental agency involved. Objects launched into space are subject to their nation of belonging, including people. Objects, parts, and components discovered outside the jurisdiction of a nation will be returned upon identification. If a nation launches an object into space, they are responsible for any damages that occur internationally.</dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>The Principles Governing the Use by States of Artificial Earth Satellites for International Direct Television Broadcasting</i> (1982)</dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>The Principles Relating to Remote Sensing of the Earth from Outer Space</i> (1986)</dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>The Principles Relevant to the Use of Nuclear Power Sources in Outer Space</i> (1992)</dd></dl>
<dl><dd><i>The Declaration on International Cooperation in the Exploration and Use of Outer Space for the Benefit and in the Interest of All States, Taking into Particular Account the Needs of Developing Countries</i> (1996)</dd></dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_policy_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eisenhower_administration">Eisenhower administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_policy_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Eisenhower administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Eisenhower_administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Eisenhower administration">President Dwight Eisenhower</a> was skeptical about <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> but sought to advance the commercial and military applications of satellite technology.  Prior to the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>'s launch of <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a>, Eisenhower had already authorized <a href="/wiki/Project_Vanguard" title="Project Vanguard">Project Vanguard</a>, a scientific satellite program associated with the <a href="/wiki/International_Geophysical_Year" title="International Geophysical Year">International Geophysical Year</a>.  As a supporter of <a href="/wiki/Small_government" title="Small government">small government</a>, he sought to avoid a <a href="/wiki/Space_race" class="mw-redirect" title="Space race">space race</a> which would require an expensive bureaucracy to conduct, and was surprised by, and sought to downplay, the <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_crisis" title="Sputnik crisis">public response to the Soviet launch of Sputnik</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-presidency_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-presidency-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In an effort to prevent similar technological surprises by the Soviets, Eisenhower authorized the creation in 1958 of the <a href="/wiki/Defense_Advanced_Research_Projects_Agency" class="mw-redirect" title="Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency">Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency</a> (DARPA), responsible for the development of advanced military technologies.<sup id="cite_ref-Commission2008_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Commission2008-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Space programs such as the <a href="/wiki/Explorers_program" class="mw-redirect" title="Explorers program">Explorer</a> satellite were proposed by the <a href="/wiki/Army_Ballistic_Missile_Agency" title="Army Ballistic Missile Agency">Army Ballistic Missile Agency</a> (ABMA), but Eisenhower, seeking to avoid giving the US space program the militaristic image Americans had of the Soviet program, had rejected Explorer in favor of the Vanguard, but after numerous embarrassing Vanguard failures, was forced to give the go-ahead to the Army's launch
</p><p>Later in 1958, Eisenhower asked Congress to create an agency for civilian control of non-military space activities. At the suggestion of Eisenhower's science advisor <a href="/wiki/James_R._Killian" class="mw-redirect" title="James R. Killian">James R. Killian</a>, the drafted bill called for creation of the new agency out of the <a href="/wiki/National_Advisory_Committee_for_Aeronautics" title="National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics">National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics</a>.  The result was the  <a href="/wiki/National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Act" title="National Aeronautics and Space Act">National Aeronautics and Space Act</a> passed in July 1958, which created the <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">National Aeronautics and Space Administration</a> (NASA). Eisenhower appointed <a href="/wiki/T._Keith_Glennan" title="T. Keith Glennan">T. Keith Glennan</a> as NASA's first Administrator, with the last NACA Director <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Latimer_Dryden" title="Hugh Latimer Dryden">Hugh Dryden</a> serving as his Deputy.
</p><p>NASA as created in the act passed by Congress was substantially stronger than the Eisenhower administration's original proposal.  NASA took over the space technology research started by DARPA.<sup id="cite_ref-presidency_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-presidency-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> NASA also took over the US manned satellite program, <a href="/wiki/Man_In_Space_Soonest" class="mw-redirect" title="Man In Space Soonest">Man In Space Soonest</a>, from the Air Force, as <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Project Mercury</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kennedy_administration">Kennedy administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_policy_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Kennedy administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Early in <a href="/wiki/Kennedy_administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Kennedy administration">John F. Kennedy's presidency</a>, he was inclined to dismantle plans for the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a>, which he had opposed as a senator, but postponed any decision out of deference to his vice president whom he had appointed chairman of the <a href="/wiki/National_Space_Council" title="National Space Council">National Advisory Space Council</a><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and who strongly supported NASA due to its Texas location.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> This changed with his January 1961 State of the Union address, when he suggested international cooperation in space.
</p><p>In response to the flight of <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">Yuri Gagarin</a> as the first man in space, Kennedy in 1961 committed the United States to <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing" title="Moon landing">landing a man on the Moon</a> by the end of the decade.  At the time, the administration believed that the Soviet Union would be able to land a man on the Moon by 1967, and Kennedy saw an American Moon landing as critical to the nation's global prestige and status.  His pick for NASA administrator, <a href="/wiki/James_E._Webb" title="James E. Webb">James E. Webb</a>, however pursued a broader program incorporating space applications such as weather and communications satellites.  During this time the Department of Defense pursued military space applications such as the <a href="/wiki/Boeing_X-20_Dyna-Soar" title="Boeing X-20 Dyna-Soar">Dyna-Soar</a> spaceplane program and the <a href="/wiki/Manned_Orbiting_Laboratory" title="Manned Orbiting Laboratory">Manned Orbiting Laboratory</a>.  Kennedy also had elevated the status of the National Advisory Space Council by assigning the Vice President as its chair.<sup id="cite_ref-presidency_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-presidency-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Johnson_administration">Johnson administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_policy_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Johnson administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson_Administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Lyndon B. Johnson Administration">President Lyndon Johnson</a> was committed to space efforts, and as <a href="/wiki/Senate_majority_leader" class="mw-redirect" title="Senate majority leader">Senate majority leader</a> and Vice President, he had contributed much to setting up the organizational infrastructure for the space program.  However, the costs of the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> and the programs of the <a href="/wiki/Great_Society" title="Great Society">Great Society</a> forced cuts to NASA's budget as early as 1965.  However, the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">Apollo 8</a> mission carrying the first men into lunar orbit occurred just before the end of his term in 1968.<sup id="cite_ref-presidency_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-presidency-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nixon_administration">Nixon administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_policy_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Nixon administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:President_Nixon_welcomes_the_Apollo_11_astronauts_aboard_the_U.S.S._Hornet.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/President_Nixon_welcomes_the_Apollo_11_astronauts_aboard_the_U.S.S._Hornet.jpg/220px-President_Nixon_welcomes_the_Apollo_11_astronauts_aboard_the_U.S.S._Hornet.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/President_Nixon_welcomes_the_Apollo_11_astronauts_aboard_the_U.S.S._Hornet.jpg/330px-President_Nixon_welcomes_the_Apollo_11_astronauts_aboard_the_U.S.S._Hornet.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/President_Nixon_welcomes_the_Apollo_11_astronauts_aboard_the_U.S.S._Hornet.jpg/440px-President_Nixon_welcomes_the_Apollo_11_astronauts_aboard_the_U.S.S._Hornet.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6862" data-file-height="6862" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:President_Nixon_welcomes_the_Apollo_11_astronauts_aboard_the_U.S.S._Hornet.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>President Nixon visits the <i><a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">astronauts</a> in <a href="/wiki/Quarantine" title="Quarantine">quarantine</a> after observing their landing in the ocean from the deck of the aircraft carrier <i><a href="/wiki/USS_Hornet_(CV-12)" title="USS Hornet (CV-12)">USS Hornet</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a>, the first Moon landing, occurred early in <a href="/wiki/Nixon_administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Nixon administration">Richard Nixon's presidency</a>, but NASA's budget continued to decline and three of the planned Apollo Moon landings <a href="/wiki/Canceled_Apollo_missions" title="Canceled Apollo missions">were cancelled</a>.  The Nixon administration approved the beginning of the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_program" title="Space Shuttle program">Space Shuttle program</a>, but did not support funding of other projects such as a <a href="/wiki/Mars_landing" title="Mars landing">Mars landing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Colonization_of_the_Moon" title="Colonization of the Moon">colonization of the Moon</a>, or a permanent <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">space station</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-presidency_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-presidency-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On January 5, 1972, Nixon approved the development of <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA's</a> <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_program" title="Space Shuttle program">Space Shuttle program</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-NixonSpaceShuttle_19720105_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NixonSpaceShuttle_19720105-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> a decision that profoundly influenced American efforts to explore and develop space for several decades thereafter. Under the Nixon administration, however, NASA's budget declined.<sup id="cite_ref-NASA_Butrica_p256_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASA_Butrica_p256-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> NASA Administrator <a href="/wiki/Thomas_O._Paine" title="Thomas O. Paine">Thomas O. Paine</a> was drawing up ambitious plans for the establishment of a permanent base on the <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">Moon</a> by the end of the 1970s and the launch of a manned expedition to <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">Mars</a> as early as 1981. Nixon, however, rejected this proposal.<sup id="cite_ref-SpaceReview_Handlin_20051128_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SpaceReview_Handlin_20051128-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  On May 24, 1972, Nixon approved a five-year cooperative program between NASA and the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_space_program" title="Soviet space program">Soviet space program</a>, which would culminate in the <a href="/wiki/Apollo-Soyuz_Test_Project" class="mw-redirect" title="Apollo-Soyuz Test Project">Apollo-Soyuz Test Project</a>, a joint-mission of an American <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo</a> and a Soviet <a href="/wiki/Soyuz_program" class="mw-redirect" title="Soyuz program">Soyuz</a> spacecraft, during Gerald Ford's presidency in 1975.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ford_administration">Ford administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_policy_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Ford administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Space policy had little momentum during the <a href="/wiki/Ford_administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Ford administration">presidency of Gerald Ford</a>.  NASA funding improved somewhat, the <a href="/wiki/Apollo%E2%80%93Soyuz_Test_Project" title="Apollo–Soyuz Test Project">Apollo–Soyuz Test Project</a> occurred and the Shuttle program continued, and the <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Science_and_Technology_Policy" title="Office of Science and Technology Policy">Office of Science and Technology Policy</a> was formed.<sup id="cite_ref-presidency_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-presidency-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Carter_administration">Carter administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_policy_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Carter administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Carter_administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Carter administration">Jimmy Carter administration</a> was also fairly inactive on space issues, stating that it was "neither feasible nor necessary" to commit to an Apollo-style space program, and his space policy included only limited, short-range goals.<sup id="cite_ref-presidency_11-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-presidency-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  With regard to military space policy, the Carter space policy stated, without much specification in the unclassified version, that "The United States will pursue Activities in space in support of its right of self-defense."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reagan_administration">Reagan administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_policy_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Reagan administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:C13571-8a.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/C13571-8a.jpg/220px-C13571-8a.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/C13571-8a.jpg/330px-C13571-8a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/C13571-8a.jpg/440px-C13571-8a.jpg 2x" data-file-width="750" data-file-height="512" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:C13571-8a.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>President Reagan delivering the March 23, 1983 speech initiating the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Defense_Initiative" title="Strategic Defense Initiative">Strategic Defense Initiative</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The first flight of the Space Shuttle occurred in April 1981, early in <a href="/wiki/Reagan_administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Reagan administration">President Ronald Reagan</a>'s first term.  Reagan in 1982 announced a renewed active space effort, which included initiatives such as privatization of the <a href="/wiki/Landsat_program" title="Landsat program">Landsat program</a>, a new commercialization policy for NASA, the construction of <a href="/wiki/Space_Station_Freedom" title="Space Station Freedom">Space Station Freedom</a>, and the military <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Defense_Initiative" title="Strategic Defense Initiative">Strategic Defense Initiative</a>.  Late in his term as president, Reagan sought to increase NASA's budget by 30 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-presidency_11-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-presidency-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  However, many of these initiatives would not be completed as planned.
</p><p>The January 1986 <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Challenger_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Challenger disaster">Space Shuttle Challenger disaster</a> led to the <a href="/wiki/Rogers_Commission_Report" title="Rogers Commission Report">Rogers Commission Report</a> on the causes of the disaster, and the <a href="/wiki/Thomas_O._Paine#National_Commission_on_Space" title="Thomas O. Paine">National Commission on Space</a> report and <a href="/wiki/Ride_Report" title="Ride Report">Ride Report</a> on the future of the national space program.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="George_H._W._Bush_administration">George H. W. Bush administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_policy_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: George H. W. Bush administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Presidency_of_George_H._W._Bush" title="Presidency of George H. W. Bush">President George H. W. Bush</a> continued to support space development, announcing the bold <a href="/wiki/Space_Exploration_Initiative" title="Space Exploration Initiative">Space Exploration Initiative</a>, and ordering a 20 percent increase in NASA's budget in a tight budget era.<sup id="cite_ref-presidency_11-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-presidency-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The Bush administration also commissioned another report on the future of NASA, the <a href="/wiki/Advisory_Committee_on_the_Future_of_the_United_States_Space_Program" class="mw-redirect" title="Advisory Committee on the Future of the United States Space Program">Advisory Committee on the Future of the United States Space Program</a>, also known as the Augustine Report.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Clinton_administration">Clinton administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_policy_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Clinton administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Clinton_administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Clinton administration">Clinton administration</a>, Space Shuttle flights continued, and the construction of the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> began.
</p><p>The Clinton administration's National Space Policy (Presidential Decision Directive/NSC-49/NSTC-8) was released on September 14, 1996.<sup id="cite_ref-2006policy_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2006policy-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  Clinton's top goals were to "enhance knowledge of the Earth, the solar system and the universe through human and robotic exploration" and to "strengthen and maintain the <a href="/wiki/National_security_of_the_United_States" title="National security of the United States">national security of the United States</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-wp_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wp-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  The Clinton space policy, like the space policies of Carter and Reagan, also stated that "The United States will conduct those space activities necessary for national security." These activities included "providing support for the United States' inherent right of self-defense and our defense commitments to allies and friends; deterring, warning, and if necessary, defending against enemy attack; assuring that hostile forces cannot prevent our own use of space; and countering, if necessary, space systems and services used for hostile purposes."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  The Clinton policy also said the United States would develop and operate "space control capabilities to ensure freedom of action in space" only when such steps would be "consistent with treaty obligations."<sup id="cite_ref-wp_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wp-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="George_W._Bush_administration">George W. Bush administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_policy_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: George W. Bush administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ares_I-X_launch_08.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Ares_I-X_launch_08.jpg/150px-Ares_I-X_launch_08.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Ares_I-X_launch_08.jpg/225px-Ares_I-X_launch_08.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Ares_I-X_launch_08.jpg/300px-Ares_I-X_launch_08.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1996" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ares_I-X_launch_08.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The launch of the <a href="/wiki/Ares_I-X" title="Ares I-X">Ares I-X</a> prototype on October 28, 2009 was the only flight performed under the Bush administration's <a href="/wiki/Constellation_program" title="Constellation program">Constellation program</a>.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Space_policy_of_the_George_W._Bush_administration" title="Space policy of the George W. Bush administration">Space policy of the George W. Bush administration</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia_disaster" title="Space Shuttle Columbia disaster">Space Shuttle Columbia disaster</a> occurred early in George W. Bush's term, leading to the report of the <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Accident_Investigation_Board" title="Columbia Accident Investigation Board">Columbia Accident Investigation Board</a> being released in August 2003.  The Vision for Space Exploration, announced on January 14, 2004 by President <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>, was seen as a response to the Columbia disaster and the general state of <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a> at <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a>, as well as a way to regain public enthusiasm for <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">space exploration</a>.  The Vision for Space Exploration sought to implement a sustained and affordable human and robotic program to explore the solar system and beyond; extend human presence across the solar system, starting with a human return to the Moon by the year 2020, in preparation for human exploration of Mars and other destinations; develop the innovative technologies, knowledge, and infrastructures both to explore and to support decisions about the destinations for human exploration; and to promote international and commercial participation in exploration to further U.S. scientific, security, and economic interests<sup id="cite_ref-NASAdocument_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASAdocument-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>To this end, the <a href="/wiki/President%27s_Commission_on_Implementation_of_United_States_Space_Exploration_Policy" title="President&#39;s Commission on Implementation of United States Space Exploration Policy">President's Commission on Implementation of United States Space Exploration Policy</a> was formed by President Bush on January 27, 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Its final report was submitted on June 4, 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>  This led to the NASA <a href="/wiki/Exploration_Systems_Architecture_Study" title="Exploration Systems Architecture Study">Exploration Systems Architecture Study</a> in mid-2005, which developed technical plans for carrying out the programs specified in the Vision for Space Exploration.  This led to the beginning of execution of <a href="/wiki/Constellation_program" title="Constellation program">Constellation program</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Orion_(Constellation_program)" class="mw-redirect" title="Orion (Constellation program)">Orion</a> crew module, the <a href="/wiki/Altair_(spacecraft)" title="Altair (spacecraft)">Altair</a> lunar lander, and the <a href="/wiki/Ares_I" title="Ares I">Ares I</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ares_V" title="Ares V">Ares V</a> rockets.  The <a href="/wiki/Ares_I-X" title="Ares I-X">Ares I-X</a> mission, a test launch of a prototype Ares I rocket, was successfully completed in October 2009.
</p><p>A new National Space Policy was released on August 31, 2006 that established overarching national policy that governs the conduct of U.S. space activities.  The document, the first full revision of overall space policy in 10 years, emphasized security issues, encouraged private enterprise in space, and characterized the role of U.S. space diplomacy largely in terms of persuading other nations to support U.S. policy.  The <a href="/wiki/United_States_National_Security_Council" title="United States National Security Council">United States National Security Council</a> said in written comments that an update was needed to "reflect the fact that space has become an even more important component of U.S. <a href="/wiki/Economic_security" title="Economic security">Economic security</a>, <a href="/wiki/National_security" title="National security">National security</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Homeland_security" title="Homeland security">homeland security</a>."  The Bush policy accepted current international agreements, but stated that it "rejects any limitations on the fundamental right of the United States to operate in and acquire data from space,"<sup id="cite_ref-BroadChang2010_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BroadChang2010-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> and that "The United States will oppose the development of new legal regimes or other restrictions that seek to prohibit or limit U.S. access to or use of space."<sup id="cite_ref-wp_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wp-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Space_policy_of_the_Barack_Obama_administration" title="Space policy of the Barack Obama administration">Space policy of the Barack Obama administration</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Obama_administration" class="mw-redirect" title="Obama administration">Obama administration</a> commissioned the <a href="/wiki/Review_of_United_States_Human_Space_Flight_Plans_Committee" title="Review of United States Human Space Flight Plans Committee">Review of United States Human Space Flight Plans Committee</a> in 2009 to review the <a href="/wiki/Human_spaceflight" title="Human spaceflight">human spaceflight</a> plans of the United States and to ensure the nation is on "a vigorous and sustainable path to achieving its boldest aspirations in space," covering human spaceflight options after the time <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">NASA</a> plans to retire the <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ostp090507_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ostp090507-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On April 15, 2010, <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama_space_policy_speech_at_Kennedy_Space_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Barack Obama space policy speech at Kennedy Space Center">President Obama spoke at the Kennedy Space Center</a> announcing the administration's plans for NASA.  None of the 3 plans outlined in the Committee's final report<sup id="cite_ref-Committee_Final_Report_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Committee_Final_Report-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> were completely selected.  The President cancelled the <a href="/wiki/Constellation_program" title="Constellation program">Constellation program</a> and rejected immediate plans to return to the Moon on the premise that the current plan had become nonviable.  He instead promised $6 billion in additional funding and called for development of a new heavy lift rocket program to be ready for construction by 2015 with manned missions to Mars orbit by the mid-2030s.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>  The Obama administration released its new formal space policy on June 28, 2010, in which it also reversed the Bush policy's rejection of international agreements to curb the militarization of space, saying that it would "consider proposals and concepts for arms control measures if they are equitable, effectively verifiable and enhance the national security of the United States and its allies."<sup id="cite_ref-BroadChang2010_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BroadChang2010-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/NASA_Authorization_Act_of_2010" title="NASA Authorization Act of 2010">NASA Authorization Act of 2010</a>, passed on October 11, 2010, enacted many of these space policy goals.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trump_administration">Trump administration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Space_policy_of_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Trump administration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Space_policy_of_the_Donald_Trump_administration" title="Space policy of the Donald Trump administration">Space policy of the Donald Trump administration</a></div>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:National_Space_Council_Executive_Order_(NHQ201706300004).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/National_Space_Council_Executive_Order_%28NHQ201706300004%29.jpg/220px-National_Space_Council_Executive_Order_%28NHQ201706300004%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/National_Space_Council_Executive_Order_%28NHQ201706300004%29.jpg/330px-National_Space_Council_Executive_Order_%28NHQ201706300004%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/National_Space_Council_Executive_Order_%28NHQ201706300004%29.jpg/440px-National_Space_Council_Executive_Order_%28NHQ201706300004%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4836" data-file-height="3219" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:National_Space_Council_Executive_Order_(NHQ201706300004).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>President Trump signs an executive order re-establishing the National Space Council, with astronauts Dave Wolf and Al Drew, and Apollo 11 astronaut Buzz Aldrin (left-to-right) looking on.</div></div></div>

<p>On June 30, 2017, President <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> signed an executive order to re-establish the <a href="/wiki/National_Space_Council" title="National Space Council">National Space Council</a>, chaired by Vice President <a href="/wiki/Mike_Pence" title="Mike Pence">Mike Pence</a>.  The Trump administration's first budget request keeps Obama-era human spaceflight programs in place: commercial spacecraft to ferry astronauts to and from the <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a>, the government-owned <a href="/wiki/Space_Launch_System" title="Space Launch System">Space Launch System</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Orion_crew_capsule" class="mw-redirect" title="Orion crew capsule">Orion crew capsule</a> for deep space missions, while reducing Earth science research and calling for the elimination of NASA's education office.<sup id="cite_ref-Trump_NSC_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Trump_NSC-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On December 11, 2017, President Trump signed Space Policy Directive 1, a change in national space policy that provides for a U.S.-led, integrated program with private sector partners for a human return to the Moon, followed by missions to Mars and beyond.   The policy calls for the NASA administrator to "lead an innovative and sustainable program of exploration with commercial and international partners to enable human expansion across the solar system and to bring back to Earth new knowledge and opportunities." The effort will more effectively organize government, private industry, and international efforts toward returning humans on the Moon, and will lay the foundation that will eventually enable human exploration of Mars.
</p><p>The President stated "The directive I am signing today will refocus America's space program on human exploration and discovery." "It marks a first step in returning American astronauts to the Moon for the first time since 1972, for long-term exploration and use.  This time, we will not only plant our flag and leave our footprints – we will establish a foundation for an eventual mission to Mars, and perhaps someday, to many worlds beyond."
</p><p>"Under President Trump's leadership, America will lead in space once again on all fronts," said Vice President Pence. "As the President has said, space is the 'next great American frontier' – and it is our duty – and our destiny – to settle that frontier with American leadership, courage, and values. The signing of this new directive is yet another promise kept by President Trump."
</p><p>Among other dignitaries on hand for the signing, were NASA astronauts Sen. <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Schmitt" title="Harrison Schmitt">Harrison "Jack" Schmitt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">Buzz Aldrin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peggy_Whitson" title="Peggy Whitson">Peggy Whitson</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Christina_Hammock" class="mw-redirect" title="Christina Hammock">Christina Koch</a>. Schmitt landed on the Moon 45 years to the minute that the policy directive was signed as part of NASA's Apollo 17 mission, and is the most recent living person to have set foot on our lunar neighbor. Aldrin was the second person to walk on the Moon during the Apollo 11 mission. Whitson spoke to the president from space in April aboard the International Space Station and while flying back home after breaking the record for most time in space by a U.S. astronaut in September. Koch is a member of NASA's astronaut class of 2013.
</p>
