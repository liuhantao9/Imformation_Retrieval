<p>A <b>courtesy name</b> (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hans">字</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">zì</span></i>; literally&#58; 'character'), also known as a <b>style name</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is a name bestowed upon one at adulthood in addition to one's given name.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This practice is a tradition in the <a href="/wiki/Sinosphere" class="mw-redirect" title="Sinosphere">Sinosphere</a>, including <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In pre-modern China, the <i>zi</i> would replace a man's given name when he turned twenty, as a symbol of adulthood and respect.<sup id="cite_ref-Rites_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rites-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="Is it so in every country mentioned above? (November 2013)">specify</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It could be given either by the parents or by the first personal teacher on the first day of <span class="clarify-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">family school</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Clarify" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="This passage needs to be better explained. (March 2019)">clarify</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. Women might adopt a <i>zi</i> in place of their given name upon marriage.<sup id="cite_ref-Rites_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rites-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> One also may adopt a self-chosen courtesy name.
</p><p>In China, the popularity of the custom has declined to a large extent since the <a href="/wiki/May_Fourth_Movement" title="May Fourth Movement">May Fourth Movement</a> in 1919.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="I suppose it refers to China only. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>A courtesy name is not to be confused with an <a href="/wiki/Art_name" title="Art name">art name</a> (<i>hào</i>, <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hant">號</span>, <a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">Korean</a>: <span lang="ko" title="Korean language text">호</span>), another frequently mentioned term for an alternative name in Asian culture-based context. An art name is usually associated with art and is more of a <a href="/wiki/Pen_name" title="Pen name">pen name</a> or a <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a> that is more spontaneous, compared to a courtesy name.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Courtesy_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Courtesy_name&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p>The <i>zì</i>, sometimes called the <i>biǎozì</i>（<span lang="zh-Hani" title="Chinese language text">表字</span>）or "courtesy name", is a name traditionally given to Chinese men at the age of 20, marking their <a href="/wiki/Coming_of_age" title="Coming of age">coming of age</a>. It was sometimes given to women upon marriage. The practice is no longer common in modern Chinese society. According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Rites" title="Book of Rites">Book of Rites</a></i>, after a man reaches adulthood, it is disrespectful for others of the same generation to address him by his given name, or <i>míng</i>. Thus, the given name was reserved for oneself and one's elders, whereas the <b>zì</b> would be used by adults of the same generation to refer to one another on formal occasions or in writing; hence the term "courtesy name".
</p><p>The <i>zì</i> is mostly <a href="/wiki/Disyllabic" class="mw-redirect" title="Disyllabic">disyllabic</a>, consisting of two <a href="/wiki/Chinese_character" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese character">Chinese characters</a>, and is often based on the meaning of the <i>míng</i> or <a href="/wiki/Chinese_given_name" title="Chinese given name">given name</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Chiang_Kai-shek" title="Chiang Kai-shek">Chiang Kai-shek</a>'s <i>zì</i> (<span lang="zh-Hani" title="Chinese language text">介石</span>, romanized as Kai-shek) and <i>ming</i> (<span lang="zh-Hani" title="Chinese language text">中正</span>, <a href="/wiki/Romanized" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanized">romanized</a> as Chung-cheng) are both from the <i>yù</i> hexagram of <a href="/wiki/I_Ching" title="I Ching">I Ching</a>. 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Yan_Zhitui" title="Yan Zhitui">Yan Zhitui</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Qi" title="Northern Qi">Northern Qi</a> dynasty asserted that whereas the purpose of the <i>míng</i> was to distinguish one person from another, the <i>zì</i> should express the bearer's moral integrity.
</p><p>Another way to form a <i>zì</i> is to use the homophonic character <i>zǐ</i> (<span lang="zh-Hani" title="Chinese language text">子</span>) – a respectful title for a man – as the first character of the disyllabic <i>zì</i>. Thus, for example, <a href="/wiki/Gongsun_Qiao" class="mw-redirect" title="Gongsun Qiao">Gongsun Qiao</a>'s <i>zì</i> was Zǐchǎn (<span lang="zh-Hani" title="Chinese language text">子產</span>), and <a href="/wiki/Du_Fu" title="Du Fu">Du Fu</a>'s: Zǐměi (<span lang="zh-Hani" title="Chinese language text">子美</span>).
</p><p>It is also common to construct a <i>zì</i> by using as the first character one which expresses the bearer's birth order among male siblings in his family. Thus <a href="/wiki/Confucius" title="Confucius">Confucius</a>, whose name was Kǒng Qiū (<span lang="zh-Hani" title="Chinese language text">孔丘</span>), was given the <i>zì</i> Zhòngní (<span lang="zh-Hani" title="Chinese language text">仲尼</span>), where the first character <i>zhòng</i> indicates that he was the second son born into his family. The characters commonly used are bó (<span lang="zh-Hani" title="Chinese language text">伯</span>) for the first, zhòng (<span lang="zh-Hani" title="Chinese language text">仲</span>) for the second, shū (<span lang="zh-Hani" title="Chinese language text">叔</span>) for the third, and jì (<span lang="zh-Hani" title="Chinese language text">季</span>) typically for the youngest, if the family consists of more than three sons. General <a href="/wiki/Sun_Jian" title="Sun Jian">Sun Jian</a>'s four sons, for instance, were <a href="/wiki/Sun_Ce" title="Sun Ce">Sun Ce</a> (<span lang="zh-Hani" title="Chinese language text">伯符</span>, Bófú), <a href="/wiki/Sun_Quan" title="Sun Quan">Sun Quan</a> (<span lang="zh-Hani" title="Chinese language text">仲謀</span>, Zhòngmóu), <a href="/wiki/Sun_Yi" title="Sun Yi">Sun Yi</a> (<span lang="zh-Hani" title="Chinese language text">叔弼</span>, Shūbì) and <a href="/wiki/Sun_Kuang" title="Sun Kuang">Sun Kuang</a> (<span lang="zh-Hani" title="Chinese language text">季佐</span>, Jìzuǒ).
</p><p>The use of <i>zì</i> began during the <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang dynasty</a>, and slowly developed into a system which became most widespread during the succeeding <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou dynasty</a>. During this period, women were also given <i>zì</i>. The <i>zì</i> given to a woman was generally composed of a character indicating her birth order among female siblings and her surname. For example, Mèng Jiāng (<span lang="zh-Hani" title="Chinese language text">孟姜</span>) was the eldest daughter in the Jiāng family.
</p><p>Prior to the twentieth century, <a href="/wiki/Sinicization" title="Sinicization">sinicized</a> <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Koreans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnamese</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> were also referred to by their <i>zì</i>. The practice was also adopted by some <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">Mongols</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manchus" class="mw-redirect" title="Manchus">Manchus</a> after the Qing conquest of China.
</p>
