	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Name blending</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Name blending</b> or <b>meshing</b> is the practice of combining two existing names to form a new name.<sup id="cite_ref-Barnett_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barnett-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> An example is the combination of the <a href="/wiki/Surnames" class="mw-redirect" title="Surnames">surnames</a> Dresser and McLoughlin to form the new surname of game designer <a href="/wiki/Clay_Dreslough" title="Clay Dreslough">Clay Dreslough</a>. It is most commonly performed upon <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a>. According to Western tradition, the wife normally adopts the husband's surname upon marriage. Name blending is an alternative practice that attempts to assign equal cultural value to each partner's surname. In November 2012 it was reported that 800 couples in the United Kingdom had opted to blend their surnames thus far that year, primarily among "younger couples in their twenties or early thirties", with this being a leading reason for the issuance of <a href="/wiki/Deed_Polls" class="mw-redirect" title="Deed Polls">Deed Polls</a> to change names.<sup id="cite_ref-Barnett_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barnett-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the late 1990s, it has also become common for <a href="/wiki/Celebrity" title="Celebrity">celebrity</a> couples to be given blended names in the media, usually made by combining elements of the <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> of the people involved.<sup id="cite_ref-Clements_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clements-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This practice has been adopted by <a href="/wiki/Shipping_(fandom)" title="Shipping (fandom)">shippers</a> within <a href="/wiki/Fandom" title="Fandom">fandoms</a> to describe relationships between fictional characters.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Name blending can also occur unintentionally when a double name created in one generation becomes combined and condensed in later generations.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Motivation">Motivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_blending&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Motivation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many reasons are given for name-blending.
</p>
<ul><li>Couples may choose to adopt a blended name to enter into marriage "with a completely new start without any history being tied to their surname". Name blending confers the same surname upon both spouses. This allows the family to conform to the expectation that the family (and any children) will all share the same name, and avoid confusion that can arise when spouses retain differing surnames.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Barnett_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barnett-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Name blending avoids the patriarchal practice of having the wife take the husband's name. In doing so, it is considered by many to be an extension of the <a href="/wiki/Feminist_movement" title="Feminist movement">feminist movement</a>. <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Name blending avoids hyphenation and the complications associated with having a <a href="/wiki/Double-barreled_surname" class="mw-redirect" title="Double-barreled surname">double-barreled surname</a> or other form of combined name that may be too long for use in some circumstances (for example, many computer databases limit last names to 16 characters).<sup id="cite_ref-Tuhus-Dubrow_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuhus-Dubrow-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Name blending often creates a unique surname. With over 1 billion internet users, having a unique last name can make it easier for people to find an individual using search engines. It also increases the chance that the name will be available as a username in e-mail systems and online communities.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Name blending also provides an alternative for <a href="/wiki/Same-sex_marriage" title="Same-sex marriage">same-sex marriages</a>, where there are not longstanding traditions regarding the taking of one participants surname by the other.<sup id="cite_ref-Tuhus-Dubrow_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tuhus-Dubrow-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>In the case of celebrity couples, where the names are chosen by the media (or arise from the public) rather than reflecting a choice by the couple, it has been suggested that the assignment of a nickname makes fans feel closer to the couple.<sup id="cite_ref-Clements_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clements-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The popularity of celebrity <a href="/wiki/Supercouple" title="Supercouple">supercouple</a> <a href="/wiki/Ben_Affleck" title="Ben Affleck">Ben Affleck</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jennifer_Lopez" title="Jennifer Lopez">Jennifer Lopez</a> from 2002 to 2004 resulted in their being known by the <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">portmanteau</a> "Bennifer" (for Ben and Jennifer) to the media, as well as to fans using the name combination.<sup id="cite_ref-NEws.bbc.co.uk_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NEws.bbc.co.uk-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-www.comcast.net_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www.comcast.net-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The term <i>Bennifer</i> itself became popular, and started the trend of other celebrity couples being referred to by the combination of each other's first names, as with <a href="/wiki/Brad_Pitt" title="Brad Pitt">Brad Pitt</a> and <a href="/wiki/Angelina_Jolie" title="Angelina Jolie">Angelina Jolie</a> ("Brangelina"),<sup id="cite_ref-www.comcast.net_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www.comcast.net-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-www.theage.com.au_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www.theage.com.au-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Kanye_West" title="Kanye West">Kanye West</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kim_Kardashian" title="Kim Kardashian">Kim Kardashian</a> ("Kimye").<sup id="cite_ref-Clements_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Clements-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Robert Thompson, director of the Centre for the Study of Popular Television, said "as silly as it sounds, this new tendency to make up single names for two people, like 'Bennifer' (Ben Affleck and Jennifer Lopez) and 'TomKat' (Tom Cruise and Katie Holmes), is an insightful idea'. 'Brangelina' has more cultural equity than their two star parts".<sup id="cite_ref-www.theage.com.au_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-www.theage.com.au-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
