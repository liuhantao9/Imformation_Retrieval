<p><b>Caribbean Hindustani</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Indo-Aryan_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Indo-Aryan language">Indo-Aryan language</a> historically spoken by <a href="/wiki/Indo-Caribbeans" title="Indo-Caribbeans">Indo-Caribbeans</a> and the Indo-Caribbean diaspora. It is based on <a href="/wiki/Bhojpuri_language" title="Bhojpuri language">Bhojpuri</a> with influences from <a href="/wiki/Awadhi" class="mw-redirect" title="Awadhi">Awadhi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> These were spoken by <a href="/wiki/Indian_indenture_system" title="Indian indenture system">indentured laborers</a> who came as immigrants to the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">Indian subcontinent</a>. It is closely related to <a href="/wiki/Fijian_Hindi" class="mw-redirect" title="Fijian Hindi">Fijian Hindi</a>, the Bhojpuri spoken in <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">Mauritius</a> and the Hindustani spoken in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>.
</p><p>Because a majority of people came from <a href="/wiki/Bihar" title="Bihar">Bihar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jharkhand" title="Jharkhand">Jharkhand</a> and <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a>, Caribbean Hindustani is most influenced by <a href="/wiki/Bhojpuri" class="mw-redirect" title="Bhojpuri">Bhojpuri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Awadhi_language" title="Awadhi language">Awadhi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Braj_Bhasha" title="Braj Bhasha">Braj Bhasha</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Bihari_languages" title="Bihari languages">Bihari dialects</a>. It has a very minor influence from <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Telugu_language" title="Telugu language">Telugu</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages">Dravidian languages</a>. It has also borrowed many words from <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> in <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Dutch_colonies" class="mw-redirect" title="Dutch colonies">Dutch colonies</a>, and <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> in former <a href="/wiki/British_colonies" class="mw-redirect" title="British colonies">British colonies</a> and <a href="/wiki/French_colonies" class="mw-redirect" title="French colonies">French colonies</a>. Many words unique to Caribbean Hindustani have been created to cater for the new environment that Indo-Caribbeans now live in. 
</p><p>Like the <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">Hindustani</a> spoken in the Indian subcontinent, Caribbean Hindustani is broken up into Caribbean <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a> and Caribbean <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a>. <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">Hindustani</a> refers to both Hindi and Urdu. Hindi is spoken by <a href="/wiki/Hindu" class="mw-redirect" title="Hindu">Hindus</a> and people of Hindu descent, whereas Urdu is spoken by <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslims</a> and people of Muslim descent. Hindi has more influence from <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>, while Urdu has more influence from <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">Arabic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>. Hindi is written in the <a href="/wiki/Devanagari" title="Devanagari">Devanagari</a>, <a href="/wiki/Devanagari_Braille" title="Devanagari Braille">Devanagari Braille</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kaithi" title="Kaithi">Kaithi</a> script, whereas Urdu is written in the <a href="/wiki/Perso-Arabic_script" class="mw-redirect" title="Perso-Arabic script">Perso-Arabic script</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urdu_Braille" title="Urdu Braille">Urdu Braille</a>, although in more recent times they both are written in the <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">Latin script</a> and <a href="/wiki/English_Braille" title="English Braille">English Braille</a>. 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Chutney_music" title="Chutney music">Chutney music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chutney_Soca" class="mw-redirect" title="Chutney Soca">chutney soca</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chutney_parang" title="Chutney parang">chutney parang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baithak_Gana" title="Baithak Gana">baithak gana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_folk_music" title="Indian folk music">folk music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_classical_music" title="Indian classical music">classical music</a>, some <a href="/wiki/Hindu_music" title="Hindu music">Hindu religious songs</a>, some <a href="/wiki/Islamic_music" title="Islamic music">Muslim religious songs</a>, and some <a href="/wiki/Christian_music" title="Christian music">Christian religious songs</a> are sung in Caribbean Hindustani, sometimes being mixed with <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_English" title="Caribbean English">English</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Caribbean" title="Commonwealth Caribbean">Anglophone Caribbean</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> in <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Caribbean" title="Dutch Caribbean">Dutch Caribbean</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Guyanese_Hindustani">Guyanese Hindustani</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Hindustani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Guyanese Hindustani">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Caribbean Hindustani of <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> is known as <i>Aili Gaili</i> (Aaye-Gaye [I] Came-Gone is spoken as Aili-Gaili in <a href="/wiki/Bhojpuri" class="mw-redirect" title="Bhojpuri">Bhojpuri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maithili_language" title="Maithili language">Maithili</a> and <a href="/wiki/Awadhi" class="mw-redirect" title="Awadhi">Awadhi</a> and almost all dialects of <a href="/wiki/Bihar" title="Bihar">Bihar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jharkhand" title="Jharkhand">Jharkhand</a> and is spoken by some members in a community of 300,000 Indo-Guyanese.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trinidadian_Hindustani">Trinidadian Hindustani</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Hindustani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Trinidadian Hindustani">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The variant that is spoken in <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a> is known as <i>Trinidadian Hindustani</i>, <i>Trinidadian Bhojpuri</i>, <i>Plantation Hindustani</i>, or <i>Gaon ke Bolee (Village Speech)</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> A majority of the early <a href="/wiki/Indian_indenture_system" title="Indian indenture system">Indian immigrants</a> spoke the <a href="/wiki/Bhojpuri" class="mw-redirect" title="Bhojpuri">Bhojpuri</a> and <a href="/wiki/Awadhi" class="mw-redirect" title="Awadhi">Awadhi</a> dialect of <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">Hindustani (Hindi-Urdu)</a>, which later formed into Trinidadian Hindustani. In 1935, Indian movies began showing to audiences in Trinidad. Most of the Indian movies were in the <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">Standard Hindustani (Hindi-Urdu) dialect</a> and this modified Trinidadian Hindustani slightly by adding Standard Hindu and Urdu phrases and vocabulary to Trinidadian Hindustani. Indian movies also revitalized Hindustani among Indo-Trinidadian and Tobagonians.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Around the mid to late 1970s the <i>lingua franca</i> of Indo-Trinidaian and Tobagonians switched from Trinidadian Hindustani to a sort of <i>Hindinized</i> version of English. Today Hindustani survives on through <a href="/wiki/Indo-Caribbean_music" title="Indo-Caribbean music">Indo-Trinidadian and Tobagonian musical forms</a> such as, <a href="/wiki/Bhajan" title="Bhajan">Bhajan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_classical_music" title="Indian classical music">Indian classical music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indian_folk_music" title="Indian folk music">Indian folk music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Filmi" title="Filmi">Filmi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pichakaree" title="Pichakaree">Pichakaree</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chutney_music" title="Chutney music">Chutney</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chutney_soca" title="Chutney soca">Chutney soca</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chutney_parang" title="Chutney parang">Chutney parang</a>. Presently there are about 26,000 people, which is 5.53% of the Indo-Trinidadian and Tobagonian population, who speak Trinidadian Hindustani. Many Indo-Trinidadians and Tobagonians today speak a type of <a href="/wiki/Hinglish" title="Hinglish">Hinglish</a> that consist of Trinidadian and Tobagonian English that is heavily laced with Trinidadian Hindustani vocabulary and phrases and many Indo-Trinidadians and Tobagonians can recite phrases or prayers in Hindustani today. There are many places in Trinidad and Tobago that have names of Hindustani origin. Some phrases and vocabulary have even made its way into the mainstream English and English Creole dialect of the country.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sarnami_Hindustani">Sarnami Hindustani</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Hindustani&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sarnami Hindustani">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Sarnami Hindustani</i> meaning <i>Surinamese Hindustani</i> is the most widely spoken language in Suriname after Dutch, <a href="/wiki/Sranan_Tongo" title="Sranan Tongo">Sranan Tongo</a> (the two <i><a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua francas</a></i>) and English. It is a  distinct dialect of the <a href="/wiki/Bhojpuri_language" title="Bhojpuri language">Bhojpuri language</a> (the language spoken in north Indian states <a href="/wiki/Bihar" title="Bihar">Bihar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uttar_Pradesh" title="Uttar Pradesh">Uttar Pradesh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jharkhand" title="Jharkhand">Jharkhand</a>) with heavy lexical influence from other languages spoken in Suriname. It is mainly spoken by and within Suriname’s <a href="/wiki/Indo-Surinamese" title="Indo-Surinamese">Indo-Surinamese</a> (ca. 27% of the population) community and therefore it is not considered to be a third lingua franca.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Baithak_Gana" title="Baithak Gana">Baithak Gana</a></i> is the most famous form of the song being sung in Sarnami Hindustani.
</p>
