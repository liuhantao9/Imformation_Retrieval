<p><b>Caribbean Airlines Limited</b> is the <a href="/wiki/State-owned" class="mw-redirect" title="State-owned">state-owned</a> <a href="/wiki/Airline" title="Airline">airline</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flag_carrier" title="Flag carrier">flag carrier</a> of <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>. Headquartered in Iere House in <a href="/wiki/Piarco" title="Piarco">Piarco</a>, the airline operates flights to the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a> from its base at <a href="/wiki/Piarco_International_Airport" title="Piarco International Airport">Piarco International Airport</a>. Presently Caribbean Airlines employs more than 1,700 people and is the largest airline in the Caribbean.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Caribbean Airlines was <a href="/wiki/Incorporation_(business)" title="Incorporation (business)">incorporated</a> in the Republic of Trinidad and Tobago on 27 September 2006.
</p><p>In September 2006, following the recommendation of Peter Davies, the CEO of <a href="/wiki/BWIA_West_Indies_Airways" title="BWIA West Indies Airways">BWIA West Indies Airways</a>, Caribbean Airlines got approval from the Trinidad and Tobago government to begin operations, after the failed negotiations between the unions and the management of its predecessor, BWIA. As a result, it was announced on 8 September 2006, that BWIA was to be shut down before the launch of Caribbean Airlines. During the last quarter of 2006, in an effort to scale down operations for the start of Caribbean Airlines, BWIA's management cut routes such as <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Airport" title="Manchester Airport">Manchester Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/London_Heathrow_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="London Heathrow Airport">London Heathrow Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, with intermediate stops at <a href="/wiki/Barbados" title="Barbados">Barbados</a> or <a href="/wiki/Antigua" title="Antigua">Antigua</a>, ceased services to and from <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lucia" title="Saint Lucia">Saint Lucia</a>, cut its fleet to six Boeing 737-800 aircraft retrofitted with <a href="/wiki/Wingtip_device" title="Wingtip device">wingtip devices</a> (winglets) and reduced its staff to 800, with a majority of the staff former BWIA workers now contracted.
</p><p>The new airline's capital included funds to close and settle BWIA's operations.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The company commenced Operations on 1 January 2007, servicing the remaining routes of BWIA.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Caribbean Airlines began operations with a fleet of six Boeing 737-800 aircraft and one Airbus A340-313, operating the London Heathrow route until May 2007. The airline offered two classes of service, first/business class and economy class on both the Boeing and Airbus aircraft. Caribbean Airlines operated daily direct services to Miami, Toronto, New York, Jamaica (with stops in Barbados and Antigua/St. Maarten), Guyana, Suriname and <a href="/wiki/London_Heathrow_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="London Heathrow Airport">London Heathrow</a> till May 2007 due to the ICC 2007 Cricket World Cup, all out of its hub at <a href="/wiki/Piarco_International_Airport" title="Piarco International Airport">Piarco International Airport</a>. Peter Davies resigned from his position as CEO, effective 30 September 2007, but remained as a strategic advisor to Caribbean Airlines.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In the first half of 2007, two <a href="/wiki/Airbus_A340" title="Airbus A340">Airbus A340-300</a> planes and a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_Next_Generation" title="Boeing 737 Next Generation">Boeing 737 Next Generation</a> Boeing 737-800, 9Y-GND, were returned to <a href="/wiki/International_Lease_Finance_Corporation" title="International Lease Finance Corporation">International Lease Finance Corporation</a> (ILFC) and <a href="/wiki/GE_Commercial_Aviation_Services" class="mw-redirect" title="GE Commercial Aviation Services">GE Commercial Aviation Services</a> (GECAS), respectively.
</p><p>On 1 October 2007, Philip Saunders, <a href="/wiki/Star_Alliance" title="Star Alliance">Star Alliance</a> VP Commercial, was appointed the new Chief Executive Officer of Caribbean Airlines. Subsequently, the airline took over operations of <a href="/wiki/Tobago_Express" title="Tobago Express">Tobago Express</a>, its domestic arm at a cost of US$24 million with the intentions to upgrade the fleet of <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Dash_8" class="mw-redirect" title="Bombardier Dash 8">Bombardier Dash-8 Q300</a> to international standards and also adding new Caribbean destinations to be served via this regional airline <a href="/wiki/Subsidiary" title="Subsidiary">subsidiary</a>.
</p>

<p>In March 2008, the airline added a daily direct service to the <a href="/wiki/Sim%C3%B3n_Bol%C3%ADvar_International_Airport_(Venezuela)" title="Simón Bolívar International Airport (Venezuela)">Simón Bolívar International Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Caracas" title="Caracas">Caracas</a>, using the Dash 8 aircraft. In May 2008, Caribbean Airlines acquired a seventh <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_Next_Generation" title="Boeing 737 Next Generation">Boeing 737-800</a> aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The airline added a new U.S. route on 22 May to <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale-Hollywood_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International Airport">Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International Airport</a> from <a href="/wiki/Piarco_International_Airport" title="Piarco International Airport">Piarco International Airport</a>. This route began operations on a four times per week schedule and later in July of the same year, the frequency was increased to daily service.
</p><p>On 15 November 2008, <a href="/wiki/Transavia_Airlines" class="mw-redirect" title="Transavia Airlines">Transavia Airlines</a> agreed to operate a wet-lease operation on behalf of Caribbean Airlines, it increased its fleet to eight <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_Next_Generation" title="Boeing 737 Next Generation">Boeing 737-800</a>. The aircraft was re-fitted to the standard 16/138 configuration including an extra nine inches of seat pitch in the business class cabin, due to a different galley and closet placement. This allowed the airline to increase flight frequency to meet demand for the peak travel periods. The lease was contracted to be in effect until 15 April 2009. The aircraft was returned in June 2009, and <a href="/wiki/Sun_Country_Airlines" title="Sun Country Airlines">Sun Country Airlines</a>, a U.S. air carrier, then leased a Boeing 737-800 to Caribbean Airlines.
</p><p>In April 2009, the airline increased its frequency of service to <a href="/wiki/Sim%C3%B3n_Bol%C3%ADvar_International_Airport_(Venezuela)" title="Simón Bolívar International Airport (Venezuela)">Simón Bolívar International Airport</a> in <a href="/wiki/Caracas" title="Caracas">Caracas</a> from a daily service to ten flights per week. The route was operated by the <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Dash_8" class="mw-redirect" title="Bombardier Dash 8">Bombardier Dash-8 Q300</a>, configured for 50 passengers in an all economy service. Daily flights to its gateways <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale,_Florida" title="Fort Lauderdale, Florida">Fort Lauderdale</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a> continued. Services to <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a> and <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> remained at twice daily and 20 weekly (up to 28 weekly in peak season), respectively.
</p><p>On 27 July 2009, Philip Saunders announced his resignation as CEO of Caribbean Airlines, due to personal reasons. Caribbean Airlines appointed Captain Ian Brunton in October 2009 as CEO of the airline.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Also in 2009, Caribbean Airlines operated services from the southern <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> to <a href="/wiki/Jamaica" title="Jamaica">Jamaica</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, including <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>. The airline also continued to serve the United States and Canada, in <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">Miami</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fort_Lauderdale,_Florida" title="Fort Lauderdale, Florida">Fort Lauderdale</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, having also established a <a href="/wiki/Codeshare_agreement" title="Codeshare agreement">codeshare agreement</a> with <a href="/wiki/British_Airways" title="British Airways">British Airways</a>, for services to <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> and beyond. As of 2009, the airline operated a fleet of eight <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_Next_Generation" title="Boeing 737 Next Generation">Boeing 737-800</a> aircraft and five <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Dash_8" class="mw-redirect" title="Bombardier Dash 8">Bombardier Dash-8 Q300</a> aircraft, out of its main hub at <a href="/wiki/Piarco_International_Airport" title="Piarco International Airport">Piarco International Airport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a>. The Dash 8 aircraft have since been replaced with new ATR 72-600 propjets.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acquisition_of_Air_Jamaica">Acquisition of Air Jamaica</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Acquisition of Air Jamaica">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Air_Jamaica_Boeing_737-800_Heisterkamp.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Air_Jamaica_Boeing_737-800_Heisterkamp.jpg/220px-Air_Jamaica_Boeing_737-800_Heisterkamp.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Air_Jamaica_Boeing_737-800_Heisterkamp.jpg/330px-Air_Jamaica_Boeing_737-800_Heisterkamp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Air_Jamaica_Boeing_737-800_Heisterkamp.jpg/440px-Air_Jamaica_Boeing_737-800_Heisterkamp.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="801" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Air_Jamaica_Boeing_737-800_Heisterkamp.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A former <a href="/wiki/Air_Jamaica" title="Air Jamaica">Air Jamaica</a> <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737-800" class="mw-redirect" title="Boeing 737-800">Boeing 737-800</a> operated by Caribbean Airlines</div></div></div>
<p>Caribbean Airlines Limited, through the government of Trinidad and Tobago, announced plans to operate a base in Jamaica following the divestment of <a href="/wiki/Air_Jamaica" title="Air Jamaica">Air Jamaica</a> which was slated to occur between 30 April 2010 to that same period in 2011. Caribbean Airlines is providing a seamless transition following Air Jamaica's closure.
When the deal is finalized, Caribbean Airlines Limited will become the largest airline in the Caribbean with a new hub in Kingston Jamaica's <a href="/wiki/Norman_Manley_International_Airport" title="Norman Manley International Airport">Norman Manley International Airport</a>. The divestment was scheduled to become effective on 30 April 2010 after it was rescheduled from 12 April 2010. On this day, it was planned that Air Jamaica would cease operations and Caribbean Airlines will then acquire the airline's most profitable routes and their remaining six aircraft. Plans were also announced to replace the aircraft fleet with all Boeing 737-800 Next Generation jets.
</p><p>On 4 March 2010, Caribbean Airlines announced they would terminate their codeshare agreement with British Airways for the Port of Spain-London Gatwick route effective 27 March 2010 thus paving the way for a nonstop service to be operated by Caribbean between <a href="/wiki/Piarco_International_Airport" title="Piarco International Airport">Piarco International Airport</a> and London, UK.
</p><p>On 28 April 2010, Caribbean Airlines Limited and <a href="/wiki/Air_Jamaica" title="Air Jamaica">Air Jamaica</a> signed a final agreement for the purchase of Air Jamaica by Caribbean Airlines Limited, following the signing of a letter of intent to merge in January of the same year. The agreement stated that Caribbean would continue operating Air Jamaica's routes and also retain 900 of Air Jamaica's employees. Financing was provided by the government of Trinidad and Tobago, which contributed around $50 million to complete the merger, and Jamaica, which assumed more than $800 million in costs related to the closure of Air Jamaica.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In August 2010, Caribbean Airlines and Air India signed a travel pact for Trinidadians travelling to India. In October, the airline announced a fleet renewal consisting of fifteen (15) <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_Next_Generation" title="Boeing 737 Next Generation">Boeing 737-800</a> Next Generation jets and nine <a href="/wiki/ATR_72-600" class="mw-redirect" title="ATR 72-600">ATR 72-600</a> <a href="/wiki/Turboprop" title="Turboprop">turboprop</a> aircraft. The new Boeing jets were intended to replace Air Jamaica's operating fleet of Airbus aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-av_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-av-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 27 May 2011, the acquisition of Air Jamaica was completed, with Finance Minister Winston Dookeran and Jamaican Finance Minister Audley Shaw at the Prime Minister' St. Clair office, signing the shareholding agreement. This agreement allows the Jamaican government to own a 16% stake of Caribbean Airlines Limited. On 28 October 2011, the U.S. Department of Transportation fined Caribbean Airlines $60,000 for limiting reimbursements for lost, damaged and delayed baggage to less than consumers were entitled under the Montreal Convention.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Talks have begun between Caribbean Airlines' management and <a href="/wiki/LIAT" title="LIAT">LIAT</a> (<a href="/wiki/Leeward_Islands_Air_Transport" class="mw-redirect" title="Leeward Islands Air Transport">Leeward Islands Air Transport</a>, a regional airline operating <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Dash_8" class="mw-redirect" title="Bombardier Dash 8">Dash 8</a> as well as <a href="/wiki/ATR_42" title="ATR 42">ATR 42</a> and <a href="/wiki/ATR_72" title="ATR 72">ATR 72</a> turboprop aircraft in the Caribbean) regarding a possible partnership or merger between the two airlines. The former <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Trinidad_and_Tobago" class="mw-redirect" title="Prime Minister of Trinidad and Tobago">Prime Minister of Trinidad and Tobago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Manning" title="Patrick Manning">Patrick Manning</a> had also expressed interest in <a href="/wiki/Cayman_Airways" title="Cayman Airways">Cayman Airways</a> also being merged into Caribbean Airlines, but, with the recent changes in government, the future of these plans is uncertain.
</p><p>According to Chairman of the Board of Caribbean Airlines George Nicholas III, Caribbean Airlines is pursuing a relationship with <a href="/wiki/Star_Alliance" title="Star Alliance">Star Alliance</a>, the world's largest airline alliance, in an effort to support Jamaican hoteliers.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The text near this tag is dated. (July 2012)">needs update</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It is also reported they are preparing to start a route to Mumbai, but no date has been given. Also, Nicholas announced that Johannesburg, Nigeria, and Brazil are possible contenders for new routes. Chicago and Atlanta are the two new U.S. gateways that are being considered, Nicholas stated.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 29 March 2013, the U.S. Department of Transportation (DOT) fined the airline $100,000 for not providing passengers with an opportunity to leave a plane that was delayed on the tarmac at New York's JFK Airport for more than four hours.
</p><p>In early 2016, the management of Caribbean Airlines has set as an agenda to consider replacing its <a href="/wiki/ATR_(aircraft_manufacturer)" title="ATR (aircraft manufacturer)">ATR</a> fleet with comparable turboprops sourced from <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Aerospace" class="mw-redirect" title="Bombardier Aerospace">Bombardier</a> due to persistent reliability problems. The former Chief Executive Officer (CEO) Michael Di Lollo described the ATRs as "not mission capable" after they suffered from frequent AOG (Aircraft On Ground) occurrences. On his recommendation, efforts were made to sourcing <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Dash_8" class="mw-redirect" title="Bombardier Dash 8">Dash 8-400 aircraft</a> to replace the carrier's fleet of five ATR72-600s.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Also in early 2016, the airline returned its two <a href="/wiki/Boeing_767-300ER" class="mw-redirect" title="Boeing 767-300ER">Boeing 767-300ER</a> aircraft to their lessor, coinciding with termination of its only European route to <a href="/wiki/London_Gatwick_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="London Gatwick Airport">London Gatwick Airport</a> on 10 January 2016, as part of a streamlining exercise to remove unprofitable routes.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Corporate_affairs">Corporate affairs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Corporate affairs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Branding">Branding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Branding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The colors of Caribbean Airlines are blue, green and purple and are represented in the new staff uniforms, all featuring the <a href="/wiki/Hummingbird" title="Hummingbird">hummingbird</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Logo" title="Logo">logo</a> of Caribbean Airlines.
</p><p>BWIA's logo was a <a href="/wiki/Steelpan" title="Steelpan">steelpan</a>. Caribbean Airlines' image is a <a href="/wiki/Hummingbird" title="Hummingbird">hummingbird</a> in flight.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The image is a reference to the Republic of Trinidad and Tobago, where the island of Trinidad is known as "the land of the hummingbird".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> All aircraft in the fleet carry the flag of Trinidad and Tobago, as well as the flag of <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Community" title="Caribbean Community">CARICOM</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The airline had four designs on the tails of its <a href="/wiki/De_Havilland_Canada_DHC-8_Dash_8" class="mw-redirect" title="De Havilland Canada DHC-8 Dash 8">de Havilland Canada DHC-8 Dash 8</a> regional aircraft before these twin turboprops were phased out of the fleet (9Y-WIT was not painted), One of the designs raised controversy, due to its usage of the <a href="/wiki/Heliconia_bihai" title="Heliconia bihai">Balisier flower</a>, the symbol of the former ruling <a href="/wiki/People%27s_National_Movement" title="People&#39;s National Movement">People's National Movement</a> political party.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The logo was subsequently redesigned without the flower<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and replaced with fruits. The tail designs featured a <a href="/wiki/Steelpan" title="Steelpan">steelpan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a> balls, <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coral" title="Coral">corals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">fish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Butterfly" title="Butterfly">butterflies</a>.
</p><p>Caribbean Airlines has a theme song arranged in a traditional <a href="/wiki/Calypso_music" title="Calypso music">Trinidadian calypso</a> music style. It was originally recorded by Explainer and the song is called "Lorraine".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The original lyrics, which talk about a man who needs to escape the cold United States and get back to the Caribbean,<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> were modified to fit Caribbean Airlines.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Incidents_and_accidents">Incidents and accidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Airlines&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Incidents and accidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Airlines_Flight_523" title="Caribbean Airlines Flight 523">Caribbean Airlines Flight 523</a></div>
<ul><li>On 30 July 2011, <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Airlines_Flight_523" title="Caribbean Airlines Flight 523">Caribbean Airlines Flight 523</a> (reg. 9Y-PBM) overran the runway in rainy weather and crashed through the perimeter fence while landing at the <a href="/wiki/Cheddi_Jagan_International_Airport" title="Cheddi Jagan International Airport">Cheddi Jagan International Airport</a> in Guyana. The incident occurred at approximately 1:32 am. The aircraft, a <a href="/wiki/Boeing_737_Next_Generation" title="Boeing 737 Next Generation">Boeing 737-800</a>, broke into two just behind the first class area. There were no fatalities.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Caribbean Airlines confirmed 157 passengers and 6 crew members were on board the aircraft.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>On 26 October 2012, Caribbean Airlines Flight BW 300 took off at 6:49&#160;a.m. from Piarco International Airport bound for Caracas carrying four crew and 44 passengers. A section of exterior panelling became detached from the aircraft on lift-off. The aircraft, an ATR 72-600, landed back at the Piarco International Airport safely at 7:15 a.m, without injuries.</li>
<li>On 6 March 2019, a Caribbean Airlines ATR 72-600 was being taxied from the ramp to an aircraft hangar when it crashed into the wall of the terminal building. There were no passengers or crew on board and Caribbean Airlines stated that the aircraft was not in active service. There were no injuries. It was later confirmed that the aircraft braking system was non-functional, which led to the incident.</li></ul>
