	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Paraboloid</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Paraboloid_of_Revolution.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Paraboloid_of_Revolution.svg/220px-Paraboloid_of_Revolution.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="189" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Paraboloid_of_Revolution.svg/330px-Paraboloid_of_Revolution.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Paraboloid_of_Revolution.svg/440px-Paraboloid_of_Revolution.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="440" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Paraboloid_of_Revolution.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Paraboloid of revolution</div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">geometry</a>, a <b>paraboloid</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Quadric_surface" class="mw-redirect" title="Quadric surface">quadric surface</a> that has exactly one <a href="/wiki/Axial_symmetry" class="mw-redirect" title="Axial symmetry">axis of symmetry</a> and no <a href="/wiki/Central_symmetry" class="mw-redirect" title="Central symmetry">center of symmetry</a>. The term "paraboloid" is derived from <a href="/wiki/Parabola" title="Parabola">parabola</a>, which refers to a <a href="/wiki/Conic_section" title="Conic section">conic section</a> that has a similar property of symmetry. 
</p><p>Every <a href="/wiki/Plane_section" class="mw-redirect" title="Plane section">plane section</a> of a paraboloid by a plane <a href="/wiki/Parallel_(geometry)#A_line_and_a_plane" title="Parallel (geometry)">parallel</a> to the axis of symmetry is a parabola. The paraboloid is <b>hyperbolic</b> if every other plane section is either a <a href="/wiki/Hyperbola" title="Hyperbola">hyperbola</a>, or two crossing lines (in the case of a section by a tangent plane). The paraboloid is <b>elliptic</b> if every other nonempty plane section is either an <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">ellipse</a>, or a single point (in the case of a section by a tangent plane). A paraboloid is either elliptic or hyperbolic.
</p><p>Equivalently, a paraboloid may be defined as a quadric surface that is not a <a href="/wiki/Cylinder" title="Cylinder">cylinder</a>, and has an <a href="/wiki/Implicit_surface" title="Implicit surface">implicit equation</a> whose part of degree two may be factored over the <a href="/wiki/Complex_number" title="Complex number">complex numbers</a> into two different linear factors. The paraboloid is hyperbolic if the factors are real; elliptic if the factors are <a href="/wiki/Complex_conjugate" title="Complex conjugate">complex conjugate</a>.
</p><p>An elliptic paraboloid is shaped like an oval cup and has a <a href="/wiki/Maximum" class="mw-redirect" title="Maximum">maximum</a> or minimum point when its axis is vertical. In a suitable <a href="/wiki/Coordinate_system" title="Coordinate system">coordinate system</a> with three axes <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i></span>, <span class="texhtml"><i>y</i></span>, and <span class="texhtml"><i>z</i></span>, it can be represented by the equation<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>892</span></sup>

</p>

<p>In this position, the hyperbolic paraboloid opens downward along the <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i></span>-axis and upward along the <span class="texhtml"><i>y</i></span>-axis (that is, the parabola in the plane <span class="texhtml"><i>x</i> = 0</span> opens upward and the parabola in the plane <span class="texhtml"><i>y</i> = 0</span> opens downward).
</p><p>Any paraboloid (elliptic or hyperbolic) is a <a href="/wiki/Translation_surface_(differential_geometry)" title="Translation surface (differential geometry)">translation surface</a>, as it can be generated by a moving parabola directed by a second parabola.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Properties_and_applications">Properties and applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paraboloid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties and applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elliptic_paraboloid">Elliptic paraboloid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paraboloid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Elliptic paraboloid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>With <span class="texhtml"><i>a</i> = <i>b</i></span> an elliptic paraboloid is a <i>paraboloid of revolution</i>: a surface obtained by revolving a <a href="/wiki/Parabola" title="Parabola">parabola</a> around its axis. It is the shape of the <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_reflector" title="Parabolic reflector">parabolic reflectors</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Mirror" title="Mirror">mirrors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(electronics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Antenna (electronics)">antenna</a> dishes, and the like; and is also the shape of the surface of a rotating liquid, a principle used in <a href="/wiki/Liquid_mirror_telescope" title="Liquid mirror telescope">liquid mirror telescopes</a> and in making solid telescope mirrors (see <a href="/wiki/Rotating_furnace" title="Rotating furnace">rotating furnace</a>). This shape is also called a <i>circular paraboloid</i>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Paraboloid-3dmesh.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Paraboloid-3dmesh.png/220px-Paraboloid-3dmesh.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Paraboloid-3dmesh.png/330px-Paraboloid-3dmesh.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Paraboloid-3dmesh.png/440px-Paraboloid-3dmesh.png 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Paraboloid-3dmesh.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mesh Grid of Paraboloid surface</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Paraboloid3d.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Paraboloid3d.png/220px-Paraboloid3d.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Paraboloid3d.png/330px-Paraboloid3d.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Paraboloid3d.png/440px-Paraboloid3d.png 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Paraboloid3d.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Paraboloid Surface (0.5 transparency)</div></div></div>
<p>There is a point called the <a href="/wiki/Focus_(optics)" title="Focus (optics)"><i>focus</i></a> (or <i>focal point</i>) on the axis of a circular paraboloid such that, if the paraboloid is a mirror, light from a point source at the focus is reflected into a parallel beam, parallel to the axis of the paraboloid. This also works the other way around: a parallel beam of light incident on the paraboloid parallel to its axis is concentrated at the focal point. This applies also for other waves, hence <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_antenna" title="Parabolic antenna">parabolic antennas</a>. For a geometrical proof, click <a href="/wiki/Parabola#Proof_of_the_reflective_property" title="Parabola">here</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hyperbolic_paraboloid">Hyperbolic paraboloid</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Paraboloid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hyperbolic paraboloid">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The hyperbolic paraboloid is a <a href="/wiki/Doubly_ruled_surface" class="mw-redirect" title="Doubly ruled surface">doubly ruled surface</a>: it contains two families of mutually <a href="/wiki/Skew_lines" title="Skew lines">skew lines</a>. The lines in each family are parallel to a common plane, but not to each other. Hence the hyperbolic paraboloid is a <a href="/wiki/Conoid" title="Conoid">conoid</a>.
</p><p>These properties characterize hyperbolic paraboloids and are used in one of the oldest definitions of hyperbolic paraboloids: <i>a hyperbolic paraboloid is a surface that may be generated by a moving line that is parallel to a fixed plane and crosses two fixed <a href="/wiki/Skew_lines" title="Skew lines">skew lines</a></i>. This property makes it simple to manufacture a hyperbolic paraboloid from a variety of materials and for a variety of purposes, from concrete roofs to snack foods.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Pringles" title="Pringles">Pringles</a> potato chips resemble a truncated hyperbolic paraboloid.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Their uniform shape allows them to be stacked in sturdy tubular containers, and the strength of the hyperbolic paraboloid shape helps prevent them from breaking while stacked.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

They must all be in the same <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_length" title="Unit of length">unit of length</a>. If two of these three lengths are known, this equation can be used to calculate the third.
</p><p>A more complex calculation is needed to find the diameter of the dish <i>measured along its surface</i>. This is sometimes called the "linear diameter", and equals the diameter of a flat, circular sheet of material, usually metal, which is the right size to be cut and bent to make the dish. Two intermediate results are useful in the calculation: <span class="texhtml"><i>P</i> = 2<i>F</i></span> (or the equivalent: <span class="texhtml"><i>P</i> = <span role="math" class="sfrac nowrap tion" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;"><span class="num" style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;"><i>R</i><sup>2</sup></span><span class="slash visualhide">/</span><span class="den" style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">2<i>D</i></span></span></span>) and <span class="texhtml"><i>Q</i> = <span class="nowrap">&#8730;<span style="border-top:1px solid; padding:0 0.1em;"><i>P</i><sup>2</sup> + <i>R</i><sup>2</sup></span></span></span>, where <span class="texhtml"><i>F</i></span>, <span class="texhtml"><i>D</i></span>, and <span class="texhtml"><i>R</i></span> are defined as above.
