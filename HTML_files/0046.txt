<p><b>Rosanne Cash</b> (born May 24, 1955) is an American singer-songwriter and author. She is the eldest daughter of <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">country musician</a> <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Cash" title="Johnny Cash">Johnny Cash</a> and Vivian Liberto Cash Distin, Johnny Cash's first wife.
</p><p>Although Cash is often classified as a country artist, her music draws on many genres, including <a href="/wiki/Folk_music" title="Folk music">folk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blues_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Blues music">blues</a>, and most notably <a href="/wiki/Americana_(music)" title="Americana (music)">Americana</a>. In the 1980s, she had a string of chart-topping singles, which crossed musical genres and landed on both the country and pop charts, the most commercially successful being her 1981 breakthrough hit "<a href="/wiki/Seven_Year_Ache_(song)" title="Seven Year Ache (song)">Seven Year Ache</a>", which topped the U.S. country singles charts and reached the Top 30 on the U.S. pop singles charts.
</p><p>In 1990, Cash released <i><a href="/wiki/Interiors_(Rosanne_Cash_album)" title="Interiors (Rosanne Cash album)">Interiors</a></i>, a spare, introspective album which signaled a break from her pop country past.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The following year, in 1991, Cash ended her marriage and moved from <a href="/wiki/Nashville" class="mw-redirect" title="Nashville">Nashville</a> to New York City, where she has continued to write, record, and perform. Since 1991 she has released six albums, written three books, and edited a collection of short stories. Her fiction and essays have been published in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i>, <i>The Oxford American</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Magazine">New York Magazine</a></i>, and other periodicals and collections.
</p><p>Cash won a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a> in 1985 for "<a href="/wiki/I_Don%27t_Know_Why_You_Don%27t_Want_Me" title="I Don&#39;t Know Why You Don&#39;t Want Me">I Don't Know Why You Don't Want Me</a>" and has received 12<sup id="cite_ref-rosannecash_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rosannecash-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> other Grammy nominations. She has had 11 No.&#160;1 country hit singles, 21 Top 40 country singles, and two <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" class="mw-redirect" title="Music recording sales certification">gold records</a>. Cash was the 2014 recipient of Smithsonian magazine's American Ingenuity Award in the Performing Arts category.
</p><p>On February 8, 2015, Cash won three Grammy awards for Best Americana Album for <i>The River &amp; the Thread</i>, Best American Roots Song with John Leventhal and Best American Roots Performance for <i>A Feather's Not A Bird</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-grammy.com_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grammy.com-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cash was honored further on October 11, 2015, when she was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/Nashville_Songwriters_Hall_of_Fame" title="Nashville Songwriters Hall of Fame">Nashville Songwriters Hall of Fame</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-billboard.com_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-billboard.com-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cash was portrayed, as a child, by <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hailey_Anne_Nelson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hailey Anne Nelson (page does not exist)">Hailey Anne Nelson</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Walk_the_Line" title="Walk the Line">Walk the Line</a></i>, the 2005 Academy-award-winning film about her father's life.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosanne_Cash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cash was born in <a href="/wiki/Memphis,_Tennessee" title="Memphis, Tennessee">Memphis, Tennessee</a>, in 1955, just as father Johnny was recording his first tracks at <a href="/wiki/Sun_Records" title="Sun Records">Sun Records</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-OxAm2013_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OxAm2013-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sunrecords_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sunrecords-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The family moved to California in 1958, first to Los Angeles, then <a href="/wiki/Casitas_Springs,_California" title="Casitas Springs, California">Ventura</a>, where Cash and her sisters were raised by mother Vivian. (Vivian and Johnny separated in the early 1960s<sup id="cite_ref-roughguide_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-roughguide-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and divorced in 1966.) After graduating from <a href="/wiki/St._Bonaventure_High_School" title="St. Bonaventure High School">St. Bonaventure High School</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> she joined her father's road show for two and a half years, first as a wardrobe assistant,<sup id="cite_ref-rollingstone_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> then as a background vocalist and occasional soloist.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  She made her studio recording debut on Johnny Cash's 1974 album <i>The <a href="/wiki/Junkie_and_the_Juicehead_Minus_Me" class="mw-redirect" title="Junkie and the Juicehead Minus Me">Junkie and the Juicehead Minus Me</a></i>, singing lead vocal on a version of <a href="/wiki/Kris_Kristofferson" title="Kris Kristofferson">Kris Kristofferson</a>'s "Broken Freedom Song".
</p><p>In 1976, Johnny Cash recorded the Rosanne Cash composition "Love Has Lost Again"<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> on his album <i><a href="/wiki/One_Piece_at_a_Time_(album)" title="One Piece at a Time (album)">One Piece At A Time</a></i>.  Though she did not appear on this track, it was Rosanne Cash's first professionally recorded work as a composer.  That same year, she briefly worked for <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">CBS Records</a> in London before returning to <a href="/wiki/Nashville" class="mw-redirect" title="Nashville">Nashville</a> to study English and drama at <a href="/wiki/Vanderbilt_University" title="Vanderbilt University">Vanderbilt University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-OxAm2013_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OxAm2013-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> She then relocated to Los Angeles to study at the <a href="/wiki/Lee_Strasberg_Theatre_Institute" class="mw-redirect" title="Lee Strasberg Theatre Institute">Lee Strasberg Theatre Institute</a> in Hollywood.<sup id="cite_ref-centuryofcountry_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-centuryofcountry-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> She recorded a demo in January 1978 with <a href="/wiki/Emmylou_Harris" title="Emmylou Harris">Emmylou Harris</a>' songwriter/sideman <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Crowell" title="Rodney Crowell">Rodney Crowell</a>, which led to a full album with German label <a href="/wiki/Ariola_Records" title="Ariola Records">Ariola Records</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-centuryofcountry_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-centuryofcountry-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_career">Music career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosanne_Cash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span id="1978.E2.80.931980:_First_American_release"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1978–1980:_First_American_release">1978–1980: First American release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosanne_Cash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1978–1980: First American release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Her self-titled debut album was recorded in 1978, but Ariola never released it in the United States, and it has since become a collector's item. Mainly recorded and produced in <a href="/wiki/Munich" title="Munich">Munich</a>, Germany, with German-based musicians, it also included three tracks recorded in Nashville and produced by Crowell.<sup id="cite_ref-cmt_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cmt-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Though Cash was unhappy with the album, it attracted the attention of <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a>, which offered her a recording contract.<sup id="cite_ref-musicianguide_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-musicianguide-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> She began playing with Crowell's band <a href="/wiki/The_Notorious_Cherry_Bombs" title="The Notorious Cherry Bombs">The Cherry Bombs</a> in California clubs. Crowell and Cash married in 1979,<sup id="cite_ref-rollingstone_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and Cash started work on her first Columbia LP.
</p><p>The album, <i><a href="/wiki/Right_or_Wrong_(Rosanne_Cash_album)" title="Right or Wrong (Rosanne Cash album)">Right or Wrong</a></i>, was released in early 1980,<sup id="cite_ref-countrymusicencyclopedia_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-countrymusicencyclopedia-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and produced three Top 25 singles.<sup id="cite_ref-cmt_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cmt-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> The first, "No Memories Hangin' Around", a duet with country singer <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Bare" title="Bobby Bare">Bobby Bare</a>, reached 17 on the Country Singles chart in 1979. It was followed by "<a href="/wiki/Couldn%27t_Do_Nothing_Right" class="mw-redirect" title="Couldn&#39;t Do Nothing Right">Couldn't Do Nothing Right</a>" and "Take Me, Take Me" in 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusicawards_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicawards-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Cash, pregnant with her first child, was unable to tour in support of the album, which was nevertheless a critical success.<sup id="cite_ref-countrymusicencyclopedia_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-countrymusicencyclopedia-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Cash and Crowell moved to Nashville in 1981.
</p>
<h3><span id="1981.E2.80.931989:_Critical_and_commercial_success"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1981–1989:_Critical_and_commercial_success">1981–1989: Critical and commercial success</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosanne_Cash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 1981–1989: Critical and commercial success">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cash's career picked up considerable momentum with the release of her second album, <i><a href="/wiki/Seven_Year_Ache" title="Seven Year Ache">Seven Year Ache</a></i>, in 1981. The album achieved critical raves and solid sales, and the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Year_Ache_(song)" title="Seven Year Ache (song)">title track</a> was a No.&#160;1 hit on the <a href="/wiki/Hot_Country_Songs" title="Hot Country Songs">Billboard Country Chart</a> and crossed over to the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Billboard Pop Chart</a>, peaking at No.&#160;22. The album yielded two additional No.&#160;1 country hits, "<a href="/wiki/My_Baby_Thinks_He%27s_a_Train" title="My Baby Thinks He&#39;s a Train">My Baby Thinks He's a Train</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Blue_Moon_with_Heartache" title="Blue Moon with Heartache">Blue Moon with Heartache</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-countrymusicencyclopedia_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-countrymusicencyclopedia-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and was certified Gold by the <a href="/wiki/RIAA" class="mw-redirect" title="RIAA">RIAA</a>.
</p><p>Cash's third album, <i><a href="/wiki/Somewhere_in_the_Stars" title="Somewhere in the Stars">Somewhere in the Stars</a></i> (1982), was considered a disappointment after the commercial success of <i>Seven Year Ache</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-centuryofcountry_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-centuryofcountry-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The album still reached the Top 100 of the U.S. pop album charts and included three U.S. country chart singles, "<a href="/wiki/Ain%27t_No_Money" title="Ain&#39;t No Money">Ain't No Money</a>", "I Wonder", and "It Hasn't Happened Yet".<sup id="cite_ref-oldies_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oldies-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Cash struggled with <a href="/wiki/Substance_abuse" title="Substance abuse">substance abuse</a> during this time, and in 1984 she sought medical treatment.<sup id="cite_ref-countrymusicencyclopedia_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-countrymusicencyclopedia-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After a three-year hiatus, Cash released her fourth studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Rhythm_%26_Romance_(Rosanne_Cash_album)" title="Rhythm &amp; Romance (Rosanne Cash album)">Rhythm &amp; Romance</a></i> (1985), which yielded two No.&#160;1 hits, "<a href="/wiki/I_Don%27t_Know_Why_You_Don%27t_Want_Me" title="I Don&#39;t Know Why You Don&#39;t Want Me">I Don't Know Why You Don't Want Me</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Never_Be_You" title="Never Be You">Never Be You</a>", and two other Country Top 10 singles, "Hold On" and "Second to No One". <i>Rhythm &amp; Romance</i> drew high critical praise for its fusion of country and pop.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> "I Don't Know Why You Don't Want Me" won the 1985 <a href="/wiki/Grammy_award" class="mw-redirect" title="Grammy award">Grammy award</a> for Best Female Country Vocal Performance; "Hold On" won the 1987 Robert J. Burton Award from <a href="/wiki/Broadcast_Music_Incorporated" class="mw-redirect" title="Broadcast Music Incorporated">BMI</a> as the Most Performed Song of the Year.<sup id="cite_ref-centuryofcountry_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-centuryofcountry-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the '80s, Cash curtailed her touring for childbearing and raising a family (three daughters with Crowell, as well as Crowell's daughter by his first marriage, Hannah). She continued to record and in 1987 released the most critically acclaimed album of her career, <i><a href="/wiki/King%27s_Record_Shop" title="King&#39;s Record Shop">King's Record Shop</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusickings_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusickings-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> It spawned four No.&#160;1 hits, including a cover version of her father's "<a href="/wiki/Tennessee_Flat_Top_Box" title="Tennessee Flat Top Box">Tennessee Flat Top Box</a>", John Hiatt's "<a href="/wiki/The_Way_We_Make_a_Broken_Heart" title="The Way We Make a Broken Heart">The Way We Make a Broken Heart</a>", "<a href="/wiki/If_You_Change_Your_Mind" title="If You Change Your Mind">If You Change Your Mind</a>", John Stewart's "<a href="/wiki/Runaway_Train_(Rosanne_Cash_song)" title="Runaway Train (Rosanne Cash song)">Runaway Train</a>", and became Cash's second gold album. In 1988 Cash recorded a duet with Crowell, "<a href="/wiki/It%27s_Such_a_Small_World" title="It&#39;s Such a Small World">It's Such a Small World</a>" (released on his <i><a href="/wiki/Diamonds_%26_Dirt" title="Diamonds &amp; Dirt">Diamonds &amp; Dirt</a></i> album), which also went to No.&#160;1 on the country charts, and Cash was named <a href="/wiki/Billboard_magazine" class="mw-redirect" title="Billboard magazine"><i>Billboard</i></a>'s Top Singles Artist of the year.<sup id="cite_ref-centuryofcountry_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-centuryofcountry-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1989, Columbia released her first compilation album, <i><a href="/wiki/Hits_1979%E2%80%931989" title="Hits 1979–1989">Hits 1979–1989</a></i>. The album yielded two new hit singles, the <a href="/wiki/Beatles" class="mw-redirect" title="Beatles">Beatles</a> cover "<a href="/wiki/I_Don%27t_Want_to_Spoil_the_Party" title="I Don&#39;t Want to Spoil the Party">I Don't Want to Spoil the Party</a>", which landed at No.&#160;1 on the <i>Billboard</i> country charts, and "Black and White", which earned Cash her fifth Grammy nomination.<sup id="cite_ref-cmt_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cmt-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="1990.E2.80.931995:_Break_up_and_relocation"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1990–1995:_Break_up_and_relocation">1990–1995: Break up and relocation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosanne_Cash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1990–1995: Break up and relocation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1990, Cash released the critically acclaimed, deeply personal <i><a href="/wiki/Interiors_(Rosanne_Cash_album)" title="Interiors (Rosanne Cash album)">Interiors</a>.</i> Cash produced herself for the first time and wrote or co-wrote all the songs. "Her brutally dark take on intimate relationships was reflected throughout and made clear the marital problems that had been hinted at on earlier albums."<sup id="cite_ref-rollingstone_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> "Highly autobiographical (though Cash has often insisted it isn't quite as true to life as everyone assumes), Interiors was a brilliant, introspective album"<sup id="cite_ref-popentertainment_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-popentertainment-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and "her masterpiece".<sup id="cite_ref-allmusicjurek_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusicjurek-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Other critics called it "maudlin"<sup id="cite_ref-centuryofcountry_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-centuryofcountry-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and "pessimistic".<sup id="cite_ref-oldies_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oldies-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> <i>Interiors</i> topped many best album lists in 1990<sup id="cite_ref-popentertainment_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-popentertainment-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and received a Grammy award nomination for Best Contemporary Folk Album. It yielded one Top 40 single ("What We Really Want") and marked the beginning of sharp commercial decline for Cash.
</p><p>Though it may have been inspired by the breakup of her marriage, it also signified her departure from Nashville and its country music establishment.<sup id="cite_ref-OxAm2013_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OxAm2013-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 1991 Cash relocated to New York City; in 1992, she and Crowell divorced.<sup id="cite_ref-cmt_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cmt-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wheel_(Rosanne_Cash_album)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Wheel (Rosanne Cash album)">The Wheel</a></i>, released in 1993, was "an unflinchingly confessional examination of the marriage's failure that ranked as her most musically diverse effort to date".<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The album was Cash's last for Columbia Records. It received considerable acclaim from critics, though neither of its two singles, "The Wheel" or "You Won't Let Me In", charted.
</p>
<h3><span id="1995.E2.80.93present:_New_York.2C_new_albums_and_books"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1995–present:_New_York,_new_albums_and_books">1995–present: New York, new albums and books</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosanne_Cash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1995–present: New York, new albums and books">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Cash settled in lower <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> and in 1995 married producer/songwriter/guitarist <a href="/wiki/John_Leventhal" title="John Leventhal">John Leventhal</a>, with whom she had co-produced <i>The Wheel</i>. She signed with <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Records" title="Capitol Records">Capitol Records</a> and in 1996 released <i><a href="/wiki/10_Song_Demo" title="10 Song Demo">10 Song Demo</a></i>, a collection of stark home recordings with minimal accompaniment. She also pursued a career as a writer and in 1996 <a href="/wiki/Hyperion_(publisher)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hyperion (publisher)">Hyperion</a> published the short story collection <i>Bodies of Water</i> to favorable reviews.<sup id="cite_ref-rollingstone_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In 1997, Cash was awarded an honorary doctorate from <a href="/wiki/Memphis_College_of_Art" title="Memphis College of Art">Memphis College of Art</a>. She gave the commencement address that year<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and continues to speak publicly on writing and music.
</p><p>In 1998, she and Leventhal began working on what would later become <i>Rules of Travel</i>. The recording sessions were cut short when she became pregnant and was unable to sing for two and a half years, due to a <a href="/wiki/Polyp_(medicine)" title="Polyp (medicine)">polyp</a> on her vocal cords.<sup id="cite_ref-oldies_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oldies-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Unable to record, Cash focused on her writing. Her children's book <i>Penelope Jane: A Fairy's Tale</i>, which included an exclusive CD, was published by <a href="/wiki/Harper_Collins" class="mw-redirect" title="Harper Collins">Harper Collins</a> in 2000, and in 2001 she edited a collection of short fiction by songwriters titled <i>Songs Without Rhyme: Prose by Celebrated Songwriters</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-rollingstone_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rollingstone-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Recovering her voice, she resumed recording and in 2003 released <i><a href="/wiki/Rules_of_Travel" title="Rules of Travel">Rules of Travel</a></i>, her first full-fledged studio album for Capitol. The album had guest appearances by <a href="/wiki/Sheryl_Crow" title="Sheryl Crow">Sheryl Crow</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steve_Earle" title="Steve Earle">Steve Earle</a>, a song co-written by <a href="/wiki/Joe_Henry" title="Joe Henry">Joe Henry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jakob_Dylan" title="Jakob Dylan">Jakob Dylan</a>, and the poignant "September When It Comes," a duet with her father.<sup id="cite_ref-cmt_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cmt-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> <i>Rules of Travel</i> was nominated for a 2003 Grammy Award for Best Contemporary Folk Album.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cash was also an inaugural member of the <a href="/wiki/AIM_Independent_Music_Awards" title="AIM Independent Music Awards">Independent Music Awards'</a> judging panel to support independent artists.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2005, <a href="/wiki/Legacy_Recordings" title="Legacy Recordings">Legacy Recordings</a> reissued <i>Seven Year Ache</i> (1981), <i>King's Record Shop</i> (1987), and <i>Interiors</i> (1990), plus a new collection spanning 1979–2003, <i>The Very Best of Rosanne Cash</i>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Rosanne_Cash_SXSW_2006_crop.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Rosanne_Cash_SXSW_2006_crop.jpg/220px-Rosanne_Cash_SXSW_2006_crop.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="237" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Rosanne_Cash_SXSW_2006_crop.jpg/330px-Rosanne_Cash_SXSW_2006_crop.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c8/Rosanne_Cash_SXSW_2006_crop.jpg 2x" data-file-width="438" data-file-height="471" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Rosanne_Cash_SXSW_2006_crop.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Rosanne Cash at the 2006 South by Southwest</div></div></div>
<p>In 2006, Cash released <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Cadillac_(album)" title="Black Cadillac (album)">Black Cadillac</a></i>, an album marked by the loss of stepmother <a href="/wiki/June_Carter" class="mw-redirect" title="June Carter">June</a>, and father Johnny, who both died in 2003, as well as mother Vivian, Johnny's first wife, who died as Rosanne finished the album in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-netglimpse_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-netglimpse-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> The album was critically praised, and named to the Top 10 lists of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-metacritic_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-metacritic-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <i>Billboard</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> PopMatters,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> NPR<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> and other general interest and music publications. The album was nominated for a 2006 Grammy Award for Best Contemporary Folk/Americana Album.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cash toured extensively in support of the album, and created a <a href="/wiki/Multimedia" title="Multimedia">multimedia</a> performance, with video, imagery and narration drawn from the songs and from Cash's family history.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In 2006, a short documentary by filmmaker Steve Lippman, "Mariners and Musicians", based on the album and interviews with Cash, premiered at the <a href="/wiki/Tribeca_Film_Festival" title="Tribeca Film Festival">Tribeca Film Festival</a> and was screened at festivals worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Cash's music was also featured prominently in an <a href="/wiki/American_Masters" title="American Masters">American Masters</a> biography of photographer <a href="/wiki/Annie_Leibovitz" title="Annie Leibovitz">Annie Leibovitz</a>, who has photographed Cash and her family numerous times.<sup id="cite_ref-pbs_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pbs-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In late 2007, Cash underwent <a href="/wiki/Brain_surgery" class="mw-redirect" title="Brain surgery">brain surgery</a> for a rare condition (<a href="/wiki/Arnold-Chiari_malformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Arnold-Chiari malformation">Chiari I malformation</a>) and was forced to cancel her remaining concert dates.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> After a successful recovery,<sup id="cite_ref-cmt.com_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cmt.com-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> she resumed writing and live appearances. In 2008 she wrote for <i>Measure for Measure</i>, the songwriters' column in <i>The New York Times</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> recorded with <a href="/wiki/Kris_Kristofferson" title="Kris Kristofferson">Kris Kristofferson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Costello" title="Elvis Costello">Elvis Costello</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> and appeared on Costello's TV series <i>Spectacle</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cash released her next studio album, entitled <i><a href="/wiki/The_List_(album)" title="The List (album)">The List</a></i>, on October 6, 2009. The album is based on a list of 100 greatest country and American songs that Johnny Cash gave her when she was 18.<sup id="cite_ref-aboutfa_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aboutfa-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Cash picked 12 songs out of the 100 for the album. The album features vocal duets with <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Springsteen" title="Bruce Springsteen">Bruce Springsteen</a>, Elvis Costello, <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Tweedy" title="Jeff Tweedy">Jeff Tweedy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rufus_Wainwright" title="Rufus Wainwright">Rufus Wainwright</a>. An <a href="/wiki/ITunes_Store" title="ITunes Store">iTunes Store</a>-only 13th song features a duet with <a href="/wiki/Neko_Case" title="Neko Case">Neko Case</a>. On September 9, 2010, the Americana Music Association named <i><a href="/wiki/The_List_(album)" title="The List (album)">The List</a></i> the Album of the Year.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to her own recordings, Cash has made guest appearances on albums by <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Bridges" title="Jeff Bridges">Jeff Bridges</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Crowell" title="Rodney Crowell">Rodney Crowell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guy_Clark" title="Guy Clark">Guy Clark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vince_Gill" title="Vince Gill">Vince Gill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lyle_Lovett" title="Lyle Lovett">Lyle Lovett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Chapin_Carpenter" title="Mary Chapin Carpenter">Mary Chapin Carpenter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marc_Cohn" title="Marc Cohn">Marc Cohn</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Chieftains" title="The Chieftains">The Chieftains</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Stewart_(musician)" title="John Stewart (musician)">John Stewart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Willy_Mason" title="Willy Mason">Willy Mason</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Doughty" title="Mike Doughty">Mike Doughty</a>, and others, as well as children's albums by <a href="/wiki/Larry_Kirwan" title="Larry Kirwan">Larry Kirwan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tom_Chapin" title="Tom Chapin">Tom Chapin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dan_Zanes_and_Friends" class="mw-redirect" title="Dan Zanes and Friends">Dan Zanes and Friends</a>. She has also appeared on tribute albums to <a href="/wiki/The_Band" title="The Band">The Band</a>, <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Cash" title="Johnny Cash">Johnny Cash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Woody_Guthrie" title="Woody Guthrie">Woody Guthrie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">Jimi Hendrix</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Hiatt" title="John Hiatt">John Hiatt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kris_Kristofferson" title="Kris Kristofferson">Kris Kristofferson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Laura_Nyro" title="Laura Nyro">Laura Nyro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yoko_Ono" title="Yoko Ono">Yoko Ono</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doc_Pomus" title="Doc Pomus">Doc Pomus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tammy_Wynette" title="Tammy Wynette">Tammy Wynette</a>.
</p><p>Cash wrote New York Times Bestseller <i>Composed: A Memoir</i> in 2010 "a pointillistic memoir about growing up with and without her father, and about how she slid out from under his shadow to become a gifted artist in her own right."<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 2011, Cash performed with the Minnesota Orchestra. In preparation for the event, she worked with composer Stephen Barber to orchestrate nine of her songs.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The tourism organization Brand USA enlisted Cash to develop a song to promote foreign tourism to the United States. In April 2012, she released the song "Land of Dreams", which was utilized by Brand USA in video advertisements and online as part of a global tourism campaign.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On February 6, 2012, Cash received the AFTRA Media and Entertainment Excellence Award in Sound Recordings.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cash sang the part of Monique on the 2013 album <i><a href="/wiki/Ghost_Brothers_of_Darkland_County" title="Ghost Brothers of Darkland County">Ghost Brothers of Darkland County</a></i>, a collaboration between rock singer <a href="/wiki/John_Mellencamp" title="John Mellencamp">John Mellencamp</a> and novelist <a href="/wiki/Stephen_King" title="Stephen King">Stephen King</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cash gave the closing speech at the Association of Performing Arts Presenters' conference, APAP|NYC, in January 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Rosanne Cash signed with Blue Note Records in 2013 to release a new original album. <i><a href="/wiki/The_River_%26_the_Thread" title="The River &amp; the Thread">The River &amp; the Thread</a></i> was released on January 14, 2014. It was Cash's first album in more than four years.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>The River &amp; the Thread</i> is a collection of songs written with husband and collaborator John Leventhal, inspired by trips through the American South. Cash describes <i>The River &amp; The Thread</i> as "a mini-travelogue of the South, and of the soul." The Journey included visits to father Johnny Cash's childhood home in Dyess, Arkansas; her own early childhood home in Memphis, TN; William Faulkner's house; <a href="/wiki/Dockery_Plantation" title="Dockery Plantation">Dockery Plantation</a> in Cleveland, MS, the plantation where Howlin' Wolf and Charley Patton worked and sang; Natchez, MS; the blues trail; the Tallahatchie Bridge; as well as a visit with Natalie Chanin, a master seamstress in Florence, Alabama.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Throughout 2014, Cash toured extensively with partner John Leventhal, performing <i>The River &amp; The Thread</i> in sequence with first-person stories woven through historical time to much critical acclaim.<sup id="cite_ref-rosannecash.com_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rosannecash.com-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> <i>The River &amp; The Thread</i> was the Number One album of 2014 on Americana radio, and was honored by <i><a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Huffington_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="The Huffington Post">The Huffington Post</a>, NPR Fresh Air, <a href="/wiki/Uncut_(magazine)" title="Uncut (magazine)">Uncut (magazine)</a>, No Depression, <a href="/wiki/The_Sun_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Sun (UK)">The Sun (UK)</a>,</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/American_Songwriter" title="American Songwriter">American Songwriter</a></i> as one of the top albums of 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-rosannecash.com_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rosannecash.com-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On February 8, 2015, Cash won three Grammy awards for Best Americana Album for <i>The River &amp; The Thread</i>, and Best American Roots Song with John Leventhal and Best American Roots Performance for "A Feather's Not A Bird".<sup id="cite_ref-grammy.com_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-grammy.com-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2015, Cash was inducted into the Nashville Songwriters Hall of Fame,<sup id="cite_ref-billboard.com_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-billboard.com-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> honored as Artist-in-Residence at the Country Hall Music Hall of Fame and Museum,<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> and selected as Carnegie Hall's 2015-2016 Perspective Series Artist.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, Cash signed with <a href="/wiki/ICM_Partners" title="ICM Partners">ICM Partners</a> and is working on a new album entitled "She Remembers Everything", scheduled for release on November 2.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosanne_Cash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosanne_Cash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cash_and_Leventhal_2013.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Cash_and_Leventhal_2013.jpg/220px-Cash_and_Leventhal_2013.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="185" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Cash_and_Leventhal_2013.jpg/330px-Cash_and_Leventhal_2013.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Cash_and_Leventhal_2013.jpg/440px-Cash_and_Leventhal_2013.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1972" data-file-height="1661" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cash_and_Leventhal_2013.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Cash and Leventhal 2013</div></div></div>
<p>Cash's parents, Johnny Cash and Vivian Liberto, were married in <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio,_Texas" class="mw-redirect" title="San Antonio, Texas">San Antonio, Texas</a>, in 1954. She has three younger sisters, Kathy, Cindy, and Tara.<sup id="cite_ref-OxAm2013_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OxAm2013-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Johnny and Vivian divorced in 1966, and he married <a href="/wiki/June_Carter_Cash" title="June Carter Cash">June Carter</a> in 1968. Cash's stepsisters are country singers <a href="/wiki/Carlene_Carter" title="Carlene Carter">Carlene Carter</a> (from June’s marriage to singer <a href="/wiki/Carl_Smith_(musician)" title="Carl Smith (musician)">Carl Smith</a>) and the late <a href="/wiki/Rosie_Nix_Adams" title="Rosie Nix Adams">Rosie Nix Adams</a>, also known as Rosie Carter (from June’s marriage to Edwin “Rip” Nix). Johnny and June's son, <a href="/wiki/John_Carter_Cash" title="John Carter Cash">John Carter Cash</a>, is Rosanne's half-brother. Cash's stepmother and father died in 2003.
</p><p>Cash married country music singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Rodney_Crowell" title="Rodney Crowell">Rodney Crowell</a> in 1979. They have three daughters: Caitlin, Chelsea, and Carrie. Cash also raised Crowell's daughter, Hannah, from a previous marriage. Cash and Crowell divorced in 1992. She married her second husband, <a href="/wiki/John_Leventhal" title="John Leventhal">John Leventhal</a>, in 1995, and they have one son, Jakob. Cash lives with her husband and son in <a href="/wiki/Chelsea,_Manhattan" title="Chelsea, Manhattan">Chelsea, Manhattan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health">Health</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosanne_Cash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Health">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On November 27, 2007, Cash was admitted to New York's Presbyterian Hospital for brain surgery. In a press statement, she announced that she suffered from <a href="/wiki/Chiari_malformation" title="Chiari malformation">Chiari malformation type I</a> and expected to "make a full recovery".<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> The surgery was successful,<sup id="cite_ref-cmt.com_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cmt.com-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> though recovery was slow, and in March 2008 she was forced to cancel her spring tour dates for further recuperation. She wrote about the experience in her <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> article "Well, Actually, It <i>Is</i> Brain Surgery".<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> She resumed writing, recording and performing in late summer of 2008.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_projects">Other projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosanne_Cash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Cash supports several charitable organizations. She is a longtime board member of The Center To Prevent Youth Violence (CPYV), formally known as PAX,<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> an organization dedicating to preventing gun violence among children. She was honored by PAX at their fifth annual benefit gala in 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cash is a frequent guest teacher at the English and Songwriting programs of various colleges and universities including <a href="/wiki/LeMoyne%E2%80%93Owen_College" title="LeMoyne–Owen College">LeMoyne</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Fairleigh_Dickinson_University" title="Fairleigh Dickinson University">Farleigh-Dickinson</a><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/NYU" class="mw-redirect" title="NYU">NYU</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cash has been associated with <a href="/wiki/Children_Incorporated" title="Children Incorporated">Children, Incorporated</a> for more than 25 years and has sponsored many children through the organization, which works to support and educate needy children and young adults worldwide.<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cash was elected to the <a href="/wiki/Century_Association" title="Century Association">Century Association</a> in 2009<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>She also works with <a href="/wiki/Arkansas_State_University" title="Arkansas State University">Arkansas State University</a> on the Johnny Cash Boyhood Home project, which is working to restore her father's childhood home in <a href="/wiki/Dyess,_Arkansas" title="Dyess, Arkansas">Dyess, Arkansas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> The Cash family has supported the restoration by raising money through annual music festivals. Rosanne hosted the first and second annual Johnny Cash Music Festivals in 2011 and 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2014 Cash contributed essays to the <i><a href="/wiki/The_Oxford_American" class="mw-redirect" title="The Oxford American">Oxford-American</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> and the book of collected essays edited by Sari Botton <i>Never Can Say Goodbye: Writers On Their Unshakable Love For New York</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> She was also featured in Gael Towey's <i>Portraits in Creativity</i> as a featured artist for her Profile Series.<sup>[<i>citation needed</i>]</sup>
</p><p>Cash is a dedicated supporter of artists' rights in the digital age and sits on the board of the Content Creators Coalition. On June 25, 2014, Cash testified before The House of Representatives, Judiciary Committee on intellectual property rights and Internet music licensing.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2018, Cash was a recipient of an honorary doctorate degree from Berklee College of Music.
</p>
