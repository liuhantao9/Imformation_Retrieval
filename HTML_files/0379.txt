	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Nomen nescio</i></h1>

<p><i><b>Nomen nescio</b></i> (<small>pronounced&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Latin" title="Help:IPA/Latin">[ˈnoːmẽ ˈnɛskɪ.oː]</a></span>), abbreviated to <i><b>N.N.</b></i>, is used to signify an <a href="/wiki/Anonymity" title="Anonymity">anonymous</a> or unnamed person. From Latin <i>nomen</i>, "name", and <i>nescio</i>, "I do not know", it literally means "I do not know the name".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The generic name <a href="/wiki/Numerius_Negidius" title="Numerius Negidius">Numerius Negidius</a> used in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Roman</a> times was chosen partly because it shared initials with this phrase.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nomen_nescio&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>One use for this name is to protect against retaliation when reporting a <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a> or company <a href="/wiki/Fraud" title="Fraud">fraud</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, a police suspect who refuses to give his name is given an "N.N. number." In <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <i>N.N.</i> is also frequently seen in <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> course lists, indicating that a course will take place but that the lecturer is not yet known; the abbreviation in this case means <i>nomen nominandum</i> ("the name is to be announced"). Thus, the meaning is different from the above definition and is the same as <a href="/wiki/TBD" class="mw-redirect" title="TBD">TBD</a> (to be decided).
</p><p><i>N. N.</i> is commonly used in the scoring of <a href="/wiki/Chess" title="Chess">chess</a> games,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> not only when one participant's name is genuinely unknown but when an untitled player faces a master, as in a <a href="/wiki/Simultaneous_exhibition" title="Simultaneous exhibition">simultaneous exhibition</a>. Another reason is to protect a known player from the insult of a painful defeat.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Genealogy" title="Genealogy">Genealogists</a> often use the abbreviation to signify an unknown or partially unknown name (such as <i>N.N.</i> Jones).
</p>
