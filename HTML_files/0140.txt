<p><b>Saramaccan</b> (<a href="/wiki/Endonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Endonym">autonym</a>: <i>Saamáka</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">creole language</a> spoken by about 58,000 ethnic African people near the <a href="/wiki/Saramacca_River" title="Saramacca River">Saramacca</a> and upper <a href="/wiki/Suriname_River" title="Suriname River">Suriname River</a>, as well as in the capital <a href="/wiki/Paramaribo" title="Paramaribo">Paramaribo</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> (formerly also known as <a href="/wiki/Surinam_(Dutch_colony)" title="Surinam (Dutch colony)">Dutch Guiana</a>), 25,000 in <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">French Guiana</a>, and 8,000 in the Netherlands.<sup id="cite_ref-Price2013_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Price2013-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It has three main dialects. The speakers are mostly descendants of fugitive <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">slaves</a> who were native to <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Africa" title="Central Africa">Central Africa</a>; they form a group called Saamacca, also spelled <a href="/wiki/Saramaka" title="Saramaka">Saramaka</a>.
</p><p>Linguists consider Saramaccan notable because its vocabulary is based on two European source languages, <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> (30%) and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a> (20%), and various West and Central African languages (50%), but it diverges considerably from all of them. The African component accounts for about 50% once ritual use is taken into account, the highest percentage in the Americas, and is derived from <a href="/wiki/Niger%E2%80%93Congo_languages" title="Niger–Congo languages">Niger–Congo languages</a> of West Africa, especially <a href="/wiki/Fon_language" title="Fon language">Fon</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Gbe_languages" title="Gbe languages">Gbe languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Akan_language" title="Akan language">Akan</a> and Central African languages such as <a href="/wiki/Kikongo" class="mw-redirect" title="Kikongo">Kikongo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saramaccan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Saramaccan lexicon is largely drawn from English, Portuguese, and, to a lesser extent, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>, among European languages, and <a href="/wiki/Niger-Congo_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="Niger-Congo languages">Niger-Congo languages</a> of West Africa, especially <a href="/wiki/Fon_language" title="Fon language">Fon</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Gbe_languages" title="Gbe languages">Gbe languages</a>, <a href="/wiki/Akan_language" title="Akan language">Akan</a>, and Central African languages, such as <a href="/wiki/KiKongo" class="mw-redirect" title="KiKongo">KiKongo</a>. The African component accounts for about 50% of the total.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Saramaccan <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">phonology</a> has traits similar to languages of West Africa. It has developed the use of <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">tones</a>, which are common in Africa, rather than <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">stress</a>, which is typical of European languages.
</p><p>Over a fourth of words are from English. It is generally agreed that the Portuguese influence originated from enslaved peoples who lived on plantations with Portuguese masters and possibly with other slaves who spoke a Portuguese creole. The masters might have brought the latter in migrating to Suriname from <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Saramaccan originators began with an early form of <a href="/wiki/Sranan_Tongo" title="Sranan Tongo">Sranan Tongo</a>, an English-based creole, and transformed it into a new creole via the Portuguese influx, combined with influence from the grammars of <a href="/wiki/Fongbe_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Fongbe language">Fongbe</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Gbe_languages" title="Gbe languages">Gbe languages</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An earlier idea that Saramaccan was an offshoot of a Portuguese <a href="/wiki/Pidgin" title="Pidgin">pidgin</a> that had been spoken by slaves who had learned it on the West African coast is no longer<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (January 2013)">dubious</span></a>&#32;<span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Saramaccan_language#Dubious" title="Talk:Saramaccan language">discuss</a></span></i>&#93;</sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> subscribed to by working creolists.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Monogenetic_theory_of_pidgins#Evidence" title="Monogenetic theory of pidgins"><span title="This text contradicts material at the article &#39;Monogenetic theory of pidgins#Evidence&#39;. (January 2013)">contradictory</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> See <a href="/wiki/Monogenetic_theory_of_pidgins" title="Monogenetic theory of pidgins">monogenetic theory of pidgins</a> for more information.
</p><p>Certain common words in Sranan Tongo, the most common creole spoken in Suriname, also derive from Portuguese words, but it remains a largely English-based creole.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">Dialects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saramaccan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dialects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Saramaccan is divided into three main dialects. The Upper Suriname River dialect and the Lower Suriname River dialect are both spoken by members of the <a href="/wiki/Saramaka" title="Saramaka">Saramaccan tribe</a>. The Matawari tribe has its own dialect.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tone">Tone</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saramaccan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tone">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The language has two surface <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">tones</a>: high and low. <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">Stress</a> in European words is replaced by high tone in Saramaccan.<sup id="cite_ref-bsv170_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bsv170-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lexicon">Lexicon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saramaccan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lexicon">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Saramaccan's vocabulary is 30% derived from <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>, 20% from <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>. It is one of the few known creoles to derive a large percentage of its lexicon from more than one source (most creoles have one main <a href="/wiki/Lexifier" title="Lexifier">lexifier</a> language). Also, it is said to be both an <a href="/wiki/English-based_creole_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="English-based creole languages">English-based creole</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Creole" class="mw-redirect" title="Portuguese Creole">Portuguese-based creole</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bsv168-189_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bsv168-189-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>About 50% of the vocabulary of Saramaccan is of <a href="/wiki/African_languages" class="mw-redirect" title="African languages">African</a> origin,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> the largest percentage of any creole in the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>. Source languages for these words include <a href="/wiki/Kikongo_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Kikongo language">Kikongo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gbe_languages" title="Gbe languages">Gbe languages</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Twi" title="Twi">Twi</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bsv168-189_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bsv168-189-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saramaccan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To English-speakers not familiar with it, the English basis of this language is almost unrecognizable. Here are some examples of Saramaccan sentences, taken from the SIL dictionary:
</p><p><i>De waka te de aan sinkii möön.</i><br />"They walked until they were worn out."
</p><p><i>U ta mindi kanda fu dee soni dee ta pasa ku u.</i><br />"We make up songs about things that happen to us."
</p><p><i>A suku di soni te wojo fëën ko bëë.</i><br />"He searched for it in vain."
</p><p><i>Mi puu tu dusu kölu bai ën.</i><br />"I paid two thousand guilders to buy it."
</p><p>Examples of words originally from Portuguese or a Portuguese creole are <i>mujee</i> (<i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">mulher</i>) "woman", <i>womi</i> (<i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">o homem</i>) "man", <i>da</i> (<i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">dar</i>) "to give", <i>bunu</i> (<i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">bom</i>) "good", <i>kaba</i> (<i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">acabar</i>) "to end", <i>ku</i> (<i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">com</i>) "with", <i>kuma</i> (<i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">como</i>, cf. vernacular Brazilian <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">cuma?</i> for <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">como é?</i>, "come again?") "as, like", <i>faka</i> (<i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">faca</i>) "knife", <i>aki</i> (<i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">aqui</i>) "here", <i>ma</i> (<i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">mas</i>) "but", <i>kendi</i> (<i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">quente</i>) "hot", <i>liba</i> (<i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">riba</i>) "above", and <i>lio</i> (<i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">rio</i>) "river".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Saramaccan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Two books have now been published in Saamaka, at the request of the Saamaka people, who have distributed them in their schools:<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <i>Fesiten</i> and <i>Boo go a Kontukonde</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Both have used the orthography that is now accepted by the Saamaka people, which was developed by Saamaka linguist Vinije Haabo.
</p>
