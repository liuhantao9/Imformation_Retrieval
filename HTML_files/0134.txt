<p><b>Elisabeth Noelle-Neumann</b> (19 December 1916 – 25 March 2010) was a <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a> <a href="/wiki/Political_scientist" class="mw-redirect" title="Political scientist">political scientist</a>. Her most famous contribution is the model of the <a href="/wiki/Spiral_of_silence" title="Spiral of silence">spiral of silence</a>, detailed in <i>The Spiral of Silence&#160;: Public Opinion – Our Social Skin</i>. The model is an explanation of how perceived <a href="/wiki/Public_opinion" title="Public opinion">public opinion</a> can influence individual opinions or actions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elisabeth_Noelle-Neumann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Elisabeth Noelle was born to Ernst and Eve Noelle in 1916 in <a href="/wiki/Dahlem_(Berlin)" title="Dahlem (Berlin)">Dahlem</a>, a suburb of Berlin.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> First Elisabeth went to several schools in Berlin and then switched to the prestigious <a href="/wiki/Schule_Schloss_Salem" title="Schule Schloss Salem">Salem Castle School</a>, which she also left one year later. She earned her <a href="/wiki/Abitur" title="Abitur">Abitur</a> in 1935 in <a href="/wiki/University_of_G%C3%B6ttingen" title="University of Göttingen">Göttingen</a> and then studied <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a>, <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">history</a>, <a href="/wiki/Journalism" title="Journalism">journalism</a>, and <a href="/wiki/American_studies" title="American studies">American studies</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_University_of_Berlin" title="Humboldt University of Berlin">Friedrich Wilhelm University</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg_Albertina_University" class="mw-redirect" title="Königsberg Albertina University">Königsberg Albertina University</a>. When she visited <a href="/wiki/Obersalzberg" title="Obersalzberg">Obersalzberg</a>, she by chance had an encounter with <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">Adolf Hitler</a>, which she later called "one of the most intensive and strangest experiences in her life".<sup id="cite_ref-clauer_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-clauer-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> She stayed in the US from 1937 to 1938 and studied at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Missouri" title="University of Missouri">University of Missouri</a>. In 1940 she received her <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">Ph.D.</a> concentrating on public opinion research in the USA.
</p><p>In 1940 she briefly worked for the <a href="/wiki/Nazism" title="Nazism">Nazi</a> newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Das_Reich_(newspaper)" title="Das Reich (newspaper)">Das Reich</a></i>. On 8 June 1941 <i>Das Reich</i> published Noelle-Neumann's article entitled "Who Informs America?" in which she propagated the idea that a <a href="/wiki/Jew" class="mw-redirect" title="Jew">Jewish</a> syndicate ran the American media.  She wrote, "Jews write in the paper, own them, have virtually monopolized the advertising agencies and can therefore open and shut the gates of advertising income as they wish." She was fired when she exchanged unfavourable photos of <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> for better looking ones. She then worked for the <i><a href="/wiki/Frankfurter_Zeitung" title="Frankfurter Zeitung">Frankfurter Zeitung</a></i> until it was banned in 1943.
</p><p>In 1947 she and her first husband <a href="/w/index.php?title=Erich_Peter_Neumann&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Erich Peter Neumann (page does not exist)">Erich Peter Neumann</a> founded a <a href="/wiki/Opinion_poll" title="Opinion poll">public opinion research</a> organization—the <i><a href="/wiki/Institut_f%C3%BCr_Demoskopie_Allensbach" class="mw-redirect" title="Institut für Demoskopie Allensbach">Institut für Demoskopie Allensbach</a></i>, which today is one of the best known and most prestigious polling organizations in Germany. She, along with her husband, created the first German opinion-polling body.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From 1964 to 1983 she held a professorate at the <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Gutenberg_University_of_Mainz" class="mw-redirect" title="Johannes Gutenberg University of Mainz">Johannes Gutenberg University of Mainz</a>.
</p><p>Noelle-Neumann was the president of the <a href="/wiki/World_Association_for_Public_Opinion_Research" title="World Association for Public Opinion Research">World Association for Public Opinion Research</a> from 1978 to 1980 and worked as a guest <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">professor</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a> from 1978 to 1991.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Allegations_of_anti-Semitism">Allegations of anti-Semitism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elisabeth_Noelle-Neumann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Allegations of anti-Semitism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1991, <a href="/wiki/Leo_Bogart" title="Leo Bogart">Leo Bogart</a> criticized Noelle-Neumann, accusing her of anti-Semitic passages in her dissertation and articles she wrote for Nazi newspapers. As a young woman, she had "superb credentials as an activist and leader" of Nazi youth and students' organizations, he wrote.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In fact, when she published her 1940 dissertation in Germany, entitled "Opinion and mass research in the USA", having spent a year at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Missouri" title="University of Missouri">University of Missouri</a> researching <a href="/wiki/George_Gallup" title="George Gallup">George Gallup</a>'s methodology, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Goebbels" title="Joseph Goebbels">Joseph Goebbels</a> called the 24-year-old woman as an adjutant and intended her to build up, for the ministry of propaganda, Germany's first public opinion research organization. She declined, having fallen ill, which angered Goebbels; she later became a newspaper journalist with Nazi publications where she wrote some articles on Jewish influence over U.S. news and elite opinion.
</p><p>Bogart's article appeared just weeks before Noelle-Neumann took up a visiting position in the Political Science Department at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Chicago" title="University of Chicago">University of Chicago</a>, where she had held similar appointments since 1978. <a href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Kochin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Michael Kochin (page does not exist)">Michael Kochin</a>, then a graduate student at the university, noticed the article and circulated it on campus prior to her arrival,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> igniting a vigorous debate on Noelle-Neumann's past.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> While the administration and students at the university,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> the local Jewish defense groups,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and Chicago newspapers<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> remained disengaged from the issue, <a href="/wiki/John_J._Mearsheimer" class="mw-redirect" title="John J. Mearsheimer">John J. Mearsheimer</a>, then chairman of the university's political science department, spoke with Bogart, met for over three hours with Noelle-Neumann,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and called a departmental meeting about her on October 16.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Some at the university claimed Noelle-Neumann was being slandered, and Mearsheimer's colleagues were not of one opinion about the case. Mearsheimer, however, widely publicized his views concerning the allegations themselves and as they related to academic freedom and opposition to bigotry. "I believe Noelle-Neumann was an anti-Semite," Mearsheimer stated, "and was not forced to write the anti-Semitic words she published. Moreover, I believe that the anti-Semitic writers and publicists of Germany – to include Noelle-Neumann – jointly share some responsibility for the Holocaust. For this she owes an apology."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> "The thing to remember about the killing of the Jews," he said, "is that it was not done by a handful of people. … It was also a result of the Reich of normal – or of average – German citizens. Like Elisabeth Noelle-Neumann."<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In private letters and in written responses, Noelle-Neumann acknowledged being in a Nazi student organization but denied being a Nazi. "I am anguished by the suffering of Jews in Nazi Germany," she wrote.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Bogart, Mearsheimer and others remained dissatisfied with her response.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Noelle-Neumann completed her visiting position in Chicago in mid-December 1991 and returned to Germany. When some University of Chicago students learned that she was to return there on March 13, 1992, they called a rally to protest against her return.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Reached by telephone at her office in Allensbach am Bodensee, Germany, on March 10, Noelle-Neumann told a reporter she was unaware of the proposed rally but intended on coming to the university as planned.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> That day, her hosts at the <a href="/wiki/National_Opinion_Research_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="National Opinion Research Center">National Opinion Research Center</a> announced that she had cancelled her appearance "in light of serious threats".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several years later, Noelle-Neumann's Nazi connection came under scrutiny from another American academic,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> but she never explicitly apologized for her past.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Interviewed on the subject in 1997, she said, "I did my duty and would do my duty again in a second life. I'd even say I was proud of what I did back then because I opposed the Nazis by working from within."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Mearsheimer" title="John Mearsheimer">John Mearsheimer</a>, Professor of Political Science at the University of Chicago wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> on December 16, 1991:</p><blockquote><p>"She has admitted she was not hostile to the Nazis before 1940. She says she was anti-Nazi after 1940, but has produced no evidence that she criticized the Nazis then. She wrote anti-Semitic words in 1938–41, and there is no evidence she was compelled to write them. Queried on her anti-Semitic writings, she told me: "I have never written anything in my life that I did not believe to be true."
</p></blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Elisabeth_Noelle-Neumann&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>She was married to the <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_(Germany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian Democratic Union (Germany)">Christian Democratic</a> politician Erich Peter Neumann (1912–1973) from 1946 until his death. She was married to the physicist <a href="/wiki/Heinz_Maier-Leibnitz" title="Heinz Maier-Leibnitz">Heinz Maier-Leibnitz</a> (1911–2000) from 1979 until his death.
</p>
