	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Warao language</h1>

<p><b>Warao</b> (also known as <b>Guarauno</b>, <b>Guarao</b>, <b>Warrau</b>) is the native language of the <a href="/wiki/Warao_people" title="Warao people">Warao people</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Language_isolate" title="Language isolate">language isolate</a>, it is spoken by about 33,000 people primarily in northern <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>. It is notable for its unusual <a href="/wiki/Object%E2%80%93subject%E2%80%93verb" title="Object–subject–verb">object–subject–verb</a> <a href="/wiki/Word_order" title="Word order">word order</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The 2015 Venezuelan  film <i><a href="/wiki/Gone_with_the_River" title="Gone with the River">Gone with the River</a></i> was spoken in Warao.<sup id="cite_ref-film_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-film-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warao_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The language had an estimated 28,100 speakers in <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> as of 2007. The <a href="/wiki/Warao_people" title="Warao people">Warao people</a> live chiefly in the  <a href="/wiki/Orinoco_Delta" title="Orinoco Delta">Orinoco Delta</a> region of northeastern Venezuela, with smaller communities in southwestern <a href="/wiki/Trinidad" title="Trinidad">Trinidad</a> (<a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">Trinidad and Tobago</a>), western <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">Guyana</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The language is considered endangered by <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warao_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Grammar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The language's basic <a href="/wiki/Word_order" title="Word order">word order</a> has been analyzed as <a href="/wiki/Object%E2%80%93subject%E2%80%93verb" title="Object–subject–verb">object–subject–verb</a>, a very rare word order among <a href="/wiki/Nominative%E2%80%93accusative_language" title="Nominative–accusative language">nominative–accusative languages</a> such as Warao.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>[b] and [d, l̆] are allophones of /<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">p</span>/ and /ɾ/. There are five oral vowels <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/a, ɛ, i, ɔ, u/</span> and five nasal vowels <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ã, ẽ, ĩ, õ, ũ/</span>. /u/ after /k/ within the beginning of words has a sound as [ɨ].<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relatives">Relatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Warao_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Relatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Warao appears to be a <a href="/wiki/Language_isolate" title="Language isolate">language isolate</a>, unrelated to any recorded language in the region or elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Terrence Kaufman (1994) included it in his hypothetical <a href="/wiki/Macro-Paezan" class="mw-redirect" title="Macro-Paezan">Macro-Paezan</a> family, but the necessary supporting work was never done.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Julian Granberry connected many of the grammatical forms, including nominal and verbal <a href="/wiki/Suffixes" class="mw-redirect" title="Suffixes">suffixes</a>, of Warao to the <a href="/wiki/Timucua_language" title="Timucua language">Timucua language</a> of North Florida, also a language isolate.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> However, he has also derives Timucua morphemes from Muskogean, Chibchan, Paezan, Arawakan, and other Amazonian languages, suggesting multi-language <a href="/wiki/Creolization" title="Creolization">creolization</a> as a possible explanation for these similarities. This notion has met with skepticism and described by <a href="/wiki/Lyle_Campbell" title="Lyle Campbell">Lyle Campbell</a> as "in no way convincing".<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Granberry also finds "Waroid" vocabulary items in <a href="/wiki/Goajiro_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Goajiro language">Guajiro</a> (from toponymic evidence it seems that the Warao or a related people once occupied Goajiro country) and in <a href="/wiki/Taino_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Taino language">Taino</a> (<i>nuçay/nozay</i> [nosái] "gold" in Ciboney — cf. Warao <i>naséi símo</i> "gold" (lit. "yellow pebble") — and <i>duho</i> "ceremonial stool" in Classic Taino — cf. Warao <i>duhu</i> "sit, stool"). Granberry &amp; Vescelius (2004) note that toponymic evidence suggests that the pre-Taino <a href="/wiki/Macorix_language" title="Macorix language">Macorix language</a> of Hispaniola and the <a href="/wiki/Guanahatabey_language" title="Guanahatabey language">Guanahatabey language</a> of Cuba may have been Waroid languages as well.
</p>
