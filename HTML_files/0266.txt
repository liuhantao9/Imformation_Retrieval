<p><b>Ewe</b> (<i>Èʋe</i> or <i>Èʋegbe</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">[èβeɡ͡be]</a></span>)<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Niger%E2%80%93Congo_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Niger–Congo language">Niger–Congo language</a> spoken in southeastern <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a> by approximately 4.5 million people as a first language and a million or so  more as a second language.<sup id="cite_ref-e21_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-e21-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Ewe is part of a cluster of related languages commonly called <a href="/wiki/Gbe_languages" title="Gbe languages">Gbe</a>; the other major Gbe language is <a href="/wiki/Fon_language" title="Fon language">Fon</a> of Benin. Like many African  languages, Ewe is <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">tonal</a>.
</p><p>The German Africanist <a href="/wiki/Diedrich_Hermann_Westermann" title="Diedrich Hermann Westermann">Diedrich Hermann Westermann</a> published many dictionaries and grammars of Ewe and several other Gbe languages. Other linguists who have worked on Ewe and closely related languages include <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gilbert_Ansre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gilbert Ansre (page does not exist)">Gilbert Ansre</a> (tone, syntax), Herbert Stahlke (morphology, tone), <a href="/wiki/Nick_Clements" title="Nick Clements">Nick Clements</a> (tone, syntax), Roberto Pazzi (anthropology, lexicography), Felix K. Ameka (semantics, cognitive linguistics), Alan Stewart Duthie (semantics, phonetics), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hounkpati_B._Capo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hounkpati B. Capo (page does not exist)">Hounkpati B. Capo</a> (phonology, phonetics), Enoch Aboh (syntax), and Chris Collins (syntax).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">Dialects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ewe_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Dialects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some of the commonly named Ewe ('Vhe') dialects are <i>Aŋlɔ, Tɔŋu (Tɔŋgu), <a href="/wiki/Avenor_Ewe" title="Avenor Ewe">Avenor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Agave_people" title="Agave people">Agave people</a>, Evedome, Awlan, Gbín, Pekí, Kpándo, Vhlin, Hó, Avɛ́no, Vo, Kpelen, Vɛ́, Danyi, Agu, Fodome, Wancé, Wací, Adángbe</i> (Capo).
</p><p><i>Ethnologue</i> 16 considers <a href="/wiki/Waci" class="mw-redirect" title="Waci">Waci</a> and Kpesi (Kpessi) to be distinct enough to be considered separate languages. They form a <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">dialect continuum</a> with Ewe and <a href="/wiki/Gen_language" title="Gen language">Gen</a> (Mina), which share a mutual intelligibility level of 85%;<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> the Ewe varieties Gbin, Ho, Kpelen, Kpesi, and Vhlin might be considered a third cluster of Western Gbe dialects between Ewe and Gen, but Kpesi is as close or closer to the Waci and Vo dialects, which remain in Ewe in that scenario. Waci intervenes geographically between Ewe proper and Gen; Kpesi forms a Gbe island in the <a href="/wiki/Kabye_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Kabye language">Kabye</a> area.
</p><p>Ewe is itself a dialect cluster of the Gbe languages, which include Gen, Aja, and Xwla and are spoken from the southern part of Ghana to Togo, Benin and Western Nigeria. All Gbe languages share at least some intelligibility with one another. Some coastal and southern dialects of Ewe include Aŋlɔ, Tongu (Tɔŋu), Avenor, Dzodze, and Watsyi. Some inland dialects indigenously characterized as Ewedomegbe include: Ho, Kpedze, Hohoe, Peki, Kpando, Liati,  Fódome, Danyi, and Kpele. Though there are many classifications, distinct variations exist between towns that are just miles away from one another.
</p>

<p><i>H</i> is a voiced fricative, which has been described as <a href="/wiki/Uvular_consonant" title="Uvular consonant">uvular</a>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ʁ]</span>, <a href="/wiki/Pharyngeal_consonant" title="Pharyngeal consonant">pharyngeal</a>, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ʕ]</span>, or glottal <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɦ]</span>.
</p><p>The nasal consonants <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[m, n, ɲ, ŋ]</span> are not distinctive since they appear before only nasal vowels; therefore, Ewe is sometimes said to have no nasal consonants. However, it is more economical to argue that nasal <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/m, n, ɲ, ŋ/</span> are the underlying form and so are denasalized before oral vowels. 
</p><p><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɣ]</span> occurs before unrounded (non-back) vowels and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[w]</span> before rounded (back) vowels.
</p><p>Ewe is one of the few languages known to contrast <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[f]</span> vs. <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɸ]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[v]</span> vs. <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[β]</span>. The <i>f</i> and <i>v</i> are stronger than in most languages, <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[f͈]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[v͈]</span>, with the upper lip noticeably raised, and thus more distinctive from the rather weak <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɸ]</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[β]</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/l/</span> may occur in consonant clusters. It becomes <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɾ]</span> (or <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ɾ̃]</span>) after coronals.
</p>

<p>The tilde (˜) marks nasal vowels, though the Peki dialect lacks <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/õ/</span>. Many varieties of Ewe lack one or another of the front mid vowels, and some varieties in Ghana have the additional vowels <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ə/</span> and <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">/ə̃/</span>.
</p><p>Ewe does not have a nasal–oral contrast in consonants. It does, however, have a syllabic nasal, which varies as <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[m n ŋ]</span>, depending on the following consonant, and carries tone. Some authors treat it as a vowel, with the odd result that Ewe would have more nasal than oral vowels, and one of these vowels has no set place of articulation. If it is taken to be a consonant, there is the odd result of a single nasal consonant that cannot appear before vowels. If nasal consonants are taken to underlie <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[b ɖ ɡ]</span>, however, there is no such odd restriction, and the only difference from other consonants is that only nasal stops may be syllabic, a common pattern cross-linguistically.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tones">Tones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ewe_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ewe is a <a href="/wiki/Tonal_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Tonal language">tonal language</a>.  In a tonal language, pitch differences are used to distinguish one word from another.  For example, in Ewe the following three words differ only by tones:
</p>
<ul><li>tó  'mountain' (High tone)</li>
<li>tǒ  'mortar'   (Rising tone)</li>
<li>tò  'buffalo'  (Low tone)</li></ul>
<p>Phonetically, there are three tone registers, High, Mid, and Low, and three rising and falling contour tones. However, in most Ewe dialects havee only two distinctive registers, High and Mid. These are <a href="/wiki/Depressor_consonant" title="Depressor consonant">depressed</a> in nouns after voiced obstruents: High becomes Mid (or Rising), and Mid becomes Low. Mid is also realized as Low at the end of a phrase or utterance, as in the example 'buffalo' above.
</p><p>In writing, tones are marked by acute accent, grave accent, caron, and circumflex. They may be used along with the tilde that marks nasal vowels.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pragmatics">Pragmatics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ewe_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Pragmatics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ewe has phrases of overt politeness, such as <i>meɖekuku</i> (meaning "please") and <i>akpe</i> (meaning "thank you").<sup id="cite_ref-omniglot_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-omniglot-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_system">Naming system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ewe_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Naming system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Ewe use a system of giving the first name to a child, based on the day of the week that the child was born. That arises from a belief that the real name of a child can be determined only after the child has shown its character. However, as a child is a person, not an object, the child must be referred to by some name in the interim and so a name is provided based on the day of birth. A final name is given at a naming ceremony, seven days after the date of birth.
</p><p>As a matter of pride in their heritage, especially since the 1970's, many educated Ewe, who were given Western names, have dropped those names formally/legally or informally and use their birthday name as their official name.
</p><p>The Ewe birthday-naming system is as follows:

<p>Often, people are called by their birthday name most of the time; the given name being used only on formal documents. In such cases, children with the same birth name are delineated by suffixes: -gã meaning big, -vi meaning little. So for example, after the birth of another Kofi, the first child called Kofi becomes Kofigã, and the new child Kofi. A subsequent Kofi, would be Kofivi, or (Kofitse mostly among Wedome and Tɔngu Ewes). Sometimes, the renaming happens twice, as the second Kofi might have originally been called Kofivi, while the eldest retained Kofi, thereby necessitating that they both be renamed on the birth of a third Kofi.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">Grammar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ewe_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Grammar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Expand_section plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg" class="image"><img alt="[icon]" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" decoding="async" width="20" height="14" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="44" data-file-height="31" /></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ewe_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">June 2009</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Ewe is a <a href="/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93verb%E2%80%93object" title="Subject–verb–object">subject–verb–object</a> language.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The possessive precedes the head noun.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">Adjectives</a>, numerals, demonstratives and <a href="/wiki/Relative_clause" title="Relative clause">relative clauses</a> follow the head noun. Ewe also has <a href="/wiki/Postposition" class="mw-redirect" title="Postposition">postpositions</a> rather than prepositions.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ewe is well known as a language having <a href="/wiki/Logophoric_pronoun" class="mw-redirect" title="Logophoric pronoun">logophoric pronouns</a>.  Such pronouns are used to refer to the source of a reported statement or thought in <a href="/wiki/Reported_speech" class="mw-redirect" title="Reported speech">indirect discourse</a>, and can disambiguate sentences that are ambiguous in most other languages. The following examples illustrate:
</p>
<ul><li>Kofi be e-dzo  'Kofi said he left' (he ≠ Kofi)</li>
<li>Kofi be yè-dzo 'Kofi said he left' (he = Kofi)</li></ul>
<p>In the second sentence, yè is the logophoric pronoun.
</p><p>Ewe also has a rich system of <a href="/wiki/Serial_verb_construction" title="Serial verb construction">serial verb constructions</a>.
</p>
