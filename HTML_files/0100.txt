	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Ever Ever After</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>"Ever Ever After"</b> is a song by American singer <a href="/wiki/Carrie_Underwood" title="Carrie Underwood">Carrie Underwood</a>, written by composer <a href="/wiki/Alan_Menken" title="Alan Menken">Alan Menken</a> and lyricist <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Schwartz_(composer)" title="Stephen Schwartz (composer)">Stephen Schwartz</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/Enchanted_(film)" title="Enchanted (film)">Enchanted</a> </i>(2007). The song, which was the last of several written for the film, appears as the fifth track on its <a href="/wiki/Enchanted_(soundtrack)" title="Enchanted (soundtrack)">soundtrack album</a>. A mid-tempo <a href="/wiki/Country_pop" title="Country pop">country pop</a> ballad that incorporates elements of both pop and rock music, the lyrics of "Ever Ever After" speak of falling in love and discovering one's happily ever after, as well as several other traditional elements that are often associated with fairy tales.
</p><p>"Ever Ever After" was written by Menken and Schwartz to replace a duet by actors <a href="/wiki/Idina_Menzel" title="Idina Menzel">Idina Menzel</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Marsden" title="James Marsden">James Marsden</a> that was meant to take place during the film's narrative. "Ever Ever After" appears as a voiceover following the film's climax. Most music critics have received the song with enthusiasm, praising its catchy melody and Underwood's vocals.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ever_Ever_After&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The musical numbers featured in <i><a href="/wiki/Enchanted_(2007_film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Enchanted (2007 film)">Enchanted</a></i> are meant to serve as <a href="/wiki/Plot_device" title="Plot device">plot devices</a>, gradually progressing and modernizing in terms of style, theme and composition as the film approaches its end, evolving from a traditional musical or Broadway score into a more contemporary one. The final song to appear in the film, composer <a href="/wiki/Alan_Menken" title="Alan Menken">Alan Menken</a> described "Ever Ever After" as the most modern of <i>Enchanted'</i>s musical numbers.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Actress <a href="/wiki/Amy_Adams" title="Amy Adams">Amy Adams</a>, who portrays the film's heroine <a href="/wiki/Giselle_(Disney)" class="mw-redirect" title="Giselle (Disney)">Giselle</a>, commented, "we end up with Carrie Underwood's 'Ever Ever After' which is a country rock ballad. So the music continues to evolve in the film".<sup id="cite_ref-:1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>"Ever Ever After" was the last song to be written for <i><a href="/wiki/Enchanted_(2007_film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Enchanted (2007 film)">Enchanted</a></i>. Originally, a traditional "Disney-style" musical number<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> titled  "Enchanted"<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> was written by Menken and Schwartz, scheduled to appear in the film as a duet between Broadway performer Idina Menzel, who portrays the character Nancy Tremaine, and actor James Marsden, who portrays Prince Edward.<sup id="cite_ref-:2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> However, the filmmakers got the impression that the duet only succeeded in slowing down the pace of film.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Menken explained, "it was just really extremely difficult that late in the game to deliver that kind of song."<sup id="cite_ref-:2_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Ultimately, the songwriters decided to replace it with country singer Carrie Underwood's voiceover performance "Ever Ever After" that occurs following the film's climax.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In the film, the song is first heard after <a href="/wiki/Giselle_(Disney)" class="mw-redirect" title="Giselle (Disney)">Giselle</a> and Robert finally confess their love for each other after they defeat Queen Narissa.  While Giselle and Robert reunite with Robert's daughter Morgan during the montage, Edward and Nancy, who were both at one point affianced to Giselle and Robert respectively, are shown deciding to marry each other.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">Composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ever_Ever_After&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Ever Ever After" is a country<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> power ballad,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> described by lyricist Stephen Schwartz as "a grown-up, contemporary pop number".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  The song also incorporates some elements of <a href="/wiki/Country_rock" title="Country rock">country rock</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> According to Musicnotes.com, "Ever Ever After" is written in <a href="/wiki/Common_time" class="mw-redirect" title="Common time">common time</a> in the key of <a href="/wiki/G_major" title="G major">G major</a> at a moderately fast tempo of 112 <a href="/wiki/Beats_per_minute" class="mw-redirect" title="Beats per minute">beats per minute</a>. The song's instrumentation includes both guitar and piano, as well as a combination of both lead and background vocals.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ever_Ever_After&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Ever Ever After" has been positively received by music critics. <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a>'s </i>Joy Piedmont described the song as both "catchy" and listenable. She also praised Underwood's vocal performance, writing, "she sounds great singing it."<sup id="cite_ref-:3_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Jacqueline Rupp of <a href="/wiki/Common_Sense_Media" title="Common Sense Media">Common Sense Media</a> wrote, "Carrie Underwood's power ballad, 'Ever Ever After' will have widespread appeal for all ages".<sup id="cite_ref-soundtrack_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soundtrack-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Allmusic" class="mw-redirect" title="Allmusic">Allmusic</a> commented, "Carrie Underwood steals the show with her rapturous pop tune".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Amazon.com" class="mw-redirect" title="Amazon.com">Amazon.com</a> reviewer Elisabeth Vincentelli wrote, "Carrie Underwood brings us back into the 21st century with the single 'Ever Ever After'".<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Filmtracks.com" title="Filmtracks.com">Filmtracks.com</a> reacted less favorably towards the song, describing it as "stale" and "a bit too grating in its instrumentation and vocalization".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music_video">Music video</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ever_Ever_After&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Music video">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ever_Ever_After_music_video.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/16/Ever_Ever_After_music_video.jpg/250px-Ever_Ever_After_music_video.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/1/16/Ever_Ever_After_music_video.jpg/375px-Ever_Ever_After_music_video.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/1/16/Ever_Ever_After_music_video.jpg 2x" data-file-width="410" data-file-height="231" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ever_Ever_After_music_video.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Carrie_Underwood" title="Carrie Underwood">Carrie Underwood</a> as her animated counterpart in the music video for "Ever Ever After."</div></div></div>
<p>The release of "Ever Ever After" was accompanied by a music video,<sup id="cite_ref-:4_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> in which Underwood appears as its protagonist.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The video was directed by <a href="/wiki/Roman_White" title="Roman White">Roman White</a>, who has directed the majority of Underwood's music videos,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and borrows its visual style from <i>Enchanted </i>by combining both animated and live-action sequences and "interspersing them with scenes from the film."<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In the video, Underwood first appears as an animated version of herself in the fictional kingdom of Andalasia, eventually transporting into the live-action world of present-day Manhattan, New York, much like Giselle does in the film. Once in New York, Underwood enamors a young man who pursues her as she makes her way through the city, all-the-while singing the song's lyrics that appear to have a positive influence on many of city's residents. The video concludes with Underwood finally uniting with her pursuer. They finally get married in Andalasia, imitating a scene from the film.
</p><p>Critical reception towards the video, although generally taciturn, has been mostly mixed. <a href="/wiki/PopSugar" title="PopSugar">PopSugar</a> wrote, "as ridiculous as the video is, it definitely made me giggle".<sup id="cite_ref-:4_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:4-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Joy Piedmont of <i>Entertainment Weekly</i> questioned the video's logic.  The reviewer also felt that Underwood is less expressive than her animated counterpart.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
