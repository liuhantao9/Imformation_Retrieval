	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Music download</h1>

<p>A <b>music download</b> is the digital transfer of music via the Internet into a device capable of decoding and playing it, such as a home computer, <a href="/wiki/MP3_player" title="MP3 player">MP3 player</a> or <a href="/wiki/Smartphone" title="Smartphone">smartphone</a>. This term encompasses both legal <a href="/wiki/Download" title="Download">downloads</a> and downloads of <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyrighted</a> material without permission or legal payment. According to a <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="Nielsen Company">Nielsen</a> report, downloadable music accounted for 55.9% of all <a href="/wiki/Music_sales" class="mw-redirect" title="Music sales">music sales</a> in the US in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;nb 1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> By the beginning of 2011, Apple's iTunes Store alone made <span style="white-space: nowrap">US$</span>1.1 billion of revenue in the first quarter of its fiscal year.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Online_music_store">Online music store</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_download&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Online music store">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Online_music_store" title="Online music store">Online music store</a> and <a href="/wiki/Comparison_of_online_music_stores" title="Comparison of online music stores">Comparison of online music stores</a></div>
<p>Paid downloads are sometimes encoded with <a href="/wiki/Digital_Rights_Management" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital Rights Management">Digital Rights Management</a> that restricts copying the music or playing purchased songs on certain digital audio players. They are almost always <a href="/wiki/Data_compression" title="Data compression">compressed</a> using a <a href="/wiki/Lossy_compression" title="Lossy compression">lossy</a> <a href="/wiki/Codec" title="Codec">codec</a> (usually MPEG-1 Layer 3, Windows Media, or AAC), which reduces file size and bandwidth requirements. These music resources have been created as a response to expanding technology and needs of customers that wanted easy, quick access to music. Their business models respond to the "download revolution" by making legal services attractive for users.
</p><p>Even legal music downloads have faced a number of challenges from artists, record labels and the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a>. In July 2007, the Universal Music Group decided not to renew their long-term contracts with iTunes. This decision was primarily based upon the issue of pricing of songs, as Universal wanted to be able to charge more or less depending on the artist, a shift away from iTunes' standard—at the time—99 cents per song pricing. Many industry leaders feel that this is only the first of many show-downs between <a href="/wiki/Apple_Inc." title="Apple Inc.">Apple Inc.</a> and the various record labels.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to research by the website <a href="/wiki/TorrentFreak" title="TorrentFreak">TorrentFreak</a>, 38% of <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> artists support file share downloading and claim that it helps artists in early career stages. The Swedish rock group Lamont has profited from file sharing.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="RIAA_against_illegal_downloading">RIAA against illegal downloading</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_download&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: RIAA against illegal downloading">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA) oversees about 85% of published music production, distribution and manufacturing in the United States. They work to protect musicians while supporting the <a href="/wiki/First_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="First Amendment to the United States Constitution">First Amendment</a> rights. Their stated goal is to support artists' creativity and help them not be cheated out of money by illegal downloading.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAA_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAA-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The Recording Industry Association of America launched its first lawsuits on 8 September 2003, against individuals who illegally downloaded music files from the <a href="/wiki/Kazaa" title="Kazaa">Kazaa</a> <a href="/wiki/FastTrack" title="FastTrack">FastTrack</a> network.
</p><p>Two years after it began, the campaign survived at least one major legal challenge.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The RIAA said it filed 750 suits in February 2006<sup id="cite_ref-Riaa_Announces_New_Round_of_Music_Theft_Lawsuits_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Riaa_Announces_New_Round_of_Music_Theft_Lawsuits-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> against individuals downloading music files without paying for them in hopes of putting an end to Internet <a href="/wiki/Music_piracy" title="Music piracy">music piracy</a>. The RIAA hopes their campaign will force people to respect the copyrights of music labels and eventually minimize the number of illegal downloads.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Official_Charts_Company" title="Official Charts Company">Official Charts Company</a> began to incorporate downloads in the <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a> on 17 April 2005, at which time Radio 1 stopped broadcasting the separate download chart,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> although the chart is still compiled. Initially this was on condition that the song must have a physical media release at the same time; this rule was fully lifted on 1 January 2007, meaning all download sales are now eligible in the chart.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sales_records">Sales records</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_download&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sales records">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_download&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Music downloads have been measured by the <a href="/wiki/Official_Charts_Company" title="Official Charts Company">Official Charts Company</a> since 2004 and included in the main <a href="/wiki/UK_Singles_Chart" title="UK Singles Chart">UK Singles Chart</a> from 2005. The most downloaded song in the UK is "<a href="/wiki/Happy_(Pharrell_Williams_song)" title="Happy (Pharrell Williams song)">Happy</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Pharrell_Williams" title="Pharrell Williams">Pharrell Williams</a> with over 1.8 million downloads.<sup id="cite_ref-Top_100_tracks_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Top_100_tracks-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_download&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In November 2005, the record for the best-selling downloaded single in the United States was held by <a href="/wiki/Gwen_Stefani" title="Gwen Stefani">Gwen Stefani</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Hollaback_Girl" title="Hollaback Girl">Hollaback Girl</a>", which sold over one million downloads, making it the first song to achieve platinum download status.<sup id="cite_ref-RSMillion_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RSMillion-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> As of July&#160;2012<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Music_download&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, the record for the best-selling downloaded single in the United States on the iTunes Store is held by <a href="/wiki/The_Black_Eyed_Peas" title="The Black Eyed Peas">The Black Eyed Peas</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/I_Gotta_Feeling" title="I Gotta Feeling">I Gotta Feeling</a>", which has sold over 8 million downloads.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Soon after his death in 2009, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a> became the first artist to sell over one million songs downloaded via the Internet in one week.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> However, <a href="/wiki/Adele" title="Adele">Adele</a> marks the most downloads sold by a single song in a week, with "<a href="/wiki/Hello_(Adele_song)" title="Hello (Adele song)">Hello</a>" selling 1.12 million copies in November 2015.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Eminem" title="Eminem">Eminem</a>'s seventh studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Recovery_(Eminem_album)" title="Recovery (Eminem album)">Recovery</a></i> (2010), became the first album to sell one million digital copies.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Beyonc%C3%A9" title="Beyoncé">Beyoncé</a>'s self-titled fifth studio album became the fastest-selling album within 24 hours in iTunes history after its release in December 2013. Within 24 hours of availability, the album sold 430,000 digital copies. Adele's third studio album <i>25</i> became the fastest-selling album in a week iTunes history after it was released on 20 November 2015. It sold 1.64 million digital copies in its first week (included preorders on the iTunes store since the release of the album's lead single "Hello" in October 2015).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_download&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2006, the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_Japan" title="Recording Industry Association of Japan">Recording Industry Association of Japan</a> began issuing certifications for digitally released music in Japan, compiling data from the early 2000s onwards.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAJ-aug2006digi_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAJ-aug2006digi-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The best-selling song is <a href="/wiki/Fukushima_Prefecture" title="Fukushima Prefecture">Fukushima</a>-based vocal group <a href="/wiki/Greeeen" title="Greeeen">Greeeen</a>'s song "<a href="/wiki/Kiseki_(Greeeen_song)" title="Kiseki (Greeeen song)">Kiseki</a>" (2008), which was certified for being legally downloaded four million times between 2008 and 2015,<sup id="cite_ref-RIAJ-july2015digi_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAJ-july2015digi-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> followed by R&amp;B singer <a href="/wiki/Thelma_Aoyama" title="Thelma Aoyama">Thelma Aoyama</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Soba_ni_Iru_ne" title="Soba ni Iru ne">Soba ni Iru ne</a>" (2008) featuring rapper <a href="/wiki/SoulJa" title="SoulJa">SoulJa</a>, which was certified for three million downloads between 2008 and 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAJ-mar2014digi_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAJ-mar2014digi-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Greeeen's song "Ai Uta" (2007) ranks as the third highest certified song, with 2.5 million downloads tracked between 2007 and 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAJ-aug2008digi_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAJ-aug2008digi-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RIAJ-jul2009digi_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAJ-jul2009digi-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Two more songs have sold more than two million paid downloads: <a href="/wiki/Ayaka" title="Ayaka">Ayaka</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Mikazuki_(song)" title="Mikazuki (song)">Mikazuki</a>" (2006) and <a href="/wiki/Kobukuro" title="Kobukuro">Kobukuro</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Tsubomi_(song)" title="Tsubomi (song)">Tsubomi</a>" (2007).<sup id="cite_ref-RIAJ-jan2014digi_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAJ-jan2014digi-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The most successful ringtone in Japan is Moldovan-Romanian band <a href="/wiki/O-Zone" title="O-Zone">O-Zone</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Dragostea_din_tei" class="mw-redirect" title="Dragostea din tei">Dragostea din tei</a>" (2003), known locally as "Koi no Maiahi"<span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">恋のマイアヒ</span>)</span>, which was certified as having four million units sold.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAJ-aug2006digi_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAJ-aug2006digi-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Japan, only two albums have received digital certifications by the RIAJ. The first was <i><a href="/wiki/Songs_for_Japan" title="Songs for Japan">Songs for Japan</a></i> (2011), a charity compilation album raising profits for the <a href="/wiki/2011_T%C5%8Dhoku_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami">2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami</a>, which was certified gold for 100,000 downloads in June 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAJ-may2011digi_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAJ-may2011digi-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The second album was the Japanese language cast recording of the <i><a href="/wiki/Frozen_(soundtrack)" title="Frozen (soundtrack)">Frozen</a></i> soundtrack, which sold 100,000 copies between its release in March 2014 and January 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-RIAJ-jan2015digi_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RIAJ-jan2015digi-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Korea">South Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_download&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: South Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In South Korea, <a href="/wiki/Gaon_Digital_Chart" title="Gaon Digital Chart">Gaon Digital Chart</a> has been tracking digital sales since 2009. The most successful song according to their published data is <a href="/wiki/Busker_Busker" title="Busker Busker">Busker Busker</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Cherry_Blossom_Ending" title="Cherry Blossom Ending">Cherry Blossom Ending</a>" (2012), which was downloaded over 7 million times between 2012 and 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-Gaon2012_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gaon2012-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gaon2013_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gaon2013-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gaon2014_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gaon2014-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Gaon2015_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gaon2015-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In 2011, "<a href="/wiki/Roly-Poly_(T-ara_song)" title="Roly-Poly (T-ara song)">Roly-Poly</a>" by <a href="/wiki/T-ara" title="T-ara">T-ara</a> was the most successful song of the year, selling 4.1 million digital copies.<sup id="cite_ref-Gaon2011_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gaon2011-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> In 2012 this accolade went to <a href="/wiki/Psy" title="Psy">Psy</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Gangnam_Style" title="Gangnam Style">Gangnam Style</a>", after selling 3.8 million units.<sup id="cite_ref-Gaon2012_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Gaon2012-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
