<p><b><a href="/wiki/Biodiversity" title="Biodiversity">Biodiversity</a> in Suriname</b> is high,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> mostly because of the variety of habitats and the temperature. The average annual temperature in the coastal area is between 26° and 28° Celsius. Suriname can be divided into four major ecological zones, namely from north to south;
</p>
<ol><li>The young coastal plain</li>
<li>The old coastal plain</li>
<li>The Savannah or Zanderij belt</li>
<li>The interior residual uplands.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li></ol>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Habitats_and_species">Habitats and species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity_in_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Habitats and species">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The coast of <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a> is wild and consists of mud banks, sand beaches, mangrove forests and lagoons.  The Guiana current flows along the coast of Suriname. The <a href="/wiki/North_Brazil_Current" title="North Brazil Current">North Brazil Current</a>, a warm water ocean current, is renamed as the Guiana current. The confusion surrounding its name is due partly to the seasonal change in flow of nearby currents.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Mangrove" title="Mangrove">mangrove</a> forests have only one story with a height of 20–25 meters. They are almost completely homogenous and dominated by black mangrove (<i>Avicenna germinans</i>). The mangrove forests are important as staging and wintering areas for birds, such as the <a href="/wiki/Scarlet_ibis" title="Scarlet ibis">scarlet ibis</a> (<i>Eudocimus ruber</i>). However, poaching is a major problem in the coastal area. According to Ottema,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> a 2006 study reveals that tens of thousands of protected shorebirds and waders are poached annually. The Nature Conservation Division of the Forestry ministry attempts to prevent poaching by employing more forest guards as well as through environmental education. Furthermore,  Ottema opined that the Large-billed seed finch or Twatwa is almost extirpated.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Inland from the mangroves lie salt water and brackish <a href="/wiki/Lagoons" class="mw-redirect" title="Lagoons">lagoons</a>, surrounded by grass and fern vegetation with low plant species diversity. The <a href="/wiki/Orange-winged_amazon" title="Orange-winged amazon">orange-winged amazon</a> (parrot), creates it nest in the lagoons.  Further inland, species rich freshwater and shrub <a href="/wiki/Swamps" class="mw-redirect" title="Swamps">swamps</a> can be found. On the coastal plan, parallel to the shoreline, lie old sand and shell ridges.  The savannabelt,  a mosaic of diverse landscapes and ecosystems, is located south of the coastal plan. Different plants and animals,  such as the love-vine (lenkiwisi) ( <i><a href="/wiki/Cassytha_filiformis" title="Cassytha filiformis">Cassytha filiformis</a></i>), sundews (<i><a href="/wiki/Drosera" title="Drosera">Drosera</a></i> sp.), sabana-fungu (<i>Licaniai acana</i>), <a href="/wiki/Tortoises" class="mw-redirect" title="Tortoises">tortoises</a> (<i>Chelonodis</i> sp.), <a href="/wiki/Snakes" class="mw-redirect" title="Snakes">snakes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iguanas" class="mw-redirect" title="Iguanas">iguanas</a> , <a href="/wiki/Deer" title="Deer">deer</a>  and the long-nosed or naked- tailed armadillo  (<i><a href="/wiki/Dasypus" title="Dasypus">Dasypus</a></i> sp.)  grow and live in the savannabelt of Suriname.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Leatherback_Turtle_near_Galibi.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Leatherback_Turtle_near_Galibi.jpg/220px-Leatherback_Turtle_near_Galibi.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Leatherback_Turtle_near_Galibi.jpg/330px-Leatherback_Turtle_near_Galibi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Leatherback_Turtle_near_Galibi.jpg/440px-Leatherback_Turtle_near_Galibi.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2272" data-file-height="1704" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Leatherback_Turtle_near_Galibi.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Leatherback_turtle" class="mw-redirect" title="Leatherback turtle">Leatherback turtle</a></div></div></div>
<p>Four kind of <a href="/wiki/Sea_turtles" class="mw-redirect" title="Sea turtles">sea turtles</a> create nest on the beaches. These sea turtles are:
</p>
<ol><li>Aitkanti or <a href="/wiki/Leatherback_turtle" class="mw-redirect" title="Leatherback turtle">leatherback turtle</a> (<i>Dermochelys corriacea</i>)</li>
<li>Krape or <a href="/wiki/Green_turtle" class="mw-redirect" title="Green turtle">Green turtle</a> (<i>Chelonia mydas</i>)</li>
<li>Warana or <a href="/wiki/Olive_Ridley_sea_turtle" class="mw-redirect" title="Olive Ridley sea turtle">Olive Ridley sea turtle</a> (<i>Lepidochelys olivacea</i>)</li>
<li>Karet or <a href="/wiki/Hawksbill_sea_turtle" title="Hawksbill sea turtle">Hawksbill sea turtle</a> (<i>Eretmochelys imbricata</i>).</li></ol>
<p>Suriname is rich in freshwater resources. Suriname has 228.00 per capita cubic meters of renewable freshwater resources annually.  However, these resources are seriously threatened by human activities, such as gold mining.  Suriname has more or less 61 endemic freshwater fishes. According to Mol,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_goliath_grouper" title="Atlantic goliath grouper">Atlantic goliath grouper</a> (<i>Epinephelus itajara</i>) is endangered. 
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:White_faced_saki_(5941062382).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/White_faced_saki_%285941062382%29.jpg/220px-White_faced_saki_%285941062382%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/White_faced_saki_%285941062382%29.jpg/330px-White_faced_saki_%285941062382%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/White_faced_saki_%285941062382%29.jpg/440px-White_faced_saki_%285941062382%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2992" data-file-height="1991" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:White_faced_saki_(5941062382).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/White_faced_saki" class="mw-redirect" title="White faced saki">White faced saki</a></div></div></div>
<p>In the highlands, mountains, such as <a href="/wiki/Bakhuys_Mountains" class="mw-redirect" title="Bakhuys Mountains">Bakhuys Mountains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Van_Asch_Van_Wijck_Mountains" title="Van Asch Van Wijck Mountains">Van Asch Van Wijck Mountains</a>, <a href="/wiki/Julianatop" title="Julianatop">Julianatop</a>,  are part of the <a href="/wiki/Guiana_Shield" title="Guiana Shield">Guiana Shield</a>. The Guiana Shield is one of the regions of highest biodiversity in the world, and has many <a href="/wiki/Endemic" class="mw-redirect" title="Endemic">endemic</a> species. It can be said that the <a href="/wiki/White-faced_saki" title="White-faced saki">white-faced saki</a> is endemic to the Guyana’s.  The <a href="/wiki/Guianan_piculet" class="mw-redirect" title="Guianan piculet">Guianan piculet</a> (<i>Picumnus minutissimis</i>) is probably endemic to Suriname. It has not been confirmed for neighboring countries.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The rest of Suriname is covered with forests. Most of the forests are undisturbed because of the low human population density of 2.7 people /km. There are still new species discovered in Suriname. In 2012, <a href="/wiki/Conservation_International" title="Conservation International">Conservation International</a> Suriname (CIS) announced that 60 new species were discovered in het Grensgebergte and <a href="/wiki/Kasikasima" title="Kasikasima">Kasikasima</a>. Among the newly discovered species were: the chocolate- colored “cocoa” frog (<i>Hypsiboas</i> sp.) and the juvenile planthopper.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nature_Protection_Act_1954_and_the_Game_Act_1954">Nature Protection Act 1954 and the Game Act 1954</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity_in_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nature Protection Act 1954 and the Game Act 1954">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Nature Protection Act 1954<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and the Game Act 1954<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> are important for the protection of nature in Suriname. The Nature Protection Act 1954 regulates the establishment and the management of <a href="/wiki/Nature_reserves" class="mw-redirect" title="Nature reserves">nature reserves</a> and other protected areas. The Game Act 1954 states which species of wild life may be hunted and during which period. There are four categories of wild animals, namely protected animals, game species, cage species and predominantly harmful species. The <a href="/wiki/Giant_anteater" title="Giant anteater">giant anteater</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red-faced_spider_monkey" title="Red-faced spider monkey">red-faced spider monkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ocelot" title="Ocelot">ocelot</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jaguar" title="Jaguar">jaguar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bush_dog" title="Bush dog">bush dog</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Guiana_dolphin" title="Guiana dolphin">Guiana dolphin</a> are some of the mammals that are under complete protection in Suriname.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protected_areas">Protected areas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity_in_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Protected areas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Suriname has 11 Nature Reserves, 1 Nature park and 4 Multi Use Management Areas (MUMA"S). A protected area is an area set aside for the preservation and protection of highly important natural and cultural features, for the regulation of the scientific, educational and recreational use.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> On March 5, 2015,the leaders of the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_in_Suriname" title="Indigenous peoples in Suriname">indigenous peoples in Suriname</a> presented a declaration for the protection of a 7.2 million preserve, to members of parliament. <a href="/wiki/Conservation_International" title="Conservation International">Conservation International</a>-Suriname and the <a href="/wiki/World_Wide_Fund_For_Nature" class="mw-redirect" title="World Wide Fund For Nature">World Wide Fund For Nature</a> worked to support the vision of the Trio and Wayana communities to map the preserve and lay out strategies to fund and ensure the success of the initiative. The plan includes the provision of jobs for tribe members as rangers and guards. The indigenous leaders also intent to establish the value maintaining the unspoiled rivers.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_conventions">International conventions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity_in_Suriname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: International conventions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Suriname is signatory to many international conventions aimed at protection and conservation of the biodiversity. The most important are the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_Biological_Diversity" title="Convention on Biological Diversity">Convention on Biological Diversity</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_International_Trade_in_Endangered_Species_of_Wild_Fauna_and_Flora" class="mw-redirect" title="Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora">Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ramsar_Convention_on_Wetlands" class="mw-redirect" title="Ramsar Convention on Wetlands">Ramsar Convention on Wetlands</a>. In 2007, the ministry of the Environment presented the National Biodiversity Strategy<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and in 2013 the National Biodiversity Action Plan.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
