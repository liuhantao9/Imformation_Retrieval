	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Maritime boundary</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Zonmar-en.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Zonmar-en.svg/220px-Zonmar-en.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="328" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Zonmar-en.svg/330px-Zonmar-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Zonmar-en.svg/440px-Zonmar-en.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="493" data-file-height="735" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Zonmar-en.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Features, limits and zones</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>maritime boundary</b> is a conceptual division of the <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">Earth</a>'s water surface areas using <a href="/wiki/Physical_geography" title="Physical geography">physiographic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Human_geography" title="Human geography">geopolitical</a> criteria. As such, it usually bounds areas of exclusive <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">national rights</a> over mineral and biological resources,<sup id="cite_ref-vliz_info_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vliz_info-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> encompassing maritime features, limits and zones.<sup id="cite_ref-geoscience_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-geoscience-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Generally, a maritime boundary is delineated at a particular distance from a jurisdiction's coastline. Although in some countries the term <i>maritime boundary</i> represents <a href="/wiki/Border" title="Border">borders</a> of a <a href="/wiki/Maritime_nation" title="Maritime nation">maritime nation</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> that are recognized by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea" title="United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea">United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea</a>, maritime borders usually serve to identify the edge of <a href="/wiki/International_waters" title="International waters">international waters</a>.
</p><p>Maritime boundaries exist in the context of <a href="/wiki/Territorial_waters" title="Territorial waters">territorial waters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contiguous_zone" class="mw-redirect" title="Contiguous zone">contiguous zones</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Exclusive_economic_zone" title="Exclusive economic zone">exclusive economic zones</a>; however, the terminology does not encompass <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a> or <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> boundaries, which are considered within the context of land boundaries.
</p><p>Some maritime boundaries have remained indeterminate despite efforts to clarify them. This is explained by an array of factors, some of which involve regional problems.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The delineation of maritime boundaries has strategic, economic and environmental implications.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maritime_boundary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The terms <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/boundary" class="extiw" title="wikt:boundary">boundary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frontier" title="Frontier">frontier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Border" title="Border">border</a> are often used as if they were interchangeable, but they are also terms with precise meanings.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A boundary is a line. The terms "frontier", "borderland" and "border" are zones of indeterminate width.  Such areas forms the outermost part of a country. Borders are bounded on one side by a national boundary.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> There are variations in the specific terminology of maritime boundary agreements which have been concluded since the 1970s. Such differences are less important than what is being delimited.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Features">Features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maritime_boundary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Features that affect maritime boundaries include <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">islands</a> and the submerged seabed of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">continental shelf</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-geoscience_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-geoscience-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The process of <a href="/wiki/Boundary_delimitation" title="Boundary delimitation">boundary delimitation</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">ocean</a> encompasses the <a href="/wiki/Natural_prolongation" class="mw-redirect" title="Natural prolongation">natural prolongation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">geological</a> features and <a href="/wiki/Outlying_territory" title="Outlying territory">outlying territory</a>. The process of establishing "positional" borders encompasses the distinction between previously resolved and never-resolved controversies.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Limits">Limits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maritime_boundary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Limits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The limits of maritime boundaries are expressed in <a href="/wiki/Polyline" class="mw-redirect" title="Polyline">polylines</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Polygon" title="Polygon">polygon</a> layers of sovereignty and control,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> calculated from the declaration of a baseline. The conditions under which a state may establish such baseline are described in the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS). A baseline of a country can be the low water line, a straight baseline (a line that encloses bays, estuaries, inland waters,...) or a combination of the two.<sup id="cite_ref-vliz_info_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vliz_info-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Zones">Zones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maritime_boundary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Zones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The zones of maritime boundaries are expressed in concentric limits surrounding coastal and feature baselines.<sup id="cite_ref-vliz_info_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vliz_info-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Inland waters&#8212;the zone inside the baseline.</li>
<li>Territorial sea&#8212;the zone extending 12&#160;nm. from the baseline.<sup id="cite_ref-geoscience_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-geoscience-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Contiguous zone&#8212;the area extending 24&#160;nm. from the baseline.<sup id="cite_ref-geoscience_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-geoscience-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Exclusive Economic Zone&#8212;the area extending 200&#160;nm. from the baseline except when the space between two countries is less than 400&#160;nm.<sup id="cite_ref-geoscience_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-geoscience-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>In the case of overlapping zones, the boundary is presumed to conform to the <a href="/wiki/Equidistance_principle" title="Equidistance principle">equidistance principle</a> or it is explicitly described in a multilateral treaty.<sup id="cite_ref-vliz_info_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vliz_info-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Contemporary negotiations have produced <a href="/wiki/Tripoint" title="Tripoint">tripoint</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quadripoint" title="Quadripoint">quadripoint</a> determinations. For example, in the 1982 <a href="/wiki/Australia%E2%80%93France_Marine_Delimitation_Agreement" title="Australia–France Marine Delimitation Agreement">Australia–France Marine Delimitation Agreement</a>, for the purposes of drawing the treaty's equidistant lines it was assumed that France has sovereignty over <a href="/wiki/Matthew_and_Hunter_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Matthew and Hunter Islands">Matthew and Hunter Islands</a>, a territory that is also claimed by <a href="/wiki/Vanuatu" title="Vanuatu">Vanuatu</a>. The northernmost point in the boundary is a tripoint with the <a href="/wiki/Solomon_Islands" title="Solomon Islands">Solomon Islands</a>. The boundary runs in a roughly north–south direction and then turns and runs west–east until it almost reaches the <a href="/wiki/170th_meridian_east" title="170th meridian east">170th meridian east</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-anderson_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anderson-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maritime_boundary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_and_territories_by_maritime_boundaries" title="List of countries and territories by maritime boundaries">List of countries and territories by maritime boundaries</a></div>
<p>The concept of maritime boundaries is a relatively new concept.<sup id="cite_ref-vliz_info_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vliz_info-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The historical record is a backdrop for evaluating border issues.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  The evaluation of historic rights are governed by distinct legal regimes in customary international law, including research and analysis based on
</p>
<ul><li>acquisition and occupation<sup id="cite_ref-jagota183_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jagota183-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>the existence of rights <i><a href="/wiki/Ipso_facto" title="Ipso facto">ipso facto</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Ab_initio" title="Ab initio">ab initio</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-jagota183_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jagota183-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The study of treaties on maritime boundaries is important as (a) as a source of general or particular international law; (b) as evidence of existing customary law; and (c) as evidence of the emerging development of custom.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The development of "customary law" affects all nations.
</p><p>The attention accorded this subject has evolved beyond formerly-conventional norms like the <a href="/wiki/Three-mile_limit" title="Three-mile limit">three-mile limit</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Treaties">Treaties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maritime_boundary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Treaties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_maritime_boundary_treaties" title="List of maritime boundary treaties">List of maritime boundary treaties</a></div>
<p>Multilateral treaties and documents describing the baselines of countries can be found on the website of the United Nations.<sup id="cite_ref-vliz_info_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vliz_info-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For example, the Australia–France Marine Delimitation Agreement establishes ocean boundaries between Australia and New Caledonia in the Coral Sea (including the boundary between Australia's Norfolk Island and New Caledonia). It consists of 21 straight-line maritime segments defined by 22 individual coordinate points forming a modified equidistant line between the two territories.<sup id="cite_ref-anderson_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-anderson-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disputes">Disputes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maritime_boundary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Disputes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_territorial_disputes" title="List of territorial disputes">List of territorial disputes</a></div>
<p>Controversies about territorial waters tend to encompass two dimensions: (a) territorial sovereignty, which are a legacy of history; and (b) relevant jurisdictional rights and interests in maritime boundaries, which are mainly due to differing interpretations of the law of the sea.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> An example of this may be reviewed in the context of the ongoing Kuwait-Iraq maritime dispute over the <a href="/wiki/Khawr_Abd_Allah" title="Khawr Abd Allah">Khawr Abd Allah</a> waterway.
</p><p>Many disputes have been resolved through negotiations,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> but not all.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unresolved_maritime_boundaries">Unresolved maritime boundaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Maritime_boundary&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Unresolved maritime boundaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Among the array of unsettled disputes, the maritime borders of the two Koreas in the Yellow Sea represent a visually stark contrast.<sup id="cite_ref-ryoo13_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ryoo13-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Northern_Limit_Line" title="Northern Limit Line">Northern Limit Line</a></div>
<p>A western line of military control between the two Koreas was unilaterally established by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Command" title="United Nations Command">United Nations Command</a> in 1953.<sup id="cite_ref-elferllink314_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elferllink314-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-kim2010_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kim2010-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Although the North asserts a differently configured boundary line, there is no dispute that a few small islands close to the North Korean coastline have remained jurisdiction of the United Nations since 1953.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The map at the right shows the differing maritime boundary lines of the two Koreas. The ambits of these boundaries encompass overlapping jurisdictional claims.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The explicit differences in the way the boundary lines are configured is shown in the map at the right.
</p><p>In a very small area, this represents a unique illustration of differences in mapping and delineation strategies.
</p>
<ul><li>On one hand, the boundary line created by the United Nations ("A") reflects the geographic features of the coastal baseline.<sup id="cite_ref-reuters2010_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-reuters2010-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>On the other hand, while the boundary line declared by North Korea does acknowledge specific non-DPRK island enclaves, its "Military Demarcation Line" in the ocean ("B") is essentially a straight line.<sup id="cite_ref-peoplesdaily2010_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-peoplesdaily2010-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Violent clashes in these disputed waters include what are known as the <a href="/wiki/First_Battle_of_Yeonpyeong" title="First Battle of Yeonpyeong">first Yeonpyeong incident</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Second_Battle_of_Yeonpyeong" title="Second Battle of Yeonpyeong">second Yeonpyeong incident</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Bombardment_of_Yeonpyeong" title="Bombardment of Yeonpyeong">Bombardment of Yeonpyeong</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
