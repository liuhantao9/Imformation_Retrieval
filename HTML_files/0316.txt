<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavic_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Polytheism" title="Polytheism">pre-Christian</a> traditions, a child less than 7&#8211;10 years old would bear a "substitutional name", the purpose of which was to deflect attention from the child and thereby to protect it from the curiosity of evil powers. The practice was largely the effect of the high mortality rate for young children at the time.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> A child who survived to 7&#8211;10 years was considered worthy of care and was granted adult status and a new adult name during a ritual <a href="/wiki/First_haircut" title="First haircut">first haircut</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Traditional names remained dominant until the <a href="/wiki/Slavic_peoples" class="mw-redirect" title="Slavic peoples">Slavic nations</a> converted to <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a>. Since then, however, baptismal names came into use, which were given after the <a href="/wiki/Patron_saint" title="Patron saint">patron saint</a> of the newly baptized. Even after that, the traditional names persisted in everyday use, while in religious matters <a href="/wiki/Baptismal_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Baptismal name">baptismal name</a> was involved; thus, many persons had and used two names simultaneously. This is exemplified by how the Slavic saints of that time are referred to up to nowadays:e.g. St. <a href="/wiki/Boris_and_Gleb" title="Boris and Gleb">Boris and Gleb</a>, in holy baptism Roman and David. As the Slavic saints became more numerous, more traditional names entered the Church calendar; but more prominent was the overall decline in the number of people bearing traditional names. Finally, in 16th&#8211;17th century the traditional Slavic names which did not enter the calendar of either Orthodox or Catholic Church generally fell out of use. For Catholic Slavs, the decisive event was the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">Council of Trent</a> (1545&#8211;63) decreed that every <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a> should have a <a href="/wiki/Christian_name" title="Christian name">Christian name</a> instead of a native one.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_in_Poland">Names in Poland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavic_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Names in Poland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the ban on native non-Christian names imposed by the Council of Trent, the <a href="/wiki/Szlachta" title="Szlachta">Polish nobility</a> (especially <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestants</a>) attempted to preserve traditional names, such as <a href="/wiki/Zbigniew" title="Zbigniew">Zbigniew</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jaroslav_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jaroslav (disambiguation)">Jarosław</a>. Ordinary people, however, tended to choose names solely from the Christian calendar, which contained only a handful of Slavic saints' names, in particular: <a href="/wiki/Casimir" title="Casimir">Kazimierz</a> (St. Casimir), <a href="/wiki/Stanislav_(given_name)" title="Stanislav (given name)">Stanisław</a> (St. Stanislaus), <a href="/wiki/Wenceslaus_I,_Duke_of_Bohemia" title="Wenceslaus I, Duke of Bohemia">Wacław</a> (St. Wenceslaus) and <a href="/wiki/Vladislav" title="Vladislav">Władysław</a> (St. Ladislaus).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Slavic names that referred to God (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Bogdan" title="Bogdan">Bogdan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bogomil_(name)" title="Bogomil (name)">Bogumił</a>) were also permitted.<sup id="cite_ref-zielarze.pl_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zielarze.pl-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Names_in_Kievan_Rus.27"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_in_Kievan_Rus'">Names in Kievan Rus'</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavic_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Names in Kievan Rus&#039;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Kievan_Rus%27" title="Kievan Rus&#39;">Rus'</a> names were based on common Slavic names such as <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_(name)" title="Vladimir (name)">Volodiměrŭ</a> (<i>Володимѣръ</i>), <a href="/wiki/Sviatopolk_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Sviatopolk (disambiguation)">Svętopŭlkŭ</a> (<i>Свѧтопълкъ</i>) (holy regiment), <a href="/wiki/Yaropolk_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Yaropolk (disambiguation)">Jęropŭlkŭ</a> (<i>Ѩропълкъ</i>), <a href="/wiki/Vojislav" title="Vojislav">Voislavŭ</a> (<i>Воиславъ</i>) (glorious warrior), <a href="/wiki/Borislav" title="Borislav">Borislav</a>ŭ (<i>Бориславъ</i>) (glorious warrior), <a href="/wiki/Boris_(given_name)" title="Boris (given name)">Borisŭ</a> (<i>Борисъ</i>) (fighter), <a href="/wiki/Lubomir" title="Lubomir">Liubomirŭ</a> (<i>Любомиръ</i>) (loves the peace), Ratibor (war fighter), <a href="/wiki/Vadim_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Vadim (name)">Vadim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yaroslav_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Yaroslav (disambiguation)">Yaroslav</a>, Izyaslav, <a href="/wiki/Mstislav_(given_name)" title="Mstislav (given name)">Mstislav</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vsevolod_(given_name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Vsevolod (given name)">Vsevolod</a>. In the 11th century, after the growing influence of the Christian Church, the tendency to use the names of saints of the Greek Church has increased and most of old Rus' names were displaced by Dmytriy, Andrey, Nikolay, Terentiy, Sergey, Anton, Kirill, Georgiy, Konstantin, Alexandr, Foma, Akakiy, etc.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_today">Names today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavic_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Names today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kresimir_(boat).JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Kresimir_%28boat%29.JPG/220px-Kresimir_%28boat%29.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Kresimir_%28boat%29.JPG/330px-Kresimir_%28boat%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Kresimir_%28boat%29.JPG/440px-Kresimir_%28boat%29.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kresimir_(boat).JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Slavic origin name on the boat</div></div></div>
<p>Since <a href="/wiki/Romantic_nationalism" title="Romantic nationalism">national revivals</a> during 19th and 20th centuries, traditional names, especially of historical rulers and heroes, regained popularity. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">Poland</a> many forgotten names were resurrected, such as <a href="/wiki/Bronis%C5%82aw_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Bronisław (disambiguation)">Bronisław</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boleslaus" class="mw-redirect" title="Boleslaus">Bolesław</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dobies%C5%82aw_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Dobiesław (disambiguation)">Dobiesław</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dobroslaw_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dobroslaw (name)">Dobrosław</a>, Jarosław, <a href="/wiki/Miros%C5%82aw_(given_name)" title="Mirosław (given name)">Mirosław</a>, <a href="/wiki/Przemys%C5%82aw" title="Przemysław">Przemysław</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rados%C5%82aw_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Radosław (disambiguation)">Radosław</a>, <a href="/wiki/S%C5%82awomir" title="Sławomir">Sławomir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wies%C5%82aw" title="Wiesław">Wiesław</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zdzis%C5%82aw_(given_name)" title="Zdzisław (given name)">Zdzisław</a>, and Zbigniew; and new ones created, such as Lechosław and Wieńczysław.<sup id="cite_ref-zielarze.pl_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zielarze.pl-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Today, traditional Slavic names are accepted by the <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian Church</a> and are given at a child's <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptism</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meanings">Meanings</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavic_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Meanings">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Old Slavic names were built with one or two <a href="/wiki/Lexeme" title="Lexeme">lexemes</a>:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Single-lexeme_names">Single-lexeme names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavic_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Single-lexeme names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Single-lexeme names were derived from ordinary or adjectival words and were usually, though not always, borne by peasants, e.g.: Baran (ram), Szydło (awl), Kąkol (cockle), Broda (beard, chin), Żyła (vein), Uchacz (ear-man), Łopata (shovel), Żaba (frog), Rus (Ruthenian/Russian man), Cich (silent man), etc.<sup id="cite_ref-zielarze.pl_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zielarze.pl-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Many names of this kind are used today, for example:

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dithematic_names">Dithematic names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavic_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Dithematic names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>Dithematic names are built with two lexemes. Kaleta 1995 notes that "In the case of Old Germanic and Old Slavic personal names, the dithematic name form contained a wish for the new-born child. These wishes pertained to the values that obtained in these early times".<sup id="cite_ref-Langendonck2007_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Langendonck2007-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In Poland alone, over 600 <a href="/wiki/Masculine_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Masculine name">masculine names</a>, 120 <a href="/wiki/Feminine_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Feminine name">feminine names</a> and 150 different affixes (<a href="/wiki/Lexeme" title="Lexeme">lexemes</a>) are known. These have been reconstructed from place names and the (scarce) written sources such as the <a href="/wiki/Bull_of_Gniezno" title="Bull of Gniezno">Bull of Gniezno</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-zielarze.pl_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zielarze.pl-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Certain names were reserved for monarchs (e.g. in Poland: Kazimierz, Władysław, Bolesław).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Examples are listed below. As an example of the pattern: Władysław contains the prefix <i>wład</i> (to rule, ruler) and the suffix <i>sław</i> (fame, glory). Note that feminine equivalents usually end in <i>a</i> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Bogus%C5%82aw_(given_name)" title="Bogusław (given name)">Bogusław</a> - Bogusław<i>a</i>).
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Participle-built_names">Participle-built names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavic_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Participle-built names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These are derived either from the <a href="/wiki/Past_participle" class="mw-redirect" title="Past participle">past participle</a> (in the passive voice), e.g.: <a href="/wiki/Bojan" title="Bojan">Bojan</a>, Chocian, Kochan, Miłowan, Pomian, Stator, Wygnan, or the <a href="/wiki/Present_participle" class="mw-redirect" title="Present participle">present participle</a> (in the active voice), e.g.: Cieszym, Myślim, Radzim, Borzym. Such names are repositories of perhaps the largest source of sociological data about the ancient <a href="/wiki/Slavic_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Slavic people">Slavic people</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
They have a variety of purposes, which can be listed as follows:
</p>
<ul><li>names containing a good wish, e.g. Kochan ('let him be loved'), <a href="/wiki/Milan_(given_name)" title="Milan (given name)">Milan</a>.</li>
<li>names referring to affection for the new born child, e.g. Obiecan ('promised'), Żdan ('promised', 'expected'),</li>
<li>names protecting from evil (consisting of lexemes with a negative, deterring effect) e.g. Wygnan, Mazan, Grozim.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Other examples: Poznan ('known', 'recognized'), Goszczon (being a guest at someone's place), Krszczon ('baptized'), <a href="/wiki/Radovan_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Radovan (name)">Radovan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dragan" title="Dragan">Dragan</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=%C5%BDeljan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Željan (page does not exist)">Željan</a>, Dejan, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nayden&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nayden (page does not exist)">Nayden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mirjana" title="Mirjana">Mirjana</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diminutive_and_hypocoristic_names">Diminutive and hypocoristic names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slavic_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Diminutive and hypocoristic names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">Diminutive</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hypocorism" title="Hypocorism">hypocoristic</a> (<a href="/wiki/Term_of_endearment" title="Term of endearment">endearing</a>) names deriving from the above-mentioned dithematic names are created by using different diminutive <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffixes</a>. Such names are very popular in everyday usage, and usually are created by replacing part of the name with the suffix <i>-ek</i> (masculine, predominantly West Slavic; e.g. Polish Włodzi<i>mierz</i> &#8211; Włod<i>ek</i>), <i>-ko</i> (masculine, predominantly South Slavic and Ukrainian), <i>-ka</i> (feminine; also masculine in Russian), or <i>-a</i>: Mila, <a href="/wiki/Luba_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Luba (disambiguation)">Luba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stanislav_(given_name)" title="Stanislav (given name)">Staszek</a>, Radek, <a href="/wiki/Vladislav" title="Vladislav">Władek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zlatan" title="Zlatan">Zlatko</a>, Zlata, <a href="/wiki/Vladimir,_Russia" title="Vladimir, Russia">Volodya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Branislav" title="Branislav">Bronek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leszek" title="Leszek">Leszek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dobroniega" title="Dobroniega">Dobrusia</a>, <a href="/wiki/S%C5%82awomir" title="Sławomir">Slavko</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wojtek" class="mw-redirect" title="Wojtek">Wojtek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mirka" title="Mirka">Mirka</a>, Bogusia, <a href="/wiki/Slava" title="Slava">Slava</a>, Zdravko, <a href="/wiki/Zbyszko" title="Zbyszko">Zbyszko</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milosz" class="mw-redirect" title="Milosz">Miłosz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stanislav_(given_name)" title="Stanislav (given name)">Staś</a>, <a href="/wiki/Przemys%C5%82aw" title="Przemysław">Przemek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boleslaw_(given_name)" title="Boleslaw (given name)">Bolko</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dragan" title="Dragan">Draho</a>, <a href="/wiki/%C5%BDeljko" title="Željko">Željko</a>, <a href="/wiki/Borys" title="Borys">Borya</a> (fight),Boško, Božica, Božana, Branko, Branka, Braniša, Borko, Budimka, Hvališa, Dobar, Dobra, Dragoš, Dragica, Dragi, Draga, <a href="/wiki/Drago%C5%9F_(given_name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dragoş (given name)">Dragoş</a>, Miloš, Slavko, Slavica, <a href="/wiki/Slavisa" class="mw-redirect" title="Slavisa">Slavisa</a>, Svetlana, Wít, Zdenka, Bratko, Braco, Braca, Bato, Bata, Batica, etc.
