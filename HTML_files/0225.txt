<p>The <b>Akurio</b> are an <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_people_of_the_Americas" class="mw-redirect" title="Indigenous people of the Americas">indigenous people</a>, living in <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">Suriname</a>. They are <a href="/wiki/Hunter-gatherers" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunter-gatherers">hunter-gatherers</a>, who were first contacted in 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-ethno_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ethno-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name">Name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akurio_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Akurio are also called Akoerio, Akuliyo, Akuri, Akurijo, Akuriyo, Oyaricoulet, Triometesem, Triometesen, Wama, or Wayaricuri people.<sup id="cite_ref-ethno_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ethno-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Population">Population</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akurio_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Population">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The population was estimated to be 50 in 2000. It fell to 40 by 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-ethno_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ethno-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Language">Language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Akurio_people&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The group used the <a href="/wiki/Akurio_language" title="Akurio language">Akurio language</a>, also known as Akuriyó, until the late <a href="/wiki/20th_century" title="20th century">20th century</a>, when they began using the <a href="/wiki/Trio_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Trio language">Trio language</a>. The last native speaker is believed to have died in the first decade of the 2000s, at which time only 10 people were estimated to have Akuriyó as a second language. By 2012, only two semi-speakers remained.<sup id="cite_ref-ethno_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ethno-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In December 2018, Sepi Akuriyó, one of the last surviving speakers of Akuriyó, went missing when a small plane carrying 8 people disappeared during a flight over the <a href="/wiki/Amazon_rainforest" title="Amazon rainforest">Amazon rainforest</a>. A search and rescue operation was called off after two weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
