<p><b>Rose Marie</b> "<b>Rosemary</b>" <b>Kennedy</b> (September 13, 1918 – January 7, 2005) was the oldest daughter born to <a href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Kennedy_Sr." title="Joseph P. Kennedy Sr.">Joseph P. Kennedy Sr.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rose_Kennedy" title="Rose Kennedy">Rose Fitzgerald Kennedy</a>. She was a sister of <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">Senators</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_F._Kennedy" title="Robert F. Kennedy">Robert F. Kennedy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Ted Kennedy</a>.
</p><p>Kennedy was intellectually disabled and was educated separately from other students. In her early young adult years, she experienced seizures and violent mood swings. In response to these and other difficulties, Kennedy's father arranged a prefrontal <a href="/wiki/Lobotomies" class="mw-redirect" title="Lobotomies">lobotomy</a> for Kennedy when she was 23 years of age. The procedure failed, leaving Kennedy permanently incapacitated and rendering her unable to speak intelligibly. Kennedy spent most of the rest of her life being cared for in an institution in <a href="/wiki/Jefferson,_Wisconsin" title="Jefferson, Wisconsin">Jefferson, Wisconsin</a>. While she was initially isolated from her relatives following her lobotomy, Kennedy visited with her family during her later life.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_and_early_life">Family and early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosemary_Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Family and early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TheKennedyFamily1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/TheKennedyFamily1.jpg/250px-TheKennedyFamily1.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="198" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/TheKennedyFamily1.jpg/375px-TheKennedyFamily1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/TheKennedyFamily1.jpg/500px-TheKennedyFamily1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="760" data-file-height="602" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TheKennedyFamily1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Kennedy family at <a href="/wiki/Hyannis_Port,_Massachusetts" title="Hyannis Port, Massachusetts">Hyannis Port, Massachusetts</a>, in 1931 with Rosemary on the far right.</div></div></div>
<p>Rose Marie Kennedy was born at <a href="/wiki/John_Fitzgerald_Kennedy_National_Historic_Site" title="John Fitzgerald Kennedy National Historic Site">her parents' home</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brookline,_Massachusetts" title="Brookline, Massachusetts">Brookline, Massachusetts</a>. She was the third child and first daughter of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_P._Kennedy_Sr." title="Joseph P. Kennedy Sr.">Joseph P. Kennedy Sr.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rose_Kennedy" title="Rose Kennedy">Rose Fitzgerald</a>. She was named after her mother<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and was commonly called Rosemary or Rosie. During her birth, the doctor was not immediately available and the nurse ordered Rose Kennedy to keep her legs closed, forcing the baby's head to stay in the birth canal for two hours. The action resulted in a harmful <a href="/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical)" title="Hypoxia (medical)">loss of oxygen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt.gordon_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt.gordon-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> As Rosemary began to grow, her parents noticed she was not reaching the basic development steps an infant or a toddler normally reach at a certain month or year. At two years old, she had a hard time sitting up, crawling, and learning to walk.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS"><span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (May 2019)">better&#160;source&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Accounts of Rosemary's life indicated that she was intellectually disabled,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-auto1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> although some have raised questions about the Kennedys' accounts of the nature and scope of her disability.<sup id="cite_ref-independent.co.uk_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent.co.uk-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> A biographer wrote that Rose Kennedy did not confide in her friends and that she pretended her daughter was developing typically, with relatives other than the immediate family knowing nothing of Rosemary's reported low IQ.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Despite the help of tutors, Rosemary had trouble learning to read and write. At age 11, she was sent to a Pennsylvania boarding school for the intellectually disabled.<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>At age 15, Rosemary was sent to the Sacred Heart Convent in <a href="/wiki/Elmhurst,_Providence,_Rhode_Island" title="Elmhurst, Providence, Rhode Island">Elmhurst, Providence, Rhode Island</a>, where she was educated separately from the other students. Two nuns and a special teacher, Miss Newton, worked with her all day in a separate classroom. The Kennedys gave the school a new tennis court for their efforts. Her reading, writing, spelling, and counting skills were reported to be at a fourth-grade level. During this period, her mother arranged for her older brother <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John</a> to accompany her to a tea-dance. Thanks to him, she appeared "not different at all" during the dance.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Rosemary read few books but could read <i><a href="/wiki/Winnie-the-Pooh" title="Winnie-the-Pooh">Winnie-the-Pooh</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Diaries written by her in the late 1930s, and published in the 1980s, reveal a young woman whose life was filled with outings to the opera, tea dances, dress fittings, and other social interests.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy accompanied her family to the coronation of <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XII" title="Pope Pius XII">Pope Pius XII</a> in Rome in 1939. She also visited the White House.<sup id="cite_ref-independent.co.uk_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent.co.uk-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy's parents told <i><a href="/wiki/Woman%27s_Day" title="Woman&#39;s Day">Woman's Day</a></i> that she was "studying to be a kindergarten teacher," and <i><a href="/wiki/Parents_(magazine)" title="Parents (magazine)">Parents</a></i> was told that while she had "an interest in social welfare work, she is said to harbor a secret longing to go on the stage." When <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boston_Globe" title="The Boston Globe">The Boston Globe</a></i> requested an interview with Rosemary, her father's assistant prepared a response which Rosemary copied out laboriously:
</p>
<blockquote><p>I have always had serious tastes and understand life is not given us just for enjoyment. For some time past, I have been studying the well known psychological method of Dr. <a href="/wiki/Maria_Montessori" title="Maria Montessori">Maria Montessori</a> and I got my degree in teaching last year.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>In 1938, Kennedy was presented to King <a href="/wiki/George_VI" title="George VI">George VI</a> and Queen <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Bowes-Lyon" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth Bowes-Lyon">Elizabeth</a> at Buckingham Palace during her father's service as the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Ambassador_to_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Ambassador to the United Kingdom">United States Ambassador to the United Kingdom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy practiced the complicated royal <a href="/wiki/Curtsy" title="Curtsy">curtsy</a> for hours. At the event, she tripped and nearly fell. Rose Kennedy never discussed the incident and treated the debut as a triumph. The crowd made no sign, and the King and the Queen smiled as if nothing had happened.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lobotomy">Lobotomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosemary_Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lobotomy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Eunice Kennedy Shriver, when Rosemary returned to the United States from England in 1940, she regressed; Shriver later stated that Rosemary became "'increasingly irritable and difficult'" at age 22.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Rosemary would often experience convulsions<sup id="cite_ref-auto_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and fly into violent rages in which she would hit and injure others<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> during this period. After being expelled from a summer camp in western Massachusetts and staying only a few months at a Philadelphia boarding school, Rosemary was sent to a convent school in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a><sup id="cite_ref-auto1_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Rosemary began sneaking out of the convent school at night.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The nuns at the convent thought that Rosemary might be involved with men, and that she could contract a sexually transmitted disease<sup id="cite_ref-independent.co.uk_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent.co.uk-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> or become pregnant.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Her occasionally erratic behavior frustrated her parents; Joseph P. Kennedy was especially worried that his daughter's behavior would shame and embarrass the family and damage his and his children's political careers.<sup id="cite_ref-independent_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-auto1_5-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 1941, when Rosemary Kennedy was 23 (and hence legally an adult), doctors told Joseph P. Kennedy that a form of <a href="/wiki/Psychosurgery" title="Psychosurgery">psychosurgery</a> known as a <a href="/wiki/Lobotomy" title="Lobotomy">lobotomy</a> would help calm her mood swings and stop her occasional violent outbursts.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> He decided that his daughter should have the lobotomy performed; however, he did not inform his wife Rose of this until after the procedure was completed.<sup id="cite_ref-independent_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Because Rosemary had been diagnosed as "mentally retarded", only his consent was necessary.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/James_W._Watts" title="James W. Watts">James W. Watts</a>, who carried out the procedure with <a href="/wiki/Walter_Jackson_Freeman_II" title="Walter Jackson Freeman II">Walter Freeman</a>, both of <a href="/wiki/George_Washington_University_School_of_Medicine" class="mw-redirect" title="George Washington University School of Medicine">George Washington University School of Medicine</a>, described what happened next as quoted by <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Kessler" title="Ronald Kessler">Ronald Kessler</a> in his book <i>The Sins of the Father: Joseph P. Kennedy and the Dynasty He Founded</i> in the only interview the doctor ever gave:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>We went through the top of the head, I think Rosemary was awake. She had a mild tranquilizer. I made a surgical incision in the brain through the skull. It was near the front. It was on both sides. We just made a small incision, no more than an inch." The instrument Dr. Watts used looked like a butter knife. He swung it up and down to cut brain tissue. "We put an instrument inside", he said. As Dr. Watts cut, Dr. Freeman asked Rosemary some questions. For example, he asked her to recite the <a href="/wiki/Lord%27s_Prayer" title="Lord&#39;s Prayer">Lord's Prayer</a> or sing "<a href="/wiki/God_Bless_America" title="God Bless America">God Bless America</a>" or count backward. "We made an estimate on how far to cut based on how she responded." When Rosemary began to become incoherent, they stopped.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>It quickly became apparent that the procedure had not been successful. Kennedy's mental capacity diminished to that of a two-year-old child. She could not walk or speak intelligibly and was <a href="/wiki/Urinary_incontinence" title="Urinary incontinence">incontinent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath">Aftermath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosemary_Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Aftermath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the lobotomy, Rosemary was immediately institutionalized. She initially lived for several years at Craig House, a private psychiatric hospital an hour north of <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In 1949, she was relocated to <a href="/wiki/Jefferson,_Wisconsin" title="Jefferson, Wisconsin">Jefferson, Wisconsin</a>, where she lived for the rest of her life on the grounds of the St. Coletta School for Exceptional Children (formerly known as "St. Coletta Institute for Backward Youth").<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Archbishop <a href="/wiki/Richard_Cushing" title="Richard Cushing">Richard Cushing</a> had told her father about St. Coletta's, an institution for more than three hundred people with <a href="/wiki/Disability" title="Disability">disabilities</a>, and her father traveled to and built a private house for her about a mile outside St. Coletta's main campus near Alverno House, which was designed for adults who needed lifelong care.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The nuns called the house "the Kennedy cottage".<sup id="cite_ref-Leamer680_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leamer680-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Two Catholic nuns, Sister Margaret Ann and Sister Leona, provided her care along with a student and a woman who worked on <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">ceramics</a> with Rosemary three nights a week.<sup id="cite_ref-Leamer760_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leamer760-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Rosemary had a car that could be used to take her for rides and a dog whom she could take on walks.<sup id="cite_ref-Leamer680_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Leamer680-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In response to her condition, Rosemary's parents separated her from her family. Rose Kennedy did not visit her for 20 years.<sup id="cite_ref-independent_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Joseph P. Kennedy Sr. did not visit his daughter at the institution.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> In <i>Rosemary: The Hidden Kennedy Daughter,</i> author Kate Clifford Larson stated that Rosemary's lobotomy was hidden from the family for twenty years.<sup id="cite_ref-people.com_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-people.com-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> While her older brother John was campaigning for re-election for the Senate in 1958, the Kennedy family explained away her absence by claiming she was reclusive. The Kennedy family did not publicly explain her absence until 1961, after John had been elected <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President</a>. The Kennedys did not reveal that she was institutionalized because of a failed lobotomy, but instead said that she was deemed "mentally retarded".<sup id="cite_ref-independent_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> In 1961, after Joseph P. Kennedy Sr. suffered a stroke that left him unable to speak, Rosemary's siblings were made aware of her location.<sup id="cite_ref-people.com_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-people.com-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Her lobotomy did not become public knowledge until 1987.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Rosemary_Kennedy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Following Rosemary's father's death in 1969, the Kennedys gradually involved Rosemary in family life again.<sup id="cite_ref-independent.co.uk_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent.co.uk-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Rosemary was occasionally taken to visit relatives<sup id="cite_ref-people.com_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-people.com-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a> and <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, and to her childhood home on <a href="/wiki/Cape_Cod" title="Cape Cod">Cape Cod</a>. By that time, Rosemary had learned to walk again, but did so with a limp. She never regained the ability to speak clearly, and her arm was <a href="/wiki/Erb%27s_palsy" title="Erb&#39;s palsy">palsied</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-independent_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Her condition is sometimes credited as the inspiration for <a href="/wiki/Eunice_Kennedy_Shriver" title="Eunice Kennedy Shriver">Eunice Kennedy Shriver</a> to later found the <a href="/wiki/Special_Olympics" title="Special Olympics">Special Olympics</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-independent_19-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-independent-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> although Shriver told <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> in 1995 that this was not entirely the case.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Rosemary Kennedy died from natural causes<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> on January 7, 2005, at the age of 86.<sup id="cite_ref-auto2_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto2-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy died at the Fort Atkinson Memorial Hospital in <a href="/wiki/Fort_Atkinson,_Wisconsin" title="Fort Atkinson, Wisconsin">Fort Atkinson, Wisconsin</a><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> with her surviving siblings (sisters <a href="/wiki/Jean_Kennedy_Smith" title="Jean Kennedy Smith">Jean</a>, Eunice, and Patricia and brother <a href="/wiki/Ted_Kennedy" title="Ted Kennedy">Ted</a>) by her side.<sup id="cite_ref-auto2_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto2-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> She was buried beside her parents in <a href="/wiki/Holyhood_Cemetery" title="Holyhood Cemetery">Holyhood Cemetery</a> in Brookline, Massachusetts.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
