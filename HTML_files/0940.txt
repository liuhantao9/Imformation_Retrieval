<p><b>John G. F. "Jack" Speiden</b> (March 4, 1900 − July 30, 1970) was an American <a href="/wiki/Stockbroker" title="Stockbroker">stockbroker</a> and ranch owner. Speiden fought in both World Wars, attended <a href="/wiki/Yale" class="mw-redirect" title="Yale">Yale</a> and received a letter for <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">football</a> while playing on the <a href="/wiki/Hockey" title="Hockey">hockey</a> team, taught in China, worked on <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a>, and bought a ranch.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  He ran for Congress for the 2nd District of Arizona in 1956<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and 1958,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> defeated by <a href="/wiki/Stewart_Udall" title="Stewart Udall">Stewart Udall</a> both times. Charlie Ohrel, who inherited most of the information about Speiden, after his death, summed up his life with a humorous understatement; "He sure did give it a good shot." Speiden's ranch, the <b>Jay Six Ranch</b>, left a legacy of its own. The ranch played host to political figures like the young brothers <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Kennedy_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph Kennedy Jr.">Joseph</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/John_Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="John Kennedy">John Kennedy</a>, and to senior statesman <a href="/wiki/Barry_Goldwater" title="Barry Goldwater">Barry Goldwater</a>. The Kennedy brothers (Joe age 21 and John age 19) were sent out to the 40,000-acre (160&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) Jay Six Ranch in 1936 so that Jack Kennedy could recuperate in the dry desert heat. It is reported that Speiden worked them both "very hard".
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_Speiden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At sixteen, Speiden joined an American volunteer ambulance unit headed for France in World War I. The United States entered the war a year later in 1917. Speiden, already in France, promptly joined the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Marine_Corps" title="United States Marine Corps">Marines</a> and was returned to the States to train at <a href="/wiki/Marine_Corps_Recruit_Depot_Parris_Island" title="Marine Corps Recruit Depot Parris Island">Parris Island</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, then back to France with the <a href="/wiki/U.S._2nd_Marine_Division" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. 2nd Marine Division">2nd Division</a>. When the war ended, Speiden entered <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">Yale University</a>. He played on the <a href="/wiki/Hockey" title="Hockey">hockey</a> team and received his letter as a <a href="/wiki/Fullback_(American_football)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fullback (American football)">fullback</a> on the <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">football</a> team. After graduation, he still craved adventure, so he went to China with a Yale group. He taught English and <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">economics</a> at <a href="/wiki/Changsha" title="Changsha">Changsha</a>, deep in the interior of <a href="/wiki/Hunan_Province" class="mw-redirect" title="Hunan Province">Hunan Province</a>. He remained there for a year and spent the next year visiting  <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a>, India, <a href="/wiki/British_Malaya" title="British Malaya">Malaya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Burma" class="mw-redirect" title="Burma">Burma</a> with fellow Yale alumni Ward Cheney and <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Woodward" title="Stanley Woodward">Stanley Woodward</a>. During these travels he went hunding along with two American friends of his with the Nawab of Bhopal, quite probably <a href="/wiki/Hamidullah_Khan" title="Hamidullah Khan">Hamidullah Khan</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  After returning to the United States in 1924, Speiden embarked on a successful <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a> career. That career came to an end with the depression and <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street_Crash_1929" class="mw-redirect" title="Wall Street Crash 1929">Wall Street crash of 1929</a>. After a trip down to <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">Honduras</a> that same year, he caught a mysterious virus and was told to head west to the dry heat until his ailment was cured.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_in_the_West">Life in the West</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_Speiden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Life in the West">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Speiden recovered from the virus in twelve months. A chance meeting with <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Brisbane" title="Arthur Brisbane">Arthur Brisbane</a>, the famous Hearst newspaper editor, in the spring of 1933, convinced him that he should make the American west his home. Brisbane told young Speiden that the country was rounding out the bottom of a business cycle and the smart thing to do was to invest in land commodities. Speiden took the advice which subsequently proved sound.
</p><p>His boyhood had been spent in the back country of <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> and he had learned to ride almost before he could walk. The cattle business seemed natural enough, but he thought it best to break in gradually. He acquired a small registered herd of 60 cows which, with the aid of one cowboy, he ran as part of the 76 Ranch in <a href="/wiki/Cochise_County,_Arizona" title="Cochise County, Arizona">Cochise County</a>. He concurrently embarked on course of self-education. Speiden read every available book on <a href="/wiki/Animal_husbandry" title="Animal husbandry">animal husbandry</a> and was greatly helped by the excellent Animal Husbandry Department of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Arizona" title="University of Arizona">University of Arizona</a>. After several years of solid study along with practical experience, Speiden purchased what is now known as the <b>Jay-Six Ranch</b> near <a href="/wiki/Benson,_Arizona" title="Benson, Arizona">Benson, Arizona</a>.
</p><p>When Speiden took possession of the ranch it was carrying upwards of 1000 head. The initial problem was to thin-out the herd and leave only registered stock. It was a slow tedious process. He realized that it was of the utmost importance to bring in the best possible breeding stock so he acquired bulls which were direct descendants of well-known strains as Royal Domino 2nd, Royal Triumph and Anxiety 4th − names which spell top quality to those in the business. The policy of weeding out the undesirables proved effective. At the ranch's peak, he had one of the most representative herds in the country. His cows were raised strictly for breeding purposes and not for the show ring, although they could have undoubtedly held their own against some of the blue ribbon winners of the day.
</p><p>When Speiden took on <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> and his brother <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Kennedy_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph Kennedy Jr.">Joe</a> he paid them a dollar a day. At that point Speiden had two other employees.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Speiden's passion for the improvement of breed carried into the <a href="/wiki/Quarter_horse" class="mw-redirect" title="Quarter horse">quarter horse</a> field, but on a much smaller scale.  Again the emphasis was on quality. Although the venture was largely a hobby, it was obvious that a great amount of time went into selecting the best possible bloodlines. The results were gratifying as the Speiden colors were often first at the finish line.
</p><p>The layout of the ranch was perfectly suited to the improvement of the breed and to the conserve of the range. The huge area allowed the cattle to graze on healthy, knee-high grass.  Each year during the growing season, several of the fourteen pastures remained unused which gave the new grass an opportunity of coming in good and strong. This method of keeping the grass so high was both aesthetically beautiful and practical, as soil <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a> was kept to a minimum and no deep gullies marred the landscape. These gorgeous grassy pastures extended up the mountain slope. The tall pine trees and cooler temperatures made a perfect summer retreat from the exhausting sun. A freshening stream satisfied the water problem and irrigated a lush <a href="/wiki/Peach" title="Peach">peach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pear" title="Pear">pear</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Apple" title="Apple">apple</a> orchard.
</p><p>The ranch's beauty and isolation did not go without notice. People from around the nation came to the ranch for varied reasons. Many came to the ranch for enjoyment and relaxation. Included on the list was <a href="/wiki/Barry_Goldwater" title="Barry Goldwater">Barry Goldwater</a> "to find relaxation, or to toughen up".
</p><p>In 1969 a gala dinner was held honoring Speiden's 25 years of contributions to Arizona.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Speiden was made a member of the National Cowboy Hall of Fame and for a time was the head of the Arizona Hereford Association.<sup id="cite_ref-obit_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obit-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_Speiden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Speiden rose to the rank of Lt. Colonel while in the service during World War II. He also received the Bronze Star.<sup id="cite_ref-obit_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obit-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_involvement">Political involvement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_Speiden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Political involvement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Speiden not only ran twice as a Republican for Congress, but he was Pima County Republican Chairman in 1958, 1960 and 1961.<sup id="cite_ref-obit_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-obit-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family">Family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_Speiden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Speiden married Caroline Bayard Stevens. His daughter Leith was a child from his previous marriage to Rachel Hammond.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_Speiden&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to an obituary, printed in the <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> on August 2, 1970, John G. F. Speiden ("Arizona Ranchman") died the previous Thursday, July 30, 1970. Age 70. Cause of death was listed as drowning in his swimming pool. The article states that Mr. Speiden drowned in his pool, at his home.
</p><p>A July 31, 1970 article in the <i><a href="/wiki/Tucson_Daily_Citizen" class="mw-redirect" title="Tucson Daily Citizen">Tucson Daily Citizen</a></i> remarked on the passing of Speiden, and carried a statement from Gov Jack Williams which read, in part, that "Mr Speiden (was) a gallant gentleman and a courageous soldier and citizen, who has been lost to Arizona and the world" The newspaper noted that "survivors include Caroline Stevens Speiden, a Leith Klauber of Montreal, two sisters Countess Eleanor Davico of Milan Italy and Dr Katherine Caddick of London England and a grand-son."
</p>
