<p><b>New Milford</b> is a town in <a href="/wiki/Litchfield_County,_Connecticut" title="Litchfield County, Connecticut">Litchfield County</a>, <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, located in <a href="/wiki/Western_Connecticut" title="Western Connecticut">Western Connecticut</a>. The town is located 14 miles (23&#160;km) north of <a href="/wiki/Danbury,_Connecticut" title="Danbury, Connecticut">Danbury</a>, on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Housatonic_River" title="Housatonic River">Housatonic River</a>. It is the largest town in the state in terms of land area at nearly 62 square miles (161&#160;km<sup>2</sup>). The population was 28,142 according to the 2010 <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census" title="United States Census">Census</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The town center is also listed as a <a href="/wiki/Census-designated_place" title="Census-designated place">census-designated place</a> (CDP).  The northern portion of the town is situated in the region considered Northwestern CT and the far eastern portions are part of the Litchfield Hills region.
</p><p>It is located roughly 55 miles from Hartford, 75 miles from Springfield, 100 miles from Albany and 85 from NYC.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Milford,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Native_Americans">Native Americans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Milford,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Native Americans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Weantinock" title="Weantinock">Weantinock</a> were a sub-group of the <a href="/wiki/Paugussett" class="mw-redirect" title="Paugussett">Paugussett</a> Nation, who lived the area of modern New Milford both before and during the colonial era. They had a <a href="/wiki/Farming" class="mw-redirect" title="Farming">farming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fishing" title="Fishing">fishing</a> culture, cultivating corn, squash, beans and tobacco, and fishing in freshwater areas. They may have also travelled to the coast to fish during the summer months.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> They spoke an Algonquian language.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonial_times">Colonial times</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Milford,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Colonial times">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1707, John Noble Sr., previously of <a href="/wiki/Westfield,_Massachusetts" title="Westfield, Massachusetts">Westfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a>, and his eight-year-old daughter Sarah Noble were the first Anglo-American settlers.  (A public school was later named after Sarah Noble.)  They were soon joined by others who had also bought land there.<sup id="cite_ref-hist_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hist-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On October 17, 1711, twelve families (including a total about 70 people) petitioned the General Assembly to create the town, together with the associated privilege of levying a tax to support a minister. With the legislature's approval, the town was organized the next year. The residents soon secured Daniel Boardman to preach and he was ordained as the minister of the <a href="/wiki/Congregational_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Congregational Church">Congregational Church</a> on November 21, 1716.<sup id="cite_ref-hist_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hist-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_Revolution">American Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Milford,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: American Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Roger_Sherman" title="Roger Sherman">Roger Sherman</a> lived in New Milford before moving to New Haven in 1761.<sup id="cite_ref-hist_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hist-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He later became a member of the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Congress" title="Continental Congress">Continental Congress</a> and signed both the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Declaration_of_Independence" title="United States Declaration of Independence">Declaration of Independence</a> and <a href="/wiki/U.S._Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Constitution">U.S. Constitution</a>.  The lot of his former house is the site of the present Town Hall.
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolution" title="American Revolution">American Revolution</a>, the <a href="/wiki/7th_Connecticut_Regiment" title="7th Connecticut Regiment">7th Connecticut Regiment</a> (also known as 19th Continental Regiment) was raised in town on September 16, 1776. The regiment, and the New Milford men in it, would see action in the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Brandywine" title="Battle of Brandywine">Battle of Brandywine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Germantown" title="Battle of Germantown">Battle of Germantown</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Monmouth" title="Battle of Monmouth">Battle of Monmouth</a>. In total, the town "sent 285 men to fight in the War out of a total population of 2,776."<sup id="cite_ref-hist_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hist-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Boardman_family">The Boardman family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Milford,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Boardman family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<ul><li><a href="/wiki/David_Sherman_Boardman" title="David Sherman Boardman">David Sherman Boardman</a> (1768–1864), was the youngest child of <a href="/wiki/Deacon" title="Deacon">Deacon</a> Sherman and Sarah (Bostwick) Boardman. He became a lawyer in town and later chief judge in Litchfield County Court. He served as judge of probate for the district of New Milford in 1805, and held the place by successive annual appointments for sixteen years. He was elected Representative to the General Assembly eight times.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Elijah_Boardman" title="Elijah Boardman">Elijah Boardman</a> (1760–1823) was a <a href="/wiki/U.S._Senator" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Senator">U.S. Senator</a> representing Connecticut. Born in New Milford, he was educated by private tutors, and served in the <a href="/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War" title="American Revolutionary War">American Revolutionary War</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/William_Whiting_Boardman" title="William Whiting Boardman">William Whiting Boardman</a> (1794–1871), a  <a href="/wiki/U.S._Representative" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Representative">U.S. Representative</a> born in town, was the son of Elijah Boardman. He was a Connecticut <a href="/wiki/State_senator" title="State senator">state senator</a> in the fourth district, 1830–32, a member of the Connecticut State House of Representatives, 1836–39, 1845, and 1849–51; Speaker of the Connecticut State House of Representatives, 1836, 1839, and 1845; US Representative from Connecticut's second district, 1840-43. He died in New Haven, Connecticut, and is interred at Grove Street Cemetery in New Milford.</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_and_20th_centuries">19th and 20th centuries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Milford,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 19th and 20th centuries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the second half of the 19th century, many new industries came to town. The Water Witch Hose Company No. 2, local telephone and electricity companies, and newspapers were all founded. Factories in town made buttons, paint and varnish, hats, furniture, pottery, lime, dairy products and pasteboard, among other goods. <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">Tobacco</a> became the major crop in the area, and tobacco warehouses sprang up to handle its storage and processing before sales.<sup id="cite_ref-hist_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hist-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1931 Housatonic Valley Insurance Agency was established, and became one of the founding members of the New Milford Chamber of Commerce later that year. Today Housatonic Valley Insurance Agency is still located on the New Milford Green in the charming historic white colonial house built in 1795, directly next door to Town Hall.
</p><p>In 1942 <a href="/wiki/Buck%27s_Rock" title="Buck&#39;s Rock">Buck's Rock Camp</a> was founded off of Bucks Rock Road, and has remained in operation ever since.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Milford,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 21st century">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The town has constructed a 1,000,000-gallon sewer plant expansion on West Street, a sewer pump station on Boardman Road, reconstruction of the Rte. 67/ Grove Street Intersection, and ambulance facility on Scovill Street.
</p><p>The town has added a skate park at Young's Field (2006), reconstructed the tennis and basketball courts at Young's Field (2010), reconstructed the basketball court at Williamson Park in Gaylordsville (2010), and improved Lynn Deming Park (2017), and is working on the New Milford River Trail,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> which will eventually join the existing 1.5-mile Sega Meadows Park trail (2012), 3.5 miles of River Road, and the 0.25-mile Young's Field River Trail (2017) and link them to the greenways in the neighboring towns of Brookfield and Kent.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  Several streetscape projects were completed by the Department of Public Works (DPW) with grant money on Church Street, Whittlesey Avenue, and the west side of East Street (2009/2010). Candlewoof Dog Park is completed on Pickett District Road. A <a href="/wiki/Bocce" title="Bocce">bocce</a> ball court was constructed at the Senior Center by Boy Scout Troop 66 (2012).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Milford,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>New Milford is located on the northeastern shore of <a href="/wiki/Candlewood_Lake" title="Candlewood Lake">Lake Candlewood</a>. The Aspetuck River, Still River and <a href="/wiki/Housatonic_River" title="Housatonic River">Housatonic River</a> flow through the town.
</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">United States Census Bureau</a>, the town has a total area of 63.7 square miles (165&#160;km<sup>2</sup>), making it the largest town in Connecticut.  61.6 square miles (160&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of it is land and 2.1 square miles (5.4&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of it (3.31%) is water. The CDP corresponding to the town center has a total area of 3.4 square miles (8.8&#160;km<sup>2</sup>).  3.4 square miles (8.8&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of it is land and 0.04 square miles (0.10&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) of it (0.88%) is water.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Milford,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>New Milford has a humid <a href="/wiki/Continental_climate" title="Continental climate">continental climate</a>, with mild to warm humid summers and cold to very cold winters. The highest recorded temperature was 103&#160;°F (39&#160;°C) in July 1966, while the lowest recorded temperature was -15&#160;°F (-26&#160;°C) in 1968.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Snowfall" class="mw-redirect" title="Snowfall">Snowfall</a> is generally frequent in winter while average precipitation is most common in September.

<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> of 2010, there were 28,142 people, 10,618 households, and 7,503 families residing in the town.  The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 456.9 people per square mile (176.4/km²).  There were 11,731 housing units at an average density of 190.4 per square mile (73.5/km²).  The racial makeup of the town was 91.71% <a href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 1.72% <a href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="African American (U.S. Census)">Black</a> or <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">African American</a>, 0.24% <a href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 2.77% <a href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 0.04% <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 1.65% from <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 1.87% from two or more races. <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race was 6.02% of the population.
</p><p>Of the 10,618 households 33.4% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 58.0% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 8.8% had a female householder with no husband present, and 29.3% were non-families. 23.0% of all households were made up of individuals and 7.9% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older.  The average household size was 2.62 and the average family size was 3.13.
</p><p>In the town, the population had 24.30% under the age of 18, 6.87% from 18 to 24, 24.90% from 25 to 44, 31.75% from 45 to 64, and 12.18% who were 65 years of age or older.  The median age was 41.4 years. For every 100 females, there were 97.4 males.  For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 93.6 males.
</p><p>As of the 2000 Census the median income for a household in the town was $65,354, and the median income for a family was $75,775. Males had a median income of $50,523 versus $34,089 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the town was $29,630.  About 2.1% of families and 3.3% of the population were below the <a href="/wiki/Poverty_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 2.7% of those under age 18 and 5.5% of those age 65 or over.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Town_center">Town center</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Milford,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Town center">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As of the <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">census</a><sup id="cite_ref-GR2_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GR2-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> of 2000, there were 6,633 people, 2,756 households, and 1,603 families residing in the town center <a href="/wiki/Census-designated_place" title="Census-designated place">CDP</a>.  The <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a> was 1,955.7 inhabitants per square mile (755.5/km²).  There were 2,872 housing units at an average density of 846.8 per square mile (327.1/km²).  The racial makeup of the CDP was 93.19% <a href="/wiki/White_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="White (U.S. Census)">White</a>, 1.82% <a href="/wiki/African_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="African American (U.S. Census)">Black</a> or <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">African American</a>, 0.27% <a href="/wiki/Native_American_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Native American (U.S. Census)">Native American</a>, 2.11% <a href="/wiki/Asian_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Asian (U.S. Census)">Asian</a>, 0.02% <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islander_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pacific Islander (U.S. Census)">Pacific Islander</a>, 0.84% from <a href="/wiki/Race_(United_States_Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Race (United States Census)">other races</a>, and 1.75% from two or more races. <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hispanic (U.S. Census)">Hispanic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Latino_(U.S._Census)" class="mw-redirect" title="Latino (U.S. Census)">Latino</a> of any race were 3.09% of the population.
</p><p>There were 2,756 households out of which 30.9% had children under the age of 18 living with them, 42.4% were <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">married couples</a> living together, 11.4% had a female householder with no husband present, and 41.8% were non-families. 33.2% of all households were made up of individuals and 9.4% had someone living alone who was 65 years of age or older.  The average household size was 2.37 and the average family size was 3.07.
</p><p>In the CDP the population was spread out with 24.4% under the age of 18, 8.2% from 18 to 24, 35.9% from 25 to 44, 20.3% from 45 to 64, and 11.2% who were 65 years of age or older.  The median age was 35 years. For every 100 females, there were 93.8 males.  For every 100 females age 18 and over, there were 88.5 males.
</p><p>The median income for a household in the CDP was $48,186, and the median income for a family was $58,367. Males had a median income of $38,571 versus $26,833 for females. The <a href="/wiki/Per_capita_income" title="Per capita income">per capita income</a> for the CDP was $22,912.  About 3.7% of families and 6.3% of the population were below the <a href="/wiki/Poverty_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Poverty line">poverty line</a>, including 8.7% of those under age 18 and 6.7% of those age 65 or over.
</p>

<p>New Milford is home to the GMS Rowing Center.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Founded in 2003, it manages a US Rowing Training Center Program. It has a highly successful Middle and High School (Junior) Program which competes at Youth National Championships, Junior National Team Trials, The "Royal Canadian Henley" and has sent rowers to the Junior World Rowing Championships.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In 2011 GMS also had rowers representing the USA at the Under 23 World Championships in Amsterdam, The Netherlands and at the <a href="/wiki/World_Rowing_Championships" title="World Rowing Championships">World Rowing Championships</a> at Bled, Slovenia.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Infrastructure">Infrastructure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Milford,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Infrastructure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Milford,_Connecticut&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>New Milford is served by fixed-bus routes of the <a href="/wiki/Housatonic_Area_Regional_Transit" title="Housatonic Area Regional Transit">Housatonic Area Regional Transit</a>. The main highways of the town are <a href="/wiki/Route_7_(Connecticut)" class="mw-redirect" title="Route 7 (Connecticut)">Route 7</a> and <a href="/wiki/Route_202_(Connecticut)" class="mw-redirect" title="Route 202 (Connecticut)">Route 202</a>.  There has been continued talk about a proposal to electrify and extend the <a href="/wiki/Danbury_Branch" title="Danbury Branch">Danbury Branch</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Metro-North_Railroad" title="Metro-North Railroad">Metro-North Railroad</a> north of <a href="/wiki/Danbury,_Connecticut" title="Danbury, Connecticut">Danbury</a> to New Milford, including a Rail Study in 2008 <sup id="cite_ref-newmilford_danbury_branch_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-newmilford_danbury_branch-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and proposed state legislation in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The long-awaited completion of Super 7 happened in November 2009.  The realignment of Grove Street and Prospect Hill Road (Rte. 67) was completed in the Fall of 2010.  The Department of Public Works (DPW) awarded Stimulus ARRA Project 95-249 Grove Street (south of Anderson Ave) and Boardman Road (west of O+G Quarry). This was completed in the fall of 2010.
</p>
