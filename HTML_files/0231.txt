<p>The <b>Kingdom of France</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <i lang="fr">Royaume de France</i>) was a <a href="/wiki/France_in_the_Middle_Ages" title="France in the Middle Ages">medieval</a> and <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_France" title="Early modern France">early modern</a> monarchy in <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a>. It was among the most powerful states in Europe and a <a href="/wiki/Great_power" title="Great power">great power</a> since the <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">Late Middle Ages</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years&#39; War">Hundred Years' War</a>. It was also an early <a href="/wiki/French_colonial_empire" title="French colonial empire">colonial power</a>, with possessions around the world.
</p><p>France originated as <a href="/wiki/West_Francia" title="West Francia">West Francia</a> (<i>Francia Occidentalis</i>), the western half of the <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Empire" title="Carolingian Empire">Carolingian Empire</a>, with the <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Verdun" title="Treaty of Verdun">Treaty of Verdun</a> (843). A branch of the Carolingian dynasty continued to rule until 987, when <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Capet" title="Hugh Capet">Hugh Capet</a> was elected king and founded the <a href="/wiki/Capetian_dynasty" title="Capetian dynasty">Capetian dynasty</a>. The territory remained known as <i>Francia</i> and its ruler as <i>rex Francorum</i> ("king of the Franks") well into the <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">High Middle Ages</a>. The first king calling himself <i>Roi de France</i> ("King of France") was <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_France" title="Philip II of France">Philip II</a>, in 1190. France continued to be ruled by the Capetians and their cadet lines—the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Valois" title="House of Valois">Valois</a> and <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">Bourbon</a>—until the monarchy was abolished in 1792 during the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/France_in_the_Middle_Ages" title="France in the Middle Ages">France in the Middle Ages</a> was a de-centralised, <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudal</a> monarchy. In <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Brittany" title="Duchy of Brittany">Brittany</a> and <a href="/wiki/Principality_of_Catalonia" title="Principality of Catalonia">Catalonia</a> (now a part of Spain) the authority of the French king was barely felt. <a href="/wiki/Lorraine_(duchy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lorraine (duchy)">Lorraine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Arles" title="Kingdom of Arles">Provence</a> were states of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> and not yet a part of France. Initially, West Frankish kings were elected by the secular and ecclesiastic magnates, but the regular coronation of the eldest son of the reigning king during his father's lifetime established the principle of male primogeniture, which became codified in the <a href="/wiki/Salic_law" title="Salic law">Salic law</a>. During the <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">Late Middle Ages</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Kings_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Kings of England">Kings of England</a> laid claim to the French throne, resulting in a series of conflicts known as the Hundred Years' War (1337–1453). Subsequently, France sought to extend its influence into Italy, but was defeated by <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">Spain</a> in the ensuing <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a> (1494–1559).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Early_modern_France" title="Early modern France">France in the early modern era</a> was increasingly centralised; the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French language</a> began to displace other languages from official use, and the monarch expanded his <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolute power</a>, albeit in an administrative system (the <i><a href="/wiki/Ancien_R%C3%A9gime" title="Ancien Régime">Ancien Régime</a></i>) complicated by historic and regional irregularities in taxation, legal, judicial, and ecclesiastic divisions, and local prerogatives. Religiously France became divided between the Catholic majority and a Protestant minority, the <a href="/wiki/Huguenots" title="Huguenots">Huguenots</a>, which led to a series of civil wars, the <a href="/wiki/French_Wars_of_Religion" title="French Wars of Religion">Wars of Religion</a> (1562–1598). France laid claim to large stretches of North America, known collectively as <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a>. Wars with <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Great Britain</a> led to the loss of much of this territory by 1763. <a href="/wiki/France_in_the_American_Revolutionary_War" title="France in the American Revolutionary War">French intervention in the American Revolutionary War</a> helped secure the independence of  the new United States of America but was costly and achieved little for France.
</p><p>The Kingdom of France <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France_(1791%E2%80%9392)" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of France (1791–92)">adopted a written constitution</a> in 1791, but the Kingdom was abolished a year later and replaced with the <a href="/wiki/First_French_Republic" class="mw-redirect" title="First French Republic">First French Republic</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_Restoration" title="Bourbon Restoration">monarchy was restored</a> by the other great powers in 1814 and lasted (except for the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Days" title="Hundred Days">Hundred Days</a> in 1815) until the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution_of_1848" title="French Revolution of 1848">French Revolution of 1848</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Political_history">Political history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Political history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="West_Francia">West Francia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: West Francia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/West_Francia" title="West Francia">West Francia</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_Empire" title="Carolingian Empire">Carolingian Empire</a></div>
<p>During the later years of the elderly <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a>'s rule, the <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Vikings</a> made advances along the northern and western perimeters of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Franks" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of the Franks">Kingdom of the Franks</a>. After Charlemagne's death in 814 his heirs were incapable of maintaining political unity and the empire began to crumble. The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Verdun" title="Treaty of Verdun">Treaty of Verdun</a> of 843 divided the Carolingian Empire into three parts, with  <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Bald" title="Charles the Bald">Charles the Bald</a> ruling over <a href="/wiki/West_Francia" title="West Francia">West Francia</a>, the nucleus of what would develop into the kingdom of France.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
Charles the Bald was also crowned <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Lotharingia" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Lotharingia">King of Lotharingia</a> after the death of <a href="/wiki/Lothair_II" title="Lothair II">Lothair II</a> in 869, but in the  <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Meerssen" title="Treaty of Meerssen">Treaty of Meerssen</a> (870) was forced to cede much of Lotharingia to his brothers, retaining the <a href="/wiki/Rhone" class="mw-redirect" title="Rhone">Rhone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Meuse" title="Meuse">Meuse</a> basins (including <a href="/wiki/Verdun" title="Verdun">Verdun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vienne" title="Vienne">Vienne</a> and <a href="/wiki/Besan%C3%A7on" title="Besançon">Besançon</a>) but leaving the <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">Rhineland</a> with <a href="/wiki/Aachen" title="Aachen">Aachen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metz" title="Metz">Metz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Trier" title="Trier">Trier</a> in <a href="/wiki/East_Francia" title="East Francia">East Francia</a>.
</p><p>Viking advances were allowed to increase, and their dreaded <a href="/wiki/Longship" title="Longship">longships</a> were sailing up the <a href="/wiki/Loire" title="Loire">Loire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Seine" title="Seine">Seine</a> rivers and other inland waterways, wreaking havoc and spreading terror. During the reign of <a href="/wiki/Charles_the_Simple" title="Charles the Simple">Charles the Simple</a> (898–922), Normans under <a href="/wiki/Rollo" title="Rollo">Rollo</a> from <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>, were settled in an area on either side of the River Seine, downstream from Paris, that was to become <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="High_Middle_Ages">High Middle Ages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: High Middle Ages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/France_in_the_Middle_Ages" title="France in the Middle Ages">France in the Middle Ages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Capetian_dynasty" title="Capetian dynasty">Capetian dynasty</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Carolingians" class="mw-redirect" title="Carolingians">Carolingians</a> were to share the fate of their predecessors: after an intermittent power struggle between the two dynasties, the accession in 987 of <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Capet" title="Hugh Capet">Hugh Capet</a>, Duke of France and Count of Paris, established the <a href="/wiki/Capetian_dynasty" title="Capetian dynasty">Capetian dynasty</a> on the throne. With its offshoots, the houses of <a href="/wiki/House_of_Valois" title="House of Valois">Valois</a> and <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">Bourbon</a>, it was to rule France for more than 800 years.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The old order left the new dynasty in immediate control of little beyond the middle Seine and adjacent territories, while powerful territorial lords such as the 10th- and 11th-century <a href="/wiki/Counts_of_Blois" title="Counts of Blois">counts of Blois</a> accumulated large domains of their own through marriage and through private arrangements with lesser nobles for protection and support.
</p><p>The area around the lower Seine became a source of particular concern when <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="William I of England">Duke William</a> took possession of the kingdom of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a> of 1066, making himself and his heirs the King's equal outside France (where he was still nominally subject to the Crown).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II</a> inherited the Duchy of Normandy and the <a href="/wiki/Counts_and_dukes_of_Anjou" title="Counts and dukes of Anjou">County of Anjou</a>, and married France's newly divorced ex-queen, <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_of_Aquitaine" title="Eleanor of Aquitaine">Eleanor of Aquitaine</a>, who ruled much of southwest France, in 1152. After defeating a <a href="/wiki/Revolt_of_1173%E2%80%931174" class="mw-redirect" title="Revolt of 1173–1174">revolt</a> led by Eleanor and three of their four sons, Henry had Eleanor imprisoned, made the <a href="/wiki/List_of_rulers_of_Brittany" title="List of rulers of Brittany">Duke of Brittany</a> his vassal, and in effect ruled the western half of France as a greater power than the French throne. However, disputes among Henry's descendants over the division of his French territories, coupled with <a href="/wiki/John_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="John of England">John of England</a>'s lengthy quarrel with Philip II, allowed <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_France" title="Philip II of France">Philip II</a> to recover influence over most of this territory. After the French victory at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Bouvines" title="Battle of Bouvines">Battle of Bouvines</a> in 1214, the English monarchs maintained power only in southwestern Duchy of <a href="/wiki/Guyenne" title="Guyenne">Guyenne</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Late_Middle_Ages_and_the_Hundred_Years.27_War"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_Middle_Ages_and_the_Hundred_Years'_War">Late Middle Ages and the Hundred Years' War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Late Middle Ages and the Hundred Years&#039; War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/France_in_the_Middle_Ages" title="France in the Middle Ages">France in the Middle Ages</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years&#39; War">Hundred Years' War</a></div>
<p>The death of <a href="/wiki/Charles_IV_of_France" title="Charles IV of France">Charles IV of France</a> in 1328 without male heirs ended the main Capetian line. Under <a href="/wiki/Salic_law" title="Salic law">Salic law</a> the crown could not pass through a woman (Philip IV's daughter was Isabella, whose son was <a href="/wiki/Edward_III_of_England" title="Edward III of England">Edward III of England</a>), so the throne passed to <a href="/wiki/Philip_VI_of_France" title="Philip VI of France">Philip VI</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Charles_of_Valois" class="mw-redirect" title="Charles of Valois">Charles of Valois</a>. This, in addition to a long-standing dispute over the rights to Gascony in the south of France, and the relationship between England and the Flemish cloth towns, led to the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years&#39; War">Hundred Years' War</a> of 1337–1453. The following century was to see devastating warfare, peasant revolts (the <a href="/wiki/English_peasants%27_revolt_of_1381" class="mw-redirect" title="English peasants&#39; revolt of 1381">English peasants' revolt of 1381</a> and the <i><a href="/wiki/Jacquerie" title="Jacquerie">Jacquerie</a></i> of 1358 in France) and the growth of nationalism in both countries.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The losses of the century of war were enormous, particularly owing to the plague (the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a>, usually considered an outbreak of <a href="/wiki/Bubonic_plague" title="Bubonic plague">bubonic plague</a>), which arrived from Italy in 1348, spreading rapidly up the Rhone valley and thence across most of the country: it is estimated that a population of some 18–20 million in modern-day France at the time of the 1328 <a href="/wiki/Hearth_tax" title="Hearth tax">hearth tax</a> returns had been reduced 150 years later by 50 percent or more.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance_and_Reformation">Renaissance and Reformation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Renaissance and Reformation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Renaissance era was noted for the emergence of powerful centralized institutions, as well as a flourishing culture (much of it imported from Italy).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The kings built a strong fiscal system, which heightened the power of the king to raise armies that overawed the local nobility.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  
In Paris especially there emerged strong traditions in literature, art and music.  The prevailing style was <a href="/wiki/Classicism" title="Classicism">classical</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ordinance_of_Villers-Cotter%C3%AAts" title="Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts">Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts</a> was signed into law by <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">Francis I</a> in 1539.
Largely the work of Chancellor <a href="/wiki/Guillaume_Poyet" title="Guillaume Poyet">Guillaume Poyet</a>, it  dealt with a number of government, judicial and ecclesiastical matters. Articles 110 and 111, the most famous, called for the use of the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French language</a> in all legal acts, notarised contracts and official legislation.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Italian_Wars">Italian Wars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Italian Wars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a></div>
<p>After the Hundred Years' War, <a href="/wiki/Charles_VIII_of_France" title="Charles VIII of France">Charles VIII of France</a> signed three additional treaties with <a href="/wiki/Henry_VII_of_England" title="Henry VII of England">Henry VII of England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maximilian_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Maximilian I, Holy Roman Emperor">Maximilian I of Habsburg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferdinand II of Aragon</a> respectively at <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Etaples" class="mw-redirect" title="Peace of Etaples">Étaples</a> (1492), <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Senlis" title="Treaty of Senlis">Senlis</a> (1493) and in <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Barcelona" class="mw-redirect" title="Treaty of Barcelona">Barcelona</a> (1493). These three treaties cleared the way for France to undertake the long <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a> (1494–1559), which marked the beginning of early modern France. French efforts to gain dominance resulted only in the increased power of the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg">Habsburg</a> house.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wars_of_Religion">Wars of Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Wars of Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/French_Wars_of_Religion" title="French Wars of Religion">French Wars of Religion</a></div>
<p>Barely were the Italian Wars over, when France was plunged into a domestic crisis with far-reaching consequences. Despite the conclusion of <a href="/wiki/Concordat_of_Bologna" title="Concordat of Bologna">a Concordat</a> between France and the Papacy (1516), granting the crown unrivalled power in senior ecclesiastical appointments, France was deeply affected by the <a href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant Reformation">Protestant Reformation</a>'s attempt to break the hegemony of Catholic Europe. A growing urban-based Protestant minority (later dubbed <i><a href="/wiki/Huguenots" title="Huguenots">Huguenots</a></i>) faced ever harsher repression under the rule of Francis I's son <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_France" title="Henry II of France">King Henry II</a>. After Henry II's death in a joust, the country was ruled by his widow <a href="/wiki/Catherine_de%27_Medici" title="Catherine de&#39; Medici">Catherine de' Medici</a> and her sons <a href="/wiki/Francis_II_of_France" title="Francis II of France">Francis II</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_IX_of_France" title="Charles IX of France">Charles IX</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_France" title="Henry III of France">Henry III</a>. Renewed Catholic reaction headed by the powerful dukes of <a href="/wiki/Guise" title="Guise">Guise</a> culminated in a massacre of Huguenots (1562), starting the first of the <a href="/wiki/French_Wars_of_Religion" title="French Wars of Religion">French Wars of Religion</a>, during which English, German, and Spanish forces intervened on the side of rival Protestant and Catholic forces. Opposed to absolute monarchy, the Huguenot <a href="/wiki/Monarchomachs" title="Monarchomachs">Monarchomachs</a> theorized during this time the <a href="/wiki/Right_of_rebellion" class="mw-redirect" title="Right of rebellion">right of rebellion</a> and the legitimacy of <a href="/wiki/Tyrannicide" title="Tyrannicide">tyrannicide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Wars of Religion culminated in the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Three_Henrys_(1584-1598)" class="mw-redirect" title="War of the Three Henrys (1584-1598)">War of the Three Henrys</a> in which <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_France" title="Henry III of France">Henry III</a> assassinated <a href="/wiki/Henry_I,_Duke_of_Guise" title="Henry I, Duke of Guise">Henry de Guise</a>, leader of the Spanish-backed <a href="/wiki/Catholic_League_(French)" title="Catholic League (French)">Catholic league</a>, and the king was murdered in return. After the assassination of both Henry of Guise (1588) and Henry III (1589), the conflict was ended by the accession of the Protestant king of <a href="/wiki/Navarre" title="Navarre">Navarre</a> as <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_France" title="Henry IV of France">Henry IV</a> (first king of the <a href="/wiki/Bourbon_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Bourbon dynasty">Bourbon dynasty</a>) and his subsequent abandonment of Protestantism (Expedient of 1592) effective in 1593, his acceptance by most of the Catholic establishment (1594) and by the Pope (1595), and his issue of the toleration decree known as the <a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Nantes" title="Edict of Nantes">Edict of Nantes</a> (1598), which guaranteed freedom of private worship and civil equality.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Modern_period">Early Modern period</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Early Modern period">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_France" title="Early modern France">Early modern France</a></div>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonial_France">Colonial France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Colonial France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a></div>
<p>France's pacification under <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_France" title="Henry IV of France">Henry IV</a> laid much of the ground for the beginnings of France's rise to European hegemony. France was expansive during all but the end of the seventeenth century: the French began trading in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, founded <a href="/wiki/Quebec_City" title="Quebec City">Quebec</a> and penetrated the North American <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">Great Lakes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a>, established plantation economies in the <a href="/wiki/West_Indies" title="West Indies">West Indies</a> and extended their trade contacts in the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a> and enlarged their <a href="/wiki/Merchant_marine" class="mw-redirect" title="Merchant marine">merchant marine</a>.
</p>
<h4><span id="Thirty_Years.27_War"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Thirty_Years'_War">Thirty Years' War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Thirty Years&#039; War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Henry IV's son <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIII_of_France" title="Louis XIII of France">Louis XIII</a> and his minister (1624–1642) <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Richelieu" title="Cardinal Richelieu">Cardinal Richelieu</a>, elaborated a policy against Spain and the Holy Roman Empire during the <a href="/wiki/Thirty_Years%27_War" title="Thirty Years&#39; War">Thirty Years' War</a> (1618–48) which had broken out in Germany. After the death of both king and cardinal, the <a href="/wiki/Peace_of_Westphalia" title="Peace of Westphalia">Peace of Westphalia</a> (1648) secured universal acceptance of Germany's political and religious fragmentation, but the Regency of <a href="/wiki/Anne_of_Austria" title="Anne of Austria">Anne of Austria</a> and her minister <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Mazarin" title="Cardinal Mazarin">Cardinal Mazarin</a> experienced a civil uprising known as the <a href="/wiki/Fronde" title="Fronde">Fronde</a> (1648–1653) which expanded into a <a href="/wiki/Fronde" title="Fronde">Franco-Spanish War</a> (1653–59). The <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_the_Pyrenees" title="Treaty of the Pyrenees">Treaty of the Pyrenees</a> (1659) formalised France's seizure (1642) of the Spanish territory of <a href="/wiki/Roussillon" title="Roussillon">Roussillon</a> after the crushing of the ephemeral <a href="/wiki/Catalan_Republic_(1641)" title="Catalan Republic (1641)">Catalan Republic</a> and ushered a short period of peace.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Administrative_structures">Administrative structures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Administrative structures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ancien_R%C3%A9gime" title="Ancien Régime">Ancien Régime</a></div>
<p>The <i>Ancien Régime</i>, a French term rendered in English as "Old Rule", or simply "Former Regime", refers primarily to the aristocratic, social and political system of early modern France under the <a href="/wiki/Valois_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Valois dynasty">late Valois</a> and <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">Bourbon</a> dynasties.  The administrative and social structures of the Ancien Régime were the result of years of state-building, legislative acts (like the <a href="/wiki/Ordinance_of_Villers-Cotter%C3%AAts" title="Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts">Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts</a>), internal conflicts and civil wars, but they remained a confusing patchwork of local <a href="/wiki/Privilege_(legal_ethics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Privilege (legal ethics)">privilege</a> and historic differences until the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> brought about a radical suppression of administrative incoherence.
</p>
<h4><span id="Louis_XIV.2C_the_Sun_King"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Louis_XIV,_the_Sun_King">Louis XIV, the Sun King</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Louis XIV, the Sun King">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV of France</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg/150px-Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="213" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg/225px-Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg/300px-Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1390" data-file-height="1975" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Louis_XIV_of_France.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV</a>, by <a href="/wiki/Hyacinthe_Rigaud" title="Hyacinthe Rigaud">Hyacinthe Rigaud</a>, 1701</div></div></div>
<p>For most of the reign of <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">Louis XIV</a> (1643–1715), ("The Sun King"), France was the dominant power in Europe, aided by the diplomacy of Cardinal Richelieu's successor as the King's chief minister, (1642–61) <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Jules_Mazarin" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardinal Jules Mazarin">Cardinal Jules Mazarin</a>, (1602–61). Cardinal Mazarin oversaw the creation of a French Royal Navy that rivalled <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">England's</a>, expanding it from 25 ships to almost 200. The size of the Army was also considerably increased. Renewed wars (the <a href="/wiki/War_of_Devolution" title="War of Devolution">War of Devolution</a>, 1667–68 and the <a href="/wiki/Franco-Dutch_War" title="Franco-Dutch War">Franco-Dutch War</a>, 1672–78) brought further territorial gains (<a href="/wiki/Artois" title="Artois">Artois</a> and western <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a> and the free <a href="/wiki/County_of_Burgundy" title="County of Burgundy">county of Burgundy</a>, previously left to the Empire in 1482), but at the cost of the increasingly concerted opposition of rival royal powers, and a legacy of an increasingly enormous national debt. An adherent of the theory of the <a href="/wiki/Divine_right_of_kings" title="Divine right of kings">"Divine Right of Kings"</a>, which advocates the divine origin of temporal power and any lack of earthly restraint of monarchical rule, Louis XIV continued his predecessors' work of creating a <a href="/wiki/Centralized_government" title="Centralized government">centralized state</a> governed from the capital of Paris. He sought to eliminate the remnants of <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudalism</a> still persisting in parts of France and, by compelling the noble elite to regularly inhabit his lavish <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Versailles" title="Palace of Versailles">Palace of Versailles</a>, built on the outskirts of Paris, succeeded in pacifying the aristocracy, many members of which had participated in the earlier "<a href="/wiki/Fronde" title="Fronde">Fronde</a>" rebellion during Louis' minority youth. By these means he consolidated a system of absolute monarchy in France that endured 150 years until the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  McCabe says critics used fiction to portray the degraded Turkish Court, using "the harem, the Sultan court, oriental despotism, luxury, gems and spices, carpets, and silk cushions" as an unfavorable analogy to the corruption of the French royal court.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The king sought to impose total religious uniformity on the country, repealing the "<a href="/wiki/Edict_of_Nantes" title="Edict of Nantes">Edict of Nantes</a>" in 1685. The infamous practice of "<a href="/wiki/Dragonnades" title="Dragonnades">dragonnades</a>" was adopted, whereby intentionally rough soldiers were quartered in the homes of Protestant families and allowed to have their way with them — stealing, raping, torturing and killing adults and infants in their hovels. It is estimated that anywhere between 150,000 and 300,000 Protestants fled France during the wave of persecution that followed the repeal,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> (following "<a href="/wiki/Huguenots" title="Huguenots">Huguenots</a>" beginning a hundred and fifty years earlier until the end of the 18th century) costing the country a great many intellectuals, artisans, and other valuable people. Persecution extended to unorthodox Roman Catholics like the <a href="/wiki/Jansenists" class="mw-redirect" title="Jansenists">Jansenists</a>, a group that denied free will and had already been condemned by the popes. Louis was no theologian and understood little of the complex doctrines of Jansenism, satisfying himself with the fact that they threatened the unity of the state. In this, he garnered the friendship of the papacy, which had previously been hostile to France because of its policy of putting all church property in the country under the jurisdiction of the state rather than that of Rome.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In November 1700, the Spanish king <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_Spain" title="Charles II of Spain">Charles II</a> died, ending the Habsburg line in that country. Louis had long waited for this moment, and now planned to put a Bourbon relative, <a href="/wiki/Philip,_Duke_of_Anjou" class="mw-redirect" title="Philip, Duke of Anjou">Philip, Duke of Anjou</a>, (1683–1746), on the throne. Essentially, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Spain">Spain</a> was to become a perpetual ally and even obedient satellite of France, ruled by a king who would carry out orders from Versailles. Realizing how this would upset the balance of power, the other European rulers were outraged. However, most of the alternatives were equally undesirable. For example, putting another Habsburg on the throne would end up recreating the grand multi-national empire of <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">Charles V</a> (1500–58), of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> (German First Reich), <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Spain">Spain</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Two_Sicilies" title="Kingdom of the Two Sicilies">Two Sicilies</a> which would also grossly upset the power balance. After nine years of exhausting war, the last thing Louis wanted was another conflict. However, the rest of Europe would not stand for his ambitions in Spain, and so the long <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Spanish_Succession" title="War of the Spanish Succession">War of the Spanish Succession</a> began (1701–14), a mere three years after the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Grand_Alliance" class="mw-redirect" title="War of the Grand Alliance">War of the Grand Alliance</a>, (1688–97, aka "War of the League of Augsburg") had just concluded.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Dissent_and_revolution">Dissent and revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Dissent and revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:France_anciennes_provinces_1789.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/France_anciennes_provinces_1789.jpg/250px-France_anciennes_provinces_1789.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/France_anciennes_provinces_1789.jpg/375px-France_anciennes_provinces_1789.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/France_anciennes_provinces_1789.jpg/500px-France_anciennes_provinces_1789.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1147" data-file-height="916" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:France_anciennes_provinces_1789.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Provinces in 1789</div></div></div>
<p>The reign (1715–74) of <a href="/wiki/Louis_XV_of_France" title="Louis XV of France">Louis XV</a> saw an initial return to peace and prosperity under the regency (1715–23) of <a href="/wiki/Philippe_II,_Duke_of_Orl%C3%A9ans" title="Philippe II, Duke of Orléans">Philippe II, Duke of Orléans</a>, whose policies were largely continued (1726–1743) by <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_Fleury" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardinal Fleury">Cardinal Fleury</a>, prime minister in all but name. The exhaustion of Europe after two major wars resulted in a long period of peace, only interrupted by minor conflicts like the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Polish_Succession" title="War of the Polish Succession">War of the Polish Succession</a> from 1733–35. Large-scale warfare resumed with the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Austrian_Succession" title="War of the Austrian Succession">War of the Austrian Succession</a> (1740–48). But alliance with the traditional Habsburg enemy (the "<a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_Revolution" title="Diplomatic Revolution">Diplomatic Revolution</a>" of 1756) against the rising power of Britain and <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a> led to costly failure in the <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years&#39; War">Seven Years' War</a> (1756–63) and the loss of France's North American colonies.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>On the whole, the 18th century saw growing discontent with the monarchy and the established order. Louis XV was a highly unpopular king for his sexual excesses, overall weakness, and for losing Canada to the British. A strong ruler like Louis XIV could enhance the position of the monarchy, while Louis XV weakened it. The writings of the <a href="/wiki/Philosophes" title="Philosophes">philosophes</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Voltaire" title="Voltaire">Voltaire</a> were a clear sign of discontent, but the king chose to ignore them. He died of <a href="/wiki/Smallpox" title="Smallpox">smallpox</a> in 1774, and the French people shed few tears at his passing. While France had not yet experienced the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> that was beginning in Britain, the rising middle class of the cities felt increasingly frustrated with a system and rulers that seemed silly, frivolous, aloof, and antiquated, even if true feudalism no longer existed in France.
</p><p>Upon Louis XV's death, his grandson <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVI_of_France" title="Louis XVI of France">Louis XVI</a> became king. Initially popular, he too came to be widely detested by the 1780s. He was married to an Austrian archduchess, <a href="/wiki/Marie_Antoinette" title="Marie Antoinette">Marie Antoinette</a>. French intervention in the American War of Independence was also very expensive.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With the country deeply in debt, Louis XVI permitted the radical reforms of <a href="/wiki/Anne_Robert_Jacques_Turgot,_Baron_de_Laune" class="mw-redirect" title="Anne Robert Jacques Turgot, Baron de Laune">Turgot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guillaume-Chr%C3%A9tien_de_Lamoignon_de_Malesherbes" title="Guillaume-Chrétien de Lamoignon de Malesherbes">Malesherbes</a>, but noble disaffection led to Turgot's dismissal and Malesherbes' resignation in 1776. They were replaced by <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Necker" title="Jacques Necker">Jacques Necker</a>. Necker had resigned in 1781 to be replaced by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Alexandre_de_Calonne" title="Charles Alexandre de Calonne">Calonne</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C3%89tienne_Charles_de_Lom%C3%A9nie_de_Brienne" title="Étienne Charles de Loménie de Brienne">Brienne</a>, before being restored in 1788. A harsh winter that year led to widespread food shortages, and by then France was a powder keg ready to explode.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
On the eve of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> of July 1789, France was in a profound institutional and financial crisis, but the ideas of the <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">Enlightenment</a> had begun to permeate the educated classes of society.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Limited_monarchy">Limited monarchy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Limited monarchy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On September 3, 1791, the absolute monarchy which had governed France for 948 years was forced to limit its power and become a provisional constitutional monarchy. However, this too would not last very long and on September 21, 1792 the French monarchy was effectively abolished by the proclamation of the <a href="/wiki/French_First_Republic" title="French First Republic">French First Republic</a>. The role of the King in France was finally ended with the execution of <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVI" class="mw-redirect" title="Louis XVI">Louis XVI</a> by <a href="/wiki/Guillotine" title="Guillotine">guillotine</a> on Monday, January 21, 1793, followed by the "<a href="/wiki/Reign_of_Terror" title="Reign of Terror">Reign of Terror</a>", mass executions and the provisional "<a href="/wiki/French_Directory" title="French Directory">Directory</a>" form of <a href="/wiki/French_First_Republic" title="French First Republic">republican government</a>, and the eventual beginnings of twenty-five years of reform, upheaval, dictatorship, wars and renewal, with the various <a href="/wiki/Napoleonic_Wars" title="Napoleonic Wars">Napoleonic Wars</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Restoration">Restoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Restoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The monarchy was briefly restored following the successive events of the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">French Revolution</a> (1789–99), and the <a href="/wiki/First_French_Empire" title="First French Empire">First French Empire under Napoleon</a> (1804–1814/15)&#160;– when a <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Sixth_Coalition" title="War of the Sixth Coalition">coalition of European powers</a> restored by arms the monarchy to the heirs of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Bourbon" title="House of Bourbon">House of Bourbon</a> in 1814. However the deposed Emperor <a href="/wiki/Napoleon_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Napoleon I">Napoleon I</a> returned triumphantly to Paris from his exile in <a href="/wiki/Elba" title="Elba">Elba</a> and ruled France for a short period known as the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Days" title="Hundred Days">Hundred Days</a>.
</p><p>When a <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Seventh_Coalition" class="mw-redirect" title="War of the Seventh Coalition">Seventh European Coalition</a> deposed Napoleon after the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Waterloo" title="Battle of Waterloo">Battle of Waterloo</a> in 1815, the Bourbon monarchy was once again restored. 
The <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Provence" class="mw-redirect" title="Count of Provence">Count of Provence</a>, brother of Louis XVI, who was guillotined in 1793, was crowned as <a href="/wiki/Louis_XVIII_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Louis XVIII of France">Louis XVIII</a>, nicknamed "The Desired". Louis XVIII tried to conciliate the legacies of the Revolution and Ancien Régime both, also permitting the formation of a <a href="/wiki/French_Parliament" title="French Parliament">Parliament</a> and <a href="/wiki/Charter_of_1814" title="Charter of 1814">constitutional Charter</a>, usually "<i>Charte octroyée</i>" ("Granted Charter"). His reign was characterized by disagreements between the <a href="/wiki/Doctrinaires" title="Doctrinaires">Doctrinaires</a>, liberal thinkers who supported the Charter and rising <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">bourgeoisie</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Ultra-royalist" title="Ultra-royalist">Ultra-royalists</a>, aristocrats and clergymen who totally refused the Revolution's heritage. However, peace was maintained by statesmen like <a href="/wiki/Charles_Maurice_de_Talleyrand-P%C3%A9rigord" title="Charles Maurice de Talleyrand-Périgord">Talleyrand</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Armand-Emmanuel_de_Vignerot_du_Plessis,_Duc_de_Richelieu" class="mw-redirect" title="Armand-Emmanuel de Vignerot du Plessis, Duc de Richelieu">Duke of Richelieu</a>, as well as the King's moderation and prudent intervention.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In 1823, the liberal agitations in Spain led to a <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Thousand_Sons_of_Saint_Louis" title="Hundred Thousand Sons of Saint Louis">French intervention</a> on the royalist's side, who permitted to King <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_VII_of_Spain" title="Ferdinand VII of Spain">Ferdinand VII</a> to reject the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Constitution_of_1812" title="Spanish Constitution of 1812">Constitution</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (May 2018)">clarification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>However, the work of Louis XVIII was frustrated when, after his death in 16 September 1824, his brother the <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Artois" title="Count of Artois">Count of Artois</a> became King under the name of <a href="/wiki/Charles_X_of_France" title="Charles X of France">Charles X</a>. Charles X was a strong <a href="/wiki/Reactionary" title="Reactionary">reactionary</a> who supported ultra-royalists and <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church_in_France" title="Catholic Church in France">Catholic Church</a>. Under his reign, the censorship of newspapers was reinforced, the <a href="/wiki/Anti-Sacrilege_Act" title="Anti-Sacrilege Act">Anti-Sacrilege Act</a> passed and compensations to <a href="/wiki/French_emigration_(1789%E2%80%931815)" title="French emigration (1789–1815)">French Émigré</a> were increased. However, there were also the intervention in the <a href="/wiki/Greek_War_of_Independence" title="Greek War of Independence">Greek Revolution</a> and the first phase of the <a href="/wiki/French_conquest_of_Algeria" title="French conquest of Algeria">conquest of Algeria</a>.
</p><p>The absolutist tendencies of the King were disliked by Doctrinaire majority in the <a href="/wiki/Chamber_of_Deputies_(France)" title="Chamber of Deputies (France)">Chamber of Deputies</a>, that in 18 March 1830 <a href="/wiki/Address_of_the_221" title="Address of the 221">sent and cordial address</a> to the King, remembering to him his vote on the Charter. However, Charles X received this address as a veiled threat, and in 25 July of the same year, he emanated the <a href="/wiki/July_Ordinances" title="July Ordinances">St. Cloud Ordinances</a>, a plot to reduce Parliament powers and re-establish absolute rule.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The opposition reacted with riots in Parliament and <a href="/wiki/Barricades" class="mw-redirect" title="Barricades">barricades</a> in Paris, that erupteds in the <a href="/wiki/July_Revolution" title="July Revolution">July Revolution</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The King abdicated, such as his son the <a href="/wiki/Louis_Antoine,_Duke_of_Angoul%C3%AAme" title="Louis Antoine, Duke of Angoulême">Prince Louis Antoine</a>, in favour to his grandson <a href="/wiki/Henri,_Count_of_Chambord" title="Henri, Count of Chambord">Count of Chambord</a>, nominating his cousin the <a href="/wiki/Louis_Philippe_d%27Orl%C3%A9ans" class="mw-redirect" title="Louis Philippe d&#39;Orléans">Duke of Orléans</a> as regent.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> However, it was too late, and the liberal opposition won over the monarchy.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Aftermath_and_July_Monarchy">Aftermath and July Monarchy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Aftermath and July Monarchy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/July_Monarchy" title="July Monarchy">July Monarchy</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1841_portrait_painting_of_Louis_Philippe_I_(King_of_the_French)_by_Winterhalter.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/1841_portrait_painting_of_Louis_Philippe_I_%28King_of_the_French%29_by_Winterhalter.jpg/150px-1841_portrait_painting_of_Louis_Philippe_I_%28King_of_the_French%29_by_Winterhalter.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="232" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/1841_portrait_painting_of_Louis_Philippe_I_%28King_of_the_French%29_by_Winterhalter.jpg/225px-1841_portrait_painting_of_Louis_Philippe_I_%28King_of_the_French%29_by_Winterhalter.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/1841_portrait_painting_of_Louis_Philippe_I_%28King_of_the_French%29_by_Winterhalter.jpg/300px-1841_portrait_painting_of_Louis_Philippe_I_%28King_of_the_French%29_by_Winterhalter.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1293" data-file-height="2000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1841_portrait_painting_of_Louis_Philippe_I_(King_of_the_French)_by_Winterhalter.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Louis Philippe I by <a href="/wiki/Franz_Xaver_Winterhalter" title="Franz Xaver Winterhalter">Franz Xaver Winterhalter</a> (1841)</div></div></div>
<p>On 9 August 1830, the Chamber of Deputies elected <a href="/wiki/Louis_Philippe_d%27Orl%C3%A9ans" class="mw-redirect" title="Louis Philippe d&#39;Orléans">Louis Philippe, Duke of Orléans</a> as "King of the French": for the first time since French Revolution, the King reigned on a people and not on a country. The Bourbon <a href="/wiki/White_flag" title="White flag">white flag</a> was substituted with the <a href="/wiki/French_tricolour" class="mw-redirect" title="French tricolour">French tricolour</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and a <a href="/wiki/Charter_of_1830" title="Charter of 1830">new Charter</a> was introduced in August 1830.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
The <a href="/wiki/French_conquest_of_Algeria" title="French conquest of Algeria">conquest of Algeria</a> continued, and new settlements were established in the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Guinea" title="Gulf of Guinea">Gulf of Guinea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">Gabon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Merina_Kingdom" title="Merina Kingdom">Madagascar</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mayotte" title="Mayotte">Mayotte</a> and also put <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Tahiti" title="Kingdom of Tahiti">Tahite</a> under <a href="/wiki/Protectorate" title="Protectorate">protectorate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, despite the initial reforms, Louis Philippe was little different from his predecessors. The old <a href="/wiki/French_nobility" title="French nobility">nobility</a> was replaced by urban bourgeoisie, and the <a href="/wiki/Working_class" title="Working class">working class</a> was excluded from voting.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Louis Philippe appointed notable bourgeois as <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_France" title="Prime Minister of France">Prime Minister</a>, like banker <a href="/wiki/Casimir_P%C3%A9rier" class="mw-redirect" title="Casimir Périer">Casimir Périer</a>, academic <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Guizot" title="François Guizot">François Guizot</a>, general <a href="/wiki/Jean-de-Dieu_Soult" title="Jean-de-Dieu Soult">Jean-de-Dieu Soult</a>, and thus obtained the nickname of "Citizen King" (<i>Roi Bourgeois</i>). The July Monarchy was beset by corruption scandals and financial crisis. The opposition of the King was composed of <a href="/wiki/Legitimists" title="Legitimists">Legitimists</a>, supporting the <a href="/wiki/Henri,_Count_of_Chambord" title="Henri, Count of Chambord">Count of Chambord</a>, Bourbon claimant to the Throne, and of <a href="/wiki/Bonapartist" class="mw-redirect" title="Bonapartist">Bonapartists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Republicanism_in_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Republicanism in France">Republicans</a> who fought against royalty and supported the principles of democracy.
The King tried to suppress the opposition with censorship, but when the <i><a href="/wiki/Campagne_des_banquets" title="Campagne des banquets">Campagne des banquets</a></i> ("Banquets' Campaign") was repressed in February 1848,<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> riots and seditions erupted in Paris and later all France, resulting in the <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution_of_1848" title="French Revolution of 1848">February Revolution</a>. The <a href="/wiki/National_Guard_(France)" title="National Guard (France)">National Guard</a> refused to repress the rebellion, resulting in Louis Philippe abdicating and fleeing to <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. On 24 February 1848, the monarchy was abolished and the <a href="/wiki/French_Second_Republic" title="French Second Republic">Second Republic</a> was proclaimed.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Despite later attempts to re-establish the Kingdom in the 1870s, during the <a href="/wiki/French_Third_Republic" title="French Third Republic">Third Republic</a>, the French monarchy has not returned.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Territories_and_provinces">Territories and provinces</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_France&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Territories and provinces">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Before the 13th century, only a small part of what is now France was under control of the Frankish king; in the north there were Viking incursions leading to the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Normandy" title="Duchy of Normandy">Duchy of Normandy</a>; in the west, the <a href="/wiki/Counts_of_Anjou" class="mw-redirect" title="Counts of Anjou">counts of Anjou</a> established themselves as powerful rivals of the king, by the late 11th century ruling over the "<a href="/wiki/Angevin_Empire" title="Angevin Empire">Angevin Empire</a>", which included the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">kingdom of England</a>. 
It was only with <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_France" title="Philip II of France">Philip II of France</a> that the bulk of the territory of Western Francia came under the rule of the Frankish kings, and Philip was consequently the first king to call himself "king of France" (1190). 
The division of France between the Angevin (Plantagenet) kings of England and the Capetian kings of France would lead to the <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years&#39; War">Hundred Years' War</a>, and France would regain control over these territories only by the mid 15th century.
What is now eastern France (Lorraine, Arelat) was not part of Western Francia to begin with and was only incorporated into the kingdom during  the <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_France" title="Early modern France">early modern period</a>.
