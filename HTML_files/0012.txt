	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Record label</h1>

<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p><p>A <b>record label</b>, or <b>record company</b>, is a brand or trademark associated with the marketing of <a href="/wiki/Sound_recording_and_reproduction" title="Sound recording and reproduction">music recordings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Music_videos" class="mw-redirect" title="Music videos">music videos</a>. Sometimes, a record label is also a <a href="/wiki/Music_publisher_(popular_music)" title="Music publisher (popular music)">publishing</a> company that manages such brands and trademarks, coordinates the production, <a href="/wiki/Manufacturing" title="Manufacturing">manufacture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Distribution_(business)" class="mw-redirect" title="Distribution (business)">distribution</a>, marketing, promotion, and enforcement of <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">copyright</a> for sound recordings and music videos, while also conducting talent scouting and development of new artists ("artists and repertoire" or "<a href="/wiki/A%26R" class="mw-redirect" title="A&amp;R">A&amp;R</a>"), and maintaining <a href="/wiki/Recording_contract" title="Recording contract">contracts</a> with recording artists and their managers. The term "record label" derives from the circular label in the center of a vinyl record which prominently displays the manufacturer's name, along with other information.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Within the mainstream <a href="/wiki/Music_industry" title="Music industry">music industry</a>, recording artists have traditionally been reliant upon record labels to broaden their consumer base, market their albums, and be both promoted and heard on music streaming services, radio, and television. Record labels also provide <a href="/wiki/Publicist" title="Publicist">publicists</a>, who assist performers in gaining positive media coverage, and arrange for their merchandise to be available via stores and other media outlets.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_versus_independent_record_labels">Major versus independent record labels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Record_label&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Major versus independent record labels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Record labels may be small, localized and "<a href="/wiki/Independent_record_label" title="Independent record label">independent</a>" ("indie"), or they may be part of a large <a href="/wiki/Multinational_corporation" title="Multinational corporation">international media group</a>, or somewhere in between. The <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Independent_Music" title="Association of Independent Music">Association of Independent Music</a> (AIM) defines a 'major' as "a multinational company which (together with the companies in its group) has more than 5% of the world market(s) for the sale of records or music videos." As of 2012, there are only three labels that can be referred to as "major labels" (<a href="/wiki/Universal_Music_Group" title="Universal Music Group">Universal Music Group</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sony_Music_Entertainment" class="mw-redirect" title="Sony Music Entertainment">Sony Music Entertainment</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Warner_Music_Group" title="Warner Music Group">Warner Music Group</a>). In 2014 AIM estimated that the majors had a collective global market share of some 65–70%.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_labels">Major labels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Record_label&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Major labels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Record labels are often under the control of a corporate umbrella organization called a "music <a href="/wiki/Corporate_group" title="Corporate group">group</a>". A music group is typically owned by an international <a href="/wiki/Conglomerate_(company)" title="Conglomerate (company)">conglomerate</a> "<a href="/wiki/Holding_company" title="Holding company">holding company</a>", which often has non-music divisions as well. A music group controls and consists of music publishing companies, record (sound recording) manufacturers, record distributors, and record labels. Record companies (manufacturers, distributors, and labels) may also constitute a "record group" which is, in turn, controlled by a music group. The constituent companies in a music group or record group are sometimes marketed as being "divisions" of the group.
</p><p><span id="Major_labels_1988–1999_(Big_Six)"></span>From 1988 to 1999, there were six major record labels, known as the Big Six:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/Warner_Music_Group" title="Warner Music Group">Warner Music Group</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sony_Music" title="Sony Music">Sony Music</a> (known as CBS Records until January 1991)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bertelsmann_Music_Group" title="Bertelsmann Music Group">BMG</a> (formed in 1984 as RCA/Ariola International)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Universal_Music_Group" title="Universal Music Group">Universal Music Group</a> (known as MCA Music until 1996)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/PolyGram" title="PolyGram">PolyGram</a></li></ol>
<p><span id="Major_labels_1999–2004_(Big_Five)"></span>PolyGram was merged into UMG in 1999, leaving the rest to be known as the Big Five.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><span id="Major_labels_2004–2012_(Big_Four)"></span>In 2004, Sony and BMG agreed to a joint venture to create the <a href="/wiki/Sony_BMG" title="Sony BMG">Sony BMG</a> label (which would be renamed Sony Music Entertainment after a 2008 merger). In 2007, the four remaining companies—known as the Big Four—controlled about 70% of the <a href="/wiki/World_music_market" class="mw-redirect" title="World music market">world music market</a>, and about 80% of the United States music market.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><span id="Big_Three"></span><span id="Big_3"></span><span id="big_three"></span><span id="3"></span><span id="Major_labels_since_2012"></span><span id="Major_labels_after_collapse_of_EMI"></span><span id="Major_labels_since_2012_(Big_Three)"></span>In 2012, the major divisions of <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a> were sold off separately by owner <a href="/wiki/Citigroup" title="Citigroup">Citigroup</a>: most of EMI's recorded music division was absorbed into UMG; EMI Music Publishing was absorbed into Sony/ATV Music Publishing; finally, EMI's <a href="/wiki/Parlophone" title="Parlophone">Parlophone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Classics" title="Virgin Classics">Virgin Classics</a> labels were absorbed into Warner Music Group in July 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This left the so-called Big Three labels:

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Independent">Independent</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Record_label&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Independent">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Independent_record_label" title="Independent record label">Independent record label</a></div>
<p>Record labels and music publishers that are not under the control of the big three are generally considered to be <i>independent</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Indie_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Indie (music)">indie</a></i>), even if they are large corporations with complex structures. The term <i>indie label</i> is sometimes used to refer to only those independent labels that adhere to independent criteria of corporate structure and size, and some consider an indie label to be almost any label that releases non-mainstream music, regardless of its corporate structure.
</p><p>Independent labels are often considered more artist-friendly. Though they may have less financial clout, indie labels typically offer larger artist royalty with a 50% profit-share agreement, aka 50-50 deal, not uncommon.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In addition, independent labels are often artist-owned (although not always), with a stated intent often being to control the quality of the artist's output. Independent labels usually do not enjoy the resources available to the "big three" and as such will often lag behind them in market shares. However, frequently independent artists manage a return by recording for a much smaller production cost of a typical big label release. Sometimes they are able to recoup their initial advance even with much lower sales numbers.
</p><p>On occasion, established artists, once their record contract has finished, move to an independent label. This often gives the combined advantage of name recognition and more control over one's music along with a larger portion of royalty profits. Artists such as <a href="/wiki/Dolly_Parton" title="Dolly Parton">Dolly Parton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aimee_Mann" title="Aimee Mann">Aimee Mann</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince</a>, <a href="/wiki/Public_Enemy_(band)" title="Public Enemy (band)">Public Enemy</a>, BKBravo (Kua and Rafi), among others, have done this. Historically, companies started in this manner have been re-absorbed into the major labels (two examples are American singer <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sinatra" title="Frank Sinatra">Frank Sinatra</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Reprise_Records" title="Reprise Records">Reprise Records</a>, which has been owned by <a href="/wiki/Warner_Music_Group" title="Warner Music Group">Warner Music Group</a> for some time now, and musician <a href="/wiki/Herb_Alpert" title="Herb Alpert">Herb Alpert</a>'s <a href="/wiki/A%26M_Records" title="A&amp;M Records">A&amp;M Records</a>, now owned by Universal Music Group). Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a>'s Maverick Records (started by Madonna with her manager and another partner) was to come under control of Warner Music when Madonna <a href="/wiki/Divest" class="mw-redirect" title="Divest">divested</a> herself of controlling shares in the company.
</p><p>Some independent labels become successful enough that major record companies negotiate contracts to either distribute music for the label or in some cases, purchase the label completely.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Imprint">Imprint</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Record_label&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Imprint">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A label used as a trademark or brand and not a company is called an <a href="/wiki/Imprint_(trade_name)" title="Imprint (trade name)">imprint</a>, a term used for the same concept in <a href="/wiki/Publishing" title="Publishing">publishing</a>. An imprint is sometimes marketed as being a "project", "unit", or "division" of a record label company, even though there is no legal business structure associated with the imprint.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sublabel">Sublabel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Record_label&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sublabel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Music collectors often use the term <i>sublabel</i> to refer to either an imprint or a subordinate label company (such as those within a group). For example, in the 1980s and 1990s, "4th &amp; B'way" was a trademarked brand owned by <a href="/wiki/Island_Records" title="Island Records">Island Records</a> Ltd. in the UK and by a subordinate branch, Island Records, Inc., in the United States. The center label on a 4th &amp; Broadway record marketed in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> would typically bear a 4th &amp; B'way logo and would state in the fine print, "4th &amp; B'way™, an Island Records, Inc. company". Collectors discussing labels as brands would say that 4th &amp; B'way is a sublabel or imprint of just "Island" or "Island Records". Similarly, collectors who choose to treat corporations and trademarks as equivalent might say 4th &amp; B'way is an imprint and/or sublabel of both Island Records, Ltd. and that company's sublabel, Island Records, Inc. However, such definitions are complicated by the corporate <a href="/wiki/Mergers_and_acquisitions" title="Mergers and acquisitions">mergers</a> that occurred in 1989 (when Island was sold to PolyGram) and 1998 (when PolyGram merged with Universal). Island remained registered as corporations in both the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>, but control of its brands changed hands multiple times as new companies were formed, diminishing the corporation's distinction as the "parent" of any sublabels.  <a href="/w/index.php?title=My_Ami&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="My Ami (page does not exist)">My Ami</a> is the early imprint of Columbia records.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vanity_labels">Vanity labels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Record_label&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vanity labels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Vanity_label" title="Vanity label">Vanity label</a></div>
<p>Vanity labels are labels that bear an imprint that gives the impression of an artist's ownership or control, but in fact represent a standard artist/label relationship. In such an arrangement, the artist will control nothing more than the usage of the name on the label, but may enjoy a greater say in the packaging of his or her work. An example of such a label is the Neutron label owned by <a href="/wiki/ABC_(band)" title="ABC (band)">ABC</a> while at <a href="/wiki/Phonogram_Inc." title="Phonogram Inc.">Phonogram Inc.</a> in the UK. At one point artist Lizzie Tear (under contract with ABC themselves) appeared on the imprint, but it was devoted almost entirely to ABC's offerings and is still used for their re-releases (though Phonogram owns the masters of all the work issued on the label).
</p><p>However, not all labels dedicated to particular artists are completely superficial in origin. Many artists, early in their careers, create their own labels which are later bought out by a bigger company. If this is the case it can sometimes give the artist greater freedom than if they were signed directly to the big label. There are many examples of this kind of label, such as <a href="/wiki/Nothing_Records" title="Nothing Records">Nothing Records</a>, owned by <a href="/wiki/Trent_Reznor" title="Trent Reznor">Trent Reznor</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nine_Inch_Nails" title="Nine Inch Nails">Nine Inch Nails</a>; and Morning Records, owned by the <a href="/wiki/Cooper_Temple_Clause" class="mw-redirect" title="Cooper Temple Clause">Cooper Temple Clause</a>, who were releasing EPs for years before the company was bought by <a href="/wiki/RCA" title="RCA">RCA</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_with_artists">Relationship with artists</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Record_label&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Relationship with artists">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A label typically enters into an exclusive <a href="/wiki/Recording_contract" title="Recording contract">recording contract</a> with an artist to market the artist's recordings in return for <a href="/wiki/Music_royalties" class="mw-redirect" title="Music royalties">royalties</a> on the selling price of the recordings. Contracts may extend over short or long durations, and may or may not refer to specific recordings. Established, successful artists tend to be able to renegotiate their contracts to get terms more favorable to them, but <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince</a>'s much-publicized 1994–1996 feud with <a href="/wiki/Warner_Bros._Records" class="mw-redirect" title="Warner Bros. Records">Warner Bros. Records</a> provides a strong counterexample,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> as does <a href="/wiki/Roger_McGuinn" title="Roger McGuinn">Roger McGuinn</a>'s claim, made in July 2000 before a US Senate committee, that <a href="/wiki/The_Byrds" title="The Byrds">the Byrds</a> never received any of the royalties they had been promised for their biggest hits, "<a href="/wiki/Mr._Tambourine_Man" title="Mr. Tambourine Man">Mr.&#160;Tambourine Man</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Turn!_Turn!_Turn!_(to_Everything_There_Is_a_Season)" class="mw-redirect" title="Turn! Turn! Turn! (to Everything There Is a Season)">Turn! Turn!, Turn!</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A contract either provides for the artist to deliver completed recordings to the label, or for the label to undertake the recording with the artist. For artists without a recording history, the label is often involved in selecting producers, <a href="/wiki/Recording_studio" title="Recording studio">recording studios</a>, additional musicians, and songs to be recorded, and may supervise the output of recording sessions. For established artists, a label is usually less involved in the recording process.
</p><p>The relationship between record labels and artists can be a difficult one. Many artists have had albums altered or censored in some way by the labels before they are released—songs being edited, artwork or titles being changed, etc.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>  Record labels generally do this because they believe that the album will sell better if the changes are made.  Often the record label's decisions are prudent ones from a commercial perspective, but these decisions may frustrate artists who feel that their art is being diminished or misrepresented by such actions.
</p><p>In the early days of the recording industry, recording labels were absolutely necessary for the success of any artist.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The first goal of any new artist or band was to get signed to a contract as soon as possible. In the 1940s, 1950s, and 1960s, many artists were so desperate to sign a contract with a record company that they sometimes ended up signing agreements in which they sold the rights to their recordings to the record label in perpetuity. <a href="/wiki/Entertainment_law" title="Entertainment law">Entertainment lawyers</a> are usually employed by artists to discuss contract terms.
</p><p>Through the advances of the Internet the role of labels is becoming increasingly changed, as artists are able to freely distribute their own material through web radio, peer to peer file sharing such as <a href="/wiki/BitTorrent" title="BitTorrent">BitTorrent</a>, and other services, for little or no cost but with little financial return. Established artists, such as Nine Inch Nails, whose career was developed with major label backing, announced an end to their major label contracts, citing that the uncooperative nature of the recording industry with these new trends is hurting musicians, fans and the industry as a whole.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> However, Nine Inch Nails later returned to working with a major label,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> admitting that they needed the international marketing and promotional reach that a major label can provide. <a href="/wiki/Radiohead" title="Radiohead">Radiohead</a> also cited similar motives with the end of their contract with <a href="/wiki/EMI" title="EMI">EMI</a> when their album <i><a href="/wiki/In_Rainbows" title="In Rainbows">In Rainbows</a></i> was released as a "<a href="/wiki/Pay_what_you_want" title="Pay what you want">pay what you want</a>" sales model as an online download, but they also returned to a label for a conventional release.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Research shows that record labels still control most access to distribution.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_label_strategies">New label strategies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Record_label&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: New label strategies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With the advancement of the computer and technology such as the Internet, leading to an increase in file sharing and <a href="/wiki/Direct-to-Fan" class="mw-redirect" title="Direct-to-Fan">direct-to-fan</a> digital distribution, combined with music sales plummeting in recent years,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> labels and organizations have had to change their strategies and the way they work with artists. New types of deals are being made with artists called "multiple rights" or "360" deals with artists. These types of pacts give labels rights and percentages to artist's touring, merchandising, and <a href="/wiki/Testimonial" title="Testimonial">endorsements</a>. In exchange for these rights, labels usually give higher advance payments to artists, have more patience with artist development, and pay higher percentages of CD sales. These 360 deals are most effective when the artist is established and has a loyal fan base. For that reason, labels now have to be more relaxed with the development of artists because longevity is the key to these types of pacts. Several artists such as <a href="/wiki/Paramore" title="Paramore">Paramore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maino" title="Maino">Maino</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Madonna_(entertainer)" title="Madonna (entertainer)">Madonna</a> have signed such types of deals.
</p><p>A look at an actual 360 deal offered by <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Records" title="Atlantic Records">Atlantic Records</a> to an artist shows a variation of the structure. Atlantic's document offers a conventional cash advance to sign the artist, who would receive a royalty for sales after expenses were recouped. With the release of the artist's first album, however, the label has an option to pay an additional $200,000 in exchange for 30 percent of the net income from all touring, merchandise, endorsements, and fan-club fees. Atlantic would also have the right to approve the act's tour schedule, and the salaries of certain tour and merchandise sales employees hired by the artist. In addition, the label also offers the artist a 30 percent cut of the label's album profits—if any—which represents an improvement from the typical industry royalty of 15 percent.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internet_and_digital_labels">Internet and digital labels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Record_label&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Internet and digital labels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Netlabel" title="Netlabel">Netlabel</a></div>
<p>With the Internet now being a viable source for obtaining music, <a href="/wiki/Netlabel" title="Netlabel">netlabels</a> have emerged. Depending on the ideals of the net label, music files from the artists may be downloaded free of charge or for a fee that is paid via <a href="/wiki/PayPal" title="PayPal">PayPal</a> or other online payment system. Some of these labels also offer hard copy CDs in addition to direct download.  Digital Labels are the latest version of a 'net' label. Whereas 'net' labels were started as a free site, digital labels represent more competition for the major record labels.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Open-source_labels">Open-source labels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Record_label&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Open-source labels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Open-source_record_label" title="Open-source record label">Open-source record label</a></div>
<p>The new century brought the phenomenon of <i>open-source</i> or <i>open-content</i> record labels. These are inspired by the <a href="/wiki/Free_software" title="Free software">free software</a> and <a href="/wiki/Open-source_model" title="Open-source model">open source</a> movements and the success of <a href="/wiki/Linux" title="Linux">GNU/Linux</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Publishers_as_labels">Publishers as labels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Record_label&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Publishers as labels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the mid-2000s, some music publishing companies began undertaking the work traditionally done by labels. The publisher Sony/ATV Music, for example, leveraged its connections within the Sony family to produce, record, distribute, and promote <a href="/wiki/Elliott_Yamin" title="Elliott Yamin">Elliott Yamin</a>'s debut album under <a href="/wiki/Hickory_Records" title="Hickory Records">a dormant Sony-owned imprint</a>, rather than waiting for a deal with a proper label.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
