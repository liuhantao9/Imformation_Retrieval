<p><b>Naval Hospital Boston</b> was a hospital in <a href="/wiki/Chelsea,_Massachusetts" title="Chelsea, Massachusetts">Chelsea, Massachusetts</a>. With the closure of the nearby <a href="/wiki/Boston_Navy_Yard" title="Boston Navy Yard">Boston Navy Yard</a>, the hospital closed in 1974.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Naval_Hospital_Boston&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On January 7, 1836 the Chelsea Naval Hospital was completed and commissioned. Located on a hill on the banks of the <a href="/wiki/Mystic_River" title="Mystic River">Mystic River</a> in Chelsea, MA, it is 112 feet (34&#160;m) above sea level. The original building was built of Vermont granite. The hospital was a three-story building with a 100-bed capacity. A wing was added on the west side of the building in 1865.<sup id="cite_ref-NPS_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPS-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chelsea Naval Hospital was one of the first three hospitals authorized by Congress to accommodate naval personnel. Previously, personnel received treatment at marine hospitals operated by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Department of the Treasury">Department of the Treasury</a> for mariners, both naval and merchant. The hospital served naval personnel and others during the <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">American Civil War</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">Spanish–American War</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NPS_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPS-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Boston_Naval_Hospital_Chelsea_MA_01.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Boston_Naval_Hospital_Chelsea_MA_01.jpg/200px-Boston_Naval_Hospital_Chelsea_MA_01.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="121" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Boston_Naval_Hospital_Chelsea_MA_01.jpg/300px-Boston_Naval_Hospital_Chelsea_MA_01.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Boston_Naval_Hospital_Chelsea_MA_01.jpg/400px-Boston_Naval_Hospital_Chelsea_MA_01.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1220" data-file-height="738" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Boston_Naval_Hospital_Chelsea_MA_01.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chelsea Naval Hospital building</div></div></div>
<p>In 1970, a plaque in remembrance of <a href="/wiki/Medal_of_Honor" title="Medal of Honor">Medal of Honor</a> recipient <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Maurice_Caron" title="Wayne Maurice Caron">Wayne Maurice Caron</a>, a hospital corpsman, was placed on the grounds of the hospital.<sup id="cite_ref-NPS_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPS-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 1973, the hospital and the surrounding grounds were added to the <a href="/wiki/Naval_Hospital_Boston_Historic_District" title="Naval Hospital Boston Historic District">Naval Hospital Boston Historic District</a>.
</p><p>When it was decommissioned in 1974, it was the oldest naval hospital in service in the United States and consisted of 88 acres (360,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) of land on the Mystic River. Notable patients during the hospital's history include Presidents <a href="/wiki/John_Quincy_Adams" title="John Quincy Adams">John Quincy Adams</a> (after his presidency) and <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> (before his presidency).<sup id="cite_ref-NPS_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NPS-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The original hospital buildings were converted into condominiums while adjacent land was dotted with single family townhouses and high rise apartment complexes. Still extant are the perimeter wall and guard shack, pier, chapel, ordnance buildings, nurses' quarters, and the Captain's House. In addition to the redevelopment of the housing and hospital portion of the property, several acres on the Mystic River were taken over by the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Conservation_and_Recreation" title="Department of Conservation and Recreation">Metropolitan District Commission</a> for Mary O'Malley Park.
</p>
