<p><b>Gross domestic products</b> (<b>GDP</b>) is a monetary <a href="/wiki/Measurement_in_economics" title="Measurement in economics">measure</a> of the market value of all the <a href="/wiki/Final_good" title="Final good">final goods</a> and services produced in a specific time period, often annually.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> 
<a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_(nominal)_per_capita" title="List of countries by GDP (nominal) per capita">GDP (nominal) per capita</a> does not, however, reflect differences in the <a href="/wiki/Cost_of_living" title="Cost of living">cost of living</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Inflation" title="Inflation">inflation rates</a> of the countries; therefore using a basis of <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_by_GDP_(PPP)_per_capita" title="List of countries by GDP (PPP) per capita">GDP per capita at purchasing power parity (PPP)</a> is arguably more useful when comparing differences in <a href="/wiki/Standard_of_living" title="Standard of living">living standards</a> between nations.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gross_domestic_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a> defines GDP as "an aggregate measure of production equal to the sum of the <a href="/wiki/Gross_value_added" title="Gross value added">gross values added</a> of all resident and institutional units engaged in production and services (plus any taxes, and minus any subsidies, on products not included in the value of their outputs)."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  An <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">IMF</a> publication states that, "GDP measures the monetary value of final goods and services—that are bought by the final user—produced in a country in a given period of time (say a quarter or a year)."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Total GDP can also be broken down into the contribution of each industry or sector of the economy.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The ratio of GDP to the total population of the region is the <a href="/wiki/GDP_per_capita" class="mw-redirect" title="GDP per capita">per capita GDP</a> and the same is called Mean Standard of Living. GDP is considered the "world's most powerful statistical indicator of national development and progress".<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gross_domestic_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/William_Petty" title="William Petty">William Petty</a> came up with a basic concept of GDP to attack landlords against unfair taxation during <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Dutch_Wars" title="Anglo-Dutch Wars">warfare between the Dutch and the English</a> between 1654 and 1676.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Charles_Davenant" title="Charles Davenant">Charles Davenant</a> developed the method further in 1695.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The modern concept of GDP was first developed by <a href="/wiki/Simon_Kuznets" title="Simon Kuznets">Simon Kuznets</a> for a <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">US Congress</a> report in 1934.<sup id="cite_ref-kuznets_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kuznets-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> In this report, Kuznets warned against its use as a measure of welfare<sup id="cite_ref-CIA_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CIA-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> (see below under <i><a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product#Limitations_and_criticisms" title="Gross domestic product">limitations and criticisms</a></i>). After the <a href="/wiki/Bretton_Woods_conference" class="mw-redirect" title="Bretton Woods conference">Bretton Woods conference</a> in 1944, GDP became the main tool for measuring a country's economy.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> At that time <a href="/wiki/Gross_national_product" class="mw-redirect" title="Gross national product">gross national product</a> (GNP) was the preferred estimate, which differed from GDP in that it measured production by a country's citizens at home and abroad rather than its 'resident institutional units' (see <a href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a> definition above). The switch from GNP to GDP in the US was in 1991, trailing behind most other nations. The role that measurements of GDP played in World War II was crucial to the subsequent political acceptance of GDP values as indicators of national development and progress.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> A crucial role was played here by the <a href="/wiki/US_Department_of_Commerce" class="mw-redirect" title="US Department of Commerce">US Department of Commerce</a> under <a href="/wiki/Milton_Gilbert" title="Milton Gilbert">Milton Gilbert</a> where ideas from Kuznets were embedded into governmental institutions.
</p><p>The history of the concept of GDP should be distinguished from the history of changes in ways of estimating it. The value added by firms is relatively easy to calculate from their accounts, but the value added by the public sector, by financial industries, and by <a href="/wiki/Intangible_asset" title="Intangible asset">intangible asset</a> creation is more complex. These activities are increasingly important in developed economies, and the international conventions governing their estimation and their inclusion or exclusion in GDP regularly change in an attempt to keep up with industrial advances. In the words of one academic economist "The actual number for GDP is therefore the product of a vast patchwork of statistics and a complicated set of processes carried out on the raw data to fit them to the conceptual framework."<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Determining_gross_domestic_product_.28GDP.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Determining_gross_domestic_product_(GDP)">Determining gross domestic product (GDP)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gross_domestic_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Determining gross domestic product (GDP)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:How_is_GDP_calculated%3F.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/How_is_GDP_calculated%3F.png/220px-How_is_GDP_calculated%3F.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="646" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/How_is_GDP_calculated%3F.png/330px-How_is_GDP_calculated%3F.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/How_is_GDP_calculated%3F.png/440px-How_is_GDP_calculated%3F.png 2x" data-file-width="900" data-file-height="2642" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:How_is_GDP_calculated%3F.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An infographic explaining how GDP is calculated in the UK</div></div></div>
<p>GDP can be determined in three ways, all of which should, in principle, give the same result. They are the production (or output or value added) approach, the income approach, or the speculated expenditure approach.
</p><p>The most direct of the three is the production approach, which sums the outputs of every class of enterprise to arrive at the total. The expenditure approach works on the principle that all of the product must be bought by somebody, therefore the value of the total product must be equal to people's total expenditures in buying things. The income approach works on the principle that the incomes of the productive factors ("producers," colloquially) must be equal to the value of their product, and determines GDP by finding the sum of all producers' incomes.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Production_approach">Production approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gross_domestic_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>This approach mirrors the <a href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a> definition given above.
</p>
<ol><li>Estimate the gross value of domestic output out of the many various economic activities;</li>
<li>Determine the <a href="/wiki/Intermediate_consumption" title="Intermediate consumption">intermediate consumption</a>, i.e., the cost of material, supplies and services used to produce final goods or services.</li>
<li>Deduct intermediate consumption from gross value to obtain the gross value added.</li></ol>
<p><a href="/wiki/Gross_value_added" title="Gross value added">Gross value added</a> =                                             gross value of output – value of intermediate consumption.
</p><p>Value of output = value of the total sales of goods and services plus value of changes in the inventory.
</p><p>The sum of the gross value added in the various economic activities is known as "GDP at factor cost".
</p><p>GDP at factor cost plus indirect taxes less subsidies on products = "GDP at producer price".
</p><p>For measuring output of domestic product, economic activities (i.e. industries) are classified into various sectors. After classifying economic activities, the output of each sector is calculated by any of the following two methods:
</p>
<ol><li>By multiplying the output of each sector by their respective market price and adding them together</li>
<li>By collecting data on gross sales and inventories from the records of companies and adding them together</li></ol>
<p>The value of output of all sectors is then added to get the gross value of output at factor cost. Subtracting each sector's intermediate consumption from gross output value gives the GVA (=GDP) at factor cost. Adding indirect tax minus subsidies to GVA (GDP) at factor cost gives the "GVA (GDP) at producer prices".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Income_approach">Income approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gross_domestic_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Income approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The second way of estimating GDP is to use "the sum of primary incomes distributed by resident producer units".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>If GDP is calculated this way it is sometimes called gross domestic income (GDI), or GDP (I). GDI should provide the same amount as the expenditure method described later. By definition, GDI is equal to GDP. In practice, however, measurement errors will make the two figures slightly off when reported by national statistical agencies.
</p><p>This method measures GDP by adding incomes that firms pay households for factors of production they hire - wages for labour, interest for capital, rent for land and profits for entrepreneurship.
</p><p>The US "National Income and Expenditure Accounts" divide incomes into five categories:
</p>
<ol><li>Wages, salaries, and supplementary labour income</li>
<li>Corporate profits</li>
<li>Interest and miscellaneous investment income</li>
<li>Farmers' incomes</li>
<li>Income from non-farm unincorporated businesses</li></ol>
<p>These five income components sum to net domestic income at factor cost.
</p><p>Two adjustments must be made to get GDP:
</p>
<ol><li>Indirect taxes minus subsidies are added to get from factor cost to market prices.</li>
<li>Depreciation (or <a href="/wiki/Capital_consumption_allowance" class="mw-redirect" title="Capital consumption allowance">capital consumption allowance</a>) is added to get from net domestic product to gross domestic product.</li></ol>
<p>Total income can be subdivided according to various schemes, leading to various formulae for GDP measured by the income approach. A common one is:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>GDP = <a href="/wiki/Compensation_of_employees" title="Compensation of employees">compensation of employees</a> + <a href="/wiki/Gross_operating_surplus" title="Gross operating surplus">gross operating surplus</a> + <a href="/wiki/Gross_mixed_income" class="mw-redirect" title="Gross mixed income">gross mixed income</a> + taxes less subsidies on production and imports</i></dd>
<dd><b>GDP</b> = <b>COE</b> + <b>GOS</b> + <b>GMI</b> + <b>T<sub>P &amp; M</sub></b> – <b>S<sub>P &amp; M</sub></b></dd></dl>
<ul><li><b>Compensation of employees</b> (COE) measures the total remuneration to employees for work done. It includes wages and salaries, as well as employer contributions to <a href="/wiki/Social_security" title="Social security">social security</a> and other such programs.</li>
<li><b>Gross operating surplus</b> (GOS) is the surplus due to owners of incorporated businesses. Often called <a href="/wiki/Profit_(accounting)" title="Profit (accounting)">profits</a>, although only a subset of total costs are subtracted from <a href="/wiki/Gross_output" title="Gross output">gross output</a> to calculate GOS.</li>
<li><b>Gross mixed income</b> (GMI) is the same measure as GOS, but for unincorporated businesses. This often includes most small businesses.</li></ul>
<p>The sum of <b>COE</b>, <b>GOS</b> and <b>GMI</b> is called total factor income; it is the income of all of the factors of production in society. It measures the value of GDP at factor (basic) prices. The difference between basic prices and final prices (those used in the expenditure calculation) is the total taxes and subsidies that the government has levied or paid on that production. So adding taxes less subsidies on production and imports converts GDP(I) at <a href="/wiki/Factor_cost" title="Factor cost">factor cost</a> to GDP(I) at final prices.
</p><p>Total factor income is also sometimes expressed as:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>Total factor income = employee compensation + corporate profits + proprietor's income + rental income + net interest</i><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Expenditure_approach">Expenditure approach</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gross_domestic_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Expenditure approach">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The third way to estimate GDP is to calculate the sum of the final uses of goods and services (all uses except intermediate consumption) measured in purchasers' prices.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Market goods which are produced are purchased by someone. In the case where a good is produced and unsold, the standard accounting convention is that the producer has bought the good from themselves. Therefore, measuring the total expenditure used to buy things is a way of measuring production. This is known as the expenditure method of calculating GDP.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Components_of_GDP_by_expenditure">Components of GDP by expenditure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gross_domestic_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Components of GDP by expenditure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;"><a href="/wiki/File:GDP_Categories_-_United_States.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/GDP_Categories_-_United_States.png/230px-GDP_Categories_-_United_States.png" decoding="async" width="230" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/GDP_Categories_-_United_States.png/345px-GDP_Categories_-_United_States.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/GDP_Categories_-_United_States.png/460px-GDP_Categories_-_United_States.png 2x" data-file-width="960" data-file-height="720" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:GDP_Categories_-_United_States.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>U.S. GDP computed on the expenditure basis.</div></div></div>
<p><b>GDP (Y)</b> is the sum of <b>consumption (C)</b>, <b>investment (I)</b>, <b>government spending (G)</b> and <b>net exports (X – M)</b>.
</p>
<dl><dd><b>Y</b> = <b>C</b> + <b>I</b> + <b>G</b> + <b>(X − M)</b></dd></dl>
<p>Here is a description of each GDP component:
</p>
<ul><li><b>C (consumption)</b> is normally the largest GDP component in the economy, consisting of private expenditures in the economy (<a href="/wiki/Household_final_consumption_expenditure" title="Household final consumption expenditure">household final consumption expenditure</a>). These personal expenditures fall under one of the following categories: <a href="/wiki/Durable_good" title="Durable good">durable goods</a>, nondurable goods, and services. Examples include food, rent, jewelry, gasoline, and medical expenses, but not the purchase of new housing.</li>
<li><b>I (investment)</b> includes, for instance, business investment in equipment, but does not include exchanges of existing assets. Examples include construction of a new <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mine</a>, purchase of software, or purchase of machinery and equipment for a factory. Spending by households (not government) on new houses is also included in investment. In contrast to its colloquial meaning, "investment" in GDP does not mean purchases of <a href="/wiki/Financial_market" title="Financial market">financial products</a>. Buying financial products is classed as '<a href="/wiki/Saving" title="Saving">saving</a>', as opposed to investment. This avoids double-counting: if one buys shares in a company, and the company uses the money received to buy plant, equipment, etc., the amount will be counted toward GDP when the company spends the money on those things; to also count it when one gives it to the company would be to count two times an amount that only corresponds to one group of products. Buying <a href="/wiki/Bond_(finance)" title="Bond (finance)">bonds</a> or <a href="/wiki/Stock" title="Stock">stocks</a> is a swapping of <a href="/wiki/Deed" title="Deed">deeds</a>, a transfer of claims on future production, not directly an expenditure on products.</li>
<li><b>G (government spending)</b> is the sum of <a href="/wiki/Government_spending" title="Government spending">government expenditures</a> on <a href="/wiki/Final_good" title="Final good">final goods</a> and services. It includes salaries of <a href="/wiki/Public_servants" class="mw-redirect" title="Public servants">public servants</a>, purchases of weapons for the military and any investment expenditure by a government. It does not include any <a href="/wiki/Transfer_payment" title="Transfer payment">transfer payments</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Social_security" title="Social security">social security</a> or <a href="/wiki/Unemployment_benefits" title="Unemployment benefits">unemployment benefits</a>.</li>
<li><b>X (exports)</b> represents gross exports. GDP captures the amount a country produces, including goods and services produced for other nations' consumption, therefore exports are added.</li>
<li><b>M (imports)</b> represents gross imports. Imports are subtracted since imported goods will be included in the terms <b>G</b>, <b>I</b>, or <b>C</b>, and must be deducted to avoid counting foreign <a href="/wiki/Supply_and_demand" title="Supply and demand">supply</a> as domestic.</li></ul>
<p>Note that <b>C</b>, <b>G</b>, and <b>I</b> are expenditures on <a href="/wiki/Final_good" title="Final good">final goods</a> and services; expenditures on intermediate goods and services do not count. (Intermediate goods and services are those used by businesses to produce other goods and services within the accounting year.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>)
</p><p>According to the U.S. Bureau of Economic Analysis, which is responsible for calculating the national accounts in the United States, "In general, the source data for the expenditures components are considered more reliable than those for the income components [see income method, below]."<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="GDP_vs_GNI">GDP vs GNI</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gross_domestic_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: GDP vs GNI">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>GDP can be contrasted with <a href="/wiki/Gross_national_product" class="mw-redirect" title="Gross national product">gross national product</a> (GNP) or, as it is now known, <a href="/wiki/Gross_National_Income" class="mw-redirect" title="Gross National Income">gross national income</a> (GNI). The difference is that GDP defines its scope according to location, while GNI defines its scope according to ownership. In a global context, <a href="/wiki/Gross_world_product" title="Gross world product">world GDP and world GNI</a> are, therefore, equivalent terms.
</p><p>GDP is product produced within a country's borders; GNI is product produced by enterprises owned by a country's citizens. The two would be the same if all of the productive enterprises in a country were owned by its own citizens, and those citizens did not own productive enterprises in any other countries. In practice, however, foreign ownership makes GDP and GNI non-identical. Production within a country's borders, but by an enterprise owned by somebody outside the country, counts as part of its GDP but not its GNI; on the other hand, production by an enterprise located outside the country, but owned by one of its citizens, counts as part of its GNI but not its GDP.
</p><p>For example, the GNI of the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">USA</a> is the value of output produced by American-owned firms, regardless of where the firms are located. Similarly, if a country becomes increasingly in debt, and spends large amounts of income servicing this debt this will be reflected in a decreased GNI but not a decreased GDP. Similarly, if a country sells off its resources to entities outside their country this will also be reflected over time in decreased GNI, but not decreased GDP. This would make the use of GDP more attractive for politicians in countries with increasing national debt and decreasing assets.
</p><p>Gross national income (GNI) equals GDP plus income receipts from the rest of the world minus income payments to the rest of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1991, the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> switched from using GNP to using GDP as its primary measure of production.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
The relationship between United States GDP and GNP is shown in table 1.7.5 of the <i><a href="/wiki/National_Income_and_Product_Accounts" title="National Income and Product Accounts">National Income and Product Accounts</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_standards">International standards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gross_domestic_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: International standards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The international standard for measuring GDP is contained in the book <i><a href="/wiki/United_Nations_System_of_National_Accounts_(UNSNA)" class="mw-redirect" title="United Nations System of National Accounts (UNSNA)">System of National Accounts</a></i> (1993), which was prepared by representatives of the <a href="/wiki/International_Monetary_Fund" title="International Monetary Fund">International Monetary Fund</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, <a href="/wiki/Organisation_for_Economic_Co-operation_and_Development" class="mw-redirect" title="Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development">Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development</a>, <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a>. The publication is normally referred to as SNA93 to distinguish it from the previous edition published in 1968 (called SNA68) <sup id="cite_ref-Central_Bureau_of_Statistics_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Central_Bureau_of_Statistics-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>SNA93 provides a set of rules and procedures for the measurement of national accounts. The standards are designed to be flexible, to allow for differences in local statistical needs and conditions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nominal_GDP_and_adjustments_to_GDP">Nominal GDP and adjustments to GDP</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gross_domestic_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Nominal GDP and adjustments to GDP">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The raw GDP figure as given by the equations above is called the nominal, historical, or current, GDP. When one compares GDP figures from one year to another, it is desirable to compensate for changes in the value of money –  for the effects of inflation or deflation. To make it more meaningful for year-to-year comparisons, it may be multiplied by the ratio between the value of money in the year the GDP was measured and the value of money in a base year.
</p><p>For example, suppose a country's GDP in 1990 was $100 million and its GDP in 2000 was $300 million. Suppose also that inflation had halved the value of its currency over that period. To meaningfully compare its GDP in 2000 to its GDP in 1990, we could multiply the GDP in 2000 by one-half, to make it relative to 1990 as a base year. The result would be that the GDP in 2000 equals $300 million × one-half = $150 million, <i>in 1990 monetary terms.</i> We would see that the country's GDP had realistically increased 50 <a href="/wiki/Percentage#Percentage_increase_and_decrease" title="Percentage">percent</a> over that period, not 200 percent, as it might appear from the raw GDP data. The GDP adjusted for changes in money value in this way is called the <a href="/wiki/Real_gross_domestic_product" title="Real gross domestic product">real, or constant, GDP</a>.
</p><p>The factor used to convert GDP from current to constant values in this way is called the <i><a href="/wiki/GDP_deflator" title="GDP deflator">GDP deflator</a></i>. Unlike <a href="/wiki/Consumer_price_index" title="Consumer price index">consumer price index</a>, which measures inflation or deflation in the price of household consumer goods, the GDP deflator measures changes in the prices of all domestically produced goods and services in an economy including investment goods and government services, as well as household consumption goods.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Constant-GDP figures allow us to calculate a GDP growth rate, which indicates how much a country's production has increased (or decreased, if the growth rate is negative) compared to the previous year.
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>Real GDP growth rate for year <i>n</i></dd></dl></dd>
<dd>= [(Real GDP in year <i>n</i>) − (Real GDP in year <i>n</i> − 1)] / (Real GDP in year <i>n</i> − 1)</dd></dl>
<p>Another thing that it may be desirable to account for is population growth. If a country's GDP doubled over a certain period, but its population tripled, the increase in GDP may not mean that the standard of living increased for the country's residents; the average person in the country is producing less than they were before. <i>Per-capita GDP</i> is a measure to account for population growth.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cross-border_comparison_and_purchasing_power_parity">Cross-border comparison and purchasing power parity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gross_domestic_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cross-border comparison and purchasing power parity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The level of GDP in countries may be compared by converting their value in national currency according to <i>either</i> the current currency exchange rate, or the purchasing power parity exchange rate.
</p>
<ul><li><b>Current currency exchange rate</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Exchange_rate" title="Exchange rate">exchange rate</a> in the international <a href="/wiki/Foreign_exchange_market" title="Foreign exchange market">foreign exchange market</a>.</li>
<li><b>Purchasing power parity exchange rate</b> is the exchange rate based on the <a href="/wiki/Purchasing_power_parity" title="Purchasing power parity">purchasing power parity</a> (PPP) of a currency relative to a selected standard (usually the <a href="/wiki/United_States_dollar" title="United States dollar">United States dollar</a>). This is a comparative (and theoretical) exchange rate, the only way to directly realize this rate is to sell an entire <a href="/wiki/CPI" class="mw-redirect" title="CPI">CPI</a> basket in one country, convert the cash at the currency market rate &amp; then rebuy that same basket of goods in the other country (with the converted cash). Going from country to country, the distribution of prices within the basket will vary; typically, <a href="/wiki/Tradability" title="Tradability">non-tradable</a> purchases will consume a greater proportion of the basket's total cost in the higher GDP country, per the <a href="/wiki/Balassa-Samuelson_effect" class="mw-redirect" title="Balassa-Samuelson effect">Balassa-Samuelson effect</a>.</li></ul>
<p>The ranking of countries may differ significantly based on which method is used.
</p>
<ul><li>The <i>current exchange rate method</i> converts the value of goods and services using global currency <a href="/wiki/Exchange_rate" title="Exchange rate">exchange rates</a>. The method can offer better indications of a country's international purchasing power. For instance, if 10% of GDP is being spent on buying hi-tech foreign <a href="/wiki/Weapon" title="Weapon">arms</a>, the number of weapons purchased is entirely governed by <i>current exchange rates</i>, since arms are a traded product bought on the international market. There is no meaningful 'local' price distinct from the international price for high technology goods. The PPP method of GDP conversion is more relevant to non-traded goods and services. In the above example if hi-tech weapons are to be produced internally their amount will be governed by GDP (PPP) rather than nominal GDP.</li></ul>
<p>There is a clear pattern of the <i>purchasing power parity method</i> decreasing the disparity in GDP between high and low income (GDP) countries, as compared to the <i>current exchange rate method</i>. This finding is called the <a href="/wiki/Penn_effect" title="Penn effect">Penn effect</a>.
</p><p>For more information, see <a href="/wiki/Measures_of_national_income_and_output" title="Measures of national income and output">Measures of national income and output</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_of_living_and_GDP:_wealth_distribution_and_externalities">Standard of living and GDP: wealth distribution and externalities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gross_domestic_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Standard of living and GDP: wealth distribution and externalities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>GDP per capita is often used as an indicator of living standards.<sup id="cite_ref-HDWMSL_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDWMSL-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The major advantage of GDP per capita as an indicator of standard of living is that it is measured frequently, widely, and consistently. It is measured frequently in that most countries provide information on GDP on a quarterly basis, allowing trends to be seen quickly. It is measured widely in that some measure of GDP is available for almost every country in the world, allowing inter-country comparisons. It is measured consistently in that the technical definition of GDP is relatively consistent among countries.
</p><p>GDP does not include several factors that influence the standard of living. In particular, it fails to account for:
</p>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Externalities" class="mw-redirect" title="Externalities">Externalities</a></b> – Economic growth may entail an increase in negative externalities that are not directly measured in GDP.<sup id="cite_ref-soL_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soL-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-limitations_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-limitations-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Increased industrial output might grow GDP, but any pollution is not counted.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Non-market transactions</b>– GDP excludes activities that are not provided through the market, such as household production, bartering of goods and services, and volunteer or unpaid services.</li>
<li><b>Non-monetary economy</b>– GDP omits economies where no money comes into play at all, resulting in inaccurate or abnormally low GDP figures. For example, in countries with major business transactions occurring informally, portions of local economy are not easily registered. <a href="/wiki/Barter_(economics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Barter (economics)">Bartering</a> may be more prominent than the use of money, even extending to services.<sup id="cite_ref-limitations_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-limitations-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Quality improvements and inclusion of new products</b>– by not fully adjusting for quality improvements and new products, GDP understates true <a href="/wiki/Economic_growth" title="Economic growth">economic growth</a>. For instance, although computers today are less expensive and more powerful than computers from the past, GDP treats them as the same products by only accounting for the monetary value. The introduction of new products is also difficult to measure accurately and is not reflected in GDP despite the fact that it may increase the standard of living. For example, even the richest person in 1900 could not purchase standard products, such as antibiotics and cell phones, that an average consumer can buy today, since such modern conveniences did not exist then.</li>
<li><b>Sustainability of growth</b>– GDP is a measurement of economic historic activity and is not necessarily a projection.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Wealth_distribution" class="mw-redirect" title="Wealth distribution">Wealth distribution</a></b>  – GDP does not account for variances in incomes of various demographic groups. See <a href="/wiki/Income_inequality_metrics" title="Income inequality metrics">income inequality metrics</a> for discussion of a variety of inequality-based economic measures.<sup id="cite_ref-limitations_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-limitations-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>It can be argued that GDP per capita as an indicator standard of living is correlated with these factors, capturing them indirectly.<sup id="cite_ref-HDWMSL_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDWMSL-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> As a result, GDP per capita as a standard of living is a continued usage because most people have a fairly accurate idea of what it is and know it is tough to come up with quantitative measures for such constructs as happiness, quality of life, and well-being.<sup id="cite_ref-HDWMSL_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HDWMSL-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations_and_criticisms">Limitations and criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gross_domestic_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Limitations and criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations_at_introduction">Limitations at introduction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gross_domestic_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Limitations at introduction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Simon_Kuznets" title="Simon Kuznets">Simon Kuznets</a>, the economist who developed the first comprehensive set of measures of national income, stated in his first report to the US Congress in 1934, in a section titled "Uses and Abuses of National Income Measurements":<sup id="cite_ref-kuznets_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kuznets-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></p><blockquote><p> The valuable capacity of the human mind to simplify a complex situation in a compact characterization becomes dangerous when not controlled in terms of definitely stated criteria. With quantitative measurements especially, the definiteness of the result suggests, often misleadingly, a precision and simplicity in the outlines of the object measured. Measurements of national income are subject to this type of illusion and resulting abuse, especially since they deal with matters that are the center of conflict of opposing social groups where the effectiveness of an argument is often contingent upon oversimplification.  [...]</p></blockquote>
<blockquote><p>All these qualifications upon estimates of national income as an index of productivity are just as important when income measurements are interpreted from the point of view of economic welfare. But in the latter case additional difficulties will be suggested to anyone who wants to penetrate below the surface of total figures and market values. Economic welfare cannot be adequately measured unless the personal distribution of income is known. And no income measurement undertakes to estimate the reverse side of income, that is, the intensity and unpleasantness of effort going into the earning of income. The welfare of a nation can, therefore, scarcely be inferred from a measurement of national income as defined above.</p></blockquote><p> In 1962, Kuznets stated:<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup></p><blockquote><p>Distinctions must be kept in mind between quantity and quality of growth, between costs and returns, and between the short and long run. Goals for more growth should specify more growth of what and for what.</p></blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Further_criticisms">Further criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gross_domestic_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Further criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ever since the development of GDP, multiple observers have pointed out limitations of using GDP as the overarching measure of economic and social progress.
</p><p>Many <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmentalists</a> argue that GDP is a poor measure of social progress because it does not take into account harm to the <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">environment</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although a high or rising level of GDP is often associated with increased economic and social progress within a country, a number of scholars have pointed out that this does not necessarily play out in many instances. For example, <a href="/wiki/Jean_Dr%C3%A8ze" title="Jean Drèze">Jean Drèze</a> and <a href="/wiki/Amartya_Sen" title="Amartya Sen">Amartya Sen</a> have pointed out that an increase in GDP or in GDP growth does not necessarily lead to a higher standard of living, particularly in areas such as healthcare and education.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Another important area that does not necessarily improve along with GDP is political liberty, which is most notable in China, where GDP growth is strong yet political liberties are heavily restricted.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>GDP does not account for the distribution of income among the residents of a country, because GDP is merely an aggregate measure. An economy may be highly developed or growing rapidly, but also contain a wide gap between the rich and the poor in a society. These inequalities often occur on the lines of race, ethnicity, gender, religion, or other minority status within countries.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This can lead to misleading characterizations of economic well-being if the income distribution is heavily skewed toward the high end, as the poorer residents will not directly benefit from the overall level of wealth and income generated in their country. Even GDP per capita measures may have the same downside if inequality is high. For example, South Africa during apartheid ranked high in terms of GDP per capita, but the benefits of this immense wealth and income were not shared equally among the country.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>GDP does not take into account the value of household and other <a href="/wiki/Unpaid_work" title="Unpaid work">unpaid work</a>. Some, including <a href="/wiki/Martha_Nussbaum" title="Martha Nussbaum">Martha Nussbaum</a>, argue that this value should be included in measuring GDP, as household labor is largely a substitute for goods and services that would otherwise be purchased for value.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Even under conservative estimates, the value of unpaid labor in Australia has been calculated to be over 50% of the country's GDP.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> A later study analyzed this value in other countries, with results ranging from a low of about 15% in Canada (using conservative estimates) to high of nearly 70% in the United Kingdom (using more liberal estimates). For the United States, the value was estimated to be between about 20% on the low end to nearly 50% on the high end, depending on the methodology being used.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Because many public policies are shaped by GDP calculations and by the related field of <a href="/wiki/National_accounts" title="National accounts">national accounts</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> the non-inclusion of unpaid work in calculating GDP can create distortions in public policy, and some economists have advocated for changes in the way public policies are formed and implemented.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The UK's <a href="/wiki/Natural_Capital_Committee" title="Natural Capital Committee">Natural Capital Committee</a> highlighted the shortcomings of GDP in its advice to the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a> Government in 2013, pointing out that GDP "focuses on flows, not stocks.  As a result, an economy can run down its assets yet, at the same time, record high levels of GDP growth, until a point is reached where the depleted assets act as a check on future growth". They then went on to say that "it is apparent that the recorded GDP growth rate overstates the sustainable growth rate.  Broader measures of wellbeing and wealth are needed for this and there is a danger that short-term decisions based solely on what is currently measured by national accounts may prove to be costly in the long-term".
</p><p>It has been suggested that countries that have authoritarian governments, such as the People's Republic of <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, inflate their GDP figures.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proposals_to_overcome_GDP_limitations">Proposals to overcome GDP limitations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gross_domestic_product&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Proposals to overcome GDP limitations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In response to these and other limitations of using GDP, alternative approaches have emerged.
</p>
<ul><li>In the 1980s, <a href="/wiki/Amartya_Sen" title="Amartya Sen">Amartya Sen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Martha_Nussbaum" title="Martha Nussbaum">Martha Nussbaum</a> developed the <a href="/wiki/Capability_approach" title="Capability approach">capability approach</a>, which focuses on the functional capabilities enjoyed by people within a country, rather than the aggregate wealth held within a country. These capabilities consist of the functions that a person is able to achieve.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In 1990 <a href="/wiki/Mahbub_ul_Haq" title="Mahbub ul Haq">Mahbub ul Haq</a>, a Pakistani Economist at the United Nations, introduced the <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">Human Development Index</a> (HDI). The HDI is a composite index of life expectancy at birth, adult literacy rate and standard of living measured as a logarithmic function of GDP, adjusted to purchasing power parity.</li>
<li>In 1989, <a href="/wiki/John_B._Cobb" title="John B. Cobb">John B. Cobb</a> and <a href="/wiki/Herman_Daly" title="Herman Daly">Herman Daly</a> introduced <a href="/wiki/Measure_of_Economic_Welfare" class="mw-redirect" title="Measure of Economic Welfare">Index of Sustainable Economic Welfare (ISEW)</a> by taking into account various other factors such as consumption of nonrenewable resources and degradation of the environment. The new formula deducted from GDP (personal consumption + public non-defensive expenditures - private defensive expenditures + capital formation + services from domestic labour - costs of environmental degradation - depreciation of natural capital)</li>
<li>In 2005, <a href="/wiki/Med_Jones" title="Med Jones">Med Jones</a>, an American Economist, at the International Institute of Management, introduced the first secular Gross National Happiness Index a.k.a. <a href="/wiki/Gross_National_Well-being" title="Gross National Well-being">Gross National Well-being</a> framework and Index to complement GDP economics with additional seven dimensions, including environment, education, and government, work, social and health (mental and physical) indicators. The proposal was inspired by the King of Bhutan's <a href="/wiki/Gross_National_Happiness" title="Gross National Happiness">GNH</a> philosophy.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2009 the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> released a communication titled <i>GDP and beyond: Measuring progress in a changing world</i><sup id="cite_ref-euro_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-euro-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> that identified five actions to improve the indicators of progress in ways that make it more responsive to the concerns of its citizens: Introduced a proposal to complementing GDP with environmental and social indicators</li>
<li>In 2009 Professors <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Stiglitz" title="Joseph Stiglitz">Joseph Stiglitz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amartya_Sen" title="Amartya Sen">Amartya Sen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jean-Paul_Fitoussi" title="Jean-Paul Fitoussi">Jean-Paul Fitoussi</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Commission_on_the_Measurement_of_Economic_Performance_and_Social_Progress" title="Commission on the Measurement of Economic Performance and Social Progress">Commission on the Measurement of Economic Performance and Social Progress</a> (CMEPSP), formed by French President, <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Sarkozy" title="Nicolas Sarkozy">Nicolas Sarkozy</a> published a proposal to overcome the limitation of GDP economics to expand the focus to well-being economics with wellbeing framework consisting of health, environment, work, physical safety, economic safety, political freedom.</li>
<li>In 2012, the Karma Ura of the Center for Bhutan Studies published Bhutan Local GNH Index contributors to happiness—physical, mental and spiritual health; time-balance; social and community vitality; cultural vitality; education; living standards; good governance; and ecological vitality. The <a href="/wiki/Bhutan_GNH_Index" class="mw-redirect" title="Bhutan GNH Index">Bhutan GNH Index</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>In 2013 <a href="/wiki/OECD_Better_Life_Index" title="OECD Better Life Index">OECD Better Life Index</a> was published by the OECD. The dimensions of the index included health, economic, workplace, income, jobs, housing, civic engagement, <a href="/wiki/Life_satisfaction" title="Life satisfaction">life satisfaction</a></li>
<li>In 2013 professors John Helliwell, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Layard,_Baron_Layard" title="Richard Layard, Baron Layard">Richard Layard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Sachs" title="Jeffrey Sachs">Jeffrey Sachs</a> published <a href="/wiki/World_Happiness_Report" title="World Happiness Report">World Happiness Report</a> and proposed to measure other wellbeing indicators in addition to GDP. the evaluation framework included GDP per capita, Gini (income inequality), life satisfaction, health, freedom of life choices, trust and absence of corruption.</li></ul>
