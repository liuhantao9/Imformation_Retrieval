	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Scipio Vaughan</h1>

<p><b>Scipio Vaughan</b> (1784–1840) was an <a href="/wiki/African-American" class="mw-redirect" title="African-American">African-American</a> <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">artisan</a> and former <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_United_States" title="Slavery in the United States">slave</a><sup id="cite_ref-pan_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pan-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> who inspired a "<a href="/wiki/Back-to-Africa_movement" title="Back-to-Africa movement">back to Africa</a>" movement among some of his offspring to connect with their roots in Africa, specifically, the <a href="/wiki/Yoruba_people" title="Yoruba people">Yoruba</a> of <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a> in the early 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-cousins_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cousins-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> After gaining his freedom, he spent the latter part of his life in the United States and started the movement with his immediate family members in his final moments. Several generations of Scipio's descendants are dispersed across three continents where they mostly live or lived,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> except for occasional cousin reunions, which includes Nigeria, Sierra Leone, Liberia, Ghana and Tanzania in Africa; Jamaica, Barbados in the Caribbean; the United States, Canada in North America; and the United Kingdom in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_career">Early life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scipio_Vaughan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Scipio was born a prince in 1784 in the <a href="/wiki/Owu_kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Owu kingdom">Owu kingdom</a> of <a href="/wiki/Yorubaland" title="Yorubaland">Yorubaland</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Abeokuta" title="Abeokuta">Abeokuta</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-pan_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pan-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He was captured by European  trans-Atlantic slave traders in 1805<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and taken together with other captured slaves to the <a href="/wiki/Velekete_Slave_Market" title="Velekete Slave Market">Velekete Slave Market</a> in <a href="/wiki/Badagry" title="Badagry">Badagry</a>, Nigeria's slave port from where he was shipped in a slave ship and taken upcountry to Camden, about 30 miles northeast of Columbia, South Carolina to Charleston, <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, United States where he was sold as a slave to a white master, Wiley Vaughan and brought to live in <a href="/wiki/Camden,_South_Carolina" title="Camden, South Carolina">Camden</a>, so he traditionally took the surname of his master in addition to his given name; Scipio, as <i>Scipio Vaughan</i>. Scipio was so skilled as an ironmonger that he established a reputation in the area as a talented artisan for his work in fashioning iron gates and fences.<sup id="cite_ref-cousins_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cousins-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> As a result of his exceptional gifts,  his master Wiley Vaughan valued him so much that he granted his freedom, his tools, and one hundred dollars as stated in his will after his death. In 1827, Scipio Vaughan became a free man for the remaining part of his life.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_children">Marriage and children</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scipio_Vaughan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Marriage and children">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1815, Scipio married Maria Theresa Louisa Matilda of <a href="/wiki/Cherokee_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Cherokee people">Cherokee</a> descent,<sup id="cite_ref-pan_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pan-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> the second daughter of Bonds Conway. Maria's father, Bonds Conway  was born in Virginia, had come to Camden from Virginia as the body servant of his Master Peter Conway. He was also the first free black of Camden and a successful small businessman and land-owner. Scipio Vaughan and Maria Theresa Louisa Matilda Conway bore and raised 9 children; 7 daughters and 2 sons. All were born in South Carolina, namely: Burrell Churchill Vaughan  in 1816; Elizabeth Margaret Hall in 1818; Kitty Ann Hammond in 1820; Nancy Carter Vaughan in 1822; Sarah Ann Vaughan in 1825; James Churchill Vaughan in 1828; Harriet Amanda in 1829; Maria Virginia Vaughan in 1832 and Mary Elizabeth Vaughan Mac Laughlin in 1838. By the early 1800s, Conway had become a successful landowner in Camden. One of his properties included  a "small Charleston type house," which has been restored to its former state.
</p>
<h2><span id=".22Back-to-Africa.22_Movement.2C_Descendancy_and_Legacy"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;Back-to-Africa&quot;_Movement,_Descendancy_and_Legacy">"Back-to-Africa" Movement, Descendancy and Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scipio_Vaughan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: &quot;Back-to-Africa&quot; Movement, Descendancy and Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On his deathbed in 1840, Scipio told his sons to return to his native Yorubaland in Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-cousins_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cousins-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pan_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pan-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It was most likely that Scipio was determined to reverse the effects of the transAtlantic slave trade, through some members of his immediate family by rebuilding their roots in Africa in order to restore some of their lost dignity, pride, wealth, power and security. To grant their father's last request, James Churchill Vaughan, 24 years old at the time, and his elder brother Burrell Vaughan, enrolled with the <a href="/wiki/American_Colonization_Society" title="American Colonization Society">American Colonization Society</a> as emigrants to Liberia. They left Camden in 1852 in an attempt to also escape the oppressive laws against coloured men and sailed to Liberia in 1853.  There, they started a new life and James Churchill Vaughan became prominent there. However, after arriving Liberia, they did not settle for long. They lived there for two years before accepting an offer of employment to work with Thomas Jefferson Bowen, a Missionary with the Southern Baptist Convention and his wife to Yorubaland in 1855 to spread the Baptist religion. They came to Nigeria in 1854<sup id="cite_ref-chicago_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chicago-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and arrived in Ijaye to work as a builder in 1855.  During the brutal Ibadan-Ijaye War, James was taken captive. He escaped, took refuge in Abeokuta and served as a military sharpshooter. After missionaries were driven out of Abeokuta in 1867, he and other Christian refugees resettled in Lagos, where he built a successful hardware business and he raised his family. He became part of the Lagos elites and became a wealthy and prosperous merchant. He also led a revolt against white missionaries, in the 1880s, helping to establish the Ebenezer Baptist Church, the first indigenous and independent church in West Africa in 1888, located at 50a, Campbell Street, <a href="/wiki/Lagos_Island" title="Lagos Island">Lagos Island</a>. James also kept in touch with his relatives in America, who were also embroiled in their own separate struggles for survival, prosperity, and dignity in post-Civil War South Carolina, and elsewhere. James Churchill Vaughan revisited his South Carolinian home and family before his death in Lagos, Nigeria in 1893. Scipio Vaughan's descendants included several state legislators during the Reconstruction period, politicians, diplomats, entrepreneurs and a high proportion of teachers, doctors and lawyers, among other professionals. Members of the Vaughans of Nigeria and the United States have maintained contact with one another for several decades over the century.<sup id="cite_ref-cousins_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cousins-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nigerian_lineage">Nigerian lineage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scipio_Vaughan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nigerian lineage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Vaughan's descendants include the Nigerian nationalist <a href="/wiki/James_Churchill_Vaughan" title="James Churchill Vaughan">Dr. James C. Vaughan</a>, and Nigerian educationist <a href="/wiki/Lady_Kofo_Ademola" class="mw-redirect" title="Lady Kofo Ademola">Kofoworola, Lady Ademola</a> (the wife of Nigeria's first African Chief Justice, the Rt. Hon. <a href="/wiki/Oba_(ruler)#Oloye" title="Oba (ruler)">Omoba</a> Sir <a href="/wiki/Adetokunbo_Ademola" title="Adetokunbo Ademola">Adetokunbo Ademola</a>). James Churchill Vaughan's daughter, Aida Arabella Vaughan Moore, married the Hon. Omoba Eric Olawolu Moore, a prominent Lagos lawyer and one-time member of the Nigerian Legislative Council, and they gave birth to three children, including Kofoworola, Lady Ademola and Chief Oladipo Moore, Q.C., another of Nigeria's most prominent lawyers, whose wife Aduke Moore was legal counsel to Mobil Oil, West Africa, and later went to New York as a Nigerian delegate to the United Nations General Assembly. 
James Churchill Vaughan's first son, James Wilson Vaughan, married Clara Zenobia Allen and had five  children, including the famous Lagos medical doctor, activist and founder of the Nigerian Youth Movement, Dr. James Churchill Vaughan Jr.
James Churchhill Vaughan Jr.'s brother James Richard Oladeinde Vaughan married Remi Taiwo (also a descendant of royalty); they had two children; Apinke Coker and Ayo Vaughan. Ayo Vaughan became a nursing administrator<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and married a British architect, <a href="/wiki/Alan_Richards_(architect)" class="mw-redirect" title="Alan Richards (architect)">Alan Richards</a>, with whom she had four children including the filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Remi_Vaughan-Richards" title="Remi Vaughan-Richards">Remi Vaughan-Richards</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_lineage">American lineage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scipio_Vaughan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: American lineage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>James Churchill Vaughan's sister, Mary Elizabeth Vaughan Mac Laughlin (1838-1863) who remained in the United States  married a Scot named Mac Laughlin. They had one child, Harriet Josephine Mac Laughlin Carter (1856-1917). Harriet Josephine Mac Laughlin had 12 children. Aida Arabella Stradford was Harriet's third child. Arabella Stradford who married Cornelius Francis Stradford, a renowned Chicago attorney and historic activist, was the driving force behind the survival of the family tradition in the United States. Aida Arabella Stradford had three children, <a href="/wiki/Jewel_Lafontant-Mankarious" class="mw-redirect" title="Jewel Lafontant-Mankarious">Jewel Lafontant-Mankarious</a>, Burrell Carter and Cornelius Francis Jr. Jewel Lafontant-Mankarious (1922-1997) was the first woman to earn a law degree from the University of Chicago. In 1973, the then President Nixon also appointed her to be the first woman to hold the post of Deputy Solicitor General in the Justice Department. Jewel's son is the American businessman <a href="/wiki/John_W._Rogers_Jr." title="John W. Rogers Jr.">John W. Rogers Jr.</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cousin_Reunions">Cousin Reunions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scipio_Vaughan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cousin Reunions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The American Vaughans started the incentive to trace their African heritage and re-unite with the African group of the Vaughan family. The Vaughans first attempt to convene a reunion started in August, 1970, when several family members convened a meeting in Pittsburgh and decided to arrange an annual reunion of all their known relatives. They read the research of a deceased family member, Aida Arabella Stradford, a South Carolina school teacher, and studied census figures, family Bible records and other documents.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The Nigerian Vaughans and their American relatives stayed in touch through the years after Churchill's death with periodic "Cousin" reunions. Today, the American Vaughans are now a network of more than 3,000 cousins from over 22 states – along with their Nigerian cousins. From the daughters, who remained in the United States, the cousins have traced the eight main family lines - Barnes, Brevard, Bufford, Cauthen, McGriff, Peay, Truesdale and Vaughan.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-chicago_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chicago-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ebony_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ebony-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
