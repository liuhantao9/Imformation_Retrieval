<p>The <b>fire services in the United Kingdom</b> operate under separate legislative and administrative arrangements in <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales" title="England and Wales">England and Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>.
</p><p>Emergency cover is provided by over fifty agencies. These are officially known as a <b>fire and rescue service</b> (FRS) which is the term used in modern legislation and by government departments.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The older terms of <i>fire brigade</i> and <i>fire service</i> survive in informal usage and in the names of a few organisations. England and Wales (and formerly Scotland) have local fire services which are each overseen by a <a href="/wiki/Fire_authority" title="Fire authority">fire authority</a>, which is made up of representatives of local governments. Fire authorities have the power to raise a <a href="/wiki/Council_Tax" title="Council Tax">Council Tax</a> levy for funding, with the remainder coming from the government. Scotland and Northern Ireland have centralised fire services, and so their authorities are effectively committees of the devolved parliaments. The total budget for fire services in 2014-15 was Â£2.9 billion.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Central government maintains national standards and a body of independent advisers through the <a href="/wiki/Chief_Fire_and_Rescue_Adviser" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief Fire and Rescue Adviser">Chief Fire and Rescue Adviser</a>, which was created in 2007, while <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Inspectorate_of_Constabulary_and_Fire_%26_Rescue_Services" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Inspectorate of Constabulary and Fire &amp; Rescue Services">Her Majesty's Inspectorate of Constabulary and Fire &amp; Rescue Services</a> provides direct oversight. The devolved government in Scotland has a similar agency, <a href="/wiki/HMFSI_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="HMFSI Scotland">HMFSI Scotland</a>.
</p><p>Firefighters in the United Kingdom are allowed to join unions, the main one being the <a href="/wiki/Fire_Brigades_Union" title="Fire Brigades Union">Fire Brigades Union</a>, while chief fire officers (the heads of the various FRS) are members of the National Fire Chiefs Council,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>(formerly the Chief Fire Officers Association), which has some role in national co-ordination.
</p><p>The fire services have undergone significant changes since the beginning of the 21st century, a process that has been propelled by a devolution of central government powers, new legislation and a change to operational procedures in the light of terrorism attacks and threats.
</p>

<h3><span id="Legislation_.28United_Kingdom.2C_Great_Britain_or_England_and_Wales.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation_(United_Kingdom,_Great_Britain_or_England_and_Wales)">Legislation (United Kingdom, Great Britain or England and Wales)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legislation (United Kingdom, Great Britain or England and Wales)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="box-Prose plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-Prose" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png" decoding="async" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/60px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/80px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="48" data-file-height="48" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>is in <a href="/wiki/MOS:LIST" class="mw-redirect" title="MOS:LIST">list</a> format, but may read better as <a href="/wiki/MOS:PROSE" class="mw-redirect" title="MOS:PROSE">prose</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> You can help by <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit">converting this section</a>, if appropriate. <a href="/wiki/Help:Editing" title="Help:Editing">Editing help</a> is available.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">February 2012</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Fire services are established and granted their powers under fairly new legislation which has replaced a number of <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Acts of Parliament">Acts of Parliament</a> dating back more than 60 years, but is still undergoing change; a rough timeline can be seen below.
</p>
<ul><li>1938: <a href="/wiki/Fire_Brigades_Act_1938" title="Fire Brigades Act 1938">Fire Brigades Act 1938</a>. This Act (which is no longer in force) provided for centralised co-ordination of fire brigades in Great Britain and made it mandatory for local authorities to arrange an effective fire service.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>1947: <a href="/wiki/Fire_Services_Act_1947" title="Fire Services Act 1947">Fire Services Act 1947</a> This Act transferred the functions of the National Fire Service to local authorities. Now repealed entirely in England and Wales by Schedule 2 of the Fire and Rescue Services Act 2004.</li>
<li>1959: <a href="/wiki/Fire_Services_Act_1959" title="Fire Services Act 1959">Fire Services Act 1959</a> This Act amended the 1947 Act; it dealt with pensions, staffing arrangements and provision of services by other authorities. It was repealed in England and Wales along with the 1947 Act.</li>
<li>1999: <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Authority_Act_1999" title="Greater London Authority Act 1999">Greater London Authority Act 1999</a> This act was necessary to allow for the formation of the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Authority" title="Greater London Authority">Greater London Authority</a> and in turn the <a href="/wiki/London_Fire_and_Emergency_Planning_Authority" title="London Fire and Emergency Planning Authority">London Fire and Emergency Planning Authority</a>.</li></ul>
<p>In 2002, there was a series of national <a href="/wiki/UK_Firefighter_dispute_2002/2003" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Firefighter dispute 2002/2003">fire strikes</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> with much of the discontent caused by the aforementioned report into the fire service conducted by Prof Sir George Bain. In December 2002, the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Review_of_the_Fire_Service" title="Independent Review of the Fire Service">Independent Review of the Fire Service</a> was published with the industrial action still ongoing; it made radical proposals to how the fire service should be organised and managed. Bain's report ultimately led to a change in the laws relating to firefighting.
</p>
<ul><li>2002: Independent Review of the Fire Service published<sup id="cite_ref-irfs_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irfs-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>2004: <a href="/wiki/Fire_and_Rescue_Services_Act_2004" title="Fire and Rescue Services Act 2004">Fire and Rescue Services Act 2004</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> generally only applying to England and Wales.</li>
<li>2006: The <a href="/wiki/Regulatory_Reform_(Fire_Safety)_Order_2005" title="Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005">Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>   This piece of secondary legislation or <a href="/wiki/Statutory_instrument" title="Statutory instrument">statutory instrument</a> replaces several other acts that dealt with fire precautions and fire safety in premises, including the now defunct process of issuing fire certificates. It came into force on 1 October 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The DfCLG has published a set of guides for non-domestic premises:<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.communities.gov.uk/index.asp?id=1162101">[3]</a></li>
<li>2006: The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Wales_Act_2006" title="Government of Wales Act 2006">Government of Wales Act 2006</a> gave the <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_for_Wales" title="National Assembly for Wales">National Assembly for Wales</a> powers to pass laws on "Fire and rescue services. [and] Promotion of fire safety otherwise than by prohibition or regulation." but does not prevent future legislation being passed by the UK government which applies to two or more constituent countries.</li></ul>
<p>There are further plans to modernise the fire service according to the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Association" title="Local Government Association">Local Government Association</a>. Its website outlines future changes, and specific projects:
</p><p><i>"The aim of the Fire Modernisation Programme is to adopt modern work practices within the Fire &amp; Rescue Service to become more efficient and effective, while strengthening the contingency and resilience of the Service to react to incidents.</i>
"<i><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></i>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Select_committee">Select committee</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Select committee">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The fire service in England and Wales is scrutinised by a <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a> <a href="/wiki/Select_Committee_(Westminster_System)" class="mw-redirect" title="Select Committee (Westminster System)">select committee</a>.  In June 2006, the fire and rescue service select committee, under the auspices of the Communities and Local Government Committee, published its latest report.
</p><p><b>Committee report</b>
</p><p>The committee's brief is described on its website:
</p><p><i>The Communities and Local Government Committee is appointed by the House of Commons to examine the expenditure, administration, and policy of the Department for Communities and Local Government and its associated bodies.</i><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Government response</b>
</p><p>This document, and the subsequent government response<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> in September 2006, are important as they outlined progress on the FiReControl, efforts to address diversity and the planned closure of HMFSI in 2007 among many issues.
</p><p>Both documents are interesting as they refer back to Professor Bain's report and the many recommendations it made and continue to put forward the notion that there is an ongoing need to modernise FRSs.  For example, where FRSs were historically inspected by HMFSI, much of this work is now carried out by the <a href="/wiki/National_Audit_Office_(United_Kingdom)" title="National Audit Office (United Kingdom)">National Audit Office</a>.
</p><p><b>Fire Control</b>
</p><p>On 8 February 2010 the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a> <a href="/wiki/Communities_and_Local_Government_Select_Committee" class="mw-redirect" title="Communities and Local Government Select Committee">Communities and Local Government Select Committee</a> heard evidence on the Fire Control project.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Called to give evidence were Cllr <a href="/wiki/Brian_Coleman" title="Brian Coleman">Brian Coleman</a> and Cllr James Pearson from the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20101227021348/http://www.lga.gov.uk/lga/core/page.do?pageId=21575">Local Government Association.</a> Also giving evidence <a href="/wiki/Matt_Wrack" title="Matt Wrack">Matt Wrack</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Fire_Brigades_Union" title="Fire Brigades Union">Fire Brigades Union</a> and John Bonney <a href="/wiki/Chief_Fire_Officers_Association" title="Chief Fire Officers Association">Chief Fire Officers Association</a>. The second session heard evidence from <a href="/wiki/Shahid_Malik" title="Shahid Malik">Shahid Malik</a> <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament#United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> Fire Minister, Sir <a href="/wiki/Ken_Knight" title="Ken Knight">Ken Knight</a> <a href="/wiki/Chief_Fire_and_Rescue_Adviser" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief Fire and Rescue Adviser">Chief Fire and Rescue Adviser</a>, Shona Dunn Director for Fire and Resilience <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Communities_and_Local_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Department for Communities and Local Government">Department for Communities and Local Government</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robin_Southwell" title="Robin Southwell">Robin Southwell</a> CEO and Roger Diggle Project Director <a href="/wiki/EADS" class="mw-redirect" title="EADS">EADS</a>. The committee was chaired by Dr <a href="/wiki/Phyllis_Starkey" title="Phyllis Starkey">Phyllis Starkey</a> <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament#United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a> and attracted significant media attention.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

</p>
<h3><span id="Legislation_and_administration_.28Northern_Ireland.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation_and_administration_(Northern_Ireland)">Legislation and administration (Northern Ireland)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Legislation and administration (Northern Ireland)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fire and rescue services in Northern Ireland are provided by a single entity, the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Fire_and_Rescue_Service" title="Northern Ireland Fire and Rescue Service">Northern Ireland Fire and Rescue Service</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Statutory_Corporation" class="mw-redirect" title="Statutory Corporation">Statutory Corporation</a> funded by the <a href="/wiki/Department_of_Health,_Social_Services_and_Public_Safety" class="mw-redirect" title="Department of Health, Social Services and Public Safety">Department of Health, Social Services and Public Safety</a>.
</p>
<ul><li>1950: When the <a href="/wiki/National_Fire_Service" title="National Fire Service">National Fire Service</a> Northern Ireland was disbanded, three fire authorities took control of firefighting but were unified as the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Belfast_Fire_Brigade" title="History of the Belfast Fire Brigade">Northern Ireland Fire Authority in 1950</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>    The Fire Services (Amendment) Act (Northern Ireland) 1950 became primary legislation in the same year.</li>
<li>2006: Fire and Rescue Services (Northern Ireland) Order 2006 S.I. No. 1254 (N.I.9)<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span id="Legislation_and_administration_.28Scotland.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation_and_administration_(Scotland)">Legislation and administration (Scotland)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Legislation and administration (Scotland)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Fire services in Scotland are the responsibility of the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_Secretary_for_Justice" title="Cabinet Secretary for Justice">Cabinet Secretary for Justice</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Previously the responsibility lay with the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Scotland" title="Secretary of State for Scotland">Secretary of State for Scotland</a> (the relevant "Secretary of State" referred to in legislation applicable to Scotland).
</p>
<ul><li>1824: Establishment of municipal fire service in <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> the first public fire brigade in the UK<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>1885: Creation of <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Office" title="Scottish Office">Scottish Office</a> transferring administrative (but not legislative) responsibility to <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Scotland" title="Secretary of State for Scotland">Secretary of State for Scotland</a>.</li>
<li>1938: Fire Services Act 1938 combined the functions of 185 fire brigades and imposed fire-fighting duties upon local authorities,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>1941: Fire brigades transferred to <a href="/wiki/National_Fire_Service" title="National Fire Service">National Fire Service</a> (NFS) to form No.11 Region<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>].</li>
<li>1947: <a href="/wiki/Fire_Services_Act_1947" title="Fire Services Act 1947">Fire Services Act 1947</a> returned fire brigades in Scotland to local authority control,<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> mostly via joint boards. 11 brigades were created<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> resembling somewhat the NFS areas. Section 36 of the Act dealt with its application to Scotland. Parts of the Act remain in force in Scotland.</li>
<li>1959: Parts of the UK <a href="/wiki/Fire_Services_Act_1959" title="Fire Services Act 1959">Fire Services Act 1959</a> remain in force in Scotland.</li>
<li>1996: Creation of additional joint boards consequent to local government re-organisation.</li>
<li>1999: Responsibility transfers from Secretary of State for Scotland to the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Executive" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish Executive">Scottish Executive</a></li>
<li>2002: Consultation Paper: <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.scotland.gov.uk/Publications/2002/04/sfsf/0">"The Scottish Fire Service of the Future"</a></li>
<li>2003: Consultation Paper: <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.scotland.gov.uk/Publications/2003/10/18316/File-1">"The Scottish Fire and Rescue Service: legislative proposals"</a>.</li>
<li>2004: introduction of Fire (Scotland) Bill consequent to 2003 consultation paper.</li>
<li>2005: <a href="/wiki/Fire_(Scotland)_Act_2005" title="Fire (Scotland) Act 2005">Fire (Scotland) Act 2005</a> The scope of this act includes a "fire safety regime for non-domestic premises", but it also includes legislation that allows for the provision and operation of fire and rescue services for the eight local authority and joint board FRS in Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>2012: Police and Fire Reform (Scotland) Act 2012, creating a single <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Fire_and_Rescue_Service" title="Scottish Fire and Rescue Service">Scottish Fire and Rescue Service</a> with effect from 1 April 2013</li></ul>

[and] Promotion of fire safety otherwise than by prohibition or regulation.".</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Government_responsibility_for_fire_services">Government responsibility for fire services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Government responsibility for fire services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_government">Central government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Central government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Historically, there had been about 1600 local authority <a href="/wiki/Fire_services" class="mw-redirect" title="Fire services">fire services</a> under the responsibility of the <a href="/wiki/Home_Office" title="Home Office">Home Office</a> in England and Wales until <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. In 1941, the creation of the <a href="/wiki/National_Fire_Service" title="National Fire Service">National Fire Service</a> brought all UK fire brigades under central government control.  The National Fire Service was in turn under the auspices of the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Defence_Service" title="Civil Defence Service">Civil Defence Service</a>. Post-war legislation returned control to the Northern Ireland Government, the Home Office (for services in England and Wales) and the Secretary of State for Scotland.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="England">England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:West_Pier_fire_with_boat_20030328.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/West_Pier_fire_with_boat_20030328.jpg/220px-West_Pier_fire_with_boat_20030328.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/West_Pier_fire_with_boat_20030328.jpg/330px-West_Pier_fire_with_boat_20030328.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/West_Pier_fire_with_boat_20030328.jpg/440px-West_Pier_fire_with_boat_20030328.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="450" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:West_Pier_fire_with_boat_20030328.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Fire on <a href="/wiki/West_Pier" title="West Pier">West Pier</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brighton" title="Brighton">Brighton</a>, England</div></div></div>
<p>The fire service has always been the ultimate responsibility of a government department, historically assisted by an executive agency called <a href="/wiki/Chief_Fire_and_Rescue_Adviser" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief Fire and Rescue Adviser">Her Majesty's Fire Service Inspectorate</a> or HMFSI,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> its function was described thus:
</p><p><i>"To achieve our vision by education and legislation, in an environment that encourages best practice, equality and diversity, health and safety and best value, and through inspection, to advance the development and continuous improvement of fire brigades."</i>
</p><p>Directly after the May 2001 general election, control of the fire service in England and Wales passed from the Home Office to the DTLR - or Department for Transport, Local Government and Regions.  This department was then broken up creating the Office of the Deputy Prime Minister (ODPM) which took central government responsibility.
</p><p>In May 2006, the ODPM was re-structured creating the <a href="/wiki/Department_for_Communities_and_Local_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Department for Communities and Local Government">Department for Communities and Local Government</a> or CLG, and it became the central government department for fire authorities in England.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> but would be advised by a new department under the direction of the <a href="/wiki/Chief_Fire_and_Rescue_Adviser" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief Fire and Rescue Adviser">Chief Fire and Rescue Adviser</a> - see below.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_service_ministers">Fire service ministers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Fire service ministers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The fire service minister is the most senior politician whose brief directly includes fire and rescue issues in England and Wales, other parts of the UK never having had the matter specifically under the control of a UK minister in peacetime.  The fire service minister is not part of the prime minister's cabinet. The post is held by a junior minister, or <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_Under-Secretary_of_State" title="Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State">Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State</a> who reports to the secretary of state for Communities and Local Government, who takes ultimate responsibility for fire and rescue, but that is part of a much wider brief.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Greg_Clark" title="Greg Clark">Greg Clark</a>:  Secretary of State for Communities and Local Government</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mark_Francois" title="Mark Francois">Mark Francois</a>:  Appointed in 2015 as Minister of State for Fire and Resilience at CLG<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_government">Regional government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Regional government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Below national level, there are regional and local bodies whose role it is to establish a fire authority, implement the legislation from the tier above, while working alongside the relevant HMFSI and other interested bodies.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Authority" title="Greater London Authority">Greater London Authority</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/London_Fire_and_Emergency_Planning_Authority" title="London Fire and Emergency Planning Authority">London Fire and Emergency Planning Authority</a> (under direct control of Greater London Authority)</li>
<li>Metropolitan and county fire and rescue services or brigades - see below</li></ul>
<p>The next level beneath that of local authority, is a brigade which usually comes under the operational command of a high ranking senior officer.  Traditionally <a href="/wiki/Chief_Fire_Officer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief Fire Officer">Chief Fire Officers</a> have risen through the ranks from firefighter, although under modernisation plans brigades can now operate graduate entry, and fast track promotion as is already the case with the <a href="/wiki/Armed_forces" class="mw-redirect" title="Armed forces">armed forces</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Police" title="Police">police</a>. The London Fire Brigade announced details of its graduate scheme in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>  Chief Fire Officers (CFO) 'speak' collectively via the <a href="/wiki/Chief_Fire_Officers_Association" title="Chief Fire Officers Association">Chief Fire Officers Association</a>.
</p>
<ul><li>Fire authority: local councillors elected to set policy on its fire and rescue services, and distribute funding, and approve major spending</li>
<li>Local authority:  Chief executive - overall powers for all an authority's functions, including fire, rescue and resilience</li>
<li>Brigade: Chief Fire Officer (CFO), Brigade Manager, or (in Greater London) Commissioner - overall operational, strategic and command of a brigade or fire and rescue service</li></ul>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Buncefield3.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Buncefield3.jpg/220px-Buncefield3.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Buncefield3.jpg/330px-Buncefield3.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Buncefield3.jpg/440px-Buncefield3.jpg 2x" data-file-width="491" data-file-height="327" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Buncefield3.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Buncefield_fire" title="Buncefield fire">Buncefield fire</a></div></div></div>
<p>CFOs do attend some operational incidents. <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire_Fire_and_Rescue_Service" title="Hertfordshire Fire and Rescue Service">Hertfordshire</a>'s CFO, Roy Wilsher, took command at the <a href="/wiki/Buncefield_fire" title="Buncefield fire">Buncefield oil depot fire</a> in 2005, forming part of the <a href="/wiki/Gold_Silver_Bronze_command_structure" class="mw-redirect" title="Gold Silver Bronze command structure">gold command</a> team. If a CFO attends an incident, he will usually be the commanding officer of that incident.
</p>
<h4><span id="Her_Majesty.27s_Chief_Inspector_of_Fire_Services_.28England.2C_Wales_and_Northern_Ireland.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Her_Majesty's_Chief_Inspector_of_Fire_Services_(England,_Wales_and_Northern_Ireland)">Her Majesty's Chief Inspector of Fire Services (England, Wales and Northern Ireland)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Her Majesty&#039;s Chief Inspector of Fire Services (England, Wales and Northern Ireland)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li><i>See main article <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Chief_Inspector_of_Fire_Services" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Chief Inspector of Fire Services">Her Majesty's Chief Inspector of Fire Services</a></i></li></ul>
<p>In England, Wales and Northern Ireland fire services are inspected by what was known as the Fire Service Inspectorate. However, in February 2007, the government announced it was establishing a new unit to provide ministers and civil servants with "independent professional advice on fire and rescue issues".  It will be headed by a new role that will be known as the <a href="/wiki/Chief_Fire_and_Rescue_Adviser" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief Fire and Rescue Adviser">Chief Fire and Rescue Adviser</a> The roles carries the historical function of Her Majesty's Fire Service Inspectorate. The Chief Fire and Rescue Adviser is also title Her Majesty's Chief Inspector of Fire Services.
Its role (among others)is to work towards reducing the number of fire deaths in England and Wales, and implement changes to FRS required by the <a href="/wiki/Fire_and_Rescue_Services_Act_2004" title="Fire and Rescue Services Act 2004">Fire and Rescue Services Act 2004</a><sup id="cite_ref-rel_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rel-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>    In May 2007, <a href="/wiki/Ken_Knight" title="Ken Knight">Sir Ken Knight</a> commissioner of the London Fire and Emergency Planning Authority was appointed as the first ever Chief Fire and Rescue Adviser.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Her_Majesty.27s_Chief_Inspector_of_Fire_Services_.28Scotland.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Her_Majesty's_Chief_Inspector_of_Fire_Services_(Scotland)">Her Majesty's Chief Inspector of Fire Services (Scotland)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Her Majesty&#039;s Chief Inspector of Fire Services (Scotland)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Scotland, the Scottish Fire and Rescue Service is inspected by <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Fire_Service_Inspectorate_for_Scotland" title="Her Majesty&#39;s Fire Service Inspectorate for Scotland">Her Majesty's Fire Service Inspectorate for Scotland</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Except for the period when the <a href="/wiki/National_Fire_Service" title="National Fire Service">National Fire Service</a> existed, matters concerning fire fighting fell within the remit of the Scottish Office (later the Scottish Executive, now the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> In Scotland <a href="/wiki/HMFSI_Scotland" class="mw-redirect" title="HMFSI Scotland">Her Majesty's Fire Service Inspectorate for Scotland</a> (commonly known by the shortened and nationally unqualified form "HMFSI") exists to inspect all fire Services in Scotland to ascertain how they are discharging their functions under relevant legislation <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.grampianfrs.org.uk/subdreamer/index.php?categoryid=64">[4]</a>. It functions as an autonomous body under the charge of the Justice Ministry of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a><sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With effect from 1 April 2013 is a single <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Fire_and_Rescue_Service" title="Scottish Fire and Rescue Service">Scottish Fire and Rescue Service</a> (in place of the eight former regional services).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland">Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Matters concerning fire fighting fell within the remit of the government of Northern Ireland, with two exceptions; the period of the existence of the National Fire Service (Northern Ireland), and the period of direct rule from Westminster when the government of Northern Ireland did not exist.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wales">Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Responsibility for fire and rescue services and promotion of fire safety in Wales<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> lies with the Welsh Government.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_service_structure">Fire service structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Fire service structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="FRS_or_brigade-level">FRS or brigade-level</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: FRS or brigade-level">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Brigades are further sub-divided according to local practice as follows:
</p>
<ul><li>Command: Large brigades, such as the <a href="/wiki/London_Fire_Brigade" title="London Fire Brigade">London Fire Brigade</a>, are divided into <i>commands</i></li>
<li>Area: A brigade or FRS can also be divided into <i>areas</i>, for example London used to consist of five geographical areas: northwest, north, northeast, southwest and southeast</li>
<li>Divisional: A smaller geographical area, again it can be decided locally; again London until 1986 consisted of eleven <i>divisions</i></li>
<li>Borough: Brigades are now aligned with local councils, and because of London's size, its three commands also overlap with the London boroughs, so each borough is a small division. It now has borough teams,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>  and a separate list of stations shows which borough it belongs to.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Firefighters">Firefighters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Firefighters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following types of work pattern are found at fire stations:
</p>
<ul><li><b>Wholetime:</b> Firefighters work full-time in on 2 day and night shifts within the fire service.</li>
<li><b>Day crewed:</b> Firefighters work a 10-12 hour day shift and are retained at night.</li>
<li><b>Day crew plus:</b> Firefighters work a 24-hour shift consisting of 12 hours in station and spending the night at an accommodation building on the fire station plot.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Retained_firefighters" class="mw-redirect" title="Retained firefighters">Retained</a>:</b> Retained firefighters are on call for long periods (at least 50 hours a week) to respond to incidents.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> They are paid a salary for the time they spend on call and a greater amount for each incident they respond to, on average 2 or 3 per week. Many have jobs outside of the fire service.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Volunteer_fire_department" title="Volunteer fire department">Volunteer</a>:</b> Volunteer firefighters are now rare, though some stations are run by volunteers in certain rural areas, such as Scotland and North Yorkshire. There are two small fire services that are run entirely by volunteers â the <a href="/wiki/Peterborough_Volunteer_Fire_Brigade" title="Peterborough Volunteer Fire Brigade">Peterborough Volunteer Fire Brigade</a><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> and the Borth Voluntary Fire Unit (near <a href="/wiki/Aberystwyth" title="Aberystwyth">Aberystwyth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> These are contracted to provide services for a regular fire service.</li></ul>
<p>Large town fire stations have full-time crews, though many employ retained firefighters as well. Small rural ones are usually staffed only by retained firefighters.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Resilience">Resilience</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Resilience">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Smoke_house_training.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Smoke_house_training.jpg/220px-Smoke_house_training.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Smoke_house_training.jpg/330px-Smoke_house_training.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Smoke_house_training.jpg/440px-Smoke_house_training.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5184" data-file-height="3456" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Smoke_house_training.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Staffordshire_Fire_and_Rescue_Service" title="Staffordshire Fire and Rescue Service">Staffordshire Fire and Rescue Service</a> firefighter, training in a smoke house</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_Office" title="Cabinet Office">Cabinet Office</a> is responsible for the <a href="/wiki/Civil_Contingencies_Secretariat" title="Civil Contingencies Secretariat">Civil Contingencies Secretariat</a> (CSS),<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> which provides advice for individuals in case of a major disaster:
</p><p><i>"The (web)site provides easily understandable guidance for the general public on how to prepare themselves, their families and their homes and businesses to cope during an emergency or disaster."</i>
</p><p>Any such emergency or disaster is likely to involve a UK FRS, and the generic term for such contingency plans has become known as <i>resilience</i><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> The 'Preparing for emergencies website' gives specific government advice on fire safety, specific examples include the summer fire safety campaign, and schools' fire safety guide which are just two examples.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_service_funding">Fire service funding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Fire service funding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the UK, an FRS generally provides its services for free, although there are some special services that can be charged for, and some additional services that can be paid for. The service is free to the end user in the case of an emergency. Funding for the fire service comes from two principal sources: a central government grant, and a small levy on the local <a href="/wiki/Council_tax" class="mw-redirect" title="Council tax">council tax</a>.  This levy is called a <i>precept</i>.
</p><p>There was a 17% reduction in fire service funding between 2010 and 2016, according to the <a href="/wiki/National_Audit_Office_(United_Kingdom)" title="National Audit Office (United Kingdom)">National Audit Office</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modernisation">Modernisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Modernisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_need_for_modernisation">The need for modernisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: The need for modernisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Romford_fire_station.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Romford_fire_station.JPG/220px-Romford_fire_station.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="159" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Romford_fire_station.JPG/330px-Romford_fire_station.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Romford_fire_station.JPG/440px-Romford_fire_station.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1484" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Romford_fire_station.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/London_Fire_Brigade" title="London Fire Brigade">London Fire Brigade</a> station at <a href="/wiki/Romford" title="Romford">Romford</a></div></div></div>
<p>In 2002, Professor Sir <a href="/wiki/George_Bain_(academic)" title="George Bain (academic)">George Bain</a> was asked by the government to conduct a wide-ranging review of the fire service in the UK. His report, the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Review_of_the_Fire_Service" title="Independent Review of the Fire Service">Independent Review of the Fire Service</a>, led to rapid changes to fire and rescue services, and was the basis of what eventually became the <a href="/wiki/Fire_and_Rescue_Services_Act_2004" title="Fire and Rescue Services Act 2004">Fire and Rescue Services Act 2004</a>. Bain's terms of reference were described in September 2002 as follows:
</p><p>"Having regard to the changing and developing role of the Fire Service in the United Kingdom, to inquire into and make recommendations on the future organisation and management of the Fire Service..."<sup id="cite_ref-bain_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bain-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Bain's report was unpopular with firefighters, and a long period of <a href="/wiki/UK_Firefighter_dispute_2002/2003" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Firefighter dispute 2002/2003">industrial action</a> started in 2002 and continued until 2003 when a new pay and conditions package was put together.
</p><p>One of the areas identified by Bain as being in need of modernisation was FRS approach to fire prevention and community fire safety.  There is now more emphasis on <a href="/wiki/Fire_prevention" title="Fire prevention">fire prevention</a> and providing public information coupled with encouraging businesses and individuals to take responsibility for providing a risk assessment of businesses â which became law in October 2006.   Additionally, changes to central government, local government, and geographical boundaries have affected the fire service in the UK.
</p><p>The fire service in England consists of <a href="/wiki/Local_authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Local authority">local authority</a> brigades or FRSs, which come under the administrative control of metropolitan and shire, or county fire authorities (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Essex_County_Fire_and_Rescue_Service" title="Essex County Fire and Rescue Service">Essex County Fire and Rescue Service</a>).
</p><p>Most <a href="/wiki/Statute" title="Statute">statutory</a> firefighting bodies consist of a fire authority and brigade, the former responsible for political and administrative aspects of service provision and the latter for delivering it.  For example, <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire_County_Council" title="Oxfordshire County Council">Oxfordshire County Council</a> is responsible for <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire_Fire_and_Rescue_Service" title="Oxfordshire Fire and Rescue Service">Oxfordshire Fire and Rescue Service</a>. The armed forces, private fire brigades and airports all make their own firefighting provision.
</p><p>Another area identified in Bain's report was the FRS' capacity to respond to major incidents. Bain's review stated that the fire service should have specific responsibilities for: "Emergency preparedness coupled with the capacity and resilience
to respond to major incidents of terrorism and other chemical, biological, radiological or nuclear threats".<sup id="cite_ref-bain_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bain-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>  In England and Wales, three projects are being rolled out that will help the FRS deal with incidents like these. The projects come under the umbrella of the <b>Fire Resilience</b> programme. The programme will provide the FRS with a far greater resilience and control to handle all types of incident.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Dimension">New Dimension</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: New Dimension">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/New_Dimension_programme" title="New Dimension programme">New Dimension programme</a></div>
<p><b>New Dimension</b> was the first Fire Resilience project to be implemented. It provided FRSs with a wide range of equipment and vehicles to help deal with major challenges like floods and terrorist attacks.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="FireLink">FireLink</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: FireLink">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/FireLink" title="FireLink">FireLink</a></div>
<p><b>FireLink</b> is the new digital FRS wide area communications system in England, Scotland and Wales. The FRS use wide area radios to communicate between vehicles and control rooms but there has never been a fully compatible system.  FireLink is more efficient and provides greater resilience for <a href="/wiki/Gold_Silver_Bronze_command_structure" class="mw-redirect" title="Gold Silver Bronze command structure">command and control</a>. The fire service's ability to communicate efficiently, across different brigades and with other emergency services has never been easy. The issue was raised by Sir Desmond Fennell, who conducted the <a href="/wiki/Public_inquiry" title="Public inquiry">public inquiry</a> into the <a href="/wiki/King%27s_Cross_fire" title="King&#39;s Cross fire">King's Cross fire</a> in 1987.  Fennell recommended when his report was published in 1988 that fireground communications be addressed as an issue of "high importance".<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="FiReControl">FiReControl</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: FiReControl">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/FiReControl" title="FiReControl">FiReControl</a></div>
<p>Prof Bain's report highlighted many areas of proposed change that include working practices, shift hours and time spent on fire prevention duties.  One of the proposals which is now well under way is the reduction of fire service control rooms.
</p><p>At present, each of England's 45 (the number FRS was reduced from 46 in April 2007 with the merger of Devon and Somerset FRSs<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>) FRSs handles its own calls from either the <a href="/wiki/999_(emergency_telephone_number)" title="999 (emergency telephone number)">999</a> system or <a href="/wiki/Mobile_phone" title="Mobile phone">mobile phone</a> companies.  Additionally, calls are accepted from the other emergency services by dedicated landlines. The <b>FiReControl</b> project is building nine new purpose-built <b>Regional Control Centres</b> (RCCs). The aim is to rationalise call handling and aim for greater communication between the emergency services.
</p><p>At present, 999 calls - whether by mobile telephone or landline - are answered by a <a href="/wiki/BT_Group" title="BT Group">BT</a> operator, who feeds the call to a fire, police, ambulance, or coastguard <a href="/wiki/Emergency_Control_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Emergency Control Centre">Emergency Control Centre</a> as required. Staff, known as control operators or control officers, also despatch the <a href="/wiki/Fire_apparatus" class="mw-redirect" title="Fire apparatus">fire appliances</a>, maintain radio communications and provide detailed risk and geographical information. These operators are employed by a FRS, wear a similar uniform to firefighters, and have their own rank structure. The role of specialist fire officers and control operators overlaps where they work jointly in control or command centres, but the subject of mobilising, command and control will change as the FiReControl project advances.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_safety">Fire safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Fire safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><i>See separate article <a href="/wiki/History_of_fire_safety_legislation_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="History of fire safety legislation in the United Kingdom">History of fire safety legislation in the United Kingdom</a></i></li></ul>
<p>Historically fire safety was a function of local authorities rather than the fire service however in 1947 the introduction of the Fire Services Act gave the Fire Brigades their first responsibilities for fire safety. The Fire Precautions Act 1971, the Fire Precautions (Workplace) Regulations 1997 and the Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005 gave more powers to the service.
</p><p>Today, the modernisation of the UK fire service has taken into account the role that it plays in fire safety issues and that issue is high on the agenda of most fire and rescue services. Many brigades started to produce Integrated Management Plans (IMP) to take into account these new responsibilities and produced plans for not only fire safety in the workplace but also in the community. Now all fire and rescue services have community based fire safety departments.
</p><p>The Fire and Rescue Services Act 2004 now lays out in Section 6 what the fire service must do. It states that a fire and rescue authority must make provision for the purpose of promoting fire safety in its area and this must include the provision of information, publicity and encouragement in respect of the steps to be taken to prevent fires and death or injury by fire not only by the enforcement of specific fire safety legislation, but also by a proactive strategy targeted at all sections of the community.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Incident_reporting">Incident reporting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Incident reporting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Information about each fire incident is collected by each FRS to DCLG standards. The reporting standards include the following parameters:<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Primary or secondary fire - primary fires involve damage to property with an identifiable owner and are coded FDR1. Secondary fires do not and are coded FDR3.</li>
<li>Accidental or deliberate fire</li>
<li>Occupancy of dwellings for accidental residential fires:  Single Occupancy; Multiple Occupancy (Same); Multiple Occupancy (Different; No Occupancy</li>
<li>Deliberate secondary fires involving rubbish:  domestic wheelie bins; commercial/business refuse bins; fixed/street litter bins; refuse /litter in the open.</li>
<li>The involvement of children, youths or adults</li>
<li>The registration plate details where available in relation to vehicle fires</li>
<li>Identified building names and street locations</li></ul>

<p>Wales saw a reduction in the number of fire brigades on <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_(Wales)_Act_1994" title="Local Government (Wales) Act 1994">local government reorganisation</a> in 1996, from eight (the number of former administrative counties) to three, made up of groups of the new principal areas.
</p>

<p>Although not part of the United Kingdom, the <a href="/wiki/Crown_dependencies" title="Crown dependencies">Crown dependencies</a> fire and rescue services operate in close cooperation with, and to the same standards as, the UK fire and rescue services. They are:
</p>

<li>Guernsey Fire and Rescue Service</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man_Fire_and_Rescue_Service" title="Isle of Man Fire and Rescue Service">Isle of Man Fire and Rescue Service</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_UK_fire_and_rescue_services">Other UK fire and rescue services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Other UK fire and rescue services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are several specialised and private FRSs in the United Kingdom.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Airport_fire_services">Airport fire services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Airport fire services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Airport_rescue_and_firefighting_services_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Airport rescue and firefighting services in the United Kingdom">Airport rescue and firefighting services in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Manchester_Airport_Fire_Engine.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Manchester_Airport_Fire_Engine.jpg/220px-Manchester_Airport_Fire_Engine.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Manchester_Airport_Fire_Engine.jpg/330px-Manchester_Airport_Fire_Engine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Manchester_Airport_Fire_Engine.jpg/440px-Manchester_Airport_Fire_Engine.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="3000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Manchester_Airport_Fire_Engine.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A fire appliance at <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Airport" title="Manchester Airport">Manchester Airport</a></div></div></div>
<p>Private airport fire services in the UK protect all categories of airports and aerodromes. They are usually referred to as rescue and firefighting services. One of the biggest aviation fire services is operated by <a href="/wiki/BAA_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="BAA Limited">BAA</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>  Non-BAA airports operate their own fire services to comply with legislation which states that airports must be provided with RFFS. One such example is <a href="/wiki/London_City_Airport" title="London City Airport">London City Airport</a>; its website describes the principal objective of an airport fire and rescue service: "as to save lives in the event of an aircraft accident or incident".<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> The number and type of firefighting appliances based at an airport will be determined by the airport's category.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Airports in the UK are categorised from 1 to 10.  A category 10 airport caters for the biggest aircraft; the standards are determined by the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Civil_Aviation_Authority" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Civil Aviation Authority">Civil Aviation Authority</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defence_Fire_and_Rescue_Service">Defence Fire and Rescue Service</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: Defence Fire and Rescue Service">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Defence_Fire_and_Rescue_Service" title="Defence Fire and Rescue Service">Defence Fire and Rescue Service</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Defence_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Defence (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Defence</a> operates its own fire and rescue organisation. Originally this consisted of a civilian fire service known as the "Defence Fire Service" and the <i>RAF Firefighting and Rescue Service</i>. They were known collectively as the <i>Ministry of Defence Fire Services</i>, but in 2004 were formed into the Defence Fire and Rescue Service. This also includes private contractors brought in to protect sites such as small defence establishments.
</p><p>The DFRS is the largest non geographical or local authority FRS in the UK, though the training, rank structure and equipment used are similar to that operated by their local authority fire service counterparts.
</p><p>As a general rule the RAF personnel cover runways or airfields with particular expertise in defusing aircraft munitions,<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>  with the civilians covering domestic sites, but there were cross overs and both services could be called to airfield and domestic situations.
</p><p>Both RAF and defence personnel can serve abroad both in peacetime and at war.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_services_protecting_royal_properties">Fire services protecting royal properties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=44" title="Edit section: Fire services protecting royal properties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Royal_Household_Fire_Service_van.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Royal_Household_Fire_Service_van.jpg/220px-Royal_Household_Fire_Service_van.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="156" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Royal_Household_Fire_Service_van.jpg/330px-Royal_Household_Fire_Service_van.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Royal_Household_Fire_Service_van.jpg/440px-Royal_Household_Fire_Service_van.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3534" data-file-height="2510" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Royal_Household_Fire_Service_van.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Van of the Royal Household Fire Service, Buckingham Palace</div></div></div>
<p>Several state properties, including royal residences and the <a href="/wiki/Palace_of_Westminster" title="Palace of Westminster">Palace of Westminster</a>, are protected by their own fire services in the interests of protecting sites of heritage and royal importance. The Royal Household Fire Service is responsible for firefighting at Royal properties.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_and_industrial_FRS">Private and industrial FRS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=45" title="Edit section: Private and industrial FRS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some large factories operate their own private or industrial fire services to protect their interests and provide a first attack capability until local authority fire crews arrive.
</p><p>These companies often combine fire and medial response under the banner of "Plant Protection" BMW and Jaguar Land Rover are two examples of factories with this provision.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="BNFL">BNFL</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=46" title="Edit section: BNFL">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><a href="/wiki/British_Nuclear_Fuels" class="mw-redirect" title="British Nuclear Fuels">British Nuclear Fuels</a> and some other <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Nuclear power station">nuclear power station</a> operators have their own on-site fire service.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ports">Ports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=47" title="Edit section: Ports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Several large ports have their own fire service, such as <a href="/wiki/Port_of_Felixstowe" title="Port of Felixstowe">Felixstowe</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Event_fire_safety_services">Event fire safety services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Fire_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=48" title="Edit section: Event fire safety services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Several private event fire safety units operate in the UK at major events such as air shows, regattas, concerts and on film sets.  They sometimes use the services of off-duty fire fighters and emergency personnel to provide fire cover at outdoor events. The cover normally consists of one or two standard fire appliances with perhaps a rapid response or control vehicle.
</p><p>A smaller number of companies offer complex event fire safety packages for large events or civil contingencies, including the provision of specialist off-road fire appliances, dedicated pump rescue ladders and other technical and specialist vehicles. Their roles are to provide a niche response where the fire services have no legal obligation or the specialist vehicles required for the high risk category.
</p>
