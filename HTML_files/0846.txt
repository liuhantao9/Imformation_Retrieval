<p><b>F. Ernest Jackson</b> <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Associate_Member_of_the_Royal_Academy" class="mw-redirect" title="Associate Member of the Royal Academy">ARA</a></span> (1872–1945) was a British painter, <a href="/wiki/Drawing" title="Drawing">draughtsman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poster#Propaganda_and_political_posters" title="Poster">poster designer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lithographer" class="mw-redirect" title="Lithographer">lithographer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ra_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ra-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Ernest_Jackson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Francis Ernest Jackson was born on 15 August 1872 in <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>, the son of a printer.<sup id="cite_ref-tate_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tate-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He was apprenticed as a lithographer, and later attended life-drawing classes at the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_College" class="mw-redirect" title="Yorkshire College">Yorkshire College</a>. He then studied in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_Julian" title="Académie Julian">Académie Julian</a> and the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_des_Beaux-Arts" title="École des Beaux-Arts">École des Beaux-Arts</a> under Bouguereau, Ferrier, J.-P. Laurens and Constant.
</p><p>On his return to Britain he designed posters and practised lithography. In 1907 he became a co-founder of <i>The Neolith</i> and member of the <a href="/wiki/Senefelder_Club" title="Senefelder Club">Senefelder Club</a>.
</p><p>Between 1913 and 1925 Jackson designed <a href="/wiki/Poster" title="Poster">posters</a> for the <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In addition, throughout the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="First World War">First World War</a>, he was in charge of <a href="/wiki/Propaganda" title="Propaganda">propaganda</a> lithography for the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Information_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Information (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Information</a>.
</p><p>From 1926 to 1940 he was principal of the <a href="/wiki/Byam_Shaw_School_of_Art" title="Byam Shaw School of Art">Byam Shaw School of Art</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tate_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tate-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1944 he was elected an <a href="/wiki/Associate_of_the_Royal_Academy" class="mw-redirect" title="Associate of the Royal Academy">Associate of the Royal Academy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ra_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ra-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He died at <a href="/wiki/Oxford" title="Oxford">Oxford</a> on 11 March 1945, from injuries sustained after he was knocked down by a motorcycle. 
</p><p>A memorial exhibition to Jackson was held at the <a href="/wiki/Beaux_Arts_Gallery" title="Beaux Arts Gallery">Beaux Arts Gallery</a> in 1946. A memorial to him lies in <a href="/wiki/St_James%27s_Church,_Piccadilly" title="St James&#39;s Church, Piccadilly">St James's Church, Piccadilly</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy_of_Art">Philosophy of Art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Ernest_Jackson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Philosophy of Art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Discussing his influence on him, the former painter <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lancelot_Glasson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lancelot Glasson (page does not exist)">Lancelot Glasson</a> wrote in 1947 that "his thoughts on Art, and on Life, of which he deemed Art to the expression, were based, not on abstract thinking nor on his own more than ordinary erudition, but on that understanding which the daily practice of a craft gives to man." He himself believed unity to be the first quality of any picture, in which the simplest conception will be found always to be the most beautiful. Reality and art were not connected, but running in parallel one unto the other, and therefore in the final conception of the painting there ought to be no instant of accident, as though nature itself were allowed a role in the process.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
