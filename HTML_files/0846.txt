<p>"<b>We choose to go to the Moon</b>", officially titled as the <b>Address at Rice University on the Nation's Space Effort</b>, is a speech delivered by <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">United States President</a> <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> about the effort to reach the Moon to a large crowd gathered at <a href="/wiki/Rice_Stadium_(Rice_University)" title="Rice Stadium (Rice University)">Rice Stadium</a> in <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>, Texas, on September 12, 1962. The speech was intended to persuade the American people to support the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a>, the national effort to <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing" title="Moon landing">land a man on the Moon</a>.
</p><p>In his speech, Kennedy characterized space as a new frontier, invoking the pioneer spirit that dominated American folklore. He infused the speech with a sense of urgency and destiny, and emphasized the freedom enjoyed by Americans to choose their destiny rather than have it chosen for them. Although he called for competition with the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, he also proposed making the Moon landing a joint project.
</p><p>The speech resonated widely and is still remembered, although at the time there was disquiet about the cost and value of the Moon-landing effort. Kennedy's goal was realized in July 1969, with the successful <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a> mission.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=We_choose_to_go_to_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> became <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> in January 1961, many Americans perceived that the United States was losing the <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">Space Race</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, which had successfully launched the first artificial satellite, <a href="/wiki/Sputnik_1" title="Sputnik 1">Sputnik 1</a>, almost four years earlier. The perception increased when, on April 12, 1961, Russian <a href="/wiki/Cosmonaut" class="mw-redirect" title="Cosmonaut">cosmonaut</a> <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">Yuri Gagarin</a> became the first man in space before the U.S. could launch its first <a href="/wiki/Project_Mercury" title="Project Mercury">Project Mercury</a> astronaut.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELogsdon201129_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELogsdon201129-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> American prestige was further damaged by the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Pigs_Invasion" title="Bay of Pigs Invasion">Bay of Pigs</a> fiasco five days later.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYoungSilcockDunn1969109_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYoungSilcockDunn1969109-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan2003209_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan2003209-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Convinced of the political need for an achievement which would decisively demonstrate America's space superiority, Kennedy asked his <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_United_States" title="Vice President of the United States">vice president</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson" title="Lyndon B. Johnson">Lyndon B. Johnson</a>, in his role as chairman of the <a href="/wiki/National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="National Aeronautics and Space Council">National Aeronautics and Space Council</a>, to identify such an achievement. He specifically asked him to investigate whether the United States could beat the Soviet Union in putting a laboratory in space, or orbiting a man around the Moon, or landing a man on the Moon, and to find out what such a project would cost. Johnson consulted with officials of the <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">National Aeronautics and Space Administration</a> (NASA). Its new <a href="/wiki/List_of_Administrators_and_Deputy_Administrators_of_NASA" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Administrators and Deputy Administrators of NASA">administrator</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_E._Webb" title="James E. Webb">James E. Webb</a>, told him that there was no chance of beating the Russians to launching a space station, and it was uncertain as to whether NASA could orbit a man around the Moon first, so the best option would be to attempt to land a man on the Moon. This would also be the most expensive option; Webb believed it would require $22 billion to achieve it by 1970. Johnson also consulted with <a href="/wiki/Wernher_von_Braun" title="Wernher von Braun">Wernher von Braun</a>; military leaders, including <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_General" class="mw-redirect" title="Lieutenant General">Lieutenant General</a> Bernard Schriever; and three captains of industry: <a href="/wiki/Frank_Stanton_(executive)" title="Frank Stanton (executive)">Frank Stanton</a> from <a href="/wiki/CBS" title="CBS">CBS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Donald_C._Cook" title="Donald C. Cook">Donald C. Cook</a> from <a href="/wiki/American_Electric_Power" title="American Electric Power">American Electric Power</a>, and <a href="/wiki/George_R._Brown" title="George R. Brown">George R. Brown</a> from <a href="/wiki/Brown_%26_Root" class="mw-redirect" title="Brown &amp; Root">Brown &amp; Root</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYoungSilcockDunn1969109–112_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYoungSilcockDunn1969109–112-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kennedy stood before Congress on May 25, 1961, and proposed that the US "should commit itself to achieving the goal, before this decade is out, of landing a man on the Moon and returning him safely to the Earth."<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Not everyone was impressed; a <a href="/wiki/Gallup_Poll" class="mw-redirect" title="Gallup Poll">Gallup Poll</a> indicated that 58 percent of Americans were opposed.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYoungSilcockDunn1969109–112_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYoungSilcockDunn1969109–112-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kennedy's goal gave a specific mission to NASA's <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">Apollo program</a>. This required the expansion of NASA's <a href="/wiki/Space_Task_Group" title="Space Task Group">Space Task Group</a> into a <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson_Space_Center" class="mw-redirect" title="Lyndon B. Johnson Space Center">Manned Spacecraft Center</a>. <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">Houston</a>, Texas was chosen as the site, and the <a href="/wiki/Humble_Oil" title="Humble Oil">Humble Oil and Refining Company</a> donated the land during 1961, with <a href="/wiki/Rice_University" title="Rice University">Rice University</a> as an intermediary.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYoungSilcockDunn1969162_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYoungSilcockDunn1969162-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy took a two-day visit to Houston in September 1962 to view the new facility. He was escorted by astronauts <a href="/wiki/Scott_Carpenter" title="Scott Carpenter">Scott Carpenter</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Glenn" title="John Glenn">John Glenn</a>, and shown models of the <a href="/wiki/Gemini_spacecraft" class="mw-redirect" title="Gemini spacecraft">Gemini</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apollo_spacecraft" class="mw-redirect" title="Apollo spacecraft">Apollo spacecraft</a>; he also viewed the <a href="/wiki/Mercury_spacecraft" class="mw-redirect" title="Mercury spacecraft">Mercury spacecraft</a>, in which Glenn had made America's first orbital flight. He took advantage of the opportunity to deliver a speech to drum up support for the nation's space effort.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan2003211_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan2003211-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-The_Rice_Thresher_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Rice_Thresher-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Initial drafts of the speech were written by <a href="/wiki/Ted_Sorensen" title="Ted Sorensen">Ted Sorensen</a>, with changes by Kennedy.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>On September 12, 1962, a warm and sunny day, President Kennedy delivered his speech before a crowd of about 40,000 people in the <a href="/wiki/Rice_Stadium_(Rice_University)" title="Rice Stadium (Rice University)">Rice University football stadium</a>, many of them students.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Rice_Thresher_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Rice_Thresher-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rice_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The middle portion of the speech has been widely quoted, and reads as follows:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>We set sail on this new sea because there is new knowledge to be gained, and new rights to be won, and they must be won and used for the progress of all people. For space science, like nuclear science and all technology, has no conscience of its own. Whether it will become a force for good or ill depends on man, and only if the United States occupies a position of pre-eminence can we help decide whether this new ocean will be a sea of peace or a new terrifying theater of war. I do not say that we should or will go unprotected against the hostile misuse of space any more than we go unprotected against the hostile use of land or sea, but I do say that space can be explored and mastered without feeding the fires of war, without repeating the mistakes that man has made in extending his writ around this globe of ours.
</p><p>There is no strife, no prejudice, no national conflict in outer space as yet. Its hazards are hostile to us all. Its conquest deserves the best of all mankind, and its opportunity for peaceful cooperation may never come again. But why, some say, the Moon? Why choose this as our goal? And they may well ask, why climb the highest mountain? Why, 35 years ago, <a href="/wiki/Spirit_of_St._Louis" title="Spirit of St. Louis">fly the Atlantic</a>?  Why does <a href="/wiki/Rice%E2%80%93Texas_football_rivalry" title="Rice–Texas football rivalry">Rice play Texas</a>?
</p><p><b>We choose to go to the Moon!</b>  We choose to go to the Moon...We choose to go to the Moon in this decade and do the other things, not because they are easy, but because they are hard; because that goal will serve to organize and measure the best of our energies and skills, because that challenge is one that we are willing to accept, one we are unwilling to postpone, and one we intend to win, and the others, too.<sup id="cite_ref-transcript_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-transcript-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The joke referring to the <a href="/wiki/Rice%E2%80%93Texas_football_rivalry" title="Rice–Texas football rivalry">Rice–Texas football rivalry</a> was handwritten by Kennedy into the speech text,<sup id="cite_ref-Rice_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> and is the part of the speech remembered by sports fans.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Since then, Rice has beaten Texas in 1965 and 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Later in the speech Kennedy also made a joke about the heat. The jokes elicited cheers and laughter from the audience. While these side comments may have diminished the rhetorical power of the speech, and do not resonate outside Texas, they stand as a reminder of the part Texas played in the space race.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhetoric">Rhetoric</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=We_choose_to_go_to_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rhetoric">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:JFKWHP-KN-C23687_Address_at_Rice_University.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/JFKWHP-KN-C23687_Address_at_Rice_University.jpg/220px-JFKWHP-KN-C23687_Address_at_Rice_University.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="216" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/JFKWHP-KN-C23687_Address_at_Rice_University.jpg/330px-JFKWHP-KN-C23687_Address_at_Rice_University.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/JFKWHP-KN-C23687_Address_at_Rice_University.jpg/440px-JFKWHP-KN-C23687_Address_at_Rice_University.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1000" data-file-height="984" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:JFKWHP-KN-C23687_Address_at_Rice_University.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The crowd at Rice University watching Kennedy's speech</div></div></div>
<p>Kennedy's speech used three strategies: "a characterization of space as a beckoning frontier; an articulation of time that locates the endeavor within a historical moment of urgency and plausibility; and a final, cumulative strategy that invites audience members to live up to their pioneering heritage by going to the Moon."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan2003214_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan2003214-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When addressing the crowd at Rice University, he equated the desire to explore space with the pioneering spirit that had dominated American folklore since the nation's foundation.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan2003214_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan2003214-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This allowed Kennedy to reference back to his <a href="/wiki/Inauguration_of_John_F._Kennedy#Inaugural_address" title="Inauguration of John F. Kennedy">inaugural address</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  when he declared to the world "Together let us explore the stars". When he met with <a href="/wiki/Nikita_Khrushchev" title="Nikita Khrushchev">Nikita Khrushchev</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Premier_of_the_Soviet_Union" title="Premier of the Soviet Union">Premier of the Soviet Union</a> in June 1961, Kennedy proposed making the Moon landing a joint project, but Khrushchev did not take up the offer.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELogsdon201132_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELogsdon201132-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> There was rhetorical opposition in the speech to extending the <a href="/wiki/Militarisation_of_space" title="Militarisation of space">militarization of space</a>.
</p><p>Kennedy verbally condensed human history to fifty years, in which "only last week did we develop penicillin and television and nuclear power, and now if America's new spacecraft succeeds in reaching Venus [<a href="/wiki/Mariner_2" title="Mariner 2">Mariner 2</a>], we will have literally reached the stars before midnight tonight."<sup id="cite_ref-auto_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-transcript_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-transcript-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> With this extended metaphor, Kennedy sought to imbue a sense of urgency and change in his audience.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan2003217–218_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan2003217–218-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Most prominently, the phrase "We choose to go to the Moon" in the Rice speech was repeated three times consecutively, followed by an explanation that climaxes in his declaration that the challenge of space is "one that we are willing to accept, one we are unwilling to postpone, and one which we intend to win."<sup id="cite_ref-transcript_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-transcript-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Considering the line before rhetorically asked the audience why they choose to compete in tasks that challenge them, Kennedy highlighted here the nature of the decision to go to space as being a choice, an option that the American people have elected to pursue. Rather than claim it as essential, he emphasized the benefits such an endeavor could provide – uniting the nation and the competitive aspect of it. As Kennedy told Congress earlier, "whatever mankind must undertake, free men must fully share."<sup id="cite_ref-Urgent_National_Needs_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Urgent_National_Needs-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> These words emphasized the freedom enjoyed by Americans to choose their destiny rather than have it chosen for them. Combined with Kennedy's overall usage of rhetorical devices in the Rice University speech, they were particularly apt as a declaration that began the American space race.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan2003219–220_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan2003219–220-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Kennedy was able to describe a romantic notion of space in the Rice University speech with which all citizens of the United States, and even the world could participate, vastly increasing the number of citizens interested in space exploration. He began by talking about space as the new frontier for all of mankind, instilling the dream within the audience.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan2003220–221_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan2003220–221-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> He then condensed human history to show that within a very brief period of time space travel will be possible, informing the audience that their dream is achievable. Lastly, he uses the first-personal plural "we" to represent all the people of the world that would allegedly explore space together, but also involves the crowd.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJordan2003224_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJordan2003224-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=We_choose_to_go_to_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

Webb">James Webb</a>, Vice President <a href="/wiki/Lyndon_Johnson" class="mw-redirect" title="Lyndon Johnson">Lyndon Johnson</a>, NASA Launch Center director <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Debus" class="mw-redirect" title="Kurt Debus">Kurt Debus</a>, Lieutenant General <a href="/wiki/Leighton_I._Davis" title="Leighton I. Davis">Leighton I. Davis</a> and Secretary of Defense <a href="/wiki/Robert_McNamara" title="Robert McNamara">Robert McNamara</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Paul Burka, the executive editor of <i><a href="/wiki/Texas_Monthly" title="Texas Monthly">Texas Monthly</a></i> magazine, a Rice alumnus who was present in the crowd that day, recalled 50 years later that the speech "speaks to the way Americans viewed the future in those days. It is a great speech, one that encapsulates all of recorded history and seeks to set it in the history of our own time. Unlike today’s politicians, Kennedy spoke to our best impulses as a nation, not our worst."<sup id="cite_ref-Rice_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Ron Sass and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Curl" title="Robert Curl">Robert Curl</a> were among the many members of the Rice University faculty present. Curl was amazed by the cost of the space exploration program. They recalled that the ambitious goal did not seem so remarkable at the time, and that Kennedy's speech was not regarded as so different from one delivered by President <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a> at Rice's <a href="/wiki/Tudor_Fieldhouse" title="Tudor Fieldhouse">Autry Court</a> in 1960; but that speech has long since been forgotten, while Kennedy's is still remembered.<sup id="cite_ref-Rice_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rice-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The speech did not stem a rising tide of disquiet about the Moon landing effort. There were many other things that the money could be spent on. Eisenhower declared, "To spend $40 billion to reach the Moon is just nuts."<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYoungSilcockDunn1969197_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYoungSilcockDunn1969197-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senator" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Senator">Senator</a> <a href="/wiki/Barry_Goldwater" title="Barry Goldwater">Barry Goldwater</a> argued that the civilian space program was pushing the more important military one aside. Senator <a href="/wiki/William_Proxmire" title="William Proxmire">William Proxmire</a> feared that scientists would be diverted away from military research into space exploration. A budget cut was only narrowly averted.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYoungSilcockDunn1969197–198_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYoungSilcockDunn1969197–198-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy gave a speech to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> on September 20, 1963, in which he again proposed a joint expedition to the Moon.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Khrushchev remained cautious about participating, and responded with a statement in October 1963 in which he declared that the Soviet Union had no plans to send cosmonauts to the Moon.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYoungSilcockDunn1969202–207_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYoungSilcockDunn1969202–207-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> However, his military advisors persuaded him that the offer was a good one, as it would enable the Soviet Union to acquire American technology.<sup id="cite_ref-Politico_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Politico-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Kennedy ordered reviews of the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_project" class="mw-redirect" title="Apollo project">Apollo project</a> in April, August and October 1963. The final report was received on November 29, 1963, a week after Kennedy's assassination.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELogsdon201132_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELogsdon201132-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=We_choose_to_go_to_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Aldrin_Apollo_11_original.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Aldrin_Apollo_11_original.jpg/220px-Aldrin_Apollo_11_original.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="221" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Aldrin_Apollo_11_original.jpg/330px-Aldrin_Apollo_11_original.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Aldrin_Apollo_11_original.jpg/440px-Aldrin_Apollo_11_original.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3912" data-file-height="3936" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Aldrin_Apollo_11_original.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">Buzz Aldrin</a> on the Moon, 1969 (photograph by <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">Neil Armstrong</a>)</div></div></div>
<p>The idea of a joint Moon mission was abandoned after Kennedy's death,<sup id="cite_ref-Politico_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Politico-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> but the Apollo Project became a memorial to him. His goal was fulfilled in July 1969, with the successful <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">Apollo 11</a> <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing" title="Moon landing">Moon landing</a>. This accomplishment remains an enduring legacy of Kennedy's speech, but his deadline demanded a necessarily narrow focus, and there was no indication of what should be done next once it was achieved.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELogsdon201129_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELogsdon201129-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELogsdon201132_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELogsdon201132-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Apollo did not usher in an era of lunar exploration, and no further missions were sent to the Moon after <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">Apollo 17</a> in 1972. Subsequent planned Apollo missions were canceled.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELogsdon201132_17-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELogsdon201132-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">Space Shuttle</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">International Space Station</a> projects never captured the public imagination the way the Apollo Project did, and NASA would struggle to realize its visions with inadequate resources. Ambitious visions of space exploration were proclaimed by Presidents <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">George H. W. Bush</a> in 1989,  <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a> in 2004, and <a href="/wiki/Donald_J._Trump" class="mw-redirect" title="Donald J. Trump">Donald J. Trump</a> in 2017, but the future of the American space program remains uncertain.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELogsdon201133_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELogsdon201133-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
