<p><i><b>Between Two Women</b></i> (2000) is a 1950s set feature film by <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> writer-director <a href="/wiki/Steven_Woodcock_(film_director)" title="Steven Woodcock (film director)">Steven Woodcock</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> It tells the story of Ellen, a factory worker's wife trapped in an unhappy marriage amidst the grime and industrial noise of north <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Between_Two_Women_(2000_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Working class Ellen (<a href="/wiki/Barbara_Marten" title="Barbara Marten">Barbara Marten</a>) makes friends with her young son's middle-class schoolteacher, Miss Thompson (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Andrina_Carroll&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Andrina Carroll (page does not exist)">Andrina Carroll</a>), and their growing <a href="/wiki/Lesbian" title="Lesbian">lesbian</a> relationship is tastefully explored as Ellen's marriage to the clumsy factory worker, Hardy (<a href="/wiki/Andrew_Dunn_(actor)" title="Andrew Dunn (actor)">Andrew Dunn</a>), gradually falls apart. In the end Ellen finds the strength to follow her true path and her marriage is pretty much over. Because of the stifling social attitudes of the 1950s she and Hardy seem like they'll pretend to still be together. The film closes on a happy note as Ellen catches a train away from the factory town where she lives, to spend time with Kathy.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Between_Two_Women_(2000_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It's the stifling social conventions of the 1950s that are at the root of the movie's <a href="/wiki/Poetic" class="mw-redirect" title="Poetic">poetic</a> and very understated style. Anybody with low concentration parameters could easily miss the lesbian aspect altogether. In the <a href="/wiki/DVD_documentary" class="mw-redirect" title="DVD documentary">DVD documentary</a> <i>The Making of Between Two Women</i> (only on UK DVD) Steven Woodcock says that Miss Thompson was originally intended to be a man but he couldn't get the story to work. He claims not to have based the story of the two women on anything that happened to him in real life but that it came as a flash of inspiration when he woke up one morning.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The movie is in the tradition of the British northern-set <a href="/wiki/Kitchen_sink_drama" class="mw-redirect" title="Kitchen sink drama">kitchen sink drama</a> and is a homage to the <a href="/wiki/British_New_Wave" title="British New Wave">British New Wave</a> of the late 1950s and early 1960s, being filmed in the same naturalistic way, drawing on <a href="/wiki/Cin%C3%A9ma_v%C3%A9rit%C3%A9" title="Cinéma vérité">Cinéma vérité</a> methods, and using entirely real locations. Its main difference is that its theme of a lesbian relationship across the class divide in a gritty working class setting would have been considered too risky, had the film been made at the time of the original British New Wave movement. It's this that gives it, in the words of the British TV magazine the <i><a href="/wiki/Radio_Times" title="Radio Times">Radio Times</a></i>, "a refreshing contemporary spin".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_Production">Background and Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Between_Two_Women_(2000_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Background and Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The movie was shot mostly in and around <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rochdale" title="Rochdale">Rochdale</a> during the summer and autumn of 1999. It has become an international cult movie, garnering many positive reviews and winning the Best Feature Film Award at the <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a> backed <a href="/wiki/New_York_Lesbian_%26_Gay_Film_Festival" class="mw-redirect" title="New York Lesbian &amp; Gay Film Festival">New York Lesbian &amp; Gay Film Festival</a> soon after release.<sup id="cite_ref-britfilms1_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britfilms1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It has been screened in Hollywood at the <a href="/wiki/Directors_Guild_of_America" title="Directors Guild of America">Directors Guild of America</a>. It has been shown on British TV more than 250 times.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Although a very <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> film filled with well-known British actors, it was released on DVD by <a href="/wiki/Image_Entertainment" class="mw-redirect" title="Image Entertainment">Image Entertainment</a> in <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">America</a> before the UK, to some good reviews in <a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-britfilms1_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-britfilms1-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="US_vs_UK_versions">US vs UK versions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Between_Two_Women_(2000_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: US vs UK versions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Region 2 UK version (79 mins) is shorter than the Region 1 US version (93 mins) and is the preferred version of the director. The UK version also contains the behind-the-scenes documentaries, giving nearly an hour of extras with the trailer. Some people prefer the longer version because greater character interplay is evident between the two women.
</p><p>The second documentary on the UK DVD is about deleted scenes, and in it Woodcock explains why he remastered the movie in 2005 and cut it back by about 15 minutes for the 2006 British release because he thought these scenes of interplay between the two women worked in the novel but slowed the story down on screen.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The documentary also shows complete scenes and parts of scenes that were filmed but not included in either the US or UK versions of the movie.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Trivia">Trivia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Between_Two_Women_(2000_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Trivia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ellen's son Victor was played by Edward Woodcock, the director's son in real life. He is on screen more than any other actor in the film.
</p><p>The small grey car that Miss Thompson drives appears again in Steven Woodcock's next film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Jealous_God_(film)" title="The Jealous God (film)">The Jealous God</a></i>. There is a deliberate cross-referencing between the films when Vincent (<a href="/wiki/Jason_Merrells" title="Jason Merrells">Jason Merrells</a>) says that he bought the car from a woman school teacher - a direct reference to it being the same car in the previous film.
</p><p>Andrina Carroll is (in real-life) married to Andrew Dunn.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
