<p><b>Hungarian names</b> include surnames and given names. Occasionally there are more than one of the latter, but normally just one is used. In the <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian language</a>, whether written or spoken, these names are invariably given in the "<a href="/wiki/Eastern_name_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern name order">Eastern name order</a>", or family name followed by given name (In foreign language texts, names are often given with the family name last). The Hungarian language is one of the few national languages in Europe to use the Eastern name order, among languages like <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">Korean</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Alemannic_German" title="Alemannic German">Alemannic German</a> dialect<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and some <a href="/wiki/Basque_nationalism" title="Basque nationalism">Basque nationalists</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">Orthography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hungarian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Orthography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_alphabet" title="Hungarian alphabet">Hungarian alphabet</a></div>
<p>Modern <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_alphabet" title="Hungarian alphabet">Hungarian orthography</a> is slightly simpler than that of the 18th and 19th centuries, but many Hungarians still use the older spelling for their names. For example, the letter 'c' is often written as 'cz'. Letters such as Q, W, X, or Y, which are usually only seen in foreign words, can also be seen in these older spellings of names, especially in old noble family names which stem from the Middle Ages. Some family names refer to a place of origin, and may be written ending in "Y" instead of "I". So someone from <a href="/wiki/Szolnok" title="Szolnok">Szolnok</a> may spell his family name "Szolnoky" instead of "Szolnoki" but only if the chosen Y variant of the family name is not a protected name of a historically documented noble family.
</p><p>Unless acknowledged officially, such spelling variants cannot be used as legal names. Except in special cases, changing one's name to any historical or old-style written name is forbidden.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hungarian_surnames">Hungarian surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hungarian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hungarian surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Hungarian-language_surnames" title="Category:Hungarian-language surnames">Category:Hungarian-language surnames</a> and <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Hungarian_surnames" class="extiw" title="wikt:Appendix:Hungarian surnames">wikt:Appendix:Hungarian surnames</a></div>
<p>The Hungarian language normally puts family names first, except for foreign names, in Hungarian speech and text.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Some Hungarian surnames relate to professions, for example <a href="/wiki/Szab%C3%B3" title="Szabó">Szabó</a> "tailor," <a href="/wiki/Kov%C3%A1cs" title="Kovács">Kovács</a> "smith," <a href="/wiki/Hal%C3%A1sz" class="mw-redirect" title="Halász">Halász</a> "fisher." Other surnames relate to non-Magyar ethnic origin. For example, common Hungarian surnames include <a href="/wiki/N%C3%A9meth" title="Németh">Németh</a> "German," <a href="/wiki/Horv%C3%A1t" class="mw-redirect" title="Horvát">Horvát</a> "Croat," <a href="/wiki/T%C3%B3th" title="Tóth">Tóth</a> an outdated term for "Slovak" (now <i>Szlovák</i> in modern Hungarian), <a href="/wiki/Ol%C3%A1h_(surname)" title="Oláh (surname)">Oláh</a>, an outdated term for "Wallachian", and <a href="/wiki/Lengyel_(surname)" title="Lengyel (surname)">Lengyel</a>, "Polish".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian" class="mw-redirect" title="Austro-Hungarian">Austro-Hungarian</a> empire, in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Hungary" title="Kingdom of Hungary">kingdom of Hungary</a> people of non-Hungarian ethnicity — people of Jewish, German and Slovak ancestry — were encouraged to adopt Hungarian surnames.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Some people with German names translated them directly into Hungarian.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Some of them just <a href="/wiki/Magyarization" title="Magyarization">magyarized</a> their original German surnames into Hungarian forms, like Zsengeller, Scheiberné, etc. But many Hungarians of German descent retained their original surnames like Horn, Deutsch, Staller, Keller, Rockenbauer, Hoffmann, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A few given names are also used as family names, and this practice may confuse even a native Hungarian speaker. For example, in the unlikely case that a Hungarian speaker has never heard of <a href="/wiki/Attila_J%C3%B3zsef" title="Attila József">Attila József</a>, they would be unable to tell in an English context which of the two was his family name, because it could be either.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hungarian_given_names">Hungarian given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hungarian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hungarian given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The origin of Hungarian names is closely related to the religious and dynastic history of the country. Many saints' names and royal names have Hungarian equivalents.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_given_names_and_religious_names">Second given names and religious names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hungarian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Second given names and religious names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hungarians do not commonly use second given names or what Americans call <a href="/wiki/Middle_name" title="Middle name">middle names</a>, nor their corresponding initials. While it is increasingly frequent that they are given one, they tend to choose one they prefer to use.
</p><p>When <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">baptised</a>, a child can get an additional name (baptismal name), especially if there is no saint who bears their name, so they take a name associated with a patron saint. In <a href="/wiki/Confirmation" title="Confirmation">confirmation</a>, children receive another given name, but it is not used. Both baptismal and confirmation names have religious significance only, and they are not on any official records.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Married_names">Married names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hungarian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Married names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is a wide range of selection of a married name. Until about the 18th century <a href="/wiki/Noblewomen" class="mw-redirect" title="Noblewomen">noblewomen</a> kept their names at marriage and their children received their father's name; it became compulsory only under the reign of <a href="/wiki/Joseph_II,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Joseph II, Holy Roman Emperor">Joseph&#160;II</a>). When Hungary was under <a href="/wiki/Habsburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg">Habsburg</a> rule and became influenced by Western European traditions, women became known by their husbands' names. So for example <a href="/w/index.php?title=Szendrey_J%C3%BAlia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Szendrey Júlia (page does not exist)">Szendrey Júlia</a>, marrying <a href="/wiki/Pet%C5%91fi_S%C3%A1ndor" class="mw-redirect" title="Petőfi Sándor">Petőfi Sándor</a>, became <i>Petőfi Sándorné</i> (the <i>-né</i> suffix approximately means "wife of", and this is the Hungarian equivalent of "Mrs." as in "Mrs. John Smith"). This was both the law and the tradition until the 1950s. During the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_Hungary" class="mw-redirect" title="People&#39;s Republic of Hungary">Communist rule of Hungary</a>, great emphasis was put upon the equality of women and men, and from that time, women could either choose to keep their maiden name or take that of their husband. Most women did the latter except for artists.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Now, the alternatives for a woman when she marries are as shown below (using the examples of <i lang="hu" title="Hungarian language text">Szendrey Júlia</i> and <i lang="hu" title="Hungarian language text">Petőfi Sándor</i> &#8211; Júlia and Sándor are their given names):
</p>
<ul><li>Júlia can keep her maiden name, as <i>Szendrey Júlia</i> (now very popular, especially among more-educated women).</li>
<li>Júlia gives up her name, adds the suffix <i>-né</i> to her husband's full name, and will be called <i lang="hu" title="Hungarian language text">Petőfi Sándorné</i>.</li>
<li>Júlia adds the suffix <i lang="hu" title="Hungarian language text">-né</i> to her husband's family name, adds her full name and will be called <i lang="hu" title="Hungarian language text">Petőfiné Szendrey Júlia</i>.</li>
<li>Júlia adds the suffix <i lang="hu" title="Hungarian language text">-né</i> to her husband's full name, adds her full name and will be called <i>Petőfi Sándorné Szendrey Júlia</i> (less popular these days, because it is long to write).</li>
<li>Júlia takes her husband's family name, keeps her given name "Júlia" and will be called <i lang="hu" title="Hungarian language text">Petőfi Júlia</i>.</li></ul>
<p>The applicable law,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> which used to give substantially different sets of options to women and men, was declared sexist and unconstitutional. The ensuing amendment, in force since 2004, also lists options for men. Thus:
</p>
<ul><li>Sándor can keep his birth name, as <i>Petőfi Sándor</i> (the most common choice).</li>
<li>Sándor takes his wife's family name, keeps his given name "Sándor" and will be called <i lang="hu" title="Hungarian language text">Szendrey Sándor</i> (often considered when the wife's family name sounds remarkably better than the husband's one).</li>
<li>A further new option is hyphenation. In our example, the family name <i lang="hu" title="Hungarian language text">Petőfi-Szendrey</i> <b>or</b> <i lang="hu" title="Hungarian language text">Szendrey-Petőfi</i> will be introduced for one or both parties. A sole party not assuming the hyphenated form keeps his or her original family name.</li></ul>
<p>Note that using opposing hyphenations (i.e. Szendrey-Petőfi Sándor and Petőfi-Szendrey Júlia) and exchanging names (i.e. Petőfi Sándor and Szendrey Júlia become Szendrey Sándor and Petőfi Júlia) are not allowed. Also, one can have a maximum of two last names. If one or both partners-to-be come to the marriage with more than one surname, they will have to agree which ones to keep.
</p><p>Both the bride and groom have to declare at the wedding which name they will use, and they have to declare which family name their children will get (which can be changed until the birth of the first child). Children can get either parent's surname, if it is on the marriage certificate, but all children must have the same surname. Since 2004 they can also get a hyphenated name, but only if both parents kept their birth names at least as one part of their new name. Children usually get their father's surname, but hyphenated names are becoming more common.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Same-sex_couple" class="mw-redirect" title="Same-sex couple">Couples of the same sex</a> are not allowed to marry in Hungary, so they cannot legally use each other's names<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> unless they change their names through a <a href="/wiki/Deed_of_change_of_name" title="Deed of change of name">deed of change of name</a>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Women.27s_names_in_everyday_life"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Women's_names_in_everyday_life">Women's names in everyday life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hungarian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Women&#039;s names in everyday life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When a woman takes her husband's name in the traditional way, as in <i>Petőfi Sándorné</i>, her female first name no longer forms part of her official name, yet this is the name she will be called by even after her wedding, in all but the most formal contexts. Thus, Hungarian radio speakers and others often resort to a compromise like <i>Kovács Jánosné, Juli néni</i> (Mrs. János Kovács, aunt Juli) to indicate how the woman should be called by others. (<i>Néni</i> and <i>bácsi</i>, "aunt" and "uncle", are traditional polite forms to address older people, and, for children, to address all adults; it does not indicate a family relationship.)
</p><p>Some women who officially bear the -né form will nevertheless introduce themselves with their husband's family name and their own first name (in our example, <i>Kovács Júlia</i> or <i>Kovácsné Júlia</i>, rather than <i>Kovács Jánosné</i>), to avoid confusion about how to address them.
</p><p>If the woman takes her husband's full name, the couple can easily be referred to in writing as <i>Petőfi Sándor és neje</i> (Sándor Petőfi and wife), equivalent to the English form "Mr. and Mrs. John Smith". This can be seen on older tombstones in Hungarian cemeteries.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hungarian_law_on_names">Hungarian law on names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hungarian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hungarian law on names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By law, children born as Hungarian citizens may bear no more than two surnames (most people have only one; those who have two may hyphenate them). They can also have only one or two given names (religious names not included since they are not official, see above). Given names can be chosen by the parents from an official list of several thousand names (technically, one list for each gender).<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> If the intended name is not on the list, the parents need to apply for approval. Applications are considered by the <a href="/wiki/Research_Institute_for_Linguistics_of_the_Hungarian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Research Institute for Linguistics of the Hungarian Academy of Sciences">Research Institute for Linguistics of the Hungarian Academy of Sciences</a> following a set of principles.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Thus, names are approved if they are not derogatory or overly diminutive, can be written and pronounced easily, can be recognised as either male or female, etc. Approved names expand the official list, the newest edition of which is regularly published. A lot of recent additions are foreign names, which, however, must be spelled following Hungarian phonetics, e.g. <i>Jennifer</i> becomes <i>Dzsenifer</i> or <i>Joe</i> becomes <i>Dzsó</i>.
</p><p>Those who belong to an officially recognized <a href="/wiki/Minorities_in_Hungary" class="mw-redirect" title="Minorities in Hungary">minority in Hungary</a> can also choose names from their own culture, whereby a register of given names maintained by the respective minority governance must be observed.
</p><p>If one or both parents of a child to be named are foreign citizens, the given name(s) may be chosen in accordance with the respective foreign law.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Treatment_of_Hungarian_names_in_English_and_other_languages">Treatment of Hungarian names in English and other languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hungarian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Treatment of Hungarian names in English and other languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Outside Hungary, Hungarian names are usually rendered by the Western convention of other European languages. In English language academic publishing, archiving and cataloguing, different <a href="/wiki/Manuals_of_style" class="mw-redirect" title="Manuals of style">manuals of style</a> treat Hungarian names in different ways. The <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Manual_of_Style" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago Manual of Style">Chicago Manual of Style</a></i> 16th Edition (2010) reverses the Hungarian order to put given name first, but allows all diacritics on the name:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p><b>8.13 Hungarian names.</b> In Hungarian practice the family name precedes the given name — for example, Molnár Ferenc, Kodály Zoltán. In English contexts, however, such names are usually inverted — <a href="/wiki/Ferenc_Moln%C3%A1r" title="Ferenc Molnár">Ferenc Molnár</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zolt%C3%A1n_Kod%C3%A1ly" title="Zoltán Kodály">Zoltán Kodály</a> [...]
</p></blockquote> 
<p>When indexing names, Hungarian names are re-inverted so the surname comes first in English indexes, the same as English names.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hungarian_treatment_of_foreign_names">Hungarian treatment of foreign names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hungarian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Hungarian treatment of foreign names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This way of writing names is not used for people who are not Hungarian (or not from another country where Eastern name order is used). For example, "<a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a>" will stay as "Tony Blair" in Hungarian texts. However, names of historical importance are generally translated and written in the Hungarian way, e.g. <i>Kálvin János</i> for John Calvin. Names from languages using a different script (Arabic, Chinese, Cyrillic, Greek, etc.) are transcribed according to pronunciation.
</p><p>Leaders of countries are translated only in the case of <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarchs</a> and members of their families. For example, "<a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth II of the United Kingdom">Elizabeth II of the United Kingdom</a>" becomes <i lang="hu" title="Hungarian language text">II. Erzsébet</i> and "<a href="/wiki/Pope_Benedict_XVI" title="Pope Benedict XVI">Pope Benedict XVI</a>" becomes <i lang="hu" title="Hungarian language text">XVI. Benedek pápa</i>, but "<a href="/wiki/Fidel_Castro" title="Fidel Castro">Fidel Castro</a>" is not changed.
</p><p>Before the 20th century, foreign names were often translated, for example, <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">Jules Verne</a>'s name was written as "<i lang="hu" title="Hungarian language text">Verne Gyula</i>", and a Hungarian pronunciation was used.
</p>
