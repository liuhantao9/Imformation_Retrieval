<p>A <b>market cross</b>, or in <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">Scots</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Mercat_cross" title="Mercat cross">mercat cross</a>, is a structure used to mark a <a href="/wiki/Market_square" title="Market square">market square</a> in <a href="/wiki/Market_town" title="Market town">market towns</a>, where historically the right to hold a regular market or fair was granted by the monarch, a bishop or a baron. Market crosses were originally from the distinctive tradition in <a href="/wiki/Early_Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Medieval">Early Medieval</a> <a href="/wiki/Insular_art" title="Insular art">Insular art</a> of free-standing stone standing or <a href="/wiki/High_cross" title="High cross">high crosses</a>, often elaborately carved, which goes back to the 7th century.  Market crosses can be found in most market towns in Britain. British emigrants often installed such crosses in their new cities, and several can be found in Canada and Australia.
</p><p>These structures range from carved stone <a href="/wiki/Spire" title="Spire">spires</a>, <a href="/wiki/Obelisk" title="Obelisk">obelisks</a> or <a href="/wiki/Cross" title="Cross">crosses</a>, common to small market towns such as that in <a href="/wiki/Stalbridge" title="Stalbridge">Stalbridge</a>, Dorset, to large, ornate covered structures, such as the <a href="/wiki/Chichester_Cross" title="Chichester Cross">Chichester Cross</a> or <a href="/wiki/Malmesbury_Market_Cross" title="Malmesbury Market Cross">Malmesbury Market Cross</a>. Market Crosses can also be constructed from wood; an example is at <a href="/wiki/Wymondham" title="Wymondham">Wymondham</a>, Norfolk.
</p>
