	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">James Mason</h1>

<p><b>James Neville Mason</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/eɪ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;face&#39;">eɪ</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="/ən/: &#39;on&#39; in &#39;button&#39;">ən</span></span>/</a></span></span>; 15 May 1909&#160;– 27 July 1984) was an English actor. Mason achieved considerable success in British cinema before becoming one of Hollywood's biggest stars. He was the top box office attraction in the UK in 1944 and 1945, with notable films including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seventh_Veil" title="The Seventh Veil">The Seventh Veil</a></i> (1945) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wicked_Lady" title="The Wicked Lady">The Wicked Lady</a></i> (1945). He starred in <i><a href="/wiki/Odd_Man_Out" title="Odd Man Out">Odd Man Out</a></i> (1947), the first recipient of the <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Award_for_Best_British_Film" class="mw-redirect" title="BAFTA Award for Best British Film">BAFTA Award for Best British Film</a>.
</p><p>He starred in a number of successful British and American films from the 1950s to the early 1980s, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Desert_Fox:_The_Story_of_Rommel" title="The Desert Fox: The Story of Rommel">The Desert Fox</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Star_Is_Born_(1954_film)" title="A Star Is Born (1954 film)">A Star Is Born</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/20,000_Leagues_Under_the_Sea_(1954_film)" title="20,000 Leagues Under the Sea (1954 film)">20,000 Leagues Under the Sea</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Lolita_(1962_film)" title="Lolita (1962 film)">Lolita</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/North_by_Northwest" title="North by Northwest">North by Northwest</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prisoner_of_Zenda_(1952_film)" title="The Prisoner of Zenda (1952 film)">The Prisoner of Zenda</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Journey_to_the_Center_of_the_Earth_(1959_film)" title="Journey to the Center of the Earth (1959 film)">Journey to the Centre of the Earth</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Touch_of_Larceny" title="A Touch of Larceny">A Touch of Larceny</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bigger_Than_Life" title="Bigger Than Life">Bigger Than Life</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar_(1953_film)" title="Julius Caesar (1953 film)">Julius Caesar</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Georgy_Girl" title="Georgy Girl">Georgy Girl</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Deadly_Affair" title="The Deadly Affair">The Deadly Affair</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Age_of_Consent_(film)" title="Age of Consent (film)">Age of Consent</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Heaven_Can_Wait_(1978_film)" title="Heaven Can Wait (1978 film)">Heaven Can Wait</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boys_from_Brazil_(film)" title="The Boys from Brazil (film)">The Boys from Brazil</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Verdict" title="The Verdict">The Verdict</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mandingo_(film)" title="Mandingo (film)">Mandingo</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Murder_by_Decree" title="Murder by Decree">Murder by Decree</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Salem%27s_Lot_(1979_miniseries)" title="Salem&#39;s Lot (1979 miniseries)">Salem's Lot</a>.</i>
</p><p>Mason was nominated for three <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Academy Awards</a>, three <a href="/wiki/Golden_Globes" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden Globes">Golden Globes</a> (winning the Golden Globe in 1955 for <a href="/wiki/A_Star_Is_Born_(1954_film)" title="A Star Is Born (1954 film)"><i>A Star is Born</i></a>) and two <a href="/wiki/BAFTA_Awards" class="mw-redirect" title="BAFTA Awards">BAFTA Awards</a> throughout his career. Following his death in 1984, his ashes were interred near the tomb of his close friend, fellow English actor Sir <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Chaplin" title="Charlie Chaplin">Charlie Chaplin</a>.
</p>

<h2><span id="Early_life.2C_family_and_education"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life,_family_and_education">Early life, family and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Mason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life, family and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mason was born in <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>, to Mabel Hattersley (Gaunt) and John Mason.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>His father was a wealthy textile merchant. He was educated at <a href="/wiki/Marlborough_College" title="Marlborough College">Marlborough College</a>, and earned a <a href="/wiki/British_undergraduate_degree_classification" title="British undergraduate degree classification">first</a> in Architecture at <a href="/wiki/Peterhouse,_Cambridge" title="Peterhouse, Cambridge">Peterhouse, Cambridge</a>, where he became involved in stock theatre companies in his spare time. Mason had no formal training in acting and initially embarked upon it for fun.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Mason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_stage_appearances">Early stage appearances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Mason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early stage appearances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After Cambridge, Mason made his stage debut in <a href="/wiki/Aldershot" title="Aldershot">Aldershot</a> in <i>The Rascal</i> in 1931.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>He joined <a href="/wiki/The_Old_Vic" title="The Old Vic">the Old Vic</a> theatre in London under the guidance of <a href="/wiki/Tyrone_Guthrie" title="Tyrone Guthrie">Tyrone Guthrie</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> While there he appeared in productions of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cherry_Orchard" title="The Cherry Orchard">The Cherry Orchard</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_(play)" title="Henry VIII (play)">Henry VIII</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Measure_for_Measure" title="Measure for Measure">Measure for Measure</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Importance_of_Being_Earnest" title="The Importance of Being Earnest">The Importance of Being Earnest</a></i>, <i>Love for Love</i>, <i>The Tempest</i>, <i>Twelfth Night</i>, and <i>MacBeth</i>. Featuring in many of these were <a href="/wiki/Charles_Laughton" title="Charles Laughton">Charles Laughton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elsa_Lanchester" title="Elsa Lanchester">Elsa Lanchester</a>). 
</p><p>In 1933 <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Korda" title="Alexander Korda">Alexander Korda</a> gave Mason a small role in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Private_Life_of_Don_Juan" title="The Private Life of Don Juan">The Private Life of Don Juan</a></i> but sacked him three days into shooting.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_films">Early films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Mason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1935 to 1938, he starred in many British <a href="/wiki/Cinematograph_Films_Act_1927" title="Cinematograph Films Act 1927">quota quickies</a>, starting with his first film <i><a href="/wiki/Late_Extra" title="Late Extra">Late Extra</a></i> (1935), in which he played the lead. Albert Parker directed.
</p><p>Mason went on to appear in <i><a href="/wiki/Twice_Branded" title="Twice Branded">Twice Branded</a></i> (1936); <i><a href="/wiki/Troubled_Waters_(1936_film)" title="Troubled Waters (1936 film)">Troubled Waters</a></i> (1936), also directed by Parker; <i><a href="/wiki/Prison_Breaker" title="Prison Breaker">Prison Breaker</a></i> (1936); <i><a href="/wiki/Blind_Man%27s_Bluff_(1936_film)" title="Blind Man&#39;s Bluff (1936 film)">Blind Man's Bluff</a></i> (1936), for Parker' <i><a href="/wiki/The_Secret_of_Stamboul" class="mw-redirect" title="The Secret of Stamboul">The Secret of Stamboul</a></i> (1936), and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Mill_on_the_Floss_(film)" title="The Mill on the Floss (film)">The Mill on the Floss</a></i> (1936), an "A" movie.
</p><p>Mason had a key support role in Korda's <i><a href="/wiki/Fire_Over_England" title="Fire Over England">Fire Over England</a></i> (1937) with <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Olivier" title="Laurence Olivier">Laurence Olivier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vivien_Leigh" title="Vivien Leigh">Vivien Leigh</a>. He was in another "A", <i><a href="/wiki/The_High_Command" title="The High Command">The High Command</a></i> (1937) directed by <a href="/wiki/Thorold_Dickinson" title="Thorold Dickinson">Thorold Dickinson</a> then went back to quickies, starring in <i><a href="/wiki/Catch_as_Catch_Can_(1937_film)" title="Catch as Catch Can (1937 film)">Catch As Catch Can</a></i> (1937), directed by <a href="/wiki/Roy_Kellino" title="Roy Kellino">Roy Kellino</a>.
</p><p>Korda used him again as the villain in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Return_of_the_Scarlet_Pimpernel" title="The Return of the Scarlet Pimpernel">The Return of the Scarlet Pimpernel</a></i> (1937).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Mason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mason began appearing in some televised productions of plays, made in the very early days of television: <i><a href="/wiki/Cyrano_de_Bergerac_(play)" title="Cyrano de Bergerac (play)">Cyrano de Bergerac</a></i> (1938), <i>The Moon in the Yellow River</i> (1938), <i>Bees on the Boat-Deck</i> (1939), <i>Square Pegs</i> (1939), <i>L’Avare</i> (1939), and <i>The Circle</i> (1939).
</p><p>He returned to features with <i><a href="/wiki/I_Met_a_Murderer" title="I Met a Murderer">I Met a Murderer</a></i> (1939) based on a story by Mason and Pamela Kellino, who also starred with Mason and who he would marry. Her then-husband <a href="/wiki/Roy_Kellino" title="Roy Kellino">Roy Kellino</a> directed.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_Two">World War Two</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Mason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: World War Two">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>He registered as a <a href="/wiki/Conscientious_objector" title="Conscientious objector">conscientious objector</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a><sup id="cite_ref-Thomson_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thomson-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> (causing his family to break with him for many years), but his tribunal exempted him only on the requirement to do non-combatant military service, which he refused; his appeal against this became irrelevant by including him in a general exemption for film work.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1941-42 he returned to the stage to appear in <i>Jupiter Laughs</i> by <a href="/wiki/A.J._Cronin" class="mw-redirect" title="A.J. Cronin">A.J. Cronin</a>.
</p><p>He established himself as a leading man in Britain in a series of films: <i><a href="/wiki/The_Patient_Vanishes" class="mw-redirect" title="The Patient Vanishes">The Patient Vanishes</a></i> (1941); <i><a href="/wiki/Hatter%27s_Castle_(film)" title="Hatter&#39;s Castle (film)">Hatter's Castle</a></i> (1941) with Robert Newton and <a href="/wiki/Deborah_Kerr" title="Deborah Kerr">Deborah Kerr</a>; <i><a href="/wiki/The_Night_Has_Eyes" title="The Night Has Eyes">The Night Has Eyes</a></i> (1941); <i><a href="/wiki/Alibi_(1942_film)" title="Alibi (1942 film)">Alibi</a></i> (1942) with <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Lockwood" title="Margaret Lockwood">Margaret Lockwood</a>; <i><a href="/wiki/Secret_Mission" title="Secret Mission">Secret Mission</a></i> (1942); <i><a href="/wiki/Thunder_Rock_(film)" title="Thunder Rock (film)">Thunder Rock</a></i> (1942) with <a href="/wiki/Michael_Redgrave" title="Michael Redgrave">Michael Redgrave</a>; and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bells_Go_Down" title="The Bells Go Down">The Bells Go Down</a></i> (1943) with <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Trinder" title="Tommy Trinder">Tommy Trinder</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gainsborough_melodramas_and_stardom">Gainsborough melodramas and stardom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Mason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Gainsborough melodramas and stardom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mason became hugely popular for his brooding anti-heroes in the <a href="/wiki/Gainsborough_Pictures" title="Gainsborough Pictures">Gainsborough</a> <a href="/wiki/Gainsborough_melodramas" title="Gainsborough melodramas">series of melodramas</a> of the 1940s, starting with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Man_in_Grey" title="The Man in Grey">The Man in Grey</a></i> (1943). The film was a huge hit and launched him and co-stars Lockwood, <a href="/wiki/Stewart_Granger" title="Stewart Granger">Stewart Granger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Phyllis_Calvert" title="Phyllis Calvert">Phyllis Calvert</a>, to top level stars.
</p><p>Mason starred in two war time dramas, <i><a href="/wiki/They_Met_in_the_Dark" title="They Met in the Dark">They Met in the Dark</a></i> (1943) and <i><a href="/wiki/Candlelight_in_Algeria" title="Candlelight in Algeria">Candlelight in Algeria</a></i> (1944), then returned to Gainbsorough melodrama with <i><a href="/wiki/Fanny_by_Gaslight_(film)" title="Fanny by Gaslight (film)">Fanny By Gaslight</a></i> (1944) with Granger and Calvert; it was another big hit.
</p><p>Mason starred in <i><a href="/wiki/Hotel_Reserve" title="Hotel Reserve">Hotel Reserve</a></i> (1944), a thriller, then did a ghost story for Gainsborough with Lockwood, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Place_of_One%27s_Own" title="A Place of One&#39;s Own">A Place of One's Own</a></i> (1945). Far more popular was a melodrama, <i><a href="/wiki/They_Were_Sisters" title="They Were Sisters">They Were Sisters</a></i> (1945).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sydney_Box" title="Sydney Box">Sydney Box</a> cast Mason in the lead of a musical melodrama, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Seventh_Veil" title="The Seventh Veil">The Seventh Veil</a></i> (1945) alongside Ann Todd. It was a huge success in Britain and the US and demand for Mason was at a fever pitch.  Exhibitors voted him the most popular star in <a href="/wiki/UK" class="mw-redirect" title="UK">Britain</a> in each year between 1944 and 1947. They also thought he was the most popular international star in 1946; he dropped to second place the following year.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> He was the most popular male star in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> in 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mason had a relatively minor role in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wicked_Lady" title="The Wicked Lady">The Wicked Lady</a></i> (1946) with Lockwood, a big hit. Mason then received his best reviews to date playing a mortally wounded <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army" title="Irish Republican Army">IRA</a> bank robber on the run in <a href="/wiki/Carol_Reed" title="Carol Reed">Carol Reed</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Odd_Man_Out" title="Odd Man Out">Odd Man Out</a></i> (1947).<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mason was able to turn producer on a film with Box, written by his wife and starring Mason, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Upturned_Glass" title="The Upturned Glass">The Upturned Glass</a></i> (1947). It was not a noted success. Neither was <i>Bathsheba</i>, a play he and his wife did on Broadway.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hollywood">Hollywood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Mason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hollywood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mason went to Hollywood where his first film was <i><a href="/wiki/Caught_(1949_film)" title="Caught (1949 film)">Caught</a></i> (1949), directed by <a href="/wiki/Max_Ophuls" class="mw-redirect" title="Max Ophuls">Max Ophuls</a>.  He played <a href="/wiki/Gustave_Flaubert" title="Gustave Flaubert">Gustave Flaubert</a> in MGM's <i><a href="/wiki/Madame_Bovary_(1949_film)" title="Madame Bovary (1949 film)">Madame Bovary</a></i> (1949).
</p><p>Mason did another with Ophuls, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Reckless_Moment" title="The Reckless Moment">The Reckless Moment</a></i> (1949), then did <i><a href="/wiki/East_Side,_West_Side_(1949_film)" title="East Side, West Side (1949 film)">East Side, West Side</a></i> (1949) with <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Stanwyck" title="Barbara Stanwyck">Barbara Stanwyck</a> at MGM and <i><a href="/wiki/One_Way_Street" title="One Way Street">One Way Street</a></i> (1950) at Universal. He made <i><a href="/wiki/Pandora_and_the_Flying_Dutchman" title="Pandora and the Flying Dutchman">Pandora and the Flying Dutchman</a></i> (1951) with <a href="/wiki/Ava_Gardner" title="Ava Gardner">Ava Gardner</a>. None of these films was particularly successful.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Desert_Fox_and_20th_Century_Fox"><i>The Desert Fox</i> and 20th Century Fox</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Mason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Desert Fox and 20th Century Fox">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mason's Hollywood career was revived when cast as <a href="/wiki/General_Rommel" class="mw-redirect" title="General Rommel">General Rommel</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Desert_Fox:_The_Story_of_Rommel" title="The Desert Fox: The Story of Rommel">The Desert Fox: The Story of Rommel</a></i> (1951), directed by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Hathaway" title="Henry Hathaway">Henry Hathaway</a>. To do the film he agreed to sign a contract with <a href="/wiki/20th_Century_Fox" title="20th Century Fox">20th Century Fox</a> for seven years at one film a year.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mason did a film at <a href="/wiki/Republic_Pictures" title="Republic Pictures">Republic Pictures</a> written by his wife and directed by Roy Kellino, <i><a href="/wiki/Lady_Possessed" title="Lady Possessed">Lady Possessed</a></i> (1951). At Fox he played a spy in <i><a href="/wiki/5_Fingers" title="5 Fingers">5 Fingers</a></i> (1951) directed by <a href="/wiki/Joseph_L._Mankiewicz" title="Joseph L. Mankiewicz">Joseph L. Mankiewicz</a>. 
</p><p>MGM hired him to play <a href="/wiki/Rupert_of_Hentzau" title="Rupert of Hentzau">Rupert of Hentzau</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prisoner_of_Zenda_(1952_film)" title="The Prisoner of Zenda (1952 film)">The Prisoner of Zenda</a></i> (1952) opposite Granger. He was in the lower budgeted <i><a href="/wiki/Face_to_Face_(1952_film)" title="Face to Face (1952 film)">Face to Face</a></i> (1952) then went to Paramont to play a villainous sea captain opposite <a href="/wiki/Alan_Ladd" title="Alan Ladd">Alan Ladd</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Botany_Bay_(film)" title="Botany Bay (film)">Botany Bay</a></i> (1953).
</p><p>Mason was one of many stars in MGM's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Story_of_Three_Loves" title="The Story of Three Loves">The Story of Three Loves</a></i> (1953). At Fox he reprised his role as Rommel in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Desert_Rats_(film)" title="The Desert Rats (film)">The Desert Rats</a></i> (1953), then he was reunited with Mankiewicz at MGM, playing Brutus in <i><a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar_(1953_film)" title="Julius Caesar (1953 film)">Julius Caesar</a></i> (1953), opposite <a href="/wiki/Marlon_Brando" title="Marlon Brando">Marlon Brando</a>. The film was very successful.
</p><p>Mason was reunited with Carol Reed in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Man_Between" title="The Man Between">The Man Between</a></i> (1954), then Fox used him as a villain again in <i><a href="/wiki/Prince_Valiant_(1954_film)" title="Prince Valiant (1954 film)">Prince Valiant</a></i> (1954).
</p><p>Mason did another film written by his wife and directed by his father in law, <i><a href="/wiki/Charade_(1954_film)" title="Charade (1954 film)">Charade</a></i> (1954).
</p><p>Warner Bros hired him to play <a href="/wiki/Judy_Garland" title="Judy Garland">Judy Garland</a>'s leading man in <i><a href="/wiki/A_Star_Is_Born_(1954_film)" title="A Star Is Born (1954 film)">A Star Is Born</a></i> (1954). He went over to Disney to play <a href="/wiki/Captain_Nemo" title="Captain Nemo">Captain Nemo</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/20,000_Leagues_Under_the_Sea_(1954_film)" title="20,000 Leagues Under the Sea (1954 film)">20,000 Leagues Under the Sea</a></i> (1954), a huge hit.
</p><p>Mason appeared with <a href="/wiki/Lucille_Ball" title="Lucille Ball">Lucille Ball</a> and <a href="/wiki/Desi_Arnaz" title="Desi Arnaz">Desi Arnaz</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Forever,_Darling" title="Forever, Darling">Forever, Darling</a></i> (1956) then starred in and produced a film at Fox, <i><a href="/wiki/Bigger_Than_Life" title="Bigger Than Life">Bigger Than Life</a></i> (1956), directed by <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Ray" title="Nicholas Ray">Nicholas Ray</a>. Mason played a small-town school teacher driven insane by the effects of cortisone. He did another for Fox, the hugely popular melodrama, <i><a href="/wiki/Island_in_the_Sun_(film)" title="Island in the Sun (film)">Island in the Sun</a></i> (1957).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television_2">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Mason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:North_by_Northwest_movie_trailer_screenshot_(27)_James_Mason.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/North_by_Northwest_movie_trailer_screenshot_%2827%29_James_Mason.jpg/170px-North_by_Northwest_movie_trailer_screenshot_%2827%29_James_Mason.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/North_by_Northwest_movie_trailer_screenshot_%2827%29_James_Mason.jpg/255px-North_by_Northwest_movie_trailer_screenshot_%2827%29_James_Mason.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/80/North_by_Northwest_movie_trailer_screenshot_%2827%29_James_Mason.jpg 2x" data-file-width="312" data-file-height="416" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:North_by_Northwest_movie_trailer_screenshot_(27)_James_Mason.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mason in Hitchcock's <i>North by Northwest</i> (1959)</div></div></div>
<p>Mason began appearing regularly on television in shows such as <i>Panic!</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/General_Electric_Theater" title="General Electric Theater">General Electric Theater</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Schlitz_Playhouse" class="mw-redirect" title="Schlitz Playhouse">Schlitz Playhouse</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Goodyear_Theatre" title="Goodyear Theatre">Goodyear Theatre</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Playhouse_90" title="Playhouse 90">Playhouse 90</a></i> (several episodes including <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Brown%27s_Raid_(1960_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="John Brown&#39;s Raid (1960 film) (page does not exist)">John Brown's Raid</a></i>). In the 1950s, Mason was host of <i><a href="/wiki/Lux_Video_Theatre" title="Lux Video Theatre">Lux Video Theatre</a></i> on CBS television.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He starred in two thrillers for <a href="/wiki/Andrew_L._Stone" title="Andrew L. Stone">Andrew L. Stone</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Cry_Terror!" title="Cry Terror!">Cry Terror!</a></i> (1958) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Decks_Ran_Red" title="The Decks Ran Red">The Decks Ran Red</a></i> (1958) then played a suave master spy in <i><a href="/wiki/North_by_Northwest" title="North by Northwest">North by Northwest</a></i> (1959) directed by <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Hitchcock" title="Alfred Hitchcock">Alfred Hitchcock</a>.
</p><p>At Fox he had a huge hit playing a determined scientist and explorer in <i><a href="/wiki/Journey_to_the_Center_of_the_Earth_(1959_film)" title="Journey to the Center of the Earth (1959 film)">Journey to the Centre of the Earth</a></i> (1959), taking over a role meant for <a href="/wiki/Clifton_Webb" title="Clifton Webb">Clifton Webb</a>.  He did a comedy <i><a href="/wiki/A_Touch_of_Larceny" title="A Touch of Larceny">A Touch of Larceny</a></i> (1960) and was <a href="/wiki/Sir_Edward_Carson" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Edward Carson">Sir Edward Carson</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Trials_of_Oscar_Wilde" title="The Trials of Oscar Wilde">The Trials of Oscar Wilde</a></i> (1960).
</p><p>He continued to appear on TV shows like <i><a href="/wiki/The_DuPont_Show_with_June_Allyson" title="The DuPont Show with June Allyson">The DuPont Show with June Allyson</a></i>, <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Golden_Showcase&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Golden Showcase (page does not exist)">Golden Showcase</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Theatre_%2762" title="Theatre &#39;62">Theatre '62</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Alfred_Hitchcock_Hour" class="mw-redirect" title="The Alfred Hitchcock Hour">The Alfred Hitchcock Hour</a></i>.
</p><p>He did <i><a href="/wiki/The_Marriage-Go-Round_(film)" title="The Marriage-Go-Round (film)">The Marriage-Go-Round</a></i> (1961), then played Humbert Humbert in <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kubrick" title="Stanley Kubrick">Stanley Kubrick</a>'s version of <i><a href="/wiki/Lolita_(1962_film)" title="Lolita (1962 film)">Lolita</a></i> (1962).
</p><p>He starred in <i><a href="/wiki/Tiara_Tahiti" title="Tiara Tahiti">Tiara Tahiti</a></i> (1962), then <i><a href="/wiki/Hero%27s_Island" title="Hero&#39;s Island">Hero's Island</a></i> (1962), which he also produced. He was in <i><a href="/wiki/Torpedo_Bay" title="Torpedo Bay">Torpedo Bay</a></i> (1963).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1960s">1960s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Mason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 1960s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1963 Mason settled in Switzerland, and embarked on a transatlantic career.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> He began to drift into support roles, or second leads: the epic <i><a href="/wiki/The_Fall_of_the_Roman_Empire_(film)" title="The Fall of the Roman Empire (film)">The Fall of the Roman Empire</a></i> (1964); <i><a href="/wiki/The_Pumpkin_Eater" title="The Pumpkin Eater">The Pumpkin Eater</a></i> (1964), with <a href="/wiki/Anne_Bancroft" title="Anne Bancroft">Anne Bancroft</a>; a river pirate who betrays <a href="/wiki/Peter_O%27Toole" title="Peter O&#39;Toole">Peter O'Toole</a>'s character in <i><a href="/wiki/Lord_Jim_(1965_film)" title="Lord Jim (1965 film)">Lord Jim</a></i> (1965); a Chinese noble in <i><a href="/wiki/Genghis_Khan_(1965_film)" title="Genghis Khan (1965 film)">Genghis Khan</a></i> (1965); <i><a href="/wiki/The_Uninhibited" title="The Uninhibited">The Uninhibited</a></i> (1965); a guest role on <i><a href="/wiki/Dr._Kildare_(TV_series)" title="Dr. Kildare (TV series)">Dr Kildare</a></i>; James Leamington in the <a href="/wiki/Swinging_London" class="mw-redirect" title="Swinging London">Swinging London</a>-set <i><a href="/wiki/Georgy_Girl" title="Georgy Girl">Georgy Girl</a></i> (1966), a role that earned him a second Academy Award nomination.
</p><p>He was in several episodes of <i><a href="/wiki/ITV_Play_of_the_Week" title="ITV Play of the Week">ITV Play of the Week</a></i> and he had the lead in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Deadly_Affair" title="The Deadly Affair">The Deadly Affair</a></i> (1967) for Sidney Lumet and <i><a href="/wiki/Stranger_in_the_House_(1967_film)" title="Stranger in the House (1967 film)">Stranger in the House</a></i> (1968). 
</p><p>He provided a supporting role in <i><a href="/wiki/Duffy_(film)" title="Duffy (film)">Duffy</a></i> (1968) and <i><a href="/wiki/Mayerling_(1968_film)" title="Mayerling (1968 film)">Mayerling</a></i> (1968) but was top billed in <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Sea_Gull_(1968_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="The Sea Gull (1968 film) (page does not exist)">The Sea Gull</a></i> (1968) for <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Lumet" title="Sidney Lumet">Sidney Lumet</a> and starred as Bradley Morahan in <i><a href="/wiki/Age_of_Consent_(film)" title="Age of Consent (film)">Age of Consent</a></i> (1969) for Michael Powell, which Mason also produced. He also had the star role in <i><a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Port_Wine" title="Spring and Port Wine">Spring and Port Wine</a></i> (1970).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1970s">1970s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Mason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 1970s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mason supported Charles Bronson in <i><a href="/wiki/Cold_Sweat_(1970_film)" title="Cold Sweat (1970 film)">Cold Sweat</a></i> (1970), <a href="/wiki/Alain_Delon" title="Alain Delon">Alain Delon</a> in <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Crepa_padrone,_crepa_tranquillo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Crepa padrone, crepa tranquillo (page does not exist)">Crepa padrone, crepa tranquillo</a></i> (1970) and <a href="/wiki/Lee_Van_Cleef" title="Lee Van Cleef">Lee Van Cleef</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Bad_Man%27s_River" title="Bad Man&#39;s River">Bad Man's River</a></i> (1971). He was a support in <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kill!_Kill!_Kill!_Kill!&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kill! Kill! Kill! Kill! (page does not exist)">Kill! Kill! Kill! Kill!</a></i> (1971) and top billed in <i><a href="/wiki/Child%27s_Play_(1972_film)" title="Child&#39;s Play (1972 film)">Child's Play</a></i> (1972) for Lumet, replacing Marlon Brando.
</p><p>He was one of many stars in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Last_of_Sheila" title="The Last of Sheila">The Last of Sheila</a></i> (1973) and played the evil <a href="/wiki/John_William_Polidori" title="John William Polidori">Doctor Polidori</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/Frankenstein:_The_True_Story" title="Frankenstein: The True Story">Frankenstein: The True Story</a></i> (1973). <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1980s">1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Mason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mason was in <i><a href="/wiki/North_Sea_Hijack" title="North Sea Hijack">North Sea Hijack</a></i> (1980), <i><a href="/wiki/Evil_Under_the_Sun_(film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Evil Under the Sun (film)">Evil Under the Sun</a></i> (1982), <i><a href="/wiki/Ivanhoe_(1982_film)" title="Ivanhoe (1982 film)">Ivanhoe</a></i> (1982), and <i><a href="/wiki/A_Dangerous_Summer" title="A Dangerous Summer">A Dangerous Summer</a></i> (1982).
</p><p>One of his last roles, that of the corrupt lawyer Ed Concannon in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Verdict" title="The Verdict">The Verdict</a></i> (1982), earned him his third and final Oscar nomination.
</p><p>He had parts in <i><a href="/wiki/Yellowbeard" title="Yellowbeard">Yellowbeard</a></i> (1983), <i><a href="/wiki/Alexandre_(film)" class="mw-redirect" title="Alexandre (film)">Alexandre</a></i> (1983), and <i><a href="/wiki/George_Washington_(miniseries)" title="George Washington (miniseries)">George Washington</a></i> (1984).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Narrator">Narrator</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Mason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Narrator">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1967 Mason narrated the documentary <i>The London Nobody Knows</i>. An ardent cinephile on top of his career interests, Mason then went on to narrate two British documentary series supervised by <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Brownlow" title="Kevin Brownlow">Kevin Brownlow</a>: <i><a href="/wiki/Hollywood_(1980_TV_series)" title="Hollywood (1980 TV series)">Hollywood</a></i> (1980), on the silent cinema and <i><a href="/wiki/Unknown_Chaplin" title="Unknown Chaplin">Unknown Chaplin</a></i> (1983), devoted to out-take material from the films of Sir <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Chaplin" title="Charlie Chaplin">Charlie Chaplin</a>. Mason had been a long-time neighbour and friend of the comedian. In the late 1970s, Mason became a mentor to up-and-coming actor <a href="/wiki/Sam_Neill" title="Sam Neill">Sam Neill</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Final_performances">Final performances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Mason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Final performances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Having completed playing the lead role in <i><a href="/wiki/Dr._Fischer_of_Geneva" class="mw-redirect" title="Dr. Fischer of Geneva">Dr. Fischer of Geneva</a></i> (1985), adapted from <a href="/wiki/Graham_Greene" title="Graham Greene">Graham Greene</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Fischer_of_Geneva" title="Doctor Fischer of Geneva">eponymous novella</a> for the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>, he stepped into the role in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Shooting_Party" title="The Shooting Party">The Shooting Party</a></i> originally meant for <a href="/wiki/Paul_Scofield" title="Paul Scofield">Paul Scofield</a>, who was unable to continue after being seriously injured in an accident on the first day of shooting. This was to be Mason's final screen performance in a feature film.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He did appear on TV in <i><a href="/wiki/A.D._(miniseries)" title="A.D. (miniseries)">A.D.</a></i> (1985) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Assisi_Underground" title="The Assisi Underground">The Assisi Underground</a></i> (1985).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Mason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:James_Mason_and_Family_1957.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/James_Mason_and_Family_1957.JPG/220px-James_Mason_and_Family_1957.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/James_Mason_and_Family_1957.JPG/330px-James_Mason_and_Family_1957.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/James_Mason_and_Family_1957.JPG/440px-James_Mason_and_Family_1957.JPG 2x" data-file-width="927" data-file-height="655" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:James_Mason_and_Family_1957.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mason and his family in 1957 in the television programme <i>Panic!</i> From left, son Morgan, Mason's wife Pamela, daughter Portland and Mason</div></div></div>
<p>Mason was a devoted lover of animals, particularly cats. He and his wife, <a href="/wiki/Pamela_Mason" title="Pamela Mason">Pamela Mason</a>, co-authored the book <i>The Cats in Our Lives</i>, which was published in 1949. James Mason wrote most of the book and also illustrated it. In <i>The Cats in Our Lives</i>, he recounted humorous and sometimes touching tales of the cats (as well as a few dogs) he had known and loved.
</p><p>In 1952, Mason purchased a house previously owned by <a href="/wiki/Buster_Keaton" title="Buster Keaton">Buster Keaton</a>. He discovered several <a href="/wiki/Nitrocellulose#Nitrate_film" title="Nitrocellulose">nitrate film</a> reels of films thought to have been lost, stored in the house and produced by the comedian, such as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Boat_(film)" title="The Boat (film)">The Boat</a></i>. Mason arranged to have the decomposing films transferred to <a href="/wiki/Cellulose_acetate_film" title="Cellulose acetate film">safety stock</a> and thus saved them from oblivion.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In his youth, Mason was a keen fan of his local <a href="/wiki/Rugby_League" class="mw-redirect" title="Rugby League">Rugby League</a> team, <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_Giants" title="Huddersfield Giants">Huddersfield</a>. In later years he also began to follow the fortunes of <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_Town" class="mw-redirect" title="Huddersfield Town">Huddersfield Town</a><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mason was married twice:
</p>
<ul><li>From 1941 to 1964 to British actress <a href="/wiki/Pamela_Mason" title="Pamela Mason">Pamela Mason</a> (née Ostrer) (1916–1996); one daughter, <a href="/wiki/Portland_Mason" title="Portland Mason">Portland Mason Schuyler</a> (1948–2004), and one son, <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Mason" title="Morgan Mason">Morgan</a> (who is married to <a href="/wiki/Belinda_Carlisle" title="Belinda Carlisle">Belinda Carlisle</a>, the lead singer of <a href="/wiki/The_Go-Go%27s" title="The Go-Go&#39;s">the Go-Go's</a>). Pamela Mason was widely reported to be a devotee of the Hollywood social scene and was frequently unfaithful to her husband. Nevertheless, she initiated divorce proceedings against him in 1962, claiming adultery on his part.  This led to a $1m divorce settlement, and made a star of her attorney Marvin Mitchelson.<sup id="cite_ref-simon_edge_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-simon_edge-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Australian actress <a href="/wiki/Clarissa_Kaye" title="Clarissa Kaye">Clarissa Kaye</a> (1971–his death). Tobe Hooper's DVD commentary for <i><a href="/wiki/Salem%27s_Lot_(1979_TV_miniseries)" class="mw-redirect" title="Salem&#39;s Lot (1979 TV miniseries)">Salem's Lot</a></i> reveals that Mason regularly worked contractual clauses into his later work guaranteeing Kaye bit parts in his film appearances.</li></ul>
<p>Mason's autobiography, <i>Before I Forget</i>, was published in 1981.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=James_Mason&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Mason survived a severe <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_infarction" title="Myocardial infarction">heart attack</a> in 1959.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> He died as result of another heart attack on 27 July 1984 in <a href="/wiki/Lausanne" title="Lausanne">Lausanne</a>, Switzerland<sup id="cite_ref-WVobit_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WVobit-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> and was <a href="/wiki/Cremated" class="mw-redirect" title="Cremated">cremated</a>.
</p><p>Mason left his entire estate to his second wife, Clarissa Kaye, but his will was challenged by his two children. The lawsuit had not been settled when she died on 21 July 1994 from cancer.<sup id="cite_ref-simon_edge_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-simon_edge-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Clarissa Kaye Mason left her holdings to the religious guru <a href="/wiki/Sathya_Sai_Baba" title="Sathya Sai Baba">Sathya Sai Baba</a>, including the actor's ashes which she had retained in their shared home. Mason's children sued Sai Baba and subsequently had Mason's ashes interred in <a href="/wiki/Corsier-sur-Vevey" title="Corsier-sur-Vevey">Corsier-sur-Vevey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vaud" title="Vaud">Vaud</a>, Switzerland.<sup id="cite_ref-Davies_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The remains of Mason's friend <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Chaplin" title="Charlie Chaplin">Charlie Chaplin</a> are in a tomb a few steps away.<sup id="cite_ref-Davies_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Davies-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Mason's children specified that his headstone read: "Never say in grief you are sorry he's gone. Rather, say in thankfulness you are grateful he was here," words that were spoken to Portland Mason by U.S. Senator <a href="/wiki/Edward_Kennedy" class="mw-redirect" title="Edward Kennedy">Ted Kennedy</a> after the actor's death.<sup id="cite_ref-Dan_Glaister_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dan_Glaister-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
