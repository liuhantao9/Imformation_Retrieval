<p>A <b>plantation economy</b> is an economy based on <a href="/wiki/Agricultural" class="mw-redirect" title="Agricultural">agricultural</a> <a href="/wiki/Mass_production" title="Mass production">mass production</a>, usually of a few <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">commodity</a> crops grown on large farms called <a href="/wiki/Plantation" title="Plantation">plantations</a>. Plantation economies rely on the <a href="/wiki/Export" title="Export">export</a> of <a href="/wiki/Cash_crop" title="Cash crop">cash crops</a> as a source of income. Prominent crops included <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">cotton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rubber" class="mw-redirect" title="Rubber">rubber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sugar_cane" class="mw-redirect" title="Sugar cane">sugar cane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ficus" title="Ficus">figs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ceiba_pentandra" title="Ceiba pentandra">kapok</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sisal" title="Sisal">sisal</a>, and species in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Indigofera" title="Indigofera">Indigofera</a></i>, used to produce <a href="/wiki/Indigo_dye" title="Indigo dye">indigo dye</a>.
</p><p>The longer a crop's <a href="/wiki/Harvest" title="Harvest">harvest</a> period, the more efficient plantations become. <a href="/wiki/Economies_of_scale" title="Economies of scale">Economies of scale</a> are also achieved when the distance to market is long. Plantation crops usually need processing immediately after harvesting. Sugarcane, <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">tea</a>, sisal, and <a href="/wiki/Palm_oil" title="Palm oil">palm oil</a> are most suited to plantations, while coconuts, rubber, and cotton are suitable to a lesser extent.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conditions_for_formation">Conditions for formation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantation_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Conditions for formation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Plantation economies are factory-like, industrialised and centralised forms of agriculture, owned by large corporations or affluent owners. Under normal circumstances, plantation economies are not as efficient as small farm holdings, since there is immense difficulty in proper supervision of labour over a large land area. However, when there are large distances between the plantations and their markets, expensive processing machinery to reduce the bulk of the crop greatly lowers shipping costs. This allows large plantations which produce large quantities of the good to achieve economies of scale, as the per unit cost of processing is greatly diminished. This economy of scale can be achieved best with tropical crops that are harvested continuously through the year, fully utilising the processing machinery. Examples of crops that are suitable to be processed are sugar, sisal, palm oil, and tea. <sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="North_American_plantations">North American plantations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantation_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: North American plantations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Plantations_in_the_American_South" title="Plantations in the American South">Plantations in the American South</a></div>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Thirteen_Colonies" title="Thirteen Colonies">Thirteen Colonies</a>, plantations were concentrated in the South. These colonies included Maryland, Virginia, North Carolina, South Carolina, and Georgia. They had good <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a> and almost year-round growing seasons, ideal for crops such as <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">rice</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tobacco" title="Tobacco">tobacco</a>. The existence of many <a href="/wiki/Waterway" title="Waterway">waterways</a> in the region made transportation easier. Each colony specialized in one or two crops, with Virginia standing out in tobacco production<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slavery">Slavery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantation_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Slavery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_United_States" title="Slavery in the United States">Slavery in the United States</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slavery_in_the_colonial_United_States" title="Slavery in the colonial United States">Slavery in the colonial United States</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Slaves_cutting_the_sugar_cane_-_Ten_Views_in_the_Island_of_Antigua_(1823),_plate_IV_-_BL.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Slaves_cutting_the_sugar_cane_-_Ten_Views_in_the_Island_of_Antigua_%281823%29%2C_plate_IV_-_BL.jpg/220px-Slaves_cutting_the_sugar_cane_-_Ten_Views_in_the_Island_of_Antigua_%281823%29%2C_plate_IV_-_BL.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Slaves_cutting_the_sugar_cane_-_Ten_Views_in_the_Island_of_Antigua_%281823%29%2C_plate_IV_-_BL.jpg/330px-Slaves_cutting_the_sugar_cane_-_Ten_Views_in_the_Island_of_Antigua_%281823%29%2C_plate_IV_-_BL.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Slaves_cutting_the_sugar_cane_-_Ten_Views_in_the_Island_of_Antigua_%281823%29%2C_plate_IV_-_BL.jpg/440px-Slaves_cutting_the_sugar_cane_-_Ten_Views_in_the_Island_of_Antigua_%281823%29%2C_plate_IV_-_BL.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1854" data-file-height="1290" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Slaves_cutting_the_sugar_cane_-_Ten_Views_in_the_Island_of_Antigua_(1823),_plate_IV_-_BL.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Sugar_plantations_in_the_Caribbean" title="Sugar plantations in the Caribbean">Sugar plantation</a> in the British colony of <a href="/wiki/Antigua" title="Antigua">Antigua</a>, 1823</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Planter_(American_South)" class="mw-redirect" title="Planter (American South)">Planters</a> embraced the use of <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slaves</a> mainly because <a href="/wiki/Indentured_labor" class="mw-redirect" title="Indentured labor">indentured labor</a> became expensive. Some indentured servants were also leaving to start their own farms as land was widely available. Colonists tried to use <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a> for labor, but they were susceptible to European diseases and <a href="/wiki/Population_history_of_the_indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" class="mw-redirect" title="Population history of the indigenous peoples of the Americas">died in large numbers</a>. The plantation owners then turned to enslaved <a href="/wiki/Africans" class="mw-redirect" title="Africans">Africans</a> for labor. In 1665, there were fewer than 500 Africans in Virginia but by 1750, 85 percent of the 235,000 slaves lived in the Southern colonies, Virginia included. Africans made up 40 percent of the South’s population.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/1840_United_States_Census" title="1840 United States Census">1840 United States Census</a>, one out of every four families in Virginia owned slaves. There were over 100 plantation owners who owned over 100 slaves.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The number of slaves in the  15 States was just shy of 4 million in a total population 12.4 million and the percentage was 32% of the population. 
</p>
<ul><li>Number of slaves in the Lower South:   2,312,352 (47% of total population) 4,919 million.</li>
<li>Number of slaves in the Upper South:   1,208,758 (29% of total population) 4,165 million.</li>
<li>Number of slaves in the Border States: 432,586    (13% of total population) 3,323 million.</li></ul>
<p>Fewer than one-third of Southern families owned slaves at the peak of slavery prior to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Civil_War" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Civil War">Civil War</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Mississippi" title="Mississippi">Mississippi</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a> the figure approached one half. The total number of slave owners was 385,000 (including, in <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">Louisiana</a>, some <a href="/wiki/Free_African_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Free African American">free African Americans</a>), amounting to approximately 3.8% of the Southern and Border states population.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tobacco.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Tobacco.jpg/220px-Tobacco.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="326" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Tobacco.jpg/330px-Tobacco.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ed/Tobacco.jpg 2x" data-file-width="432" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Tobacco.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tobacco field</div></div></div>
<p>On a plantation with more than 100 slaves, the capital value of the slaves was greater than the capital value of the land and farming implements.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Atlantic_slave_trade">Atlantic slave trade</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantation_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Atlantic slave trade">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">Atlantic slave trade</a></div>
<p>Enslaved Africans were brought from Africa by the English and other European powers, for their Western Hemisphere colonies. They were shipped from <a href="/wiki/Port" title="Port">ports</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a> to the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a>. The journey from Africa across the Atlantic Ocean was called "the <a href="/wiki/Middle_passage" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle passage">middle passage</a>", and was one of the three legs which comprised the [trade] among the continents of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">Americas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>.
</p><p>By some estimates, it is said that some ten million <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_groups_of_Africa" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethnic groups of Africa">Africans</a> were brought to the Americas. Only about 6% ended up in the North American colonies, while the majority were taken to the <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">Caribbean</a> colonies and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>. A reason many did not make it to the colonies at all was disease and illness. Underneath the slave ship's decks, Africans were held chest-to-chest and could not do much moving. There was waste and urine throughout the hold; this caused the captives to get sick and to die from illnesses that could not be cured.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As the plantation economy expanded, the slave trade grew to meet the growing demand for labor.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_Revolution_in_Europe">Industrial Revolution in Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantation_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Industrial Revolution in Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a></div> 
<p><a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">Western Europe</a> was the final destination for the plantation produce. At this time, Europe was starting to <a href="/wiki/Industrialize" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrialize">industrialize</a>, and it needed a lot of materials to manufacture goods. Being the power center of the world at the time, they exploited the New World and Africa to industrialize. Africa supplied slaves for the plantations; the New World produced <a href="/wiki/Raw_material" title="Raw material">raw material</a> for industries in Europe. Manufactured goods, of higher value, were then sold both to Africa and the New World. The system was largely run by European merchants<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sugar_plantations">Sugar plantations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantation_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sugar plantations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sugar_cane_workers_resting_1a34016v.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Sugar_cane_workers_resting_1a34016v.jpg/220px-Sugar_cane_workers_resting_1a34016v.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="153" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Sugar_cane_workers_resting_1a34016v.jpg/330px-Sugar_cane_workers_resting_1a34016v.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Sugar_cane_workers_resting_1a34016v.jpg/440px-Sugar_cane_workers_resting_1a34016v.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="713" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sugar_cane_workers_resting_1a34016v.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sugar cane workers in <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>, 1941</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sugar" title="Sugar">Sugar</a> has a <a href="/wiki/History_of_sugar" title="History of sugar">long history</a> as a plantation crop. Cultivation of sugar had to follow a precise <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_science" title="Agricultural science">scientific system</a> to profit from the production. Sugar plantations everywhere were disproportionate consumers of labor, often enslaved, because of the high mortality of the plantation laborers. In Brazil, plantations were called <i><a href="/wiki/Casa_grande_(sugar_plantation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Casa grande (sugar plantation)">casas grandes</a></i> and suffered from similar issues.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">slaves</a> working the sugar plantation were caught in an unceasing rhythm of arduous labor year after year. <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">Sugarcane</a> is harvested about 18 months after planting and the plantations usually divided their land for efficiency. One plot was lying <a href="/wiki/Fallow" class="mw-redirect" title="Fallow">fallow</a>, one plot was growing cane, and the final plot was being harvested. During the December–May <a href="/wiki/Rainy_season" class="mw-redirect" title="Rainy season">rainy season</a>, slaves planted, <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">fertilized</a> with animal dung, and <a href="/wiki/Weed_control" title="Weed control">weeded</a>. From January to June, they harvested the cane by chopping the plants off close to the ground, stripping the leaves and then cutting them into shorter strips to be bundled off to be sent to the <a href="/wiki/Sugar_cane_mill" class="mw-redirect" title="Sugar cane mill">sugar cane mill</a>.
</p><p>In the mill, the cane was crushed using a three-roller mill. The juice from the crushing of the cane was then boiled or clarified until it <a href="/wiki/Crystallized" class="mw-redirect" title="Crystallized">crystallized</a> into sugar. Some plantations also went a step further and <a href="/wiki/Distilled" class="mw-redirect" title="Distilled">distilled</a> the <a href="/wiki/Molasses" title="Molasses">molasses</a>, the liquid left after the sugar is boiled or clarified, to make <a href="/wiki/Rum" title="Rum">rum</a>. The sugar was then shipped back to <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. For the slave laborer, the routine started all over again.
</p><p>With the 19th-century <a href="/wiki/Abolition_of_slavery" class="mw-redirect" title="Abolition of slavery">abolition of slavery</a>, plantations continued to grow sugar cane, but <a href="/wiki/Sugar_beet" title="Sugar beet">sugar beets</a>, which can be grown in <a href="/wiki/Temperate_climate" title="Temperate climate">temperate climates</a>, increased their share of the sugar market.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indigo_plantations">Indigo plantations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Plantation_economy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Indigo plantations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Indigofera" title="Indigofera">Indigofera</a> was a major crop cultivated during the 18th century, in <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a>, <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">Guatemala</a>—and <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">Haiti</a> until the slave <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Revolution" title="Haitian Revolution">rebellion against France</a> that left them <a href="/wiki/Embargo" class="mw-redirect" title="Embargo">embargoed</a> by Europe and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> in the 19th and 20th centuries. The indigo crop was grown for making blue <a href="/wiki/Indigo_dye" title="Indigo dye">indigo dye</a> in the pre-industrial age.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mahatma_Gandhi" title="Mahatma Gandhi">Mahatma Gandhi</a>'s investigation of indigo workers' claims of exploitation led to the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Champaran" title="Champaran">Champaran</a> Agrarian Bill in 1917 by the British colonial government.
</p>

<p>The currency for the Georgia colony was the <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">Pound sterling</a>, a gold coin or very light green bill with the national pound symbol. The currency is worth about one and one half U.S. dollar.
</p><p>The currency for the New York was at the time of the colony the <a href="/wiki/New_York_pound" title="New York pound">New York pound</a>. This currency was primarily used and made in the 1700s.
</p>
