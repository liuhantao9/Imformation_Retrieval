<p>The <b>Huddersfield Narrow Canal</b> is an <a href="/wiki/Navigability" title="Navigability">inland</a> <a href="/wiki/Waterway" title="Waterway">waterway</a> in <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">northern England</a>. It runs just under 20 miles (32&#160;km) from Lock 1E at the rear of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Huddersfield" title="University of Huddersfield">University of Huddersfield</a> campus, near <a href="/wiki/Aspley,_West_Yorkshire" title="Aspley, West Yorkshire">Aspley</a> Basin in <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>, to the junction with the <a href="/wiki/Ashton_Canal" title="Ashton Canal">Ashton Canal</a> at Whitelands Basin in <a href="/wiki/Ashton-under-Lyne" title="Ashton-under-Lyne">Ashton-under-Lyne</a>. It crosses the <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennines</a> by means of 74 <a href="/wiki/Lock_(water_transport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lock (water transport)">locks</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Standedge_Tunnels" title="Standedge Tunnels">Standedge Tunnel</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Building_the_Canal">Building the Canal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huddersfield_Narrow_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Building the Canal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Planning">Planning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huddersfield_Narrow_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Planning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The canal was first proposed in 1793 at a meeting in the <a href="/wiki/George_Hotel,_Huddersfield" title="George Hotel, Huddersfield">George Hotel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>. Its engineer was <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Outram" title="Benjamin Outram">Benjamin Outram</a> on the recommendation of <a href="/wiki/William_Jessop" title="William Jessop">William Jessop</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> His plan was to start from the <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_Broad_Canal" title="Huddersfield Broad Canal">Huddersfield Broad Canal</a> and follow the <a href="/wiki/River_Colne,_West_Yorkshire" title="River Colne, West Yorkshire">River Colne</a> with a climb of 438 feet (134&#160;m) to its summit where it would pass through a tunnel at <a href="/wiki/Standedge" title="Standedge">Standedge</a> before descending through <a href="/wiki/Saddleworth" title="Saddleworth">Saddleworth</a> and the Tame valley to the <a href="/wiki/Ashton_Canal" title="Ashton Canal">Ashton Canal</a> near <a href="/wiki/Ashton-under-Lyne" title="Ashton-under-Lyne">Ashton-under-Lyne</a>. There were many <a href="/wiki/Woollen" class="mw-redirect" title="Woollen">woollen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worsted" title="Worsted">worsted</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cotton_mill" title="Cotton mill">cotton mills</a> along its route which promised ample trade. However, there was the possible problem of the loss of their water supplies and so Outram proposed to build a number of reservoirs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huddersfield_Narrow_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Construction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Construction began in 1794 with the marking out of the route. The practice was to set up a line of pegs or stakes about 50 yards (46&#160;m) apart so that their tops would indicate the intended water level. It would then be possible to construct the appropriate embankments and cuttings.
</p><p>As engineer, Outram provided an oversight of the work but he was also occupied by other projects. Day-to-day management was carried out by contractors employed and organised by the canal company committee. Progress was slow and erratic. It was also unfortunate that Outram was seriously ill for long periods between 1795 and 1797. The company was also short of money, partly because the costs had been seriously underestimated but also because shareholders were not honouring their pledges.
</p><p>In 1799, severe floods damaged earthworks along the canal and various <a href="/wiki/Reservoir" title="Reservoir">reservoirs</a>. In particular, overflow of the Tunnelend Reservoir devastated the village of <a href="/wiki/Marsden,_West_Yorkshire" title="Marsden, West Yorkshire">Marsden</a>. Two aqueducts were also destroyed, diverting the company's already-stretched funds. The Stakes Aqueduct was already in use and had to be replaced immediately. Outram had built it of stone and, due to its low height, it had needed to be constructed in four short spans. The narrow openings had impeded the unprecedented overflow and Outram replaced it with a single span cast iron structure, similar to the Holmes Aqueduct on the <a href="/wiki/Derby_Canal" title="Derby Canal">Derby Canal</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Stakes_Aqueduct.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Stakes_Aqueduct.jpg/220px-Stakes_Aqueduct.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Stakes_Aqueduct.jpg/330px-Stakes_Aqueduct.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Stakes_Aqueduct.jpg/440px-Stakes_Aqueduct.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="759" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Stakes_Aqueduct.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Outram's Stakes aqueduct at Stalybridge</div></div></div>
<p>Outram set out to overcome the problems with the Holmes Aqueduct by making the walls thicker where they joined the baseplates which were also thicker. However a major stress was the compressive force along the top of the wall plates where they bow outwards or inwards. In 1875 cross bracings were added to reinforce it. The Stakes Aqueduct is the oldest surviving aqueduct of its type that is still in use for its original purpose.
</p>
<div style="clear:left;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Standedge_Tunnel">The Standedge Tunnel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huddersfield_Narrow_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Standedge Tunnel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Standedge_Tunnels" title="Standedge Tunnels">Standedge Tunnels</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Standedge_Tunnel_End,_Marsden,_West_Yorkshire.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Standedge_Tunnel_End%2C_Marsden%2C_West_Yorkshire.jpg/220px-Standedge_Tunnel_End%2C_Marsden%2C_West_Yorkshire.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Standedge_Tunnel_End%2C_Marsden%2C_West_Yorkshire.jpg/330px-Standedge_Tunnel_End%2C_Marsden%2C_West_Yorkshire.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Standedge_Tunnel_End%2C_Marsden%2C_West_Yorkshire.jpg/440px-Standedge_Tunnel_End%2C_Marsden%2C_West_Yorkshire.jpg 2x" data-file-width="965" data-file-height="643" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Standedge_Tunnel_End,_Marsden,_West_Yorkshire.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Eastern portal</div></div></div>
<p>Although the canal uses 74 locks to climb and descend the <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennines</a>, there would have had to be many more without the digging of a very long tunnel through the Tame/Colne watershed (the <a href="/wiki/River_Colne,_West_Yorkshire" title="River Colne, West Yorkshire">River Colne</a> flowing down to Huddersfield and the <a href="/wiki/River_Calder,_West_Yorkshire" title="River Calder, West Yorkshire">River Calder</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/River_Tame,_Greater_Manchester" title="River Tame, Greater Manchester">Tame</a> flowing down to <a href="/wiki/Stockport" title="Stockport">Stockport</a> and the <a href="/wiki/River_Mersey" title="River Mersey">River Mersey</a>). The canal tunnel is 5,700 yards (5,200&#160;m) long making it the longest canal tunnel in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. It is largely brick lined but in some places the tunnel has been left with a natural rock surface.
</p>
<div style="clear:left;"></div>
<h3><span id="The_.22Black_Flood.22"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="The_&quot;Black_Flood&quot;">The "Black Flood"</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huddersfield_Narrow_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The &quot;Black Flood&quot;">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1810, the <a href="/wiki/Diggle,_Greater_Manchester" title="Diggle, Greater Manchester">Diggle</a> Moss Reservoir gave way and Marsden was again flooded along with much of the <a href="/wiki/Colne_Valley" title="Colne Valley">Colne Valley</a>. Houses and factories were wrecked and five people lost their lives. The force of the water was such that a fifteen-ton rock was carried 2 miles (3.2&#160;km) down the valley.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Completion">Completion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huddersfield_Narrow_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Completion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite multiple problems, the building of the Huddersfield Narrow Canal showed that the technique of quantity surveying had advanced greatly. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Telford" title="Thomas Telford">Thomas Telford</a>'s report during the construction of the Standedge Tunnel covered every expenditure to the last bucket; it was followed to the letter and the canal finally opened in 1811.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Operation">Operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huddersfield_Narrow_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The canal operated for approximately 140 years. Although it was moderately successful for a while, its width (limited to boats less than 7&#160;ft (2.1&#160;m) wide), the large number of locks and the long Standedge Tunnel made it much less profitable than its main rival the <a href="/wiki/Rochdale_Canal" title="Rochdale Canal">Rochdale Canal</a> which had a similar number of locks but was twice as wide with no long tunnel.
</p><p>Standedge Tunnel proved to be a bottleneck having been constructed without a towpath. <a href="/wiki/Narrowboat" title="Narrowboat">Narrowboats</a> had to be 'legged' through, eventually by professionally employed leggers. A company employee would chain the tunnel entrance behind a convoy of boats and walk over Boat Lane, accompanied by boat boys and girls leading the boat horses, to unchain the opposite end of the tunnel before the boat convoy arrived. This journey was made at least twice per day for over twenty years.
</p><p>The construction of a double railway tunnel parallel to Standedge canal tunnel adversely affected canal revenues and the canal was mostly abandoned in 1944 (a short stretch in Huddersfield survived until closure in 1963).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Restoration">Restoration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huddersfield_Narrow_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Restoration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After 27 years of campaigning and restoration by the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://huddersfieldcanal.com/">Huddersfield Canal Society</a>, the canal was fully re-opened to navigation in 2001 when it again became one of three Pennine crossings, the others being the Rochdale and the <a href="/wiki/Leeds_and_Liverpool_Canal" title="Leeds and Liverpool Canal">Leeds and Liverpool</a> (both broad canals). The canal is now entirely used by leisure boaters.
</p><p>During the period of time when the canal was closed, several lengths were culverted and infilled and in some cases built over. Over the course of the restoration project, the vast majority of the obliterated line became available to be opened out again and the canal remains on a substantially identical alignment with some minor alterations.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huddersfield_Narrow_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Huddersfield">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Huddersfield Narrow Canal connects with the Huddersfield Broad Canal at the eastern end of Lock 1E. Lock 1E is to the west of the A629 Wakefield Road and the canal had been filled in beneath the road. A new bridge was built in 1985 but the width was only sufficient for narrowboats and so the 300 yards (270&#160;m) section of broad canal from the bridge to Lock 1E is not accessible to vessels wider than 7 feet (2.1&#160;m). The bridge thus forms the effective, but unofficial, boundary between the Huddersfield Narrow Canal and the Huddersfield Broad Canal.
</p><p>Due to a legal quirk, the stretch of the canal from Lock 1E to Queen Street South Bridge was not included in the original abandonment of the canal. Whilst locks on the remainder of the canal were capped, cascaded or demolished, locks 1E and 2E remained gated but eventually fell into dereliction. The old gates were only removed when canal restoration work started.
</p><p>Two factories, Bates and Sellers Engineering, had been built immediately upstream of locks 2E and 3E respectively on the line of the canal and blocking it. In each case, the solution was the same: the lock was relocated upstream of the factory and a tunnel constructed below the factory. This avoided disruption to the firms using the sites. The remodelling of the canal can be clearly seen between the former site of Lock 2E and Queen Street South Bridge where a framework of girders sits above the channel to ensure that the deep piling remains secure.
</p><p>Eleven years later, Sellers Engineering relocated to a new site. This enabled Lock 3E to be relocated again, this time to a site near the original Lock 3E. The stretch of canal previously in a tunnel under the factory was brought back to the surface and become a feature of the Huddersfield Waterfront development.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slaithwaite">Slaithwaite</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huddersfield_Narrow_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Slaithwaite">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The section of canal through <a href="/wiki/Slaithwaite" title="Slaithwaite">Slaithwaite</a> town centre, between locks 21E and 23E, had been culverted and a car park covered part of the route.
</p><p>Restoration caused some local controversy as it involved the felling of a row of mature cherry trees which had been planted along the infilled line although the local authority claimed that the trees were in poor condition and were unlikely to last for many more years in any case.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Lock 21E was relocated a short distance upstream, to the other side of Platt Lane, in order to ensure that the Platt Lane crossing could be achieved by a conventional bridge instead of the swing bridge that had existed prior to closure.
</p><p>The stretch from 22E to 23E threads its way through an extremely tight space. Britannia Road Bridge was formerly hump backed but changes in traffic levels since closure rendered such a bridge impractical and instead the pound from 22E to 23E was lowered by 12 inches (300&#160;mm).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hartshead_Power_Station">Hartshead Power Station</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huddersfield_Narrow_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Hartshead Power Station">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_Narrow_Canal_Pylon" title="Huddersfield Narrow Canal Pylon">Huddersfield Narrow Canal Pylon</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hudders_Staly_Pylon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Hudders_Staly_Pylon.jpg/175px-Hudders_Staly_Pylon.jpg" decoding="async" width="175" height="234" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Hudders_Staly_Pylon.jpg/263px-Hudders_Staly_Pylon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Hudders_Staly_Pylon.jpg/350px-Hudders_Staly_Pylon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="483" data-file-height="645" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hudders_Staly_Pylon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Electricity Pylon straddling the canal at <a href="/wiki/Stalybridge" title="Stalybridge">Stalybridge</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>On the western side of the Pennines, the canal runs through the legs of an <a href="/wiki/Electricity_pylon" class="mw-redirect" title="Electricity pylon">electricity pylon</a> at <a href="/wiki/Heyrod" title="Heyrod">Heyrod</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Stalybridge" title="Stalybridge">Stalybridge</a>.
</p><p>The pylon had been erected during the period when the canal was closed and the only viable route for restoration was through the legs.
</p><p>No other such cases are known on navigable waterways worldwide although other pylons have been constructed across former waterways that have been filled in with rubble and soil such as the <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Bolton_%26_Bury_Canal#Restoration" title="Manchester Bolton &amp; Bury Canal">Manchester, Bolton and Bury Canal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wey_and_Arun_Canal" title="Wey and Arun Canal">Wey and Arun Canal at Rowner Lock</a>.
</p>
<div style="clear:left;"></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_canal_today">The canal today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Huddersfield_Narrow_Canal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The canal today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Huddersfield Narrow is part of the <a href="/wiki/South_Pennine_Ring" title="South Pennine Ring">South Pennine Ring</a> which is a circular route crossing the <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennines</a> twice - the other crossing is the <a href="/wiki/Rochdale_Canal" title="Rochdale Canal">Rochdale Canal</a>. The canals are linked at the western (Lancashire) end by the <a href="/wiki/Ashton_Canal" title="Ashton Canal">Ashton Canal</a> and at the eastern (Yorkshire) end by the <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_Broad_Canal" title="Huddersfield Broad Canal">Huddersfield Broad Canal</a> and a length of the <a href="/wiki/Calder_and_Hebble_Navigation" title="Calder and Hebble Navigation">Calder and Hebble Navigation</a>. The South Pennine Ring takes in <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golcar" title="Golcar">Golcar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slaithwaite" title="Slaithwaite">Slaithwaite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marsden,_West_Yorkshire" title="Marsden, West Yorkshire">Marsden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saddleworth" title="Saddleworth">Saddleworth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Diggle,_Greater_Manchester" title="Diggle, Greater Manchester">Diggle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uppermill" title="Uppermill">Uppermill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenfield,_Greater_Manchester" title="Greenfield, Greater Manchester">Greenfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mossley" title="Mossley">Mossley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stalybridge" title="Stalybridge">Stalybridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ashton-under-Lyne" title="Ashton-under-Lyne">Ashton-under-Lyne</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Failsworth" title="Failsworth">Failsworth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rochdale" title="Rochdale">Rochdale</a>, <a href="/wiki/Littleborough,_Greater_Manchester" title="Littleborough, Greater Manchester">Littleborough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Todmorden" title="Todmorden">Todmorden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hebden_Bridge" title="Hebden Bridge">Hebden Bridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sowerby_Bridge" title="Sowerby Bridge">Sowerby Bridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elland" title="Elland">Elland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brighouse" title="Brighouse">Brighouse</a>.
</p><p>The Huddersfield Canal area is also a <a href="/wiki/Site_of_Special_Scientific_Interest" title="Site of Special Scientific Interest">Site of Special Scientific Interest</a>.
</p>
