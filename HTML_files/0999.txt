<p><b>Wolfson College</b> <span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;w&#39; in &#39;wind&#39;">w</span><span title="/ʊ/: &#39;u&#39; in &#39;push&#39;">ʊ</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="&#39;f&#39; in &#39;find&#39;">f</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span><span title="/ən/: &#39;on&#39; in &#39;button&#39;">ən</span></span>/</a></span></span> is a <a href="/wiki/Colleges_of_the_University_of_Cambridge" title="Colleges of the University of Cambridge">constituent college</a> of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a> in <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>, England. The majority of students at the college are <a href="/wiki/Postgraduate_education" title="Postgraduate education">postgraduates</a>. The college also admits <a href="/wiki/Adult_learner" title="Adult learner">"mature"</a> <a href="/wiki/Undergraduate_education" title="Undergraduate education">undergraduates</a> (aged 21 and above), with around 15 per cent of students studying undergraduate degree courses at the university. The college was founded in 1965 as "<b>University College</b>", but was refounded as Wolfson College in 1973 in recognition of the benefaction of the <a href="/wiki/Wolfson_Foundation" title="Wolfson Foundation">Wolfson Foundation</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Wolfson is located to the south-west of Cambridge city centre, near the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University_Library" title="Cambridge University Library">University Library</a>.
</p><p>As one of the more modern colleges in Cambridge, Wolfson does not follow all of the traditions of some of the university's older colleges. For example, since the college's founding there has been no "<a href="/wiki/High_Table" class="mw-redirect" title="High Table">High Table</a>" reserved for Fellows at <a href="/wiki/Formal_Hall" class="mw-redirect" title="Formal Hall">Formal Hall</a> dinners; students and Fellows mix and dine together, although the tradition of wearing academic gowns to such occasions is still compulsory like in many other Cambridge colleges. Both Fellows and students at the college have access to all the facilities. With students from over 70 countries, Wolfson claims to be one of Cambridge's most cosmopolitan colleges.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It was the first college of the university to admit men and women as both students and <a href="/wiki/Research_fellow" title="Research fellow">Fellows</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The current President of Wolfson College is scientist <a href="/wiki/Jane_Clarke_(scientist)" title="Jane Clarke (scientist)">Jane Clarke</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wolfson_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>, the number of graduates of other universities who went to Cambridge to do research increased significantly. The university therefore decided to found <b>University College</b> in 1965 to help accommodate these students. The college was based at Bredon House, a property built in the early twentieth century by <a href="/wiki/John_Stanley_Gardiner" title="John Stanley Gardiner">John Stanley Gardiner</a>, who was a Professor of <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">Zoology</a> at the university from 1909 to 1937. He donated the house, with its long narrow garden running from <a href="/wiki/Barton_Road,_Cambridge" title="Barton Road, Cambridge">Barton Road</a> to Selwyn Gardens, to the university upon his death in 1946. The college then purchased further property on its eastern boundary.
</p><p>University College was opened on 30 June 1965 as a college for postgraduate students, with the classicist <a href="/wiki/John_Sinclair_Morrison" title="John Sinclair Morrison">John Sinclair Morrison</a> as its first President. At the time, all the undergraduate colleges of the university were single-sex institutions and University College was the first in the university to admit men and women as both students and Fellows. The college, from its outset, set out to be a cosmopolitan and egalitarian institution with distinct differences from the older, more traditional Cambridge colleges, with no <a href="/wiki/Common_Room_(university)" title="Common Room (university)">Senior Combination Room</a>, no "High Table" reserved for Fellows at formal dinners in the college and no portraits hung in the Dining Hall.
</p><p>The college's founding deed required the college to either find an endowment within 10 years or face dissolution. In 1972, the Wolfson Foundation agreed to provide a capital endowment and help to fund the construction of central buildings around Bredon House and the college's East and West Courts. In recognition of this, the college was renamed <b>Wolfson College</b> on 1 January 1973.
</p><p>The new buildings (designed by the architect <a href="/wiki/A._Michael_Mennim" title="A. Michael Mennim">Michael Mennim</a>) were opened by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth II</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Prince_Philip,_Duke_of_Edinburgh" title="Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh">Duke of Edinburgh</a> in 1977. Though most of the college's buildings are modern, the design of the campus is similar to that of the university's older colleges, with buildings grouped around two main courts. The floor of the entrance hall to the main building is made of thin slices of granite taken from the old <a href="/wiki/London_Bridge" title="London Bridge">London Bridge</a> (the main section of which was taken to <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">Arizona</a> to be rebuilt in the late 1960s).
</p><p>Further building and acquisition of neighbouring properties has continued. In the 1980s, the college purchased the house and garden owned by <a href="/wiki/Vivian_Fuchs" title="Vivian Fuchs">Sir Vivian Fuchs</a> on the western side on the college. Plommer House on the northern side of the college was also left to the college in his will by Dr Hugh Plommer, a founding Fellow of the college. The acquisition of property has allowed for the building of a number of new facilities, mainly funded by donations from philanthropic foundations and individuals. Other major benefactions have come from the <a href="/wiki/Fairleigh_Dickinson_Jr." title="Fairleigh Dickinson Jr.">Fairleigh Dickinson</a> Foundation and the Toda Foundation. In the 1990s, with the help of the <a href="/wiki/Gatsby_Charitable_Foundation" title="Gatsby Charitable Foundation">Gatsby Foundation</a>, the college purchased the "western field" on which was built the Chancellor's Centre and further residential blocks.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Buildings_and_Grounds">Buildings and Grounds</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wolfson_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Buildings and Grounds">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Lee_Library">The Lee Library</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wolfson_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Lee Library">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Opened in 1994, the Lee Seng Tee Library (Lee Library) was donated to the college by the Singaporean businessman and philanthropist <a href="/wiki/Lee_Seng_Tee" title="Lee Seng Tee">Dr Lee Seng Tee</a> (whose daughter studied at the college). Dr Lee also donated the funds used to build the Lee Seng Tee Hall. The library was designed by architects Brewer, Smith and Brewer. The ground floor foyer is home to a scale model of the <a href="/wiki/Armillary_sphere" title="Armillary sphere">armillary sphere</a> on the roof of the <a href="/wiki/Beijing_Ancient_Observatory" title="Beijing Ancient Observatory">Peking Observatory</a>. On the first floor there is a bust of Tan Sri Dr Lee Kong Chian, father of the benefactor. The library comprises over 60 quiet places to work and is open 24 hours a day all year, apart from the 25–26 December and 1 January.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The college is located a short walk from the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_University_Library" title="Cambridge University Library">University Library</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2018, students at the college were evacuated from the Lee library, and a number of accommodation blocks, after an unexploded WWII <a href="/wiki/Grenade" title="Grenade">grenade</a> was found in the President's Garden. There were no injuries and it was soon discovered that the device was a practice grenade with no live ammunition.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="College_Gardens">College Gardens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wolfson_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: College Gardens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the first half of the twentieth century, much of the land on which the college is now situated comprised gardens which belonged to houses in <a href="/wiki/Selwyn_College,_Cambridge" title="Selwyn College, Cambridge">Selwyn Gardens</a>. As the college grew particular natural landmarks, such as the <a href="/wiki/Mulberry_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Mulberry tree">mulberry tree</a> outside the Porters' lodge, were retained as features. The garden comprises a wide variety of trees, scented shrubs, <a href="/wiki/Conifers" class="mw-redirect" title="Conifers">dwarf conifers</a>, as well as a distinctive range of shrubs with colourful stems.
</p><p>The college's head gardener occasionally provides a guided tour of the gardens to local interested groups. During the summer, Wolfson opens its gardens to the public as part of the <a href="/wiki/National_Gardens_Scheme" title="National Gardens Scheme">NGS</a> Open Gardens network.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_life">Student life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wolfson_College,_Cambridge&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Student life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The college is known for its entertainment events and performances, which attract visitors from many other colleges of the university. These activities include formal dinners, concerts, dancing nights and music displays.
</p><p>The Club Room, at the heart of the college, includes the college bar and a dance floor space. It is the main communal space for students, and is the venue for many entertainment events.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Wolfson_College_Boat_Club_(Cambridge)" title="Wolfson College Boat Club (Cambridge)">Wolfson College Boat Club</a> is a popular society, and Wolfson is one of the strongest graduate rowing colleges in Cambridge.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
