<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bear_Butte_Scan_0001.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Bear_Butte_Scan_0001.jpg/220px-Bear_Butte_Scan_0001.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="139" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Bear_Butte_Scan_0001.jpg/330px-Bear_Butte_Scan_0001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Bear_Butte_Scan_0001.jpg/440px-Bear_Butte_Scan_0001.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="756" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bear_Butte_Scan_0001.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bear_Butte" title="Bear Butte">Bear Butte</a>, in South Dakota, is a sacred site for over 30 <a href="/wiki/Plains_tribes" class="mw-redirect" title="Plains tribes">Plains tribes</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><b>Native American religions</b> are the spiritual practices of the <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">indigenous peoples of the Americas</a>.  This article focuses on Native North Americans.  Traditional Native American ceremonial ways can vary widely and are based on the differing histories and beliefs of individual tribes, clans, and bands. Early European explorers describe individual <a href="/wiki/Tribe_(Native_American)" title="Tribe (Native American)">Native American tribes</a> and even small bands as each having their own religious practices. Theology may be <a href="/wiki/Monotheism" title="Monotheism">monotheistic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polytheism" title="Polytheism">polytheistic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Henotheism" title="Henotheism">henotheistic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Animism" title="Animism">animistic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shamanism" title="Shamanism">shamanistic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pantheism" title="Pantheism">pantheistic</a> or any combination thereof, among others. Traditional beliefs are usually passed down in the forms of <a href="/wiki/Oral_history" title="Oral history">oral histories</a>, stories, allegories, and principles, and rely on face to face teaching in one's family and community.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span></h2>
<p>From the 1600s, European Catholic and Protestant denominations sent missionaries to convert the tribes to Christianity.  Some of these conversions occurred through government and Christian church cooperative efforts that forcibly removed Native American children from their families into a Christian/state government-operated system of <a href="/wiki/American_Indian_boarding_schools" title="American Indian boarding schools">American Indian boarding schools</a> (aka The Residential Schools) where Native children were taught European Christian beliefs, the values of mainstream white culture, and the English language. This forcible conversion and suppression of Indigenous languages and cultures continued through the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As part of the US government's suppression of traditional Indigenous religions, most ceremonial ways were banned for over 80 years by a series of US Federal laws that banned traditional <a href="/wiki/Sweat_lodge" title="Sweat lodge">sweat lodge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sun_dance" class="mw-redirect" title="Sun dance">sun dance</a> ceremonies, among others.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  This government persecution and prosecution continued until 1978 with the passage of the <a href="/wiki/American_Indian_Religious_Freedom_Act" title="American Indian Religious Freedom Act">American Indian Religious Freedom Act</a> (AIRFA).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some non-Native anthropologists estimate membership in traditional Native American religions in the 21st century to be about 9000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Since Native Americans practicing traditional ceremonies do not usually have public organizations or membership rolls, these "members" estimates are likely substantially lower than the actual numbers of people who participate in traditional ceremonies. Native American spiritual leaders also note that these academic estimates substantially underestimate the numbers of participants because a century of US Federal government persecution and prosecutions of traditional ceremonies caused believers to practice their religions in secrecy. Many adherents of traditional spiritual ways also attend Christian services, at least some of the time, which can also affect statistics. Since the 80 years of those prior legal persecutions ended with AIRFA, some sacred sites in the United States are now <a href="/wiki/Protected_area" title="Protected area">protected areas</a> under law.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_Native_American_religions">Major Native American religions</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Earth_Lodge_Religion">Earth Lodge Religion</span></h3>
<p>The Earth Lodge Religion was founded in northern California and southern Oregon tribes such as the <a href="/wiki/Wintun" title="Wintun">Wintun</a>. It spread to tribes such as the <a href="/wiki/Achomawi" title="Achomawi">Achomawi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shasta_(tribe)" class="mw-redirect" title="Shasta (tribe)">Shasta</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Siletz_(tribe)" class="mw-redirect" title="Siletz (tribe)">Siletz</a>, to name a few. It was also known as the "Warm House Dance" among the <a href="/wiki/Pomo" title="Pomo">Pomo</a>. It predicted occurrences similar to those predicted by the Ghost Dance, such as the return of ancestors or the world's end. The Earth Lodge Religion impacted the later religious practice, the Dream Dance, belonging to the <a href="/wiki/Klamath_people" title="Klamath people">Klamath</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Modoc_people" title="Modoc people">Modoc</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-w230_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-w230-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ghost_Dances">Ghost Dances</span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ghost_dance.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Ghost_dance.jpg/220px-Ghost_dance.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Ghost_dance.jpg/330px-Ghost_dance.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Ghost_dance.jpg/440px-Ghost_dance.jpg 2x" data-file-width="650" data-file-height="415" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ghost_dance.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1891 Sioux Ghost Dance. Ghost Dances influenced many Native American religions.</div></div></div>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Ghost_Dance" title="Ghost Dance">Ghost Dance</a>" is a very general term that encompasses different religious revitalization movements in the Western United States. In 1870, a Ghost Dance was founded by the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Paiute" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Paiute">Paiute</a> prophet <a href="/wiki/Wodziwob" title="Wodziwob">Wodziwob</a>, and in 1889–1890, a Ghost Dance Religion was founded by <a href="/wiki/Wovoka" title="Wovoka">Wovoka</a> (Jack Wilson), who was also a <a href="/wiki/Northern_Paiute" class="mw-redirect" title="Northern Paiute">Northern Paiute</a>. The Ghost Dance was meant to serve as a connection with traditional ways of life and to honor the dead while predicting their resurrection.<sup id="cite_ref-w230_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-w230-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 1888, Wovoka, who was thought to be the son of the medicine man Tavibo (Numu-tibo'o), fell sick with a fever during an eclipse of the sun, which occurred on January 1, 1889. Upon his recovery, he claimed that he had visited the <a href="/wiki/Spirit_world_(Spiritualism)" title="Spirit world (Spiritualism)">spirit world</a> and the Supreme Being and predicted that the world would soon end, then be restored to a pure aboriginal state in the presence of the Messiah. All Native Americans would inherit this world, including those who were already dead, in order to live eternally without suffering. In order to reach this reality, Wovoka stated that all Native Americans should live honestly, and shun the ways of whites (especially the consumption of alcohol). He called for meditation, prayer, singing, and dancing as an alternative to mourning the dead, for they would soon resurrect. Wovoka's followers saw him as a form of the <a href="/wiki/Messianism" title="Messianism">messiah</a> and he became known as the "Red Man's Christ."
</p><p>Tavibo had participated in the Ghost Dance of 1870 and had a similar vision of the Great Spirit of Earth removing all white men, and then of an earthquake removing all human beings. Tavibo's vision concluded that Native Americans would return to live in a restored environment and that only believers in his revelations would be resurrected.
This religion spread to many tribes on reservations in the West, including the <a href="/wiki/Shoshone" title="Shoshone">Shoshone</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arapaho" title="Arapaho">Arapaho</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cheyenne" title="Cheyenne">Cheyenne</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sioux" title="Sioux">Sioux</a> (Dakota, Lakota, and Nakota). In fact, some bands of Lakota and Dakota were so desperate for hope during wartime that they strengthened their militancy after making a pilgrimage to Nevada in 1889–1890. They provided their own understanding to the Ghost Dance which included the prediction that the white people would disappear. A Ghost Dance gathering at <a href="/wiki/Wounded_Knee_Massacre" title="Wounded Knee Massacre">Wounded Knee</a> in December 1890 was invaded by the Seventh Cavalry, who massacred unarmed Lakota and Dakota people.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The earliest Ghost Dance heavily influenced religions such as the Earth Lodge, Bole-Maru Religion, and the Dream Dance. The <a href="/wiki/Caddo_Nation" class="mw-redirect" title="Caddo Nation">Caddo Nation</a> still practices the Ghost Dance today.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_Shaker_Religion">Indian Shaker Religion</span></h3>
<p>Also known as Tschida, the Indian Shaker Religion was influenced by the Waashat Religion and founded by <a href="/wiki/John_Slocum" title="John Slocum">John Slocum</a>, a Squaxin Island member. The name comes from the shaking and twitching motions used by the participants to brush off their sins. The religion combines <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> with traditional Indian teachings. This religion is still practiced today in the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Shaker_Church" title="Indian Shaker Church">Indian Shaker Church</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-w230_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-w230-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Longhouse_Religion">Longhouse Religion</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Longhouse_Religion" title="Longhouse Religion">Longhouse Religion</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Iroquois_longhouse.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Iroquois_longhouse.jpg/220px-Iroquois_longhouse.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Iroquois_longhouse.jpg/330px-Iroquois_longhouse.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Iroquois_longhouse.jpg/440px-Iroquois_longhouse.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="800" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Iroquois_longhouse.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>This replica of a Six Nations (Haudenosaunee) longhouse represents where the traditional practices take place.</div></div></div>
<p>The Longhouse Religion is the popular name of the religious movement known as The Code of Handsome Lake or Gaihwi:io ("Good Message"), founded in 1799 by the <a href="/wiki/Seneca_people" title="Seneca people">Seneca</a> prophet <a href="/wiki/Handsome_Lake" title="Handsome Lake">Handsome Lake</a> (Sganyodaiyoˀ).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> This movement combines and reinterprets elements of traditional Iroquois religious beliefs with elements adopted from Christianity, primarily from the <a href="/wiki/Quakers" title="Quakers">Quakers</a>. Gaihwi:io currently has about 5,000 practicing members. Originally the Gaihwi:io was known as the "new religion" in opposition to the prevailing animistic beliefs, but has since become known as the "old religion" in opposition to Christianity.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexicayotl">Mexicayotl</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Mexicayotl" title="Mexicayotl">Mexicayotl</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Maya_calendar_(Hunab-Ku).svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Maya_calendar_%28Hunab-Ku%29.svg/150px-Maya_calendar_%28Hunab-Ku%29.svg.png" decoding="async" width="150" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Maya_calendar_%28Hunab-Ku%29.svg/225px-Maya_calendar_%28Hunab-Ku%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Maya_calendar_%28Hunab-Ku%29.svg/300px-Maya_calendar_%28Hunab-Ku%29.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="500" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Maya_calendar_(Hunab-Ku).svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mesoamerican symbol widely used by the <a href="/wiki/Mexicas" class="mw-redirect" title="Mexicas">Mexicas</a> as a representation of <a href="/wiki/Ometeotl" class="mw-redirect" title="Ometeotl">Ometeotl</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Mexicayoal (<a href="/wiki/Nahuatl" title="Nahuatl">Nahuatl</a> word meaning "Essence of the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Mexican people">Mexican</a>", "Mexicanity"; <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a>: <i>Mexicanidad</i>; see <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-yotl" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:-yotl">-yotl</a></i>) is a movement reviving the indigenous <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philosophy" title="Philosophy">philosophy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Traditions" class="mw-redirect" title="Traditions">traditions</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerican_chronology" title="Mesoamerican chronology">ancient Mexico</a> (<a href="/wiki/Aztec_religion" title="Aztec religion">Aztec religion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aztec_philosophy" title="Aztec philosophy">Aztec philosophy</a>) amongst the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Mexican people">Mexican people</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Yolotl_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yolotl-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The movement came to light in the 1950s, led by <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">Mexico City</a> intellectuals, but has grown significantly on a <a href="/wiki/Grassroots" title="Grassroots">grassroots</a> level only in more recent times, also spreading to the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Mexican American">Chicanos</a> of <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Rostas_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rostas-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Their rituals involve the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mitotiliztli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mitotiliztli (page does not exist)">mitotiliztli</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Luna_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luna-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The followers, called <i>Mexicatl</i> (singular) and <i>Mexicah</i> (plural), or simply <i><a href="/wiki/Mexica" title="Mexica">Mexica</a></i>, are mostly urban and suburban people.<sup id="cite_ref-Rostas_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rostas-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Mexicayotl movement started in the 1950s with the founding of the group <i>Nueva Mexicanidad</i> by <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Velasco_Pi%C3%B1a" title="Antonio Velasco Piña">Antonio Velasco Piña</a>. In the same years, Rodolfo Nieva López founded the <i>Movimiento Confederado Restaurador de la Cultura del Anáhuac</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> the co-founder of which was <a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_Jimenez_Sanchez&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Francisco Jimenez Sanchez (page does not exist)">Francisco Jimenez Sanchez</a> who in later decades became a spiritual leader of the Mexicayotl movement, endowed with the <a href="/wiki/Honorific" title="Honorific">honorific</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Tlacaelel" title="Tlacaelel">Tlacaelel</a></i>. He had a deep influence in shaping the movement, founding the <a href="/w/index.php?title=In_Kaltonal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="In Kaltonal (page does not exist)">In Kaltonal</a> ("House of the Sun", also called Native Mexican Church) in the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>From the 1970s onwards Mexcayotl has grown developing in a web of local worship and community groups (called <i><a href="/wiki/Calpulli" title="Calpulli">calpulli</a></i> or <i>kalpulli</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-Rostas_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rostas-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and spreading to the <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Americans" title="Mexican Americans">Mexican Americans</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chicanos" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicanos">Chicanos</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. It has also developed strong ties with Mexican national identity movements and <a href="/wiki/Chicano_nationalism" title="Chicano nationalism">Chicano nationalism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Sanchez's Native Mexican Church (which is a confederation of calpullis) was officially recognized by the government of <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Native_American_Church">Native American Church</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Native_American_Church" title="Native American Church">Native American Church</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Flowering_peyote_cactus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Flowering_peyote_cactus.jpg/220px-Flowering_peyote_cactus.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Flowering_peyote_cactus.jpg/330px-Flowering_peyote_cactus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Flowering_peyote_cactus.jpg/440px-Flowering_peyote_cactus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Flowering_peyote_cactus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Peyote's illegal status in the United States prevents non-Natives from participating in peyote ceremonies.</div></div></div>
<p>The Peyote Religion legally termed and more properly known as the Native American Church has also been called the "Peyote Road" or the "Peyote Way", is a religious tradition involving the ceremonial and sacred use of <i><a href="/wiki/Lophophora_williamsii" class="mw-redirect" title="Lophophora williamsii">Lophophora williamsii</a></i> (peyote).<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Use of peyote for religious purposes is thousands of years old and some have thought it to have originated within one of the following tribes: the Carrizo, the <a href="/wiki/Lipan_Apache_people" title="Lipan Apache people">Lipan Apache</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mescalero_Apache" class="mw-redirect" title="Mescalero Apache">Mescalero Apache</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tonkawa" title="Tonkawa">Tonkawa</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Karankawa" class="mw-redirect" title="Karankawa">Karankawa</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Caddo" title="Caddo">Caddo</a>, with the Plains Cree, Carrizo, and the Lipan Apache being the three most likely sources. In <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> the <a href="/wiki/Huichol" title="Huichol">Huichol</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tepehu%C3%A1n" title="Tepehuán">Tepehuán</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Native_Mexicans" class="mw-redirect" title="Native Mexicans">Native Mexicans</a> use peyote.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Since then, despite several efforts to make peyote ceremonies illegal, ceremonial peyote use has spread from the Mexico area to Oklahoma and other western parts of the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Notable <a href="/wiki/Native_American_Church" title="Native American Church">Native American Church</a> (NAC) members include <a href="/wiki/Quannah_Parker" class="mw-redirect" title="Quannah Parker">Quannah Parker</a>, the founder of the NAC, and Big Moon of the <a href="/wiki/Kiowa" title="Kiowa">Kiowa</a> tribe.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Waashat_Religion">Waashat Religion</span></h3>
<table class="box-Cleanup_rewrite plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg" class="image"><img alt="Crystal Clear app kedit.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg/40px-Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg.png" decoding="async" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg/60px-Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg/80px-Crystal_Clear_app_kedit.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="128" data-file-height="128" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section may need to be <b>rewritten</b> to comply with Wikipedia's <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style">quality standards</a>, as it is unclear what are different versions of the faith, and what are simply different names. Change over time appears to be mostly disregarded. Important elements are not explained (At least some are covered in the references.). <p><span id="Prophet_Dance"></span><span id="Spirit_Dance"></span>The Waashat Religion is also called the Washani Religion, Longhouse Religion, Seven Drum Religion, Sunday Dance Religion, Prophet Dance, and Dreamer Faith. The <a href="/wiki/Wanapum" title="Wanapum">Wanapam Indian</a> <a href="/wiki/Smohalla" title="Smohalla">Smohalla</a> (c. 1815–1895) used wáashat rituals to build the religion in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a>. Smohalla claimed that visions came to him through dreams and that he had visited the spirit world and had been sent back to teach his people. The name waasaní spoke to what the religion was about; it meant both dancing and worship.<sup id="cite_ref-dance_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dance-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> He led a <a href="/wiki/Revitalization_movement" title="Revitalization movement">return to the original way of life</a> before white influences and established ceremonial music and dancing. Smohalla's speaking was called Yuyunipitqana for "Shouting Mountain".<sup id="cite_ref-w230_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-w230-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Dreamer Faith, and its elements of dancing, foreshadowed the later Ghost Dances of the plains peoples.<sup id="cite_ref-dance_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dance-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dance_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dance-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> It was a back&#8211;to&#8211;our&#8211;heritage religion.<sup id="cite_ref-dance_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dance-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Believers thought that white people would disappear and nature would return to the way it was before they came.<sup id="cite_ref-dance_23-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dance-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> To achieve this, the Natives must do the things required by the spirits, like a <a href="/wiki/Weyekin" title="Weyekin">Weyekin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dance_23-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dance-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> What the spirits wanted was to throw off violent ways, cast off white culture, and not buy, sell or disrespect the Earth.<sup id="cite_ref-dance_23-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dance-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> They must also dance the <b>Prophet Dance</b> (wáashat).<sup id="cite_ref-dance_23-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dance-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The religion combined elements of Christianity with Native beliefs, but it rejected white-American culture.<sup id="cite_ref-dance_23-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dance-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> This made it difficult to assimilate or control the tribes by the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-dance_23-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dance-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> The U.S. was trying to convert the <a href="/wiki/Plains_Indians" title="Plains Indians">Plains tribes</a> from hunter-gatherers to farmers, in the European-American tradition.<sup id="cite_ref-dance_23-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dance-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> They wanted to remake the Natives but found a problem with those who followed the Dreamer Cult: "Their model of a man is an Indian&#160;...They aspire to be Indian and nothing else."<sup id="cite_ref-dance_23-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dance-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Prophets of the movement included Smohalla (of the Wanapam people), Kotiakan (of the Yakama nation) and Homli (of the Walla Walla).<sup id="cite_ref-dance_23-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dance-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Their messages were carried along the Columbia River to other communities.<sup id="cite_ref-dance_23-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dance-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> It is unclear exactly how it started or when <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> influenced the earlier form, but it is thought to have something to do with the arrival of non-Indians or an epidemic and a prophet with an apocalyptic vision. The Waashat Dance involves seven drummers, a salmon feast, use of eagle and swan feathers and a sacred song sung every seventh day.<sup id="cite_ref-w230_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-w230-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ceremonies">Ceremonies</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sun_Dance">Sun Dance</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Sun_Dance" title="Sun Dance">Sun Dance</a></div>
<p>The sun dance is a religious ceremony practiced by a number of <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native American</a> and <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">First Nations</a> Peoples, primarily those of the <a href="/wiki/Plains_Indians" title="Plains Indians">Plains Nations</a>. Each tribe that has some type of sun dance ceremony has their own distinct practices and ceremonial protocols. In many cases, the ceremony is held in a private and is not open to the public. Most details of the ceremony are kept from public knowledge out of great respect for, and the desire for protection of, the traditional ways. Many of the ceremonies have featured in common, such as specific dances and songs passed down through many generations, the use of traditional drums, the sacred pipe, praying, fasting and, in some cases, the piercing of the skin.
</p><p>In Canada, the Plains Cree call this ceremony the Thirst Dance; the Saulteaux (Plains <a href="/wiki/Ojibwe" title="Ojibwe">Ojibwe</a>) call it the Rain Dance; and the Blackfoot (<i>Siksika</i>, <i>Kainai</i>, and <i>Piikani</i>) call it the Medicine Dance. It is also practiced by the Canadian <a href="/wiki/Dakota_people" title="Dakota people">Dakota</a> and <a href="/wiki/Assiniboine_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Assiniboine people">Nakoda</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Dene" title="Dene">Dene</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_leaders">Religious leaders</span></h2>
<p>Leaders in Native religions include <a href="/wiki/Pop%C3%A9" title="Popé">Popé</a>, who led the  <a href="/wiki/Pueblo_Revolt" title="Pueblo Revolt">Pueblo revolt in 1675</a>, <a href="/wiki/Quautlatas" title="Quautlatas">Quautlatas</a>, who inspired the <a href="/wiki/Tepehuan_Revolt" class="mw-redirect" title="Tepehuan Revolt">Tepehuan Revolt</a> against the Spanish in 1616, <a href="/wiki/Neolin" title="Neolin">Neolin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tenskwatawa" title="Tenskwatawa">Tenskwatawa</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kenekuk&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kenekuk (page does not exist)">Kenekuk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Smohalla" title="Smohalla">Smohalla</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Slocum" title="John Slocum">John Slocum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wovoka" title="Wovoka">Wovoka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_Elk" title="Black Elk">Black Elk</a> and many others.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ten-sq%C3%BAat-a-way.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Ten-sq%C3%BAat-a-way.jpg/175px-Ten-sq%C3%BAat-a-way.jpg" decoding="async" width="175" height="213" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Ten-sq%C3%BAat-a-way.jpg/263px-Ten-sq%C3%BAat-a-way.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Ten-sq%C3%BAat-a-way.jpg/350px-Ten-sq%C3%BAat-a-way.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1151" data-file-height="1400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ten-sq%C3%BAat-a-way.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Tenskwatawa, by <a href="/wiki/George_Catlin" title="George Catlin">George Catlin</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>From time to time important religious leaders organized revivals. In Indiana in 1805, <a href="/wiki/Tenskwatawa" title="Tenskwatawa">Tenskwatawa</a>  (called the Shawnee Prophet by Americans) led a religious revival following a smallpox epidemic and a series of witch-hunts. His beliefs were based on the earlier teachings of the <a href="/wiki/Lenape" title="Lenape">Lenape</a> prophets, <a href="/wiki/Scattamek" title="Scattamek">Scattamek</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neolin" title="Neolin">Neolin</a>, who predicted a coming apocalypse that would destroy the European-American settlers.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>  Tenskwatawa urged the tribes to reject the ways of the Americans: to give up firearms, liquor, American style clothing, to pay traders only half the value of their debts, and to refrain from ceding any more lands to the United States. The revival led to warfare led by his brother <a href="/wiki/Tecumseh" title="Tecumseh">Tecumseh</a> against the white settlers.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Congressional_legislation_affecting_Native_American_religion">Congressional legislation affecting Native American religion</span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:PeyoteWayCleanHwy.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/PeyoteWayCleanHwy.jpg/200px-PeyoteWayCleanHwy.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="277" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/PeyoteWayCleanHwy.jpg/300px-PeyoteWayCleanHwy.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/PeyoteWayCleanHwy.jpg/400px-PeyoteWayCleanHwy.jpg 2x" data-file-width="723" data-file-height="1000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:PeyoteWayCleanHwy.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sign for a <a href="/wiki/Native_American_church" class="mw-redirect" title="Native American church">Native American church</a></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="American_Indian_Religious_Freedom_Act">American Indian Religious Freedom Act</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/American_Indian_Religious_Freedom_Act" title="American Indian Religious Freedom Act">American Indian Religious Freedom Act</a></div>
<p>The American Indian Religious Freedom Act is a United States Federal Law and a joint resolution of Congress that provides protection for tribal culture and traditional religious rights such as access to sacred sites, freedom to worship through traditional ceremony, and use and possession of sacred objects for American Indians, Eskimos, Aleuts, and Native Hawaiians. It was passed on August 11, 1978.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Native_American_Graves_Protection_and_Repatriation_Act">Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Native_American_Graves_Protection_and_Repatriation_Act" title="Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act">Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act</a></div>
<p>The Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (NAGPRA), Pub.L. 101-601, 104 Stat. 3048, is a United States federal law passed on 16 November 1990 requiring federal agencies and institutions that receive federal funding[1] to return Native American cultural items and human remains to their respective peoples. Cultural items include funerary objects, sacred objects, and objects of cultural patrimony.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_Freedom_Restoration_Act">Religious Freedom Restoration Act</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Religious_Freedom_Restoration_Act" title="Religious Freedom Restoration Act">Religious Freedom Restoration Act</a></div>
<p>The Religious Freedom Restoration Act of 1993 (also known as RFRA), is a 1993 <a href="/wiki/United_States_federal_law" class="mw-redirect" title="United States federal law">United States federal law</a> aimed at preventing laws that substantially burden a person's free exercise of <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religion</a>. It was held unconstitutional as applied to the states in the <i><a href="/wiki/City_of_Boerne_v._Flores" title="City of Boerne v. Flores">City of Boerne v. Flores</a></i> decision in 1997, which ruled that the RFRA is not a proper exercise of Congress's enforcement power. However, it continues to be applied to the federal government - for instance, in <i><a href="/wiki/Gonzales_v._O_Centro_Espirita_Beneficente_Uniao_do_Vegetal" class="mw-redirect" title="Gonzales v. O Centro Espirita Beneficente Uniao do Vegetal">Gonzales v. O Centro Espirita Beneficente Uniao do Vegetal</a></i> - because Congress has broad authority to carve out exemptions from federal laws and regulations that it itself has authorized.  In response to <i>City of Boerne v. Flores</i>, some individual states passed <a href="/wiki/State_Religious_Freedom_Restoration_Acts" title="State Religious Freedom Restoration Acts">State Religious Freedom Restoration Acts</a> that apply to state governments and local municipalities.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Declaration_on_the_Rights_of_Indigenous_Peoples">Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Declaration_on_the_Rights_of_Indigenous_Peoples" title="Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples">Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples</a></div>
<p>The United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples was adopted by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">United Nations General Assembly</a> during its 61st session at <a href="/wiki/UN_Headquarters" class="mw-redirect" title="UN Headquarters">UN Headquarters</a> in New York City on 13 September 2007. Article 31, in particular, emphasizes that Indigenous Peoples have the right to their cultural heritage, including ceremonial knowledge, as protected  <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_intellectual_property" title="Indigenous intellectual property">intellectual property</a>.
</p>
