<p>A <b>style of office</b>, <b>honorific</b> or <b>manner/form of address</b>, is an official or legally recognized form of address, and may often be used in conjunction with a <a href="/wiki/Title" title="Title">title</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> A style, by <a href="/wiki/Tradition" title="Tradition">tradition</a> or <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a>, precedes a reference to a person who holds a post or <a href="/wiki/Political_office" class="mw-redirect" title="Political office">political office</a>, and is sometimes used to refer to the office itself. An <a href="/wiki/Honorific" title="Honorific">honorific</a> can also be awarded to an individual in a personal capacity. Such styles are particularly associated with <a href="/wiki/Monarchy" title="Monarchy">monarchies</a>, where they may be used by a wife of an office holder or of a prince of the blood, for the duration of their <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a>. They are also almost universally used for <a href="/wiki/President_(government_title)" title="President (government title)">presidents</a> in <a href="/wiki/Republic" title="Republic">republics</a> and in many countries for <a href="/wiki/Legislator" title="Legislator">members</a> of <a href="/wiki/Legislature" title="Legislature">legislative bodies</a>, higher-ranking <a href="/wiki/Judge" title="Judge">judges</a> and senior constitutional office holders. Leading <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">religious</a> <a href="/wiki/Leadership" title="Leadership">figures</a> also have styles.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Style_(manner_of_address)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Academia">Academia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Style_(manner_of_address)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Academia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traditional forms of address at <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>-speaking universities:
</p>
<ul><li>His/Her Magnificence – rector (president) of a university</li>
<li>His/Her Notability (<i lang="de" title="German language text">Seine Spektabilität</i>; Professors have the privilege to use the Latin <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">Spectabilis</i>) – dean of a faculty</li></ul>
<p>Traditional forms of address at <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>-speaking universities:
</p>
<ul><li>His/Her Great Honour (<i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text">Edelgrootachtbare heer/vrouwe</i>) – rector magnificus (president) of a university</li>
<li>Highly Learned Sir/Madam (<i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text">Hooggeleerde heer/vrouwe</i>) – professor or dean of a faculty</li>
<li>Well (Noble) Very Learned Sir/Madam (<i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text">Weledelzeergeleerde heer/vrouwe</i>) – a doctor</li>
<li>Well (Noble) Learned Sir/Madam (<i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text">De weledelgeleerde heer/vrouwe</i>) – a <a href="/wiki/Doctorandus" title="Doctorandus">doctorandus</a></li>
<li>Well (Noble) Strictly Sir/Madam (<i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text">De weledelgestrenge heer/vrouwe</i>) – a master in laws (<i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text">meester in de rechten</i>) or a university engineer (<i lang="nl" title="Dutch language text">ingenieur</i>)</li></ul>

<ul><li>Magnificent Rector (<i lang="it" title="Italian language text">magnifico rettore</i>) – rector (president, chancellor) of a university</li>
<li>Amplified Headmaster (<i lang="it" title="Italian language text">amplissimo preside</i>) – dean of a faculty (now uncommon)</li>
<li>Illustrious/Enlightened Professor (<i lang="it" title="Italian language text">chiarissimo professore</i>)– a full professor</li></ul>

<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Sire" title="Sire">Sire</a> (oral address first <i><a href="/wiki/Majesty" title="Majesty">Your Majesty</a></i> and then <i>Sire</i>; for a queen first <i>Your Majesty</i> and then <i><a href="/wiki/Madam" title="Madam">Ma'am</a></i>) – Reigning <a href="/wiki/King" title="King">kings</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>. It has also been used in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>.</li>
<li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Majesty_(style)" title="Imperial Majesty (style)">Imperial Majesty</a>, (abbreviation <i>HIM</i>, oral address <i>Your Imperial Majesty</i>) – Emperors and empresses. Formerly, for example, HIM the Shah of Iran. In modern times, the <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Japan" title="Emperor of Japan">Emperor of Japan</a> more often uses the simpler style of "Majesty".</li>
<li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Imperial_and_Royal_Majesty" title="Imperial and Royal Majesty">Imperial and Royal Majesty</a> (abbreviation <i>HI&amp;RM</i>, oral address <i>Your Imperial and Royal Majesty</i>) – Until 1918, the rulers of the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Austro-Hungarian Empire">Austro-Hungarian Empire</a>, who were Emperors/Empresses of Austria while also Kings/Queens of Hungary, and the German emperors/empresses, who were simultaneously <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Prussia" title="Kingdom of Prussia">Kings/Queens of Prussia</a>.</li>
<li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_Majesty" title="Apostolic Majesty">Apostolic Majesty</a> (abbreviation <i>HAM</i>, oral address <i>Your Apostolic Majesty</i>) – the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Hungary" title="King of Hungary">King of Hungary</a>, usually styled Imperial Majesty or Imperial and Royal Majesty as Emperor of Austria and King of Hungary, also sometimes Imperial and Royal Apostolic Majesty.</li>
<li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Britannic_Majesty" title="Britannic Majesty">Britannic Majesty</a> – the <a href="/wiki/British_monarch" class="mw-redirect" title="British monarch">British monarch</a> (not usual); used as a formal and official term in <a href="/wiki/Diplomacy" title="Diplomacy">diplomacy</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_nation" class="mw-redirect" title="Law of nation">law of nations</a>, and <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">international relations</a>, e.g. in <a href="/wiki/British_passport" title="British passport">British passports</a>.</li>
<li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Most_Gracious_Majesty" title="Most Gracious Majesty">Most Gracious Majesty</a> – an elaborate version of His/Her Majesty in the United Kingdom, only used in the most formal of occasions.</li>
<li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Most_Excellent_Majesty" title="Most Excellent Majesty">Most Excellent Majesty</a> – another elaborate version of His/Her Majesty in the United Kingdom, mainly used in <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Acts of Parliament">Acts of Parliament</a>.</li>
<li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Majesty" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic Majesty">Catholic Majesty</a> (abbreviation <i>HCM</i>, oral address <i>Your Catholic Majesty</i>) – the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Spain" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Spain">King of Spain</a> (not usual).</li>
<li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Most_Christian_Majesty" class="mw-redirect" title="Most Christian Majesty">Most Christian Majesty</a> – the <a href="/wiki/King_of_France" class="mw-redirect" title="King of France">King of France</a> until 1790 and from 1815 to 1830.</li>
<li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Faithful_Majesty" class="mw-redirect" title="Faithful Majesty">Faithful Majesty</a> (abbreviation <i>HFM</i>, oral address <i>Your Faithful Majesty</i>) – the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Portugal" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Portugal">King of Portugal</a> until deposed in 1910.</li>
<li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Majesty" title="Majesty">Majesty</a> (abbreviation <i>HM</i>, oral address <i>Your Majesty</i>) – kings, queens and some sultans. For example, HM Queen <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a>, HM King <a href="/wiki/Goodwill_Zwelithini" class="mw-redirect" title="Goodwill Zwelithini">Goodwill Zwelithini</a> or HM King <a href="/wiki/Willem-Alexander_of_the_Netherlands" title="Willem-Alexander of the Netherlands">Willem-Alexander</a>.</li>
<li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Highness" title="Imperial Highness">Imperial Highness</a> (abbreviation <i>HIH</i>, oral address <i>Your Imperial Highness</i>) – members of an imperial house. Currently used by the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_House_of_Japan" title="Imperial House of Japan">Imperial House of Japan</a>.</li>
<li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Imperial_and_Royal_Highness" title="Imperial and Royal Highness">Imperial and Royal Highness</a> (abbreviation <i>HI&amp;RH</i>, oral address <i>Your Imperial and Royal Highness</i>) – formerly, <a href="/wiki/Archduke" title="Archduke">archdukes</a> of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Habsburg" title="House of Habsburg">House of Habsburg</a>, the German crown prince/princess and (post-monarchy) members of the deposed <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Imperial_Family" class="mw-redirect" title="Brazilian Imperial Family">Brazilian Imperial Family</a>; also some women entitled to imperial style by birth and to royal style by marriage (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchess_Marie_Alexandrovna_of_Russia" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Duchess Marie Alexandrovna of Russia">Grand Duchess Marie Alexandrovna of Russia</a>, Duchess of Edinburgh.</li>
<li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Royal_Highness" title="Royal Highness">Royal Highness</a> (abbreviation <i>HRH</i>, oral address <i>Your Royal Highness</i>) – some monarchs, members of a <a href="/wiki/Royal_family" title="Royal family">royal family</a> (other than monarchs, <a href="/wiki/Queen_consort" title="Queen consort">queens consort</a> and <a href="/wiki/Queen_dowager" title="Queen dowager">queens dowager</a>); <a href="/wiki/Grand_duke" title="Grand duke">grand dukes</a>/duchesses who have <a href="/wiki/Reign" title="Reign">reigned</a> (but not those grand dukes who were <a href="/wiki/Cadet_(genealogy)" title="Cadet (genealogy)">cadets</a> of the former Russian Imperial Family), consorts of grand dukes, grand ducal <a href="/wiki/Heir_apparent" title="Heir apparent">heirs apparent</a> and, in Luxembourg, all dynastic male-line cadets; <a href="/wiki/British_prince" title="British prince">British princes</a>, their <a href="/wiki/Dynasty" title="Dynasty">dynastic</a> wives (including the princes consort of queens <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Victoria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Elizabeth II</a>), sons, daughters, <a href="/wiki/Patrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrilineal">patrilineal</a> grandsons and granddaughters of <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Saud" title="Ibn Saud">Ibn Saud</a> of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Saud" title="House of Saud">House of Saud</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/His_Grace" class="mw-redirect" title="His Grace">His/Her Grace</a> (oral address <i>Your Grace</i>) is a style used for various high-ranking personages. It was the style used to address <a href="/wiki/List_of_English_monarchs" title="List of English monarchs">Kings of England</a> until King <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a><sup id="cite_ref-Pollard2007_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pollard2007-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/List_of_Scottish_monarchs" title="List of Scottish monarchs">King or Queen of Scots</a> up to the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Union_(1707)" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Union (1707)">Act of Union</a> of 1707, which united the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Scotland" title="Kingdom of Scotland">Kingdom of Scotland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a>. Today, the style is used when referring to non-royal dukes and duchesses, and archbishops, in the United Kingdom. For example, His Grace The <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Devonshire" title="Duke of Devonshire">Duke of Devonshire</a> in the United Kingdom, or His Grace The <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a>; or <i>Your Grace</i> in spoken or written address. <a href="/wiki/Royal_duke" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal duke">Royal dukes</a>, for example the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_York" title="Duke of York">Duke of York</a>, are addressed with their higher royal style, <i><a href="/wiki/Royal_Highness" title="Royal Highness">Royal Highness</a></i>.</li>
<li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Grand_Ducal_Highness" title="Grand Ducal Highness">Grand Ducal Highness</a> (abbreviation <i>HGDH</i>, oral address <i>Your Grand Ducal Highness</i>) – cadets of some former ruling grand ducal dynasties (<a href="/wiki/Hesse_and_by_Rhine" class="mw-redirect" title="Hesse and by Rhine">Hesse and by Rhine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Baden" title="Grand Duchy of Baden">Baden</a>).</li>
<li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Highness" title="Highness">Highness</a> (abbreviation <i>HH</i>, oral address, <i>Your Highness</i>) – some monarchs, i.e., <a href="/wiki/Emir" title="Emir">emirs</a>, some <a href="/wiki/Sultan" title="Sultan">sultans</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Aga_Khan" title="Aga Khan">Aga Khan</a>; formerly reigning dukes, some <a href="/wiki/Maharajah" class="mw-redirect" title="Maharajah">maharajahs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rajah" class="mw-redirect" title="Rajah">rajahs</a> and the members of their dynasties; cadets of most former grand ducal houses; male-line grandchildren and remoter male-line descendants of some kingly dynasties (i.e., Denmark and formerly Brazil, Italy, Japan, UK, Yugoslavia); Belgium's <a href="/wiki/House_of_Ligne" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Ligne">House of Ligne</a>; members of France's former <a href="/wiki/Foreign_Prince" class="mw-redirect" title="Foreign Prince">Foreign Princely</a> class,  members of <a href="/wiki/Cadet_branch" title="Cadet branch">cadet branches</a> of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Saud" title="House of Saud">House of Saud</a>.</li>
<li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Ducal_Serene_Highness" title="Ducal Serene Highness">Ducal Serene Highness</a> (abbreviation <i>HDSH</i>, oral address, <i>Your Ducal Serene Highness</i>) – members of the formerly reigning ducal <a href="/wiki/House_of_Saxe-Coburg_and_Gotha" title="House of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha">House of Saxe-Coburg and Gotha</a>.</li>
<li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Serene_Highness" title="Serene Highness">Serene Highness</a> (abbreviation <i>HSH</i>, oral address <i>Your Serene Highness</i>) – German: <i lang="de" title="German language text">Seine/Ihre Durchlaucht</i>; Italian: <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">Sua Altezza Serenissima</i>; Russian: <span lang="ru" title="Russian language text">Ваша светлость</span>. <li>His <a href="/wiki/Serene_Highness" title="Serene Highness">Most Serene Highness</a> (abbreviation <i>HMSH</i>, oral address, <i>Your Most Serene Highness</i>) – <a href="/wiki/Prince-elector" title="Prince-elector">Prince-electors</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>.</li>

<li>The High-born (<i lang="de" title="German language text"><a href="/wiki/Hochgeboren" title="Hochgeboren">Hochgeboren</a></i>) – counts in some Scandinavian and Benelux monarchies and, formerly, Germany and Austria.</li>
<li>The High Well-born (<i lang="de" title="German language text"><a href="/wiki/Hochwohlgeboren" title="Hochwohlgeboren">Hochwohlgeboren</a></i>) – Dutch barons; knights and untitled members of the lower nobility in German-speaking monarchies.</li>
<li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Excellency" title="Excellency">Excellency</a> (abbreviation <i>HE</i>, oral address <i>Your Excellency</i>) – <a href="/wiki/Governors-general" class="mw-redirect" title="Governors-general">governors-general</a>, British colonial governors, state officials, and generals of Imperial Russia. Occasionally, <a href="/wiki/Prime_minister" title="Prime minister">prime ministers</a> and cabinet ministers. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_Netherlands" title="Prime Minister of the Netherlands">Prime Minister of the Netherlands</a>; in Denmark, a few high-ranked nobles (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Danneskiold-Sams%C3%B8e" title="Danneskiold-Samsøe">Counts af Danneskiold-Samsø</a>, <a href="/wiki/Danish_Royal_Family#Count_of_Rosenberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Danish Royal Family">Counts of Rosenborg</a>, <i lang="da" title="Danish language text">Lensgrever</i>, knights of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Elephant#Use" title="Order of the Elephant">Order of the Elephant</a>).</li>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Don_(honorific)" title="Don (honorific)">Don</a></i> (<small>Spanish:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">[don]</a></span>, <small>Italian:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian" title="Help:IPA/Italian">[dɔn]</a></span>, Portuguese: <i lang="pt">Dom</i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Portuguese" title="Help:IPA/Portuguese">[dõ]</a></span>), from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> word <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">dominus</i> (roughly, "Lord"), is an <a href="/wiki/Honorific" title="Honorific">honorific</a> title used in Spain, Portugal, Italy, <a href="/wiki/Iberoamerica" class="mw-redirect" title="Iberoamerica">Iberoamerica</a> and the Philippines. The female equivalent is <i lang="es" title="Spanish language text">doña</i> (<small>Spanish:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">[ˈdoɲa]</a></span>), <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">donna</i> (<small>Italian:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian" title="Help:IPA/Italian">[ˈdɔnna]</a></span>), and <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">dona</i> (<small>Portuguese:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Portuguese" title="Help:IPA/Portuguese">[ˈdonɐ]</a></span>), abbreviated as "Dª" or simply "D." Although originally a title reserved for royalty, select nobles, and church hierarchs, it is now often used as a mark of esteem for a person of personal, social or official distinction, such as a community leader of long standing, a person of significant wealth, or a <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">noble</a>, but it may also be used <a href="/wiki/Irony" title="Irony">ironically</a>. As a style, rather than a <a href="/wiki/Title" title="Title">title</a> or <a href="/wiki/Social_rank" class="mw-redirect" title="Social rank">rank</a>, it is used with, and not instead of, a person's name.</li>
<li>In Portugal and Brazil, <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text"><a href="/wiki/Don_(honorific)#Portuguese-speaking_lands" title="Don (honorific)">Dom</a></i> (<small>pronounced&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Portuguese" title="Help:IPA/Portuguese">[ˈdõ]</a></span>) is used for certain <a href="/wiki/Ordinary_(Catholic_Church)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ordinary (Catholic Church)">hierarchs</a> of the Roman Catholic Church and for <a href="/wiki/Laity" title="Laity">laymen</a> who belong to the royal and imperial families (for example, the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Aviz" title="House of Aviz">House of Aviz</a> in Portugal and the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Braganza" title="House of Braganza">House of Braganza</a> in Portugal and Brazil).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It was also accorded to members of families of the titled <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_nobility" title="Portuguese nobility">Portuguese nobility</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cedre_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cedre-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Unless ennobling <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">letters patent</a> specifically authorised its use, <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">Dom</i> was not attributed to members of Portugal's untitled nobility. Since hereditary titles in Portugal descended according to <a href="/wiki/Primogeniture" title="Primogeniture">primogeniture</a>, the right to the style of <i lang="pt" title="Portuguese language text">Dom</i> was the only apparent distinction between <a href="/wiki/Cadet_(genealogy)" title="Cadet (genealogy)">cadets</a> of titled families and members of untitled noble families.<sup id="cite_ref-cedre_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cedre-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Most High, Mighty, and Illustrious Prince – for royal dukes, oral address Your Royal Highness.</li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Custodian_of_the_Two_Holy_Mosques" title="Custodian of the Two Holy Mosques">Custodian of the Two Holy Mosques</a> (abbreviation CTHM) (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl">خادم الحرمين الشريفين</span>&#8206; <i lang="ar-Latn" title="DIN 31635 Arabic (Arabic language) transliteration">Khādim al-Ḥaramayn aš-Šarīfayn</i>), sometimes translated as Servant of the Two Noble Sanctuaries or Protector of the Two Holy Cities, is a <a href="/wiki/Royal_and_noble_styles" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal and noble styles">royal style</a> that has been used by many Islamic rulers including the <a href="/wiki/Ayyubids" class="mw-redirect" title="Ayyubids">Ayyubids</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mamluk_Sultans_of_Egypt" class="mw-redirect" title="Mamluk Sultans of Egypt">Mamluk Sultans of Egypt</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Sultans" class="mw-redirect" title="Ottoman Sultans">Ottoman Sultans</a>, and the modern <a href="/wiki/King_of_Saudi_Arabia" title="King of Saudi Arabia">Saudi kings</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The title refers to the ruler taking the responsibility of guarding and maintaining the two holiest <a href="/wiki/Mosques" class="mw-redirect" title="Mosques">mosques</a> in <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Al-Masjid_al-Haram" class="mw-redirect" title="Al-Masjid al-Haram">Al-Masjid al-Haram</a> (the Sacred Mosque) in <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">Mecca</a> and <a href="/wiki/Al-Masjid_an-Nabawi" title="Al-Masjid an-Nabawi">Al-Masjid an-Nabawi</a> (the Prophet's Mosque) in <a href="/wiki/Medina" title="Medina">Medina</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-emjap_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-emjap-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">Saudi Arabia</a> it's the official title of a king replacing "His Majesty".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Am%C4%ABr_al-Mu%27min%C4%ABn" class="mw-redirect" title="Amīr al-Mu&#39;minīn">Amīr al-Mu'minīn</a> (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl">أمير المؤمنين</span>&#8206;), usually translated Commander of the Faithful or Leader of the Faithful, is the Arabic style of some <a href="/wiki/Caliphate" title="Caliphate">Caliphs</a> and other independent sovereign <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> rulers that claim legitimacy from a community of Muslims. It has been claimed as the title of rulers in Muslim countries and empires and is still used for some Muslim leaders. The use of the title does not necessarily signify a claim to caliphate as it is usually taken to be, but described a certain form of activist leadership which may have been attached to a caliph but also could signify a level of authority beneath that. The Ottoman sultans, in particular, made scant use of it. Moreover, the term was used by men who made no claim to be caliphs.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Currently used by the Caliph of <a href="/wiki/ISIS" class="mw-redirect" title="ISIS">ISIS</a> <a href="/wiki/Abu_Bakr_al-Baghdadi" title="Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi">Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi</a>,  The Ahmadiyya Muslim Caliph, <a href="/wiki/Mirza_Masroor_Ahmad" title="Mirza Masroor Ahmad">Mirza Masroor Ahmad</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Valentine_208_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Valentine_208-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Morocco" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Morocco">King of Morocco</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Sultan_of_Sokoto" class="mw-redirect" title="Sultan of Sokoto">Sultan of Sokoto</a>, and the supreme leaders of the Afghan <a href="/wiki/Taliban" title="Taliban">Taliban</a>.</li>
<li>Kabiyesi (variously translated as His or Her Royal Majesty, His or Her Royal Highness or His or Her Highness, lit. <i>The One whose words are beyond question</i>) – Used by the <a href="/wiki/Oba_(ruler)" title="Oba (ruler)">Obas</a> of <a href="/wiki/Yorubaland" title="Yorubaland">Yorubaland</a>, other aboriginal <a href="/wiki/Yoruba_people" title="Yoruba people">Yoruba</a> high chiefs of royal background, and their counterparts in the tribe's diaspora communities.</li></ul>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Styles_and_titles_of_deposed_monarchs">Styles and titles of deposed monarchs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Style_(manner_of_address)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Styles and titles of deposed monarchs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>General tradition indicates that monarchs who have ceased to reign but not renounced their hereditary titles, retain the use of their style and title for the duration of their lifetimes, but both die with them. Hence Greece's deposed king is often still styled <i>His Majesty <a href="/wiki/Constantine_II_of_Greece" title="Constantine II of Greece">King Constantine II</a></i>, as a <i>personal</i> title, not as occupant of a constitutional office, since the abolition of the monarchy by the Hellenic Republic in 1974. Similarly, until his death, the last King of Italy, <a href="/wiki/Umberto_II_of_Italy" title="Umberto II of Italy">Umberto II</a>, was widely referred to as <i>King Umberto II</i> and sometimes addressed as <i>Your Majesty</i>. In contrast, <a href="/wiki/Simeon_Saxe-Coburg-Gotha" title="Simeon Saxe-Coburg-Gotha">Simeon of Bulgaria</a> who, subsequent to the loss of his throne in 1947, was elected to and held the premiership of his former realm as "Simeon Sakskoburggotski", and therefore is as often referred to by the latter name as by his former royal title and style.
</p><p>While this rule is generally observed, and indeed some exiled monarchs are allowed diplomatic passports by their former realm, other republics officially object to the use of such titles which are, nonetheless, generally accorded by extant monarchical regimes. In 1981, the then Greek President <a href="/wiki/Konstantinos_Karamanlis" title="Konstantinos Karamanlis">Konstantinos Karamanlis</a> declined to attend the wedding of <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">Charles, Prince of Wales</a> when it was revealed that Greece's deposed monarch, a cousin of the Prince, had been referred to as "King" in his invitation. The Hellenic Republic has challenged King Constantine's right to use his title and his passport was revoked in 1994 because he did not use a surname, as his passport at the time stated "Constantine, former King of the Hellenes.". However, Constantine II now travels in and out of Greece on a <a href="/wiki/Danish_passport" title="Danish passport">Danish diplomatic passport</a> as a descendant of <a href="/wiki/Christian_IX_of_Denmark" title="Christian IX of Denmark">Christian IX of Denmark</a>, by the name <i>Constantino de Grecia</i> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> for "Constantine of Greece").
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Republics">Republics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Style_(manner_of_address)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Republics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Excellency" title="Excellency">Excellency</a> (abbreviation <i>HE</i>, oral address <i>Your Excellency</i>) – <a href="/wiki/President_(government_title)" title="President (government title)">Presidents</a> of republics (historically, this was first used to refer to George Washington during his tenure as Commander-in-Chief of the Army during the American War of Independence; its use for presidents of republics was established as he was the first president of the first modern republic.) In some countries also the prime minister, ministers, governors, ambassadors and <a href="/wiki/High_commissioner" title="High commissioner">high commissioners</a> also use this style.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> is properly directly addressed as "Mr. President" and introduced as "The President of the United States"; however, His/Her/Your Excellency may properly be used in written communications and is sometimes used in official documents.</li>
<li>The custom in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> is to call office holders acting within their official capacity <i lang="fr" title="French language text">M. (Monsieur)</i> or <i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a href="/wiki/Mme." class="mw-redirect" title="Mme.">Mme.</a> (Madame)</i> followed by the name of their offices.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Thus, the President of the Republic is called <i lang="fr" title="French language text">M. le président</i> or <i lang="fr" title="French language text">M. le président de la République</i> if a male, and <i lang="fr" title="French language text">Mme...</i> if a female. Styles such as "excellency" or similar are not used, except for talking about foreign dignitaries. Traditionally after "Madame", the name of the office is not put into the feminine form, but this is becoming less common (hence, "Madame le président" is being replaced by "Madame la présidente").</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, members of the lower house (Chamber of Deputies) of the <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Italy" class="mw-redirect" title="Parliament of Italy">Parliament of Italy</a> are styled <i>Honourable</i> (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <i lang="it">Onorevole</i>, abbreviation <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">On.</i>). The correct form to address a member of the upper house (Senate) is <i>Senator</i> (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <i lang="it">Senatore</i>, abbreviation <i lang="it" title="Italian language text">Sen.</i>; even though, for gravitas, they may also be addressed <i>Honourable Senator</i>).</li>
<li>The incumbent president of Finland is addressed <i lang="fi" title="Finnish language text">Herra/Rouva Tasavallan Presidentti</i> (Mr./Ms. President of the Republic), while a former president is addressed as just <i lang="fi" title="Finnish language text">Herra/Rouva Presidentti</i>.</li>
<li>The style used for the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a> is normally His Excellency/Her Excellency (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">A Shoilse/A Soilse</i>); sometimes people may orally address the President as 'Your Excellency' (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">A Shoilse</i> [ə ˈhəʎʃ̪ʲə]), or simply 'President' (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">A Uachtaráin</i> [ə ˈuːəxt̪ˠəɾaːn̥] (vocative case)).</li>
<li>During the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_the_United_Netherlands" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of the United Netherlands">Republic of the United Netherlands</a>, the <a href="/wiki/States_General_of_the_Netherlands" title="States General of the Netherlands">States-General</a> were collectively addressed as "Their High and Mighty Lords" (<a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>: <i lang="nl">Hoogmogende Heren</i>).</li>
<li>The Honourable – Presidents, prime ministers, ministers, governors, members of parliament, senate and congress in some countries. (Australia, Bangladesh, Canada, India, Hong Kong, Singapore, Sri Lanka.)</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicine">Medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Style_(manner_of_address)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Doctor –  In the United Kingdom, university degrees supporting medical and dental licensure are all bachelor's degrees (MB, MBBS, BDS, MB BS BAO, BMed, etc.).  These graduates are addressed as 'doctor' by courtesy and convention.</li>
<li>Mr/Miss/Mrs – Surgeons in the UK revert to the title 'Mr', 'Miss' or 'Mrs' after obtaining the postgraduate qualification <a href="/wiki/Membership_of_the_Royal_College_of_Surgeons" class="mw-redirect" title="Membership of the Royal College of Surgeons">MRCS</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Other doctors, on the other hand, retain the title 'Doctor' after obtaining other postgraduate qualifications, such as <a href="/wiki/Membership_of_the_Royal_College_of_Physicians" class="mw-redirect" title="Membership of the Royal College of Physicians">MRCP</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nautical_and_aeronautical">Nautical and aeronautical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Style_(manner_of_address)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Nautical and aeronautical">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Captain – a person who commands and is responsible for the lives of crew and passengers on a naval or civil vessel or aircraft. In the US military, <i>captain</i> is used regardless of the actual rank of the person being addressed.  For example, on a US naval vessel commanded by someone holding a rank of <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_commander" title="Lieutenant commander">lieutenant commander</a> or lower is addressed as "Captain", in reference to his position in command of the ship, not his military rank. This would apply even to an <a href="/wiki/Enlisted_man" class="mw-redirect" title="Enlisted man">enlisted man</a> in charge of a small boat.</li></ul>

<li>The Very Reverend (abbreviation "The Very Rev."), oral address: "Overseer" – in the Anglican-Apostolic Communion (Pentecostal) tradition, the overseer is the lowest level of prelate (only non–consecrated bishop prelate), with oversight to a specific work or department, directly responsible to the primate/presiding bishop or a ordinary/diocesan bishop.</li>
<li>The Reverend (abbreviation <i>The Rev.</i> or <i>The Revd</i>) – <a href="/wiki/Protestant" class="mw-redirect" title="Protestant">Protestant</a> ordained ministers (common variants include <i>Pastor</i>, <i>Parson</i>, <i>Vicar</i>, or simply <i>Reverend</i> (Rev.), as used in American English; see: <a href="/wiki/The_Reverend" title="The Reverend">The Reverend</a>) ); some Jewish cantors also use this style, almost all Buddhist ministers in Japan use this style</li>
<li>The Reverend Canon (abbreviation <i>The Rev. Canon</i>), oral address <i>Canon</i> – <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anglican" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> canons</li>
<li>The Reverend Doctor (abbreviation <i>The Rev. Dr.</i>), oral address <i>Father</i> or <i>Doctor</i> – <a href="/wiki/Priest" title="Priest">Priests</a> and other ordained clergy with a <a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">doctorate</a></li>
<li>The Reverend Father (abbreviation <i>The Rev. Fr.</i>), oral address <i>Father</i> – <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> (and many <a href="/wiki/Anglican" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglican">Anglican</a>) <a href="/wiki/Priest" title="Priest">priests</a></li>
<li>The Reverend Mother (abbreviation <i>The Rev. Mo.</i>), oral address <i>Mother</i> – <a href="/wiki/Abbess" title="Abbess">Abbesses</a> (also, some female <a href="/wiki/Anglican" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> <a href="/wiki/Priest" title="Priest">priests</a> {abbreviated Mthr})</li>
<li>The Reverend Deacon (abbreviation <i>Rev. Deacon</i>), oral address <i>Deacon</i> <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> permanent Deacons.</li>
<li>The Reverend Mister (abbreviation <i>The Rev. Mr.</i>), oral address <i>Deacon</i> – <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> transitional deacons, i.e. those preparing for priesthood. Transitional deacons belonging to religious orders (monastic and non-monastic) are titled Reverend Brother, (similar situations and modifications apply to <a href="/wiki/Anglican" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> deacons as in <i>The Rev. Fr./Mthr</i>, above; since women can be deacons, these may be <i>The Revd Ms</i>)</li>
<li>Mother, oral address <i>Mother</i> – heads of some female <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> religious convents and other communities who are not abbesses</li>
<li>Mister (abbreviation <i>Mr.</i>), oral address <i>Mister</i> – <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> <a href="/wiki/Sulpician" class="mw-redirect" title="Sulpician">Sulpician</a> priests</li>
<li>Mister (abbreviation <i>Mr.</i>), oral address often <i>Mister</i> – <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> seminarians and scholastics (members preparing for priesthood) of <i>some</i> religious orders (notably, <a href="/wiki/Jesuits" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesuits">Jesuits</a>).</li>
<li>Brother (abbreviation <i>Bro.</i>), oral address <i>Brother</i> – <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> members of religious orders under vows (both monastic and non-monastic) who are not priests.</li>
<li>Sister (abbreviation <i>Sr.</i>), oral address <i>Sister</i> – <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> members of religious orders under vows (both monastic and non-monastic) who are not abbesses.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Elder_(Latter_Day_Saints)" title="Elder (Latter Day Saints)">Elder</a>: used generally for male missionaries of <a href="/wiki/The_Church_of_Jesus_Christ_of_Latter-day_Saints" title="The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints">The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints</a> (LDS Church) and for members of the adult leadership known as the general authorities.  Although most all male adults of the LDS church are elders, the title is reserved for the prior mentioned groups.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Grand Rabbi, oral address <i>Rabbi</i> – <a href="/wiki/Hasidic_Judaism" title="Hasidic Judaism">Hasidic</a> <a href="/wiki/Rabbi" title="Rabbi">rabbis</a>, who are scions of a Hasidic Dynasty.</li>
<li><i>Don</i> (<small>Spanish:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">[don]</a></span>, <small>Italian:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian" title="Help:IPA/Italian">[dɔn]</a></span>, Portuguese: <i lang="pt"><b>Dom</b></i> <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Portuguese" title="Help:IPA/Portuguese">[dõ]</a></span>) from Latin <a href="/wiki/Dominus_(title)" title="Dominus (title)"><i>dominus</i></a>, (roughly, "Lord") is an <a href="/wiki/Honorific" title="Honorific">honorific</a> title used in Spain, Portugal, Italy, <a href="/wiki/Iberoamerica" class="mw-redirect" title="Iberoamerica">Iberoamerica</a> and the Philippines. The female equivalent is <i><b>doña</b></i> (<small>Spanish:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">[ˈdoɲa]</a></span>), <i><b>donna</b></i> (<small>Italian:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian" title="Help:IPA/Italian">[ˈdɔnna]</a></span>), and <i><b>dona</b></i> (<small>Portuguese:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Portuguese" title="Help:IPA/Portuguese">[ˈdonɐ]</a></span>), abbreviated "Dª" or simply "D." Although originally a title reserved for royalty, select nobles, and church hierarchs, it is now often used as a mark of esteem for a person of personal, social or official distinction, such as a community leader of long standing, a person of significant wealth, or a <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">noble</a>, but may also be used <a href="/wiki/Irony" title="Irony">ironically</a>. As a style, rather than a <a href="/wiki/Title" title="Title">title</a> or <a href="/wiki/Social_rank" class="mw-redirect" title="Social rank">rank</a>, it is used with, and not instead of, a person's name.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dom_(title)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dom (title)">Dom</a> is an <a href="/wiki/Honorific" title="Honorific">honorific</a> <a href="/wiki/Prefix" title="Prefix">prefixed</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>. It derives from the Latin <i><a href="/wiki/Dominus_(title)" title="Dominus (title)">Dominus</a></i>. It is used in English for certain <a href="/wiki/Benedictine" class="mw-redirect" title="Benedictine">Benedictine</a> (including some communities which follow the <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_St._Benedict" class="mw-redirect" title="Rule of St. Benedict">Rule of St. Benedict</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Carthusian" class="mw-redirect" title="Carthusian">Carthusian</a> <a href="/wiki/Monks" class="mw-redirect" title="Monks">monks</a>, and for members of certain communities of <a href="/wiki/Canons_Regular" class="mw-redirect" title="Canons Regular">Canons Regular</a>. Examples include Benedictine monks of the <a href="/wiki/English_Benedictine_Congregation" title="English Benedictine Congregation">English Benedictine Congregation</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/John_Chapman_OSB" class="mw-redirect" title="John Chapman OSB">Dom John Chapman</a>, late <a href="/wiki/Downside_Abbey" title="Downside Abbey">Abbot of Downside</a>). The equivalent female usage for such a <a href="/wiki/Cleric" class="mw-redirect" title="Cleric">cleric</a> is "<a href="/wiki/Dame_(title)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dame (title)">Dame</a>" (e.g. Dame <a href="/wiki/Laurentia_McLachlan" title="Laurentia McLachlan">Laurentia McLachlan</a>, late <a href="/wiki/Stanbrook_Abbey" title="Stanbrook Abbey">Abbess of Stanbrook</a>, or Dame <a href="/wiki/Felicitas_Corrigan" title="Felicitas Corrigan">Felicitas Corrigan</a>, author).
<ul><li><i>Dom</i> has historically been used on occasions in French, as an honorific for Benedictine monks, such as the famous <i><a href="/wiki/Dom_P%C3%A9rignon_(monk)" title="Dom Pérignon (monk)">Dom Pérignon</a></i>.</li></ul></li>
<li>Rabbi, oral address <i>Rabbi</i> (or, if holder of the appropriate degree, <i>Doctor</i> both in oral and written communication) – rabbis</li>
<li>Grand Ayatullah, oral address <i>Ayatullah</i> or <i>Ayatullah al-Uzma</i> – <a href="/wiki/Shiite_Islam" class="mw-redirect" title="Shiite Islam">Shia</a> <a href="/wiki/Ayatullah" class="mw-redirect" title="Ayatullah">Ayatullahs</a>, who have accomplished the highest religious jurisprudent knowledge degree called as <a href="/wiki/Marja%27" title="Marja&#39;">marja'</a> and some people officially follow them.</li>
<li>Ayatullah, oral address <i>Ayatullah</i> – <a href="/wiki/Shiite_Islam" class="mw-redirect" title="Shiite Islam">Shia</a> religious degree who has accomplished a religious high course of lessons and is capable of individually issuing religious verdicts.</li>
<li>Amīr al-Mu'minīn (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl">أمير المؤمنين</span>&#8206;), usually translated Commander of the Faithful or Leader of the Faithful, is the Arabic style of some <a href="/wiki/Caliphate" title="Caliphate">Caliphs</a> and other independent sovereign <a href="/wiki/Muslim" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim">Muslim</a> rulers that claim legitimacy from a community of Muslims. It has been claimed as the title of rulers in Muslim countries and empires and is still used for some Muslim leaders. The use of the title does not necessarily signify a claim to caliphate as it is usually taken to be, but described a certain form of activist leadership which may have been attached to a caliph but also could signify a level of authority beneath that. The Ottoman sultans, in particular, made scant use of it. Moreover, the term was used by men who made no claim to be caliphs.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Currently used by the Caliph of <a href="/wiki/ISIS" class="mw-redirect" title="ISIS">ISIS</a> <a href="/wiki/Abu_Bakr_al-Baghdadi" title="Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi">Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi</a>,  The Ahmadiyya Muslim Caliph, <a href="/wiki/Mirza_Masroor_Ahmad" title="Mirza Masroor Ahmad">Mirza Masroor Ahmad</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Valentine_208_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Valentine_208-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Morocco" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Morocco">King of Morocco</a>, The <a href="/wiki/Sultan_of_Sokoto" class="mw-redirect" title="Sultan of Sokoto">Sultan of Sokoto</a>, The supreme leaders of the Afghan <a href="/wiki/Taliban" title="Taliban">Taliban</a>.</li>
<li>Cantor, oral address <i>Cantor</i> (some cantors use <i>The Reverend</i> as style, as above) – Jewish <a href="/wiki/Hazzan" title="Hazzan">cantors</a></li>
<li>Reverend, oral address <i>Reverend</i>, <i>Mister</i> or <i>Brother</i> – ordained ministers/pastors</li>
<li>Pastor (abbreviation "Pr"), oral address 'Pastor" – minister responsible for caring for the "flock" in Lutheran churches<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Pandit (sometimes spelled <i>Pundit</i>) – <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hindu</a> priests</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Swami" title="Swami">Swami</a> – in <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a> an ascetic or yogi who has been initiated into a religious monastic order.  Informally, "Swamiji".</li>
<li>Officers of <a href="/wiki/The_Salvation_Army" title="The Salvation Army">The Salvation Army</a> are addressed by their rank, e.g. "Captain" (Capt.), "Major" (Maj.), etc.</li>
<li>In some but not all Wiccan or Pagan Groups, the high priest or priestess receives the title of Lord or Lady followed by either their birth name or their magical name, an example would be Lady Jenny, or Lady Morningstarr but only in official or formal standings. During written communications, it's not uncommon for a high priest or priestess to sign their birth name followed by their formal religious title and name.  When writing to a high priest or priestess in a formal manner it is acceptable, but not required, to address the title holder as Lord or Lady, such as "Dear Lady Autumn Rose."  In a familiar setting birth name or Mr., Mrs, or other honorable title can be used.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li></ul>

<ul><li><i>Kebawah Duli Yang Maha Mulia (Kebawah DYMM)</i>, equivalent to His or <a href="/wiki/Majesty" title="Majesty">Her Majesty</a> (HM) – for <a href="/wiki/Sultan_of_Brunei" class="mw-redirect" title="Sultan of Brunei">Sultan</a> and his first royal consort. <li>Kebawah Duli Yang Maha Mulia Paduka Seri Raja — for the second wife of the Sultan during coronation</li></ul></li>
<li><i>Kebawah Duli</i>, for a Sultan that has not gone through puberty.</li>
<li><i>Duli Yang Maha Mulia Paduka Seri Pengiran Isteri</i>, for the second wife of the Sultan after coronation</li>

<li>Duli Yang Teramat Mulia Paduka Seri Pengiran Muda Mahkota — for the Crown Prince</li>

<li>Yang Teramat Mulia Paduka Seri Pengiran Anak Puteri— for the Sultan's daughter by a royal mother (non-commoner)</li>

<ul><li>Yang Amat Mulia Pengiran Anak Isteri — for the consort of the Sultan's son (full royal blood)</li>
<li>Yang Amat Mulia Pengiran Muda — for the son (full royal blood) of the Sultan's son (full royal blood)</li>
<li>Yang Amat Mulia Pengiran Anak — for the children of the Sultan that were born by a commoner mother; daughter (full royal blood) of the Sultan's son (full royal blood); children (full royal blood) of the Sultan's daughter (full royal blood); children (full royal blood) of the Sultan's children (half royal blood)</li></ul></li>
<li><i>Yang Mulia (YM)</i>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_styles_in_India">Royal styles in India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Style_(manner_of_address)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Royal styles in India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>With a long history of rulers, there are many styles which vary from territory to territory and languages for royal families in India, commonly <a href="/wiki/Maharaja" title="Maharaja">Maharaja</a> (for king), <a href="/wiki/Maharani" class="mw-redirect" title="Maharani">Maharani</a> (queen) whereas for their successors Raja, Rani (Maha meaning "Great" removed). Rajkumar (for prince) and Rajkumari (for princess).
</p><p>Others include <a href="/wiki/Hukam" title="Hukam">Hukam</a> (commonly in <a href="/wiki/Rajasthan" title="Rajasthan">Rajasthan</a>), <a href="/wiki/Sardar" title="Sardar">Sardar</a> (kings in territories of Punjab within <a href="/wiki/Sikh_Empire" title="Sikh Empire">Sikh Empire</a>), <a href="/wiki/Padishah" title="Padishah">Badshah</a> (<a href="/wiki/Mughal_Empire" title="Mughal Empire">Mughal Empire</a>), Vajeer-e-Aala (in <a href="/wiki/Mughal_Empire" title="Mughal Empire">Mughal Empire</a>) etc.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="African_traditional_rulers">African traditional rulers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Style_(manner_of_address)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: African traditional rulers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In most of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, many styles are used by <a href="/wiki/Monarchies_in_Africa" title="Monarchies in Africa">traditional royalty</a>.
</p><p>Generally the vast majority of the members of these royal families use the titles Prince and Princess, while the higher ranked amongst them also use either <a href="/wiki/Highness" title="Highness">Highness</a> or <a href="/wiki/Royal_Highness" title="Royal Highness">Royal Highness</a> to describe secondary appellations in their native languages that they hold in their realms, appellations that are intended to highlight their relative proximity to their thrones, either literally in the sense of the extant <a href="/wiki/Kingship" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingship">kingships</a> of the continent or symbolically in the sense of its varied <a href="/wiki/Chief_of_the_Name" title="Chief of the Name">chiefships of the name</a>, and which therefore serve a function similar to the said styles of Highness and Royal Highness.
</p><p>For example, the <a href="/wiki/Yoruba_people" title="Yoruba people">Yoruba people</a> of <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">West Africa</a> usually make use of the word <b>Kabiyesi</b> when speaking either to or about their <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">sovereigns</a> and other royals. As such, it is variously translated as Majesty, Royal Highness or Highness depending on the actual rank of the person in question, though a literal translation of the word would read more like this: <i>He (or She) whose words are beyond questioning, Great Lawgiver of the Nation</i>.
</p><p>Within the <a href="/wiki/Zulu_Kingdom" title="Zulu Kingdom">Zulu Kingdom</a> of <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">Southern Africa</a>, meanwhile, the monarch and other senior royals are often addressed as <b>uNdabezitha</b> meaning <i>He (or She) Who Concerns the Enemy</i>, but rendered in English as Majesty in address or reference to the king and his consorts, or Royal Highness in the case of other senior members of the royal family.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hong_Kong">Hong Kong</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Style_(manner_of_address)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Hong Kong">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chief_Executive_of_Hong_Kong" title="Chief Executive of Hong Kong">Chief Executive</a> is styled as The <a href="/wiki/Honourable" class="mw-redirect" title="Honourable">Honourable</a>.
</p><p>Certain senior government officials (such as the <a href="/wiki/Chief_Secretary_for_Administration" title="Chief Secretary for Administration">Chief Secretary for Administration</a>), <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Legislative_Council_of_Hong_Kong" title="President of the Legislative Council of Hong Kong">President of the Legislative Council</a>, members of the <a href="/wiki/Executive_Council_of_Hong_Kong" title="Executive Council of Hong Kong">Executive Council</a>, and members of the judiciary (such as the <a href="/wiki/Chief_Justice_of_the_Court_of_Final_Appeal" title="Chief Justice of the Court of Final Appeal">Chief Justice of the Court of Final Appeal</a>) are also styled as <i>The Honourable</i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Style_(manner_of_address)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Ireland</a>, holders of offices with <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> names are usually addressed in English by its <a href="/wiki/Irish_declension#Nominative" title="Irish declension">nominative form</a> (so, '<a href="/wiki/Taoiseach" title="Taoiseach">Taoiseach</a>' and '<a href="/wiki/T%C3%A1naiste" title="Tánaiste">Tánaiste</a>'), though the <a href="/wiki/Irish_declension#Vocative" title="Irish declension">Irish vocative forms</a> differ (<i>a Thaoisigh</i> and <i>a Thánaiste</i>). <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland#Official_residence,_salute,_style_and_address" title="President of Ireland">The President may be styled</a> 'His/Her Excellency' (<a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>: <i lang="ga">A Shoilse</i>, <small>IPA:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Irish" title="Help:IPA/Irish">[ə ˈhəʎʃ̪ʲə]</a></span> / <i>A Soilse</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ə ˈsəʎʃ̪ʲə]</span>) and addressed 'Your Excellency' (Irish: <i>A Shoilse</i>), or simply 'President' (Irish: <i>A Uachtaráin</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA">[ə uːəxt̪ˠəɾaːn̥]</span>).  The titles '<a href="/wiki/Government_of_Ireland" title="Government of Ireland">Minister</a>' and '<a href="/wiki/Seanad_%C3%89ireann" title="Seanad Éireann">Senator</a>' are used as forms of address; only the latter as a style.  A <a href="/wiki/Teachta_D%C3%A1la" title="Teachta Dála">TD (<i>Teachta Dála</i>)</a> is formally addressed and styled as 'Deputy', though often simply <i>Mr</i>, <i>Mrs</i>, etc.  Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_the_Republic_of_Ireland" title="Local government in the Republic of Ireland">county and city councillors</a> can be addressed as 'Councillor', abbreviated <i>Cllr.</i> which is used as a written style, but are just as frequently addressed as <i>Mr</i>, <i>Mrs</i> etc.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines">Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Style_(manner_of_address)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

The title in Tagalog is <i>"Ang Kagalanggalang"</i> (The Honorable). <li>His/Her Illustriousness (<i>su ilustrísima</i>) – marquesses, counts, viscounts, junior ministers either from the central government (<i>"secretarios de estado") or from autonomous government (</i>"vice-consejeros"<i>)members of the royal academies and the holders of certain Spanish decorations.</i></li>
<li>His/Her Most Excellent and Magnificent Lord – Rector of a university.</li>
<li>His Lordship/Her Ladyship (<i>su señoría</i>) – barons, seigneurs, members of parliament, judges.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thailand">Thailand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Style_(manner_of_address)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Thailand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>His/Her <a href="/wiki/Majesty" title="Majesty">Majesty</a> – The King and Queen of Thailand.</li>
<li>His/Her Royal Highness – Prince and princess of Thailand (used for children and grandchildren of the king)<i> </i>from "<a href="/wiki/Chao_Fa" class="mw-redirect" title="Chao Fa">Chao-Fa</a>" <i>(เจ้าฟ้า)</i> (the most senior rank of prince/princess) to "Phra Chao Worawongse Ther Phra Ong Chao" <i>(พระเจ้าวรวงศ์เธอ พระองค์เจ้า) </i>(a mid-level, lesser class of prince and princess than <a href="/wiki/Chao_Fa" class="mw-redirect" title="Chao Fa">Chao Fa</a>). This style is also used for princess consort (now obsolete).</li>
<li>His/Her Highness – Prince and princess of Thailand of the rank "Phra Worawong Ther <a href="/wiki/Phra_ong_chao" class="mw-redirect" title="Phra ong chao">Phra Ong Chao</a>" <i>(พระวรวงศ์เธอ พระองค์เจ้า)</i> which are born in the title as <a href="/wiki/Mom_Chao" class="mw-redirect" title="Mom Chao">Mom Chao</a> to whom the king later granted this higher title, either as recognition of merit, or as a special favour.</li>
<li>His/Her Serene Highness – Prince and princess of title <a href="/wiki/Mom_Chao" class="mw-redirect" title="Mom Chao">Mom Chao</a> (m)/Mom Chao Ying (f) (<i>หม่อมเจ้า/หม่อมเจ้าหญิง</i>, abbreviated in Thai as ม.จ. or in English as M.C.) is the most junior class still considered royalty. This is normally when surnames first appear among royal lineages. They are either: Children of a male <a href="/wiki/Chao_Fa" class="mw-redirect" title="Chao Fa">Chao Fa</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Commoner" title="Commoner">commoner</a>.Children of a male <a href="/wiki/Phra_ong_chao" class="mw-redirect" title="Phra ong chao">Phra Ong Chao</a>. Informally, they are styled "Than Chai" (m)... /"Than Ying" (f)... <i>(ท่านชาย.../ท่านหญิง...)</i>.</li>
<li>The Honourable – <a href="/wiki/Mom_Rajawongse" class="mw-redirect" title="Mom Rajawongse">Mom Rajawongse</a> (<i>หม่อมราชวงศ์</i>, RTGS: Mom Ratchawong; abbreviated in Thai as ม.ร.ว. or in English as M.R. and also translated into English as <i>The Honourable</i>) is the title assumed by children of male Mom Chao. The title is pronounced "Mom Rachawong". Informally, they may be styled as "Khun Chai" (m).../ "Khunying" (f)... <i>(คุณชาย.../คุณหญิง...).</i></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom_2">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Style_(manner_of_address)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Sir" title="Sir">Sir</a> – males, formally if they have an <a href="/wiki/Knighthood" class="mw-redirect" title="Knighthood">English knighthood</a> or if they are a <a href="/wiki/Baronet" title="Baronet">baronet</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dame" title="Dame">Dame</a> – female knights and baronetess <a href="/wiki/Suo_jure" title="Suo jure">in their own right</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lord" title="Lord">Lord</a> – male <a href="/wiki/Marquess" title="Marquess">marquesses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">earls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viscount" title="Viscount">viscounts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Baron" title="Baron">barons</a>, as well as some of the fruit-of-their-loins. (Style: Your Lordship or My Lord).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Lady" title="Lady">Lady</a> – females married to knights. The title is also used for the wives of <a href="/wiki/Marquess" title="Marquess">marquesses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">earls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viscount" title="Viscount">viscounts</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Baron" title="Baron">barons</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Grace_(style)" title="Grace (style)">His Grace</a> (oral address <i>Your Grace</i> or Duke) – <a href="/wiki/Duke" title="Duke">Dukes</a>. Occasionally the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_York" title="Archbishop of York">Archbishop of York</a> and other archbishops are also styled His Grace.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Royal_Highness" title="Royal Highness">His Royal Highness</a>, oral address Your Royal Highness – royal princes.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Right_Honourable" title="The Right Honourable">The Right Honourable</a> signifies membership of the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Privy Council of the United Kingdom">Privy Council of the United Kingdom</a>, but does not confer any other title, and is also a formal style of address for certain holders of <a href="/wiki/Peerage" title="Peerage">peerages</a>, namely <a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">earls</a>, <a href="/wiki/Viscount" title="Viscount">viscounts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Baron" title="Baron">barons</a>, and Lords of Parliament.</li>
<li>The Right Honourable and Reverend – as the previous explanation, used if the holder is also an ordained clergyman (parliamentary usage).</li>
<li>The Honourable – younger sons of earls, all children of viscounts and barons/Lords of Parliament</li></ul>
<p>"The Right Honourable" is added as a prefix to the name of various collective entities such as:
</p>
<ul><li>The Right Honourable the Spiritual and Temporal Lords (of the Kingdom of England) in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>.</li>
<li>The Right Honourable the Lord-Commissioners of the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Admiralty" title="Board of Admiralty">Board of Admiralty</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Most_Honourable" title="The Most Honourable">The Most Honourable</a> – marquesses, The Lords of His Majesty's Most Honourable Privy Council.</li>
<li>His Worship is an honorific prefix for <a href="/wiki/Mayor" title="Mayor">mayors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_peace" title="Justice of the peace">justices of the peace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Magistrate" title="Magistrate">magistrates</a> in present or former <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_realm" title="Commonwealth realm">Commonwealth realms</a>. In spoken address, these officials are addressed as Your Worship or referred to as His Worship. In Australia all states now use <a href="/wiki/His_Honour" title="His Honour">Your Honour</a> as the form of address for magistrates (the same as has always been used for judges in higher courts).</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_Much_Honoured" title="The Much Honoured">The Much Honoured</a> – Scottish feudal barons and lairds</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Styles_existing_through_marriage_in_the_United_Kingdom">Styles existing through marriage in the United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Style_(manner_of_address)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Styles existing through marriage in the United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Styles can be acquired through marriage, although traditionally this applies more to wives of office-holders than to husbands. Thus, in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Princess_Royal" title="Anne, Princess Royal">Anne, Princess Royal</a>, is styled Her Royal Highness (HRH), her husband, Sir <a href="/wiki/Timothy_Laurence" title="Timothy Laurence">Timothy Laurence</a>, bears no courtesy style by virtue of being her husband (although his mother-in-law, Queen Elizabeth II, has since knighted him), nor do her children bear any title or style, by right or tradition, despite being in the line of succession to the Crown, until 2015 subject to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Marriages_Act_1772" title="Royal Marriages Act 1772">Royal Marriages Act 1772</a>. In contrast, when <a href="/wiki/Sophie,_Countess_of_Wessex" title="Sophie, Countess of Wessex">Sophie Rhys-Jones</a> married <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward,_Earl_of_Wessex" title="Prince Edward, Earl of Wessex">Prince Edward</a>, she became HRH the Countess of Wessex (&amp;c.) and their children are entitled (although they do not use them) to the princely prefix and the style of HRH, and do bear <a href="/wiki/Courtesy_title" title="Courtesy title">courtesy titles</a> derived from their father.
</p><p>Styles and titles can terminate when a marriage is dissolved. <a href="/wiki/Diana,_Princess_of_Wales" title="Diana, Princess of Wales">Diana, Princess of Wales</a> held the style Her Royal Highness during her marriage to HRH The Prince of Wales and the title <a href="/wiki/Princess_of_Wales" title="Princess of Wales">Princess of Wales</a>. When the couple divorced she lost her style: she became instead <i>Diana, Princess of Wales</i>, properly a name rather than a title (although she fit the criteria which customarily accords the prefix of "Lady" to the daughter of an earl, and she had been known as such prior to marriage, she did not revert to that title following divorce).
</p><p>When applied to the current <a href="/wiki/Princess_of_Wales" title="Princess of Wales">Princess of Wales</a>, inclusion of a definite article ("The Princess of Wales"), is, like HRH, part of the style which accompanies the title.  When Charles was remarried to <a href="/wiki/Camilla_Parker-Bowles" class="mw-redirect" title="Camilla Parker-Bowles">Camilla Parker-Bowles</a> in compliance with the Royal Marriages Act, she lawfully became HRH The Princess of Wales but, as was the announced intention prior to the couple's wedding, she continues to use the lesser title derived from her husband's <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a> and is known as HRH The Duchess of Cornwall.
</p><p>From the divorce until her death in 1997, Diana ceased to hold any royal style, although the monarch declared that on occasions when members of the Royal Family appeared in public, she continued to be accorded royal precedence.  When <a href="/wiki/Sarah,_Duchess_of_York" title="Sarah, Duchess of York">Sarah Ferguson</a> was divorced from her husband, HRH <a href="/wiki/Prince_Andrew,_Duke_of_York" title="Prince Andrew, Duke of York">Prince Andrew, Duke of York</a>, she too lost her HRH style, rank as a <a href="/wiki/Royal_duke" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal duke">royal duchess</a> and as a <a href="/wiki/British_peer" class="mw-redirect" title="British peer">peeress</a>, and is known by the appellation "Sarah, Duchess of York"
</p><p>In 1936, <a href="/wiki/Wallis_Simpson" title="Wallis Simpson">Wallis Simpson</a> was denied the HRH style by <a href="/wiki/George_VI" title="George VI">George VI</a> when she married his older brother, the former <a href="/wiki/Edward_VIII" title="Edward VIII">Edward VIII</a>, who became HRH the Duke of Windsor following his abdication and receipt of a peerage.
</p>

<p>Most current and former elected federal and state officials and judges in the U.S. are styled "<a href="/wiki/The_Honorable" class="mw-redirect" title="The Honorable">The Honorable</a> [full name]" in writing,  (e.g., "The Honorable Mike Rawlings, Mayor of the City of Dallas"). Many are addressed in conversation as "Mister [title]" or "Madam [title]" ("Mr. President", "Madam Mayor") or simply by (title)+(name) e.g., "Senator Jones" or "Commissioner Smith".
</p><p>Continued use of a title after leaving office depends on the office: those of which there is only one at a time (e.g., president, speaker, governor, or mayor) are only officially used by the current office holder.  However, titles for offices of which there are many concurrent office holders (e.g., ambassador, senator, judge, professor or military ranks, especially <a href="/wiki/Colonel" title="Colonel">colonel</a> and above) are retained for life: A retired US Army general is addressed as "General (Name)" officially and socially for the rest of their life. Military retirees are entitled to receive pay and are still counted as members of the United States Armed Forces. Accordingly, all retired military ranks are retained for life pursuant to Title 10 of the United States Code. In the case of the US President, while the title is officially dropped after leaving office – e.g., Dwight Eisenhower reverted to his prior style "General Eisenhower" in retirement – it is still widely used as an informal practice; e.g., Jimmy Carter is still often called President Carter. Similarly, governors may be addressed in later life as "Governor (Name)", particularly if running for further political office. <a href="/wiki/Mitt_Romney" title="Mitt Romney">Mitt Romney</a>, for example, was frequently referred to as "Governor Romney" during his <a href="/wiki/Mitt_Romney_presidential_campaign,_2012" class="mw-redirect" title="Mitt Romney presidential campaign, 2012">2012 presidential campaign</a> (he was addressed as such formally in the debates<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>), despite leaving the office of <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Massachusetts" title="Governor of Massachusetts">Governor of Massachusetts</a> in 2007.
</p>
<ul><li>Judges are styled "The Honorable [full name]" in writing, and orally in court as "Your Honor", or as "Judge Smith". <a href="/wiki/Chief_Justice_of_the_United_States" title="Chief Justice of the United States">Chief justices of supreme courts</a> are addressed orally as "Mr. or Madame Chief Justice" or "Chief Justice"; associate justices as "Justice Jones", or "Justice".</li>
<li>Mayors are styled "The Honorable [full name]" in writing. In municipalities (e.g., New York City and Chicago), mayors are addressed in conversation as "Your Honor". This may be a vestige of the fact that the mayors (and some others) were also <a href="/wiki/Magistrate" title="Magistrate">magistrates</a> of the court system.</li>
<li>His/Her Excellency (oral address Excellency, Your Excellency) was once customarily used of governors of states, though this has given way to "The Honorable", the form used to address all elected officials in the United States.  "His/Her Excellency" has continued in the <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_(U.S._state)" title="Commonwealth (U.S. state)">Commonwealths</a> of <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts" title="Massachusetts">Massachusetts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Virginia" title="Virginia">Virginia</a> and the states of <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">South Carolina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(U.S._state)" title="Georgia (U.S. state)">Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">Connecticut</a>.</li>
<li>Members of the House of Representatives are similarly styled in writing as "The Honorable [full name]". Orally they are traditionally addressed as "Mr./Ms. [name]", but as a practice are sometimes addressed as "Representative [name]" or "Congressman/Congresswoman [name]" when it is necessary or desirable to specify the member's status. It is advisable to follow the preference of the individual official. Following precedence in Westminster style of parliament, when writing their own names, especially on stationery and <a href="/wiki/Franking" title="Franking">franks</a>, Representatives have upon occasion followed their names with "M.C." (Member of Congress).<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Senators similarly are addressed in writing as "The Honorable [full name]" and orally as "Senator Smith". Where Representatives may have used "M.C.", Senators have used "U.S.S." (United States Senator).<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> However, neither form is currently used by members in Washington, DC. On the actual floor of the houses during debate, members commonly refer to one another as the gentleman/gentlewoman "from such-and-such a state" – "As my friend, the distinguished gentleman from Ohio, just said..." or "I yield three minutes to the gentleman from New York, Mr. Smith". In debate, senators sometimes refer to colleagues as the junior or senior senator from a state, as in "I disagree with my dear friend, the junior senator from Ohio...". Senators also commonly use "my friend from X" and "the distinguished senator from X".</li>
<li>While the term "<a href="/wiki/Esquire" title="Esquire">Esquire</a>", abbreviated "Esq." after the name (John Jones, Esq.), has no legal meaning in the U.S. and may be used by anyone (or at least, customarily, by any male), it is correctly used when addressing lawyers in correspondence as an indication of their profession. At least one American jurisdiction, the District of Columbia, limits the use of "Esquire" (and similar terms) to licensed attorneys.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Although some authorities previously urged that use of "Esq." should be restricted to male lawyers, today the term is used for both male and female attorneys. The academic post-nominal <i>J.D.</i> (<a href="/wiki/Juris_Doctor" title="Juris Doctor">Juris Doctor</a>) may be used by graduates of law schools who are not members of the bar of any state or who are working outside the legal profession.</li>
<li>In academic fields, it is customary in the U.S. to refer to those holding any level of professorship (professor, assistant professor, associate professor, adjunct professor, etc.) as "Professor" – as in "Professor Jones" – orally or in writing. In writing, "professor" is often abbreviated as "Prof.", as in "Prof. Jones". Those holding academic doctorates are frequently referred to as "Dr. Jones."</li>
<li>Military personnel of any functionality (doctors, lawyers, engineers, cooks, fighter pilots, motor pool drivers, commanding officers, security guards … officers and enlisted … leaders and followers) are always addressed by rank + name; with the exception of <a href="/wiki/Chaplain" title="Chaplain">chaplains</a>, who are addressed as "Chaplain" and are addressed in writing with their rank in parentheses, e.g.: "Chaplain (Major) Jones". An exception to this is in the Navy, where in writing the rank is either not used, or is used before the person's name with the corps designator "CHC" indicating the officer is a chaplain put behind their name. e.g.: "LT George Burdell, CHC, USN". In the United States Navy, there is an internal practice aboard ships that junior officers who are not in command may be addressed by their rank or as "Mister/Miss X" as in "Lieutenant Junior Grade Smith" or "Miss Smith". This practice is also followed within the United States Coast Guard, both aboard ship and ashore. Junior officers in both services are understood to be those of <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant_commander" title="Lieutenant commander">lieutenant commander</a> and below. Senior officers (<a href="/wiki/Commander" title="Commander">commander</a> and above) are addressed by their rank as in "Commander Smith" or "Admiral Smith". While officially this manner of address is supposed to be from a senior rank to a junior rank, i.e. captain to lieutenant, in practice it is not unknown for enlisted personnel to refer to junior officers as Mister as well. While commonly referred to by their rank, i.e. Seaman/Airman/Fireman/Petty Officer X or (Senior/Master) Chief X, on formal occasions, e.g. weddings, an enlisted man's full title is sometimes used, starting with their rating, then their rank, and their name, e.g. Electronics Technician Second Class X or Chief Gunner's Mate Y. When written, e.g. in formal invitations, the enlisted man's name is written as "<i>Serviceman's name</i>, USN/USMC/USA/USAF/USCG", without one's rank preceding their name, unlike commissioned officers.</li>
<li>Retired military personnel may continue to be addressed by their rank at the time of their retirement. Those who held 'brevet' ranks higher than their permanent rank (permanent Army officers who held temporary rank in volunteer regiments during the American Civil War) also held this honor; though all such individuals have now perished, this usage is often seen in historical or fictional sources placed in the 1865–1900 period.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Former_styles">Former styles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Style_(manner_of_address)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Former styles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All former monarchies had styles, some, as in the Bourbon monarchy of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">France</a>, extremely complicated depending on the status of the office or office-holder. <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Habsburg" title="Otto von Habsburg">Otto von Habsburg</a>, who was Crown Prince of <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a> (1916–1918), had the style 'His Imperial and Royal Highness'. He was last addressed as such by church figures during the funeral of his late mother, Empress-Queen <a href="/wiki/Zita_of_Bourbon-Parma" title="Zita of Bourbon-Parma">Zita of Austria-Hungary</a> in 1989, although the use of these styles has been prohibited in Austria since 1920.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For the styles of address to government officials in <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Imperial Russia</a>, see <a href="/wiki/Table_of_Ranks" title="Table of Ranks">Table of Ranks</a>.
</p><p>The names of some offices are also <a href="/wiki/Title" title="Title">titles</a>, which are retained by the office holder for life. For example, holders of titles of which there are many at the same time, such as ambassadors, senators, judges, and military officers who retire retain use of their hierarchical honorific for life. Holders of titles of which there is only one office holder at a time such as president, chief justice or speaker revert to their previous honorific when they leave office out of deference to the current office holder.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_parallel_symbols">Other parallel symbols</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Style_(manner_of_address)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Other parallel symbols">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Styles were often among the range of symbols that surrounded figures of high office. Everything from the manner of address to the behaviour of a person on meeting that personage was surrounded by traditional symbols. Monarchs were to be bowed to by men and curtsied to by women. Senior clergy, particularly in the Roman Catholic Church, were to have their rings (the symbol of their authority) kissed by lay persons while they were on bended knee, while cardinals in an act of homage at the papal coronation were meant to kiss the feet of the Supreme Pontiff, the <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">Pope</a>.
</p><p>Many of these traditions have lapsed or been partially abandoned. At his inauguration as pope in 1978 (itself the abandonment of the traditional millennium-old papal coronation), <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">Pope John Paul II</a> himself kissed cardinals on the cheeks, rather than follow the traditional method of homage of having his feet kissed.
</p><p>Similarly, styles, though still used, are used less often. The former <a href="/wiki/President_of_Ireland" title="President of Ireland">President of Ireland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mary_McAleese" title="Mary McAleese">Mary McAleese</a>, was usually referred to as <i>President Mary McAleese</i>, not <i>President McAleese</i>, as had been the form used for the first six presidents, from President Hyde to President Hillery. <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a> asked initially to be called <i>Tony</i>. First names, or even <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nicknames</a>, are often widely used among politicians in the US, even in formal situations (as an extreme example, President <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">James Earl "Jimmy" Carter</a> chose to take the Oath of Office using his nickname). One notable exception involves judges: a judge of any court is almost invariably addressed as "Your Honor" while presiding over his or her court, and often at other times as well. This style has been removed in the <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">Republic of Ireland</a>, where judges are addressed only as "Judge".
</p><p>However, styles are still widely used in formal documents and correspondence between heads of state, such as in a <a href="/wiki/Letter_of_credence" title="Letter of credence">letter of credence</a> accrediting an ambassador from one head of state to another.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Self-styled">Self-styled</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Style_(manner_of_address)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Self-styled">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <i>self-styled</i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Soi-disant" class="mw-redirect" title="Soi-disant">soi-disant</a></i>, roughly means awarding a <i>style</i> to oneself, often without adequate justification or authority, but the expression often refers to descriptions or <a href="/wiki/Title" title="Title">titles</a> (such as "aunt", "expert", "Doctor", or "King"), rather than true <i>styles</i> in the sense of this article.
</p>
