<p><b>Simon Baron-Cohen</b> <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:85%;"><a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_British_Academy" title="Fellow of the British Academy">FBA</a>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_British_Psychological_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="Fellow of the British Psychological Society">FBPsS</a></span> (born 15 August 1958) is a British <a href="/wiki/Clinical_psychologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Clinical psychologist">clinical psychologist</a> and professor of <a href="/wiki/Developmental_psychopathology" title="Developmental psychopathology">developmental psychopathology</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a>. He is the Director of the University's <a href="/wiki/Autism_Research_Centre" title="Autism Research Centre">Autism Research Centre</a> and a Fellow of <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College</a>. In 1985, Baron-Cohen formulated the <a href="/wiki/Mind-blindness" title="Mind-blindness">mind-blindness</a> theory of <a href="/wiki/Autism" title="Autism">autism</a>, the evidence for which he collated and published in 1995. In 1997, he formulated the <a href="/wiki/Extreme_male_brain" class="mw-redirect" title="Extreme male brain">fetal sex steroid theory of autism</a>, the key test of which was published in 2015. He has also made major contributions to the fields of typical cognitive sex differences, autism prevalence and screening, autism genetics, autism <a href="/wiki/Neuroimaging" title="Neuroimaging">neuroimaging</a>, autism and technical ability, and <a href="/wiki/Synaesthesia" class="mw-redirect" title="Synaesthesia">synaesthesia</a>.  However, his views on autism and sex differences, such as the fetal sex steroid theory, are controversial, some critics asserting that Baron-Cohen's theories are based on subjective perceptions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life_and_education">Personal life and education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_Baron-Cohen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Personal life and education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Baron-Cohen was born into a middle-class <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_England" title="History of the Jews in England">Jewish</a> family in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Suzie_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Suzie-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  He completed a <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">BA</a> degree in <a href="/wiki/Human_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="Human Sciences">Human Sciences</a> at <a href="/wiki/New_College,_Oxford" title="New College, Oxford">New College, Oxford</a>, and an <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Philosophy" title="Master of Philosophy">MPhil</a> degree in Clinical Psychology at the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Psychiatry" class="mw-redirect" title="Institute of Psychiatry">Institute of Psychiatry</a>, <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_London" title="King&#39;s College London">King's College London</a>. He received a <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">PhD</a> degree in <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">Psychology</a> at <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">University College London</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-ARCBio_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ARCBio-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> his doctoral research was in collaboration with his supervisor <a href="/wiki/Uta_Frith" title="Uta Frith">Uta Frith</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-PMC2409181_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PMC2409181-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He married Bridget Lindley, a family rights lawyer whom he had met at Oxford, in 1987. She died of breast cancer in 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Baron-Cohen has three children, the eldest of whom is screenwriter and director Sam Baron.<sup id="cite_ref-Time_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Time-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He has an elder brother <a href="/wiki/Dan_Baron_Cohen" title="Dan Baron Cohen">Dan Baron Cohen</a> and three younger siblings, brother <a href="/wiki/Ash_Baron-Cohen" title="Ash Baron-Cohen">Ash Baron-Cohen</a> and sisters Suzie and Liz.<sup id="cite_ref-Suzie_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Suzie-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Their cousins are the actor and comedian <a href="/wiki/Sacha_Baron_Cohen" title="Sacha Baron Cohen">Sacha Baron Cohen</a> and the composer <a href="/wiki/Erran_Baron_Cohen" title="Erran Baron Cohen">Erran Baron Cohen</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-QA_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QA-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Simon Baron-Cohen's surname, in contrast to that of most of his family, includes a hyphen; this arose from a typographical error which he never corrected in the printing of his first professional article.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Autism_research">Autism research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_Baron-Cohen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Autism research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While a member of the Cognitive Development Unit (CDU) in <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> in 1985, Baron-Cohen was lead author (with <a href="/wiki/Alan_M._Leslie" title="Alan M. Leslie">Alan M. Leslie</a> and Uta Frith) of the first study proposing a correlation between children with <a href="/wiki/Autism" title="Autism">autism</a> and delays in the development of a <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_mind" title="Theory of mind">theory of mind</a> ("ToM").<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SFARI_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SFARI-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> A theory of mind is the ability to imagine other people's emotions and thoughts, and it is a skill that according to Baron-Cohen's research is typically delayed developmentally in children with autism.<sup id="cite_ref-SFARI_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SFARI-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Professor_Simon_Baron-Cohen.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Professor_Simon_Baron-Cohen.jpg/220px-Professor_Simon_Baron-Cohen.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="224" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Professor_Simon_Baron-Cohen.jpg/330px-Professor_Simon_Baron-Cohen.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Professor_Simon_Baron-Cohen.jpg/440px-Professor_Simon_Baron-Cohen.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3536" data-file-height="3604" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Professor_Simon_Baron-Cohen.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Baron-Cohen in 2011</div></div></div>
<p>Baron-Cohen and his colleagues discovered in 1987 the first evidence that experiences in <a href="/wiki/Synaesthesia" class="mw-redirect" title="Synaesthesia">synaesthesia</a> remain consistent over time; they also found synaesthesia to be measurable via neuroimaging techniques.<sup id="cite_ref-Synth_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Synth-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> His team has investigated whether synaesthesia is connected to autism.<sup id="cite_ref-StudyLinks_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-StudyLinks-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1997 Baron-Cohen developed the <a href="/wiki/Empathizing%E2%80%93systemizing_theory" title="Empathizing–systemizing theory">empathising–systemising theory</a>. His theory is that a cognitive profile with a systemising drive that is stronger than empathising is associated with maths, science and technology skills, and exists in families with <a href="/wiki/Autism_spectrum" title="Autism spectrum">autism spectrum</a> disorders. He suspects that if individuals with a "systemising" focus are <a href="/wiki/Assortative_mating" title="Assortative mating">selecting each other as mates</a>, they are more likely to have children with autism.<sup id="cite_ref-Time_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Time-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Scientific_American_2012_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scientific_American_2012-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> He postulates that more individuals with autistic traits are marrying each other and having children.<sup id="cite_ref-Time_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Time-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He said that "In essence, some geeks may be carriers of genes for autism: in their own life, they do not demonstrate any signs of severe autism, but when they pair up and have kids, their children may get a double dose of autism genes and traits. In this way, assortative mating between technical-minded people might spread autism genes."<sup id="cite_ref-Scientific_American_2012_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scientific_American_2012-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Baron-Cohen's work in systemising-empathising led him to investigate whether higher levels of fetal <a href="/wiki/Testosterone" title="Testosterone">testosterone</a> explain the increased prevalence of autism spectrum disorders among males;<sup id="cite_ref-Scientific_American_2012_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Scientific_American_2012-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> his theory is known as the "<a href="/wiki/Extreme_male_brain" class="mw-redirect" title="Extreme male brain">extreme male brain</a>" theory of autism.<sup id="cite_ref-QA_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QA-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> A review of his book <i>The Essential Difference</i> published in <i><a href="/wiki/Nature_(journal)" title="Nature (journal)">Nature</a></i> in 2003 summarises his proposal as: "the male brain is programmed to systemize and the female brain to empathize&#160;...&#160;<a href="/wiki/Asperger_syndrome" title="Asperger syndrome">Asperger's syndrome</a> represents the extreme male brain".<sup id="cite_ref-Benenson_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Benenson-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2001 he developed the <a href="/wiki/Autism-spectrum_quotient" title="Autism-spectrum quotient">autism-spectrum quotient</a>, a set of fifty questions that can be used to help determine whether or not an adult exhibits symptoms of autism.<sup id="cite_ref-Woodbury-Smith_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Woodbury-Smith-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The AQ has subsequently been used in hundreds of studies including one study of half a million people, showing robust sex differences and higher scores in those who work in STEM (<a href="/wiki/Science,_technology,_engineering,_and_mathematics" title="Science, technology, engineering, and mathematics">Science, Technology, Engineering and Mathematics</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Baron-Cohen developed the Mindreading software for special education,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> which was nominated for an award from the <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_of_Film_and_Television_Arts" title="British Academy of Film and Television Arts">British Academy of Film and Television Arts</a> (BAFTA) interactive award in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> His lab developed <i><a href="/wiki/The_Transporters" title="The Transporters">The Transporters</a></i>, an animation series designed to teach children with autism to recognise and understand emotions. The series was also nominated for a BAFTA award.<sup id="cite_ref-Time_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Time-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">Criticisms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_Baron-Cohen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Criticisms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="box-POV plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-POV" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Unbalanced scales.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Unbalanced_scales.svg/45px-Unbalanced_scales.svg.png" decoding="async" width="45" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Unbalanced_scales.svg/68px-Unbalanced_scales.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/Unbalanced_scales.svg/90px-Unbalanced_scales.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="400" data-file-height="354" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">The <b><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Neutral_point_of_view" title="Wikipedia:Neutral point of view">neutrality</a> of this article is <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NPOV_dispute" title="Wikipedia:NPOV dispute">disputed</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Relevant discussion may be found on the <a href="/wiki/Talk:Simon_Baron-Cohen##" title="Talk:Simon Baron-Cohen">talk page</a>. Please do not remove this message until <a href="/wiki/Template:POV#When_to_remove" title="Template:POV">conditions to do so are met</a>.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">June 2019</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table> <div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Empathizing%E2%80%93systemizing_theory#Criticism" title="Empathizing–systemizing theory">Empathizing–systemizing theory §&#160;Criticism</a></div>
<p>Baron-Cohen has been criticized for his "empathizing-systemizing theory", which claims that humans may be classified on the basis of their scores along two dimensions (empathizing and systemizing); and that females tend to score higher on the empathizing dimension and males tend to score higher on the systemizing dimension.  Columnist at <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Madeleine_Bunting" title="Madeleine Bunting">Madeleine Bunting</a> has summarized some of these aspects in the 2010 article "The truth about sex difference is that if men are from Mars, so are women".<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>Some research in systemizing and empathizing in early life seems to indicate that boys and girls develop in similar ways, casting considerable doubt on the theory of sex differences in these areas, but the findings have not been replicated.<sup id="cite_ref-Nash_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nash-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  <i>Phenomenology and the Cognitive Sciences</i> characterized <i>The Essential Difference</i> as "very disappointing" with a "superficial notion of intelligence", concluding that Baron-Cohen's major claims about mind-blindness and systemizing–empathizing are "at best, dubious".<sup id="cite_ref-Levy_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levy-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>  According to <i><a href="/wiki/Time_(magazine)" title="Time (magazine)">Time</a></i> magazine, his views on systemising traits had "earned him the ire of some parents of autistic children, who complain that he underestimates their families' suffering".<sup id="cite_ref-Time_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Time-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <i>Time</i> said that while research from <a href="/wiki/Washington_University_in_St._Louis" title="Washington University in St. Louis">Washington University in St. Louis</a> did not support the assortive mating theory, a survey finding that autism was twice as high in Eindhoven (the Silicon Valley of the Netherlands) had "breathed new life" into Baron-Cohen's theory.<sup id="cite_ref-Time_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Time-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Extreme_male_brain" class="mw-redirect" title="Extreme male brain">extreme male brain</a> theory has been criticized on several grounds. Some critics claim that the tests behind this theory are based on gender stereotypes, and not on hard science.  Professor <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Lord_(autism_researcher)" title="Catherine Lord (autism researcher)">Catherine Lord</a> of <a href="/wiki/UCLA" class="mw-redirect" title="UCLA">UCLA</a> says the theory is based on "gross misinterpretations" of developmental data.  Professor David Skuse of <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">University College London</a> claimed that communication differences between genders are likely to be small.  Meng-Chuan Lai, assistant professor of psychiatry at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">University of Toronto</a>, says the results have not been replicated.<sup id="cite_ref-Spectrum_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Spectrum-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p><i>Time</i> magazine has also criticized the assortative mating theory proposed by Baron-Cohen, claiming that it is largely speculative and based on anecdotal evidence. The theory claims that autism rates are increasing because "systemizers", individuals with more autistic traits, are more likely to marry each other and are more likely to have autistic offspring due to relatively recent societal changes.<sup id="cite_ref-Assortative_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Assortative-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>  James McGrath has criticized the <a href="/wiki/Autism-spectrum_quotient" title="Autism-spectrum quotient">autism-spectrum quotient</a>, writing that the score increases if one indicates interest in mathematics, and decreases if one indicates interest in literature or art.  He claims that this leads to the false notion that most autistic people are strong in math.<sup id="cite_ref-Conversation_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Conversation-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Critics also say that because his work has focused on higher-functioning individuals with autism spectrum disorders, it requires independent replication with broader samples<sup id="cite_ref-Buchen_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Buchen-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and that his theories are based on subjective perceptions.<sup id="cite_ref-Costandi_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Costandi-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> However, one study confirmed his prediction that prenatal testosterone is elevated in autism.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2009 study led by Baron-Cohen which reported that autistic individuals possessed superior <a href="/wiki/Visual_acuity" title="Visual acuity">visual acuity</a> has been subject to heavy criticism.  The developers of the software he used said that his results were impossible based on the technology used in the study.  Additionally, the results of the study could not be replicated in a follow-up study.<sup id="cite_ref-BachDakin_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BachDakin-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-CloseEye_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CloseEye-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Discover_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Discover-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Baron-Cohen's supposition that <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">Albert Einstein</a> displayed autistic traits has been met with scepticism by <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_San_Francisco" title="University of California, San Francisco">UCSF</a> psychiatrist Glenn Elliot. Elliot views attempting to diagnose on the basis of biographical information as extremely unreliable, and claims that any behaviour can have various causes.  Baron-Cohen responded by claiming that he wanted to find niches for autistic people in society where they could use potential strengths, stating that “This condition can make people depressed or suicidal.”<sup id="cite_ref-HistoricalFigures_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoricalFigures-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Writing for the <a href="/wiki/Autism_Society_of_America" title="Autism Society of America">Autism Society of America</a>, Jill Escher has criticized Simon Baron-Cohen's support for <a href="/wiki/Neurodiversity" title="Neurodiversity">neurodiversity</a>, claiming that many autistic people lack basic life skills and will need lifelong care because of autism, not because of <a href="/wiki/Comorbid" class="mw-redirect" title="Comorbid">comorbid</a> conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-Escher_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Escher-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>  In August 2018 Baron-Cohen criticized the <a href="/wiki/Twitter" title="Twitter">Twitter</a> <a href="/wiki/Hashtag" title="Hashtag">hashtag</a> #EndAutismNow, describing it as <a href="/wiki/Hate_speech" title="Hate speech">hate speech</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eugenics" title="Eugenics">eugenics</a>, while comparing it to the goals of the <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">Nazi Party</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ku_Klux_Klan" title="Ku Klux Klan">Ku Klux Klan</a>.  Jonathan Ferguson, writing in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times_of_Israel" title="The Times of Israel">The Times of Israel</a></i>, responded that it is inappropriate for Baron-Cohen to compare advocates for an autism cure to the Nazi Party or the Ku Klux Klan.<sup id="cite_ref-TimesofIsrael_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TimesofIsrael-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>  
</p><p>Aiyana Bailin in a <i><a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">Scientific American</a></i> blog claimed that Baron-Cohen did not acknowledge that neurodiversity also views autism as a disability through the <a href="/wiki/Social_model_of_disability" title="Social model of disability">social model of disability</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-SocialModel_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SocialModel-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>  However, Baron-Cohen tweeted that he agreed with her analysis.<sup id="cite_ref-Excellent_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Excellent-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Responding to the same article, Yuval Levental in another <i><a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">Scientific American</a></i> blog disagreed with Baron-Cohen's support for neurodiversity, claiming that the difficulties outweighed the benefits of autism to the point where autism shouldn't define a person's identity.<sup id="cite_ref-Levental_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levental-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>His theories have been described by psychologist <a href="/wiki/Cordelia_Fine" title="Cordelia Fine">Cordelia Fine</a> as "neurosexism".<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The 2017 book <i><a href="/wiki/Inferior:_How_Science_Got_Women_Wrong_and_the_New_Research_That%E2%80%99s_Rewriting_the_Story" class="mw-redirect" title="Inferior: How Science Got Women Wrong and the New Research That’s Rewriting the Story">Inferior: How Science Got Women Wrong and the New Research That’s Rewriting the Story</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Angela_Saini" title="Angela Saini">Angela Saini</a> develops a "take-down"<sup id="cite_ref-:0_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> of the sex differences research from Baron-Cohen and his colleagues, who carry on <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">Darwin</a>'s "idea that man and woman...evolved to meet their roles of hunter and gatherer, respectively."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In response to those criticisms, Baron-Cohen agrees that many of his results have not been replicated.  However, he claims that many of his theories are based on various preliminary testable evidence.<sup id="cite_ref-Buchen_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Buchen-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisations">Organisations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_Baron-Cohen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Organisations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Baron-Cohen is professor of <a href="/wiki/Developmental_psychopathology" title="Developmental psychopathology">developmental psychopathology</a> at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a> in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-ARCBio_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ARCBio-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  He is the Director of the University's <a href="/wiki/Autism_Research_Centre" title="Autism Research Centre">Autism Research Centre</a><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> and a Fellow of <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ARCBio_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ARCBio-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He is a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/British_Psychological_Society" title="British Psychological Society">British Psychological Society</a> (BPS),<sup id="cite_ref-Chartered_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chartered-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> the British Academy,<sup id="cite_ref-BAFellow_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BAFellow-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Psychological_Science" title="Association for Psychological Science">Association for Psychological Science</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> He is a <a href="/wiki/British_Psychological_Society#Chartership" title="British Psychological Society">BPS Chartered Psychologist</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Chartered_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Chartered-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He serves as Vice-President of the <a href="/wiki/National_Autistic_Society" title="National Autistic Society">National Autistic Society</a> (UK),<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> and was the 2012 Chairman of the <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_for_Health_and_Care_Excellence" title="National Institute for Health and Care Excellence">National Institute for Health and Care Excellence</a> (NICE) Guideline Development Group for adults with autism.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> He has served as Vice-President of the International Society for Autism Research (INSAR).<sup id="cite_ref-ARCBio_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ARCBio-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He is co-<a href="/wiki/Editor-in-chief" title="Editor-in-chief">editor in chief</a> of the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Molecular_Autism" title="Molecular Autism">Molecular Autism</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> He is President-Elect of INSAR.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He is the Chair of the Psychology Section of the British Academy.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recognition">Recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_Baron-Cohen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Baron-Cohen was awarded the 1990 <a href="/wiki/Spearman_Medal" title="Spearman Medal">Spearman Medal</a> from the BPS,<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> the McAndless Award from the <a href="/wiki/American_Psychological_Association" title="American Psychological Association">American Psychological Association</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> the 1993 May Davidson Award for Clinical Psychology from the BPS,<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> and the 2006 presidents' Award from the BPS.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> He was awarded the Kanner-Asperger Medal in 2013 by the <i>Wissenschaftliche Gesellschaft Autismus-Spektrum</i> as a Lifetime Achievement Award for his contributions to autism research.<sup id="cite_ref-WGAS_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WGAS-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
