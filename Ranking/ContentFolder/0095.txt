<p><b>Robert Hanbury Brown</b>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Australia" title="Order of Australia">AC</a> <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society" title="Royal Society">FRS</a><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> (31 August 1916 – 16 January 2002) was a British <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">astronomer</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physicist</a> born in <a href="/wiki/Aruvankadu" title="Aruvankadu">Aruvankadu</a>, India. He made notable contributions to the development of <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> and later conducted pioneering work in the field of <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">radio astronomy</a>.
</p><p>With <a href="/wiki/Richard_Q._Twiss" title="Richard Q. Twiss">Richard Q. Twiss</a> he developed the <a href="/wiki/Hanbury_Brown_and_Twiss_effect" title="Hanbury Brown and Twiss effect">Hanbury Brown and Twiss effect</a> leading to the creation of <a href="/wiki/Intensity_interferometer" title="Intensity interferometer">intensity interferometers</a>. Hanbury Brown was one of the main designers of the <a href="/wiki/Narrabri_Stellar_Intensity_Interferometer" title="Narrabri Stellar Intensity Interferometer">Narrabri Stellar Intensity Interferometer</a> and received a number of honours and awards for his work.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">Early years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Hanbury_Brown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hanbury Brown was born in <a href="/wiki/Aruvankadu" title="Aruvankadu">Aruvankadu</a>, the Nilgiris, British India in 1916, the son of an army officer. At age 8 he was sent to England to attend <a href="/wiki/Cottesmore_School" title="Cottesmore School">Cottesmore preparatory school</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hove" title="Hove">Hove</a>, where he was educated in primarily non-scientific subjects. In 1930, at age 14, Hanbury Brown went on to attend <a href="/wiki/Tonbridge_School" title="Tonbridge School">Tonbridge School</a> in Kent for only two years before changing to <a href="/wiki/City_College_Brighton_%26_Hove" title="City College Brighton &amp; Hove">Brighton Technical College</a>. Though originally planning to become a <a href="/wiki/Classics" title="Classics">classics</a> scholar, the change to a technical college came due to influences from those around him. His grandfather, Sir Robert Hanbury Brown, <a href="/wiki/K.C.M.G." class="mw-redirect" title="K.C.M.G.">K.C.M.G.</a>, a notable irrigation engineer, was also one of the early pioneers of radio, and following Hanbury Brown's parents' divorce, his legal guardian was a consulting radio engineer. At Brighton Technical College, Hanbury Brown studied for an external <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor&#39;s degree">Bachelor's degree</a> in <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a> from the <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a>, which he received at the age of 19. At this time he also published his first paper, 'Lamp polar curves on the cathode-ray oscillograph'.<sup id="cite_ref-physicstoday_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-physicstoday-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-archive_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-archive-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Hanbury_Brown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Though initially planning to study for a Ph.D. at <a href="/wiki/Imperial_College,_London" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial College, London">Imperial College, London</a>, Hanbury Brown drew the attention of <a href="/wiki/Henry_Tizard" title="Henry Tizard">Henry Tizard</a>. Tizard recruited Hanbury Brown to work for what would become the <a href="/wiki/Telecommunications_Research_Establishment" title="Telecommunications Research Establishment">Telecommunications Research Establishment</a> on <a href="/wiki/Chain_Home" title="Chain Home">Chain Home</a> in 1936, where he worked until joining the <a href="/wiki/Tizard_Mission" title="Tizard Mission">Tizard Mission</a> in 1942; spending 3 years in <a href="/wiki/Washington,_DC" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington, DC">Washington, D.C.</a> to work with the Combined Research Group at the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Naval_Research_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="U.S. Naval Research Laboratory">Naval Research Laboratory</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-archive_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-archive-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nature_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nature-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> By 1947 a consultancy that had been set up by Sir <a href="/wiki/Robert_Watson-Watt" title="Robert Watson-Watt">Robert Watson-Watt</a>, the father of radar, offered more interesting prospects for the conversion of wartime developments into peacetime technologies. Hanbury Brown was recruited and worked as a consulting engineer until Watson-Watt moved the firm to Canada. After considering his career possibilities, Hanbury Brown joined <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Lovell" title="Bernard Lovell">Bernard Lovell</a>'s radio astronomy group at the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_University_of_Manchester" title="Victoria University of Manchester">University of Manchester</a> in 1949 to work on his PhD.<sup id="cite_ref-archive_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-archive-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the University of Manchester, Hanbury Brown worked with <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyril_Hazard&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cyril Hazard (page does not exist)">Cyril Hazard</a> to modify the 218-ft radio telescope built for the study of <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" class="mw-redirect" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a> into a fully functioning radio telescope.<sup id="cite_ref-physicstoday_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-physicstoday-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Using it they showed, among other things, that radio waves were being emitted from the <a href="/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy" title="Andromeda Galaxy">Andromeda Galaxy</a>; proving that such emissions were not just from our own galaxy. After completion of the <a href="/wiki/Lovell_Telescope" title="Lovell Telescope">Lovell Telescope</a> Hanbury was among those who used it to discover <a href="/wiki/Quasar" title="Quasar">quasars</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nature_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nature-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hanbury Brown is primarily known for his work on <a href="/wiki/Interferometry" title="Interferometry">interferometry</a>, in particular the <a href="/wiki/Hanbury_Brown_and_Twiss_effect" title="Hanbury Brown and Twiss effect">Hanbury Brown and Twiss effect</a> which led to <a href="/wiki/Intensity_interferometer" title="Intensity interferometer">intensity interferometers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-physicstoday_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-physicstoday-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Despite opposition from some in the scientific community who argued that his predictions violated the <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_physics" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws of physics">laws of physics</a>, Hanbury Brown and Twiss showed that their theory was correct through a number of observations.<sup id="cite_ref-nature_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nature-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1962 Hanbury Brown moved to Australia to work at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Sydney" title="University of Sydney">University of Sydney</a>. There he built his next interferometer – the <a href="/wiki/Narrabri_Stellar_Intensity_Interferometer" title="Narrabri Stellar Intensity Interferometer">Narrabri Stellar Intensity Interferometer</a> – in a sheep paddock outside <a href="/wiki/Narrabri" title="Narrabri">Narrabri</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, consisting of two 23&#160;ft telescopes.<sup id="cite_ref-nature_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nature-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Using it, Hanbury Brown succeeded in measuring the diameters of 32 stars, thus producing the first <a href="/wiki/Empirical" class="mw-redirect" title="Empirical">empirical</a> temperature scale for hot stars.<sup id="cite_ref-physicstoday_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-physicstoday-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Despite only aiming to stay in Australia for two years, he stayed for a total of 27; going on to receive the Companion of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Australia" title="Order of Australia">Order of Australia</a> honour in 1986 for his work.<sup id="cite_ref-physicstoday_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-physicstoday-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Hanbury_Brown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hanbury Brown married Heather Hilda Chesterman in 1952 with whom he had one daughter and two sons (twins).<sup id="cite_ref-archive_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-archive-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He was rumoured to have prompted the term <a href="/wiki/Boffin" title="Boffin">boffin</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-physicstoday_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-physicstoday-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (July 2017)">failed verification</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Brown died in <a href="/wiki/Andover,_Hampshire" title="Andover, Hampshire">Andover</a>, Hampshire on 16 January 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-archive_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-archive-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honours_and_awards">Honours and awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Hanbury_Brown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Honours and awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In March 1960 he was elected a <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">Fellow of the Royal Society</a> of London and in 1971 was awarded their <a href="/wiki/Hughes_Medal" title="Hughes Medal">Hughes Medal</a> for " his efforts in developing the optical stellar <a href="/wiki/Intensity_interferometer" title="Intensity interferometer">intensity interferometer</a> and for his observations of <a href="/wiki/Spica" title="Spica">Spica</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In 1968, he received the <a href="/wiki/Eddington_Medal" title="Eddington Medal">Eddington Medal</a> jointly with Twiss (see <a href="/wiki/Hanbury_Brown_and_Twiss_effect" title="Hanbury Brown and Twiss effect">Hanbury Brown and Twiss effect</a>). He also won the <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Ranken_Lyle_Medal" title="Thomas Ranken Lyle Medal">Thomas Ranken Lyle Medal</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Academy_of_Science" title="Australian Academy of Science">Australian Academy of Science</a> in 1972.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In 1982 he was named President of the <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">International Astronomical Union</a>, a title he retained until the end of his term in 1985. In 1986 he was appointed Companion of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Australia" title="Order of Australia">Order of Australia</a>. He was awarded the <a href="/wiki/Albert_A._Michelson_Medal" class="mw-redirect" title="Albert A. Michelson Medal">Albert A. Michelson Medal</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Institute" title="Franklin Institute">Franklin Institute</a> in 1982, jointly with <a href="/wiki/Richard_Q._Twiss" title="Richard Q. Twiss">Richard Q. Twiss</a><sup id="cite_ref-MichelsonMedal_Laureates_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MichelsonMedal_Laureates-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Flinders_Medal_and_Lecture" title="Matthew Flinders Medal and Lecture">Matthew Flinders Medal and Lecture</a> the same year.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publications">Publications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Hanbury_Brown&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Publications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He wrote an autobiographical account of the development of airborne and ground-based radar, and his subsequent work on radio astronomy. Since he was rumoured to have been the original <a href="/wiki/Boffin" title="Boffin">boffin</a> who inspired the term, he called these recollections <i>Boffin: A Personal Story of the Early Days of Radar, Radio Astronomy and Quantum Optics</i>
</p>
