<p><b>Belsar's Hill</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Hillfort" title="Hillfort">hillfort</a> near <a href="/wiki/Willingham,_Cambridgeshire" title="Willingham, Cambridgeshire">Willingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belsar%27s_Hill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Belsar's Hill is an oval-shaped area, 265–220 metres (869–722&#160;ft), enclosing 6 acres (24,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>).<sup id="cite_ref-CastleFortsBatles_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CastleFortsBatles-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HistoricEngland_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoricEngland-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> At the time of its construction, it would have constituted an island of firm soil surrounded by waterlogged <a href="/wiki/Fen" title="Fen">fens</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CastleFortsBatles_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CastleFortsBatles-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The defences are believed to have consisted of a single 10–15 metres (33–49&#160;ft) wide, 1.5 metres (4.9&#160;ft) ditch, and a rampart on a 2–4 metres (6.6–13.1&#160;ft) high bank.<sup id="cite_ref-CastleFortsBatles_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CastleFortsBatles-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HistoricEngland_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoricEngland-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The hill was situated along the Aldreth Causeway, and is transected by a 19th-century track, believed to be a redirection of the original Causeway.<sup id="cite_ref-CastleFortsBatles_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CastleFortsBatles-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belsar%27s_Hill&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The location of the fortification, on the boundary between the tribal lands of the <a href="/wiki/Iceni" title="Iceni">Iceni</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catuvellauni" title="Catuvellauni">Catuvellauni</a>, and a similarity to other <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> hillforts such as <a href="/wiki/Arbury_Banks,_Hertfordshire" title="Arbury Banks, Hertfordshire">Arbury Banks</a> or <a href="/wiki/Wandlebury_Hill_Fort" title="Wandlebury Hill Fort">Wandlebury</a>, suggests an Iron Age provenance.<sup id="cite_ref-CastleFortsBatles_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CastleFortsBatles-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-RomanBritain_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RomanBritain-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is believed that the structure was reoccupied during the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CastleFortsBatles_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CastleFortsBatles-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HistoricEngland_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoricEngland-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> During this time period the Norman forces were combating a local revolt under <a href="/wiki/Hereward_the_Wake" title="Hereward the Wake">Hereward the Wake</a>, and the hillfort would have been strategically located on the Aldreth Causway, the main road to <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Ely" title="Isle of Ely">Ely</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-CastleFortsBatles_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CastleFortsBatles-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-HistoricEngland_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HistoricEngland-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> A postulated name of "Belassise", from the <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> "bel assis" (meaning <i>well seated/placed</i>), also suggests a Norman link.<sup id="cite_ref-GatehouseGazeteer_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GatehouseGazeteer-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-TheAnarchy_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheAnarchy-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> An alternative suggested etymology, stemming from the Norman general named "Belasius", also suggests Norman involvement.<sup id="cite_ref-HartshorneEtymology_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HartshorneEtymology-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The importance of this location in the defence of Ely has led some to suggest that the ringfort may have been the Castle of Aldreth.<sup id="cite_ref-TheAnarchy_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TheAnarchy-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to the recorded history of the revolt led by Hereward the Wake at the turn of the 11th century, Hereward's troops retreated to the Isle of Ely, surrounded by fenland which was impassable to the Norman cavalry. An attempt was made to build a mile-long causeway, but the weight of the Norman armour caused the causeway to sink. Eventually, the Norman troops bribed a local monk to show them a safe passage across the fen.<sup id="cite_ref-CastleFortsBatles_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CastleFortsBatles-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Cambridgeshire_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridgeshire-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
