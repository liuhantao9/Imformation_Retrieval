	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Superbike World Championship</h1>

<p><b>Superbike World Championship</b> (also known as <b>SBK</b>, <b>World Superbike</b>, <b>WSB</b>, or <b>WSBK</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Motorsport" title="Motorsport">motorsport</a> <a href="/wiki/Road_racing" title="Road racing">road racing</a> series for modified production <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle" title="Motorcycle">motorcycles</a> also known as <a href="/wiki/Superbike_racing" title="Superbike racing">superbike racing</a>. The championship was founded in <a href="/wiki/1988_Superbike_World_Championship" title="1988 Superbike World Championship">1988</a>. The Superbike World Championship consists of a series of rounds held on permanent racing facilities. Each round has two full length races and, from 2019, an additional ten-lap sprint race known as the Superpole race.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The results of all three races are combined to determine two annual World Championships, one for <a href="/wiki/List_of_Superbike_World_champions#Riders" title="List of Superbike World champions">riders</a> and one for <a href="/wiki/List_of_Superbike_World_champions#Manufacturers" title="List of Superbike World champions">manufacturers</a>.
</p><p>The motorcycles that race in the championship are tuned versions of <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle" title="Motorcycle">motorcycles</a> available for sale to the public, by contrast with <a href="/wiki/Grand_Prix_motorcycle_racing" title="Grand Prix motorcycle racing">MotoGP</a> where purpose built machines are used. MotoGP is the motorcycle world's equivalent of <a href="/wiki/Formula_One" title="Formula One">Formula One</a>, whereas Superbike racing is similar to <a href="/wiki/Touring_car_racing" title="Touring car racing">touring car racing</a>.
</p><p>Europe is Superbike World Championship's traditional centre and leading market.<sup id="cite_ref-revival_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-revival-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> However, rounds have been held in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Qatar" title="Qatar">Qatar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> and the series plans on keeping extra-European circuits in rotation. An <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesian</a> race was also proposed for the 2008 season, but this was later canceled by the FIM.<sup id="cite_ref-Indonesia_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Indonesia-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The championship is regulated by the <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_Motocyclisme" title="Fédération Internationale de Motocyclisme">FIM</a>, the international governing body of motorcycle racing. As of 2013 the championship is organised by <a href="/wiki/Dorna_Sports" title="Dorna Sports">Dorna</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superbike_World_Championship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Recentism plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-style ambox-Recentism" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/40px-Edit-clear.svg.png" decoding="async" width="40" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/60px-Edit-clear.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Edit-clear.svg/80px-Edit-clear.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="48" data-file-height="48" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article or section <b>appears to be <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Recentism" title="Wikipedia:Recentism">slanted towards recent events</a>.</b><span class="hide-when-compact"> Please try to keep recent events in historical perspective and add more content related to non-recent events.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">April 2010</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Superbike_World_champions" title="List of Superbike World champions">List of Superbike World champions</a></div>
<p>The Superbike World Championship began in <a href="/wiki/1988_Superbike_World_Championship" title="1988 Superbike World Championship">1988</a>, being open to modified versions of road bike models available to the public. For many years, the formula allowed for machines with 1,000&#160;cc <a href="/wiki/V-twin_engine" title="V-twin engine">V-twin engines</a> (principally <a href="/wiki/Ducati" class="mw-redirect" title="Ducati">Ducati</a>, but later <a href="/wiki/Aprilia" title="Aprilia">Aprilia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Honda" title="Honda">Honda</a>) to go up against the 750&#160;cc <a href="/wiki/Inline-four_engine" title="Inline-four engine">four-cylinder engines</a> (<a href="/wiki/Honda" title="Honda">Honda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_Motor_Company" title="Yamaha Motor Company">Yamaha</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kawasaki_motorcycles" title="Kawasaki motorcycles">Kawasaki</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suzuki" title="Suzuki">Suzuki</a>). For the first few seasons Honda won with the <a href="/wiki/Honda_VFR750R" title="Honda VFR750R">RC30</a>, but gradually the twins got the upper hand. Using 1,000&#160;cc V-twin engines benefited Ducati and it was able to dominate the championship for many years, but the 750&#160;cc was second or third each year between 1994 and 1999.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Held under the <a href="/wiki/International_Motorcycling_Federation" class="mw-redirect" title="International Motorcycling Federation">FIM</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Formula_TT" title="Formula TT">Formula TT</a> from 1977 to 1989 once constituted the official motorcycle World Cup. Having proven itself both popular and commercially viable, it was decided by the end of the 1990 season to end the Formula TT and the Superbike World Championship would succeed it.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cf1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Cf1.jpg/220px-Cf1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Cf1.jpg/330px-Cf1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Cf1.jpg/440px-Cf1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="683" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cf1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Carl_Fogarty" title="Carl Fogarty">Carl Fogarty</a> won the Superbike World Championship four times with <a href="/wiki/Ducati_Corse" title="Ducati Corse">Ducati</a></div></div></div>
<p>From <a href="/wiki/1993_Superbike_World_Championship" title="1993 Superbike World Championship">1993</a> to <a href="/wiki/1999_Superbike_World_Championship" title="1999 Superbike World Championship">1999</a> <a href="/wiki/Carl_Fogarty" title="Carl Fogarty">Carl Fogarty</a> and Ducati dominated, Fogarty won the title a record four times and finished as runner-up twice on factory Ducatis. <a href="/wiki/Troy_Corser" title="Troy Corser">Troy Corser</a> also won the 1996 title and finished as runner-up in 1995, both times on a Ducati.
</p><p>Realizing that 1,000&#160;cc V-twin engines suited the superbike racing formula more, Honda introduced its own V-Twin powered motorcycle the <a href="/wiki/Honda_RC51" title="Honda RC51">VTR1000 SPW</a> in <a href="/wiki/2000_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2000 Superbike World Championship">2000</a>. The result was clear right away as <a href="/wiki/Colin_Edwards" title="Colin Edwards">Colin Edwards</a> won the championship in the bike's first year of competition. Ducati regained the title in <a href="/wiki/2001_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2001 Superbike World Championship">2001</a> with <a href="/wiki/Troy_Bayliss" title="Troy Bayliss">Troy Bayliss</a>. Colin Edwards again reclaimed the title in 2002 on the same VTR1000 SPW bike.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2002">2002</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superbike_World_Championship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 2002">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2002_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2002 Superbike World Championship">2002 Superbike World Championship</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Colin_Edwards" title="Colin Edwards">Colin Edwards</a> won his second <a href="/wiki/List_of_Superbike_World_champions" title="List of Superbike World champions">championship</a> in what was arguably the most impressive comeback in the history of motorcycle racing. The season started with <a href="/wiki/Troy_Bayliss" title="Troy Bayliss">Troy Bayliss</a> winning the first 6 races and by the end of race 1 at <a href="/wiki/Mazda_Raceway_Laguna_Seca" class="mw-redirect" title="Mazda Raceway Laguna Seca">WeatherTech Raceway Laguna Seca</a> he had 14 wins and was leading the championship by 58 points. Race 2 at WeatherTech Raceway Laguna Seca was the start of <a href="/wiki/Colin_Edwards" title="Colin Edwards">Colin Edwards</a>' comeback, he went on to win all 9 remaining races and (aided by a race 2 crash for Bayliss at <a href="/wiki/TT_Circuit_Assen" title="TT Circuit Assen">Assen</a>) Edwards won the championship at the final race of the season at <a href="/wiki/Autodromo_Enzo_e_Dino_Ferrari" title="Autodromo Enzo e Dino Ferrari">Imola</a>. The final race of the season saw both riders fighting wheel to wheel for the entire race. The race is known by fans as the "Showdown at Imola".
</p><p>The manufacturer's championship was won by <a href="/wiki/Ducati" class="mw-redirect" title="Ducati">Ducati</a>. During these years the Superbike World Championship reached the zenith of its popularity, with global fan and full factory support.<sup id="cite_ref-rules2_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rules2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2003">2003</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superbike_World_Championship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2003">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2003_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2003 Superbike World Championship">2003 Superbike World Championship</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/2003_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2003 Superbike World Championship">2003</a> the FIM changed the rules to allow 1,000&#160;cc machines (twins, triples or four-cylinder) to race. Rule changes in <a href="/wiki/MotoGP" class="mw-redirect" title="MotoGP">MotoGP</a> to allow <a href="/wiki/Four-stroke_engine" title="Four-stroke engine">four-stroke engines</a> meant that the Japanese manufacturers focused their resources there, leaving the Superbike World Championship with limited factory involvement<sup id="cite_ref-1000ccSBK_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1000ccSBK-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> (only Ducati and Suzuki).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/2003_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2003 Superbike World Championship">2003</a> also saw the entry of <a href="/wiki/Carl_Fogarty" title="Carl Fogarty">Carl Fogarty</a>’s Foggy <a href="/wiki/Petronas" title="Petronas">Petronas</a> FP1. The bike was developed under the previous regulations and was powered by a <a href="/wiki/Inline-three_engine" class="mw-redirect" title="Inline-three engine">three cylinder</a> 900&#160;cc engine. With most of the field running Ducati motorcycles, the championship received the derogatory title "the Ducati Cup".<sup id="cite_ref-rules2_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rules2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rules_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rules-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The factory Ducati Team entered the only two Ducati 999s in the field, taking 20 wins from 24 races in a season where all races were won by Ducati. <a href="/wiki/Neil_Hodgson" title="Neil Hodgson">Neil Hodgson</a> won the title on a factory Ducati.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2004">2004</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superbike_World_Championship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2004">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2004_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2004 Superbike World Championship">2004 Superbike World Championship</a></div>
<p>In an effort to create a more competitive field in <a href="/wiki/2004_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2004 Superbike World Championship">2004</a> organizers announced a series of changes to the championship. The most significant was that from <a href="/wiki/2004_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2004 Superbike World Championship">2004</a> the teams have had to run on <a href="/wiki/Pirelli" title="Pirelli">Pirelli</a> control or 'spec' tyres. The decision to award the control tyre to Pirelli was controversial. The Pirelli tyres were considered to be below the standard of <a href="/wiki/Dunlop_Tyres" title="Dunlop Tyres">Dunlop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Michelin" title="Michelin">Michelin</a> that most of the teams had been using. Dunlop looked to take legal action against the decision<sup id="cite_ref-Dunlop_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dunlop-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> while Pirelli claimed that Michelin and Dunlop were also asked if they would be interested in the one-make tyre rule contract.<sup id="cite_ref-Pirelli_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pirelli-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
Partly as a result of the control tyres, Motorcycle Sports Manufacturer Association (Aprilia, Ducati, Honda, Kawasaki, Suzuki and Yamaha) announced that no MSMA teams would participate in the Superbike World Championship, later modifying their statement allowing Ducati to participate.<sup id="cite_ref-rules2_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rules2-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rules_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rules-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A few <a href="/wiki/Privateer_(motorsport)" title="Privateer (motorsport)">privateers</a> chose to run Japanese bikes in <a href="/wiki/2004_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2004 Superbike World Championship">2004</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ten_Kate_Racing" title="Ten Kate Racing">Ten Kate Honda</a> with <a href="/wiki/Chris_Vermuelen" class="mw-redirect" title="Chris Vermuelen">Chris Vermuelen</a> as its rider, won races and actually contended for the title that eventually was won by <a href="/wiki/James_Toseland" title="James Toseland">James Toseland</a> and Ducati.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2005_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2005 Superbike World Championship">2005 Superbike World Championship</a></div>
<p>Following Ten Kate Honda's success Japanese motorcycles made a return in <a href="/wiki/2005_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2005 Superbike World Championship">2005</a> with major teams from all four Japanese manufacturers run through teams ran by European importers.<sup id="cite_ref-rules_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rules-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Troy_Corser" title="Troy Corser">Troy Corser</a> won the 2005 championship, giving Suzuki its first Superbike World Championship title.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2006">2006</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superbike_World_Championship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 2006">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2006_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2006 Superbike World Championship">2006 Superbike World Championship</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/2006_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2006 Superbike World Championship">2006</a> saw the return of Australian <a href="/wiki/Troy_Bayliss" title="Troy Bayliss">Troy Bayliss</a> to the Superbike World Championship after three years in <a href="/wiki/MotoGP" class="mw-redirect" title="MotoGP">MotoGP</a>. The combination of Bayliss and Ducati proved unstoppable and they dominated the season, winning 12 races. Honda-mounted <a href="/wiki/James_Toseland" title="James Toseland">James Toseland</a> and Yamaha's <a href="/wiki/Noriyuki_Haga" title="Noriyuki Haga">Noriyuki Haga</a> battled for second with the British rider coming out on top. Defending champion <a href="/wiki/Troy_Corser" title="Troy Corser">Troy Corser</a> on a Suzuki was fourth. <a href="/wiki/2006_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2006 Superbike World Championship">2006</a> gave the feeling that the Superbike World Championship was 'back' following the years of decline in <a href="/wiki/2003_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2003 Superbike World Championship">2003</a> and <a href="/wiki/2004_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2004 Superbike World Championship">2004</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rules_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rules-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2007">2007</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superbike_World_Championship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 2007">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Max_biaggi_wk_sbk_assen_2007.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Max_biaggi_wk_sbk_assen_2007.jpg/220px-Max_biaggi_wk_sbk_assen_2007.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Max_biaggi_wk_sbk_assen_2007.jpg/330px-Max_biaggi_wk_sbk_assen_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Max_biaggi_wk_sbk_assen_2007.jpg/440px-Max_biaggi_wk_sbk_assen_2007.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="533" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Max_biaggi_wk_sbk_assen_2007.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Max_Biaggi" title="Max Biaggi">Max Biaggi</a> riding his Alstare Suzuki <a href="/wiki/Suzuki_GSX-R1000" title="Suzuki GSX-R1000">GSX-R1000 K7</a> at <a href="/wiki/TT_Circuit_Assen" title="TT Circuit Assen">Assen</a></div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2007_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2007 Superbike World Championship">2007 Superbike World Championship</a></div>
<p>With <a href="/wiki/MotoGP" class="mw-redirect" title="MotoGP">MotoGP</a> machines reduced in capacity from 990&#160;cc to an 800&#160;cc maximum displacement, 1,000&#160;cc Superbikes, both at World Championship and top national championships (<a href="/wiki/AMA_Superbike" class="mw-redirect" title="AMA Superbike">AMA Superbike</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Superbike" class="mw-redirect" title="British Superbike">British Superbike</a>) become the largest capacity bikes (but not the most powerful) being road raced in <a href="/wiki/2007_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2007 Superbike World Championship">2007</a>. While superbikes remained two or more seconds per lap slower than MotoGP bikes at most tracks where both raced, they had equal or more power.<sup id="cite_ref-powerful_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-powerful-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-collision_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-collision-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Troy_Bayliss" title="Troy Bayliss">Troy Bayliss</a> attempted to defend his title, riding once again a <a href="/wiki/Ducati_999" title="Ducati 999">Ducati 999</a>. Though 999 production ended in 2006 and the bike was replaced by the <a href="/wiki/Ducati_1098" title="Ducati 1098">Ducati 1098</a>, Ducati produced 150 limited-edition 999s at an elevated race specification to satisfy <a href="/wiki/Homologation" title="Homologation">homologation</a> requirements. Bayliss' main rivals in his title defense included former MotoGP rider <a href="/wiki/Max_Biaggi" title="Max Biaggi">Max Biaggi</a> riding a Suzuki, 2004 champion <a href="/wiki/James_Toseland" title="James Toseland">James Toseland</a> riding a Honda and <a href="/wiki/Noriyuki_Haga" title="Noriyuki Haga">Noriyuki Haga</a> riding a Yamaha.
</p><p>The combination of some uneventful races in MotoGP<sup id="cite_ref-boring1_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boring1-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-boring2_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-boring2-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and some exciting<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> races in SBK saw the championship's popularity increase even more.
</p><p>The championship was won by James Toseland in the season's last race. His 415 points put him two points ahead of Noriyuki Haga, with former MotoGP winner Max Biaggi following with 397 points on a Suzuki.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2008">2008</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superbike_World_Championship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2008">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2008_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2008 Superbike World Championship">2008 Superbike World Championship</a></div>
<p>After introducing the <a href="/wiki/Ducati_1098" title="Ducati 1098">Ducati 1098</a> in 2007 powered by a 1,099&#160;cc v-twin engine Ducati requested that Superbike rules be changed to allow v-twins of up to 1,200&#160;cc compete against 1,000&#160;cc four-cylinder bikes. Ducati argued that they no longer produced a road-going 1,000&#160;cc V-twin superbike<sup id="cite_ref-Ducati_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ducati-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and that the level of tuning now needed to make their 999 competitive on the race track was too expensive.<sup id="cite_ref-1200_cc_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1200_cc-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Ducati said they would quit if the rules were not changed,<sup id="cite_ref-Ducati_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ducati-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> while Alstare Suzuki team boss Francis Batta also said that his team would quit if the new rules gave Ducati an unfair advantage.<sup id="cite_ref-Suzuki_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Suzuki-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The FIM eventually included the 1,200&#160;cc displacement limit for twins in the <a href="/wiki/2008_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2008 Superbike World Championship">2008</a> superbike rules. According to the new rules, twin-cylinder motorcycles would be 6&#160;kg heavier than four-cylinder machines (168&#160;kg to 162&#160;kg) and would also have a 50&#160;mm <a href="/wiki/Air_restrictor" class="mw-redirect" title="Air restrictor">air restrictor</a> fitted. The weight limit and the intake-restrictor size of twin machines would be updated, if needed, during the Championship, by a system analysing the race points obtained.<sup id="cite_ref-tech_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tech-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The new rules also changed the minimum number of bikes required to acquire <a href="/wiki/Homologation" title="Homologation">homologation</a>. For 2008 and 2009, all manufacturers, regardless of total production numbers, had to produce a minimum of 1,000 bikes to acquire homologation. From 2010 onwards, the minimum production number was increased to 3,000 bikes. In the past, smaller manufacturers were allowed to build as few as 150 bikes to meet the homologation requirements. Manufacturers took advantage of this by producing 'homologation specials'--highly tuned versions of their road bikes with performance parts designed especially for racing.<sup id="cite_ref-meaning_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-meaning-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 2008 SBK championship was dominated by <a href="/wiki/Troy_Bayliss" title="Troy Bayliss">Troy Bayliss</a> of Australia, on his Ducati 1098, who concluded his season and his career with a double win at the brand new, 195-million-Euro Portimao circuit in Portugal, after which he retired.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2009">2009</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superbike_World_Championship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2009">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2009_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2009 Superbike World Championship">2009 Superbike World Championship</a></div> 
<p>During the offseason, <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_Motor_Company" title="Yamaha Motor Company">Yamaha</a> lost <a href="/wiki/Noriyuki_Haga" title="Noriyuki Haga">Noriyuki Haga</a> to <a href="/wiki/Ducati" class="mw-redirect" title="Ducati">Ducati</a>, who signed him to replace the retired <a href="/wiki/Troy_Bayliss" title="Troy Bayliss">Troy Bayliss</a>. His place was taken by 3-times <a href="/wiki/AMA_Superbike_Championship" title="AMA Superbike Championship">AMA</a> champion <a href="/wiki/Ben_Spies" title="Ben Spies">Ben Spies</a>, who was expected to give Haga serious competition.
</p><p>Ben Spies took a record 11 poles in the 14 round series and 14 wins (17 podiums) in 28 races; his main rival Haga was more consistent, finishing on the podium 19 times but winning only 8 races. 2009 also saw the debut of <a href="/wiki/BMW_Motorrad" title="BMW Motorrad">BMW</a> and the return of <a href="/wiki/Aprilia" title="Aprilia">Aprilia</a>. Aprilia took a fourth final place in the championship with <a href="/wiki/Max_Biaggi" title="Max Biaggi">Max Biaggi</a>, while BMW finished thirteenth with <a href="/wiki/Troy_Corser" title="Troy Corser">Troy Corser</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2010">2010</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superbike_World_Championship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2010">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2010_Superbike_World_Championship" title="2010 Superbike World Championship">2010 Superbike World Championship</a></div> 
<p>2009 Champion <a href="/wiki/Ben_Spies" title="Ben Spies">Ben Spies</a> moved to <a href="/wiki/MotoGP" class="mw-redirect" title="MotoGP">MotoGP</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/James_Toseland" title="James Toseland">James Toseland</a> returned to the championship after 2 seasons in MotoGP and took Spies place at the Sterilgarda Yamaha World Superbike team, partnered by fellow Brit <a href="/wiki/Cal_Crutchlow" title="Cal Crutchlow">Cal Crutchlow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> The factory <a href="/wiki/Ducati" class="mw-redirect" title="Ducati">Ducati</a> team retained their two riders.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The 2010 season started on February 28 at <a href="/wiki/Phillip_Island_Grand_Prix_Circuit" title="Phillip Island Grand Prix Circuit">Phillip Island</a> and ended on October 3 at <a href="/wiki/Circuit_de_Nevers_Magny-Cours" title="Circuit de Nevers Magny-Cours">Magny-Cours</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Riders">Riders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superbike_World_Championship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Riders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Superbike_World_Championship_racers" title="List of Superbike World Championship racers">List of Superbike World Championship racers</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_Superbike_World_Championship_race_winners" title="List of Superbike World Championship race winners">List of Superbike World Championship race winners</a></div>
<p>Riders from all over the world compete in the Superbike World Championship. The championship is perhaps most closely followed in Italy because of <a href="/wiki/Ducati" class="mw-redirect" title="Ducati">Ducati</a> and the United Kingdom where <a href="/wiki/Superbike_racing" title="Superbike racing">superbike racing</a> has been the most popular form of <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_racing" title="Motorcycle racing">motorcycle racing</a>. National-championship superbike racing is conducted in several countries, including the United States, the UK and Japan. Riders from Australia and the United States have traditionally been successful in the world championship. No American rider had won a race since <a href="/wiki/Colin_Edwards" title="Colin Edwards">Colin Edwards</a> won the 2002 championship until <a href="/wiki/Ben_Spies" title="Ben Spies">Ben Spies</a> joined the series in 2009, but no Americans competed in the series between 2003 and 2007.
</p><p>British rider <a href="/wiki/Carl_Fogarty" title="Carl Fogarty">Carl Fogarty</a> had long been the most successful rider in the championship's history, winning the championship four times, and amassing a total of 59 race wins. Jonathan Rea cemented his overtaking of Fogarty in the history books by winning his fourth consecutive world championship title in 2018, amassing a new accord amount of race wins, too.
</p><p>Many riders successful in the Superbike World Championship have gone on to MotoGP, such as 2002 champion <a href="/wiki/Colin_Edwards" title="Colin Edwards">Colin Edwards</a>, 2007 champion <a href="/wiki/James_Toseland" title="James Toseland">James Toseland</a>, and 2005 runner-up <a href="/wiki/Chris_Vermeulen" title="Chris Vermeulen">Chris Vermeulen</a>. The championship has seen several former <a href="/wiki/MotoGP" class="mw-redirect" title="MotoGP">MotoGP</a> riders move to it, usually after failing to earn competitive rides. The 2008 field includes five former MotoGP winners: <a href="/wiki/Max_Biaggi" title="Max Biaggi">Max Biaggi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Checa" title="Carlos Checa">Carlos Checa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Makoto_Tamada" title="Makoto Tamada">Makoto Tamada</a> all raced exclusively in MotoGP before joining SBK, while <a href="/wiki/Troy_Bayliss" title="Troy Bayliss">Troy Bayliss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Noriyuki_Haga" title="Noriyuki Haga">Noriyuki Haga</a>, and <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9gis_Laconi" title="Régis Laconi">Régis Laconi</a> had alternating spells in both.
</p><p>Except for Frenchman <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Roche" title="Raymond Roche">Raymond Roche</a>, who won the championship in <a href="/wiki/1990_Superbike_World_Championship" title="1990 Superbike World Championship">1990</a>, all Superbike World Champions had been native <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a> speakers, until Max Biaggi won the championship in 2010 and 2012 and also 2011 champion Carlos Checa and 2014 champion Sylvain Guintoli becoming the 2nd Frenchman to take the title. Italian riders <a href="/wiki/Davide_Tardozzi" title="Davide Tardozzi">Davide Tardozzi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Marco_Lucchinelli" title="Marco Lucchinelli">Marco Lucchinelli</a> won the first two races of the series, and Frenchman <a href="/wiki/Adrien_Morillas" title="Adrien Morillas">Adrien Morillas</a> was also victorious in 1988; Germany had to wait for <a href="/wiki/Max_Neukirchner" title="Max Neukirchner">Max Neukirchner</a> to achieve this in 2008, although Austrian <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Meklau" title="Andreas Meklau">Andreas Meklau</a> was the first German-speaker to win a race, in 1993. Spain’s first race winner was <a href="/wiki/Ruben_Xaus" class="mw-redirect" title="Ruben Xaus">Ruben Xaus</a> in 2001.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Race_weekend">Race weekend</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superbike_World_Championship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Race weekend">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Up to 2013 season
</p>
<ul><li><b>Friday</b>
<ul><li><i>1st free practice</i> (60 minutes) and <i>1st qualifying</i> (60 minutes)</li></ul></li>
<li><b>Saturday</b>
<ul><li><i>2nd qualifying</i> (60 minutes) and <i>2nd free practice</i> (60 minutes)</li></ul></li></ul>
<dl><dd>The times of 1st and 2nd qualifying are combined and the 15 fastest riders qualify for Superpole. The rest receive a grid position based on lap time, starting with 16th. To qualify for the race, riders must record a lap time no longer than 107% of the time recorded by the pole-position rider.
<ul><li><i>Superpole</i></li></ul></dd></dl>
<ul><li><ul><li>The first 15 riders of the qualifying practice on the track participate in a Knockout session.</li>
<li>All sessions are 12 minutes each, with a seven-minute interval between sessions.
<ul><li>The first round consists of 15 riders.  The slowest three riders are eliminated and start 13th to 15th.</li>
<li>The second round consists of 12 riders.  The slowest three riders start 10th to 12th.</li>
<li>The final round consists of nine riders.  The top nine are set in this session.</li></ul></li>
<li>All times for riders advancing are wiped, requiring advancing riders to set a best time in every Superpole session in which they participate.</li></ul></li></ul>
<p>Starting grid.
</p>
<ul><li><b>Sunday</b>
<ul><li><i>Warm-up</i> (20 minutes) <i>Race 1</i> and <i>Race 2</i></li></ul></li></ul>
<dl><dd>Race distance must be from a minimum of 90&#160;km to a maximum of 110&#160;km.</dd></dl>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Supersport_World_Championship">Supersport World Championship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superbike_World_Championship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Supersport World Championship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Supersport_World_Championship" title="Supersport World Championship">Supersport World Championship</a></div>
<p>Supersport World Championship has been a support class to the Superbike World Championship since 1990.
</p><p>To be eligible for World Supersport, a motorcycle must have a four stroke engine of between 400 and 600&#160;cc for <a href="/wiki/Inline-four_engine" title="Inline-four engine">four cylinder</a>, 500 and 675 cubic centimetres for <a href="/wiki/Straight-three_engine" title="Straight-three engine">triples</a> and between 600 and 750&#160;cc for <a href="/wiki/V-twin_engine" title="V-twin engine">twins</a> and must satisfy the FIM <a href="/wiki/Homologation" title="Homologation">homologation</a> requirements.
World Supersport regulations are much tighter than in World Superbike. The chassis of a supersport machine must remain largely as production, while engine tuning is possible, but tightly regulated. As in world superbike a control tyre is used, although supersport regulations dictate that the tyres must be road legal and therefore race slicks are not allowed.
</p><p>A World Supersport race takes place at every World Superbike round.
</p>
<h3><span id="FIM_Superstock_1.2C000_Cup"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="FIM_Superstock_1,000_Cup">FIM Superstock 1,000&#160;Cup</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superbike_World_Championship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: FIM Superstock 1,000 Cup">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/FIM_Superstock_1000_Championship" class="mw-redirect" title="FIM Superstock 1000 Championship">FIM Superstock 1000 Championship</a></div>
<p>The FIM Superstock 1,000&#160;Cup was a support class to the Superbike World Championship at the European rounds.
Motorcycles with the same displacement as superbikes can run in superstock 1000 (though 1,200&#160;cc twins were allowed for 2007). Superstock rules are much more restrictive and most components on the bike remain stock. The bikes run on Grooved Pirelli tyres.
The Superstock 1000 championship is open to riders up to 24 years of age.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Superstock_600_Championship">European Superstock 600 Championship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superbike_World_Championship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: European Superstock 600 Championship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/European_Superstock_600_Championship" title="European Superstock 600 Championship">European Superstock 600 Championship</a></div>
<p>The Superstock 600 European Championship was a support class to the Superbike World Championship. The championship uses 600 cc production motorcycles and is reserved for riders between 15 and 24 years of age. Same rules as Superstock 1000 apply, but the series is organized by <a href="/wiki/FIM_Europe" title="FIM Europe">FIM Europe</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_other_media">In other media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Superbike_World_Championship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: In other media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As the World Superbike Championship has grown in popularity over the years, video games have been developed to incorporate its growing fan base. Originally EA Sports held the licence to produce SBK videos games until 2001 when they discontinued the series. SBK returned to video games in 2007 thanks to <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> publisher <a href="/wiki/Black_Bean_Games" title="Black Bean Games">Black Bean Games</a>, deal signed in 2006 via <a href="/wiki/RTR_Sports" title="RTR Sports">RTR Sports</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RTR_Black_Bean_SBK_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RTR_Black_Bean_SBK-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Black Bean has realised 3 games to date with <i><a href="/wiki/SBK_X:_Superbike_World_Championship" title="SBK X: Superbike World Championship">SBK X: Superbike World Championship</a></i> being the latest installment of the series.
</p>
