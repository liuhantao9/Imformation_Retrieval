	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Personal name</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">This article is about the full names of individual human beings. This shows a structure typical for English-speaking cultures (and some others). Other cultures use other structures for full names.</div></div></div>
<p>A <b>personal name</b> or <b>full name</b> is the set of names by which an individual is known and that can be recited as a <a href="/wiki/Word-group" class="mw-redirect" title="Word-group">word-group</a>, with the understanding that, taken together, they all relate to that one individual. In many cultures, the term is synonymous with the <i><a href="/wiki/Birth_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Birth name">birth name</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Legal_name" title="Legal name">legal name</a></i> of the individual. The academic study of personal names is called <a href="/wiki/Anthroponymy" title="Anthroponymy">anthroponymy</a>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western culture</a>, nearly all individuals possess at least one <i><a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a></i> (also known as a <i>first name</i>, <i>forename</i>, or <i>Christian name</i>), together with a <i><a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a></i> (also known as a <i>last name</i> or <i>family name</i>)—respectively, the Abraham and Lincoln in <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">Abraham Lincoln</a>—the latter to indicate that the individual belongs to a family, a tribe, or a clan.  Where there are two or more given names, typically only one (in English-speaking cultures usually the first) is used in normal speech.
</p><p>Another naming convention that is used mainly in the <a href="/wiki/Arab_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab culture">Arabic culture</a> and in different other areas across Africa and Asia is connecting the person's given name with a chain of names, starting with the name of the person's father and then the father's father and so on, usually ending with the family name (tribe or clan name). However, the legal full name of a person usually contains the first three names with the family name at the end, to limit the name in government-issued ID. Also note that the wife's name does not change after marriage, and it follows the naming convention described above.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some cultures, including Western ones, also add (or once added) <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Matronymic" title="Matronymic">matronymics</a>. For instance, as a middle name as with <a href="/wiki/Pyotr_Ilyich_Tchaikovsky" title="Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky">Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky</a> (whose father's given name was Ilya), or as a last name as with <a href="/wiki/Bj%C3%B6rk_Gu%C3%B0mundsd%C3%B3ttir" class="mw-redirect" title="Björk Guðmundsdóttir">Björk Guðmundsdóttir</a> (whose father was named Guðmundur) or <a href="/wiki/Hei%C3%B0ar_Helguson" title="Heiðar Helguson">Heiðar Helguson</a> (whose mother was named Helga). Similar concepts are present in <a href="/wiki/Eastern_world" title="Eastern world">Eastern</a> cultures.
</p><p>However, in some areas of the world, many people are known by a single name, and so are said to be <a href="/wiki/Mononymous_person" title="Mononymous person">mononymous</a>. Still other cultures lack the concept of specific, fixed names designating people, either individually or collectively. Certain isolated tribes, such as the <a href="/wiki/Machiguenga_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Machiguenga people">Machiguenga</a> of the Amazon, do not use personal names.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;i&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A person's full name usually identifies that person for legal and administrative purposes, although it may not be the name by which the person is commonly known; some people use only a portion of their full name, or are known by <a href="/wiki/Title" title="Title">titles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nicknames</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonyms</a> or other formal or informal designations.
</p><p>It is nearly universal for people to have names; the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> <a href="/wiki/Convention_on_the_Rights_of_the_Child" title="Convention on the Rights of the Child">Convention on the Rights of the Child</a> declares that a child has the right to a name from birth.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Common components of names given at birth include:
</p>
<ul><li>Personal name: The <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> (or <a href="/w/index.php?title=Acquired_name&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Acquired name (page does not exist)">acquired name</a> in some cultures)  can precede a <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a> (as in some European cultures), or it can come after the family name (as in some East Asian cultures), or be used without a family name.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">Patronymic</a>: A surname based on the given name of the father.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Matronymic" title="Matronymic">Matronymic</a>: A surname based on the given name of the mother.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">Family name</a>: A name used by all members of a family. In <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, surnames gradually came into common use beginning in the 3rd century BC (having been common only among the nobility before that). In some areas of East Asia (e.g. Vietnam and Korea), surnames developed in the next several centuries, while in other areas (like Japan), surnames did not become prevalent until the 19th century. In <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, after the loss of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions" title="Roman naming conventions">Roman system</a>, the common use of family names started quite early in some areas (<a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> in the 13th century, and <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> in the 16th century), but it often did not happen until much later in areas that used a patronymic naming custom, such as the <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavian</a> countries, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a>, and some areas of <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>. The compulsory use of surnames varied greatly. France required a priest to write surnames in baptismal records in 1539 (but did not require surnames for Jews, who usually used <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymics</a>, until 1808). On the other hand, surnames were not compulsory in the Scandinavian countries until the 19th or 20th century (1923 in <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> still <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_name" title="Icelandic name">does not use surnames</a> for its native inhabitants. In <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a>, <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> and most Latin American countries, two surnames are used, one being the father's family name and the other being the mother's family name. Whereas Spain used to put the father's family name before the mother's family name, Portugal and Brazil keep the inverse order but use the father's family name as the principal one. A Portuguese man named António de Oliveira Guterres would therefore be known commonly as <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_Guterres" title="António Guterres">António Guterres</a>. In Spain, though, the second surname is frequently used if the first one is too common to allow an easy identification. For example, Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Luis_Rodr%C3%ADguez_Zapatero" title="José Luis Rodríguez Zapatero">José Luis Rodríguez Zapatero</a> is often called <a href="/wiki/Zapatero" class="mw-redirect" title="Zapatero">Zapatero</a>. In most of the cultures of the Middle East and South Asia, surnames were not generally used until European influence took hold in the 19th century.</li>
<li>In many families, single or multiple <a href="/wiki/Middle_name" title="Middle name">middle names</a> are simply alternative names, names honoring an ancestor or relative, or, for married women, sometimes their maiden names. In some traditions, however, the roles of the first and middle given names are reversed, with the first given name being used to honor a family member and the middle name being used as the usual method to address someone informally. Many <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a> families choose a <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saint</a>'s name as their child's middle name or this can be left until the child's <a href="/wiki/Confirmation_(sacrament)" class="mw-redirect" title="Confirmation (sacrament)">confirmation</a> when they choose a saint's name for themselves. Cultures that use <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Matronymic" title="Matronymic">matronymics</a> will often give middle names to distinguish between two similarly named people: e.g., Einar Karl Stefánsson and Einar Guðmundur Stefánsson. This is especially done in Iceland (as shown in example) where people are known and referred to almost exclusively by their given name/s.</li></ul>
<p>Some people (called <i>anonyms</i>) choose to be <a href="/wiki/Anonymity" title="Anonymity">anonymous</a>, that is, to hide their true names, for fear of governmental prosecution or social ridicule of their works or actions. Another method to disguise one's identity is to employ a <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a>.
</p><p>For some people, their name is a single word, known as a <a href="/wiki/Mononym" class="mw-redirect" title="Mononym">mononym</a>. This can be true from birth, or occur later in life. For example, <a href="/wiki/Teller_(entertainer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Teller (entertainer)">Teller</a>, of the magician duo <a href="/wiki/Penn_and_Teller" class="mw-redirect" title="Penn and Teller">Penn and Teller</a>, was named Raymond Joseph Teller at birth, but changed his name both legally and socially to be simply "Teller". In some official government documents, such as his <a href="/wiki/Driver%27s_license" title="Driver&#39;s license">driver's license</a>, his given name is listed as <i>NFN</i>, an acronym for "no first name".
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Inuit" title="Inuit">Inuit</a> believe that the <a href="/wiki/Soul_(spirit)" class="mw-redirect" title="Soul (spirit)">souls</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Namesake" title="Namesake">namesakes</a> are one, so they traditionally refer to the junior namesakes, not just by the names (<i>atiq</i>), but also by <a href="/wiki/Kinship" title="Kinship">kinship</a> title, which applies across gender and generation without implications of disrespect or seniority. In <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a>, someone's name is considered intimately connected with his fate, and adding a name (e.g. on the sickbed) may avert a particular danger. Among <a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi_Jews" title="Ashkenazi Jews">Ashkenazi Jews</a> it is also considered bad luck to take the name of a living ancestor, as the <a href="/wiki/Death_(personification)#In_Judaism" title="Death (personification)">Angel of Death</a> may mistake the younger person for his namesake (although there is no such custom among <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">Sephardi Jews</a>). Jews may also have a Jewish name for intra-community use and use a different name when engaging with the <a href="/wiki/Goyim" class="mw-redirect" title="Goyim">Gentile</a> world.
</p><p>Chinese children are called diminutive or pejorative names to make them appear worthless to evil spirits. They receive a definitive name as they grow up.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Chinese_emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese emperor">Chinese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japanese_emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese emperor">Japanese emperors</a> receive <a href="/wiki/Posthumous_name" title="Posthumous name">posthumous names</a>.
</p><p>In some <a href="/wiki/Polynesian_culture" title="Polynesian culture">Polynesian cultures</a>, the name of a deceased chief becomes <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">taboo</a>. If he is named after a common object or concept, a different word has to be used for it.
</p><p>Depending on national convention, additional given names (and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Title" title="Title">titles</a>) are considered part of the name.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Feudal_names">Feudal names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Feudal names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Disputed plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-disputed" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="System-search.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/System-search.svg/45px-System-search.svg.png" decoding="async" width="45" height="45" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/System-search.svg/68px-System-search.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/System-search.svg/90px-System-search.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="48" data-file-height="48" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section's <b>factual accuracy is <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute">disputed</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Relevant discussion may be found on <a href="/wiki/Talk:Personal_name#Dispute" title="Talk:Personal name">Talk:Personal name</a>. Please help to ensure that disputed statements are <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Reliable sources">reliably sourced</a>.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">February 2013</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Royal_family" title="Royal family">royalty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobility</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gentry" title="Gentry">gentry</a> of Europe traditionally have many names, including phrases for the lands that they own. The French developed the method of putting the term by which the person is referred in <a href="/wiki/Small_capital" class="mw-redirect" title="Small capital">small capital</a> letters. It is this habit which transferred to names of the Far East, as seen below. An example is that of Marie-Joseph-Paul-Yves-Roch Gilbert du Motier, who is known as the <span style="font-variant:small-caps"><a href="/wiki/Gilbert_du_Motier,_marquis_de_Lafayette" class="mw-redirect" title="Gilbert du Motier, marquis de Lafayette">Marquis de Lafayette</a></span>. Notice that he possessed the lands both of Motier and Lafayette.
</p><p>The bare place name was used formerly to refer to the person who owned it, rather than the land itself (the word "Gloucester" in "What will Gloucester do?" meant the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Gloucester" title="Duke of Gloucester">Duke of Gloucester</a>). As a development, the bare name of a ship in the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> meant its captain (e.g., "Cressy didn't learn from Aboukir") while the name with an article referred to the ship (e.g., "The <i>Cressy</i> is foundering").
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_conventions">Naming conventions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Naming conventions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A personal naming system, or anthroponymic system, is a system describing the choice of personal name in a certain society. Personal names consists of one or more parts, such as <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> and <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a>. Personal naming systems are studied within the field of <a href="/wiki/Anthroponymy" title="Anthroponymy">anthroponymy</a>.
</p><p>In contemporary Western societies (except for <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a>, and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>, depending on the occasion), the most common naming convention is that a person must have a <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>, which is usually gender-specific, followed by the parents' <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a>.  Some given names are bespoke, but most are repeated from earlier generations in the same culture.  Many are drawn from mythology, some of which span multiple language areas.  This has resulted in related names in different languages (e.g. <a href="/wiki/George_(given_name)" title="George (given name)">George</a>, <a href="/wiki/Georg_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Georg (disambiguation)">Georg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jorge" title="Jorge">Jorge</a>), which might be translated or might be maintained as immutable proper nouns.
</p><p>In earlier times, Scandinavian countries followed patronymic naming, with people effectively called "X's son/daughter"; this is now the case only in <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Iceland</a> and was recently re-introduced as an option in the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>. It is legally possible in Finland as people of Icelandic ethnic naming are specifically named in the name law. When people of this name convert to standards of other cultures, the phrase is often condensed into one word, creating last names like Jacobsen (Jacob's Son). In <a href="/wiki/Kafirstan" class="mw-redirect" title="Kafirstan">Kafirstan</a> (now part of Pakistan) "Children are named as soon as born. The infant is given to the mother to suckle, while a wise woman rapidly recites the family ancestral names; the name pronounced at the instant the baby begins to feed is that by which it is thereafter known." <sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There is a range of personal naming systems:<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>Binomial systems: apart for given name, people are described by their surnames, which they obtain from one of their parents. Most modern European personal naming systems, such as <a href="/wiki/Russian_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian name">Russian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish name">Spanish</a>, are of this type.</li>
<li>Patronymic systems: apart for given name, people are described by their <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymics</a>, that is given names (not surnames) of parents or other ancestors. Such systems were in wide use throughout Europe in the first millennium C.E., but were replaced by binomial systems. <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_name" title="Icelandic name">Icelandic system</a> is still patronymic.</li>
<li>More complex systems like <a href="/wiki/Arabic_name" title="Arabic name">Arabic system</a>, consisting of <a href="/wiki/Paedonymic" class="mw-redirect" title="Paedonymic">paedonymic</a> (son's name), given name, patronymic and one or two bynames.</li></ul>
<p>Different cultures have different conventions for personal names. This is a list of articles about particular cultures' naming conventions.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_order">Name order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Name order">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MomoiExpoCrop.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/MomoiExpoCrop.png/220px-MomoiExpoCrop.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="186" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/MomoiExpoCrop.png/330px-MomoiExpoCrop.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/MomoiExpoCrop.png/440px-MomoiExpoCrop.png 2x" data-file-width="1592" data-file-height="1344" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MomoiExpoCrop.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Haruko_Momoi" title="Haruko Momoi">Haruko Momoi</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Anime_Expo" title="Anime Expo">Anime Expo</a> 2007 in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>; her name card features a spelling of her <a href="/wiki/Japanese_name" title="Japanese name">name</a> ("Halko Momoi") written in Western order; in Japanese her name is <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">桃井はるこ</span></span> <i>Momoi Haruko</i></div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_name_order">Western name order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Western name order">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The order <i>given name, family name</i> is commonly known as the Western order and is usually used in most <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> countries and in countries that have cultures predominantly influenced by Western Europe (e.g. <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South</a> <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">America</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_India" title="North India">North</a>, <a href="/wiki/East_India" title="East India">East</a>, <a href="/wiki/Central_India" title="Central India">Central</a> and <a href="/wiki/Western_India" title="Western India">West India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>).
</p><p>Within alphabetic lists and catalogs, however, the family name is generally put first, with the given name(s) following, separated from it by a <a href="/wiki/Comma" title="Comma">comma</a> (e.g. Smith, John), representing the "lexical name order". This convention is followed by most Western libraries, as well as on many administrative forms.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_name_order">Eastern name order</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Eastern name order">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The order <i>family name, given name</i> is commonly known as the Eastern order and is primarily used in <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a> (for example in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>), as well as in <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>), and <a href="/wiki/South_India" title="South India">Southern</a> and North-Eastern parts of India, and also in <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_names" title="Hungarian names">Hungary</a>. 
It is also common in <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> as well as in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> possibly because of the influence of the bureaucratic use of putting the family name before the given name.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>When East Asian names are <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">transliterated</a> into the <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">Latin alphabet</a>, some people prefer to convert them to the Western order, while others leave them in the Eastern order but write the family name in <a href="/wiki/Letter_case" title="Letter case">capital letters</a>. To avoid confusion, there is a convention in some language communities, e.g. French, to write the family name in all capitals when engaging in formal correspondence or writing for an international audience. In Hungarian, the Eastern order of Japanese names is officially kept and Hungarian transliteration is used (e.g. Mijazaki Hajao), but Western name order is also sometimes used with English transliteration (e.g. Hayao Miyazaki).
</p><p>Chinese people, except for those traveling or living outside of China and areas influenced by China, rarely reverse their Chinese language names to the western naming order (given name, then family name), but some may have non-Chinese given names which may use a different order.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="reliable source needed for the whole sentence (October 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Western publications usually preserve the Chinese naming order, with the family name first, followed by the given name. In regard to Japanese names, most foreign publications reverse the names of modern individuals, and most Japanese reverse their own names when creating materials for foreign consumption.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In popular journalism publications, western order is used for Japanese names.<sup id="cite_ref-Saeki_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saeki-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Japanese names of contemporary individuals and Hungarian names are usually "switched" when individuals who have such names are mentioned in media in Western countries; for example, <i>Koizumi Jun'ichirō</i> is known as <a href="/wiki/Junichiro_Koizumi" title="Junichiro Koizumi">Junichiro Koizumi</a> in English, and Puskás Ferenc is known as <a href="/wiki/Ferenc_Pusk%C3%A1s" title="Ferenc Puskás">Ferenc Puskás</a>. But Chinese, Korean and Vietnamese names are usually left in East Asian order; for instance, Máo Zédōng is known as <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a> in English.
</p><p>Names of Japanese or Chinese sportspeople generally follow the above conventions. For Japanese examples, see <a href="/wiki/Ichiro_Suzuki" title="Ichiro Suzuki">Ichiro Suzuki</a> instead of <i>Suzuki Ichirō</i> (although he is widely known simply as "Ichiro" in both Japan and North America), or <a href="/wiki/Hidetoshi_Nakata" title="Hidetoshi Nakata">Hidetoshi Nakata</a> instead of <i>Nakata Hidetoshi</i>. As for Chinese sportspeople, <i>Yáo Míng</i> is <a href="/wiki/Yao_Ming" title="Yao Ming">Yao Ming</a> and <i>Liú Xiáng</i> is <a href="/wiki/Liu_Xiang_(hurdler)" title="Liu Xiang (hurdler)">Liu Xiang</a> in the West.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Names of Korean sportspeople may be rendered in East Asian or Western order in Western countries, apparently depending on the sport. For example, names of Korean <a href="/wiki/Football_(soccer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Football (soccer)">footballers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sportsperson" class="mw-redirect" title="Sportsperson">athletes</a> are usually left in East Asian order (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Ahn_Jung-hwan" title="Ahn Jung-hwan">Ahn Jung-hwan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Myung-bo" title="Hong Myung-bo">Hong Myung-bo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Park_Ji-Sung" class="mw-redirect" title="Park Ji-Sung">Park Ji-Sung</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sohn_Kee-chung" title="Sohn Kee-chung">Sohn Kee-chung</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hwang_Young-cho" title="Hwang Young-cho">Hwang Young-cho</a>). <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">Baseball</a> players' names are usually changed to Western order; for example Park Chan-Ho is referred to in the West as <a href="/wiki/Chan-ho_Park" class="mw-redirect" title="Chan-ho Park">Chan-ho Park</a>. <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">Golfers</a>' names are also typically switched to Western order; the female golfer Pak Se-ri is known in the West as <a href="/wiki/Se-Ri_Pak" class="mw-redirect" title="Se-Ri Pak">Se-Ri Pak</a>. Confusion can be avoided by noticing that in all the above cases, the words linked by a hyphen are the given name.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mordvins" title="Mordvins">Mordvins</a> use two names - a Mordvin name and a Russian name. The Mordvin name is written in the Eastern name order. Usually, the Mordvin surname is the same as the Russian surname, for example <a href="/wiki/Aleksandr_Sharonov" title="Aleksandr Sharonov">Sharonon Sandra</a> (Russian: Alexander Sharonov), but it can be different at times, for example <a href="/wiki/Yovlan_Olo" class="mw-redirect" title="Yovlan Olo">Yovlan Olo</a> (Russian: Vladimir Romashkin).
</p><p>Mongolians use the Eastern naming order (patronymic followed by given name), which is also used there when rendering the names of other East Asians and Hungarians. Russian and other Western names, however, are still written in Western order.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-human_personal_names">Non-human personal names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Non-human personal names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Apart from the <a href="/wiki/Linnaean_taxonomy" title="Linnaean taxonomy">Linnaean taxonomy</a>, some humans give individual non-human animals and plants names, usually of endearment.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_of_pets">Names of pets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Names of pets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pet names often reflect the owner's view of the animal, and their expectations they have for their companion.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> It has been argued that giving names allows researchers to view their pets as <a href="/wiki/Ontologically" class="mw-redirect" title="Ontologically">ontologically</a> different from unnamed <a href="/wiki/Laboratory_animal" class="mw-redirect" title="Laboratory animal">laboratory animals</a> with which they work.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The name given to a pet may refer to its appearance<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> or personality,<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> or be chosen for <a href="/wiki/Endearment" class="mw-redirect" title="Endearment">endearment</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> or in honor of a favorite <a href="/wiki/Celebrity" title="Celebrity">celebrity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many pet owners give human names to their pets. This has been shown to reflect the owner having a human-like relationship with the pet.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In some cultures, <a href="/wiki/Pet" title="Pet">pets</a> or sporting <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animals</a> are sometimes given names similar to human names. Other cultures, such as the <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Chinese</a>, give animals nonhuman names because it would be seen as offensive and disrespectful to the person of the same name.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dolphin_names_for_each_other">Dolphin names for each other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Dolphin names for each other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A study published in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences claims that humans are not the only animals that use personal names. Researchers from the University of North Carolina Wilmington studying <a href="/wiki/Bottlenose_dolphin" title="Bottlenose dolphin">bottlenose dolphins</a> in Sarasota Bay, Florida, found that the dolphins had names for each other.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> A dolphin chooses its name as an infant.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
