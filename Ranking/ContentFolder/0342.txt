	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Honorific</h1>

<p>An <b>honorific</b> is a title that conveys esteem, courtesy, or respect for position or rank when used in addressing or referring to a person. Sometimes, the term "honorific" is used in a more specific sense to refer to an <a href="/wiki/Honorary_title_(academic)" title="Honorary title (academic)">honorary academic title</a>. It is also often <a href="/wiki/Conflation" title="Conflation">conflated</a> with systems of <a href="/wiki/Honorifics_(linguistics)" title="Honorifics (linguistics)">honorific speech</a> in linguistics, which are grammatical or <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">morphological</a> ways of encoding the relative social status of speakers.
</p><p>Typically, honorifics are used as a <a href="/wiki/Style_(manner_of_address)" title="Style (manner of address)">style</a> in the grammatical third <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">person</a>, and as a <a href="/wiki/Form_of_address" class="mw-redirect" title="Form of address">form of address</a> in the second person. Use in the first person, by the honored dignitary, is uncommon or considered very rude and <a href="/wiki/Egotistical" class="mw-redirect" title="Egotistical">egotistical</a>. Some languages have anti-honorific (<i>despective</i> or <i>humilific</i>) first person forms (expressions such as "your most humble servant" or "this unworthy person") whose effect is to enhance the relative honor accorded to the person addressed.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_English_honorifics">Modern English honorifics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Modern English honorifics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/English_honorifics" title="English honorifics">English honorifics</a></div>
<p>The most common <a href="/wiki/English_honorifics" title="English honorifics">honorifics in modern English</a> are usually placed immediately before a person's name. Honorifics which can be used (both as style and as form of address) include, in the case of a man, "<a href="/wiki/Mr" class="mw-redirect" title="Mr">Mr</a>" (irrespective of <a href="/wiki/Marital_status" title="Marital status">marital status</a>), and in the case of a woman the honorific may depend on her marital status: if she is unmarried, it is "<a href="/wiki/Miss" title="Miss">Miss</a>", if she has been married it is "<a href="/wiki/Mrs" class="mw-redirect" title="Mrs">Mrs</a>", and if her marital status is unknown, or it is not desired to specify it,  "<a href="/wiki/Ms." title="Ms.">Ms</a>". The honorific <a href="/wiki/Master_(form_of_address)" title="Master (form of address)">"Mstr"</a> may also be used for a boy who has not yet entered society. Someone who does not want to express a gender with their honorific may occasionally use <a href="/wiki/Mx_(title)" title="Mx (title)">Mx</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gender_neutral_title" class="mw-redirect" title="Gender neutral title">Ind.</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gender_neutral_title" class="mw-redirect" title="Gender neutral title">Misc.</a>.  
</p><p>In the U.S., these terms are styled with a period ("Mr." or "Mrs.") because they were originally abbreviations (of "Mister" and "Mistress").  "Ms." is also styled with a period for consistency.  In the United Kingdom, periods are typically not used.
</p><p>Other honorifics may denote the honored person's occupation, for instance "<a href="/wiki/Doctor_(title)" title="Doctor (title)">Doctor</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Esquire" title="Esquire">Esquire</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Captain" title="Captain">Captain</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Coach_(sport)" title="Coach (sport)">Coach</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Law_enforcement_officer" title="Law enforcement officer">Officer</a>", "<a href="/wiki/The_Reverend" title="The Reverend">The Reverend</a>" for all <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">clergy</a> or "Father" (for a <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Orthodox">Eastern Orthodox</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oriental_Orthodox" class="mw-redirect" title="Oriental Orthodox">Oriental Orthodox</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Anglican" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> Christian priest), "<a href="/wiki/Rabbi" title="Rabbi">Rabbi</a>" for Jewish clergy, or <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">Professor</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> Holders of an academic <a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">Doctorate</a> such as <a href="/wiki/PhD" class="mw-redirect" title="PhD">PhD</a> are addressed as "Doctor" (abbreviated Dr). "Master" as a prefix ahead of the name of boys and young men up to about 16 years of age is less common than it used to be, but is still used by older people addressing the young in formal situations and correspondence.
</p><p>Some honorifics act as complete replacements for a name, as "Sir" or "Ma'am", or "Your Honor". Subordinates will often use honorifics as punctuation before asking a superior a question or after responding to an order: "Yes, sir" or even "Sir, yes, sir."
</p><p>Judges are often addressed as "Your Honor" (or "Honour" in Commonwealth jurisdictions) when <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bench_(metonymy)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bench (metonymy) (page does not exist)">on the bench</a>, and the style (in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>) is  "His/Her Honor" the plural form is "Your Honors". If the judge also has a higher title, that may be the correct honorific to use, for example, in Britain: "Your Lordship".  Members of the U.S. Supreme Court are addressed as "Justice".
</p><p>Similarly, a monarch (ranking as a king/queen or emperor) and his/her <a href="/wiki/Queen_consort" title="Queen consort">consort</a> may be addressed or referred to as "Your/His/Her Majesty", "Their Majesties", etc. (but there is no customary honorific accorded to a female monarch's consort, as he is usually granted a specific style). Monarchs below <a href="/wiki/King" title="King">kingly</a> rank are addressed as "Your/His/Her <a href="/wiki/Highness" title="Highness">Highness</a>", the exact rank being indicated by an appropriate modifier, e.g. "His <a href="/wiki/Serene_Highness" title="Serene Highness">Serene Highness</a>" for a member of a <a href="/wiki/Prince" title="Prince">princely</a> dynasty, or "Her Grand Ducal Highness" for a member of a family that reigns over a <a href="/wiki/Grand_duchy" title="Grand duchy">grand duchy</a>. Verbs with these honorifics as subject are conjugated in the third person (e.g. "you are going" vs. "Your Honor is going" or "Her Royal Highness is going".) Protocol for monarchs and aristocrats can be very complex, with no general rule; great offence can be given by using a form that is not exactly correct. There are differences between "Your Highness" and "Your Royal Highness"; between "Princess Margaret" and "The Princess Margaret". All of these are correct, but apply to people of subtly different rank. An example of a non-obvious style is "Her Majesty <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_The_Queen_Mother" title="Queen Elizabeth The Queen Mother">Queen Elizabeth The Queen Mother</a>", which was an official style, but unique to one person.
</p><p>In music, a distinguished conductor or virtuoso instrumentalist may be known as "Maestro".
</p><p>In aviation, <a href="/wiki/Pilot_in_command" title="Pilot in command">pilots in command</a> of a larger <a href="/wiki/Civil_aircraft" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil aircraft">civil aircraft</a> are usually addressed as "Captain" plus their full name or surname. This tradition is slowly diminishing in the United States and most EU countries. However, many countries, especially in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a>, follow this tradition and address airline pilots, military pilots, and flight instructors exclusively as "Captain" even outside of the professional environment. In addition, such countries' <a href="/wiki/Etiquette" title="Etiquette">etiquette</a> rules dictate that this title must be placed on all the official letters and social invitations, business cards, identification documents, etc. In the United States, when addressing a pilot, common etiquette does not require the title "Captain" to be printed on official letters or invitations before the addressee's full name. However, this is optional (akin to "<a href="/wiki/Esquire" title="Esquire">Esq</a>" after an attorney's name, in the U.S.) and may be used where appropriate, especially when addressing airline pilots with many years of experience.
</p><p>Occupants of state and political office may be addressed with an honorific. A monarch may be addressed as His/Her Majesty, a president as Your Excellency or Mr/Madam President, a minister or secretary of state as "Your Excellency" or Mr/Madam Secretary, etc. A prime minister may be addressed as "the Honorable". In the UK, members of the <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Privy Council">Privy Council</a> are addressed as "the Right Honourable ...".  A member of Parliament or other legislative body may have particular honorifics. A member of a Senate, for example, may be addressed as "Senator". The etiquette varies and most countries have protocol specifying the honorifics to be used for its state, judicial, military and other officeholders.
</p><p>Former <a href="/wiki/Military_officer" class="mw-redirect" title="Military officer">military officers</a> are sometimes addressed by their last military rank, such as "Captain", "Colonel", "General", etc. This is generally only adopted by those officers who served in at least the rank equivalent of Major.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honorifics_in_other_languages_and_cultures">Honorifics in other languages and cultures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Honorifics in other languages and cultures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In areas of <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">East Africa</a>, where the <a href="/wiki/Bantu_languages" title="Bantu languages">Bantu</a> language <a href="/wiki/Swahili_language" title="Swahili language">Swahili</a> is spoken, <i>mzee</i> is frequently used for an elder to denote respect by younger speakers. It is used in direct conversation and used in referring to someone in the third person.
</p><p>While Swahili is Bantu, it is highly influenced by Arabic and Hindi languages and cultures. <i>Babu</i> is a prefix honorific used with elders, similar to <i>mzee</i>, but may also mean grandfather. Other prefix honorifics are <i>ndugu</i>, for brother or a close male friend, and <i>dada</i> for a sister or close female friend; thus, John and Jane would be Ndugu John and Dada Jane, respectively.
</p><p>Amongst the Akan ethnic groups of West Africa's <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">Ghana</a>, the word <i>nana</i> is used as an aristocratic pre-nominal by chiefs and elders alike.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Yorubaland" title="Yorubaland">Yorubaland</a>, also in West Africa, the word <i>ogbeni</i> is used as a synonym for the English "mister". Titled members of the region's aristocracy are therefore called <i>oloye</i> instead, this being the word for "chief". Although the former of the two titles is only used by men, aristocrats of either gender are addressed using the latter of them.
</p>

<p>Some honorifics used by <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">Ancient Romans</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Augustus_(honorific)" class="mw-redirect" title="Augustus (honorific)">Augustus</a>, turned into <a href="/wiki/Title" title="Title">titles</a> over time.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="China">China</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: China">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_honorifics" title="Chinese honorifics">Chinese honorifics</a></div>
<p>During the ancient and imperial periods, <a href="/wiki/Chinese_honorifics" title="Chinese honorifics">Chinese honorifics</a> varied greatly based on one's social status, but with the end of <a href="/wiki/Imperial_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial China">Imperial China</a>, many of these distinctions fell out of colloquial use. Some honorifics remain in use today, especially in formal writings for the court and business settings.
</p><p>In addition, the use of honorifics varies greatly across Chinese-speaking regions in the world. In Taiwan, for example, honorifics are more widely used in daily interactions. In Mainland China, however, honorifics tend to recede to formal settings.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p><a href="/wiki/Indian_honorifics" title="Indian honorifics">Indian honorifics</a> abound, covering formal and informal relationships for commercial, generational, social, and spiritual links. Honorifics may be prefix, suffix, or replacement types. There are many variations.
</p>
<ul><li><i>Prefix type</i>: The most common honorifics in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> are usually placed immediately before the name of the subject. Honorifics which can be used of any adult of the appropriate sex include <i><a href="/wiki/Sri" title="Sri">Sri</a></i> (also Romanised as <i>Shri</i>, abbreviation of <i>Sriman</i>), <i><a href="/wiki/Srimati" class="mw-redirect" title="Srimati">Smt</a></i> (abbreviation of <i>Srimati</i>), and <i><a href="/wiki/Kum." class="mw-redirect" title="Kum.">Kum</a></i> (abbreviation of <i>Kumari</i>). In Punjab, <i><a href="/wiki/Sardar" title="Sardar">Sardar</a></i> is used for <a href="/wiki/Sikh" class="mw-redirect" title="Sikh">Sikh</a> men and <i>Sardarni</i> for Sikh women. In Tamil, <i>Thiru</i> (abbreviation of <i>Thiruvalar</i> for men) and <i>Thirumathi</i> (for women) are used.In Telugu, Chi (abbreviation of 'chiranjeevi') is used for younger men and Chi.La.Sou (Chiranjeevini Lakshmi Soubhagyavathi) is prefixed for the names of younger women. In India, honorifics mostly come prior to the name of object.</li>
<li><i>Replacement type</i>: Some honorifics, like <i>Bhavān</i> or <i>Bhavatī</i>, act as complete replacements for a name. For example, in <a href="/wiki/Gujarati_language" title="Gujarati language">Gujarati</a>, for an uncle who is your mother's brother, the replacement honorific <i>maama</i> (long "a" then short "a") is used, and a male friend will often earn the suffix honorific of <i>bhai</i>.</li>
<li><i>Suffix type</i>:
<ul><li>The traditional <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">Hindi</a> honorific is the suffix <i>-ji</i>. For example, M.K. Gandhi (the Mahatma) was often referred to as Gandhi<i>-ji</i>. (Hindi, like many languages, distinguishes between pronouns for persons older in age or status. Such a person is referred as <i>aap</i>; a person of same status is called <i>tum</i> (both translating as "you" in English, but similar in principle to the <i>vous</i>/<i>tu</i> distinction in French or the <i>usted</i>/<i>tú</i> distinction in Spanish). A similar distinction exists for third person pronouns. When honorifics are attached in Hindi, the verb matches the plural case.)</li>
<li>The traditional <a href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">Bengali</a> honorific for ordinary men is the suffix <i>Babu</i> (বাবু), used with the person's given (first) name. Thus, Shubhash Basu would be Shubhash-Babu. For men with whom one has a more formal relationship, the suffix <i>Moshai</i> (মশাই) (<i>mohashoi</i> (মহাশয়)) is used with the person's family (last) name. Thus, Shubhash Basu would be Basu-Moshai.</li>
<li>The traditional <a href="/wiki/Kannada" title="Kannada">Kannada</a> honorific is the suffix <i>-avaru</i>. For example, Visveswariah was referred to as Visveswariah<i>-avaru</i>.</li>
<li>The traditional <a href="/wiki/Marathi_language" title="Marathi language">Marathi</a> honorific is the suffix <i>-rao</i>. For example, Madhav Scindia was referred to as Madhav<i>-rao</i>.</li>
<li>The traditional <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">Tamil</a> honorific is the suffix <i>Avargal</i>. Dalai Lama would become Dalai Lama <i>Avargal</i>.</li>
<li>The traditional <a href="/wiki/Telugu_language" title="Telugu language">Telugu</a> honorific is the suffix <i>Garu</i>. Thus, Potti Sriramulu would be Potti Sriramulu <i>Garu</i>.</li></ul></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Italy">Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Italian_honorifics" title="Italian honorifics">Italian honorifics</a> are usually limited to formal situations. Professional titles like <i>Ingegnere</i> (engineer) are often substituted for the ordinary <i>Signore</i> (mister), while <i>Dottore</i> (doctor) is used very freely for any graduate of a university.
Honorifics lose their <i>e</i> ending when juxtaposed to a surname: e.g., dottor Rossi, cardinal Martini, ragionier Fantozzi.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Japanese_honorifics" title="Japanese honorifics">Japanese honorifics</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Honorific_speech_in_Japanese" title="Honorific speech in Japanese">Honorific speech in Japanese</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:%E3%81%93%E3%81%A1%E3%82%89%E3%81%AF%E5%85%88%E7%A8%8B%E8%A6%8B%E3%82%89%E3%82%8C%E3%81%9F%E6%89%80%E3%81%A7%E3%81%99_(8317997240).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/%E3%81%93%E3%81%A1%E3%82%89%E3%81%AF%E5%85%88%E7%A8%8B%E8%A6%8B%E3%82%89%E3%82%8C%E3%81%9F%E6%89%80%E3%81%A7%E3%81%99_%288317997240%29.jpg/220px-%E3%81%93%E3%81%A1%E3%82%89%E3%81%AF%E5%85%88%E7%A8%8B%E8%A6%8B%E3%82%89%E3%82%8C%E3%81%9F%E6%89%80%E3%81%A7%E3%81%99_%288317997240%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/%E3%81%93%E3%81%A1%E3%82%89%E3%81%AF%E5%85%88%E7%A8%8B%E8%A6%8B%E3%82%89%E3%82%8C%E3%81%9F%E6%89%80%E3%81%A7%E3%81%99_%288317997240%29.jpg/330px-%E3%81%93%E3%81%A1%E3%82%89%E3%81%AF%E5%85%88%E7%A8%8B%E8%A6%8B%E3%82%89%E3%82%8C%E3%81%9F%E6%89%80%E3%81%A7%E3%81%99_%288317997240%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/%E3%81%93%E3%81%A1%E3%82%89%E3%81%AF%E5%85%88%E7%A8%8B%E8%A6%8B%E3%82%89%E3%82%8C%E3%81%9F%E6%89%80%E3%81%A7%E3%81%99_%288317997240%29.jpg/440px-%E3%81%93%E3%81%A1%E3%82%89%E3%81%AF%E5%85%88%E7%A8%8B%E8%A6%8B%E3%82%89%E3%82%8C%E3%81%9F%E6%89%80%E3%81%A7%E3%81%99_%288317997240%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:%E3%81%93%E3%81%A1%E3%82%89%E3%81%AF%E5%85%88%E7%A8%8B%E8%A6%8B%E3%82%89%E3%82%8C%E3%81%9F%E6%89%80%E3%81%A7%E3%81%99_(8317997240).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mi-rareta (Sonkeigo)</div></div></div>
<p>The word in Japanese for honorifics, <i>keigo</i><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">敬語</span>)</span>, is used in Japanese everyday conversations. <a href="/wiki/Japanese_honorifics" title="Japanese honorifics">Japanese honorifics</a> are similar to English, with titles like "Mister" and "Miss", but in Japanese, which has many honorifics, their use is mandatory in many formal and informal social situations. <a href="/wiki/Japanese_grammar" title="Japanese grammar">Japanese grammar</a>, as a whole, tends to function on hierarchy; honorific stems are appended to verbs and many nouns, primarily names, and in many cases one word may be exchanged for another word entirely with the same verb- or noun-meaning, but with different honorific connotations.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, there are three rough divisions of honorifics:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Teineigo</i><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">丁寧語</span>)</span>, the most popular <i>keigo</i> that is used in daily life, used as a formal and polite way of speaking to others in general. It is usually used when the speaker does not know the other person well. Under <i>teineigo</i> there is also <i>bikago</i> <span style="font-weight:normal">(<span class="t_nihongo_kanji" lang="ja">美化語</span>, <i>beautiful, clean language</i>)</span> which is used when people simply want to speak in a polite way regardless of the age or class of the other person.</li>
<li><i>Sonkeigo</i><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">尊敬語</span>)</span> is another type of <i>keigo</i>. It is used to make the person who is being spoken to in a higher position. It is mainly used at work and when speaking with teachers. In the past, this was a type of language that was formed based on the classes Japanese society used to have. <i>Saikoukeigo</i><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja"><a href="https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%9C%80%E9%AB%98%E6%95%AC%E8%AA%9E" class="extiw" title="ja:最高敬語">ja:最高敬語</a></span>)</span> is the highest <i>sonkeigo</i> that exists and it is only used for the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_emperor" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese emperor">Japanese emperor</a>, his family members and equivalent foreign nobles.</li>
<li><i>Kenjougo</i><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">謙譲語</span>)</span> lowers the position of the speaker or the subject of the conversation, and is primarily used at workplace and in the academia. This is also specifically used when the person is much older or in a higher position than the speaker, or often when one apologizes to someone else.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Java">Java</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Java">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Indonesia's <a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Javanese</a> majority ethnicity has many honorifics.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> For example:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Bang</i> or <i>Bung</i> is a somewhat outdated, egalitarian term to refer to a brotherhood among men. <i>Bang</i> is Betawi language for <i>Mas</i>.</li>
<li><i>Bapak</i> and its contraction <i>Pak</i> meaning: "Sir", "Mister", or literally "Father".</li>
<li><i>Bapak Cilik</i> and its contraction <i>Pak lik</i> are used for a very familiar friend or sir; they literally meaning "small father" or a relative younger than one's father.</li>
<li><i>Bapak Gede</i> and its contraction <i>Pak de</i> are used for a big father, uncle, or relative older than one's father, meaning literally "Grand Sir".</li>
<li><i>Bendara Raden Mas</i>, <i>Bendara Mas</i>, or the contraction '<i>ndoro</i>, meaning "Prince, flag-bearer 'His Highness'".</li>
<li><i>Eyang Putera Kakung</i> and its contraction <i>Eyang Kakung</i> meaning "grandfather", literally "Grand Sir".</li>
<li><i>Eyang Puteri</i> and its contraction <i>Eyang</i> meaning "grandmother", literally "Grand Lady".</li>
<li><i>Ibu</i> and its contraction <i>Bu</i> meaning: "Madam", "Ma'am", "Ms.", or "Mrs.", and literally meaning "Mother".</li>
<li><i>I Gusti</i> means "His or Her Royal Majesty"</li>
<li><i>Kyai</i> is an honorific used with a highly respected Muslim cleric (same as <a href="/wiki/Mullah" title="Mullah">mullah</a> in Iran and <a href="/wiki/Maulana" class="mw-redirect" title="Maulana">maulana</a> in South Asia).</li>
<li><i>Mbak yu</i> and the more common <i>mbak</i> are derived from Surakarta court. Initially used to address unmarried women who are adolescents or of marriagable age, they are now used with all women, with no age or marital status connotation.</li>
<li><i>Mbok</i> is not an honorific; it denotes an older woman of very low status, in some cases a <a href="/wiki/Domestic_worker" title="Domestic worker">domestic worker</a>.</li>
<li><i>Raden Behi</i>, contracted to <i>Den Behi</i>, means "Heir Apparent" and is now obsolete.</li>
<li><i>Raden Emas</i> and its contraction <i>Mas</i> denote: "Mr." among colleagues, friends, and others of slightly higher age or social status, meaning literally "Golden Son", "Lord", or "Heir Apparent".</li>
<li><i>Raden Emas Behi</i>, contracted to <i>Mas Behi</i>, means "Second Heir Apparent" and is now obsolete.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Korea">Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Korea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Korean_honorifics" title="Korean honorifics">Korean honorifics</a> are similar to Japanese honorifics, and similarly, their use is mandatory in many formal and informal social situations. Korean grammar as a whole tends to function on hierarchy;  honorific stems are appended to verbs and some nouns, and in many cases, one word may be exchanged for another word entirely with the same verb- or noun-meaning, but with different honorific connotations. Linguists say that there are six levels of honorifics in Korean but, in daily conversation, only four of them are widely used in contemporary Korean. Suffix -ssi-(씨) is used at most honorific verbs, but not always. It is considered very impolite and offensive not to use honorific sentences or words with someone who is older or has a higher social status, and most Koreans avoid using non-honorific sentences with someone they have met for the first time. In Korean, names, first or last, always precede a title, e.g., Park Sonsaengnim, Park Kwanjangnim, etc.
</p>
<h3><span id="Malaysia.2C_Brunei_and_Singapore"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Malaysia,_Brunei_and_Singapore">Malaysia, Brunei and Singapore</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Malaysia, Brunei and Singapore">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Malay_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Malay names">Malay names</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malay_styles_and_titles" title="Malay styles and titles">Malay styles and titles</a></div>
<p>A complex system of <a href="/wiki/Malay_titles#Federal_titles" class="mw-redirect" title="Malay titles">Titles and honorifics</a> is extensively used in the <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">Malay language</a>-speaking cultures in <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>. In contrast <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, whose Malay royalty was abolished by the British colonial government in 1891, has adopted civic titles for its leaders. Being Muslim, Malay people address high-ranking religious scholars as <i>tok imam</i> (grandpa imam). <i>Tok dalang</i> is a honorific used to address a village leader.
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> has numerous honorific forms that may be used with or as a substitute for names. The most common honorifics in Pakistan are usually placed immediately before the name of the subject or immediately after the subject. There are many variations across Pakistan.
</p>
<ul><li><i>Prefix type</i>: The traditional <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a> honorific in Pakistan for a man is the prefix <i>Mohtaram</i>. For example, Syed Mohammad Jahangir would become Mohtaram Syed Mohammad Jahangir. The traditional Urdu honorific in <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> for a woman is the prefix <i>Mohtarama</i>. For example, Shamim Ara would become Mohtarama Shamim Ara.</li>
<li><i>Suffix type</i>: The traditional Urdu honorific in Pakistan for a man is the suffix <i>Sahab</i>. For example, Syed Zaki Ahmed would become Syed Zaki Ahmed Sahab. The traditional Urdu honorific in Pakistan for a woman is the suffix <i>Sahiba</i>; for instance, Shamim Ara would become Shamim Ara Sahiba.</li></ul>

<p><a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> honorifics generally follow the second name, especially if they refer to gender or particular social statuses (e.g., Name Agha [Mr.], Name Khanom [Ms.], Name Ostad [teacher or cleric], Name Rayis [manager, leader or director]). Such honorifics are used both in formal and informal situations. A more formal honorific referring to gender would be <i>Jenab</i> [His Excellency], which preceds Name Agha [Mr.] and <i>Sarkar</i> [Her Excellency], which precedes Name Khanom [Ms.]. A newer honorific is <i>Arjomand</i> [esteemed], which comes after other honorifics (except those referring to gender), and is not gender-specific (e.g., Ostad Arjomand Name Surname, or Rayis Arjomand Sarkar Khanom Name Surname). They are generally used in very formal situations.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines">Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Filipino_styles_and_honorifics" title="Filipino styles and honorifics">Filipino styles and honorifics</a></div>
<p>The usage of Filipino honorifics differs from person to person, though commonalities occur like the occasional insertion of the word <i>po</i> or <i>ho</i> in conversations, and their dependence on age-structured hierarchies. Though some have become obsolete, many are still widely used in order to denote respect, friendliness, or affection. Some new "honorifics", mainly used by teenagers, are experiencing surges in popularity.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">Tagalog language</a> has honorifics like <i>Binibini/Ate</i> ("Miss", "Big sister"), <i>Ginang/Aling/Manang</i> (“Mrs.”, "Madam"), <i>Ginoo/Mang/Manong/Kuya</i> ("Mister", "Sir", "Big brother") that have roots in Chinese culture.
</p><p>Depending on one's relation with the party being addressed, various honorifics may be used.
</p><p>As such addressing a man who is older, has a higher rank at work or has a higher social standing, one may use Mr or Sir followed by the First/ last/ or full name.
Addressing a woman in a similar situation as above one may use “Miss”, or “Madam” and its contraction “Ma'am”, followed by First/ last/ or full name. Older married women may prefer to be addressed as “Mrs.”
The use of Sir/Miss/Madam or Ma’am, followed by the first name, nickname, or surname is usually restricted to Filipino vernacular and social conversation, even in television and film. Despite this, non-Filipinos and naturalized Filipinos (such as expat students and professionals) also address older people in the Filipino way.
</p><p>On a professional level, many use educational or occupational titles such as Architect, Engineer, Doctor, Attorney (often abbreviated as Arch./Archt./Ar., Engr., Dr. [or sometimes Dra. for female doctors], and Atty. respectively) on casual and even formal bases. Stricter etiquette systems frown upon this practise as a sign of Filipino professionals' obsession with flaunting their educational attainment and professional status.<sup id="cite_ref-avecilla_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avecilla-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  Despite this, some of their clients (especially non-Filipinos) would address them as simply Mr. or Mrs./Ms. followed by their surnames (or even Sir/Ma'am) in conversation. It is very rare, however, for a Filipino (especially those born and educated abroad) to address Filipino architects, engineers, and lawyers, even mentioning and referring to their names, the non-Philippine (i.e. international standard) way.
</p><p>Even foreigners who work in the Philippines or naturalized Filipino citizens, including foreign spouses of Filipinos, who hold some of these titles and descriptions (especially as instructors in Philippine colleges and universities) are addressed in the same way as their Filipino counterparts, although it may sound awkward or unnatural to some language purists who argue that the basic titles or either <i>Sir</i> or <i>Ma'am/Madam</i> are to be employed for simplicity, as they are unnecessary when he or she is included in a list of wedding sponsors, or when his or her name appears in the list of officials of a country club or similar organization.  They are uncalled for in public donations, religious activities, parents-teachers association events, athletic competitions, society pages of newspapers, and in any activity that has nothing to do with one’s title or educational attainment.<sup id="cite_ref-avecilla_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avecilla-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It is also acceptable to treat those titles and descriptions (except <i>Doctor</i>) as adjectival nouns (i.e., first letter not capitalized, e.g. <i>architect</i> &lt;name&gt;) instead.
</p><p>Even though Doctor is really a title in standard English, the "created" titles Architect, Attorney, and Engineer (among other examples) are a result of vanity (titles herald achievement and success; they distinguish the title holder from the rest of society) and insecurity (the title holder's achievements and successes might be ignored unless announced to the public),<sup id="cite_ref-avecilla_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-avecilla-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> even due to historical usage of pseudo-titles in newspapers when Filipinos first began writing in English.
</p><p>Possible reasons are firstly, the fact the English taught to Filipinos was the “egalitarian” English of the New World, and that the Americans who colonized the Philippines encountered lowland societies that already used Iberian linguistic class markers like "Don" and "Doña." Secondly, the fundamental contradiction of the American colonial project. The Americans who occupied the Philippines justified their actions through the rhetoric of "<a href="/wiki/Benevolent_assimilation" title="Benevolent assimilation">benevolent assimilation</a>". In other words, they were only subjugating Filipinos to teach them values like American egalitarianism, which is the opposite of colonial anti-equality. Thirdly, the power of American colonialism lays in its emphasis on education – an education that supposedly exposed Filipinos to the "wonders" of the American way of life. Through education, the American colonial state bred a new elite of Filipinos trained in a new, more "modern", American system. People with advanced degrees like law or engineering were at the apex of this system. Their prestige, as such, not only rested on their purported intelligence, but also their mastery of the colonizer’s way of life. This, Lisandro Claudio suspects, is the source of the magical and superstitious attachment Filipinos have to attorneys, architects and engineers. The language they use is still haunted by their colonial experience. They linguistically privilege professionals because their colonizers made us value a certain kind of white-collar work.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Again, even expatriate professionals in the Philippines were affected by these reasons when they resided and married a Filipino or were naturalized so it's not unusual for them to be addressed Filipino style.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish-speaking_cultures">Spanish-speaking cultures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Spanish-speaking cultures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> has a number of honorific forms that may be used with or as substitutes for names, such as <i>señor</i> or <i>caballero</i> ("Mr.", "Sir", "Gentleman"); <i>señora</i> ("Madam", "Mrs.", "Lady", "ma'am") and <i>señorita</i> ("Miss", "young lady"); <i>licenciado</i> for a person with bachelor's or a professional degree (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Lawyer" title="Lawyer">attorneys</a> and <a href="/wiki/Engineer" title="Engineer">engineers</a>); <i>maestro</i> for a teacher, master mechanic, or person with a master's degree; <i>doctor</i> ("doctor"); etc. Also used is <i>don</i> (male) or <i>doña</i> (female) for people of rank or, in some Latin American countries (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a>), for any senior citizen.  In some Latin American countries, like <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, "Doctor" is used for any respected figure regardless of whether they have a doctoral degree (for instance Colombian presidents are often referred to as Doctor ___); likewise "Maestro" is used for artistic masters, especially painters.
</p><p>Additionally, older people and those with whom one would speak respectfully (e.g., one's boss or teacher), are often addressed as <i>usted,</i> abbreviated <i>ud.</i>, a formal/respectful way of saying "you" (e.g. <i>Dra. Polo, ¿cómo está usted?</i> Dr. Polo, how are you?). The word <i>usted</i> historically comes from the honorific title <i>vuestra merced</i> (literally "your mercy"). This formal you is accompanied by verb conjugation that is different from the informal you <i>tú</i>. Intimate friends and relatives are addressed as <i>tú</i>. In some regions, addressing a relative stranger as <i>tú</i> can be considered disrespectful or provocative, except when it's directed to a person notably younger than the speaker, or in an especially informal context.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pingelap">Pingelap</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Pingelap">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Pingelapese is a Micronesian language spoken on the Pingelap atoll and on two of the eastern Caroline Islands, called the high island of Pohnpei. Pingelapese does not employ many honorifics into their speech. Their society is structured in a way that everyone is seen as equal, most likely due to the fact that there are so few of them due to emigration. There is no structured hierarchy to enforce the use of honorific speech. There are not many polite vocabulary words and the language they use can be classified as a commoners’ language.
</p><p>However, among the Micronesian languages, Pohnpeian is the only language that uses a thoroughly developed honorific speech. This demonstrates that a highly structured hierarchical society was very important in their culture. There are multiple ways that Pohnpeic speakers show respect through their language. In the Pohnpeic language there is royal language, which is used for the two highest-ranking chiefs. Next, respect honorifics are used with other superiors and people who are considered respected equals. There is not only the use of honorifics, but humiliative language as well, which is used to lower oneself below higher-ranking people, showing respect and reverence. This speech was lost in Pingelap when Pohnpei speakers migrated to the Pingelap atoll and adapted their more casual way of speaking.
</p><p>Even though the younger generation of Pingelapese speakers does not use honorific speech, elders in the language report being taught a form of ‘language of respect.’ This language was to be used to address elders and leaders in the community. Women were also told to use it towards their brothers and with their children. Phrases could be made polite by adding the second person singular possessive suffix –<i>mwi</i>. Other ways to utilize honorific speech is by changing words entirely.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thailand">Thailand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Thailand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>According to Thai translator, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mui_Poopoksakul&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mui Poopoksakul (page does not exist)">Mui Poopoksakul</a>, "The Thai language is absolutely immediate in its indication of the speaker and addressee's places in the society and their relationship to each other. Thai has honorifics as well as what I like to call 'dishonorifics': it has a multitude of pronouns that are extremely nuanced—for example, there are so many ways to say 'I', and most of them already indicate the speaker's gender and often their age and societal standing relative to the person they are speaking to."<sup id="cite_ref-Semel-2018_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Semel-2018-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The most common Thai honorifics are used to differentiate age between friends, family, and peers. The most commonly used are:
</p>
<ul><li>คุณ (<small><a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_General_System_of_Transcription" title="Royal Thai General System of Transcription">RTGS</a>:&#160;</small><span style="font-style:inherit" lang="th-Latn" title="Thai-language romanization"><i>khun</i></span>) (mid tone) is used the same way as "mister" or "Mrs" or "Miss". It is a formal way to refer to persons not overly familiar. It is also used as a pronoun for the word "you".</li></ul>
<ul><li>พี่ ({<small><a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_General_System_of_Transcription" title="Royal Thai General System of Transcription">RTGS</a>:&#160;</small><span style="font-style:inherit" lang="th-Latn" title="Thai-language romanization"><i>phi</i></span>) (falling tone) is used when speaking to or about an older sibling or friend. It is used for both men and women and can also be used when referring to oneself if one is older than the addressee(s)</li></ul>
<ul><li>น้อง (<small><a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_General_System_of_Transcription" title="Royal Thai General System of Transcription">RTGS</a>:&#160;</small><span style="font-style:inherit" lang="th-Latn" title="Thai-language romanization"><i>nong</i></span>) (high tone) is the exact opposite of the above. It is use when speaking to or about a younger sibling or friend. It is used between both men and women and can also be used when referring to oneself if the person speaking is younger than the addressee(s). It could be used by a babysitter to address his ward.</li></ul>
<ul><li>ครู (<small><a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_General_System_of_Transcription" title="Royal Thai General System of Transcription">RTGS</a>:&#160;</small><span style="font-style:inherit" lang="th-Latn" title="Thai-language romanization"><i>khru</i></span>) (mid tone) is used when addressing a teacher, translating literally to 'teacher'.</li></ul>
<ul><li>อาจารย์ ({<small><a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_General_System_of_Transcription" title="Royal Thai General System of Transcription">RTGS</a>:&#160;</small><span style="font-style:inherit" lang="th-Latn" title="Thai-language romanization"><i>achan</i></span>) (mid-tone both syllables) is used to address a professor. It is used much in the same way as <i>khru</i> however <i>achan</i> carries more prestige. It generally refers to someone who is a master in their field. Many <a href="/wiki/Theravada_Buddhist" class="mw-redirect" title="Theravada Buddhist">Theravada Buddhist</a> scholars and those who have dedicated their lives to Theravada Buddhism assume this title among their followers.</li></ul>
<ul><li>พระ (<small><a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_General_System_of_Transcription" title="Royal Thai General System of Transcription">RTGS</a>:&#160;</small><span style="font-style:inherit" lang="th-Latn" title="Thai-language romanization"><i>phra</i></span>) (high tone) This is perhaps one of the highest honorifics in Thai culture. It is reserved for monks and priests. It is also allows for use when referring to a most revered place or object such as a temple or palace. It is the Thai equivalent of <i><a href="/wiki/Bhikkhu" title="Bhikkhu">bhikkhu</a></i>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkey">Turkey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Turkey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">Turkish</a> honorifics generally follow the first name, especially if they refer to gender or particular social statuses (e.g. Name Bey [Mr.], Name Hanım [Ms.], Name Beyefendi [literally meaning "Lord Master"], Name Hanımefendi [literally meaning "Lady Master"], Name Hoca [teacher or cleric], Name Öğretmen [solely for teacher]). Such honorifics are used both in formal and informal situations. Another honorific is <i>Sayın</i>/Muhterem [esteemed], which precedes the surname or full name, and is not gender-specific. (e.g. Sayın/Muhterem Name Surname, or Sayın/Muhterem Surname). They are generally used in very formal situations.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vietnam">Vietnam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Vietnam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Honorifics in Vietnamese are more complex compared to Chinese, where the origins of many of these pronouns can be traced, and many have fallen out of usage or have been replaced due to the changing times. An honorific, or a pronoun, in Vietnamese when referring to a person acts as a way to define two peoples' degree of relationship with one another. Examples of these pronouns include 'chị' older sister, 'ông' male elder and 'chú' younger uncle (younger brother of father/only used on father's side). The exclusive use of the Vietnamese words for 'I' and 'you' are considered informal and rude. Rather honorifics are used to refer to oneself and to others. These terms generally differ from province to province, or region to region. As with East Asian tradition, the surname is written prior to the given name (i.e., Hoang Khai Dinh: Hoang is the surname and Khai Dinh is the given name). This occurs in all formal situations. However, placing the surname last has become a commonality in order to cater to westerners, for example, on social media sites such as Facebook. When referring to a person as Mr or Mrs (teacher, painter, etc.) as in the English tradition of 'Mr Hoang', the given name is more commonly used e.g., "Mr Khai Dinh") in order not to cause confusion. This is due to many Vietnamese sharing the same surname (e.g., up to 40% of Vietnamese share the surname Nguyen).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wuvulu-Aua">Wuvulu-Aua</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Wuvulu-Aua">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wuvulu-Aua does not normally incorporate honorifics as it is reserved for only the utmost respect. Originally without any honorifics, the semantics of pronouns change depending on the social context. In particular, the second person dual pronoun is used as an honorific address. The dual reference communicates that the second person is to be respected as two people. This honorific is typically reserved for in-laws. It is undocumented if any other honorifics exist beyond this one.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition_and_alternatives">Opposition and alternatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Honorific&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Opposition and alternatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>People who have a strong sense of <a href="/wiki/Egalitarianism" title="Egalitarianism">egalitarianism</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Quaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Quaker">Quakers</a> and certain <a href="/wiki/Socialist" class="mw-redirect" title="Socialist">socialists</a>, and others, eschew honorific titles. When addressing or referring to someone, they often use the person's name, an informal <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">pronoun</a>, or some other style implying social equality, such as "brother", "sister", "friend", or "<a href="/wiki/Comrade" title="Comrade">comrade</a>". This was also the practice in <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">Revolutionary France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Comecon" title="Comecon">socialist countries</a> which used <i>Citoyen[ne]</i> ("Citizen") as the manner of address.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Feminist criticism of the use of separate honorifics for married and unmarried women (<a href="/wiki/Mrs." title="Mrs.">Mrs.</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miss" title="Miss">Miss</a>) has led to some women's adopting the honorific "<a href="/wiki/Ms." title="Ms.">Ms.</a>" In modern United States culture, it is considered rude to assume someone is married and address them as Mrs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The all-inclusionary honorific <a href="/wiki/Mx_(title)" title="Mx (title)">Mx</a> has been adopted by some<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (June 2019)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, particularly in the UK<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>, applying to any human regardless of gender, occupation, rank, social status, or other social/cultural factors.
</p>
