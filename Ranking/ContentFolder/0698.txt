<p><b>Drum and bass</b> (also written as "<b>drum 'n' bass</b>" or "<b>drum &amp; bass</b>"; commonly abbreviated as "<b>D&amp;B</b>", "<b>DnB</b>" or "<b>D'n'B</b>") is a genre and branch of <a href="/wiki/Electronic_music" title="Electronic music">electronic music</a> characterised by fast <a href="/wiki/Break_(music)#Break_beat" title="Break (music)">breakbeats</a> (typically 160–180 <a href="/wiki/Tempo#Beats_per_minute" title="Tempo">beats per minute</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>) with heavy <a href="/wiki/Bass_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bass (music)">bass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sub-bass" title="Sub-bass">sub-bass</a> lines,<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">sampled</a> sources, and <a href="/wiki/Synthesizer" title="Synthesizer">synthesizers</a>. The style grew out of <a href="/wiki/Breakbeat_hardcore" title="Breakbeat hardcore">breakbeat hardcore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oldschool_jungle" class="mw-redirect" title="Oldschool jungle">jungle</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> during the early 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-globaldarkness_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globaldarkness-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  The popularity of drum and bass at its commercial peak ran parallel to several other homegrown dance styles in the UK. A major influence was the original Jamaican <a href="/wiki/Dub_music" title="Dub music">dub</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">reggae</a> sound. Another feature of the style is the complex <a href="/wiki/Syncopation" title="Syncopation">syncopation</a> of the drum tracks' <a href="/wiki/Break_(music)" title="Break (music)">breakbeat</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Amen_Break_video_on_youtube.com_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amen_Break_video_on_youtube.com-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Drum and bass subgenres include <a href="/wiki/Breakcore" title="Breakcore">breakcore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ragga_jungle" title="Ragga jungle">ragga jungle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hardstep" title="Hardstep">hardstep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Darkstep" title="Darkstep">darkstep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Techstep" title="Techstep">techstep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Neurofunk" title="Neurofunk">neurofunk</a>, ambient drum and bass, <a href="/wiki/Liquid_funk" title="Liquid funk">liquid funk</a> (aka liquid drum and bass), jump up, drumfunk, <a href="/wiki/Funkstep" title="Funkstep">funkstep</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sambass" title="Sambass">sambass</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Drill_%27n%27_bass" title="Drill &#39;n&#39; bass">drill 'n' bass</a>. From its roots in the UK, the style has established itself around the world. Drum and bass has influenced many other genres like <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop_music" title="Hip hop music">hip hop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Big_beat" title="Big beat">big beat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dubstep" title="Dubstep">dubstep</a>, <a href="/wiki/House_music" title="House music">house</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trip_hop" title="Trip hop">trip hop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ambient_music" title="Ambient music">ambient music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Techno" title="Techno">techno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a>. Drum and bass is dominated by a relatively small group of record labels. Major international music labels had shown very little interest in the drum and bass scene until <a href="/wiki/BMG_Rights_Management" title="BMG Rights Management">BMG Rights Management</a> acquired <a href="/wiki/Ram_Records_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ram Records (UK)">RAM</a> in February 2016.<sup id="cite_ref-bmg.com_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bmg.com-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Drum and bass remains most popular in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>, although it has developed scenes all around the world in countries such as the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>, Slovenia and <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_drum_and_bass" title="History of drum and bass">History of drum and bass</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Oldschool_jungle" class="mw-redirect" title="Oldschool jungle">Oldschool jungle</a></div>
<p>In the late 1980s and early 1990s, a growing <a href="/wiki/Nightclub" title="Nightclub">nightclub</a> and overnight outdoor event culture gave birth to a new electronic music style in the <a href="/wiki/Rave" title="Rave">rave</a> scene, which combined sampled <a href="/wiki/Syncopated" class="mw-redirect" title="Syncopated">syncopated</a> beats, or breakbeats, and other samples from a wide range of different musical genres and, occasionally, samples of music, dialogue and effects from films and television programmes. A faster subgenre was known as "<a href="/wiki/Hardcore_(electronic_dance_music_genre)" title="Hardcore (electronic dance music genre)">hardcore</a>", but from as early as 1991, some musical tracks made up of these high-tempo break beats, with heavy basslines and samples of older Jamaican music, were referred to as "jungle <a href="/wiki/Techno" title="Techno">techno</a>". (influenced by Jack Smooth and Basement Records), and later just "<a href="/wiki/Jungle_music" title="Jungle music">jungle</a>", which became recognised as a separate musical genre popular at raves and on <a href="/wiki/Pirate_radio" title="Pirate radio">pirate radio</a> in <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">Britain</a>.
</p><p>It is important to note when discussing the history of drum and bass prior to jungle, music was becoming faster and more experimental. Professional DJ and producer Costica Kristian di Vogli (known professionally as C.K.) stated, "There was a progression as far as the speed of music is concerned. Anyone buying vinyl every week from 1989 to 1992 noticed this."
</p><p>By 1994, jungle had begun to gain mainstream popularity, and fans of the music (often referred to as <a href="/wiki/Junglist" title="Junglist">junglists</a>) became a more recognisable part of youth subculture. The genre further developed, incorporating and fusing elements from a wide range of existing musical genres, including the <a href="/wiki/Raggamuffin_music" class="mw-redirect" title="Raggamuffin music">raggamuffin</a> sound, <a href="/wiki/Dancehall" title="Dancehall">dancehall</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rapping" title="Rapping">MC</a> chants, dub basslines, and increasingly complex, heavily edited breakbeat percussion. Despite the affiliation with the <a href="/wiki/MDMA" title="MDMA">ecstasy</a>-fuelled rave scene, jungle also inherited associations with violence and criminal activity, both from the gang culture that had affected the UK's hip-hop scene and as a consequence of jungle's often aggressive or menacing sound and themes of violence (usually reflected in the choice of samples). However, this developed in tandem with the often positive reputation of the music as part of the wider rave scene and dancehall-based Jamaican music culture prevalent in London. By 1995, whether as a reaction to, or independently of this cultural schism, some jungle producers began to move away from the ragga-influenced style and create what would become collectively labelled, for convenience, as drum and bass.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As the genre became generally more polished and sophisticated technically, it began to expand its reach from pirate radio to commercial stations and gain widespread acceptance (circa 1995–1997). It also began to split into recognisable subgenres such as jump-up and <a href="/wiki/Hardstep" title="Hardstep">Hardstep</a>. As a lighter and often jazz-influenced style of drum and bass called "intelligent drum and bass" gained mainstream appeal, additional subgenres emerged including <a href="/wiki/Techstep" title="Techstep">techstep</a> (circa 1996–1997), which drew greater influence from <a href="/wiki/Techno" title="Techno">techno</a> music and the <a href="/wiki/Soundscape" title="Soundscape">soundscapes</a> of science fiction and <a href="/wiki/Anime" title="Anime">anime</a> films.
</p><p>The popularity of drum and bass at its commercial peak ran parallel to several other genres native to the UK, including <a href="/wiki/Big_beat" title="Big beat">big beat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hard_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Hard house">hard house</a>. Towards the turn of the millennium, however, its popularity was deemed to have dwindled, as the <a href="/wiki/UK_garage" title="UK garage">UK garage</a> offshoot known as <a href="/wiki/Speed_garage" title="Speed garage">speed garage</a> yielded several hit singles. Speed garage shared high tempos and heavy basslines with drum and bass, but otherwise followed the established conventions of "house music", with this and its freshness giving it an advantage commercially. di Vogli says, "It is often forgotten by my students that a type of music called "<a href="/wiki/Garage_house" title="Garage house">garage house</a>" existed in the late 1980s alongside <a href="/wiki/Hip_house" title="Hip house">hip house</a>, <a href="/wiki/Acid_house" title="Acid house">acid house</a> and other forms of house music." He continues, "This new garage of the mid-90s was not a form of house or a progression of garage house. The beats and tempo that define house are entirely different. This did cause further confusion in the presence of new house music of the mid-1990s being played alongside what was now being called garage."
</p><p>Despite this, the emergence of further subgenres and related styles such as <a href="/wiki/Liquid_funk" title="Liquid funk">liquid funk</a> brought a wave of new artists incorporating new ideas and techniques, supporting continual evolution of the genre. To this day, drum and bass makes frequent appearances in mainstream media and popular culture including in <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a>, as well as being a major reference point for subsequent genres such as <a href="/wiki/Grime_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Grime (music)">grime</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dubstep" title="Dubstep">dubstep</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Global_Bass_landscape_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Global_Bass_landscape-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> and producing successful artists including <a href="/wiki/Chase_%26_Status" title="Chase &amp; Status">Chase &amp; Status</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netsky_(musician)" title="Netsky (musician)">Netsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Metrik" title="Metrik">Metrik</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pendulum_(drum_and_bass_band)" title="Pendulum (drum and bass band)">Pendulum</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Musical_features">Musical features</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Musical features">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Drum and bass incorporates a number of scenes and styles, from the highly electronic, <a href="/wiki/Industrial_music" title="Industrial music">industrial</a> sounds of techstep to the use of conventional, acoustic instrumentation that characterize the more jazz-influenced end of the spectrum.<sup id="cite_ref-allmusic_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-SimonReynoldsOnTechstep_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SimonReynoldsOnTechstep-11">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The sounds of drum and bass are extremely varied due to the range of influences behind the music. Drum and bass could at one time be defined as a strictly electronic musical genre, with the only "live" element being the DJ's selection and mixing of records during a set. "Live" drum and bass using electric, electronic and acoustic instruments played by musicians on stage emerged over the ensuing years of the genre's development.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A very obvious and strong influence on jungle and drum and bass, thanks to the <a href="/wiki/British_African-Caribbean_community" class="mw-redirect" title="British African-Caribbean community">British African-Caribbean</a> <a href="/wiki/Sound_system_(DJ)" title="Sound system (DJ)">sound system</a> scene, is the original Jamaican <a href="/wiki/Dub_music" title="Dub music">dub</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">reggae</a> sound, with pioneers like <a href="/wiki/King_Tubby" title="King Tubby">King Tubby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peter_Tosh" title="Peter Tosh">Peter Tosh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sly_%26_Robbie" class="mw-redirect" title="Sly &amp; Robbie">Sly &amp; Robbie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Laswell" title="Bill Laswell">Bill Laswell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lee_%22Scratch%22_Perry" title="Lee &quot;Scratch&quot; Perry">Lee Perry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mad_Professor" title="Mad Professor">Mad Professor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roots_Radics" title="Roots Radics">Roots Radics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bob_Marley" title="Bob Marley">Bob Marley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Buju_Banton" title="Buju Banton">Buju Banton</a> heavily influencing the music.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This influence has lessened with time, but is still evident, with many tracks containing ragga vocals.
</p><p>As a musical style built around <a href="/wiki/Funk" title="Funk">funk</a> or <a href="/wiki/Syncopation" title="Syncopation">syncopated</a> <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_roll" title="Rock and roll">rock and roll</a> <a href="/wiki/Drum_break" class="mw-redirect" title="Drum break">breaks</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Brown" title="James Brown">James Brown</a>, <a href="/wiki/Al_Green" title="Al Green">Al Green</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marvin_Gaye" title="Marvin Gaye">Marvin Gaye</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ella_Fitzgerald" title="Ella Fitzgerald">Ella Fitzgerald</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gladys_Knight_%26_the_Pips" title="Gladys Knight &amp; the Pips">Gladys Knight &amp; the Pips</a>, <a href="/wiki/Billie_Holiday" title="Billie Holiday">Billie Holiday</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aretha_Franklin" title="Aretha Franklin">Aretha Franklin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Otis_Redding" title="Otis Redding">Otis Redding</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Supremes" title="The Supremes">the Supremes</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Commodores" class="mw-redirect" title="The Commodores">the Commodores</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Lee_Lewis" title="Jerry Lee Lewis">Jerry Lee Lewis</a>, and even <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jackson" title="Michael Jackson">Michael Jackson</a> acted as funk influences on the music.<sup id="cite_ref-Fabio1_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fabio1-17">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">Jazz</a> pioneer <a href="/wiki/Miles_Davis" title="Miles Davis">Miles Davis</a> has been named as a possible influence.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">Blues</a> artists like <a href="/wiki/Lead_Belly" title="Lead Belly">Lead Belly</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Johnson" title="Robert Johnson">Robert Johnson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Patton" class="mw-redirect" title="Charlie Patton">Charlie Patton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Muddy_Waters" title="Muddy Waters">Muddy Waters</a> and <a href="/wiki/B.B_King" class="mw-redirect" title="B.B King">B.B King</a> have also been cited by producers as inspirations. Even modern avant-garde composers such as <a href="/wiki/Henryk_Gorecki" class="mw-redirect" title="Henryk Gorecki">Henryk Gorecki</a> have received mention.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> One of the most influential tracks in drum and bass history was "Amen Brother" by <a href="/wiki/The_Winstons" title="The Winstons">The Winstons</a>, which contains a drum solo that has since become known as the "<a href="/wiki/Amen_break" title="Amen break">Amen break</a>", which, after being extensively used in early hip hop music, went on to become the basis for the rhythms used in drum and bass.<sup id="cite_ref-Amen_Break_video_on_youtube.com_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amen_Break_video_on_youtube.com-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Kevin_Saunderson" title="Kevin Saunderson">Kevin Saunderson</a> released a series of bass-heavy, minimal techno cuts as Reese/The Reese Project in the late '80s, which were hugely influential in drum and bass. One of his more famous basslines (Reese – "Just Want Another Chance", Incognito Records, 1988) was indeed sampled on Renegade's <i>Terrorist</i> and countless others since, being known simply as the 'Reese' bassline. He followed these up with equally influential (and bassline-heavy) tracks in the UK hardcore style as Tronik House in 1991–1992. Another Detroit artist who was important to the scene was <a href="/wiki/Carl_Craig" title="Carl Craig">Carl Craig</a>. The sampled-up jazz break on Craig's <i>Bug in the Bassbin</i> was also influential on the newly emerging sound. DJs at the <a href="/wiki/Heaven_(nightclub)" title="Heaven (nightclub)">Heaven</a> nightclub on "Rage" nights used to play it as fast as their <a href="/wiki/Technics_(brand)" title="Technics (brand)">Technics</a> record decks would go, pitching it up in the process.<sup id="cite_ref-Fabio2_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fabio2-25">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the late 1980s and early 1990s, the tradition of breakbeat use in hip hop production had influenced the sound of <a href="/wiki/Breakbeat_hardcore" title="Breakbeat hardcore">breakbeat hardcore</a>, which in turn led to the emergence of jungle, drum and bass, and other genres that shared the same use of broken beats.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Drum and bass shares many musical characteristics with hip-hop, though it is nowadays mostly stripped of lyrics. <a href="/wiki/Grandmaster_Flash" title="Grandmaster Flash">Grandmaster Flash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roger_Troutman" title="Roger Troutman">Roger Troutman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Afrika_Bambaata" class="mw-redirect" title="Afrika Bambaata">Afrika Bambaata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Run_DMC" class="mw-redirect" title="Run DMC">Run DMC</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mac_Dre" title="Mac Dre">Mac Dre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Public_Enemy_(band)" title="Public Enemy (band)">Public Enemy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Schooly_D" class="mw-redirect" title="Schooly D">Schooly D</a>, <a href="/wiki/N.W.A" title="N.W.A">N.W.A</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kid_Frost" class="mw-redirect" title="Kid Frost">Kid Frost</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wu-Tang_Clan" title="Wu-Tang Clan">Wu-Tang Clan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dr._Dre" title="Dr. Dre">Dr. Dre</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mos_Def" title="Mos Def">Mos Def</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beastie_Boys" title="Beastie Boys">Beastie Boys</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Pharcyde" class="mw-redirect" title="Pharcyde">Pharcyde</a> are very often directly sampled, regardless of their general influence.<sup id="cite_ref-Zinc_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zinc-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Clearly, drum and bass has been influenced by other music genres, though influences from sources external to the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_dance_music" title="Electronic dance music">electronic dance music</a> scene perhaps lessened following the shifts from jungle to drum and bass, and through to so-called "intelligent drum and bass" and techstep.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> It still remains a fusion music style.
</p><p>Some tracks are illegally remixed and released on <a href="/wiki/White_label" title="White label">white label</a> (technically bootleg), often to acclaim. For example, DJ Zinc's remix of <a href="/wiki/The_Fugees" class="mw-redirect" title="The Fugees">The Fugees</a>' "<a href="/wiki/Ready_or_Not_(Fugees_song)" title="Ready or Not (Fugees song)">Ready or Not</a>", also known as "Fugee Or Not", was eventually released with the Fugees' permission after talk of legal action, though ironically, the Fugees' version infringed <a href="/wiki/Enya" title="Enya">Enya</a>'s copyright to an earlier song.<sup id="cite_ref-Zinc_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zinc-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> White labels, along with <a href="/wiki/Dubplate" title="Dubplate">dubplates</a>, played an important part in drum and bass musical culture.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drum_elements">Drum elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Drum elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Sampling">Sampling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sampling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Amen_break" title="Amen break">Amen break</a> was synonymous with early drum and bass productions but other samples have had a significant impact, including the <a href="/wiki/Apache_(single)" class="mw-redirect" title="Apache (single)">Apache</a>, <a href="/wiki/Funky_Drummer" title="Funky Drummer">Funky Drummer</a>, "Soul Pride", "Scorpio" and "<a href="/wiki/Think_(About_It)" title="Think (About It)">Think (About It)</a>" breaks.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Early pioneers often used <a href="/wiki/Akai" title="Akai">Akai</a> <a href="/wiki/Sampler_(musical_instrument)" title="Sampler (musical instrument)">samplers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Music_sequencer" title="Music sequencer">sequencers</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Atari_ST" title="Atari ST">Atari ST</a> to create their tracks.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthesis">Synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Roland_TR-808_drum_machine.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Roland_TR-808_drum_machine.jpg/220px-Roland_TR-808_drum_machine.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Roland_TR-808_drum_machine.jpg/330px-Roland_TR-808_drum_machine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Roland_TR-808_drum_machine.jpg/440px-Roland_TR-808_drum_machine.jpg 2x" data-file-width="447" data-file-height="271" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Roland_TR-808_drum_machine.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Roland_TR-808" title="Roland TR-808">Roland TR-808 Rhythm Composer</a>, produced 1980–1984, had a bass drum sound which became very important in drum and bass.</div></div></div>
<p>Of equal importance is the TR-808 kick drum, an artificially pitch-downed or elongated <a href="/wiki/Bass_drum" title="Bass drum">bass drum</a> sound sampled from <a href="/wiki/Roland_Corporation" title="Roland Corporation">Roland</a>'s classic <a href="/wiki/Roland_TR-808" title="Roland TR-808">TR-808</a> drum machine, and a sound which has been subject to an enormous amount of experimentation over the years.<sup id="cite_ref-TR-808_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TR-808-37">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhythm_composition">Rhythm composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rhythm composition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Many drum and bass tracks have featured more than one sampled breakbeat in them and a technique of switching between two breaks after each bar developed. Examples of this can be heard on mid-90s releases such as <a href="/wiki/J_Majik" title="J Majik">J Majik</a>'s "Your Sound". A more recent commonly used break is the "Tramen", which combines the <a href="/wiki/Amen_break" title="Amen break">Amen break</a>, a <a href="/wiki/James_Brown" title="James Brown">James Brown</a> funk breakbeat ("<a href="/wiki/Tighten_Up_(Archie_Bell_%26_the_Drells_song)" title="Tighten Up (Archie Bell &amp; the Drells song)">Tighten Up</a>" or "Samurai" break) and an <a href="/wiki/Alex_Reece" title="Alex Reece">Alex Reece</a> drum and bass breakbeat.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The relatively fast drum beat forms a canvas on which a producer can create tracks to appeal to almost any taste and often will form only a background to the other elements of the music. <a href="/wiki/Syncopated" class="mw-redirect" title="Syncopated">Syncopated</a> breakbeats remain the most distinctive element as without these a high-tempo <a href="/wiki/Time_signature" title="Time signature">4/4</a> dance track could be classified as <a href="/wiki/Techno" title="Techno">techno</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gabber" title="Gabber">gabber</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The complex <a href="/wiki/Syncopation" title="Syncopation">syncopation</a> of the drum tracks' <a href="/wiki/Break_(music)" title="Break (music)">breakbeat</a> is another facet of production on which producers can spend a very large amount of time. The <a href="/wiki/Amen_break" title="Amen break">Amen break</a> is generally acknowledged to have been the most-used (and often considered the most powerful) break in drum and bass.<sup id="cite_ref-Amen_Break_video_on_youtube.com_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Amen_Break_video_on_youtube.com-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bass_elements">Bass elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Bass elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The genre places great importance on the <a href="/wiki/Bassline" title="Bassline">bassline</a>, in this case a deep <a href="/wiki/Sub-bass" title="Sub-bass">sub-bass</a> musical pattern which can be felt physically through powerful sound systems due to the low-range frequencies favoured. There has been considerable exploration of different <a href="/wiki/Timbre" title="Timbre">timbres</a> in the bass line region, particularly within <a href="/wiki/Techstep" title="Techstep">techstep</a>. The bass lines most notably originate from <a href="/wiki/Sampling_(music)" title="Sampling (music)">sampled</a> sources or <a href="/wiki/Synthesizer" title="Synthesizer">synthesizers</a>. Bass lines performed with a bass instrument, whether it is electric, acoustic or a <a href="/wiki/Double_bass" title="Double bass">double bass</a>, are less common, but examples can be found in the work of artists such as <a href="/wiki/Shapeshifter_(band)" title="Shapeshifter (band)">Shapeshifter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squarepusher" title="Squarepusher">Squarepusher</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pendulum_(drum_and_bass_band)" title="Pendulum (drum and bass band)">Pendulum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roni_Size" title="Roni Size">Roni Size</a> and <a href="/wiki/STS9" class="mw-redirect" title="STS9">STS9</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_elements">Atmospheric elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Atmospheric elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Atmospheric pads and samples may be added over the fundamental drum and bass to provide different feels.   These have included "light" elements such as ambient pads as found in ambient electronica and samples of jazz and world musics, or "dark" elements such as <a href="/wiki/Consonance_and_dissonance" title="Consonance and dissonance">dissonant</a> pads and sci-fi samples to induce anxiety in the dancer.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vocal_and_Melody_elements">Vocal and Melody elements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Vocal and Melody elements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Old-school DnB usually included an MC providing vocals.   Some styles (such as jazz influenced DnB) also include melodic instruments soloing over the music.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tempo">Tempo</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tempo">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Drum and bass is usually between 160–180 BPM, in contrast to other <a href="/wiki/Breakbeat" title="Breakbeat">breakbeat</a>-based dance styles such as <a href="/wiki/Nu_skool_breaks" title="Nu skool breaks">nu skool breaks</a>, which maintain a slower pace at around 130–140 BPM. A general upward trend in tempo has been observed during the evolution of drum and bass. The earliest forms of drum and bass clocked in at around 130 bpm in 1990/1991, speeding up to around 155–165 BPM by 1993. Since around 1996, drum and bass tempos have predominantly stayed in the 170–180 range. Recently, some producers have started to once again produce tracks with slower tempos (that is, in the 150-170 bpm range), but the mid-170s tempo is still a hallmark of the drum and bass sound.<sup id="cite_ref-Zinc_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zinc-28">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Fabio3_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fabio3-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A track combining the same elements (broken beat, bass, production techniques) as a drum and bass track, but with a slower tempo (say 140 BPM), might not be drum and bass, but  instead may qualify as a drum and bass-influenced breakbeat track.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Drop">Drop</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Drop">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many mixing points begin or end with a "<a href="/wiki/Drop_(music)" title="Drop (music)">drop</a>". The drop is the point in a track where a switch of rhythm or bassline occurs and usually follows a recognisable build section and <a href="/wiki/Breakdown_(music)" title="Breakdown (music)">breakdown</a>. Sometimes, the drop is used to switch between tracks, layering components of different tracks, as the two records may be simply ambient breakdowns at this point. Some DJs prefer to combine breakbeats, a more difficult exercise. Some drops are so popular that the DJ will "rewind" or "reload" or "lift up" the record by spinning it back and restarting it at the build. The drop is often a key point from the point of view of the dance floor, since the drum breaks often fade out to leave an ambient intro playing. When the beats re-commence they are often more complex and accompanied by a heavier bassline, encouraging the crowd to begin dancing.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Live_performance">Live performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Live performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pendulumsamuelnordstrom.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Pendulumsamuelnordstrom.JPG/220px-Pendulumsamuelnordstrom.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Pendulumsamuelnordstrom.JPG/330px-Pendulumsamuelnordstrom.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Pendulumsamuelnordstrom.JPG/440px-Pendulumsamuelnordstrom.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1936" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pendulumsamuelnordstrom.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Pendulum_(drum_and_bass_band)" title="Pendulum (drum and bass band)">Pendulum</a> performing live in 2010</div></div></div>
<p>Drum and bass exhibits a full frequency response which can sometimes only be fully appreciated on sound systems which can handle very low frequencies, including <a href="/wiki/Sub-bass" title="Sub-bass">sub-bass</a> frequencies that are often felt more than heard. As befits its name, the bass element of the music is particularly pronounced, with the comparatively sparse arrangements of drum and bass tracks allowing room for basslines that are deeper than most other forms of dance music. Consequently, drum and bass parties are often advertised as featuring uncommonly loud and bass-heavy sound systems.
</p><p>There are however many albums specifically designed for personal listening. The DJ mix is a particularly popular form of release, with a popular DJ or producer mixing live, or on a computer, a variety of tracks for personal listening. Additionally, there are many albums containing unmixed tracks, suited for home or car listening.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DJ_performance">DJ performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: DJ performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although this practice has declined in popularity,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> DJs are often accompanied by one or more <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Ceremonies" class="mw-redirect" title="Master of Ceremonies">MCs</a>, drawing on the genre's roots in <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop" title="Hip hop">hip hop</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">reggae</a>/<a href="/wiki/Ragga" title="Ragga">ragga</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>MCs do not generally receive the same level of recognition as producer/DJs, and some events are specifically marketed as being MC-free. There are relatively few well-known drum and bass MCs, including <a href="/wiki/Stevie_Hyper_D" title="Stevie Hyper D">Stevie Hyper D</a> (deceased), MC GQ, MC Moose, MC Dett, MC Fearless, the <a href="/wiki/Ragga_Twins" title="Ragga Twins">Ragga Twins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dynamite_MC" title="Dynamite MC">Dynamite MC</a>, MC Fats, MC Conrad, Shabba D, <a href="/wiki/Skibadee" title="Skibadee">Skibadee</a>, Bassman, MC Stamina, MC Fun, Evil B, Trigga, Eskman, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Harry_Shotta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Harry Shotta (page does not exist)">Harry Shotta</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mr_Traumatik&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Mr Traumatik (page does not exist)">Mr Traumatik</a> and MC Infinity.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subgenres">Subgenres</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Subgenres">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:142px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Congo_Natty_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Congo_Natty_2.jpg/140px-Congo_Natty_2.jpg" decoding="async" width="140" height="211" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Congo_Natty_2.jpg/210px-Congo_Natty_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Congo_Natty_2.jpg/280px-Congo_Natty_2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1700" data-file-height="2562" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Congo_Natty_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Congo Natty, a ragga jungle artist</div></div></div>
<p>Smaller scenes within the drum and bass community have developed and the scene as a whole has become much more fractured into specific subgenres, which have been grouped<sup id="cite_ref-Ferrigno_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ferrigno-46">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> into "light" (influenced by <a href="/wiki/Ambient_music" title="Ambient music">ambient</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>, and <a href="/wiki/World_music" title="World music">world music</a>) and "heavy" (influenced by <a href="/wiki/Industrial_music" title="Industrial music">industrial music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sci-fi" class="mw-redirect" title="Sci-fi">sci-fi</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Anxiety" title="Anxiety">anxiety</a>) styles, including:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mainline_drum_and_bass">Mainline drum and bass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Mainline drum and bass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Ragga_jungle" title="Ragga jungle">Ragga drum &amp; bass</a> was inspired by the original <a href="/wiki/Ragga_jungle" title="Ragga jungle">ragga jungle</a> style, with influences from <a href="/wiki/Reggae" title="Reggae">reggae</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dancehall" title="Dancehall">dancehall</a> music. Notable artists include <a href="/wiki/Shy_FX" title="Shy FX">Shy FX</a>, <a href="/wiki/T_Power" title="T Power">T Power</a>, Congo Natty, Potential Bad Boy, Marcus Visionary, Serial Killaz, Ed Solo, Deekline, Isaac Maya, Run Tingz Cru, Psychofreud, Benny Page and vocalists such as David Boomah, Top Cat, Tenor Fly and General Levy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>
<li><a href="/w/index.php?title=Jump-up_music&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jump-up music (page does not exist)">Jump-up</a>, appearing in the mid-1990s,<sup id="cite_ref-bogdanov_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bogdanov-47">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> employs heavy and energetic drum and bass,<sup id="cite_ref-bogdanov_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bogdanov-47">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> characterized by robotic and heavy bass sounds. It also is generally less serious and contains more humor than other subgenres. Notable artists include <a href="/wiki/DJ_Hazard_(musician)" title="DJ Hazard (musician)">DJ Hazard</a>, Generation Dub (Original Sin &amp; Sub Zero), Baron, Desire, Cabbie, Macky Gee, Clipz, Nightwalker, Callide, Hedex, Epsy, Taxman, Jaydan, Sub Zero, Original Sin, Annix, Konichi, Decimal Bass, Spaow, Nu Elementz, Tyke, DJ Zen, Majistrate,<sup id="cite_ref-ishkurOnJumpUp_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ishkurOnJumpUp-48">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Twisted Individual, Distorted Minds, TC, Heist, DJ Guv, Looney, Premium, Upgrade, DJ Pleasure, <a href="/wiki/DJ_Hype" title="DJ Hype">DJ Hype</a><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> and his label Playaz Recordings.
<ul><li>"Clownstep" is a derogatory term sometimes used to describe excessively silly and/or cliched jump-up.</li></ul></li>
<li>Drumstep or halftime is a combination of drum and bass and dubstep, where the beat structure is <a href="/wiki/Half-time_(music)" title="Half-time (music)">half time</a>, while the remaining elements still adhere to the usual sub-bass and tempo of drum and bass.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Drill_%27n%27_bass" title="Drill &#39;n&#39; bass">Drill 'n' bass</a> (also known as Fungle and Spunk jazz) incorporates double-time drum 'n' bass with undanceable rhythms, low-brow humor, and ambient vibes.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> The subgenre was developed by <a href="/wiki/Squarepusher" title="Squarepusher">Squarepusher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aphex_Twin" title="Aphex Twin">Aphex Twin</a>, whose rapid and irregularly <a href="/wiki/Syncopation" title="Syncopation">syncopated</a> basslines discouraged dancing. Other pioneers include <a href="/wiki/Bogdan_Raczynski" title="Bogdan Raczynski">Bogdan Raczynski</a> and Datach'i.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Light_drum_and_bass">Light drum and bass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Light drum and bass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Intelligent drum &amp; bass or intelligent jungle is a smoother style, influenced by <a href="/wiki/Ambient_music" title="Ambient music">ambient music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chill-out_music" title="Chill-out music">chillout</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">Soul music</a>. It was pioneered by such artists as <a href="/wiki/Omni_Trio" class="mw-redirect" title="Omni Trio">Omni Trio</a>, Foul Play, <a href="/wiki/Seba_(musician)" title="Seba (musician)">Seba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blu_Mar_Ten" title="Blu Mar Ten">Blu Mar Ten</a>, Nookie, Hyper-On-Experience, DJ Pulse, Higher Sense, <a href="/wiki/Deep_Blue_(musician)" title="Deep Blue (musician)">Deep Blue</a> (Sean O'Keefe, Cause4Concern Records), <a href="/wiki/Photek" title="Photek">Photek</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jack_Smooth&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Jack Smooth (page does not exist)">Jack Smooth</a> (Basement Records), Blame,<sup id="cite_ref-ishkurOnAtmosphericDrumAndBass_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ishkurOnAtmosphericDrumAndBass-55">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/LTJ_Bukem" title="LTJ Bukem">LTJ Bukem</a><sup id="cite_ref-ishkurOnAtmosphericDrumAndBass_55-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ishkurOnAtmosphericDrumAndBass-55">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> and his label <a href="/wiki/Good_Looking_Records" title="Good Looking Records">Good Looking Records</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-globaldarkness_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globaldarkness-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and the label <a href="/wiki/Moving_Shadow" title="Moving Shadow">Moving Shadow</a>.
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_drum_and_bass" class="mw-redirect" title="Atmospheric drum and bass">Atmospheric drum and bass</a> creates a calmer yet more synthetic sound. It was pioneered by Calibre,<sup id="cite_ref-ishkurOnliquidFunk_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ishkurOnliquidFunk-56">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Zero Tolerance (Zero T) &amp; Beta 2, <a href="/wiki/London_Elektricity" title="London Elektricity">London Elektricity</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ishkurOnliquidFunk_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ishkurOnliquidFunk-56">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> High Contrast,<sup id="cite_ref-ishkurOnliquidFunk_56-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ishkurOnliquidFunk-56">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Logistics, <a href="/wiki/Nu:Tone" title="Nu:Tone">Nu:Tone</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ishkurOnliquidFunk_56-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ishkurOnliquidFunk-56">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Danny Byrd, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyantific&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cyantific (page does not exist)">Cyantific</a>, <a href="/wiki/Netsky_(musician)" title="Netsky (musician)">Netsky</a>, Lenzman, Technimatic (Technicolour &amp; Komatic), Hobzee &amp; Zyon Base, Paul T &amp; Edward Oberon, Hybrid Minds and labels such as <a href="/wiki/Hospital_Records" title="Hospital Records">Hospital Records</a>, Fokuz Records and Liquid V.</li></ul></li>
<li>Jazzstep or jazzy jungle demonstrates heavy influence by <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>. It uses typical jazz scales, rhythms and instrumentation. Notable artists include <a href="/wiki/Roni_Size_%26_Reprazent" title="Roni Size &amp; Reprazent">Roni Size &amp; Reprazent</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ishkurOnJazzstep_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ishkurOnJazzstep-57">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Goldie" title="Goldie">Goldie</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-ishkurOnJazzstep_57-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ishkurOnJazzstep-57">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Utah Jazz,<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> Morgan Sullyvan, <a href="/wiki/Makoto_(artist)" class="mw-redirect" title="Makoto (artist)">Makoto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alex_Reece" title="Alex Reece">Alex Reece</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-globaldarkness_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globaldarkness-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/DJ_Dextrous" title="DJ Dextrous">DJ Dextrous</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Liquid_funk" title="Liquid funk">Liquid funk</a> (or simply "liquid") draws heavily on harmonic and melodic grooves, and samples from <a href="/wiki/Funk" title="Funk">funk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_R%26B" title="Contemporary R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a>, <a href="/wiki/Disco" title="Disco">disco</a>, <a href="/wiki/House_music" title="House music">house</a> and <a href="/wiki/Breakbeat" title="Breakbeat">breakbeat</a>. The first liquid-only event was "Liquidiser" at Bristol Academy.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sambass" title="Sambass">Sambass</a>, a Brazilian drum and bass style, incorporates elements from <a href="/wiki/Samba" title="Samba">samba</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bossa_nova" title="Bossa nova">bossa nova</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Latin_music_(genre)" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin music (genre)">Latin music</a> styles. Pioneered by artists such as <a href="/wiki/DJ_Marky" title="DJ Marky">DJ Marky</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Xerxes_de_Oliveira" title="Xerxes de Oliveira">XRS</a>, <a href="/wiki/DJ_Patife" title="DJ Patife">DJ Patife</a> and Bryan Gee's label.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Heavy_drum_and_bass">Heavy drum and bass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Heavy drum and bass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Darkstep" title="Darkstep">Darkstep</a> is characterized by fast drums and a general dark mood, drawing influences from <a href="/wiki/Dark_ambient" title="Dark ambient">dark ambient</a>, <a href="/wiki/Industrial_music" title="Industrial music">industrial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_(electronic_dance_music_genre)" title="Hardcore (electronic dance music genre)">hardcore</a> music. Prominent artists include Technical Itch, Dylan, Kryptic Minds &amp; Leon Switch, B-Key, Resonant Evil, Infiltrata, SPL, <a href="/wiki/Counterstrike_(drum_and_bass_group)" title="Counterstrike (drum and bass group)">Counterstrike</a>, Evol Intent, <a href="/wiki/The_Panacea" class="mw-redirect" title="The Panacea">The Panacea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Limewax" title="Limewax">Limewax</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Current_Value" title="Current Value">Current Value</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. Pioneered by artists such as <a href="/wiki/Bad_Company_(drum_and_bass_group)" title="Bad Company (drum and bass group)">Bad Company UK</a> (DJ Fresh, D-Bridge, Maldini &amp; Vegas) <a href="/wiki/Ed_Rush" title="Ed Rush">Ed Rush</a>, <a href="/wiki/Optical_(musician)" title="Optical (musician)">Optical</a>, Konflict (Kemal &amp; Rob Data), <a href="/wiki/Dom_%26_Roland" title="Dom &amp; Roland">Dom &amp; Roland</a>, Dillinja, Ram Trilogy (Ant Miles, Andy C &amp; Shimon), Moving Fusion, Decoder &amp; Substance, Digital &amp; Spirit, Future Cut, Dylan, Loxy &amp; Ink, Total Science, D.Kay, Stakka &amp; Skynet and Keaton with Usual Suspects or Universal Project, Klute, Concord Dawn, and the label <a href="/wiki/Moving_Shadow" title="Moving Shadow">Moving Shadow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Neurofunk" title="Neurofunk">Neurofunk</a> or neuro is the progression from techstep<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> incorporating more elements from <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Funk" title="Funk">funk</a>. Prominent artists include <a href="/wiki/Ed_Rush" title="Ed Rush">Ed Rush</a>, <a href="/wiki/Optical_(musician)" title="Optical (musician)">Optical</a>, Matrix, Bad Company UK, Cause 4 Concern, TeeBee, Future Prophecies, Black Sun Empire, DLR, Calyx, Hive, Gridlok, Noisia, Phace &amp; Misanthrop, Silent Witness &amp; Break, State Of Mind, The Upbeats, Chase &amp; Status, <a href="/wiki/Jade_(drum_and_bass_producer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Jade (drum and bass producer)">Jade</a>, Mindscape, <a href="/wiki/Jon_Gooch" title="Jon Gooch">Spor</a>, Psidream, Catacomb, Rregula and The Clamps.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <li><a href="/wiki/Hardstep" title="Hardstep">Hardstep</a> is a harder style which uses gritty basslines and heavy yet simple electronic melodies.<sup id="cite_ref-globaldarkness_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-globaldarkness-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Notable artists include Dillinja (early work), DJ Krust, Mampi Swift, <a href="/wiki/Dieselboy" title="Dieselboy">Dieselboy</a>, Current Value, Tre Technics, MachineCode<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genres_influenced_by_drum_and_bass">Genres influenced by drum and bass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Genres influenced by drum and bass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Born around the same time as jungle, <a href="/wiki/Breakcore" title="Breakcore">breakcore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Digital_hardcore" title="Digital hardcore">digital hardcore</a> share many of the elements of drum and bass and to the uninitiated, tracks from the extreme end of drum and bass may sound identical to breakcore thanks to speed, complexity, impact and maximum sonic density combined with musical experimentation. German Drum and Bass DJ <a href="/wiki/The_Panacea" class="mw-redirect" title="The Panacea">The Panacea</a> is also one of the leading Digital Hardcore artists. <a href="/wiki/Raggacore" class="mw-redirect" title="Raggacore">Raggacore</a> resembles a faster version of the ragga influenced jungle music of the 1990s, similar to breakcore but with more friendly <a href="/wiki/Dancehall" title="Dancehall">dancehall</a> beats (dancehall itself being a very important influence on drum and bass).<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Darkcore" title="Darkcore">Darkcore</a>, a direct influence on drum and bass, was combined with influences of drum and bass itself leading to the creation of <a href="/wiki/Darkstep" title="Darkstep">darkstep</a>. There is considerable crossover from the extreme edges of drum and bass, breakcore, darkcore, digital hardcore and raggacore with fluid boundaries.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dubstep" title="Dubstep">Dubstep</a> originated from DnB slowed down to half speed and mixed with <a href="/wiki/Dub_reggae" class="mw-redirect" title="Dub reggae">dub reggae</a>, as experimental mixes on B-sides of garage records.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Intelligent_Dance_Music" class="mw-redirect" title="Intelligent Dance Music">Intelligent Dance Music</a> (IDM) is a form of <a href="/wiki/Art_music" title="Art music">art music</a> based on DnB and other electronic dance musics, exploring their boundaries using ideas from science, technology, contemporary classical music and progressive rock, often creating un-danceable, art gallery style music.  Pioneered by <a href="/wiki/Aphex_Twin" title="Aphex Twin">Aphex Twin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Squarepusher" title="Squarepusher">Squarepusher</a> and other artists on <a href="/wiki/Warp_Records" class="mw-redirect" title="Warp Records">Warp Records</a>.
</p><p>The genre has influenced many other genres like <a href="/wiki/Hip_hop" title="Hip hop">hip hop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Big_beat" title="Big beat">big beat</a>, <a href="/wiki/House_music" title="House music">house music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Trip_hop" title="Trip hop">trip hop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ambient_music" title="Ambient music">ambient music</a>, <a href="/wiki/Techno" title="Techno">techno</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a>, with artists such as <a href="/wiki/Bill_Laswell" title="Bill Laswell">Bill Laswell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Incubus_(band)" title="Incubus (band)">Incubus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pitchshifter_(band)" class="mw-redirect" title="Pitchshifter (band)">Pitchshifter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linkin_Park" title="Linkin Park">Linkin Park</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Roots" title="The Roots">The Roots</a>, <a href="/wiki/Talvin_Singh" title="Talvin Singh">Talvin Singh</a>, <a href="/wiki/MIDIval_Punditz" title="MIDIval Punditz">MIDIval Punditz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Missy_Elliott" title="Missy Elliott">Missy Elliott</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Freestylers" class="mw-redirect" title="The Freestylers">The Freestylers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bowery_Electric" title="Bowery Electric">Bowery Electric</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nine_Inch_Nails" title="Nine Inch Nails">Nine Inch Nails</a>, <a href="/wiki/David_Bowie" title="David Bowie">David Bowie</a> (the last two both using elements of Goldie's "Timeless")<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and others quoting drum and bass and using drum and bass techniques and elements. Recently created in the United States is a genre called <a href="/wiki/Ghettotech" title="Ghettotech">ghettotech</a> which contains synth and basslines similar to drum &amp; bass.<sup id="cite_ref-Fabio3_40-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Fabio3-40">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Playford_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Playford-65">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Popular_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Popular-66">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecosystem">Ecosystem</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Ecosystem">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Record_labels">Record labels</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Record labels">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Category:Drum_and_bass_record_labels" title="Category:Drum and bass record labels">Category:Drum and bass record labels</a></div>
<p>Drum and Bass is dominated by a small group of record labels. These are run mainly by DJ–producers, such as <a href="/wiki/London_Elektricity" title="London Elektricity">London Elektricity</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Hospital_Records" title="Hospital Records">Hospital Records</a>, <a href="/wiki/Andy_C" title="Andy C">Andy C</a> and Scott Bourne's <a href="/wiki/Ram_Records_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ram Records (UK)">RAM</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-68">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Goldie" title="Goldie">Goldie</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Metalheadz" title="Metalheadz">Metalheadz</a>, Kasra's <a href="/wiki/Critical_Music" title="Critical Music">Critical Music</a>, <a href="/wiki/DJ_Friction_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="DJ Friction (UK)">DJ Friction</a>'s Shogun Audio,<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/DJ_Fresh" title="DJ Fresh">DJ Fresh</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Breakbeat_Kaos" title="Breakbeat Kaos">Breakbeat Kaos</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>, <a href="/wiki/Ed_Rush" title="Ed Rush">Ed Rush</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Optical_(musician)" title="Optical (musician)">Optical</a>'s <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.discogs.com/label/275-Virus-Recordings">Virus Recordings</a>, <a href="/wiki/Futurebound" class="mw-redirect" title="Futurebound">Futurebound</a>'s Viper Recordings and <a href="/wiki/DJ_Hype" title="DJ Hype">DJ Hype</a>, Pascal, <a href="/wiki/NoCopyrightSounds" title="NoCopyrightSounds">NoCopyrightSounds</a> and formerly <a href="/wiki/DJ_Zinc" title="DJ Zinc">DJ Zinc</a>'s <a href="/w/index.php?title=True_Playaz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="True Playaz (page does not exist)">True Playaz</a> (now known as Real Playaz as of 2006).<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Prior to 2016, the major international music labels such as <a href="/wiki/Sony_Music" title="Sony Music">Sony Music</a> and <a href="/wiki/Universal_Music_Group" title="Universal Music Group">Universal</a> had shown very little interest in the drum and bass scene, with the exception of some notable signings, including <a href="/wiki/Pendulum_(drum_and_bass_band)" title="Pendulum (drum and bass band)">Pendulum</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/In_Silico_(Pendulum_album)" title="In Silico (Pendulum album)">In Silico</a></i> LP to Warner. Roni Size's label played a big, if not the biggest, part in the creation of drum and bass with their dark, baseline sounds. V Recordings also played a large part of the development of drum and bass.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> Roni Size, Krust and DJ Die produced some of the first tracks to be considered<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (March 2015)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> mainstream drum and bass tracks.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/BMG_Rights_Management" title="BMG Rights Management">BMG Rights Management</a> acquired <a href="/wiki/Ram_Records_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ram Records (UK)">Ram Records</a> in February 2016,<sup id="cite_ref-bmg.com_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bmg.com-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> making a strategic investment to help RAM Records (a London-based drum and bass record company co-owned by <a href="/wiki/Andy_C" title="Andy C">Andy C</a> and his business partner Scott Bourne). RAM Records has been pushing the boundaries of drum and bass further into the mainstream with artists such as <a href="/wiki/Chase_and_Status" class="mw-redirect" title="Chase and Status">Chase and Status</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sub_Focus" title="Sub Focus">Sub Focus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_68-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-68">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> Bringing back UK <a href="/wiki/Oldschool_jungle" class="mw-redirect" title="Oldschool jungle">jungle music</a> artists from <a href="/wiki/LTJ_Bukem" title="LTJ Bukem">LTJ Bukem</a>'s label <a href="/wiki/Good_Looking" class="mw-redirect" title="Good Looking">Good Looking</a> artists Bay B Kane, <a href="/wiki/Breakbeat_hardcore" title="Breakbeat hardcore">breakbeat hardcore</a> heavyweight Nebula II and original <a href="/wiki/Junglist" title="Junglist">junglist</a> Gappa G.
</p><p>Now defunct labels, include <a href="/wiki/Rob_Playford" class="mw-redirect" title="Rob Playford">Rob Playford</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Moving_Shadow" title="Moving Shadow">Moving Shadow</a>, running from 1990 until 2007, which played a pivotal role in the nineties drum and bass scene, releasing records by artists such as <a href="/wiki/Omni_Trio" class="mw-redirect" title="Omni Trio">Omni Trio</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Formats_and_distribution">Formats and distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Formats and distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Purchasing">Purchasing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Purchasing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Originally drum and bass was mostly sold in 12-inch <a href="/wiki/Vinyl_record" class="mw-redirect" title="Vinyl record">vinyl</a> single format. With the emergence of drum and bass into mainstream music markets, more albums, compilations and <a href="/wiki/DJ_mixes" class="mw-redirect" title="DJ mixes">DJ mixes</a> started to be sold on CDs. As digital music became more popular, websites focused on electronic music, such as <a href="/wiki/Beatport" title="Beatport">Beatport</a>, began to sell drum and bass in digital format.
</p>
<h4><span id="Distributors_.28wholesale.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Distributors_(wholesale)">Distributors (wholesale)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Distributors (wholesale)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The bulk of drum and bass vinyl records and CDs are distributed globally and regionally by a relatively small number of companies such as SRD (Southern Record Distributors), ST Holdings, &amp; Nu Urban Music Limited.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of 11 September 2012, Nu Urban ceased trading and RSM Tenon were instructed to assist in convening statutory meetings of members and creditors to appoint a liquidator. This left many labels short on sales, as Nu Urban were one of the main distributors for the vinyl market in the drum and bass scene.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_scenes">Regional scenes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Regional scenes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Anglosphere">Anglosphere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Anglosphere">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Despite its roots in the UK (with <a href="/wiki/Fabric_(club)" title="Fabric (club)">Fabric</a> being the epicenter of DnB), still treated as the "home" of drum and bass, the style has firmly established itself around the world.  There are strong scenes in other English-speaking countries including Australia, Canada, South Africa, the United States, and New Zealand.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Continental_Europe">Continental Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Continental Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>DnB is popular in continental <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Czech_Republic" title="Czech Republic">Czech Republic</a> currently hosts the largest drum and bass festival in the world, LET IT ROLL, with an annual attendance of approximately 30,000. The genre is also encountered in <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a>, and local producers in both countries such as A-Cray, Rido, Forbidden Society, L Plus, B-Complex, Changing Faces, Lixx, Dephzac, and Gabanna are becoming well known worldwide. There are several other drum and bass festivals being held each year in these countries, including Trident Festival, Exploration Festival, or Hoofbeats Open Air in the summer, or one-night events such as LET IT ROLL Winter, Imagination Festival, and LET IT ROLL Winter <a href="/wiki/Slovakia" title="Slovakia">Slovakia</a> in the colder months. During club season, promoters race between each other to organize better events, often resulting in up to ten parties being held during one weekend, with no more than a two-hour travel between them.
</p><p>Austria has a large emerging DnB scene with many artists such as <a href="/wiki/Camo_%26_Krooked" title="Camo &amp; Krooked">Camo &amp; Krooked</a>, Mefjus, Fourward, Body &amp; Soul, <a href="/wiki/Ill.Skillz" title="Ill.Skillz">Ill.Skillz</a> and mainly the Mainframe record label being all based in or around <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">Vienna</a>. Notable venues and events include "The Hive" and "Beat It" held at <a href="/wiki/Flex_(club)" title="Flex (club)">Flex</a> almost every Thursday and Saturday, "Vollkontakt" at Fluc, "Switch" at Flex, and the monthly Mainframe Recordings Label Night hosted at Arena by label head Disaszt.
</p><p>In Ireland, the biggest scene by far is in Dublin, with a night every Sunday by the Initial Kru mostly showcasing local DJ's but occasionally bringing over international guests such as Spirit and Seba. Record label Boey Audio also run events showcasing local, national and international acts, as well as promoters such as Sól Fúd, Ragga Jungle Dublin and Springfield Crew Massive. There are much smaller regional scenes in areas such as Limerick, Cork and Galway which usually have monthly nights.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="South_America">South America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: South America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Brazilian drum and bass is sometimes referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Sambass" title="Sambass">sambass</a>", with a specific <a href="/wiki/Samba" title="Samba">samba</a>-influenced style and sound made popular by artists like <a href="/wiki/DJ_Marky" title="DJ Marky">DJ Marky</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xerxes_de_Oliveira" title="Xerxes de Oliveira">XRS</a>.
</p><p>In Venezuela and Mexico, artists have created their own forms of drum and bass, combining it with experimental musical forms.
</p><p>In Colombia, there is a large underground scene. RE.set and Bogotá Project are two collectives that organize drum and bass events in the city, as well as a bi-annual event called Radikal Styles that brings together local talent and international big names.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Media_presence">Media presence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Media presence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Today, drum and bass is widely promoted using different methods such as <a href="/wiki/Video_sharing_service" class="mw-redirect" title="Video sharing service">video sharing services</a> like <a href="/wiki/YouTube" title="YouTube">YouTube</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dailymotion" title="Dailymotion">Dailymotion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">blogs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">radio</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a>, the latter being the most uncommon method. More recently, music networking websites such as <a href="/wiki/SoundCloud" title="SoundCloud">SoundCloud</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mixcloud" title="Mixcloud">Mixcloud</a> have become powerful tools for artist recognition, providing a vast platform that enables quick responses to new tracks. Record labels have adopted the use of <a href="/wiki/Podcast" title="Podcast">podcasts</a>. Prior to the rise of the internet, drum and bass was commonly broadcast over <a href="/wiki/Pirate_radio" title="Pirate radio">pirate radio</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio">Radio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Radio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The three highest-profile radio stations playing drum and bass shows are <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_1" title="BBC Radio 1">BBC Radio 1</a> with The Drum and Bass Show - formerly with <a href="/wiki/DJ_Friction_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="DJ Friction (UK)">Friction</a>, who was replaced with <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_LaVice" title="René LaVice">René LaVice</a> in 2017,<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> simulcast in the US and Canada on Sirius XM, and <a href="/wiki/DJ_Hype" title="DJ Hype">DJ Hype</a> on <a href="/wiki/Kiss_100_London" class="mw-redirect" title="Kiss 100 London">Kiss 100</a> in London. <a href="/wiki/Fabio_(DJ)" title="Fabio (DJ)">Fabio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grooverider" title="Grooverider">Grooverider</a> previously held a long-standing drum and bass show on Radio 1. Radio 1 also had a 'One in the Jungle' show.
</p><p>The BBC's "urban" station <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_1Xtra" title="BBC Radio 1Xtra">BBC Radio 1Xtra</a> used to feature the genre heavily, with DJ Bailey (show axed as of 29 August 2012) and Crissy Criss (show axed as of August 2014)<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> as its advocates. The network also organizes a week-long tour of the UK each year called <a href="/wiki/Xtra_Bass" title="Xtra Bass">Xtra Bass</a>. London pirate radio stations have been instrumental in the development of drum and bass, with stations such as <a href="/wiki/Kool_FM" title="Kool FM">Kool FM</a> (which continues to broadcast today having done so since 1991), Origin FM, <a href="/wiki/Don_FM" title="Don FM">Don FM</a> (the only drum and bass pirate to have gained a temporary legal license), <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.renegaderadio.co.uk">Renegade Radio 107.2FM</a>, Rude FM, Wax FM and Eruption among the most influential.
</p><p>As of 2014, despite higher profile stations such as 1Xtra scaling back their drum and bass specialist coverage, the genre has made its way into UK top 10 charts with drum and bass inspired tracks from artists such as Rudimental and Sigma. Earlier in August 2014, before Crissy Criss' show was axed, the BBC held a whole prime time evening event dedicated to showcasing drum and bass by allowing four major labels to participate.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As of November 2014, there have been 6 drum &amp; bass songs reaching the no.1 spot on the UK's top 40 chart, since the genre was first being played on the radio, around 1993. The first of these was in 2012. The fact that all 6 of these songs have reached number 1 in only two years shows the increase in popularity and commercialisation of the genre in recent years. The artists that produced these songs are <a href="/wiki/Sigma_(group)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sigma (group)">Sigma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rudimental" title="Rudimental">Rudimental</a> and <a href="/wiki/DJ_Fresh" title="DJ Fresh">DJ Fresh</a> (all have had two No.1 hits).
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Internet_radio">Internet radio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Internet radio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>Internet radio stations, acting in the same light as pirate stations, have also been an instrumental part in promoting drum and bass music; the majority of them funded by listener and artist donations.
</p><p>Drum and bass was supported by <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Sound" title="Ministry of Sound">Ministry of Sound</a> radio from the early 2000s until 2014 and later featuring Tuesday shows from labels such as <a href="/wiki/Metalheadz" title="Metalheadz">Metalheadz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fabio" title="Fabio">Fabio</a> &amp; <a href="/wiki/Grooverider" title="Grooverider">Grooverider</a>, DJ Marky, Viper Recordings, <a href="/wiki/Shogun_Audio" class="mw-redirect" title="Shogun Audio">Shogun Audio</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hospital_Records" title="Hospital Records">Hospital Records</a>. From September 2014, Ministry abruptly dropped all non-mainstream genres to focus on mainstream EDM, causing disappointment amongst the fans of the D&amp;B community.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> The leading Drum &amp; Bass radio Station is now Denver's <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://dnbradio.com">DnB Radio</a>, established in 2003 by M. Ramirez.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="North_American_radio">North American radio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: North American radio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Toronto" title="Toronto">Toronto</a>, The Prophecy on 89.5 <a href="/wiki/CIUT-FM" title="CIUT-FM">CIUT-FM</a> with Marcus Visionary, DJ Prime and Mr. Brown Is North America's longest running Jungle Radio show.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Album 88.5 (Atlanta) and <a href="/wiki/KNHC" title="KNHC">C89.5fm</a> (Seattle) have shows showcasing drum and bass.
</p><p>In New York, "DNB NYC RADIO" show with Host Dj Benzocaine on 90.3 <a href="/wiki/WHCR-FM" title="WHCR-FM">WHCR-FM</a> has a weekly 3 hour show which broadcasts in New York and New Jersey which plays only Drum and Bass every Thursday starting at 3 am. "DNB NYC RADIO" is the only weekly Drum and Bass show on the FM dial in Northeastern United States.
</p><p>Seattle also has a long-standing electronica show known as Expansions on 90.3 FM KEXP. The rotating DJs include Kid Hops, whose shows are made up mostly of drum and bass. In Columbus, Ohio WCBE 90.5 has a two-hour electronic only showcase, "All Mixed Up," Saturday nights at 10 pm. At the same time WUFM 88.7 plays its "Electronic Playground."
</p><p>Also, <a href="/wiki/Tulsa,_Oklahoma" title="Tulsa, Oklahoma">Tulsa, Oklahoma</a>'s rock station, 104.5 The Edge, has a two-hour show starting at 10 pm Saturday nights called Edge Essential Mix mixed by DJ Demko showcasing electronic and drum and bass style. While the aforementioned shows in Ohio rarely play drum and bass, the latter plays the genre with some frequency.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Tucson,_Arizona" title="Tucson, Arizona">Tucson, Arizona</a>, 91.3 FM <a href="/wiki/KXCI" title="KXCI">KXCI</a> has a two-hour electronic show known as "Digital Empire", Friday nights at 10 pm (MST). Resident DJ Trinidad showcases various styles of electronica, with the main focus being drum and bass, jungle &amp; <a href="/wiki/Dubstep" title="Dubstep">dubstep</a>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Augusta,_Georgia" title="Augusta, Georgia">Augusta, Georgia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Zarbizarre" class="mw-redirect" title="Zarbizarre">Zarbizarre</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Cereal_Killaz" title="Cereal Killaz">Cereal Killaz</a> hosts a show called FreQuency on <a href="/wiki/WHHD" title="WHHD">WHHD</a> on Friday nights from 11 pm until 1 am, showcasing drum and bass during the 2nd hour of the show.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Magazines">Magazines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Drum_and_bass&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Magazines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The best-known drum and bass publication was <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20101111182847/http://www.kmag.co.uk/"><i>Kmag</i></a> magazine (formerly called <i>Knowledge Magazine</i>) before it went completely online in August 2009. Although it's still live, after 20 years Kmag ceased updating their site at the end of 2014. Kmag has announced a book to celebrate their 25th anniversary to be published in December 2019. Kmag's publishing arm, Vision, published Brian Belle-Fortune's <i>All Crews Journeys Through Jungle Drum &amp; Bass Culture</i> in 2004.
</p><p>Other publications include the longest running drum and bass magazine worldwide, <i>ATM Magazine</i>, and Austrian-based <i><a href="/wiki/Resident_(magazine)" title="Resident (magazine)">Resident</a></i>. London-based <i>DJ</i> magazine has also been running a widely respected drum and bass reviews page since 1994, written by Alex Constantinides, which many followers refer to when seeking out new releases to investigate. In 2012 Alex stopped writing the reviews, and they are now contributed by Whisky Kicks.
</p>

<p>The earliest mainstream drum and bass releases include <a href="/wiki/Shy_FX" title="Shy FX">Shy FX</a> and UK Apache's single "Original Nuttah" from 1994 and <a href="/wiki/Goldie" title="Goldie">Goldie</a>'s album <i><a href="/wiki/Timeless_(Goldie_album)" title="Timeless (Goldie album)">Timeless</a></i> from 1995. Other early examples include the <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Music_Prize" class="mw-redirect" title="Mercury Music Prize">Mercury Music Prize</a> winning albums <i><a href="/wiki/New_Forms" title="New Forms">New Forms</a></i> (1997) from Reprazent and <i><a href="/wiki/OK_(Talvin_Singh_album)" title="OK (Talvin Singh album)">OK</a></i> (1998) from <a href="/wiki/Talvin_Singh" title="Talvin Singh">Talvin Singh</a>, <a href="/wiki/4hero" title="4hero">4hero</a>'s Mercury-nominated <i><a href="/wiki/Two_Pages" title="Two Pages">Two Pages</a></i> from 1998, and <a href="/wiki/Pendulum_(drum_and_bass_band)" title="Pendulum (drum and bass band)">Pendulum</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Hold_Your_Colour" title="Hold Your Colour">Hold Your Colour</a></i> in 2005 (the best selling drum and bass album of all time).<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Video games such as <i><a href="/wiki/Bomberman_Hero" title="Bomberman Hero">Bomberman Hero</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Hi-Rez_Studios" title="Hi-Rez Studios">Hi-Rez Studios</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Tribes:_Ascend" title="Tribes: Ascend">Tribes: Ascend</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Electronic_Arts" title="Electronic Arts">Electronic Arts</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Need_for_Speed:_Undercover" title="Need for Speed: Undercover">Need for Speed: Undercover</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Rockstar_Games" title="Rockstar Games">Rockstar Games</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Grand_Theft_Auto" title="Grand Theft Auto">Grand Theft Auto</a></i> series, and Sony's <a href="/wiki/Wipeout_(series)" title="Wipeout (series)">Wipeout</a> series from <a href="/wiki/Wipeout_Pure" title="Wipeout Pure">Pure</a> onward have contained drum and bass tracks. <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Studios" class="mw-redirect" title="Microsoft Studios">Microsoft Studios</a>' <i><a href="/wiki/Forza_(series)" title="Forza (series)">Forza Horizon 2, 3 and 4</a></i> feature a Hospital Records radio channel.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The genre has some popularity in soundtracks; for instance, "Ultrasonic Sound" was used in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Matrix" title="The Matrix">The Matrix</a>'s</i> soundtrack, and the <a href="/wiki/E-Z_Rollers" title="E-Z Rollers">E-Z Rollers</a>' song "Walk This Land" appeared in the film <i><a href="/wiki/Lock,_Stock_and_Two_Smoking_Barrels" title="Lock, Stock and Two Smoking Barrels">Lock, Stock and Two Smoking Barrels</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> Ganja Kru's "Super Sharp Shooter" is heard in the 2006 film <i><a href="/wiki/Johnny_Was" title="Johnny Was">Johnny Was</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Channel 4 show <i><a href="/wiki/Skins_(UK_TV_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="Skins (UK TV series)">Skins</a></i> uses the genre in some episodes, notably in the first series' third episode, "Jal", where <a href="/wiki/Shy_FX" title="Shy FX">Shy FX</a> and UK Apache's "Original Nuttah" was played in Fazer's club.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
