	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Thai name</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The modern <b>Thai name</b> is a 20th century innovation. For centuries, inhabitants of Siam, now Thailand, did not have <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surnames</a> (family names; last names). Thais identified themselves by referring to their parents <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given names</a> or the place they resided. The Siamese government started recording data on Siamese citizens during the reign of <a href="/wiki/King_Rama_V" class="mw-redirect" title="King Rama V">King Rama V</a> (1868–1910). The data recorded was minimal: birth date, date of death, household members. It was difficult to distinguish between the king's subjects as many shared the same name. In 1912, two years after the Western-educated <a href="/wiki/Rama_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="Rama VI">Rama VI</a> ascended to the throne, he declared that a birth, death, and marriage registration system would be instituted in Siam. Everyone had to bear a surname to identify theselves properly. In 1913, the first Surname Act was promulgated.<sup id="cite_ref-Shigetomi-2014_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shigetomi-2014-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Thai names follow the Western European pattern of <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a>. This differs from the family-name-first patterns of <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, and <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">East Asia</a>. Thai names (given and family) are often long, and there are a great many of them. The diversity of family names is due to the fact that Thai surnames are a recent introduction, and are required to be unique to a family.  
</p><p>Thai given names generally convey positive attributes, and a number of Thai people change their given names relatively frequently (and their family names less frequently, as it requires permission from the head of a family or, in the case of children, father and mother). This practice is virtually unknown in most countries outside of marriage and <a href="/wiki/Fortune-telling" title="Fortune-telling">fortune-telling</a> traditions. Besides standard reasons of separation and divorce, many name changes are done to get rid of bad luck (which, if caused by a ghost or spirit, would confuse the malignant entity, allowing the victim to get free from them). Additionally, while given names are used for official purposes and record-keeping, most Thais are also given <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nickname</a> at birth which they use in their daily life, including at school and in the workplace. Oftentimes, the nickname takes precedence over the real name and very few people besides close friends, family, and employers will know the real name.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thai_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Last names became legally required of Thai citizens in 1913 with the passing of the Surname Act 1913.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Shigetomi-2014_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shigetomi-2014-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Until then, most Thais used only a first or given name. Thai surnames are often long, particularly among <a href="/wiki/Thai_Chinese" title="Thai Chinese">Thais of Chinese descent</a>. For example, the family of former Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Thaksin_Shinawatra" title="Thaksin Shinawatra">Thaksin Shinawatra</a>, who is of Chinese descent, adopted the name Shinawatra ('does good routinely') in 1938. According to the current law, Person Name Act, <a href="/wiki/Thai_solar_calendar" title="Thai solar calendar">BE</a> 2505 (1962), to create a new Thai surname, it must be no longer than ten <a href="/wiki/Thai_alphabet" class="mw-redirect" title="Thai alphabet">Thai letters</a>, excluding vowel symbols and diacritics.<sup id="cite_ref-Nittaya-Royin-1_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nittaya-Royin-1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The same law also forbids the creation of a surname which is a duplicate of any existing surnames, however there are some duplicates dating to the time before computer databases were available to prevent this.<sup id="cite_ref-Nittaya-Royin-2_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nittaya-Royin-2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Some creations incorporated the name of their location (<a href="/wiki/Muban" title="Muban">muban</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tambon" title="Tambon">tambon</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Amphoe" title="Amphoe">amphoe</a>) into their surnames, similar to <a href="/wiki/Family_name_affixes" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name affixes">family name suffixes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Korat-surnamesuffixes1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Korat-surnamesuffixes1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Non_Sung-surnamesuffixes1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Non_Sung-surnamesuffixes1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Thaisong-surnamesuffix_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Thaisong-surnamesuffix-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As a measure of the diversity of Thai names, in a sample of 45,665 names, 81% of family names were unique and 35% of given names were unique. The people with shared family names are related, and the diversity of given names is conventional.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Official_surnames">Official surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thai_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Official surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Formal surnames were a 20th century innovation of <a href="/wiki/Royal_Military_College,_Sandhurst" title="Royal Military College, Sandhurst">Sandhurst-educated</a> King <a href="/wiki/Vajiravudh#Education" title="Vajiravudh">Vajiravudh</a> (Rama VI, r. 1910–1925).
</p><p>The "Surname Act of 2013" decreed that "married woman can bear her husband's surname or keep her maiden name" (Clause 6 of the act). A woman's right to choose her surname ended in 1941 with the passage of the "Personal Name Act 1941". The law forced women to use their husband's surname after marriage (Clause 13). The Personal Name Act of 1941 was revised in 1962. The 1962 law allowed a divorced woman to resume her maiden surname (Clause 13 of the "Personal Name Act 1962"). A widow could keep her husband's surname or could revert to her maiden surname (Clause 14). The Personal Name Act 2002 gave a married woman the right to use her maiden name or assume her spouse's surname. She has to choose one or the other when the marriage is registered. A couple also has the right to use a different surname.<sup id="cite_ref-Shigetomi-2014_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shigetomi-2014-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nicknames">Nicknames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thai_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nicknames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Informal names are awarded at birth and may continue in use to the extent one may have to check the formal registration to find a person's given formal name. Thais typically address one other by <a href="/wiki/Nicknames" class="mw-redirect" title="Nicknames">nicknames</a> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th">ชื่อเล่น</span>; <small><a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_General_System_of_Transcription" title="Royal Thai General System of Transcription">RTGS</a>:&#160;</small><span style="font-style:inherit" lang="th-Latn" title="Thai-language romanization"><i>chue len</i></span>). Bestowed by relatives or playmates in early childhood, these are commonly one syllable (or worn down from two to one). These may often be nonsense words or humorous and seldom relate to the registered name except in cases where it is a <a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">diminutive</a>, such as <i>Nok</i> for <i>Noknoi</i>, or 'bird' from 'little bird'. All Thais have such names; they are freely used in everyday life. Some may have additional nicknames bestowed by friends or colleagues, especially during school or adolescence. Nicknames may link with a notable physical feature or behavior. In everyday life, a Thai is introduced by nickname and others may not know the person's formal name. When so introduced, one usually continues to use the nickname.
</p><p>The evolution of Thai nicknames dates back to the <a href="/wiki/Sukhothai_Kingdom" title="Sukhothai Kingdom">Sukhothai era</a>, when names were used to mark the order of children. Nicknames such as <i>Ai</i>, <i>Yee</i>, and <i>Sam</i> designated children as 'one', 'two', and 'three'. Later, in the <a href="/wiki/Ayutthaya_Kingdom" title="Ayutthaya Kingdom">Ayutthaya</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rattanakosin_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Rattanakosin Kingdom">Rattanakosin</a> periods, children began to be named for physical attributes, such as <i>Daeng</i> ('red') or <i>Uan</i> ('fat') or for desirable items such as <i>Thong</i> ('gold').  Conversely, unflattering nicknames such as <i>Mah</i> ('dog'), <i>Moo</i> ('pig'), or <i>Gop</i> ('frog') were employed to keep malign spirits from coveting the child. <a href="/wiki/King_Mongkut" class="mw-redirect" title="King Mongkut">King Mongkut</a> (Rama IV) (1804-1868) stimulated interest in naming babies in accordance with astrological principles as outlined in the ancient scripture, <i>Namtaksapakorn</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-KU-2013_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KU-2013-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the time of Field Marshal <a href="/wiki/Plaek_Phibunsongkhram" title="Plaek Phibunsongkhram">Plaek Phibunsongkhram</a> (1897-1964), gender-based naming was introduced. Names ending in "-sak", "-chai", and "-yot" were for male babies, "-porn" or "-sri" for females. In the 21st century, assigning nicknames still relies heavily on astrological beliefs, but also in keeping up with current naming fashions. Observers have noted such modern nicknames as "Porsche", "Mercedes", "Benz", "Man U", "Big Mac", "Internet", and "Google", among others.<sup id="cite_ref-BP-20161102_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BP-20161102-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/King_Bhumibol_Adulyadej" class="mw-redirect" title="King Bhumibol Adulyadej">King Bhumibol Adulyadej</a>'s nickname, for example, was <i>Ong Lek</i> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th">องค์เล็ก</span>; <i>Ong</i> is a numerative noun for kings, princes, princesses, priests, images of Buddha, gods, angels, palaces, pagodas; <i>lek</i> means 'little (one)', a common name for younger siblings).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Former Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Thaksin_Shinawatra" title="Thaksin Shinawatra">Thaksin Shinawatra</a>'s nickname is (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th">แม้ว</span>; <small><a href="/wiki/Royal_Thai_General_System_of_Transcription" title="Royal Thai General System of Transcription">RTGS</a>:&#160;</small><span style="font-style:inherit" lang="th-Latn" title="Thai-language romanization"><i>maeo</i></span>), Thai for the <a href="/wiki/Miao_people" title="Miao people">Miao people</a>. By way of example preceding formal naming, <a href="/wiki/Plaek_Pibulsongkram#Early_years" class="mw-redirect" title="Plaek Pibulsongkram">Plaek Pibulsongkram</a>'s childhood name meant 'strange'. He later adopted as a surname what was originally an award for academic excellence and generally known in public life by the shortened form <i>Pibun</i>. Thailand's first female Prime Minister, <a href="/wiki/Yingluck_Shinawatra" title="Yingluck Shinawatra">Yingluck Shinawatra</a>, is nicknamed <i>Pu</i> 'crab' (<span lang="th" title="Thai language text">ปู</span>; <small>Thai pronunciation:</small>&#160;<span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Thai" title="Help:IPA/Thai">[pūː ]</a></span>).<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forms_of_address">Forms of address</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thai_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Forms of address">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the past, different words were used to address men and women. <i>Nai</i> (นาย) or <i>Ay</i> (อ้าย) were used before a man's given name. <i>Am daeng</i> (อําแดง) or <i>Ii</i> (อี) were used before a woman's given name. A man named Somchai was addressed as <i>Nai Somchai</i> or <i>Ay Somchai</i>. A woman whose name was Somsri was addressed as <i>Am daeng Somsri</i> or <i>Ii Somsri</i>. There was no law concerning this matter, it was purely a matter of custom.<sup id="cite_ref-Shigetomi-2014_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shigetomi-2014-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1917, Rama VI declared a new law, the "Form of Address for Woman Act, 1917". The act mandated a new form of address, <i>Nangsao</i> (นางสาว) ('Miss') for women who were unmarried (the wording used in the act was "woman who has no husband") and <i>Nang</i> (นาง) ('Missus' [Mrs.]) for women who were married ('married woman or woman who has husband'). Once a woman married, she had to use the address <i>Nang</i> before her given name for the rest of her life even if she divorced or widowed. This form of address applied to commoners, not to women in royal or noble families.<sup id="cite_ref-Shigetomi-2014_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shigetomi-2014-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>In 1921, the king proclaimed the form of address for girls. He specified that that "girl" meant a female under 15 years old; they were addressed as <i>nangsao</i>, as were females older than 15 years of age and unmarried (meaning 'married to a man').<sup id="cite_ref-Shigetomi-2014_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shigetomi-2014-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i>Form of Address for Women Act 2008</i> mandates that married or divorced women can choose to use either <i>Nang</i> or <i>Nangsao</i> before their given names. It gives a married woman the right to change all her documents (ID card, driver's license, bank account) to include the title <i>Nangsao</i> before her given name.<sup id="cite_ref-Shigetomi-2014_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Shigetomi-2014-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Today, in <a href="/wiki/Manners" class="mw-redirect" title="Manners">polite speech</a>, Thais address each other by a given name, preceded by the <a href="/wiki/Thai_royal_and_noble_titles#Khun_(courtesy_title)" title="Thai royal and noble titles">courtesy title <i>khun</i></a>, particularly with persons of higher status or public distinction.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_and_feudal_names">Royal and feudal names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thai_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Royal and feudal names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Rama_(Kings_of_Thailand)" title="Rama (Kings of Thailand)">Rama (Kings of Thailand)</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_Thai_Monarchs" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Thai Monarchs">List of Thai Monarchs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thai_royal_and_noble_titles" title="Thai royal and noble titles">Thai royal and noble titles</a></div>
<p>East Asian monarchs often adopted <a href="/wiki/Regnal_name#East_Asia" title="Regnal name">regnal names</a> upon ascending the throne, as was done in <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a> until the present day. In addition, subjects of a monarch may be awarded both a title and a name, such as in the case of Sing (or <a href="/wiki/Singh" title="Singh">Singh</a>) Sinhaseni (<span lang="th" title="Thai language text">สิงห์ สิงหเสนี</span>) who was awarded the title of <a href="/wiki/Thai_royal_and_noble_titles#Chao_Phraya" title="Thai royal and noble titles">Chao Phraya</a> and the name of <a href="/wiki/Bodindecha" title="Bodindecha">Bodindecha</a> (<a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">Thai</a>: <span lang="th">เจ้าพระยาบดินทรเดชา</span>.) 
</p><p>Kings <a href="/wiki/Rama_I" title="Rama I">Rama I</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rama_II_of_Siam" title="Rama II of Siam">Rama II</a> were awarded noble titles and names before they assumed regnal names, which were then changed by subsequent kings. As neither noble titles nor names are necessarily unique, it is customary to list the highest title and awarded named first, followed by former names and titles (and personal and family names in parentheses) as needed.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Noble_names">Noble names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thai_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Noble names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Descendants of the nobility, both hereditary and non-hereditary positions, generally take the <a href="/wiki/Thai_royal_and_noble_titles#Descendants_of_a_Sovereign" title="Thai royal and noble titles">noble name</a> of their ancestor for a surname. For instance, <a href="/wiki/Hugo_(musician)" title="Hugo (musician)">Hugo Chakrabongse</a> is a descendant of Prince <a href="/wiki/Chakrabongse_Bhuvanath" title="Chakrabongse Bhuvanath">Chakrabongse Bhuvanath</a>. Some (far removed) descendants of royalty add the preposition and <a href="/wiki/Nobility_particle" class="mw-redirect" title="Nobility particle">nobility particle</a> "na" (<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E0%B8%93" class="extiw" title="wikt:ณ">ณ</a>) to geographical names to create surnames, in the same way that members of German noble families use <i><a href="/wiki/Von" title="Von">von</a></i>. 
</p><p>Thus <a href="/wiki/Mongkol_Na_Songkhla" title="Mongkol Na Songkhla">Mongkol Na Songkhla</a>, a minister in the Surayud government, has a name indicating that he is a distant descendant of royalty or nobility in that geographical region (for instance, the surname "Na Chiangmai", belonging to descendants of the rulers of Chiang Mai, which was a vassal state of Siam). The name of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kasem_Sanitwong_Na_Ayutthaya&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kasem Sanitwong Na Ayutthaya (page does not exist)">Kasem Sanitwong Na Ayutthaya</a>, another minister, indicates that he is related to the royal family, as it is also tradition for far-removed descendants to add "<a href="/wiki/Thai_royal_and_noble_titles#Na_Ayutthaya" title="Thai royal and noble titles">Na Ayutthaya</a>" after their surnames. In this case, Sanitwong is the family name of Kasem. Sanitwong itself being the name of Prince <a href="/wiki/Wongsa_Dhiraj_Snid" title="Wongsa Dhiraj Snid">Wongsa Dhiraj Snid</a>, a son of <a href="/wiki/Rama_II_of_Siam" title="Rama II of Siam">King Buddha Loetla Nabhalai</a>, and the subsequent family name for his descendants.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indexing">Indexing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thai_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Indexing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Manual_of_Style" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago Manual of Style">Chicago Manual of Style</a></i>, Thai names may be <a href="/wiki/Subject_indexing" title="Subject indexing">indexed</a> depending upon the individual practice. Often they may be alphabetized under the given name with no comma or inversion, but they may also be alphabetized under the surname with a comma and with an inversion.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
