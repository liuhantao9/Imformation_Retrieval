<p>A <b>slave name</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Personal_name" title="Personal name">personal name</a> given by others to an <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">enslaved</a> person, or a name inherited from enslaved ancestors. The modern use of the term applies mostly to <a href="/wiki/African_Americans" title="African Americans">African Americans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Afro-Caribbean" title="Afro-Caribbean">West Indians</a> who are descended from enslaved Africans who retain their name given to their ancestors by the enslavers.
</p><p>Changing from a slave name to a name embodying an <a href="/wiki/Africanization" title="Africanization">African identity</a> became common after <a href="/wiki/Emancipation" title="Emancipation">emancipation</a> in the 1960s by those in the <a href="/wiki/African_diaspora_in_the_Americas" title="African diaspora in the Americas">African diaspora in the Americas</a> seeking a reconnection to their African cultural roots.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<p>In Rome slaves were given a single name by their owner. A slave who was <a href="/wiki/Manumission" title="Manumission">freed</a> might keep his or her slave name and adopt the former owner's name as a <a href="/wiki/Praenomen" title="Praenomen">praenomen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions" title="Roman naming conventions">nomen</a>. As an example, one historian says that "a man named Publius Larcius freed a male slave named Nicia, who was then called Publius Larcius Nicia."<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Historian <a href="/wiki/Harold_Whetstone_Johnston" title="Harold Whetstone Johnston">Harold Whetstone Johnston</a> writes of instances in which a slave's former owner chose to ignore custom and simply chose a name for the <a href="/wiki/Freedman" title="Freedman">freedman</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="African_Americans">African Americans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slave_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: African Americans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A number of African-Americans and <a href="/wiki/Afro-Caribbeans" class="mw-redirect" title="Afro-Caribbeans">Afro-Caribbeans</a> have changed their names out of the belief that the names they were given at birth were slave names. An individual's name change often coincides with a religious conversion (<a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ali" title="Muhammad Ali">Muhammad Ali</a> changed his name from Cassius Clay, <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_X" title="Malcolm X">Malcolm X</a> from Malcolm Little, and <a href="/wiki/Louis_Farrakhan" title="Louis Farrakhan">Louis Farrakhan</a> changed his from Louis Eugene Walcott, for example)<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  or involvement with the <a href="/wiki/Black_nationalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Black nationalist">black nationalist</a> movement (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Amiri_Baraka" title="Amiri Baraka">Amiri Baraka</a> and <a href="/wiki/Assata_Shakur" title="Assata Shakur">Assata Shakur</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some organizations encourage African-Americans to abandon their slave names. The <a href="/wiki/Nation_of_Islam" title="Nation of Islam">Nation of Islam</a> is perhaps the best-known of them. In his book, <i><a href="/wiki/Message_to_the_Blackman_in_America" title="Message to the Blackman in America">Message to the Blackman in America</a></i>, Nation of Islam leader <a href="/wiki/Elijah_Muhammad" title="Elijah Muhammad">Elijah Muhammad</a> writes often of slave names. Some of his comments include:
</p>
<ul><li>"You must remember that slave-names will keep you a slave in the eyes of the civilized world today. You have seen, and recently, that Africa and Asia will not honor you or give you any respect as long as you are called by the white man's name."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>"You are still called by your slave-masters' names. By rights, by international rights, you belong to the white man of America. He knows that. You have never gotten out of the shackles of slavery. You are still in them."<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Black_nationalism" title="Black nationalism">black nationalist</a> <a href="/wiki/US_Organization" title="US Organization">US Organization</a> also advocates for African-Americans to change their slave names.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_references">Other references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slave_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>White singer <a href="/wiki/Sin%C3%A9ad_O%27Connor" title="Sinéad O&#39;Connor">Sinéad O'Connor</a> stated in 2017 that she had changed her legal name to Magda Davitt, saying in an interview that she wished to be "free of the <a href="/wiki/Patriarchal" class="mw-redirect" title="Patriarchal">patriarchal</a> slave names.”<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> On her conversion to Islam in 2018, she adopted the <a href="/wiki/Muslim_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Muslim name">Muslim name</a> Shuhada' Sadaqat.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
