	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Dit name</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>The <b><i>dit</i> name</b> (in French <i>nom-dit</i> <span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">&#91;</a><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">n</a><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">ɔ̃</a><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">&#160;</a><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">d</a><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">i</a><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">&#93;</a></span>) was a common <a href="/wiki/French_Canada" class="mw-redirect" title="French Canada">French-Canadian</a> custom by which families often adopted an alternate surname. They were also  used in France, Italy and Scotland.  <sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGeyhBanachowskiBoyea200214_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGeyhBanachowskiBoyea200214-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  The practice lasted until the 19th century, and in a few cases into the 20th century.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGeyhBanachowskiBoyea200214_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGeyhBanachowskiBoyea200214-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  The <i>dit</i> name poses challenges for <a href="/wiki/Genealogy" title="Genealogy">genealogists</a> confronted with different surnames in different documents, particularly if they are not familiar with the custom.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGeyhBanachowskiBoyea200214_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGeyhBanachowskiBoyea200214-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Dit</i> and the feminine form <i>dite</i> translate as "called" and are the <a href="/wiki/Past_participle" class="mw-redirect" title="Past participle">past participle</a> of the French word <i>dire</i>, "to say".  A name such as <a href="/wiki/Adolphe_Guillet_dit_Tourangeau" title="Adolphe Guillet dit Tourangeau">Adolphe Guillet <i>dit</i> Tourangeau</a> can translate as "Adolphe Guillet, called Tourangeau", where both "Guillet" and "Tourangeau" are used as surnames, sometimes together and sometimes individually in different situations.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGeyhBanachowskiBoyea200214_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGeyhBanachowskiBoyea200214-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  The <i>dit</i> name carried the same legal weight as the original family name with regard to land transfers and the naming of children.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBonvillain199743_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBonvillain199743-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> <i>Dit</i> names developed for a variety of reasons, such as distinguishing one family from another nearby family with the same surname, or allowing an adopted child to retain both their birth and adopted family names. In some cases, both the original name and the <i>dit</i> name were retained. For example, Miville-Deschênes.
</p><p>A family's <i>dit</i> name often derived from a personal attribute (Leblond, Leblanc, Leroux), place of origin (St-Onge, Coderre, LeBreton), or profession (Chartier, Meunier, Vanier).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOuimette200236_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOuimette200236-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> For example, an immigrant to New France from <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> might receive the dit name <i>Parisien</i>, and a person who worked as a blacksmith might receive the dit name <i>Lefebvre</i>. Sometimes, the name could be ironic. A large person could be 'Tiny' or Petit. The custom originated in the military, where those with the same name adopted <i><a href="/wiki/Noms_de_guerre" class="mw-redirect" title="Noms de guerre">noms de guerre</a></i> to distinguish themselves.  Children often adopted the <i>dit</i> name, sometimes dropping the original family name.  Sometimes some of the children chose to take only the family name and others only the <i>dit</i> name.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBonvillain199742–43_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBonvillain199742–43-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
