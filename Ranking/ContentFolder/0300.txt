<p>
Before the 1800s, <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">Māori</a> children would be called by one <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> (simple or composite). These names were attributed to remarkable events around birth. Later in life a person might be given a new name relating to subsequent events.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="1800.E2.80.931900"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1800–1900">1800–1900</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=M%C4%81ori_naming_customs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: 1800–1900">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With the arrival of Europeans, surnames were introduced and soon after a Māori surname system was devised where a person would take their father's name as a surname, for example:
</p>
<dl><dd>Ariki – Maunga Ariki – Waiora Maunga – Te Awa Waiora – Waipapa Te Awa</dd></dl>
<p>Māori would also have translations of their names, for example:
</p>
<dl><dd>John Te Awa – Hone River – John River – Hone Waipapa Te Awa – John Waipapa Te Awa – Hone Waipapa – John Waipapa</dd></dl>
