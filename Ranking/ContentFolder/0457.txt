	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">de Lacy</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">

<p><b>de Lacy</b> (<b>Laci</b>, <b>Lacie</b>, <b>Lascy</b>, <b>Lacey</b>, <b>Lassey</b>) is the surname of an old <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a> family which originated from <a href="/wiki/Lassy,_Calvados" title="Lassy, Calvados">Lassy, Calvados</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The family took part in the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman conquest of England</a> and the later <a href="/wiki/Norman_invasion_of_Ireland" title="Norman invasion of Ireland">Norman invasion of Ireland</a>. The name is first recorded for Hugh de Lacy (1020–1085).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> His sons, Walter and Ilbert, left <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a> and travelled to England with <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="William I of England">William the Conqueror</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The awards of land by the Conqueror to the de Lacy sons led to two distinct branches of the family: the northern branch, centred on <a href="/wiki/Blackburnshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Blackburnshire">Blackburnshire</a> and west <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> was held by Ilbert's descendants; the southern branch of <a href="/wiki/Marcher_Lord" title="Marcher Lord">Marcher Lords</a>, centred on <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a>, was held by Walter's descendants.
</p><p>Until 1361, the northern branch of the family held the great <a href="/wiki/Lordship_of_Bowland" title="Lordship of Bowland">Lordship of Bowland</a> before it passed through marriage to the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Lancaster" title="Duchy of Lancaster">Duchy of Lancaster</a>. They were also Barons of <a href="/wiki/Pontefract" title="Pontefract">Pontefract</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Earls_of_Lincoln" class="mw-redirect" title="Earls of Lincoln">Earls of Lincoln</a>.
</p><p>The southern branch of the family became substantial landholders in the <a href="/wiki/Lordship_of_Ireland" title="Lordship of Ireland">Lordship of Ireland</a> and was linked to the Scottish royal family; <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_de_Burgh" title="Elizabeth de Burgh">Elizabeth de Burgh</a>, great granddaughter of <a href="/wiki/Walter_de_Lacy,_Lord_of_Meath" title="Walter de Lacy, Lord of Meath">Walter de Lacy</a>, married <a href="/wiki/Robert_the_Bruce" title="Robert the Bruce">Robert the Bruce</a>, King of Scotland.
</p>

<h2><span id="Lords_of_Pontefract.2C_Bowland_and_Clitheroe"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Lords_of_Pontefract,_Bowland_and_Clitheroe">Lords of Pontefract, Bowland and Clitheroe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_Lacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lords of Pontefract, Bowland and Clitheroe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pontefract_Castle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Pontefract_Castle.jpg/220px-Pontefract_Castle.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="127" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Pontefract_Castle.jpg/330px-Pontefract_Castle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Pontefract_Castle.jpg/440px-Pontefract_Castle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="944" data-file-height="544" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pontefract_Castle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Pontefract Castle</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bowland_%26_Lancs_labelled_with_UK.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Bowland_%26_Lancs_labelled_with_UK.png/220px-Bowland_%26_Lancs_labelled_with_UK.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="262" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Bowland_%26_Lancs_labelled_with_UK.png/330px-Bowland_%26_Lancs_labelled_with_UK.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Bowland_%26_Lancs_labelled_with_UK.png/440px-Bowland_%26_Lancs_labelled_with_UK.png 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="714" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bowland_%26_Lancs_labelled_with_UK.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bowland, northern England</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Clitheroe_Castle.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Clitheroe_Castle.JPG/150px-Clitheroe_Castle.JPG" decoding="async" width="150" height="200" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Clitheroe_Castle.JPG/225px-Clitheroe_Castle.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Clitheroe_Castle.JPG/300px-Clitheroe_Castle.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1536" data-file-height="2048" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Clitheroe_Castle.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Clitheroe Castle, founded by Robert de Lacy</div></div></div>
<p>Brothers Ilbert and Walter jointly held the Norman lands that were held of the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Bayeux" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Bayeux">Bishop of Bayeux</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DNB_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DNB-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> They participated in the Norman conquest of England. While there is evidence that Ilbert fought at William's side at Hastings,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> there is no record of Walter fighting at Hastings. Ilbert was a major participant in the <a href="/wiki/Harrying_of_the_North" title="Harrying of the North">Harrying of the North</a> (1069–70) which effectively ended the quasi-independence of the region through large-scale destruction that resulted in the relative "pacification" of the local population and the replacement of local <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo</a>-<a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Danish</a> lords with <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a>. In return, he received vast grants of land in West Yorkshire, where he built <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Castle" title="Pontefract Castle">Pontefract Castle</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Honour_(feudal_barony)" class="mw-redirect" title="Honour (feudal barony)">Honour</a> of Pontefract, which included the manor of <a href="/wiki/Stanbury" title="Stanbury">Stanbury</a>, was maintained by Ilbert's <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_de_Lacys%27_family_tree" title="Pontefract de Lacys&#39; family tree">direct male descendants</a> for the next three generations until 1192. It continued in the female line until 1348.
</p><p>Some of the English holdings lost by <a href="/wiki/Roger_the_Poitevin" title="Roger the Poitevin">Roger the Poitevin</a> due to his rebellion were awarded to Robert de Lacy, the son of Ilbert de Lacy.<sup id="cite_ref-VCHL6Blackburnshire_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VCHL6Blackburnshire-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-VCHL6_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VCHL6-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In 1102, King <a href="/wiki/Henry_I_of_England" title="Henry I of England">Henry I of England</a> granted the fee of the ancient <a href="/wiki/Wapentake" class="mw-redirect" title="Wapentake">wapentake</a> of <a href="/wiki/Blackburnshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Blackburnshire">Blackburnshire</a> and further holdings in <a href="/wiki/Hornby,_Lancashire" class="mw-redirect" title="Hornby, Lancashire">Hornby</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Vill" title="Vill">vills</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chipping,_Lancashire" title="Chipping, Lancashire">Chipping</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aighton" class="mw-redirect" title="Aighton">Aighton</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dutton,_Lancashire" title="Dutton, Lancashire">Dutton</a> in <a href="/wiki/Amounderness" class="mw-redirect" title="Amounderness">Amounderness</a> to de Lacy while confirming his possession of the <a href="/wiki/Lordship_of_Bowland" title="Lordship of Bowland">Lordship of Bowland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-VCHL1Grant_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VCHL1Grant-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> These lands formed the basis of what became known as the <a href="/wiki/Honour_of_Clitheroe" title="Honour of Clitheroe">Honour of Clitheroe</a>.
</p><p>By marriage, <a href="/wiki/John_de_Lacy,_2nd_Earl_of_Lincoln" title="John de Lacy, 2nd Earl of Lincoln">John de Lacy</a> gained more titles, including that of the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Lincoln" title="Earl of Lincoln">Earldom of Lincoln</a> in 1221.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_family_members">Notable family members</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_Lacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Notable family members">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_de_Lacys%27_family_tree" title="Pontefract de Lacys&#39; family tree">Pontefract de Lacys' family tree</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:81px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Arm_Jean_de_Lacy.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/15/Arm_Jean_de_Lacy.png" decoding="async" width="79" height="99" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="79" data-file-height="99" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Arm_Jean_de_Lacy.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Arms of John de Lacy,<br />2nd Earl of Lincoln <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.earlyblazon.com">[2]</a></div></div></div>
<p><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hugh_de_Lacy_(c.1020-1085)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hugh de Lacy (c.1020-1085) (page does not exist)">Hugh de Lacy</a> (c.1020, lord of Lassy (Normandy) – 27 March 1085, Hereford)
</p>
<ul><li>Ilbert de Lacy (1045, Lassy – 1093, Pontefract), 1st Baron of Pontefract, son of Hugh de Lacy,<sup id="cite_ref-DP452_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DP452-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> who received a large <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fief</a> in <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire" title="Nottinghamshire">Nottinghamshire</a> and built <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Castle" title="Pontefract Castle">Pontefract Castle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-VCHL1BaronClitheroe_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VCHL1BaronClitheroe-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
<ul><li>Robert de Lacy, (probably died before 1130)<sup id="cite_ref-VCHL1BaronClitheroe_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VCHL1BaronClitheroe-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> 2nd Baron of Pontefract, 2nd Lord of Bowland, the son of Ilbert. He founded <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Priory" title="Pontefract Priory">Pontefract Priory</a> about 1090 and built <a href="/wiki/Clitheroe_castle" class="mw-redirect" title="Clitheroe castle">Clitheroe castle</a>.
<ul><li>Ilbert de Lacy, (died c.1141) 3rd Baron of Pontefract, 3rd Lord of Bowland, the eldest son of Robert de Lacy. He was captured with <a href="/wiki/Stephen,_King_of_England" title="Stephen, King of England">King Stephen</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Lincoln_(1141)" title="Battle of Lincoln (1141)">Battle of Lincoln (1141)</a>, possibly dying in captivity.<sup id="cite_ref-VCHL1BaronClitheroe_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VCHL1BaronClitheroe-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Henry de Lacy (died 1177<sup id="cite_ref-VCHL1BaronClitheroe_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VCHL1BaronClitheroe-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>), 4th Baron of Pontefract, 4th Lord of Bowland, the second son of Robert de Lacy. He built <a href="/wiki/Kirkstall_Abbey" title="Kirkstall Abbey">Kirkstall Abbey</a>.
<ul><li>Robert de Lacy (died 1193), 5th Baron of Pontefract, 5th Lord of Bowland, son of Henry. Although he married he had no children. Buried at Kirkstall.<sup id="cite_ref-VCHL1BaronClitheroe_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-VCHL1BaronClitheroe-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li></ul></li>
<li>Albreda de Lacy, daughter of Robert de Lacy the 2nd Baron, who married Robert de Lissours.
<ul><li>Albreda de Lissours, the daughter of Albreda de Lacy who married <a href="/wiki/Richard_fitz_Eustace" title="Richard fitz Eustace">Richard fitz Eustace</a>.

<p>Walter de Lacy, the son of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hugh_de_Lacy,_Lord_of_Lassy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hugh de Lacy, Lord of Lassy (page does not exist)">Hugh de Lacy, Lord of Lassy</a>, was granted the lordship of <a href="/wiki/Weobley_Castle,_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Weobley Castle, England">Weobley</a> in <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a> after the Conquest.<sup id="cite_ref-DP452_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DP452-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He is already attested in the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Marches" title="Welsh Marches">Welsh Marches</a> by 1069.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> By the time of Walter's death, he held blocks of land in Herefordshire (including <a href="/wiki/Holme_Lacy" title="Holme Lacy">Holme Lacy</a>) along the border with Wales with another group of lands centered on <a href="/wiki/Ludlow" title="Ludlow">Ludlow</a> in <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a>. These groupings allowed Walter to help defend the <a href="/wiki/England%E2%80%93Wales_border" title="England–Wales border">England–Wales border</a> against Welsh raids. He also had smaller holdings in <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Worcestershire" title="Worcestershire">Worcestershire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a>. Walter was second in the region only to <a href="/wiki/William_FitzOsbern,_1st_Earl_of_Hereford" title="William FitzOsbern, 1st Earl of Hereford">William FitzOsbern, 1st Earl of Hereford</a> and his son, <a href="/wiki/Roger_de_Breteuil,_2nd_Earl_of_Hereford" title="Roger de Breteuil, 2nd Earl of Hereford">Roger de Breteuil</a> although he was not subordinate to them. After the  latter's rebellion against the king in 1075 [which Walter de Lacy helped to ensure failed] Walter became the leading baron in the region.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lordship_of_Meath">Lordship of Meath</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_Lacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lordship of Meath">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:81px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Buckler_Hugh_de_Lacy.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b9/Buckler_Hugh_de_Lacy.png" decoding="async" width="79" height="99" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="79" data-file-height="99" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Buckler_Hugh_de_Lacy.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Arms of Hugh de Lacy, Lord of Meath <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.earlyblazon.com">[3]</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Trim_Castle_6.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Trim_Castle_6.jpg/250px-Trim_Castle_6.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="188" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Trim_Castle_6.jpg/375px-Trim_Castle_6.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Trim_Castle_6.jpg/500px-Trim_Castle_6.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Trim_Castle_6.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Trim_Castle" title="Trim Castle">Trim Castle</a>, County Meath, Ireland</div></div></div>
<p>In addition to his substantial land holdings in <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">England</a> as 4th Baron de Lacy, <a href="/wiki/Hugh_de_Lacy,_Lord_of_Meath" title="Hugh de Lacy, Lord of Meath">Hugh de Lacy</a> was also a substantial land holder in Ireland. Following his participation in the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Invasion_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Invasion of Ireland">Norman Invasion of Ireland</a>, he was granted the lands of a <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_Ireland" title="Gaelic Ireland">Gaelic</a> <a href="/wiki/Kingdoms_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdoms of Ireland">medieval kingdom</a> by the Anglo-Norman King <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry II of England</a> in 1172 by the service of fifty knights. The <a href="/wiki/Lordship_of_Meath" title="Lordship of Meath">Lordship of Meath</a> was an extensive seigniorial <a href="/wiki/Liberty_(division)" title="Liberty (division)">liberty</a> in medieval <a href="/wiki/History_of_Ireland_(1169%E2%80%931536)" title="History of Ireland (1169–1536)">Ireland</a> with almost royal authority. The Lordship was roughly co-extensive with the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Meath" title="Kingdom of Meath">Kingdom of Meath</a>. At its greatest extent, it included all of the modern <a href="/wiki/Counties_of_Ireland" title="Counties of Ireland">counties</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fingal" title="Fingal">Fingal</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Meath" title="County Meath">Meath</a> (which takes its name from the kingdom), <a href="/wiki/County_Westmeath" title="County Westmeath">Westmeath</a> as well as parts of counties <a href="/wiki/County_Cavan" title="County Cavan">Cavan</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Kildare" title="County Kildare">Kildare</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Longford" title="County Longford">Longford</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Louth" title="County Louth">Louth</a> and <a href="/wiki/County_Offaly" title="County Offaly">Offaly</a>. The Lordship's <i><a href="/wiki/Caput" title="Caput">caput</a></i> was <a href="/wiki/Trim_Castle" title="Trim Castle">Trim Castle</a>. With an area of 30,000 m², it is the largest <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castle</a> in Ireland. The design of the central three-story <a href="/wiki/Keep" title="Keep">keep</a> (also known as a donjon or great tower) is unique for a Norman keep being of <a href="/wiki/Cruciform" title="Cruciform">cruciform</a> shape, with twenty corners.
</p><p>These lords were reliant on their own aggression for laying claim to their lands and for securing them. Castles, by virtue of their defensive and offensive capabilities as well as their symbolic status, were indispensable for dominating the area of the lordship.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> Known as a great builder of castles, by c. 1200, de Lacy had settlements all over the lordship, either in his own hands or the hands of his barons. With his son <a href="/wiki/Walter_de_Lacy,_Lord_of_Meath" title="Walter de Lacy, Lord of Meath">Walter</a> (1180 &#8211; 1240) he built <a href="/wiki/Trim_Castle" title="Trim Castle">Trim Castle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kilkea_Castle" title="Kilkea Castle">Kilkea Castle</a>. Some time after 1196, Walter granted "the whole land of Rathtowth" to his younger brother, <a href="/wiki/Hugh_de_Lacy,_1st_Earl_of_Ulster" title="Hugh de Lacy, 1st Earl of Ulster">Hugh</a>. This sub-division, named the <a href="/wiki/Ratoath_(barony)" title="Ratoath (barony)">Barony of Ratoath</a>, was perhaps the first instance of the use of the term <a href="/wiki/Barony_(Ireland)" title="Barony (Ireland)">barony</a> in Ireland for a division of a county. By letters patent from <a href="/wiki/John,_King_of_England" title="John, King of England">John, King of England</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Prescriptive_barony" class="mw-redirect" title="Prescriptive barony">prescriptive barony</a> was granted to Walter de Lacy and his heirs in perpetuity in 1208.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_possible_notable_members_of_the_family">Other possible notable members of the family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=De_Lacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other possible notable members of the family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several later families claim descent from the Hiberno-Norman Lacys.  
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Lacy_baronets" title="Lacy baronets">Lacy baronets</a> of Ampton Hall are said to be related to Peter.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is claimed that the Limerick Lacy family that gave rise to several continental generals descends from <a href="/wiki/Hugh_de_Lacy,_Lord_of_Meath" title="Hugh de Lacy, Lord of Meath">Hugh de Lacy, Lord of Meath</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> but this claim has been challenged by Synnott<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>, who suggested that the Limerick families may be Lees, a name of frequent occurrence in Limerick records from the 12th to the 15th century.  Members of this family include:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Peter_Lacy" title="Peter Lacy">Peter Count de Lacy</a></li>
