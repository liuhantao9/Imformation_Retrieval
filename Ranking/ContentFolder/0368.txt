<p>A <b>name day</b> is a tradition in some countries in Europe, Latin America, and <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic">Roman Catholic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox</a> countries in general. It consists of celebrating a day of the year that is associated with one's <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>. The celebration is similar to a <a href="/wiki/Birthday" title="Birthday">birthday</a>.
</p><p>The custom originated with the Christian <a href="/wiki/Calendar_of_saints" title="Calendar of saints">calendar of saints</a>: believers named after a <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saint</a> would celebrate that saint's feast day, or in the Eastern Orthodox tradition, the day of a saint's death. Name days have greater resonance in the Catholic and Orthodox parts of Europe; Protestant churches practice less <a href="/wiki/Veneration" title="Veneration">veneration</a> of saints. In many countries, however, name-day celebrations no longer have connection to explicitly <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> traditions.<sup id="cite_ref-rus_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rus-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-gr_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gr-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The celebration of name days has been a tradition in Catholic and Eastern Orthodox countries since the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, and has also continued in some measure in countries, such as the Scandinavian countries, whose <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> <a href="/wiki/Established_church" class="mw-redirect" title="Established church">established church</a> retains certain Catholic traditions. The name days originate in the list of holidays celebrated in commemoration of saints and <a href="/wiki/Martyr" title="Martyr">martyrs</a> of the church. For example, the name <i>Karl</i> or <i>Carl</i> is celebrated in Sweden on January 28, the anniversary of the death of <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">Charlemagne</a> (Charles <i>magnus</i>, i.e., "the great"). The church promoted celebration of name days (or rather saints' <a href="/wiki/Feast_day" class="mw-redirect" title="Feast day">feast days</a>) over <a href="/wiki/Birthday" title="Birthday">birthdays</a>, as the latter was seen as a pagan tradition.
</p><p>Where name days occur an official list is held containing the current assignations of names to <a href="/wiki/Days" class="mw-redirect" title="Days">days</a>. There are different lists for <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">Finnish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">Swedish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sami_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Sami language">Sami</a>, and other countries that celebrate namedays, though some names are celebrated on the same day in many countries. From the 18th century and onwards the list of name days has been modified in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_various_countries">In various countries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In various countries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bulgaria">Bulgaria</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Bulgaria">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_Bulgaria" title="Name days in Bulgaria">Name days in Bulgaria</a></div>
<p>Name days (именни дни) in Bulgaria are almost always associated with <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">Bulgarian</a> <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox" class="mw-redirect" title="Eastern Orthodox">Eastern Orthodox</a> celebrations. Some names can be celebrated on more than one day and some have even started following foreign traditions<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> (like Valentina being celebrated on the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic</a> St. Valentine's Day).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
St. George's day (Гергьовден, celebrated on May 6) and St. John's day (Ивановден, celebrated on January 7) are two of the most popular name days in Bulgaria.
</p><p>Another example of a name day connected with Christianity is <i>Tsvetnitsa</i> (Цветница, <a href="/wiki/Palm_Sunday" title="Palm Sunday">Palm Sunday</a>). On this day people with names derived from flowers, trees, herbs, etc., celebrate. Name days are frequently connected with some year or season features like <i>Dimitrovden</i> (Dimitar's day, Oct. 26) being the beginning of winter and <i>Gergyovden</i> (George's day, May 6) being the end of it according to traditional folklore.
</p><p>Name days in Bulgaria are important and widely celebrated. Children celebrate their name days by bringing sweets and chocolates to school. By an ancient Bulgarian tradition, everybody is welcome on name days; there is no need to invite guests. Presents are given.
</p><p>Common well-wishes include "May you hear your name from grandchildren and great-grandchildren!" (Да чуеш името си от внуци и правнуци!), "May you hear your name only with good!" (Да ти се чува името само за добро!) and "May your name be healthy and well!" (Да ти е живо и здраво името!).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Croatia">Croatia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Croatia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_Croatia" title="Name days in Croatia">Name days in Croatia</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">Croatia</a>, name day (<a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">Croatian</a>: imendan) is a day corresponding to a date in the Catholic calendar when the respective <a href="/wiki/Calendar_of_saints" title="Calendar of saints">saint's day</a> is celebrated. Even though celebration of the name day is less usual than celebrating birthday, the name day is more often congratulated by a broader number of acquaintances. This is due to the fact that the date of birth is seldom known and the person's name is known to many.
</p><p>The names that are celebrated on the certain saint's day are all the names that correspond to the respective name and all the derivative names. For example, if there are different versions of the same name in different languages (e.g. <a href="/wiki/John_(given_name)" title="John (given name)">John</a>), i.e. different versions in <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a> or other language groups, all the respective names are celebrated.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Czech_Republic">Czech Republic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Czech Republic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_the_Czech_Republic" title="Name days in the Czech Republic">Name days in the Czech Republic</a></div>
<p>In the Czech Republic, each day of the year corresponds to a personal name (or several names). People celebrate their name day (<i>svátek</i> or more formally <i>jmeniny</i>) on the date corresponding to their own given name.
</p><p>Name days are commonly of less importance than birthdays to Czech people. However, name day celebrations can be, and often are, held together with friends or co-workers of the same name and in this way it can grow in size and importance.
</p><p>In the past, by law, parents were not allowed to choose just any name for a child.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> This has changed, although it is still common to choose the name from the name day "calendar" and any highly unusual name has to be approved by a special office. The original list was the Roman Catholic calendar of saints, but changes have been made to reflect the present-day usage of names.
</p><p>Name days corresponding to some of the most frequent names in the Czech Republic gained slightly more importance than the others. For example, the dates associated with names Josef (Joseph) and Karel (Charles) are commonly known even by people with different names. However, the popularity of these names has decreased in the last years (6836 Josefs were born in 1947, but only 638 in 2014<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Denmark">Denmark</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Denmark">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Danes have their own calendar for name days (Danish 'navnedag'), see 
<a href="https://da.wikipedia.org/wiki/Danske_navnedage" class="extiw" title="da:Danske navnedage">da.wikipedia, Danske navnedage (Danish Namedays arranged in a calendar)</a>. However, the custom of celebrating one's name day is practically unknown in Denmark, and few Danes know when their name day is.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finland">Finland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Finland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.nordicnames.de/wiki/Finnish_Name_Days">Finnish Name Days</a>
</p><p>Finns celebrate their name days (Finnish <i>nimipäivä</i>, Swedish <i>namnsdag</i>) according to their <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> on the date given by the calendar published by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Helsinki" title="University of Helsinki">University of Helsinki</a> Almanac Office (<i>Almanakkatoimisto</i>). Every day except <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Day" title="New Year&#39;s Day">New Year's Day</a>, <a href="/wiki/Christmas_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Christmas Day">Christmas Day</a> and <a href="/wiki/29_February" class="mw-redirect" title="29 February">29 February</a> is a name day. For each day there are names in both Finnish and Swedish; the names are frequently, but not always, <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">cognates</a>.
</p><p>Women are slightly underrepresented in the calendar: approximately 45 percent of name days celebrate only women while some 49 per cent are name days of men. The rest are those of names that may be given to either sex, such as Rauni (July 15), or have both a man's and a woman's name, such as Oliver and Olivia (29 May).
</p><p>Many traditional beliefs attach to various name days, especially involving the weather and the appropriate times to perform seasonal agricultural tasks such as planting some particular crop. For example, there is a saying that "Jaakko (James) casts a cold rock into the water", meaning that on Jaakko's day, July 25, the waters start getting colder, which is not far from true on average<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. The seven days from the 18th to the 24th of July, being all women's name days, are known as the women's week. It is popularly believed to be an especially rainy week, and this is to some extent supported by statistics, as late July and early August are the rainiest time of the year in Finland<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>.
</p><p>The Almanac Office reviews the lists at intervals of 5&#8211;10 years, adding new names as they gain popularity and striking others that have faded into disuse. The university owns the copyright to the lists of names and their corresponding dates.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Finnish_Orthodox_Church" title="Finnish Orthodox Church">Finnish Orthodox Church</a> has its own calendar of name days, corresponding to the feasts of Orthodox <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saints</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>See <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_France" class="mw-redirect" title="Name days in France"><i>List of name days in France</i></a>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> name days (in <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: fête du prénom) have long been very important in everyday culture and it was traditional to give a small gift to a friend or family member on their name day.
</p><p>Some days of the year are commonly referred to by their saint's day: "la [<i>sc.</i> fête de] <a href="/wiki/Pope_Sylvester_I" title="Pope Sylvester I">Saint Sylvestre</a>" is <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Eve" title="New Year&#39;s Eve">New Year's Eve</a>; "la <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">Saint Jean</a>" is <a href="/wiki/Midsummer" title="Midsummer">Midsummer</a>; and so on.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Germany">Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> name days (in <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: Namenstag) used to be widely popular in traditionally Catholic southern and western regions, where historically they were more important than birthdays. Since the 1950s, the tradition has mostly disappeared even in Catholic families.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greece_and_Cyprus">Greece and Cyprus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Greece and Cyprus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_Greece" title="Name days in Greece">Name days in Greece</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">Cyprus</a>, a name day (<a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>: ονομαστική εορτή, <i> onomastikē eortē</i>, or γιορτή, <i>yiortí</i>, "feast") is celebrated in a similar way to a <a href="/wiki/Birthday" title="Birthday">birthday</a>, except for expected differences (e.g. no <a href="/wiki/Birthday_cake" title="Birthday cake">birthday cake</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-gr_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gr-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> It has been a strong Greek tradition since antiquity for newborn children to be <a href="/wiki/Greek_name" title="Greek name">named</a> after one of their grandparents. This results in a continuation of names in the family line.
</p><p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Church" title="Greek Orthodox Church">Greek Orthodox Church</a>, every day of the year is dedicated to the memory of at least one (usually more than one) <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saint</a> or <a href="/wiki/Martyr" title="Martyr">martyr</a>. If someone is named after a saint, then there is a big celebration on his or her name day. In Greece and Cyprus, many names derive from <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">pagan</a> Greek antiquity, and there may not be a Christian saint of the same name. In such a case, the person is said "not to have" a name day, or they may choose to celebrate on <a href="/wiki/All_Saints%27_Day" title="All Saints&#39; Day">All Saints' Day</a>. The vast majority of name days are on the <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_Greece" title="Name days in Greece">same date every year</a>; the few exceptions are names directly or indirectly associated with <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">Easter</a>, and so are floating. The tradition facilitates social interaction, as all <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek language</a> calendars include detailed name day lists. Some name days coincide with major Christian feasts. For example people whose names are <i>Chrēstos</i> or <i>Christine</i> have their name day on <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a>, people named after <a href="/wiki/St._Basil" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Basil">St. Basil</a> have their name day on New Year's Day, <i>Anastásios</i> and <i>Anastasía</i> on <a href="/wiki/Easter_Sunday" class="mw-redirect" title="Easter Sunday">Easter Sunday</a>, and <i>María</i> and <i>Mários</i> either on <a href="/wiki/Dormition_of_the_Mother_of_God" title="Dormition of the Mother of God">the Dormition</a> or <a href="/wiki/Presentation_of_Mary" title="Presentation of Mary">the Presentation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mary,_mother_of_Jesus" title="Mary, mother of Jesus">Mary, mother of Jesus</a>.
</p><p>The traditional format of a name day celebration is an open house: once a family or person has chosen to celebrate with invited guests (at home, at a restaurant, a bar or a club) if at all (e.g. following a recent bereavement), all well-wishers may be welcomed. Children celebrate their birthdays and name days equally festively, but as the person grows up the emphasis may shift decisively. Entertainment provided by the celebrating host may include a meal, drinks, desserts, music and <a href="/wiki/Partying" class="mw-redirect" title="Partying">partying</a>, rather than the guests fussing over the person celebrating. Gifts are expected from the guests. Optionally, an adult relative or a <a href="/wiki/Godparent" title="Godparent">godparent</a> might give <a href="/wiki/Allowance_(money)#Allowances_for_children" title="Allowance (money)">pocket money</a> to a celebrant child or teenager instead of a gift. In cases where birthdays and name days are close to each other, the celebrations are best merged. It is also common to shift a name day celebration to a more convenient day, e.g. the following Friday or a weekend. Name days can be celebrated up to 40 days after the nominal date.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hungary">Hungary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Hungary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_Hungary" title="Name days in Hungary">Name days in Hungary</a></div>
<p>Name days (in <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a>: <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://mainevnap.com/">névnap</a>) in <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">Hungary</a> are very popular, although not quite as much as a person's actual birthdate. A woman is typically given <a href="/wiki/Flowers" class="mw-redirect" title="Flowers">flowers</a> on her name day by acquaintances, including in the workplace, and the price of flowers often rises around the dates of popular names because of demand. A bottle of alcohol is a common gift for men on their name day. Children frequently bring sweets to school to celebrate their name days. Name days are more often celebrated than birthdays in workplaces, presumably because it is simpler to know the date since most calendars contain a list of name days. You can also find the name day on daily newspapers by the date and on Hungarian websites. Some highly popular names have several name days; in that case, the person chooses on which day he or she wishes to celebrate. The list of the name days is, as usual in name day celebrating cultures, based on the traditional Catholic saints' feasts, but the link of the secular name days calendar to the Catholic calendar is not maintained any more. For example, even religious Catholic people named Gergely (Gregory) after Pope Gregory the Great still celebrate their name days on March 12, although the Church moved the feast of that saint to September 3 in 1969.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Italy">Italy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Italy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, one's name day is referred to as his or her "<a href="/wiki/Onomastico" class="mw-redirect" title="Onomastico">Onomastico</a><span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">&#160;&#91;<a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onomastico" class="extiw" title="it:Onomastico">it</a>&#93;</span>" ( Greek: όνομα, spelled: "<i>onoma" /</i> name in <a href="/wiki/Name_day#Greece" title="Name day">Greek</a>). People often receive small gifts on their <i>Onomastico</i>. Name days are determined according to the <i><a href="/wiki/Sanctorale" title="Sanctorale">Sanctorale</a></i>, a cycle found in the <a href="/wiki/General_Roman_Calendar" title="General Roman Calendar">General Roman Calendar</a> giving almost each day a few saints, so different names may celebrated on the same day.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Traditionally, parents fix the name day of their child at <a href="/wiki/Infant_baptism" title="Infant baptism">christening</a>, according to the favorite saint in case of different ones (on different days) with the same name, and the child will carry it all along its life. In the case of multiple given names, the child will celebrate only one, usually the first.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Latvia">Latvia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Latvia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a>, name days (in <a href="/wiki/Latvian_language" title="Latvian language">Latvian</a> "vārda dienas") are settled on certain <a href="/wiki/Calendar_date" title="Calendar date">dates</a>; each day (except for February 29 in a <a href="/wiki/Leap_year" title="Leap year">leap year</a>) is a name day.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Usually <a href="/wiki/Latvians" title="Latvians">Latvian</a> calendars list up to four names each day—around 1,000 names a year. Recently an extended calendar with around 5,000 names was published, and there are also a few extended calendars found on the <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">Internet</a> listing names even on February 29. February 29 is a popular date to celebrate name days of people who do not have a name day; another such date is May 22. People who do not have name days in ordinary calendars can enjoy many variations when to celebrate—on February 29 or May 22 and, if they have their name in an extended calendar or in the church calendar, on the date listed there (so in a leap year such a person can choose from 2 to 4 dates when to celebrate). The Latvian name days calendar is updated at one or two-year intervals; anyone can suggest a name for the calendar, usually by sending an application to the State Language Centre ("Valsts valodas centrs").
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Celebration_(party)" class="mw-redirect" title="Celebration (party)">Celebrations</a> are very much like birthday celebrations. It is popular to celebrate name days in one's workplace—usually the one that has a name day prepares snacks for well-wishers, and during the day colleagues arrive one after another with flowers, sweets and small presents to greet him. Sometimes, especially in smaller companies, a certain time is set for the main celebrations. It is normal to come to a name day celebration without an invitation. At school one is expected to arrive with candy for classmates and teachers. Celebrating name days at home is similar to celebrating a birthday, although it may vary depending on the period of time between one's birthday and name day; usually one will eat cake with household members and receive presents.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_Macedonia">North Macedonia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: North Macedonia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Name-days (<a href="/wiki/Macedonian_language" title="Macedonian language">Macedonian</a>: <span lang="mk">Именден, Imenden</span>) in <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_North_Macedonia" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of North Macedonia">North Macedonia</a> are celebrated throughout the history of this country. It has some similarities with the other Balkan countries but there are some name-days unique for the country. The name-days are scheduled according to the <a href="/w/index.php?title=North_Macedonian_Orthodox_Church&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="North Macedonian Orthodox Church (page does not exist)">Christian Orthodox church</a> following the <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a>. Each month there are several name-days which are celebrated by the people with the same name. Some of the name-days that are more significant to the history and culture are non-working days for the whole country. At these days everyone's invited or would like to say „Кој дојде – Добредојде“ ("Whoever comes—is welcomed") in <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_language" title="Macedonian language">Macedonian</a>. There is no need for bringing presents but if you like you can bring some (usually wine or something symbolic). You salute the celebrant by saying "Let your name last forever" („Нека ти е вечно името“ / "Neka ti e vecno imeto") or "For years to come" („За многу години“ / "Za mnogu godini"). Among the most celebrated name-days in North Macedonia are St. Stefan (January 9), Epiphany (January 19), St. John (January 20), Blagovec (April 7), St. George (May 6), Ss. Cyril and Methodius (May 24), St. Kostadin and Elena (June 3), St. Peter (July 12), St. Paul (July 12), and St. Dimitar (November 8).Sv Nikola is the most celebrated (December 19)
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Poland">Poland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Poland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_Poland" title="Name days in Poland">Name days in Poland</a></div>
<p>Traditionally, name day celebrations (<a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>: <i lang="pl">imieniny</i>) have enjoyed a celebratory emphasis greater than that of birthday celebrations in Poland. However, birthday celebrations are increasingly popular and important, particularly among the younger generations. <i lang="pl" title="Polish language text">Imieniny</i> involve the gathering and socializing of friends and family at the celebrant's home, as well as the giving of gifts and flowers at home and elsewhere, such as at the workplace. Local calendars often contain the names celebrated on a given day.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Romania">Romania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Romania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Name days (onomastica) in Romania are associated with the Orthodox saints celebrations. The celebrations are made very much in the same way as in Greece (see above). Name days are almost as important as birthdays, and those who have the name of that particular saint get celebrated on that day. Some of the more important Name days are: January 1: Sf. Vasile (St. Basil), January 7: Sf. Ioan (St. John), April 23: Sf. Gheorghe (St. George), May 21: Sf. Constantin şi Elena (St. Constantine and Helen), June 29: Sf. Petru şi Pavel (St. Peter and Paul), July 20: Sf. Ilie (St. Elias), August 15 and September 8: Sf. Maria (St. Mary), September 9: Sf. Ana (St. Ann), October 14: Sf. Parascheva (St. Paraskeva), October 26: Sf. Dumitru (St. Demetrios), November 8: Sf. Mihail şi Gavril (St. Michael and Gabriel), November 25: Sf. Ecaterina (St. Catherine), November 30: Sf. Andrei (St. Andrew), December 6: Sf. Nicolae (St. Nicholas), December 27: Sf. Stefan (St. Stephen).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A special mention for persons (especially women) who have no saint name or with flower name, who celebrate their name day on Palm Sunday ("Floriile" in Romanian, which roughly translates as the Flowers Day). This Name day is unfixed and gets celebrated each year on the last Sunday before Orthodox Easter.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Russian_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian people">Russians</a> celebrate name days (<i>именины (imeniny)</i> in <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a>) separately from birthdays. Some calendars note name days, but usually one must address a special name-day calendar. Celebrations range from the gifting of cards and flowers to full-blown celebrations similar to birthday parties.<sup id="cite_ref-rus_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rus-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Such a celebration begins with attendance at the divine services marking that day (in the Russian tradition, the <a href="/wiki/All-Night_Vigil" class="mw-redirect" title="All-Night Vigil">All-Night Vigil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Divine_Liturgy" title="Divine Liturgy">Divine Liturgy</a>), and usually with a festive party thereafter. Before the <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">October Revolution</a> of 1917, Russians regarded name days as important as, or more important than, the celebration of birthdays, based on the rationale that one's baptism is the event by which people become "born anew" in Christ.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/House_of_Romanov" title="House of Romanov">Russian Imperial family</a> followed a tradition of giving name-day gifts, such as a diamond or a pearl.
</p><p>References to name days in Russian literature and theatre include of the entire first act of <a href="/wiki/Anton_Chekhov" title="Anton Chekhov">Anton Chekhov</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Three_Sisters_(play)" title="Three Sisters (play)">Three Sisters</a></i>, where Irina celebrates her name day, <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pushkin" title="Alexander Pushkin">Alexander Pushkin</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Eugene_Onegin" title="Eugene Onegin">Eugene Onegin</a></i> with the celebration of Tatiana's name day, and <a href="/wiki/Leo_Tolstoy" title="Leo Tolstoy">Leo Tolstoy</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/War_and_Peace" title="War and Peace">War and Peace</a></i>, Book I, where both the mother and the youngest daughter of the Rostov family (referred to as Natalya and Natasha, respectively) celebrate their name day.
</p><p>Note: although the name day (именины/"imeniny") celebration is not as popular as birthday celebration, the Russian word for a person having a birthday (день рождения/"den rozhdeniya") is still именинник/"imeninnik" (literally: a person whose name day is being celebrated).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slovakia">Slovakia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Slovakia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_Slovakia" title="Name days in Slovakia">Name days in Slovakia</a></div>
<p>In Slovakia name days (<a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">Slovak</a>: meniny) are widely celebrated. Name days are more often celebrated than birthdays in workplaces, presumably because it is simpler to know the date since most calendars contain a list of name days. You can also find the name day in the header of daily newspapers. Celebrations in elementary schools are different from those within the family as the celebrant gives candies to his or her classmates. Within the family, birthday-like celebrations are often held with cakes, presents and flowers. Flowers are sometimes sold out for popular name days. In the past, by law, parents were not allowed to choose just any name for their child. This has changed, although it is still common to choose the name from the nameday list in the calendar. The original list was the <a href="/wiki/General_Roman_Calendar" title="General Roman Calendar">Roman Catholic calendar of saints</a>, but changes have been made to reflect the present-day usage of names.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slovenia">Slovenia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Slovenia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Name days (called <i>god</i>, n., m., sing.) were widely celebrated, and preferred over <a href="/wiki/Birthday" title="Birthday">birthday</a> celebrations, until after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and the advent of <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">Communism</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Rural" class="mw-redirect" title="Rural">rural</a> areas as well as among certain strata of town people the custom of celebrating name days lasted longer. Nowadays, while the tradition has not been completely obliterated, name days are celebrated mostly among older people.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spain_and_Hispanic_America">Spain and Hispanic America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Spain and Hispanic America">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Until recently,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="Statement needs to be more specific about the content to which it refers. (March 2009)">specify</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> name days in Spain and several parts of Hispanic America (called <i>onomásticos</i> or <i>día de mi/su santo</i>) were widely celebrated. <i>Onomásticos</i> are not limited to saints but also include the celebration days of the different representations of the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Mary" class="mw-redirect" title="Virgin Mary">Virgin Mary</a>. For example, the name day of a woman named Carmen would be July 16, day of <a href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of_Mount_Carmel" title="Our Lady of Mount Carmel">Our Lady of Mount Carmel</a>. Currently, <i>onomásticos</i> are still remembered in more traditional families (though not usually in Argentina or Uruguay) but are not generally celebrated with festive parties and presents as they were in the past. To celebrate name days, practicing Catholics typically attend mass and have some intimate family celebration. In Spain, children often take sweets or cake to school to share with their classmates.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sweden">Sweden</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Sweden">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Fanny_Brate-Namnsdag.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Fanny_Brate-Namnsdag.jpg/220px-Fanny_Brate-Namnsdag.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="176" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Fanny_Brate-Namnsdag.jpg/330px-Fanny_Brate-Namnsdag.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Fanny_Brate-Namnsdag.jpg/440px-Fanny_Brate-Namnsdag.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3511" data-file-height="2811" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Fanny_Brate-Namnsdag.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>A Day of Celebration</i>. A painting by Swedish artist <a href="/wiki/Fanny_Brate" title="Fanny Brate">Fanny Brate</a> depicting preparations for a name day celebration. Oil on canvas, 1902.</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Swedish_name_day_list_of_2001" title="Swedish name day list of 2001">Swedish name day list of 2001</a>&#160;and <a href="/wiki/Name_days_in_Sweden" title="Name days in Sweden">Name days in Sweden</a> (the older 1901 version)</div>
<p>From the 18th century onwards, names used by the royal family were introduced to the Swedish list of name days, followed by other common names. In 1901 a comprehensive modernisation was made to make the list up to date with current names. The monopoly on almanacs, held by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Swedish_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences">Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences</a>, expired in 1972 and so did the official name day list. Competing name day lists began to emerge but the official list was still in general use until 1986 when consensus of a new list with three names on each day was reached. This list was revised in 1993 and reduced to two names on each day. However, widespread dissatisfaction with the list prompted the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Academy" title="Swedish Academy">Swedish Academy</a> to compile a new two-name list which was finally accepted and brought into use in 2001. Although it does not have the official status of the 1901 or older lists, it is now universally used in Sweden.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ukraine">Ukraine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_day&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Ukraine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Name days in Ukraine (<a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">Ukrainian</a>: <span lang="uk">день ангела</span>) are usually associated with <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_Orthodox" class="mw-redirect" title="Ukrainian Orthodox">Ukrainian Orthodox</a> and <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Churches</a> celebrations of a day when a saint was born.
</p>
