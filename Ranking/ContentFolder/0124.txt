<p><b>António Lobo Antunes</b>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_James_of_the_Sword" class="mw-redirect" title="Order of Saint James of the Sword">GCSE</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medical_Doctor" class="mw-redirect" title="Medical Doctor">MD</a> (<small>Portuguese pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Portuguese" title="Help:IPA/Portuguese">[ɐ̃ˈtɔniu ˈloβu ɐ̃ˈtunɨʃ]</a></span>; born 1 September 1942) is a <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_people" title="Portuguese people">Portuguese</a> novelist and <a href="/wiki/Medical_doctor" class="mw-redirect" title="Medical doctor">medical doctor</a>. He has been named as a contender for the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">Nobel Prize in Literature</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ant%C3%B3nio_Lobo_Antunes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>António Lobo Antunes was born in <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a> as the eldest of six sons of João Alfredo de Figueiredo Lobo Antunes (born 1915), prominent <a href="/wiki/Neurologist" class="mw-redirect" title="Neurologist">Neurologist</a> and professor, close collaborator of <a href="/wiki/Egas_Moniz" class="mw-redirect" title="Egas Moniz">Egas Moniz</a>, Nobel prize of physiology, and wife Maria Margarida Machado de Almeida Lima (born 1917). He is the brother of <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_Lobo_Antunes" title="João Lobo Antunes">João Lobo Antunes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Lobo_Antunes" title="Manuel Lobo Antunes">Manuel Lobo Antunes</a>. He is Jewish.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the age of seven he decided to be a writer, but when he was 16, his father sent him to the medical school of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Lisbon" title="University of Lisbon">University of Lisbon</a>. He graduated as a <a href="/wiki/Medical_doctor" class="mw-redirect" title="Medical doctor">medical doctor</a>, later specializing in <a href="/wiki/Psychiatry" title="Psychiatry">psychiatry</a>. During this time he never stopped writing.
</p><p>By the end of his education, Lobo Antunes had to serve with the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Army" title="Portuguese Army">Portuguese Army</a> to take part in the <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Colonial_War" title="Portuguese Colonial War">Portuguese Colonial War</a> (1961–1974). In a military hospital in <a href="/wiki/Angola_(Portugal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Angola (Portugal)">Angola</a> he became interested in the subjects of death and "the other."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lobo Antunes came back from Africa in 1973. The <a href="/wiki/Angolan_War_of_Independence" title="Angolan War of Independence">Angolan War of Independence</a> was the subject of many of his novels. He worked many months in <a href="/wiki/Federal_Republic_of_Germany" class="mw-redirect" title="Federal Republic of Germany">Germany</a> and Belgium.
</p><p>In 1979, Lobo Antunes published his first novel, <i>Memória de Elefante</i> (Elephant's Memory), in which he told the story of his separation. Due to the success of his first novel, Lobo Antunes decided to devote his evenings to writing. He has been practicing psychiatry as well, mainly at the outpatients' unit at the Hospital Miguel Bombarda of Lisbon.
</p><p>His style is considered to be very dense, heavily influenced by <a href="/wiki/William_Faulkner" title="William Faulkner">William Faulkner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Louis-Ferdinand_C%C3%A9line" title="Louis-Ferdinand Céline">Louis-Ferdinand Céline</a>, and his books also tend to be on the longer side.
</p><p>He was granted the <a href="/wiki/Grand_Cross" title="Grand Cross">Grand Cross</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_James_of_the_Sword" class="mw-redirect" title="Order of Saint James of the Sword">Order of Saint James of the Sword</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ant%C3%B3nio_Lobo_Antunes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>He married his first wife Maria José Xavier da Fonseca e Costa (1946–1999), the second of three daughters of José Hermano da Costa and wife Clara da Conceição de Barros Xavier da Fonseca e Costa, by whom he has two daughters: Maria José Lobo Antunes in 1971 and Joana Lobo Antunes in 1973. They were divorced.
</p><p>His second wife (whom he also divorced) was Maria João Espírito Santo Bustorff Silva (born Lisbon, 13 August 1950), daughter of António Sérgio Carneiro Bustorff Silva and wife Ana Maria da Anunciação de Fátima de Morais Sarmento Cohen do Espírito Santo Silva, by whom she has one daughter: Maria Isabel Bustorff Lobo Antunes (born 1983).
</p><p>He was married for the third time in 2010 to Cristina Ferreira de Almeida, daughter of João Carlos Ferreira de Almeida (Lisbon, 1941 – 2008) and wife Natércia Ribeiro da Silva.
</p>
