	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Pseudonym</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Name that a person or group assumes for a particular purpose, which differs from their original or true name (orthonym)</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Aliases" redirects here. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Alias_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Alias (disambiguation)">Alias (disambiguation)</a>.</div> 
<p>A <b>pseudonym</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/sj/: &#39;s&#39; in &#39;consume&#39;">sj</span><span title="/uː/: &#39;oo&#39; in &#39;goose&#39;">uː</span><span title="&#39;d&#39; in &#39;dye&#39;">d</span><span title="/ən/: &#39;on&#39; in &#39;button&#39;">ən</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span></span>/</a></span></span>) or <b>alias</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="/eɪ/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;face&#39;">eɪ</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/i/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;happy&#39;">i</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Name" title="Name">name</a> that a person or group assumes for a particular purpose, which can differ from their first or true name (<a href="/wiki/Orthonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Orthonym">orthonym</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The term is not used when a new name entirely replaces an individual's own.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scope">Scope</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudonym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Scope">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pseudonyms include <a href="/wiki/Stage_name" title="Stage name">stage names</a> and <a href="/wiki/User_(computing)" title="User (computing)">user names</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ring_name" title="Ring name">ring names</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pen_name" title="Pen name">pen names</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nicknames</a>, aliases, <a href="/wiki/Superhero" title="Superhero">superhero</a> or <a href="/wiki/Villain" title="Villain">villain</a> identities and code names, gamer identifications, and <a href="/wiki/Regnal_name" title="Regnal name">regnal names</a> of <a href="/wiki/Emperors" class="mw-redirect" title="Emperors">emperors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">popes</a>, and other <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarchs</a>. Historically, they have sometimes taken the form of <a href="/wiki/Anagram" title="Anagram">anagrams</a>, Graecisms, and <a href="/wiki/Latinisation_(literature)" class="mw-redirect" title="Latinisation (literature)">Latinisations</a>, although there are many other methods of choosing a pseudonym.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pseudonyms should not be confused with new names that replace old ones and become the individual's full-time name. Pseudonyms are "part-time" names, used only in certain contexts – to provide a more clear-cut separation between one's private and professional lives, to showcase or enhance a particular persona, or to hide an individual's real identity, as with writers' <a href="/wiki/Pen_name" title="Pen name">pen names</a>, <a href="/wiki/Graffiti" title="Graffiti">graffiti</a> artists' tags, <a href="/wiki/Resistance_movement" title="Resistance movement">resistance fighters'</a> or <a href="/wiki/Terrorist" class="mw-redirect" title="Terrorist">terrorists'</a> <i>noms de guerre</i>, and computer <a href="/wiki/Hacker_(computer_security)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hacker (computer security)">hackers</a>' <a href="/wiki/User_(computing)" title="User (computing)">handles</a>. <a href="/wiki/Actor" title="Actor">Actors</a>, voice-over artists, musicians, and other performers sometimes use <a href="/wiki/Stage_name" title="Stage name">stage names</a>, for example, to better channel a relevant energy, gain a greater sense of security and comfort via privacy, more easily avoid troublesome fans/"stalkers", or to mask their ethnic backgrounds.
</p><p>In some cases, pseudonyms are adopted because they are part of a cultural or organisational tradition: for example <a href="/wiki/Religious_name" title="Religious name">devotional names</a> used by members of some <a href="/wiki/Religious_institute" title="Religious institute">religious institutes</a>, and "cadre names" used by <a href="/wiki/Communist_party" title="Communist party">Communist party</a> leaders such as <a href="/wiki/Leon_Trotsky" title="Leon Trotsky">Trotsky</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Lenin</a>.
</p><p>A pseudonym may also be used for personal reasons: for example, an individual may prefer to be called or known by a name that differs from their given or <a href="/wiki/Legal_name" title="Legal name">legal name</a>, but is not ready to take the numerous steps to get their name <a href="/wiki/Name_change" title="Name change">legally changed</a>; or an individual may simply feel that the context and content of an exchange offer no reason, legal or otherwise, to provide their given or legal name.
</p><p>A <i>collective name</i> or <i>collective pseudonym</i> is one shared by two or more persons, for example the co-authors of a work, such as <a href="/wiki/Carolyn_Keene" title="Carolyn Keene">Carolyn Keene</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ellery_Queen" title="Ellery Queen">Ellery Queen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Bourbaki" title="Nicolas Bourbaki">Nicolas Bourbaki</a>. or <a href="/wiki/James_S._A._Corey" title="James S. A. Corey">James S. A. Corey</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudonym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <span lang="el" title="Greek language text">ψευδώνυμον</span> (<i>pseudṓnymon</i>), literally "false name", from <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">ψεῦδος</span> (<i>pseûdos</i>), "lie, falsehood"<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">ὄνομα</span> (<i>ónoma</i>), "name".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Distinction_from_allonyms.2C_ghost_writers_and_pseudepigrapha"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Distinction_from_allonyms,_ghost_writers_and_pseudepigrapha">Distinction from allonyms, ghost writers and pseudepigrapha</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudonym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distinction from allonyms, ghost writers and pseudepigrapha">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A pseudonym is distinct from an <i>allonym</i>, which is the (real) name of another person, assumed by the author of a work of art.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> This may occur when someone is <a href="/wiki/Ghostwriting" class="mw-redirect" title="Ghostwriting">ghostwriting</a> a book or play, or in <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parody</a>, or when using a "front" name, such as by <a href="/wiki/Screenwriter" title="Screenwriter">screenwriters</a> <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_blacklist" title="Hollywood blacklist">blacklisted</a> in <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a> in the 1950s and 1960s. See also <a href="/wiki/Pseudepigraph" class="mw-redirect" title="Pseudepigraph">pseudepigraph</a>, for <i>falsely</i> attributed authorship.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudonym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_name_change">Legal name change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudonym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legal name change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sometimes people change their name in such a manner that the new name becomes permanent and is used by all who know the person. This is not an alias or pseudonym, but in fact a new name. In many countries, including <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">common law</a> countries, a name change can be ratified by a court and become a person's new legal name.
</p><p>For example, in the 1960s, black civil rights campaigner <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_X" title="Malcolm X">Malcolm Little</a> changed his surname to "X", to represent his unknown African ancestral name that had been lost when his ancestors were brought to North America as slaves. He then changed his name again to Malik El-Shabazz when he converted to Islam.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Likewise some Jews adopted Hebrew family names upon immigrating to <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>, dropping surnames that had been in their families for generations. The politician <a href="/wiki/David_Ben-Gurion" title="David Ben-Gurion">David Ben-Gurion</a>, for example, was born David Grün in Poland. He adopted his Hebrew name in 1910, when he published his first article in a Zionist journal in <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Many <a href="/wiki/Transgender" title="Transgender">transgender</a> people also choose to adopt a new name, typically around the time of their <a href="/wiki/Transitioning_(transgender)" title="Transitioning (transgender)">social transitioning</a>, to resemble their desired gender better than their birth name.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Concealing_identity">Concealing identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudonym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Concealing identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Business">Business</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudonym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Business">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Businesspersons of ethnic minorities in some parts of the world are sometimes advised by an employer to use a pseudonym that is common or acceptable in that area when conducting business, to overcome <a href="/wiki/Racial_bias" class="mw-redirect" title="Racial bias">racial</a> or religious <a href="/wiki/Bias#Prejudices" title="Bias">bias</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Criminal_activity">Criminal activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudonym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Criminal activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Criminals may use aliases, <a href="/wiki/Fictitious_business_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Fictitious business names">fictitious business names</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dummy_corporations" class="mw-redirect" title="Dummy corporations">dummy corporations</a> (<a href="/wiki/Corporate_shells" class="mw-redirect" title="Corporate shells">corporate shells</a>) to hide their identity, or to impersonate other persons or entities in order to commit fraud. Aliases and fictitious business names used for dummy corporations may become so complex that, in the words of the <i><a href="/wiki/Washington_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a></i>, "getting to the truth requires a walk down a bizarre labyrinth" and multiple government agencies may become involved to uncover the truth.<sup id="cite_ref-RTR_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RTR-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudonym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Pen_name" title="Pen name">pen name</a>, or <i>"nom de plume"</i> (French for "pen name"), is a pseudonym (sometimes a particular form of the real name) adopted by an <a href="/wiki/Author" title="Author">author</a> (or on the author's behalf by their publishers).
</p><p>Some female authors used male pen names, in particular in the 19th century, when writing was a male-dominated profession. The <a href="/wiki/Bront%C3%AB_family" title="Brontë family">Brontë family</a> used pen names for their early work, so as not to reveal their gender (see below) and so that local residents would not know that the books related to people of the neighbourhood. The Brontës used their neighbours as inspiration for characters in many of their books. <a href="/wiki/Anne_Bront%C3%AB" title="Anne Brontë">Anne Brontë</a> published <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tenant_of_Wildfell_Hall" title="The Tenant of Wildfell Hall">The Tenant of Wildfell Hall</a></i> under the name Acton Bell. <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Bront%C3%AB" title="Charlotte Brontë">Charlotte Brontë</a> published <i><a href="/wiki/Shirley_(novel)" title="Shirley (novel)">Shirley</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Jane_Eyre" title="Jane Eyre">Jane Eyre</a></i> under the name Currer Bell. <a href="/wiki/Emily_Bront%C3%AB" title="Emily Brontë">Emily Brontë</a> published <i>Wuthering Heights</i> as Ellis Bell. Another well-known example is Mary Ann Evans, who wrote as <a href="/wiki/George_Eliot" title="George Eliot">George Eliot</a>.
</p><p>Amandine Aurore Lucile Dupin, a 19th-century French writer, used the pseudonym <a href="/wiki/George_Sand" title="George Sand">George Sand</a>. Pseudonyms may also be used due to cultural or organization or political prejudices. There is speculation, for example, that <a href="/wiki/Amelia_Bassano_Lanier" class="mw-redirect" title="Amelia Bassano Lanier">Amelia Bassano Lanier</a> wrote all the Shakespearean sonnets and plays under the name <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>, primarily because she was a woman who would have been persecuted, prosecuted, or hanged if it were known she had written plays at that time of the 16th to early 17th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>, and <sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On the other hand, some 20th and 21st-century male romance novelists have used female pen names.<sup id="cite_ref-maleauthors_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maleauthors-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> A few examples are Brindle Chase, <a href="/wiki/Peter_O%27Donnell" title="Peter O&#39;Donnell">Peter O'Donnell</a> (as Madeline Brent), <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Wood_(writer)" title="Christopher Wood (writer)">Christopher Wood</a> (as Penny Sutton and Rosie Dixon), and <a href="/wiki/Hugh_C._Rae" title="Hugh C. Rae">Hugh C. Rae</a> (as Jessica Sterling).<sup id="cite_ref-maleauthors_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maleauthors-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A pen name may be used if a writer's real name is likely to be confused with the name of another writer or notable individual, or if the real name is deemed unsuitable.
</p><p>Authors who write both fiction and non-fiction, or in different <a href="/wiki/Genres" class="mw-redirect" title="Genres">genres</a>, may use different pen names to avoid confusing their readers. For example, the romance writer <a href="/wiki/Nora_Roberts" title="Nora Roberts">Nora Roberts</a> writes mystery novels under the name <a href="/wiki/J._D._Robb" class="mw-redirect" title="J. D. Robb">J. D. Robb</a>.
</p><p>In some cases, an author may become better known by his pen name than his real name. A famous example is Samuel Clemens, writing as <a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">Mark Twain</a>. The British <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">mathematician</a> Charles Dodgson wrote fantasy novels as <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a> and mathematical treatises under his own name.
</p><p>Some authors, such as <a href="/wiki/Harold_Robbins" title="Harold Robbins">Harold Robbins</a>, use several literary pseudonyms.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some pen names have been used for long periods, even decades, without the author's true identity being discovered, as with <a href="/wiki/Elena_Ferrante" title="Elena Ferrante">Elena Ferrante</a> and <a href="/wiki/Torsten_Krol" title="Torsten Krol">Torsten Krol</a>.
</p><p>Some pen names are not strictly pseudonyms, as they are simply variants of the authors' actual names. The authors <a href="/wiki/C._L._Moore" title="C. L. Moore">C. L. Moore</a> and <a href="/wiki/S._E._Hinton" title="S. E. Hinton">S. E. Hinton</a> were female authors who used the initialled forms of their full names, Moore being Catherine Lucille Moore, writing in the 1930s male-dominated science fiction genre, and Hinton, (author of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Outsiders_(novel)" title="The Outsiders (novel)">The Outsiders</a></i>) Susan Eloise Hinton. <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Trek" title="Star Trek">Star Trek</a></i> writer <a href="/wiki/D._C._Fontana" title="D. C. Fontana">D. C. Fontana</a> (Dorothy Catherine) wrote using her own abbreviated name and under the pen names Michael Richards and J. Michael Bingham. Author <a href="/wiki/V.C._Andrews" class="mw-redirect" title="V.C. Andrews">V.C. Andrews</a> intended to publish under her given name, Virginia Andrews, but was told that, due to a production error, her first novel was being released under the name of "V.C. Andrews"; later she learned that her publisher had in fact done this deliberately. Joanne Kathleen Rowling<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> published the <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">Harry Potter</a></i> series as <a href="/wiki/J._K._Rowling" title="J. K. Rowling">J. K. Rowling</a>. Rowling also published the <a href="/wiki/Cormoran_Strike" title="Cormoran Strike">Cormoran Strike</a> series, a series of detective novels including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cuckoo%27s_Calling" title="The Cuckoo&#39;s Calling">The Cuckoo's Calling</a></i> under the pseudonym "Robert Galbraith".
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> wrote as <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill_as_writer" title="Winston Churchill as writer">Winston S. Churchill</a> (from his full surname "Spencer-Churchill" which he did not otherwise use) in an attempt to avoid confusion with an <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill_(novelist)" title="Winston Churchill (novelist)">American novelist of the same name</a>. The attempt was not wholly successful – the two are still sometimes confused by booksellers.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A pen name may be used specifically to hide the identity of the author, as with <a href="/wiki/Expos%C3%A9_(journalism)" class="mw-redirect" title="Exposé (journalism)">exposé</a> books about <a href="/wiki/Espionage" title="Espionage">espionage</a> or <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a>, or explicit erotic fiction. Some prolific authors adopt a pseudonym to disguise the extent of their published output, e. g. <a href="/wiki/Stephen_King" title="Stephen King">Stephen King</a> writing as <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bachman" title="Richard Bachman">Richard Bachman</a>. Co-authors may choose to publish under a collective pseudonym, e. g., <a href="/wiki/P._J._Tracy" title="P. J. Tracy">P. J. Tracy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Perri_O%27Shaughnessy" title="Perri O&#39;Shaughnessy">Perri O'Shaughnessy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Frederic_Dannay" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederic Dannay">Frederic Dannay</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manfred_Lee" class="mw-redirect" title="Manfred Lee">Manfred Lee</a> used the name <a href="/wiki/Ellery_Queen" title="Ellery Queen">Ellery Queen</a> as a pen name for their collaborative works and as the name of their main character.
</p><p>"Why do authors choose pseudonyms? It is rarely because they actually hope to stay anonymous forever," mused writer and columnist <a href="/wiki/Russell_Smith_(writer)" title="Russell Smith (writer)">Russell Smith</a> in his review of the Canadian novel <i>Into That Fire</i> by the pseudonymous M. J. Cates. <sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A famous case in French literature was <a href="/wiki/Romain_Gary" title="Romain Gary">Romain Gary</a>. Already a well-known writer, he started publishing books as <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Ajar" class="mw-redirect" title="Émile Ajar">Émile Ajar</a> to test whether his new books would be well received on their own merits, without the aid of his established reputation. They were: Émile Ajar, like Romain Gary before him, was awarded the prestigious <a href="/wiki/Prix_Goncourt" title="Prix Goncourt">Prix Goncourt</a> by a jury unaware that they were the same person. Similarly, TV actor <a href="/wiki/Ronnie_Barker" title="Ronnie Barker">Ronnie Barker</a> submitted comedy material under the name Gerald Wiley.
</p><p>A collective pseudonym may represent an entire publishing house, or any contributor to a long-running series, especially with juvenile literature. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Watty_Piper" class="mw-redirect" title="Watty Piper">Watty Piper</a>, <a href="/wiki/Victor_Appleton" title="Victor Appleton">Victor Appleton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erin_Hunter" title="Erin Hunter">Erin Hunter</a>, and Kamiru M. Xhan.
</p><p>Another use of a pseudonym in literature is to present a story as being written by the fictional characters in the story. The series of novels known as <i><a href="/wiki/A_Series_Of_Unfortunate_Events" class="mw-redirect" title="A Series Of Unfortunate Events">A Series Of Unfortunate Events</a></i> are written by <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Handler" title="Daniel Handler">Daniel Handler</a> under the pen name of <a href="/wiki/Lemony_Snicket" title="Lemony Snicket">Lemony Snicket</a>, a character in the series. This applies also to some of the several 18th-century English and American writers who used the name <a href="/wiki/Fidelia_(pseudonym)" title="Fidelia (pseudonym)">Fidelia</a>.
</p><p>An <b>anonymity pseudonym</b> or <a href="/wiki/Multiple-use_name" title="Multiple-use name">multiple-use name</a> is a name used by many different people to protect anonymity.<sup id="cite_ref-Home_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Home-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> It is a strategy that has been adopted by many unconnected radical groups and by cultural groups, where the construct of personal identity has been criticised. This has led to the idea of the "open pop star".
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Medicine">Medicine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudonym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Medicine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Pseudonyms and <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">acronyms</a> are often employed in <a href="/wiki/Medical_research" title="Medical research">medical research</a> to <a href="/wiki/Privacy_for_research_participants" title="Privacy for research participants">protect subjects' identities</a> through a process known as <a href="/wiki/De-identification" title="De-identification">de-identification</a>.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_and_paramilitary_organizations"><span id="Noms_de_guerre"></span>Military and paramilitary organizations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudonym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Military and paramilitary organizations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>In <a href="/wiki/Ancien_R%C3%A9gime" title="Ancien Régime">Ancien Régime</a> France, a <i>nom de guerre</i> ("war name") would be adopted by each new recruit (or assigned to them by the captain of their company) as they enlisted in the <a href="/wiki/French_army" class="mw-redirect" title="French army">French army</a>. These pseudonyms had an official character and were the predecessor of <a href="/wiki/Dog_tag" title="Dog tag">identification numbers</a>: soldiers were identified by their first names, their family names, and their <i>noms de guerre</i> (e. g. <i>Jean Amarault dit Lafidélité</i>). These pseudonyms were usually related to the soldier's place of origin (e. g. <i>Jean Deslandes dit Champigny</i>, for a soldier coming from a town named <a href="/wiki/Champigny_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Champigny (disambiguation)">Champigny</a>), or to a particular physical or personal trait (e. g. <i>Antoine Bonnet dit Prettaboire</i>, for a soldier <i>prêt à boire</i>, ready to drink). In 1716, a <i>nom de guerre</i> was mandatory for every soldier; officers did not adopt <i>noms de guerre</i> as they considered them derogatory. In daily life, these aliases could replace the real family name.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Noms de guerre</i> were adopted for security reasons by members of the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> <a href="/wiki/French_resistance" class="mw-redirect" title="French resistance">French resistance</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polish_underground_state" class="mw-redirect" title="Polish underground state">Polish resistance</a>. Such pseudonyms are often adopted by military special-forces soldiers, such as members of the SAS and similar units of <a href="/wiki/Resistance_movement" title="Resistance movement">resistance fighters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Terrorism" title="Terrorism">terrorists</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_warfare" title="Guerrilla warfare">guerrillas</a>. This practice hides their identities and may protect their families from reprisals; it may also be a form of <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dissociation" class="extiw" title="wikt:dissociation">dissociation</a> from domestic life. Some well-known men who adopted <i>noms de guerre</i> include Carlos, for <a href="/wiki/Ilich_Ram%C3%ADrez_S%C3%A1nchez" class="mw-redirect" title="Ilich Ramírez Sánchez">Ilich Ramírez Sánchez</a>; <a href="/wiki/Willy_Brandt" title="Willy Brandt">Willy Brandt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chancellor_of_Germany_(Federal_Republic)" class="mw-redirect" title="Chancellor of Germany (Federal Republic)">Chancellor</a> of <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Subcomandante_Marcos" title="Subcomandante Marcos">Subcomandante Marcos</a>, spokesman of the <a href="/wiki/Zapatista_Army_of_National_Liberation" title="Zapatista Army of National Liberation">Zapatista Army of National Liberation</a> (EZLN).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> During <a href="/wiki/Lehi_(group)" class="mw-redirect" title="Lehi (group)">Lehi</a>'s underground fight against the British in <a href="/wiki/Mandatory_Palestine" title="Mandatory Palestine">Mandatory Palestine</a>, the organization's commander <a href="/wiki/Yitzchak_Shamir" class="mw-redirect" title="Yitzchak Shamir">Yitzchak Shamir</a> (later <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Israel" title="Prime Minister of Israel">Prime Minister of Israel</a>) adopted the <i>nom de guerre</i> "Michael", in honour of <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Michael_Collins_(Irish_leader)" title="Michael Collins (Irish leader)">Michael Collins</a>.
</p><p>Revolutionaries and resistance leaders, such as <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Lenin" title="Vladimir Lenin">Lenin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leon_Trotsky" title="Leon Trotsky">Trotsky</a>, <a href="/wiki/Golda_Meir" title="Golda Meir">Golda Meir</a>, <a href="/wiki/Philippe_Leclerc_de_Hauteclocque" title="Philippe Leclerc de Hauteclocque">Philippe Leclerc de Hauteclocque</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Josip_Broz_Tito" title="Josip Broz Tito">Josip Broz Tito</a>, often adopted their <i>noms de guerre</i> as their proper names after the struggle. <a href="/wiki/George_Grivas" class="mw-redirect" title="George Grivas">George Grivas</a>, the Greek-Cypriot <a href="/wiki/EOKA" title="EOKA">EOKA</a> militant, adopted the <i>nom de guerre</i> Digenis (Διγενής). In the <a href="/wiki/French_Foreign_Legion" title="French Foreign Legion">French Foreign Legion</a>, recruits can adopt a pseudonym to break with their past lives. Mercenaries have long used "noms de guerre", sometimes even multiple identities, depending on country, conflict and circumstance.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Some of the most familiar noms de guerre today are the <a href="/wiki/Kunya_(Arabic)" title="Kunya (Arabic)">kunya</a> used by Islamic <a href="/wiki/Mujahideen" title="Mujahideen">mujahideen</a>. These take the form of a <a href="/wiki/Teknonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Teknonym">teknonym</a>, either literal or figurative.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Online_activity">Online activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudonym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Online activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Individuals using a computer <a href="/wiki/Online" class="mw-redirect" title="Online">online</a> may adopt or be required to use a form of pseudonym known as a "handle" (a term deriving from <a href="/wiki/CB_slang" class="mw-redirect" title="CB slang">CB slang</a>), "<a href="/wiki/User_(computing)" title="User (computing)">user</a> name", "<a href="/wiki/Logging_(computer_security)" class="mw-redirect" title="Logging (computer security)">login</a> name", "<a href="/wiki/Avatar_(virtual_reality)" class="mw-redirect" title="Avatar (virtual reality)">avatar</a>", or, sometimes, "<a href="/wiki/Screen_name_(computing)" class="mw-redirect" title="Screen name (computing)">screen name</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Gamertag" class="mw-redirect" title="Gamertag">gamertag</a>" "IGN (<b>I</b>n <b>G</b>ame (<b>N</b>ick)<b>N</b>ame)" or "<a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nickname</a>". On the Internet, <a href="/wiki/Pseudonymous_remailer" title="Pseudonymous remailer">pseudonymous remailers</a> use <a href="/wiki/Cryptography" title="Cryptography">cryptography</a> that achieves persistent <a href="/wiki/Pseudonymity" title="Pseudonymity">pseudonymity</a>, so that two-way communication can be achieved, and reputations can be established, without linking physical <a href="/wiki/Identity_theft" title="Identity theft">identities</a> to their respective pseudonyms. <a href="/wiki/Aliasing_(computing)" title="Aliasing (computing)">Aliasing</a> is the use of multiple names for the same data location.
</p><p>More sophisticated cryptographic systems, such as anonymous <a href="/wiki/Digital_credential" title="Digital credential">digital credentials</a>, enable users to communicate pseudonymously (i. e., by identifying themselves by means of pseudonyms). In well-defined abuse cases, a designated authority may be able to revoke the pseudonyms and reveal the individuals' real identity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Use of pseudonyms is common among professional <a href="/wiki/ESports" class="mw-redirect" title="ESports">eSports</a> players, despite the fact that many professional games are played on <a href="/wiki/LAN" class="mw-redirect" title="LAN">LAN</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Privacy">Privacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudonym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Privacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>People seeking privacy often use pseudonyms to make appointments and reservations.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Those writing to <a href="/wiki/Advice_column" title="Advice column">advice columns</a> in newspapers and magazines may use pseudonyms.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Steve_Wozniak" title="Steve Wozniak">Steve Wozniak</a> used a pseudonym when attending the <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">University of California, Berkeley</a> after co-founding <a href="/wiki/Apple_Computer" class="mw-redirect" title="Apple Computer">Apple Computer</a>, because "I knew I wouldn't have time enough to be an A+ student."<sup id="cite_ref-stix19860514_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stix19860514-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stage_names">Stage names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudonym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Stage names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Stage_name" title="Stage name">Stage name</a></div>
<p>When used by an <a href="/wiki/Actor" title="Actor">actor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Musician" title="Musician">musician</a>, radio <a href="/wiki/Disc_jockey" title="Disc jockey">disc jockey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Model_(person)" title="Model (person)">model</a>, or other performer or "<a href="/wiki/Show_business" title="Show business">show business</a>" personality a pseudonym is called a <i>stage name</i>, or, occasionally, a <i>professional name</i>, or <i>screen name</i>.
</p>
<h4><span id="Film.2C_theatre.2C_and_related_activities"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Film,_theatre,_and_related_activities">Film, theatre, and related activities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudonym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Film, theatre, and related activities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Members of a marginalized ethnic or religious group have often adopted stage names, typically changing their surname or entire name to mask their original background.
</p><p>Stage names are also used to create a more marketable name, as in the case of Creighton Tull Chaney, who adopted the pseudonym <a href="/wiki/Lon_Chaney,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="Lon Chaney, Jr.">Lon Chaney, Jr.</a>, a reference to his famous father <a href="/wiki/Lon_Chaney,_Sr." class="mw-redirect" title="Lon Chaney, Sr.">Lon Chaney, Sr.</a>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Chris_Curtis" title="Chris Curtis">Chris Curtis</a> of <a href="/wiki/Deep_Purple" title="Deep Purple">Deep Purple</a> fame was christened as Christopher Crummey ("crumby" is UK slang for poor quality). In this and similar cases a stage name is adopted simply to avoid an unfortunate <a href="/wiki/Pun" title="Pun">pun</a>.
</p><p>Pseudonyms are also used to comply with the rules of <a href="/wiki/Performing_arts" title="Performing arts">performing arts</a> <a href="/wiki/Guild" title="Guild">guilds</a> (<a href="/wiki/Screen_Actors_Guild" title="Screen Actors Guild">Screen Actors Guild</a> (SAG), <a href="/wiki/Writers_Guild_of_America,_East" title="Writers Guild of America, East">Writers Guild of America, East</a> (WGA), <a href="/wiki/AFTRA" class="mw-redirect" title="AFTRA">AFTRA</a>, etc.), which do not allow performers to use an existing name, in order to avoid confusion. For example, these rules required film and television actor Michael Fox to add a middle initial and become <a href="/wiki/Michael_J._Fox" title="Michael J. Fox">Michael J. Fox</a>, to avoid being confused with another actor named <a href="/wiki/Michael_Fox_(American_actor)" title="Michael Fox (American actor)">Michael Fox</a>. This was also true of author and actress <a href="/wiki/Fannie_Flagg" title="Fannie Flagg">Fannie Flagg</a>, who chose this pseudonym; her real name, Patricia Neal, being the name of <a href="/wiki/Patricia_Neal" title="Patricia Neal">another well-known actress</a>; and British actor <a href="/wiki/Stewart_Granger" title="Stewart Granger">Stewart Granger</a>, whose real name was James Stewart. The film-making team of <a href="/wiki/Coen_brothers" title="Coen brothers">Joel and Ethan Coen</a>, for instance, share credit for editing under the alias Roderick Jaynes.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some stage names are used to conceal a person's identity, such as the pseudonym <a href="/wiki/Alan_Smithee" title="Alan Smithee">Alan Smithee</a>, which was used by directors in the <a href="/wiki/Directors_Guild_of_America" title="Directors Guild of America">Directors Guild of America</a> (DGA) to remove their name from a film they feel was edited or modified beyond their artistic satisfaction. In theatre, the pseudonyms <a href="/wiki/George_Spelvin" title="George Spelvin">George or Georgina Spelvin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Plinge" title="Walter Plinge">Walter Plinge</a> are used to hide the identity of a performer, usually when he or she is "doubling" (playing more than one role in the same play).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/David_Agnew" title="David Agnew">David Agnew</a> was a name used by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> to conceal the identity of a scriptwriter, such as for the <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Who" title="Doctor Who">Doctor Who</a> serial <a href="/wiki/City_of_Death" title="City of Death">City of Death</a>, which had three writers, including <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Adams" title="Douglas Adams">Douglas Adams</a>, who was at the time of writing the show's Script Editor.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> In another Doctor Who serial, <a href="/wiki/The_Brain_of_Morbius" title="The Brain of Morbius">The Brain of Morbius</a>, writer <a href="/wiki/Terrance_Dicks" title="Terrance Dicks">Terrance Dicks</a> demanded the removal of his name from the credits saying it could go out under a "bland pseudonym".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup><sup id="cite_ref-codenames_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-codenames-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> This ended up as <i>Robin Bland</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-codenames_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-codenames-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pseudonym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p>Musicians and singers can use pseudonyms to allow artists to collaborate with artists on other labels while avoiding the need to gain permission from their own labels, such as the artist <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Samuels" class="mw-redirect" title="Jerry Samuels">Jerry Samuels</a>, who made songs under Napoleon XIV. Rock singer-guitarist <a href="/wiki/George_Harrison" title="George Harrison">George Harrison</a>, for example, played guitar on <a href="/wiki/Cream_(band)" title="Cream (band)">Cream</a>'s song "<a href="/wiki/Badge_(song)" title="Badge (song)">Badge</a>" using a pseudonym.<sup id="cite_ref-John_Wynn_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-John_Wynn-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In classical music, some record companies issued recordings under a <i>nom de disque</i> in the 1950s and 1960s to avoid paying royalties. A number of popular budget LPs of piano music were released under the pseudonym <a href="/wiki/Paul_Procopolis" title="Paul Procopolis">Paul Procopolis</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Another example is that <a href="/wiki/Paul_McCartney" title="Paul McCartney">Paul McCartney</a> used his fictional name "Bernerd Webb" for <a href="/wiki/Peter_and_Gordon" title="Peter and Gordon">Peter and Gordon</a>'s song <a href="/wiki/Woman_(Paul_McCartney_song)" title="Woman (Paul McCartney song)">Woman</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Pseudonyms are used as stage names in <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">heavy metal</a> bands, such as <a href="/wiki/Tracii_Guns" title="Tracii Guns">Tracii Guns</a> in <a href="/wiki/LA_Guns" class="mw-redirect" title="LA Guns">LA Guns</a>, <a href="/wiki/Axl_Rose" title="Axl Rose">Axl Rose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slash_(musician)" title="Slash (musician)">Slash</a> in <a href="/wiki/Guns_N%27_Roses" title="Guns N&#39; Roses">Guns N' Roses</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mick_Mars" title="Mick Mars">Mick Mars</a> in <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6tley_Cr%C3%BCe" title="Mötley Crüe">Mötley Crüe</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dimebag_Darrell" title="Dimebag Darrell">Dimebag Darrell</a> in <a href="/wiki/Pantera" title="Pantera">Pantera</a>, or <a href="/wiki/C.C._Deville" class="mw-redirect" title="C.C. Deville">C.C. Deville</a> in <a href="/wiki/Poison_(American_band)" title="Poison (American band)">Poison</a>. Some such names have additional meanings, like that of Brian Hugh Warner, more commonly known as <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Manson" title="Marilyn Manson">Marilyn Manson</a>: Marilyn coming from <a href="/wiki/Marilyn_Monroe" title="Marilyn Monroe">Marilyn Monroe</a> and Manson from convicted serial killer <a href="/wiki/Charles_Manson" title="Charles Manson">Charles Manson</a>. <a href="/wiki/Jacoby_Shaddix" title="Jacoby Shaddix">Jacoby Shaddix</a> of <a href="/wiki/Papa_Roach" title="Papa Roach">Papa Roach</a> went under the name "Coby Dick" during the <i><a href="/wiki/Infest_(album)" title="Infest (album)">Infest</a></i> era. He changed back to his birth name when <i><a href="/wiki/Lovehatetragedy" title="Lovehatetragedy">lovehatetragedy</a></i> was released.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/David_Johansen" title="David Johansen">David Johansen</a>, front man for the hard rock band <a href="/wiki/New_York_Dolls" title="New York Dolls">New York Dolls</a>, recorded and performed pop and lounge music under the pseudonym Buster Poindexter in the late 1980s and early 1990s. The music video for Poindexter's debt single, <a href="/wiki/Hot_Hot_Hot_(Arrow_song)#Buster_Poindexter_version" title="Hot Hot Hot (Arrow song)">Hot Hot Hot</a>, opens with a monologue from Johansen where he notes his time with the New York Dolls and explains his desire to create more sophisticated music. 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ross_Bagdasarian,_Sr." class="mw-redirect" title="Ross Bagdasarian, Sr.">Ross Bagdasarian, Sr.</a>, creator of <a href="/wiki/Alvin_and_the_Chipmunks" title="Alvin and the Chipmunks">Alvin and the Chipmunks</a>, wrote original songs, arranged, and produced the records under his real name, but performed on them as <a href="/wiki/David_Seville" title="David Seville">David Seville</a>. He also wrote songs as Skipper Adams. Danish pop pianist <a href="/wiki/Bent_Fabric" title="Bent Fabric">Bent Fabric</a>, whose full name is Bent Fabricius-Bjerre, wrote his biggest instrumental hit "<a href="/wiki/Alley_Cat_(song)" title="Alley Cat (song)">Alley Cat</a>" as Frank Bjorn.
</p><p>For a time, the musician <a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)" title="Prince (musician)">Prince</a> used an unpronounceable "<a href="/wiki/Prince_(musician)#Stage_names" title="Prince (musician)">Love Symbol</a>" as a pseudonym ("Prince" is his actual first name rather than a stage name). He wrote the song "<a href="/wiki/Sugar_Walls" title="Sugar Walls">Sugar Walls</a>" for <a href="/wiki/Sheena_Easton" title="Sheena Easton">Sheena Easton</a> as "Alexander Nevermind" and "<a href="/wiki/Manic_Monday" title="Manic Monday">Manic Monday</a>" for <a href="/wiki/The_Bangles" title="The Bangles">The Bangles</a> as "Christopher Tracy". (He also produced albums early in his career as "Jamie Starr").
</p><p>Many Italian-American singers have used stage names, as their birth names were difficult to pronounce or considered too ethnic for American tastes. Singers changing their names included <a href="/wiki/Dean_Martin" title="Dean Martin">Dean Martin</a> (born Dino Paul Crocetti), <a href="/wiki/Connie_Francis" title="Connie Francis">Connie Francis</a> (born Concetta Franconero), <a href="/wiki/Frankie_Valli" title="Frankie Valli">Frankie Valli</a> (born Francesco Castelluccio), <a href="/wiki/Tony_Bennett" title="Tony Bennett">Tony Bennett</a> (born Anthony Benedetto), and <a href="/wiki/Lady_Gaga" title="Lady Gaga">Lady Gaga</a> (born Stefani Germanotta)
</p><p>In 2009, the British rock band <a href="/wiki/Feeder_(band)" title="Feeder (band)">Feeder</a> briefly changed its name to <a href="/wiki/Renegades_(band)" title="Renegades (band)">Renegades</a> so itcould play a whole show featuring a set list in which 95 per cent of the songs played were from their forthcoming new album of the same name, with none of their singles included. Front man <a href="/wiki/Grant_Nicholas" title="Grant Nicholas">Grant Nicholas</a> felt that if they played as Feeder, there would be uproar over him not playing any of the singles, so used the pseudonym as a hint. A series of small shows were played in 2010, at 250- to 1,000-capacity venues with the plan not to say who the band really are and just announce the shows as if they were a new band.
</p><p>In many cases, hip-hop and rap artist prefer to use pseudonyms that represents some variation of their name, personality, or interests. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Iggy_Azalea" title="Iggy Azalea">Iggy Azalea</a> (her name comes from her dog name, Iggy, and her home street in <a href="/wiki/Mullumbimby" title="Mullumbimby">Mullumbimby</a>, Azalea street), <a href="/wiki/Ol%27_Dirty_Bastard" title="Ol&#39; Dirty Bastard">Ol' Dirty Bastard</a> (who was known under at least six aliases), <a href="/wiki/Sean_Combs" title="Sean Combs">Diddy</a> (previously known at various times as Puffy, P. Diddy, and Puff Daddy), <a href="/wiki/Ludacris" title="Ludacris">Ludacris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flo_Rida" title="Flo Rida">Flo Rida</a> (his name is a tribute to his home state, <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">Florida</a>), <a href="/wiki/LL_Cool_J" title="LL Cool J">LL Cool J</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chingy" title="Chingy">Chingy</a>. <a href="/wiki/Black_metal" title="Black metal">Black metal</a> artists also adopt pseudonyms, usually symbolizing dark values, such as <a href="/wiki/Nocturno_Culto" title="Nocturno Culto">Nocturno Culto</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gaahl" title="Gaahl">Gaahl</a>, Abbath, and Silenoz. In punk and hardcore punk, singers and band members often replace real names with tougher-sounding stage names such as <a href="/wiki/Sid_Vicious" title="Sid Vicious">Sid Vicious</a> (real name John Simon Ritchie) of the late 1970s band <a href="/wiki/Sex_Pistols" title="Sex Pistols">Sex Pistols</a> and "Rat" of the early 1980s band <a href="/wiki/The_Varukers" title="The Varukers">The Varukers</a> and the 2000s re-formation of <a href="/wiki/Discharge_(band)" title="Discharge (band)">Discharge</a>. Punk rock band <a href="/wiki/The_Ramones" class="mw-redirect" title="The Ramones">The Ramones</a> had every member take the last name of Ramone.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Henry John Deutschendorf Jr., an American <a href="/wiki/Singer-songwriter" title="Singer-songwriter">singer-songwriter</a> used the stage name <a href="/wiki/John_Denver" title="John Denver">John Denver</a>. The Australian country musician born Robert Lane changed his name to <a href="/wiki/Tex_Morton" title="Tex Morton">Tex Morton</a>. Reginald Kenneth Dwight legally changed his name in 1972 to <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a>
</p>
