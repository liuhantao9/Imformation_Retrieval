<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sovereign">Sovereign</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial,_royal_and_noble_ranks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sovereign">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">Monarch</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sovereign" title="Sovereign">Sovereign</a></div>
<ul><li>The word <i>monarch</i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> μονάρχης, <i>monárkhēs</i>, "sole ruler" (from μόνος, <i>mónos</i>, "single" or "sole", and <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">ἄρχων</span>, <i>árkhōn</i>, <a href="/wiki/Archon" title="Archon">archon</a>, "leader", "ruler", "chief", the word being the present participle of the verb ἄρχειν, <i>árkhein</i>, "to rule", "to lead", this from the noun ὰρχή, <i>arkhē</i>, "beginning", "authority", "principle") through the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latinized</a> form <i>monarcha</i>.</li>
<li>The word <i>sovereign</i> is derived from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>above</i>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Autocracy" title="Autocracy">Autocrat</a> is derived from the Greek <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">αὐτοκράτωρ</span>: <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">αὐτός</span> ("self") and <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">κρατείν</span> ("to hold power"), and may be translated as "one who rules by himself".</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_titles_for_European_and_Near_Eastern_monarchs">Common titles for European and Near Eastern monarchs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial,_royal_and_noble_ranks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Common titles for European and Near Eastern monarchs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Note that many titles listed may also be used by lesser nobles – non-sovereigns – depending on the historical period and state. The sovereign titles listed below are grouped together into categories roughly according to their degree of dignity; these being: imperial (Emperor, Empress, etc.), high royal (King of Kings etc.), royal (King/Queen, sovereign Grand Duke or Grand Prince, etc.), others (sovereign Prince, sovereign Duke, etc.), and religious.
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Imperial_titles">Imperial titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial,_royal_and_noble_ranks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Imperial titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<ul><li>"<a href="/wiki/Emperor" title="Emperor">Emperor</a>", from the Latin, 'imperator,' which was originally a military title<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> . Soldiers would salute the leader of a victorious army as 'imperator.'. In English, the feminine form is <b>Empress</b> (the Latin is <i>imperatrix</i>). The realm of an emperor or empress is termed an <i>Empire</i>. Other words meaning Emperor include:
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Caesar_(title)" title="Caesar (title)">Caesar</a></b>, the appellation of <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Roman</a> emperors derived from the Roman dictator <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Julius Caesar</a>, whose great-nephew and adopted son <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Julius_Caesar_Augustus" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaius Julius Caesar Augustus">Gaius Julius Caesar Augustus</a> became the first emperor of Rome. Augustus' four successors were each made the adoptive son of his predecessor, and were therefore legally entitled to use "Caesar" as a constituent of their names; after Nero, however, the familial link of the <a href="/wiki/Julio-Claudian_dynasty" title="Julio-Claudian dynasty">Julio-Claudian dynasty</a> was disrupted and use of the word <i>Caesar</i> continued as a title only.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Kaiser" title="Kaiser">Kaiser</a></b>, derived from Caesar, primarily used in Germanic countries.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Augustus_(title)" title="Augustus (title)">Augustus</a>,</b> A Roman Honoriffic which means Venerable or Majestic used by Roman Emperors from the Beginning of the Empire onwards</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Autokrator" title="Autokrator">Basileus kai Autokrator</a>,</b> <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Greek" title="Medieval Greek">Medieval Greek</a> title meaning "sovereign and autocrat", used by the <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">Roman emperors</a> from the 9th century onwards.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Tsar" title="Tsar">Tsar / Tzar / Csar / Czar</a></b>, derived as shortened variant of the Slavic pronunciation of Caesar (<i>tsyasar</i>), the feminine form <i><a href="/wiki/Tsaritsa" class="mw-redirect" title="Tsaritsa">Tsaritsa</a></i>, primarily used in Bulgaria, and after that in Russia and other Slavic countries.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_China" title="Emperor of China">Huangdi (皇帝)</a></b>, the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_monarch" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial monarch">Imperial monarch</a> during <a href="/wiki/Imperial_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial China">Imperial China</a>.</li>

<li><b><a href="/wiki/Xiang_Yu" title="Xiang Yu">Xi Chu Ba Wang （西楚霸王）</a></b> meaning the <a href="/wiki/Hegemony" title="Hegemony">Hegemon King of</a> <a href="/wiki/Chu_(state)" title="Chu (state)">Western Chu</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Khan_of_Heaven" title="Khan of Heaven">Tian Kehan（ 天可汗)</a></b> meaning <a href="/wiki/Tian" title="Tian">Heavenly</a> <a href="/wiki/Khagan" title="Khagan">Khagan</a>. <li><b><a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Ethiopia" title="Emperor of Ethiopia">Nəgusä Nägäst</a></b>, title of the Emperors of <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">Ethiopia</a>, meaning "King of Kings".</li>

It is also the title of the <a href="/wiki/Omukama_of_Toro" title="Omukama of Toro">Omukama</a> of <a href="/wiki/Toro_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Toro Kingdom">Toro</a>.</li></ul></li>

Currently, the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Morocco" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Morocco">King of Morocco</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sultan_of_Sokoto" class="mw-redirect" title="Sultan of Sokoto">Sultan of Sokoto</a> hold this title, although neither officially claims the <a href="/wiki/Caliphate" title="Caliphate">Caliphate</a>.</li>

<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_titles">Religious titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial,_royal_and_noble_ranks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Religious titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Caliph" class="mw-redirect" title="Caliph">Caliph</a></b>, was the ruler of the <a href="/wiki/Caliphate" title="Caliphate">caliphate</a>, an Islamic title indicating the successor to <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>. Both a religious and a secular leader; the Caliph was the secular head of the international Muslim community, as a nation. To claim the Caliphate was, theoretically, to claim stewardship over Muslims on earth, under the sovereignty of Allah. (See <i>Amir al-Mu'minin</i> above). This did not necessarily mean that the Caliph was himself the supreme authority on Islamic law or theology; that still fell to the <i>Ulema</i>. The role of the Caliph was to oversee and take responsibility for the Muslim community's political and governmental needs (both within and beyond the borders of his territorial realm), rather than to himself determine matters of doctrine, like the Pope.</li>

Among other incarnate Tibetan lamas, the second highest Gelug prelate is the <b><a href="/wiki/Panchen_Lama" title="Panchen Lama">Panchen Lama</a></b>. From the time of the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Dalai_Lama" class="mw-redirect" title="Fifth Dalai Lama">Fifth Dalai Lama</a> until 1950 the Dalai Lamas effectively ruled Tibet. The chief of the rival <a href="/wiki/Kagyu" title="Kagyu">Kagyu</a> school of Tibetan Buddhism is the <b><a href="/wiki/Karmapa" title="Karmapa">Karmapa</a></b>.</li>

Also the title of the leader of the <a href="/wiki/Coptic_Orthodox_Church_of_Alexandria" title="Coptic Orthodox Church of Alexandria">Coptic Church</a>.</li>

<h3><span id="Other_sovereigns.2C_royalty.2C_peers.2C_and_major_nobility"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_sovereigns,_royalty,_peers,_and_major_nobility">Other sovereigns, royalty, peers, and major nobility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial,_royal_and_noble_ranks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other sovereigns, royalty, peers, and major nobility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Royal_family" title="Royal family">Royal family</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peerage" title="Peerage">Peerage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">Nobility</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Imperial_immediacy" title="Imperial immediacy">Imperial immediacy</a></div>
<p>Several ranks were widely used (for more than a thousand years in Europe alone) for both sovereign rulers and non-sovereigns. Additional knowledge about the territory and historic period is required to know whether the rank holder was a sovereign or non-sovereign. However, joint precedence among rank holders often greatly depended on whether a rank holder was sovereign, whether of the same rank or not. This situation was most widely exemplified by the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> (HRE) in Europe. Several of the following ranks were commonly both sovereign and non-sovereign within the HRE. Outside of the HRE, the most common sovereign rank of these below was that of Prince. Within the HRE, those holding the following ranks who were also sovereigns had (enjoyed) what was known as an <i><a href="/wiki/Imperial_immediacy" title="Imperial immediacy">immediate</a></i> relationship with the Emperor. Those holding non-sovereign ranks held only a <i>mediate</i> relationship (meaning that the civil hierarchy upwards was mediated by one or more intermediaries between the rank holder and the Emperor).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Titles">Titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial,_royal_and_noble_ranks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Archduke" title="Archduke">Archduke</a></b>, ruler of an archduchy; used exclusively by the <a href="/wiki/Habsburg" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg">Habsburg</a> dynasty and its junior branch of <a href="/wiki/Habsburg-Lorraine" class="mw-redirect" title="Habsburg-Lorraine">Habsburg-Lorraine</a> which ruled the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> (until 1806), the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Empire" title="Austrian Empire">Austrian Empire</a> (1804-1867), and the <a href="/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Austro-Hungarian Empire">Austro-Hungarian Empire</a> (1867-1918) for imperial family members of the dynasty, each retaining it as a subsidiary title when founding sovereign <a href="/wiki/Cadet_branch" title="Cadet branch">cadet branches</a> by acquiring thrones under different titles (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duke_of_Tuscany" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Duke of Tuscany">Tuscany</a>, <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Modena" class="mw-redirect" title="Duke of Modena">Modena</a>); it was also used for those ruling some Habsburg territories such as those that became the modern so-called "Benelux" nations (Belgium, Netherlands, Luxembourg); The title was created in 1358 by the Habsburgs themselves to establish a precedence of their princes over the other titleholders of high nobility of the era; therefore the rank was not recognized by the other ruling dynasties until 1453<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Grand_Duke" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Duke">Grand Duke</a></b>, ruler of a grand duchy; nowadays considered to be in precedence the third highest monarchial rank in the western world, after "Emperor" and "King" or "Queen"</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Grand_Prince" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Prince">Grand Prince</a></b> (<i>Velikiy <a href="/wiki/Knyaz" title="Knyaz">Knyaz</a></i>), ruler of a grand principality; a title primarily used in the medieval Kyivan Rus' principalities; It was also used by the <a href="/wiki/Romanov" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanov">Romanovs</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a> for members of the imperial family, although then it is more commonly translated into English as <i>Grand Duke</i>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Grand_%C5%BDupan" title="Grand Župan">Grand Župan</a></b>, like a Russian Grand Prince to a Knyaz.</li></ul></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Duke" title="Duke">Duke</a></b> (<i><a href="/wiki/Herzog" title="Herzog">Herzog</a></i> in German), ruler<sup id="cite_ref-sov_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sov-4">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> of a <a href="/wiki/Duchy" title="Duchy">duchy</a>;<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;b&#93;</a></sup> also for junior members of ducal and some grand ducal families</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Prince" title="Prince">Prince</a></b> (<i>Prinz</i> in German), junior members of a royal, grand ducal, ruling ducal or princely, or mediatised family. The title of <i><a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrst" title="Fürst">Fürst</a></i> was usually reserved, from the 19th century, for rulers of principalities—the smallest sovereign entities (e.g., Liechtenstein, Lippe, Schwarzburg, Waldeck-and-Pyrmont)—and for heads of high-ranking, noble but non-ruling families (Bismarck, Clary und Aldringen, Dietrichstein, Henckel von Donnersmarck, Kinsky, Paar, Pless, Thun und Hohenstein, etc.). Cadets of these latter families were generally not allowed to use <i>Prinz</i>, being accorded only the style of count (<i><a href="/wiki/Graf" title="Graf">Graf</a></i>) or, occasionally, that of <i>Fürst</i> (Wrede, <a href="/wiki/House_of_Urach" title="House of Urach">Urach</a>) even though it was also a ruling title. Exceptional use of <i>Prinz</i> was permitted for some <a href="/wiki/Morganatic_marriage" title="Morganatic marriage">morganatic</a> families (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Battenberg_family" title="Battenberg family">Battenberg</a>, Montenuovo) and a few others (Carolath-Beuthen, Biron von Kurland).
<ul><li>In particular, <b><a href="/wiki/Crown_prince" title="Crown prince">Crown prince</a></b> (<i>Kronprinz</i> in German) was reserved for the heir apparent of an emperor or king</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Ban_(title)" title="Ban (title)">Ban</a></b>, noble title used in several states in Central and Southeastern Europe between the 7th century and the 20th century.</li></ul></li>

<h4><span id="Usages_of_the_titles_of_Grand_Duke.2C_Duke_and_Prince"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Usages_of_the_titles_of_Grand_Duke,_Duke_and_Prince">Usages of the titles of Grand Duke, Duke and Prince</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial,_royal_and_noble_ranks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Usages of the titles of Grand Duke, Duke and Prince">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In all European countries, the sovereign Grand Duke (or Grand Prince in some eastern European languages) is considered the third-highest monarchic title in precedence, after Emperor and King.
</p><p>In Germany, a sovereign Duke (<i>Herzog</i>) outranks a sovereign prince (<i>Fürst</i>). A <a href="/wiki/Cadet" title="Cadet">cadet</a> prince (<i>Prinz</i>) who belongs to an imperial or royal dynasty, however, may outrank a duke who is the cadet of a reigning house, e.g., <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Wurttemberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of Wurttemberg">Wurttemberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mecklenburg-Schwerin" class="mw-redirect" title="Mecklenburg-Schwerin">Mecklenburg</a> or <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Oldenburg" title="Grand Duchy of Oldenburg">Oldenburg</a>.
</p><p>The children of a sovereign Grand Duke may be titled "Prince" (Luxembourg, Tuscany, Baden, Hesse-Darmstadt, Saxe-Weimar) or "Duke" (Oldenburg) in accordance with the customs of the dynasty. The heir of the throne of a Grand Duchy is titled "Hereditary Grand Duke", as soon as he reaches the full legal age (majority).
</p><p>Children of a sovereign (i.e., ruling) Duke and of a ruling Prince (<i>Fürst</i>) were, however, all titled prince (<i>Prinz</i>).
</p><p>The heir apparent to a ruling or mediatised title would usually prepend the prefix <i>Erb-</i> (hereditary) to his or her title, e.g., <i><b>Erb</b>herzog</i>, <i><b>Erb</b>prinz</i>, <i><b>Erb</b>graf</i>, to distinguish their status from that of their junior siblings.
</p><p>Children of a <a href="/wiki/German_mediatisation" title="German mediatisation">mediatised</a> <i>Fürst</i> were either <i>Prinzen</i> or <i>Grafen</i> (counts), depending upon whether the princely title was limited to descent by masculine <a href="/wiki/Primogeniture" title="Primogeniture">primogeniture</a> or not. In the German non-sovereign nobility, a Duke (<i>Herzog</i>) still ranked higher than a Prince (<i>Fürst</i>).
</p>
<h3><span id="Minor_nobility.2C_gentry.2C_and_other_aristocracy"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Minor_nobility,_gentry,_and_other_aristocracy">Minor nobility, gentry, and other aristocracy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial,_royal_and_noble_ranks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Minor nobility, gentry, and other aristocracy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy_(class)" title="Aristocracy (class)">Aristocracy (class)</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gentry" title="Gentry">Gentry</a></div>
<p>The distinction between the ranks of the major nobility (listed above) and the minor nobility, listed here, was not always a sharp one in all nations. But the precedence of the ranks of a Baronet or a Knight is quite generally accepted for where this distinction exists for most nations. Here the rank of Baronet (ranking above a Knight) is taken as the highest rank among the ranks of the minor nobility or gentry that are listed below.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Titles_2">Titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial,_royal_and_noble_ranks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul><li><b><a href="/wiki/Baronet" title="Baronet">Baronet</a></b> is a hereditary title ranking below Baron but above Knight; this title is granted only in the British Isles and does not confer nobility. Ritter in German lands is the equivalent.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Dominus_(title)" title="Dominus (title)">Dominus</a></b> was the Latin title of the feudal, superior and mesne, lords, and also an ecclesiastical and academical title (equivalent of <a href="/wiki/Lord" title="Lord">Lord</a>)</li>

<li><b><a href="/wiki/Seigneur" title="Seigneur">Seigneur</a></b> or <b><a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_manor" title="Lord of the manor">Lord of the manor</a></b> rules a smaller local fief</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">Knight</a></b> is the central rank of the Medieval aristocratic system in Europe (and having its equivalents elsewhere), usually ranking at or near the top of the Minor Nobility</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Patrician_(post-Roman_Europe)" title="Patrician (post-Roman Europe)">Patrician</a></b> is a dignity of minor nobility or gentry (most often being hereditary) usually ranking below Knight but above Esquire</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Fidalgo" title="Fidalgo">Fidalgo</a></b> or <b><a href="/wiki/Hidalgo_(Spanish_nobility)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hidalgo (Spanish nobility)">Hidalgo</a></b> is a minor Portuguese and Spanish aristocrat (respectively; from <i>filho d'algo</i> / <i>hijo de algo</i>, lit. "son of something")</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Nobile_(aristocracy)" title="Nobile (aristocracy)">Nobile</a></b> is an Italian title of nobility for prestigious families that never received a title</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Edler" title="Edler">Edler</a></b> is a minor aristocrat in Germany and Austria during those countries' respective imperial periods.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Jonkheer" title="Jonkheer">Jonkheer</a></b> is an <a href="/wiki/Honorific" title="Honorific">honorific</a> for members of noble Dutch families that never received a title. An untitled noblewoman is styled <i>Jonkvrouw</i>, though the wife of a <i>Jonkheer</i> is a <i>Mevrouw</i> or, sometimes, <i>Freule</i>, which could also be used by daughters of the same.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Junker" title="Junker">Junker</a></b> is a German noble honorific, meaning "young nobleman" or otherwise "young lord"</li>

However, Scottish Barons on the European continent are considered and treated equal to European barons.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Laird" title="Laird">Laird</a></b> is a Scottish hereditary feudal dignity ranking below a Scottish Baron but above an Esquire</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Esquire" title="Esquire">Esquire</a></b> is a rank of <a href="/wiki/Gentry" title="Gentry">gentry</a> originally derived from <a href="/wiki/Squire" title="Squire">Squire</a> and indicating the status of an attendant to a knight, an apprentice knight, or a <a href="/wiki/Manorialism" title="Manorialism">manorial</a> lord;<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> it ranks below Knight (or in Scotland below Laird) but above <a href="/wiki/Gentleman" title="Gentleman">Gentleman</a><sup id="cite_ref-esquire_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-esquire-21">&#91;d&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-usesquire_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usesquire-22">&#91;e&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Gentleman" title="Gentleman">Gentleman</a></b> is the basic rank of <a href="/wiki/Gentry" title="Gentry">gentry</a> (ranking below Esquire), historically primarily associated with land; within British Commonwealth nations it is also roughly equivalent to some minor nobility of some continental European nations<sup id="cite_ref-lawrence_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lawrence-23">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></li>

<li><b><a href="/wiki/Lalla_(title)" title="Lalla (title)">Lalla</a></b>, is an Amazigh <a href="/wiki/Title_of_respect" class="mw-redirect" title="Title of respect">title of respect</a>. The title is a prefix to her <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> or <a href="/wiki/Personal_name" title="Personal name">personal name</a>, and is used by females usually of noble or royal background.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Sidi" title="Sidi">Sidi</a></b>, is a masculine title of respect, meaning "my master" in <a href="/wiki/Darija" class="mw-redirect" title="Darija">Darija</a> and <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_Arabic" title="Egyptian Arabic">Egyptian Arabic</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Qanungoh_Shaikh" title="Qanungoh Shaikh">Qanungoh Shaikh</a></b>, are a clan of Muslim Shaikhs in Punjab, other parts of Pakistan, and Afghanistan.</li></ul>
<p>In Germany, the constitution of the <a href="/wiki/Weimar_Republic" title="Weimar Republic">Weimar Republic</a> in 1919 ceased to accord privileges to members of dynastic and noble families. Their titles henceforth became legal parts of the family name, and traditional forms of address (e.g., "Hoheit" or "<a href="/wiki/Durchlaucht" class="mw-redirect" title="Durchlaucht">Durchlaucht</a>") ceased to be accorded to them by governmental entities. The last title was conferred on 12 November 1918 to Kurt von Klefeld. The actual rank of a title-holder in Germany depended not only on the nominal rank of the title, but also the degree of <a href="/wiki/Sovereignty" title="Sovereignty">sovereignty</a> exercised, the rank of the title-holder's <a href="/wiki/Suzerain" class="mw-redirect" title="Suzerain">suzerain</a>, and the length of time the family possessed its status within the nobility (<i>Uradel, Briefadel, altfürstliche, neufürstliche</i>, see: <a href="/wiki/German_nobility" title="German nobility">German nobility</a>). Thus, any reigning sovereign ranks higher than any <a href="/wiki/Abolished_monarchy" class="mw-redirect" title="Abolished monarchy">deposed</a> or <a href="/wiki/German_Mediatisation" class="mw-redirect" title="German Mediatisation">mediatized</a> sovereign (e.g., the <i><a href="/wiki/F%C3%BCrst" title="Fürst">Fürst</a></i> of <a href="/wiki/Waldeck_(state)" class="mw-redirect" title="Waldeck (state)">Waldeck</a>, sovereign until 1918, was higher than the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Arenberg" class="mw-redirect" title="Duke of Arenberg">Duke of Arenberg</a>, head of a mediatized family, although <i><a href="/wiki/Herzog" title="Herzog">Herzog</a></i> is nominally a higher title than <i>Fürst</i>). However, former holders of higher titles in extant monarchies retained their relative rank, i.e., a queen dowager of Belgium outranks the reigning Prince of Liechtenstein. Members of a formerly sovereign or mediatized house rank higher than the nobility. Among the nobility, those whose titles derive from the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a> rank higher than the holder of an equivalent title granted by one of the German monarchs after 1806.
</p><p>In Austria, nobility titles may no longer be used since 1918.<sup id="cite_ref-aut_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aut-24">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<li><b><a href="/wiki/Amir-al-Umara" class="mw-redirect" title="Amir-al-Umara">Amir-al-Umara</a></b>: It means the Head of the Amirs and was a title conferred upon <a href="/wiki/Mahadaji_Shinde" title="Mahadaji Shinde">Shrimant Maharaja Mahadaji Shinde (Scindia)</a> by the Mughals, since he helped the Mughal Emperor, Shah Alam II, ascend the throne of Delhi.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-36">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Shrimant</b>: It is a title used by Maratha royals and nobles. It was also used in recent times to formally address well achieved members of society or upperclass men in <a href="/wiki/Marathi_language" title="Marathi language">Marathi</a>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Baji_Rao_I" title="Baji Rao I">'Shrimant' Bajirao Peshwa aka Baji Rao I</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dnyaneshwar_Agashe" title="Dnyaneshwar Agashe">'Shrimant' Dnyaneshwar Agashe</a>.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Sardar" title="Sardar">Sardar</a></b>: It is a title used by the most senior Mahratta nobles,<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> for example <a href="/wiki/Ranoji_Scindia" title="Ranoji Scindia">Shrimant 'Sardar' Ranoji Rao Scindia Bahadur, Subedar of Malwa</a></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Mankari" title="Mankari">Mankari</a></b>: Mānkari (Maankari)  is a hereditary title used by <a href="/wiki/Maratha" title="Maratha">Maratha</a> <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">nobles</a> who held land grants, and cash allowances.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> They were entitled to certain ceremonial honours and held an official position at the <a href="/wiki/Durbar_(court)" title="Durbar (court)">Darbar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Royal_court" title="Royal court">court</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-google_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-google-39">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Sawai_(title)" title="Sawai (title)">Sawai</a></b>: 'Sawai' in Marathi means 'a notch above the rest'. For example, it was a title conferred upon <a href="/wiki/Madhavrao_II" title="Madhavrao II">His Highness Shrimant Sawai Madhavrao Peshwa aka Madhu Rao II Narayan</a></li>
<li><b>Pant Pratinidhi</b>: It means a <a href="/wiki/Vicegerent" title="Vicegerent">vicegerent</a>; title borne by a distinguished <a href="/wiki/Maratha_Empire" title="Maratha Empire">Maratha</a> family.<sup id="cite_ref-books.google.co.in_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-books.google.co.in-33">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Nawab" title="Nawab">Nawab</a></b>: It is a title used by the Nawabs of Banda (a vassal of <a href="/wiki/Maratha_Empire" title="Maratha Empire">Maratha polity</a>), such as <a href="/wiki/Ali_Bahadur" title="Ali Bahadur">The Nawab of Banda, Ali Bahadur</a>, the grandson of <a href="/wiki/Bajirao_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Bajirao I">Shreemant Bajirao I</a><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperial,_royal_and_noble_ranks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: lower-alpha;">
<div class="mw-references-wrap mw-references-columns"><ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-sov-4"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-sov_4-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-sov_4-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">Loss of sovereignty or <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fief</a> does not necessarily lead to loss of title. The position in the ranking table is however accordingly adjusted. The occurrence of fiefs has changed from time to time, and from country to country. For instance, dukes in England rarely had a duchy to rule.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-11"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-11">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">A duke who is not actually or formerly sovereign, or a member of a reigning or formerly reigning dynasty, such as British, French, Portuguese, Spanish and most Italian dukes, is a non-dynastic noble ranking above a marquis.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-19"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-19">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">There are actually three Scottish dignities that are types of a Scottish Baron; these are (in descending order of rank): <a href="/wiki/Feudal_earldom" title="Feudal earldom">Scottish feudal Earl</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_feudal_lordship" title="Scottish feudal lordship">Scottish Feudal Lord</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Scottish_feudal_barony" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish feudal barony">Scottish feudal Baron</a> (the general name for the dignity listed above among the ranks of aristocratic gentry).</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-esquire-21"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-esquire_21-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The meaning of the title Esquire became (and remains) quite diffuse, and may indicate anything from no aristocratic status, to some official government civil appointment, or (more historically) the son of a knight or noble who had no other title above just <a href="/wiki/Gentleman" title="Gentleman">Gentleman</a>.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-usesquire-22"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-usesquire_22-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In the United States, where there is no nobility, the title esquire is sometimes arrogated (without any governmental authorization) by lawyers admitted to the state bar.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-prinz-44"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-prinz_44-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-prinz_44-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-prinz_44-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-prinz_44-3"><sup><i><b>d</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">"Prince" (<i>Prinz</i> in German, <i>Prins</i> in Swedish, <i>Prinssi</i> in Finnish, "Principe" in Spanish) can also be a title of junior members of royal houses. In the British system, for example, <i>prince</i> is not a rank of nobility but a title held exclusively by members of the royal family.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-bart-45"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-bart_45-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Does not confer nobility in the British system.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-gbkn-46"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-gbkn_46-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Non-hereditary. Does not confer nobility in the British system. See also <a href="/wiki/Squire" title="Squire">squire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Esquire" title="Esquire">esquire</a>.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-latin-47"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-latin_47-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Latin titles are for etymological comparisons.  They do not accurately reflect their medieval counterparts.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-48"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-48">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The title <i>Markýz</i> was not used in Bohemia and thus referred only to foreign nobility, while the title <i>Markrabě</i> (the same as the German <i>Markgraf</i>) is connected only to a few historical territories (including the former marches on the borders of the Holy Roman Empire, or Moravia).</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-49"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-49">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Finland accorded the noble ranks of <i>Ruhtinas</i>, <i>Kreivi</i>, <i>Vapaaherra</i> and <i>Aatelinen</i>. The titles <i>Suurherttua</i>, <i>Arkkiherttua</i>, <i>Vaaliruhtinas</i>, <i>Prinssi</i>, <i>Markiisi</i>, <i>Jaarli</i>, <i>Varakreivi</i>, <i>Paroni</i>, and <i>Baronetti</i> were not granted in Finland, though they are used of foreign titleholders. <i>Keisari</i>, <i>Kuningas</i>, <i>Suuriruhtinas</i>, <i>Prinssi</i>, and <i>Herttua</i> have been used as official titles of members of the dynasties that ruled Finland, though not granted as titles of nobility.  Some feudally-based privileges in landowning, connected to nobily related <a href="/wiki/Lordship" class="mw-redirect" title="Lordship">lordship</a>, existed into the nineteenth century; and <a href="/wiki/Fief" title="Fief">fiefs</a> were common in the late medieval and early modern eras.  The title <i>Ritari</i> was not commonly used except in the context of knightly orders. The lowest, untitled level of hereditary nobility was that of the "Aatelinen" (i.e. "noble").</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-ohsix-50"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-ohsix_50-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-ohsix_50-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-ohsix_50-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-ohsix_50-3"><sup><i><b>d</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-ohsix_50-4"><sup><i><b>e</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-ohsix_50-5"><sup><i><b>f</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-ohsix_50-6"><sup><i><b>g</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">No noble titles were granted after 1906 when the unicameral legislatures (<a href="/wiki/Eduskunta" class="mw-redirect" title="Eduskunta">Eduskunta</a>) were established, removing the constitutional status of the so-called <a href="/wiki/Estates_of_the_realm" title="Estates of the realm">First Estate</a>. However, noble ranks were granted in Finland until 1917 (there, the lowest, untitled level of hereditary nobility was "Aatelinen", or "noble"; it was in essence a rank, not a title).</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-51"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-51">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">central Europe</a>, the title of <i>Fürst</i> or <i>kníže</i> (e.g. Fürst von Liechtenstein) ranks below the title of a duke (e.g. Duke of Brunswick). The title of <i>Vizegraf</i> was not used in German-speaking countries, and the titles of <i>Ritter</i> and <i>Edler</i> were not commonly used.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-52"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-52">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In the <a href="/wiki/German_comital_titles" class="mw-redirect" title="German comital titles">German system</a> by rank approximately equal to <i>Landgraf</i> and <i>Pfalzgraf</i>.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-53"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-53">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The "vitéz" title was introduced in Hungary after 1920. In preceding ages simply meant a warrior or a courageous man.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-54"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-54">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In keeping with the principle of equality among noblemen, no noble titles (with few exceptions) below that of prince were allowed in Poland. The titles in <i>italics</i> are simply Polish translations of western titles which were granted to some Polish nobles by foreign monarchs, especially after the <a href="/wiki/Partitions_of_Poland" title="Partitions of Poland">partitions</a>. Instead of hereditary titles, the Polish nobility developed and used <a href="/wiki/Offices_in_Polish-Lithuanian_Commonwealth" class="mw-redirect" title="Offices in Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth">a set of titles based on offices held</a>.  See "<a href="/wiki/Szlachta" title="Szlachta">szlachta</a>" for more info on Polish nobility.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-conde-55"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-conde_55-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In Portugal, a baron or viscount who was a "<i>grandee</i> of the kingdom" (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <i lang="pt">Grandes do Reino</i>) was called a "baron with grandness" (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <i lang="pt">Barão com Grandeza</i>) or "viscount with grandness" (<a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>: <i lang="pt">Visconde com Grandeza</i>); each of these <i>grandees</i> was ranked as equal to a count.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-russ-56"><span class="mw-cite-backlink">^ <a href="#cite_ref-russ_56-0"><sup><i><b>a</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-russ_56-1"><sup><i><b>b</b></i></sup></a> <a href="#cite_ref-russ_56-2"><sup><i><b>c</b></i></sup></a></span> <span class="reference-text">For domestic Russian nobility, only the titles <i>Kniaz</i> and <i>Boyar</i> were used before the 18th century, when <i>Graf</i> was added.</span>
</li>
</ol></div></div>
