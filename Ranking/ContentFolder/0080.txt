<p>The <b>peerage in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a></b> is a legal system comprising both hereditary and lifetime <a href="/wiki/Titles" class="mw-redirect" title="Titles">titles</a>, composed of various <a href="/wiki/British_nobility" title="British nobility">noble</a> ranks, and forming a constituent part of the <a href="/wiki/British_honours_system" class="mw-redirect" title="British honours system">British honours system</a>. The term <i>peerage</i> can be used both collectively to refer to the entire body of <a href="/wiki/Noble_title" class="mw-redirect" title="Noble title">nobles</a> (or a subdivision thereof), and individually to refer to a specific title (modern <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">English language</a>-style using an initial capital in the former case but not the latter). British peerage title holders are termed <a href="/wiki/Peer_of_the_realm" title="Peer of the realm">peers of the Realm</a>.
</p><p>Peerages are created by the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Monarchy of the United Kingdom">British monarch</a>, like all Crown honours, being affirmed by <a href="/wiki/Letters_Patent" class="mw-redirect" title="Letters Patent">Letters Patent</a> affixed with the <a href="/wiki/Great_Seal_of_the_Realm" title="Great Seal of the Realm">Great Seal of the Realm</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">government of the United Kingdom</a> recommends to the Sovereign who should be elevated to the peerage, after external vetting by the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords_Appointments_Commission" title="House of Lords Appointments Commission">House of Lords Appointments Commission</a>. Under present custom, the only new hereditary peerages granted are to members of the <a href="/wiki/British_royal_family" title="British royal family">royal family</a>; the last non-royal awardees of hereditary titles were in the Thatcher era.  Since then, <a href="/wiki/Government_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Government of the United Kingdom">ruling parties</a> have refrained from recommending any others to be elevated.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/New_Labour" title="New Labour">New Labour</a>, elected to power in 1997, sought to remove all the seats in the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a> reserved for hereditary peers, but Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a> relented by allowing <a href="/wiki/List_of_elected_hereditary_peers_under_the_House_of_Lords_Act_1999" title="List of elected hereditary peers under the House of Lords Act 1999">92 members</a> to remain by legislation enacted in 1999.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The House of Lords' purpose is now that of a revising legislative chamber, scrutinising and potentially changing proposed Parliamentary Bills before their enactment. Its membership for the most part comprises <a href="/wiki/Life_peer" title="Life peer">life peers</a>, created under the <a href="/wiki/Life_Peerages_Act_1958" title="Life Peerages Act 1958">Life Peerages Act 1958</a>, which includes those who can add value in specific areas of expertise in parliamentary debates, as well as former MPs and other political appointees from respective political parties.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/British_monarchy#Constitutional_role" class="mw-redirect" title="British monarchy">Sovereign</a>, traditionally the <a href="/wiki/Fount_of_honour" title="Fount of honour">fount of honour</a>, cannot hold a British peerage<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> (although the <a href="/wiki/British_Sovereign" class="mw-redirect" title="British Sovereign">British Sovereign</a>, whether male or female, is informally accorded the style of "<a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Lancaster" title="Duke of Lancaster">Duke of Lancaster</a>"). All British subjects who were neither Royal nor Peers of the Realm were previously termed Commoners, regardless of wealth or other social factors, thus all members of a peer's family are (technically) commoners too; the British system thus differs fundamentally from continental European versions, where entire families, rather than individuals, were <a href="/wiki/Ennobled" class="mw-redirect" title="Ennobled">ennobled</a>. Nobility in Britain is based on title rather than bloodline, and correspondingly <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Princess_Royal" title="Anne, Princess Royal">The Princess Royal (Princess Anne)</a> who enjoys Royal status as daughter of The Queen, opted for her children to be Commoners by refusing offers of titles, despite their being grandchildren of the Sovereign (<i>qv.</i> <a href="/wiki/Peter_Phillips" title="Peter Phillips">Peter Phillips</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zara_Tindall" title="Zara Tindall">Zara Tindall</a>).
</p><p>Certain personal privileges are afforded to all peers and peeresses, but the main distinction of a peerage nowadays, apart from access to the House of Lords for life peers and some hereditary peers, is the title and style thereby accorded. Succession claims to existing hereditary peerages are regulated by the House of Lords <a href="/wiki/Committee_for_Privileges_and_Conduct" title="Committee for Privileges and Conduct">Committee for Privileges and Conduct</a> and administered by The Crown Office.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Baronage_evolution">Baronage evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peerages_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Baronage evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Baronage" title="Baronage">Baronage</a></div>
<p>The modern-day parliamentary peerage is a continuation of the renamed medieval <a href="/wiki/Baronage" title="Baronage">baronage</a> system which existed in <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudal</a> times. The requirement of attending Parliament was both a liability and a privilege for those who held land as a <a href="/wiki/Tenant-in-chief" title="Tenant-in-chief">tenant-in-chief</a> from the <a href="/wiki/King" title="King">King</a> <i>per baroniam</i> – that is to say, under the feudal contract wherein a King's <a href="/wiki/Baron" title="Baron">Baron</a> was responsible for raising knights and troops for the royal military service. Certain other <a href="/wiki/Official" title="Official">office-holders</a> such as senior <a href="/wiki/Cleric" class="mw-redirect" title="Cleric">clerics</a> and Freemen of the <a href="/wiki/Cinque_Ports" title="Cinque Ports">Cinque Ports</a> were deemed barons.
</p><p>This right, entitlement or "title", began to be granted by decree in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Writ" title="Writ">Writ</a> of Summons from 1265 and by <a href="/wiki/Letters_Patent" class="mw-redirect" title="Letters Patent">Letters Patent</a> from 1388. Additionally, many holders of smaller fiefdoms <i>per baroniam</i> ceased to be summoned to <a href="/wiki/English_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="English Parliament">parliament</a>, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Baron" title="Baron">baronial status</a> becoming personal rather than territorial. Feudal baronies had always been hereditable by <a href="/wiki/Primogeniture" title="Primogeniture">primogeniture</a>, but on condition of payment of a fine, termed "<a href="/wiki/Feudal_relief" title="Feudal relief">relief</a>", derived from the Latin verb <i>levo</i> to lift up, meaning a "re-elevation" to a former position of honour. Baronies and other titles of nobility became unconditionally hereditable on the abolition of feudal tenure by the <a href="/wiki/Tenures_Abolition_Act_1660" title="Tenures Abolition Act 1660">Tenures Abolition Act of 1660</a>, and non-hereditable titles began to be created in 1876 for Law Lords, and in 1958 for Life Peers.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Peerage_divisions">Peerage divisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peerages_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Peerage divisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the UK, five <a href="/wiki/Peerage" title="Peerage">peerages</a> co-exist, namely:
</p>
<ul><li>The <a href="/wiki/Peerage_of_England" title="Peerage of England">Peerage of England</a> — titles created by the Kings and Queens of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> before the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Union_1707" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Union 1707">Act of Union in 1707</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Peerage_of_Scotland" title="Peerage of Scotland">Peerage of Scotland</a> — titles created by the Kings and Queens of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> before 1707.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Peerage_of_Ireland" title="Peerage of Ireland">Peerage of Ireland</a> — titles created for the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Ireland" title="Kingdom of Ireland">Kingdom of Ireland</a> before the <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Union_1800" class="mw-redirect" title="Act of Union 1800">Act of Union of 1801</a>, and some titles created later.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Peerage_of_Great_Britain" title="Peerage of Great Britain">Peerage of Great Britain</a> — titles created for the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Great_Britain" title="Kingdom of Great Britain">Kingdom of Great Britain</a> between 1707 and 1801.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Peerage_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Peerage of the United Kingdom">Peerage of the United Kingdom</a> — most titles created since 1801 to the present.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ranks">Ranks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peerages_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ranks">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Peers are of five ranks, in descending order of hierarchy:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Duke" title="Duke">Duke</a> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> <i>dux</i>, leader. The first duke in a peerage of the British Isles was created in 1337. The feminine form is Duchess.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Marquess" title="Marquess">Marquess</a> comes from the French '<a href="/wiki/Marquess" title="Marquess">marquis</a>', which is a derivative of 'marche' or march. This is a reference to the borders ('<a href="/wiki/Marches" class="mw-redirect" title="Marches">marches</a>') between England, Scotland, and Wales, a relationship more evident in the feminine form: Marchioness. The first marquess in a peerage of the British Isles was created in 1385. The feminine form is Marchioness.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">Earl</a> comes from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> or Anglo-Saxon 'eorl', a military leader. The meaning may have been affected by the <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">Old Norse</a> 'jarl', meaning free-born warrior or nobleman, during the <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">Danelaw</a>, thus giving rise to the modern sense. Since there was no feminine Old English or Old Norse equivalent for the term, 'Countess' is used (an Earl is analogous to the <a href="/wiki/Continental_Europe" title="Continental Europe">Continental</a> '<a href="/wiki/Count" title="Count">count</a>'), from the Latin 'comes'.  Created circa 800–1000.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Viscount" title="Viscount">Viscount</a> comes from the Latin 'vicecomes', vice-count. Created in 1440. The feminine form is Viscountess.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Baron" title="Baron">Baron</a> comes from the Old Germanic 'baro', freeman. Created in 1066. In the Peerage of Scotland alone, a holder of the fifth rank is not called a 'Baron' but rather a '<a href="/wiki/Lord_of_Parliament" title="Lord of Parliament">Lord of Parliament</a>'. <a href="/wiki/Barons_in_Scotland" title="Barons in Scotland">Barons in Scotland</a> were traditionally holders of feudal dignities, not peers, but they are considered minor barons and are recognized by the crown as noble. The feminine form is Baroness.</li></ul>
<p><a href="/wiki/Baronet" title="Baronet">Baronets</a>, while holders of <a href="/wiki/Hereditary_title" title="Hereditary title">hereditary titles</a>, are not <a href="/wiki/Peer_of_the_Realm" class="mw-redirect" title="Peer of the Realm">peers</a> since baronetcies have never conferred noble status, although socially they came to be regarded as part of the aristocracy. <a href="/wiki/Knights" class="mw-redirect" title="Knights">Knights</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dame_(title)" class="mw-redirect" title="Dame (title)">Dames</a> and holders of other British non-hereditary <a href="/wiki/Chivalric_orders" class="mw-redirect" title="Chivalric orders">chivalric orders</a>, decorations, and medals are likewise not peers.
</p><p>The titles of peers are in the form of '(Rank) (Name of Title)' or '(Rank) of (Name of Title)'. The name of the title can either be a place name or a surname. The precise usage depends on the rank of the peerage and on certain other general considerations. Dukes always use 'of'. Marquesses and Earls whose titles are based on place names normally use 'of', while those whose titles are based on surnames normally do not. Viscounts, Barons and Lords of Parliament generally do not use 'of'. However, there are several exceptions to the rule. For instance, Scottish vicecomital titles theoretically include 'of', though in practice it is usually dropped. (Thus, the 'Viscount of Falkland' is commonly known as the 'Viscount Falkland'.)
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_association">Geographic association</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peerages_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geographic association">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Territorial_designation" title="Territorial designation">territorial designation</a> is often added to the main peerage title, especially in the case of Barons and Viscounts: for instance, <i><a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Baroness Thatcher</a>, of Kesteven in the County of Lincolnshire</i>, or <i><a href="/wiki/Viscount_Montgomery_of_Alamein" title="Viscount Montgomery of Alamein">Viscount Montgomery of Alamein</a>, of Hindhead in the County of Surrey</i>. Any designation after the comma does not form a part of the main title. Territorial designations in titles are not updated with <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Local government in the United Kingdom">local government</a> reforms, but new creations do take them into account. Thus there is a <i><a href="/wiki/Viscount_Knollys" title="Viscount Knollys">Baron Knollys</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Caversham,_Berkshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Caversham, Berkshire">Caversham</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">County of Oxford</a></i> (created in 1902), and a <i><a href="/wiki/Jill_Pitkeathley,_Baroness_Pitkeathley" title="Jill Pitkeathley, Baroness Pitkeathley">Baroness Pitkeathley</a>, of <a href="/wiki/Caversham,_Berkshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Caversham, Berkshire">Caversham</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Royal County of Berkshire</a></i> (created in 1997).
</p><p>It was once the case that a peer administered the place associated with his title (such as an Earl administering a County as <a href="/wiki/High_Sheriff" class="mw-redirect" title="High Sheriff">High Sheriff</a> or main landowner), but lordships by tenure have not been commonplace since the early <a href="/wiki/Norman_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman period">Norman period</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The only remaining peerages with certain associated <a href="/wiki/Land_law" title="Land law">rights over land</a> are the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Cornwall" title="Duchy of Cornwall">Duchy of Cornwall</a> (place), which appertains to the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Cornwall" title="Duke of Cornwall">Dukedom of Cornwall</a>, held by the eldest son and heir to the Sovereign, and the <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Lancaster" title="Duchy of Lancaster">Duchy of Lancaster</a> (place), which regular income (revenue) appertains to the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Lancaster" title="Duke of Lancaster">Dukedom of Lancaster</a>, held by the Sovereign whose government owns the capital and all capital gains on disposals. In both cases due to the particular function of  <a href="/wiki/Bona_vacantia" title="Bona vacantia">bona vacantia</a> in these areas, these titles afford rights encompassing the whole territorial designation of the holder, donated by the holder now to registered charities. Separate estates, smaller than counties, form the bulk of the two duchies.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_peers">Types of peers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peerages_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Types of peers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hereditary_peers">Hereditary peers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peerages_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hereditary peers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hereditary_peer" title="Hereditary peer">Hereditary peer</a></div>
<p>An hereditary peer is a peer of the realm whose dignity may be inherited; those able to inherit it are said to be "in remainder". Hereditary peerage dignities may be created with <a href="/wiki/Hereditary_peer#Writs_of_summons" title="Hereditary peer">writs of summons</a> or by <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">letters patent</a>; the former method is now obsolete. Writs of summons summon an individual to Parliament, in the old <a href="/wiki/Feudal" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal">feudal</a> tradition, and merely <i>implied</i> the existence or creation of an hereditary peerage dignity, which is automatically inherited, presumably according to the traditional medieval rules (male-preference <a href="/wiki/Primogeniture" title="Primogeniture">primogeniture</a>, like the succession of the British crown until 2011). Letters patent explicitly create a dignity and specify its course of inheritance (usually <a href="/wiki/Agnatic_primogeniture" class="mw-redirect" title="Agnatic primogeniture">agnatic</a> succession, like the <a href="/wiki/Salic_Law" class="mw-redirect" title="Salic Law">Salic Law</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Some hereditary titles can pass through and vest in female heirs in a system called <a href="/wiki/Coparcenary" class="mw-redirect" title="Coparcenary">coparcenary</a>.
</p><p>Once created, a peerage dignity continues to exist as long as there are surviving legitimate descendants (or legitimate agnatic descendants) of the first holder, unless a contrary method of descent is specified in the letters patent. Once the heirs of the original peer die out, the peerage dignity becomes extinct. In former times, peerage dignities were often <i>forfeit</i> by Acts of Parliament, usually when peers were found guilty of <a href="/wiki/Treason" title="Treason">treason</a>. Often, however, the felonious peer's descendants successfully petitioned the Sovereign to restore the dignity to the family. Some dignities, such as the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Norfolk" title="Duke of Norfolk">Dukedom of Norfolk</a>, have been forfeit and restored several times. Under the <a href="/wiki/Peerage_Act_1963" title="Peerage Act 1963">Peerage Act 1963</a> an individual can <a href="/wiki/Disclaimer_of_interest" title="Disclaimer of interest">disclaim</a> his peerage dignity for his own lifetime within one year of inheriting it.
</p><p>When the holder of a peerage succeeds to the throne, the dignity "merges in the Crown" and ceases to exist.
</p><p>All hereditary peers in the Peerages of England, Scotland, Great Britain, and the United Kingdom were entitled to sit in the House of Lords, subject only to qualifications such as age and citizenship, but under section 1 of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords_Act_1999" title="House of Lords Act 1999">House of Lords Act 1999</a> they lost this right. The Act provided that 92 hereditary peers — the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Great_Chamberlain" title="Lord Great Chamberlain">Lord Great Chamberlain</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Earl_Marshal" title="Earl Marshal">Earl Marshal</a>, along with 90 others exempted through <a href="/wiki/Rules_of_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Rules of order">standing orders</a> of the House — would remain in the House of Lords in the interim,<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> pending any reform of the membership to the House. Standing Order 9 provides that those exempted are 75 hereditary peers elected by other peers from and by respective party groups in the House in proportion to their numbers, and fifteen chosen by the whole House to serve as officers of the House.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Representative_peers">Representative peers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peerages_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Representative peers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Representative_peer" title="Representative peer">Representative peer</a></div>
<p>From 1707 until 1963 <a href="/wiki/Peerage_of_Scotland" title="Peerage of Scotland">Scottish peers</a> elected 16 <a href="/wiki/Representative_peer" title="Representative peer">representative peers</a> to sit in the House of Lords.  Since 1963 they have had the same rights as Peers of the United Kingdom. From 1801 until 1922 <a href="/wiki/Peerage_of_Ireland" title="Peerage of Ireland">Irish peers</a> elected 28 representative peers to sit in the House of Lords.  Since 1922, when the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Irish Free State</a> became a separate country, no Irish representative peers have been elected, though sitting members retained their seats for life.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_peers">Life peers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peerages_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Life peers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Life_peer" title="Life peer">Life peer</a></div>
<p>Apart from hereditary peerages, there exists peerages that may be held for life and whose title cannot be passed onto someone else by inheritance. The <a href="/wiki/Appellate_Jurisdiction_Act_1876" title="Appellate Jurisdiction Act 1876">Appellate Jurisdiction Act 1876</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Life_Peerages_Act_1958" title="Life Peerages Act 1958">Life Peerages Act 1958</a> authorise the regular creation of life peerages, with the right to sit in the House of Lords. Life peers created under both acts are of baronial rank and are always created under letters patent.
</p><p>Since the loss of the right of hereditary peers to sit in the House of Lords as a result of the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords_Act_1999" title="House of Lords Act 1999">House of Lords Act 1999</a>, the majority of the House of Lords is made up of life peers. There is no limit on the number of peerages the sovereign may create under the Life Peerages Act. Normally life peerages are granted to individuals nominated by political parties or by the House of Lords Appointments Commission, and to honour important public figures such as the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishop of Canterbury</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Prime Minister of the United Kingdom">Prime Minister</a> on their retirement.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Until the formal opening of the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Supreme Court of the United Kingdom">Supreme Court of the United Kingdom</a> on 1 October 2009, life peers created under the Appellate Jurisdiction Act were known as "Lords of Appeal in Ordinary" or in common parlance "Law Lords". They performed the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_functions_of_the_House_of_Lords" title="Judicial functions of the House of Lords">judicial functions of the House of Lords</a> and served on the <a href="/wiki/Judicial_Committee_of_the_Privy_Council" title="Judicial Committee of the Privy Council">Judicial Committee of the Privy Council</a>. They remained peers for life, but ceased to receive judicial salaries at the age of 75. Under the terms of the Act, there may be no more than 12 Lords of Appeal in Ordinary under the age of 75 at one time. However, after the transfer of the judicial functions of the Lords to the Supreme Court of the United Kingdom, the Act ceased to have meaningful effect.
</p><p>Under the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords_Reform_Act_2014" title="House of Lords Reform Act 2014">House of Lords Reform Act 2014</a> and the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords_(Expulsion_and_Suspension)_Act_2015" title="House of Lords (Expulsion and Suspension) Act 2015">House of Lords (Expulsion and Suspension) Act 2015</a> a life peer may lose membership of the House of Lords permanently in one of four ways:
</p>
<ul><li>Resignation or retirement effected by writing to the <a href="/wiki/Clerk_of_the_Parliaments" title="Clerk of the Parliaments">Clerk of the Parliaments</a>;</li>
<li>Automatic expulsion through failing to attend a single sitting of the House throughout a whole session of more than six months duration without leave of absence, being suspended for that session or being exempted by the House for special circumstances;</li>
<li>Automatic expulsion through conviction of a criminal offence where the punishment is imprisonment for more than one year;</li>
<li>Expulsion by resolution of the House.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>While these provide for non-membership of the House of Lords, they do not allow a life peer to disclaim their peerage in the same way that a hereditary peer can disclaim theirs.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Styles_and_titles">Styles and titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peerages_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Styles and titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Forms_of_address_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Forms of address in the United Kingdom">Forms of address in the United Kingdom</a> and <a href="/wiki/Courtesy_title" title="Courtesy title">Courtesy title</a></div>
<p>Dukes use <i>His Grace</i>, Marquesses use <i>The Most Honourable</i> and other peers use <i>The Right Honourable</i>. Peeresses (whether they hold peerages in their own right or are wives of peers) use equivalent styles.
</p><p>In speech, any peer or peeress except a Duke or Duchess is referred to as <i>Lord X</i> or <i>Lady X</i>. The exception is a <i><a href="/wiki/Suo_jure" title="Suo jure">suo jure</a></i> baroness (that is, one holding the dignity in her own right, usually a life peeress), who may also be called <i>Baroness X</i> in normal speech, though <i>Lady X</i> is also common usage. Hence, <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Baroness Thatcher</a>, a <i>suo jure</i> life peeress, was referred to as either "Baroness Thatcher" or "Lady Thatcher". "Baroness" is incorrect for female holders of Scottish Lordships of Parliament, who are not Baronesses; for example, the <a href="/wiki/Flora_Fraser,_21st_Lady_Saltoun" title="Flora Fraser, 21st Lady Saltoun">21st Lady Saltoun</a> is known as "Lady Saltoun", not "Baroness Saltoun".
</p><p>A peer is referred to by his peerage even if it is the same as his surname, thus <a href="/wiki/David_Owen" title="David Owen">Baron Owen</a> is "Lord Owen" not "Lord David Owen", though such incorrect forms are commonly used.
</p><p>Some peers, particularly life peers who were well known before their ennoblement, do not use their peerage titles.  Others use a combination: for example, the author <a href="/wiki/John_Julius_Norwich" title="John Julius Norwich">John Julius Norwich</a> was John Julius Cooper, 2nd Viscount Norwich.
</p><p>Individuals who use the style <i>Lord</i> or <i>Lady</i> are not necessarily peers. Children of peers use special titles called <a href="/wiki/Courtesy_title" title="Courtesy title">courtesy titles</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Heir_apparent" title="Heir apparent">heir apparent</a> of a duke, a marquess, or an earl generally uses his father's highest lesser peerage dignity as his own. Hence, the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Devonshire" title="Duke of Devonshire">Duke of Devonshire</a>'s son is called <a href="/wiki/Marquess_of_Hartington" class="mw-redirect" title="Marquess of Hartington">Marquess of Hartington</a>. Such an heir apparent is called a <i>courtesy peer</i>, but is a commoner until such time as he inherits (unless summoned by a <a href="/wiki/Writ_in_acceleration" class="mw-redirect" title="Writ in acceleration">writ in acceleration</a>).
</p><p>Younger sons of dukes and marquesses prefix <i>Lord</i> to their first names as courtesy titles while daughters of dukes, marquesses and earls use <i>Lady</i>. Younger sons of earls and children of viscounts, barons and lords of Parliament use <i>The Honourable</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Precedence">Precedence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peerages_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Precedence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_precedence_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Orders of precedence in the United Kingdom">Orders of precedence in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>Peers are entitled to a special <a href="/wiki/Order_of_precedence" title="Order of precedence">precedence</a> because of their ranks. Wives and children of peers are also entitled to a special precedence because of their station.
</p><p>The Sovereign may, as <a href="/wiki/Fount_of_honour" title="Fount of honour">fount of honour</a>, vary the precedence of the peers or of any other people. For example, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabeth II of the United Kingdom">Elizabeth II</a> granted her husband, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Philip,_Duke_of_Edinburgh" title="Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh">Prince Philip, Duke of Edinburgh</a>, precedence immediately following her; otherwise, he would have ranked along with the other dukes of the peerage of the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-velde_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velde-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_precedence">General precedence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peerages_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: General precedence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In England and Wales, the Sovereign ranks first, followed by the Royal Family. Then follow the <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_Canterbury" title="Archbishop of Canterbury">Archbishops of Canterbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_York" title="Archbishop of York">York</a>, the Great Officers of State and other important state functionaries such as the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister" class="mw-redirect" title="Prime Minister">Prime Minister</a>. Thereafter, dukes precede <a href="/wiki/Marquess" title="Marquess">marquesses</a>, who precede <a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">earls</a>, who precede <a href="/wiki/Viscount" title="Viscount">viscounts</a>, who precede bishops, who precede barons and lords of Parliament.<sup id="cite_ref-velde_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velde-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Within the members of each rank of the peerage, peers of England precede peers of Scotland. English and Scottish peers together precede peers of Great Britain. All of the aforementioned precede peers of Ireland created before 1801. Last come peers of Ireland created after 1801 and peers of the United Kingdom. Among peers of the same rank and Peerage, precedence is based on the creation of the title: those whose titles were created earlier precede those whose titles were created later. But in no case would a peer of a lower rank precede one of a higher rank. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Fife" title="Duke of Fife">Duke of Fife</a>, the last non-royal to be created a duke, would come before the <a href="/wiki/Marquess_of_Winchester" title="Marquess of Winchester">Marquess of Winchester</a>, though the latter's title was created earlier and is in a more senior peerage (the peerage of England).<sup id="cite_ref-velde_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velde-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The place of a peer in the order for gentlemen is taken by his wife in the order for ladies, except that a Dowager peeress of a particular title precedes the present holder of the same title. Children of peers (and <i>suo jure</i> peeresses) also obtain a special precedence. The following algorithm may be used to determine their ranks:
</p>
<ul><li>Eldest sons of peers of rank X go after peers of rank X−1</li>
<li>Younger sons of peers of rank X go after eldest sons of peers of rank X−1</li>
<li>Wives have a precedence corresponding to those of their husbands</li>
<li>Daughters of peers of rank X go after wives of eldest sons of peers of rank X</li></ul>
<p>Over time, however, various offices were inserted at different points in the order, thereby varying it.<sup id="cite_ref-velde_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velde-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Eldest sons of dukes rank after marquesses; eldest sons of marquesses and then younger sons of dukes rank after earls; eldest sons of earls and then younger sons of marquesses rank after viscounts. Eldest sons of viscounts, younger sons of earls, and then eldest sons of barons, in that order, follow barons, with the <a href="/wiki/Treasurer_of_the_Household" title="Treasurer of the Household">Treasurer of the Household</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Comptroller_of_the_Household" title="Comptroller of the Household">Comptroller of the Household</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Vice-Chamberlain_of_the_Household" title="Vice-Chamberlain of the Household">Vice-Chamberlain of the Household</a> and Secretaries of State being interpolated between them and the barons. Younger sons of viscounts, and then younger sons of barons, come after the aforesaid eldest sons of barons, with Knights of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Garter" title="Order of the Garter">Order of the Garter</a> and <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Thistle" title="Order of the Thistle">Order of the Thistle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Privy_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Privy Council">Privy Councillors</a> and senior judges being intercalated between them and eldest sons of barons.<sup id="cite_ref-velde_10-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velde-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Children of the eldest son of a peer also obtain a special precedence. Generally, the eldest son of the eldest son of a peer comes immediately before his uncles, while the younger sons of the eldest son of a peer come after them. Therefore, eldest sons of eldest sons of dukes come before younger sons of dukes, and younger sons of eldest sons of dukes come after them, and so forth for all the ranks. Below the younger sons of barons are baronets, knights, circuit judges and companions of the various orders of Chivalry, followed by the eldest sons of younger sons of peers.<sup id="cite_ref-velde_10-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velde-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Wives of all of the aforementioned have precedence corresponding to their husbands', unless otherwise entitled to a higher precedence, for instance by virtue of holding a certain office. An individual's daughter takes precedence after the wife of that individual's eldest son and before the wives of that individual's younger sons. Therefore, daughters of peers rank immediately after wives of eldest sons of peers; daughters of eldest sons of peers rank immediately after wives of eldest sons of eldest sons of peers; daughters of younger sons of peers rank after wives of eldest sons of younger sons of peers. Such a daughter keeps her precedence if marrying a commoner (unless that marriage somehow confers a higher precedence), but rank as their husband if marrying a peer.<sup id="cite_ref-velde_10-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velde-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Precedence_within_Parliament">Precedence within Parliament</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peerages_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Precedence within Parliament">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The order of precedence used to determine seating in the House of Lords chamber is governed by the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords_Precedence_Act_1539" title="House of Lords Precedence Act 1539">House of Lords Precedence Act 1539</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Precedence as provided by the Act is similar to, but not the same as, the order outside Parliament. The Sovereign, however, does not have the authority to change the precedence assigned by the Act.<sup id="cite_ref-velde_10-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velde-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lords Temporal assume precedence similar to precedence outside Parliament. One difference in the precedence of peers relates to the positions of the Great Officers of State and the officers of the Sovereign's Household. Some Great Officers—the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor" title="Lord Chancellor">Lord Chancellor</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lord_High_Treasurer" title="Lord High Treasurer">Lord High Treasurer</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lord_President_of_the_Council" title="Lord President of the Council">Lord President of the Council</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Privy_Seal" title="Lord Privy Seal">Lord Privy Seal</a>—provided they are peers, rank before all other peers except those who are of the Blood Royal (no precedence is accorded if they are not peers). The positions of the other Great Officers—the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Great_Chamberlain" title="Lord Great Chamberlain">Lord Great Chamberlain</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Lord_High_Constable_of_England" title="Lord High Constable of England">Lord High Constable</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Earl_Marshal" title="Earl Marshal">Earl Marshal</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Admiralty" title="Admiralty">Lord High Admiral</a>—and the officers of the Household—the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Steward" title="Lord Steward">Lord Steward</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chamberlain" title="Lord Chamberlain">Lord Chamberlain</a>—are based on their respective ranks. Thus, if the Lord Steward were a duke, he would precede all dukes, if a marquess, he would precede all marquesses, and so on. If two such officers are of the same rank, the precedence of the offices (reflected by the order in which they are mentioned above) is taken into account: if the Lord Great Chamberlain and Earl Marshal were both marquesses, for example, then the Great Chamberlain would precede the Earl Marshal, as the former office precedes the latter.<sup id="cite_ref-velde_10-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-velde-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In practice, however, the Act is obsolete, as the Lords do not actually sit according to strict precedence; instead, peers sit with their political parties.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Privilege_of_peerage">Privilege of peerage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peerages_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Privilege of peerage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Privilege_of_peerage" title="Privilege of peerage">Privilege of peerage</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Privilege_of_peerage" title="Privilege of peerage">privilege of peerage</a> is the body of <a href="/wiki/Privilege_(legal_ethics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Privilege (legal ethics)">privileges</a> that belongs to peers, their wives and their unremarried widows. The privilege is distinct from <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_privilege" title="Parliamentary privilege">parliamentary privilege</a>, and applies to all peers, not just members of the House of Lords. It still exists, although "occasions of its exercise have now diminished into obscurity."<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated202_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated202-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although the extent of the privilege has been ill-defined, three features survived to the 20th century: the right to be tried by fellow peers in the <a href="/wiki/Lord_High_Steward" title="Lord High Steward">Lord High Steward</a>'s Court and in the House of Lords (abolished in 1948); the personal right of access to the Sovereign at any time, but this privilege has long been obsolete; and the right to be exempt from civil <a href="/wiki/Arrest" title="Arrest">arrest</a> (a privilege that has been used only twice since 1945<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>). All privileges of a peerage are lost if a peer disclaims his or her peerage under the <a href="/wiki/Peerage_Act_1963" title="Peerage Act 1963">Peerage Act 1963</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-autogenerated202_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autogenerated202-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Robes">Robes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peerages_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Robes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Robes_of_the_British_peerage" title="Robes of the British peerage">Robes of the British peerage</a></div>
<p>Peerage robes are currently worn in the United Kingdom by peers on certain ceremonial occasions and are of two varieties: Parliament robes, worn on ceremonial occasions in the House of Lords (such as at a peer's <a href="/wiki/Introduction_(House_of_Lords)" title="Introduction (House of Lords)">Introduction</a><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> and at the <a href="/wiki/State_opening_of_parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="State opening of parliament">state opening of parliament</a>), and Coronation robes, worn by peers at the coronations of monarchs. These robes differ slightly according to a peer's rank by identifying features that differ according to rank.
</p><p>Since the early Middle Ages, robes have been worn as a sign of nobility. At first, these seem to have been bestowed on individuals by the monarch or feudal lord as a sign of special recognition; but in the fifteenth century the use of robes became formalised with peers all wearing robes of the same design, though varied according to the rank of the wearer.<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Two distinct forms of robe emerged, and these remain in current use: one is worn for parliamentary occasions and the other is generally worn only at <a href="/wiki/Coronation_of_the_British_monarch" title="Coronation of the British monarch">coronations</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coronets_and_headgear">Coronets and headgear</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peerages_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Coronets and headgear">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United Kingdom, a peer wears his or her <a href="/wiki/Coronet" title="Coronet">coronet</a> on only one occasion: for the <a href="/wiki/Coronation_of_the_British_monarch" title="Coronation of the British monarch">monarch's coronation</a>, when it is worn along with coronation robes.
</p>
<ul><li>The coronet of a <a href="/wiki/Dukes_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Dukes in the United Kingdom">duke</a> (a silver-gilt circlet, <a href="/wiki/Repouss%C3%A9_and_chasing" title="Repoussé and chasing">chased</a> as jewelled but not actually gemmed) has eight <a href="/wiki/Strawberry" title="Strawberry">strawberry</a> leaves;</li>
<li>The coronet of a <a href="/wiki/Marquess" title="Marquess">marquess</a> has four strawberry leaves and four silver balls (known as "pearls", but not actually <a href="/wiki/Pearl" title="Pearl">pearls</a>), slightly raised on points above the rim;</li>
<li>The coronet of a <a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">earl</a> has eight strawberry leaves (four visible) and eight "pearls" raised on stalks;</li>
<li>The coronet of a <a href="/wiki/Viscount" title="Viscount">viscount</a> has sixteen "pearls" touching one another;</li>
<li>The coronet of a <a href="/wiki/Baron" title="Baron">baron</a> or Lord of Parliament in the Scots peerage (a plain silver-gilt circlet) has six "pearls".</li></ul>
<p>The robes and coronets used at Elizabeth II's coronation in 1953 cost about £1,250<sup id="cite_ref-arma_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arma-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> (roughly £34,300 in present-day terms).<sup id="cite_ref-inflation-UK_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-UK-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> (Peers under the rank of an Earl, however, were allowed in 1953 to wear a cheaper "cap of estate" in place of a coronet, as were peeresses of the same rank, for whom a simpler robe was also permitted (a one-piece gown with wrap-around fur cape, designed by <a href="/wiki/Norman_Hartnell" title="Norman Hartnell">Norman Hartnell</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With the Parliament robe, a black hat was customarily worn. The <a href="/wiki/State_Opening_of_Parliament#Origins" title="State Opening of Parliament">Wriothesley Garter Book</a> provides a contemporary illustration of the 1523 State Opening of Parliament: the two dukes present are shown wearing coronets with their parliament robes, but the other Lords Temporal are all wearing black hats. The Lords Spiritual are wearing mitres with their distinctive robes. Mitres ceased to be worn after the Reformation, and the wearing of hats in Parliament ceased, for the most part, when wigs came into fashion. They survive today only as part of the dress of Lords Commissioners, when they are worn with the parliamentary robe: a bicorn hat for men (of black beaver, edged with silk grosgrain ribbon) and a tricorne-like hat for women. (The use of these hats at Introductions of peers to the House was discontinued in 1998.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>)
</p>

<p>Peers are generally entitled to use certain <a href="/wiki/Coats_of_arms" class="mw-redirect" title="Coats of arms">heraldic devices</a>. Atop the arms, a peer may display a <a href="/wiki/Coronet" title="Coronet">coronet</a>. Dukes were the first individuals authorised to wear coronets. Marquesses acquired coronets in the 15th century, earls in the 16th and viscounts and barons in the 17th. Until the barons received coronets in 1661, the coronets of earls, marquesses and dukes were <a href="/wiki/Engraving" title="Engraving">engraved</a> while those of viscounts were plain. After 1661, however, viscomital coronets became engraved, while baronial coronets were plain. Coronets may not bear any precious or semi-precious stones.<sup id="cite_ref-cox_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cox-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Generally, only peers may use the coronets corresponding to their ranks. The <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Durham" title="Bishop of Durham">Bishop of Durham</a>, however, may use a duke's coronet atop the arms as a reference to the historical temporal authority of the Prince-Bishops of Durham.
</p><p>Peers wear their coronets at coronations. Otherwise, coronets are seen only in heraldic representations, atop a peer's arms. Coronets include a silver <a href="/wiki/Gilding" title="Gilding">gilt</a> chaplet and a base of <a href="/wiki/Ermine" class="mw-redirect" title="Ermine">ermine</a> fur. The coronet varies with the rank of the peer. A member of the Royal Family uses a royal coronet instead of the coronet he or she would use as a peer or peeress.<sup id="cite_ref-cox_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cox-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ducal coronets include eight strawberry leaves atop the chaplet, five of which are displayed in heraldic representations. Marquesses have coronets with four strawberry leaves alternating with four silver balls, of which three leaves and two balls are displayed. Coronets for earls have eight strawberry leaves alternating with eight silver balls (called "pearls" even though they are not) raised on spikes, of which five silver balls and four leaves are displayed. Coronets for viscounts have 16 silver balls, of which seven are displayed. Finally, baronial coronets have six silver balls, of which four are displayed. Peeresses use equivalent designs, but in the form of a <a href="/wiki/Circlet" title="Circlet">circlet</a>, which encircles the head, rather than a coronet, which rests atop the head.<sup id="cite_ref-cox_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cox-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Peers are entitled to the use of <a href="/wiki/Supporter" title="Supporter">supporters</a> in their achievements of arms. Hereditary supporters are normally limited to hereditary peers, certain members of the Royal Family, chiefs of Scottish Clans, Scottish feudal barons whose baronies predate 1587. Non-hereditary supporters are granted to life peers, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Garter" title="Order of the Garter">Knights of the Garter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Thistle" title="Order of the Thistle">Knights of the Thistle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Bath" title="Order of the Bath">Knights and Dames Grand Cross of the Bath</a>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_St_Michael_and_St_George" title="Order of St Michael and St George">Knights and Dames Grand Cross of St Michael and St George</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Victorian_Order" title="Royal Victorian Order">Knights and Dames Grand Cross of the Royal Victorian Order</a>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">Knights and Dames Grand Cross of the British Empire</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Knight_banneret" title="Knight banneret">knights banneret</a>.
</p><p>Peers, like most other armigers, may display <a href="/wiki/Helmet#Heraldry" title="Helmet">helms</a> atop their arms. Helms of peers are depicted in silver and facing the viewer's left. The helm is garnished in gold and the closed visor has gold bars, normally numbering five. Along with the helm, peers use a <a href="/wiki/Mantling" title="Mantling">mantling</a>, one side of which is red and the other a representation of the heraldic fur ermine. The mantling of peers is emblazoned <i>gules, doubled ermine</i>. Peeresses and other female armigers do not bear helms or mantlings.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peerages_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Peerage" class="mw-redirect" title="History of the Peerage">History of the Peerage</a></div>
<p>When <a href="/wiki/William_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="William I of England">William of Normandy</a> conquered England, he divided the nation into many "<a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_manor" title="Lord of the manor">manors</a>", the owners of which came to be known as barons; those who held many manors were known as "greater barons", while those with fewer manors were the "lesser barons". When Kings summoned their barons to Royal Councils, the greater barons were summoned individually by the Sovereign, lesser barons through sheriffs. In 1254, the lesser barons ceased to be summoned, and the body of greater barons evolved into the House of Lords. Since the Crown was itself a hereditary dignity, it seemed natural for seats in the upper House of Parliament to be so as well. By the beginning of the 14th century, the hereditary characteristics of the Peerage were well developed. The first peer to be created by patent was <a href="/wiki/John_de_Beauchamp,_1st_Baron_Beauchamp_(fourth_creation)" class="mw-redirect" title="John de Beauchamp, 1st Baron Beauchamp (fourth creation)">Lord Beauchamp</a> of Holt in the reign of <a href="/wiki/Richard_II_of_England" title="Richard II of England">Richard II</a>.
</p><p>The modern peerage system is a vestige of the custom of English kings in the 12th and 13th centuries; in the late 14th century, this right (or "title") began to be granted by decree, and titles also became inherited with the rest of an estate under the system of <a href="/wiki/Primogeniture" title="Primogeniture">primogeniture</a>.  Non-hereditary positions began to be created again in 1867 for Law Lords, and 1958 generally.
</p><p>The ranks of baron and earl date to feudal, and perhaps <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Saxon England">Anglo-Saxon</a>, times. The ranks of duke and marquess were introduced in the 14th century, and that of viscount in the 15th century. While peerages for life were often created in the early days of the peerage, their regular creation was not provided for by Act of Parliament until the <a href="/wiki/Appellate_Jurisdiction_Act" title="Appellate Jurisdiction Act">Appellate Jurisdiction Act</a> 1876.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Counterparts">Counterparts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Peerages_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Counterparts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Other <a href="/wiki/Feudal_monarchies" class="mw-redirect" title="Feudal monarchies">feudal monarchies</a> equally held a similar system, grouping high nobility of different rank titles under one term, with common privileges and/or in an assembly, sometimes legislative and/or judicial.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/It%C5%8D_Hirobumi" title="Itō Hirobumi">Itō Hirobumi</a> and the other <a href="/wiki/Meiji_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Meiji period">Meiji</a> leaders deliberately modeled the Japanese <a href="/wiki/Japanese_House_of_Peers" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese House of Peers">House of Peers</a> on the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lords" title="House of Lords">House of Lords</a>, as a counterweight to the popularly elected <a href="/wiki/House_of_Representatives_of_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="House of Representatives of Japan">House of Representatives</a> (<i>Shūgiin</i>).
</p><p>In France, the system of <a href="/wiki/Peerage_of_France" title="Peerage of France">pairies</a> (peerage) existed in two different versions: the exclusive 'old' in the French kingdom, in many respects an inspiration for the English and later British practice, and the very prolific <a href="/wiki/Chambre_des_Pairs" class="mw-redirect" title="Chambre des Pairs">Chambre des Pairs</a> under the Bourbon Restoration (1814–1848).
</p><p>In Spain and Portugal, the closest equivalent title was <a href="/wiki/Grandee" title="Grandee">Grandee</a>; in Hungary, <a href="/wiki/Magnat" class="mw-redirect" title="Magnat">Magnat</a>.
</p><p>In the Kingdom of Sicily a peerage was instituted in 1812 in connection with the abolition of feudalism: peers were nominated based on the taxable incomes of their formerly-feudal estates.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">Holy Roman Empire</a>, instead of an exclusive aristocratic assembly, the legislative body was the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Diet_(Holy_Roman_Empire)" title="Imperial Diet (Holy Roman Empire)">Imperial Diet</a>, membership of which, expressed by the title <a href="/wiki/Prince_of_the_Holy_Roman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince of the Holy Roman Empire">Prince of the Holy Roman Empire</a>, was granted to allied princely families (and various minor ones), as well as to Princes of the Church (parallel to the Lords Spiritual) and in some cases was restricted to a collective 'curiate' vote in a 'bench', such as the <a href="/wiki/Count" title="Count">Grafenbank</a>.
</p><p>In the medieval <a href="/wiki/Irish_nobility" title="Irish nobility">Irish nobility</a>, Gaelic nobles were those presented with the <a href="/wiki/White_Wand" class="mw-redirect" title="White Wand">White Wand</a> or <i>slat</i> in a formal ceremony, and presented it by another noble. It was the primary symbol of lordship and effectively reserved only for the three tiers of kings (provincial, regional, local) and for those princely and comital families descending from them in control of significant territories. The total number was between 100 and 150 at any time.
</p>
