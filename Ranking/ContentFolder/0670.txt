<p><b>Ramon Wilson</b>, <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">MBE</a> (17 December 1934 – 15 May 2018) was an English <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">footballer</a> who played at <a href="/wiki/Defender_(association_football)#Full-back" title="Defender (association football)">left back</a>. He was a member of the <a href="/wiki/England_national_football_team" title="England national football team">England</a> team that won the <a href="/wiki/1966_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1966 FIFA World Cup">1966 World Cup</a>. He was born in <a href="/wiki/Shirebrook" title="Shirebrook">Shirebrook</a>, <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Club_career">Club career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ray_Wilson_(English_footballer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Club career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Huddersfield_Town">Huddersfield Town</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ray_Wilson_(English_footballer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Huddersfield Town">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wilson became an apprentice <a href="/wiki/Railway" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway">railwayman</a> upon leaving school, but was spotted by a scout playing <a href="/wiki/Amateur" title="Amateur">amateur</a> football at <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_Town_A.F.C." title="Huddersfield Town A.F.C.">Huddersfield Town</a>. He began a combination of working on the tracks by night and training with Huddersfield by day, before being called up for <a href="/wiki/National_service" title="National service">national service</a>.
</p><p>Quickly singled out as a strong and nippy left back, with good overlapping skills, by the then-Huddersfield Town manager <a href="/wiki/Bill_Shankly" title="Bill Shankly">Bill Shankly</a>, Wilson signed professional forms with the club in 1952 after his two-year <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">army</a> posting,<sup id="cite_ref-:1_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and made his debut against <a href="/wiki/Manchester_United_F.C." title="Manchester United F.C.">Manchester United</a> in October 1955.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Two years later, Wilson was Huddersfield's established, first-choice, left back.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Everton">Everton</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ray_Wilson_(English_footballer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Everton">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1964, Wilson joined <a href="/wiki/Everton_F.C." title="Everton F.C.">Everton</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> by which time he had already played 30 times for England, and he remains Huddersfield's most-capped England international. However, a torn thigh muscle sustained in training meant that he missed most of his first season at Everton. He went on to win the <a href="/wiki/FA_Cup" title="FA Cup">FA Cup</a> with Everton in <a href="/wiki/1966_FA_Cup_Final" title="1966 FA Cup Final">1966</a>. Two years later, he was on the losing side, as Everton were beaten by <a href="/wiki/West_Bromwich_Albion_F.C." title="West Bromwich Albion F.C.">West Bromwich Albion</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1968_FA_Cup_Final" title="1968 FA Cup Final">1968 FA Cup Final</a>. Wilson's fortunes declined at Everton following another injury, and he was granted a free transfer in 1969, missing out on Everton's <a href="/wiki/Football_League_First_Division" title="Football League First Division">First Division</a> title in 1970.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_career">Later career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ray_Wilson_(English_footballer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Later career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Wilson moved to <a href="/wiki/Oldham_Athletic_A.F.C." title="Oldham Athletic A.F.C.">Oldham Athletic</a> on a free in 1969. In 1970 he moved again to Bradford City.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He served as caretaker manager at Bradford from September to November 1971 after the departure of <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Wheeler_(footballer)" title="Jimmy Wheeler (footballer)">Jimmy Wheeler</a>. He took command for ten games before being succeeded by <a href="/wiki/Bryan_Edwards_(footballer)" title="Bryan Edwards (footballer)">Bryan Edwards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="International_career">International career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ray_Wilson_(English_footballer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: International career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In April 1960, Wilson won his first cap for England in a 1–1 draw with <a href="/wiki/Scotland_national_football_team" title="Scotland national football team">Scotland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Over the next 12 months, he became a fixture in the side. The <a href="/wiki/The_Football_Association" title="The Football Association">FA</a> selection committee put him in the squad for the <a href="/wiki/1962_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1962 FIFA World Cup">1962 World Cup</a> in Chile, and Wilson played in all three group games and England's elimination in the quarter finals at the hands of <a href="/wiki/Brazil_national_football_team" title="Brazil national football team">Brazil</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After the World Cup, Wilson kept his England place under new manager <a href="/wiki/Alf_Ramsey" title="Alf Ramsey">Alf Ramsey</a>. With Ramsey successfully snatching sole responsibility for picking the team from the FA came a firm feeling that Wilson was Ramsey's highest-rated left back. Others, such as <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_F.C." title="Liverpool F.C.">Liverpool</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Byrne_(footballer,_born_1938)" title="Gerry Byrne (footballer, born 1938)">Gerry Byrne</a>, were given the odd chance, but Wilson remained Ramsey's first choice.
</p><p>As hosts of the 1966 World Cup, England did not have to partake in a rigorous qualifying campaign, and Ramsey experimented with other left backs as he shaped a squad for the tournament. Later the same year, Wilson was playing at Wembley on six more occasions, ever-present as Ramsey's England got through a World Cup group consisting of <a href="/wiki/Uruguay_national_football_team" title="Uruguay national football team">Uruguay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico_national_football_team" title="Mexico national football team">Mexico</a> and <a href="/wiki/France_national_football_team" title="France national football team">France</a>; a highly volatile quarter final against a violent <a href="/wiki/Argentina_national_football_team" title="Argentina national football team">Argentina</a>, and a semi final against the skilful but enigmatic <a href="/wiki/Portugal_national_football_team" title="Portugal national football team">Portuguese</a>, which was Wilson's 50th appearance for his country.
</p><p>Wilson was the oldest member of the England team in the World Cup final against <a href="/wiki/Germany_national_football_team" title="Germany national football team">West Germany</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Wilson's early headed clearance fell to striker <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Haller" title="Helmut Haller">Helmut Haller</a>, who gave the Germans the lead as a result, but after a <a href="/wiki/Hat-trick" title="Hat-trick">hat-trick</a> from <a href="/wiki/Geoff_Hurst" title="Geoff Hurst">Geoff Hurst</a>, England ran out 4–2 winners.
</p><p>Ramsey continued to select Wilson as England progressed through the qualification process for the <a href="/wiki/1968_UEFA_European_Football_Championship" class="mw-redirect" title="1968 UEFA European Football Championship">1968 European Championships</a>, ultimately going out in the semi finals and finishing third overall. Wilson's 63rd and final England cap came in the third-place play-off against <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union_national_football_team" title="Soviet Union national football team">the USSR</a>. At the time of his final cap, he held the record for the highest number of appearances for an outfield player without having scored a goal, a record since broken by <a href="/wiki/Gary_Neville" title="Gary Neville">Gary Neville</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ashley_Cole" title="Ashley Cole">Ashley Cole</a>.
</p><p>A serious knee injury suffered in the summer of 1968, coupled with the emergence of young <a href="/wiki/Leeds_United_F.C." title="Leeds United F.C.">Leeds United</a> full back <a href="/wiki/Terry_Cooper_(footballer,_born_1944)" title="Terry Cooper (footballer, born 1944)">Terry Cooper</a> (who would be as impressive in the <a href="/wiki/1970_FIFA_World_Cup" title="1970 FIFA World Cup">1970 World Cup</a> as Wilson was in 1966, despite England's elimination in the last eight), ended Wilson's England career.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="After_retirement_from_football">After retirement from football</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ray_Wilson_(English_footballer)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: After retirement from football">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Unquestionably the 1966 hero with the lowest public profile, Wilson nevertheless caused intrigue after his playing days ended by not staying within the game. Instead, he built a very successful <a href="/wiki/Undertaker" class="mw-redirect" title="Undertaker">undertaker</a>'s business in <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Wilson retired as an undertaker in 1997 to <a href="/wiki/Halifax,_West_Yorkshire" title="Halifax, West Yorkshire">Halifax</a>. In 2000, he and four of his 1966 teammates – Hunt, <a href="/wiki/George_Cohen" title="George Cohen">George Cohen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nobby_Stiles" title="Nobby Stiles">Nobby Stiles</a> and <a href="/wiki/Alan_Ball_Jr." title="Alan Ball Jr.">Alan Ball</a> – were appointed <a href="/wiki/MBE" class="mw-redirect" title="MBE">MBE</a> for services to football after a high-profile campaign conducted by sections of the <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">media</a>, which was surprised that their contribution to English football's greatest day had never been officially recognised. The other six, plus Ramsey, had already received various honours. In 2008, Wilson was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/English_Football_Hall_of_Fame" title="English Football Hall of Fame">English Football Hall of Fame</a> by a select committee of ex-footballers.
</p><p>He lived in <a href="/wiki/Slaithwaite" title="Slaithwaite">Slaithwaite</a> near Huddersfield with his wife Pat, who was three years his junior. They had two children.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Ray and Pat Wilson were interviewed together in the book <i>No More Worlds to Conquer</i> by Chris Wright (2015).
</p><p>Wilson has since been regarded as one of the finest left-backs that England has ever produced. Although not a goal scorer, his vision, passing ability, and strong runs down the left flank made him an invaluable and automatic member of the 1966 World Cup winning side. He is remembered as one of the greatest players to play for both Huddersfield and Everton.
</p><p>Wilson was diagnosed with <a href="/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease" title="Alzheimer&#39;s disease">Alzheimer's disease</a> in 2004,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> along with World cup winning teammates Martin Peters in 2013 and Nobby Stiles in 2012. It is feared that the disease was brought on by their heading of the heavier footballs used in their playing days.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 30 July 2016, fifty years to the day since England lifted the World Cup, Wilson's former club Huddersfield Town released its new second-change kit for the 2016–17 season in his honour. It was released with the tag line "Legends Are Rarely Made", and featured a red shirt, in homage to the 1966 World Cup winning kit, and had Wilson's signature in white, just beneath the collar on the back, and below the white badge on the front.
