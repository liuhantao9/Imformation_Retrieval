<p><b>Kirkburton</b> is a village, <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> and local government ward in the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_county" title="Metropolitan county">metropolitan county</a> of <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, England, 5 miles (8&#160;km) south east of <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Kirklees" title="Kirklees">Metropolitan Borough of Kirklees</a>. <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">Historically</a> part of the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>, the township comprises the villages of Kirkburton and <a href="/wiki/Highburton" title="Highburton">Highburton</a> and several hamlets, including <a href="/wiki/Thunder_Bridge" title="Thunder Bridge">Thunder Bridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Thorncliff,_West_Yorkshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Thorncliff, West Yorkshire">Thorncliff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Storthes_Hall" title="Storthes Hall">Storthes Hall</a>, Burton Royd, Riley, Dogley, Common Side, Causeway Foot, Lane Head and Linfit. According to the 2011 census the civil parish had a population of 26,439,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> while the village itself had a population of 4,299.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirkburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The area was populated in the <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">Iron Age</a> when a settlement was believed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (December 2018)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> to have been built on the site of the church. A <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Saxon</a> <a href="/wiki/Fort" class="mw-redirect" title="Fort">fort</a> is also believed<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (December 2018)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> to have stood on that site. The village is recorded in the <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> as <b>Bertone</b> in Wachefeld. The entry reads (translated):
</p><p><i>"In <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a>, with 9 Berewicks... are 60 <a href="/wiki/Carucate" title="Carucate">carucates</a> of land 3 <a href="/wiki/Bovate" class="mw-redirect" title="Bovate">bovates</a> and the third part of 1 bovate to the <a href="/wiki/Danegeld" title="Danegeld">geld</a>. 30 ploughs could plough this land. This manor was in the demesne of <a href="/wiki/Edward_the_Confessor" title="Edward the Confessor">King Edward</a>; now, in the king's hand, there are 4 villans, and 3 priests and 2 churches, and 7 sokemen and 16 bordars. Together, they have 7 ploughs. [There is] woodland pasture 6 leagues long and 4 leagues broad. The whole [is] 6 leagues long and 6 leagues broad... To this manor belongs the soke of these lands... Kirkburton, 3 carucates... in all, there are 30 carucates to the geld, which 20 ploughs could plough. Now they are waste"</i><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kirkburton_004.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Kirkburton_004.jpg/200px-Kirkburton_004.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Kirkburton_004.jpg/300px-Kirkburton_004.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Kirkburton_004.jpg/400px-Kirkburton_004.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kirkburton_004.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kirkburton: Looking east from Riley towards Low Town and Turnshaws</div></div></div>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest of England">Norman Conquest</a> the village grew from the waste recorded in 1086. Kirkburton was named after the church was built in 1190 and Highburton was built on the hill. In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> the township was part of the Manor of Wakefield and Kirkburton church was at the head of a 16,000 acres (60&#160;km<sup>2</sup>) <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parish</a>, that extended to the <a href="/wiki/Holme_Valley" title="Holme Valley">Holme Valley</a>.
</p><p>During the <a href="/wiki/First_English_Civil_War" title="First English Civil War">First English Civil War</a> the villagers supported the <a href="/wiki/Roundhead" title="Roundhead">Parliamentary</a> cause. The priest, Gamaliel Whitaker, angered his parishioners by supporting the <a href="/wiki/Cavalier" title="Cavalier">Royalists</a>. He was denounced to the government forces who went to arrest him in 1644. During the struggle the soldiers shot his wife, Hester, in the ensuing confusion. Legend has it that her ghost haunts the old vicarage.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kirkburton_005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Kirkburton_005.jpg/200px-Kirkburton_005.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Kirkburton_005.jpg/300px-Kirkburton_005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Kirkburton_005.jpg/400px-Kirkburton_005.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kirkburton_005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kirkburton: Looking north from Riley towards Burton Dean and Highburton</div></div></div>
<p>The population of the township increased with the growth of the textile trades. By 1800 the population was about 1,400: 60&#160;years later it was approaching 3,700. After this, there was a general decline and for nearly a century the figure was around 3,000. In 1971 there were 2,800&#160;inhabitants, but following housing developments at Highburton the population is nearer 5,000.
</p><p>The Huddersfield-Kirkburton Branch Line opened in 1867, serving two terminal railway stations as well as <a href="/wiki/Deighton_railway_station" title="Deighton railway station">Deighton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kirkheaton_railway_station" title="Kirkheaton railway station">Kirkheaton</a>, Fenay Bridge and Lepton. It was unusual in that it was operated by the <a href="/wiki/London_and_North_Western_Railway" title="London and North Western Railway">London and North Western Railway</a> company in an area where the <a href="/wiki/Lancashire_and_Yorkshire_Railway" title="Lancashire and Yorkshire Railway">Lancashire and Yorkshire Railway</a> company had a virtual monopoly. Plans to extend the line to <a href="/wiki/Barnsley" title="Barnsley">Barnsley</a> never materialised and Kirkburton remained at the end of the line which was primarily used for goods traffic, although passenger services ran until 1930. The line continued as a transport goods until the 1960s, when a combination of road haulage and a decline in industry lead to closure of the railway station in 1965. Evidence of the railway remains in the area around Northwood Park, a housing development built on the old route. Parts of the railway station still remain, albeit in ruined state, whilst the bricked up tunnel can be seen when travelling into the village centre from the Penistone Road.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Psychiatric" class="mw-redirect" title="Psychiatric">psychiatric</a> <a href="/wiki/Hospital" title="Hospital">hospital</a> operated at Storthes Hall from 1904–1991. It was founded as an <a href="/wiki/Psychiatric_hospital" title="Psychiatric hospital">asylum</a>, the Storthes Hall Mental Hospital (1929–1938), the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding</a> Mental Hospital (1939–1948) and <a href="/wiki/Storthes_Hall_Hospital" title="Storthes Hall Hospital">Storthes Hall Hospital</a> (1949–1991). After the hospital closed the land was sold to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Huddersfield" title="University of Huddersfield">University of Huddersfield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Halls_of_residence" class="mw-redirect" title="Halls of residence">halls of residence</a> were built. Most of the site is the Storthes Hall Park Student Village, and the remaining area due for further development as a retirement village.
</p><p>Kirkburton's major industries were the woollen industry and <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>. The village has a small shopping centre in Kirkburton but Highburton has become the residential centre.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry">Industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirkburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The manufacture of <a href="/wiki/Woollen" class="mw-redirect" title="Woollen">woollen</a> cloth was well established here by the time of <a href="/wiki/Queen_Elizabeth_I" class="mw-redirect" title="Queen Elizabeth I">Queen Elizabeth I</a>. It expanded rapidly after the late 18th&#160;century. The first textile mill was built at Dogley in about 1787 and used waterpower to prepare wool for spinning and for <a href="/wiki/Fulling" title="Fulling">fulling</a> the finished cloth. In about 1800 another mill opened at Linfit, which used <a href="/wiki/Steam_power" class="mw-redirect" title="Steam power">steam power</a>. Both mills gradually took on other processes and developed into substantial businesses under the Kenyon and Hey families. By 1880 there were eight mills at work in the township.
</p><p>Leather <a href="/wiki/Tanning_(leather)" title="Tanning (leather)">tanning</a> and the exploitation of <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> seams made valuable contributions to the economy of Kirkburton for several centuries. The last tannery closed in the 1830s. <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">Coal mining</a> grew in importance with the increased use of steam for pumping water and by 1850 there were 20&#160;small pits in the township. The remains of a number of mines and <a href="/wiki/Bell_pit" title="Bell pit">bell pits</a> can be seen, including the former St Helen's Colliery on Moor Lane in Highburton. In the latter half of the 19th&#160;century there were more than 30&#160;pits operating around Kirkburton employing more than 300&#160;men. The last <a href="/wiki/Colliery" class="mw-redirect" title="Colliery">colliery</a> closed about 70&#160;years ago. One old-established industry, which has moved from the village, was the manufacture of edge tools and <a href="/wiki/Shovels" class="mw-redirect" title="Shovels">shovels</a>, which was introduced in the mid-18th&#160;century. The last factory, Carter's, moved to new premises a few years ago.
</p><p>In 2006 Shepley Spring Ltd acquired the former Whitley Willows Textile site in Kirkburton and set up a volume bottled water plant, utilising the vast high quality ground water sources in this area. Known as Shepley Spring Brookfield, the site operates 24 hours a day and produces tens of millions of bottles for UK supermarkets and wholesalers.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sport">Sport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirkburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kirkburton AFC play in the 1st Division of the West Riding County Amateur Football League at the Gregory Playing Fields on Hallas Road. Kirkburton Junior Football Club train at the artificial pitches at Kirkburton Middle School. The village has training facilities for <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_Town_F.C." class="mw-redirect" title="Huddersfield Town F.C.">Huddersfield Town</a> at Storthes Hall.
</p><p>Kirkburton Cricket Club was founded at the Rose and Crown Inn on 25 February 1860. The public house, at the bottom of Slant Gate, is now a private residence. The club played at Turnshaw's before moving to Riley in 1878. The club was a founding member of the Huddersfield and District Alliance in 1893. Former <a href="/wiki/England_Cricket_Team" class="mw-redirect" title="England Cricket Team">England</a> cricketer <a href="/wiki/Phil_Defreitas" class="mw-redirect" title="Phil Defreitas">Phil Defreitas</a> played for the club in 1995.
</p><p>The Gregory Fields Tennis Club, formerly the Fenay Bridge Tennis Club, was built on Burton Acres Lane with lottery assistance in 2000. The club has changing facilities and three floodlit macadam courts.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_information">Other information</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirkburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other information">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kirkburton_Rapier_Dancers.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Kirkburton_Rapier_Dancers.jpg/200px-Kirkburton_Rapier_Dancers.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Kirkburton_Rapier_Dancers.jpg/300px-Kirkburton_Rapier_Dancers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Kirkburton_Rapier_Dancers.jpg/400px-Kirkburton_Rapier_Dancers.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2272" data-file-height="1704" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kirkburton_Rapier_Dancers.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kirkburton Rapier Dancers outside the Junction public house, Kirkburton, New Year's Day 2006</div></div></div>
<p>Kirkburton is the home to Kirkburton First School and Kirkburton Middle School. The Adult Education Centre and Special School on Turnshaw Avenue closed in 2006. The village has a masonic hall, six public houses, a purpose built health centre (opened October 2005) a dental surgery, and a partially manned police station. The largest housing areas can be found in the areas occupying Riley, Low Town, Brickfields and Turnshaws. North Road and George Street form the main road through the village and houses the majority of shops and businesses.
</p><p>Kirkburton and Highburton have a community association that meet at the Burton Village Hall, the former Highburton First School, Northfield Lane, Highburton.
</p><p>Kirkburton has a rapier dance team, who perform traditional <a href="/wiki/Long_Sword_dance" title="Long Sword dance">longsword dances</a> each <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Day" title="New Year&#39;s Day">New Year's Day</a> at pubs in the villages of Kirkburton and Highburton in addition to their numerous appearances at festivals elsewhere in the country. Rapier dancing was a tradition in the village up to the beginning of the 20th&#160;century, and was revived in 1974. The team adhere to what is largely believed to be the original dress of clogs, blue waistcoats and trousers with ribbons down the side. The final tradition, that of blackened faces, is only adopted during the New Year performances.
</p><p>Part of Kirkburton's community for a number of years, the Kirkburton Uniformed Group meet at the HQ on Hallas Road. Affiliated with the All Hallows Church in Kirkburton, the Uniformed Group have nearly 100 young members, both Scouts and Guides.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirkburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kirkburton_Church.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Kirkburton_Church.jpg/150px-Kirkburton_Church.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="222" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Kirkburton_Church.jpg/225px-Kirkburton_Church.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/Kirkburton_Church.jpg/300px-Kirkburton_Church.jpg 2x" data-file-width="756" data-file-height="1121" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kirkburton_Church.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Kirkburton All Hallows' Church</div></div></div> 
<p>Kirkburton Church is dedicated to <a href="/wiki/All_Hallows%27_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="All Hallows&#39; Day">All Hallows</a>. It was built in 1190, and is a Grade I <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed building</a>. Most of the structure is 13th&#160;century although parts have been rebuilt. The tower was added in the 15th&#160;century. Inside the church is a late-medieval nave ceiling, large wooden pulpit, stone font and <a href="/wiki/Elizabethan" class="mw-redirect" title="Elizabethan">Elizabethan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jacobean_architecture" title="Jacobean architecture">Jacobean</a> pews. A small window in the chancel may have opened from the cell of a hermit. A restored 10th century stone crucifix can be found inside, supporting the belief that another church or Christian settlement may have existed on this site. The church has undergone extensive restoration. The churchyard has also undergone landscaping and is a designated wildlife sanctuary as well as its own website kirkburtonchurchyard.co.uk  which contains the records of inscribed gravestone and memorials.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Salvation_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="Salvation Army">Salvation Army</a> Corps was established in the village in 1885. Its present headquarters were opened in 1964. The Salvation Army left the village in 2005 and the premises are owned by the Denby Dale Centre, a registered charity.
</p><p>The Methodist Chapel was built at Ponty in 1816 but moved to North Road in 1845 on the hill just off the main road that runs behind the old library. The chapel closed in 1987 and is now a private residence.
</p><p>The Primitive Methodist Chapel was built at the top of Slant Gate, Highburton in 1832. A school, now demolished, was built in 1899 and the chapel was enlarged in 1926. Like the Methodist chapel in Kirkburton it closed, this time in 1973, and is now a private house.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Places_of_interest">Places of interest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirkburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Places of interest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kirkburton">Kirkburton</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirkburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Kirkburton">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Masonic Hall</b>: opposite the police station and All Hallows' Church on George Street. The hall was built in 1889 in a mock Tudor style and was built as a meeting hall for the local <a href="/wiki/Freemasons" class="mw-redirect" title="Freemasons">Freemasons</a>. The Beaumont Lodge have met in the building ever since.
</p><p><b>George Inn</b>: the oldest <a href="/wiki/Public_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Public house">public house</a> in the village was built in the 18th&#160;century and has operated as a pub ever since. It is located on George Street, next to the old blacksmith's, and is overlooked by All Hallows' Church. In 1777 commissioners met there to agree upon the creation of the <a href="/wiki/Halifax,_West_Yorkshire" title="Halifax, West Yorkshire">Halifax</a> to <a href="/wiki/Penistone" title="Penistone">Penistone</a> Turnpike, the forerunner for the present Penistone Road.
</p><p><b>Royal Hotel</b>: <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian</a> public house built in stone in 1870. It was the second largest pub in the village and located in the centre of the village on North Road it has since been converted into a Co-operative store.
</p><p><b>Springfield Mill</b>: the oldest part was built as a warehouse about 1830. The main building, in a similar style but larger, was added for spinning in 1834 and the weaving sheds opposite in 1849. After the church this is perhaps the most important piece of architecture in the village of Denby Dale.
</p><p><b>Town Hall</b>: the owners of the nearby mill built Springfield House in the 1830s. It was bought by Kirkburton Urban District Council in 1935 and taken over as a town hall three years later. The council sold it in 1982. It is a Grade II Listed Building (1978) and a private residence with the grand, if inaccurate, name of "Kirkburton Hall".
</p><p><b>The Foxglove</b>: the largest public house in the village, it was built in 1868 in a <a href="/wiki/Mock_Tudor" class="mw-redirect" title="Mock Tudor">mock Tudor</a> style at the same time as the branch line. It was hoped that Kirkburton would become a junction to an extended line to Barnsley, hence the pub's original name the "Railway Junction", but this never materialised. The public house was more popularly known as the "Three Owls" up until 2000 when it adopted its new name. An extension was built in 2001 which is now the hotel part of the building.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Highburton">Highburton</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirkburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Highburton">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Highburton" title="Highburton">Highburton</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Highburton_Cross.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Highburton_Cross.jpg/150px-Highburton_Cross.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="181" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/79/Highburton_Cross.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="223" data-file-height="269" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Highburton_Cross.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Highburton Cross with the rear of the Smiths Arms in the background</div></div></div>
<p><b>Highburton Cross</b>: Marking the site of the medieval market, the steps at the base probably date from the 14th&#160;century, the shaft and ball from the 18th or 19th. The cross stands at the top of Far Dene at its junction with Town Gate and Hall Lane.
</p><p><b>Burton Village Hall</b>: Formerly Highburton School, the Village Hall was extensively renovated in 1999 with funds raised by the local community and a grant from the <a href="/wiki/Big_Lottery_Fund" class="mw-redirect" title="Big Lottery Fund">National Lottery Fund</a>. It is the home to Kirkburton Parish Council and a number of groups.
</p><p><b><a href="/wiki/Highburton_Co-operative_Society" title="Highburton Co-operative Society">Highburton Co-op</a></b>: Was located on Towngate in the centre of the village, the <a href="/wiki/Cooperative" title="Cooperative">Co-op</a> laid claim to being the world's oldest operating independent single retail cooperative outlet until its closure in February 2009. The building was purchased from the church in 1856 and began trading in the same year. It has re-opened since its closure.
</p><p><b>Smiths Arms</b>: at the summit of Far Dene, the Grade II Listed Smiths Arms is the only <a href="/wiki/Public_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Public house">public house</a> in the village and occupies one of its oldest buildings. Built in 1669 in a Tudor/Jacobean style, it began trading as a public house in 1830. Whilst it is the oldest pub building in the township it is not the oldest public house. That honour goes to the George Inn in Kirkburton, which dates back to the 18th&#160;century.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Linfit">Linfit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirkburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Linfit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>Manor Mill</b>: one of three former corn mills in the township, the others being at Dogley Bar and Smithy Hill. Dating from about 1832 this mill had one of the largest <a href="/wiki/Water_wheels" class="mw-redirect" title="Water wheels">water wheels</a> in the country, with a diameter of 50&#160;feet: it was removed for scrap during the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Storthes_Hall">Storthes Hall</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kirkburton&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Storthes Hall">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Storthes_Hall" title="Storthes Hall">Storthes Hall</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:StorthesHall1.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/StorthesHall1.JPG/200px-StorthesHall1.JPG" decoding="async" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/StorthesHall1.JPG/300px-StorthesHall1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/StorthesHall1.JPG/400px-StorthesHall1.JPG 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="1536" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:StorthesHall1.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Main hospital and clock tower</div></div></div>
<p><b>Storthes Hall Hospital</b>: most of the grounds of the former psychiatric hospital were sold to the University of Huddersfield and few of the buildings remain. The one remaining is the main hospital building with its clock tower. The building is in disrepair but is due for renovation as part of a proposed retirement village.
</p><p><b>Storthes Hall Mansion</b>: built in about 1788 as a private house for the Horsefall family, replacing an earlier house built on the site. Taken over as an asylum in 1904, it became known as the Mansion Hospital and operated as a psychiatric institution alongside the main hospital, its last years saw it providing institutional care for people with learning disabilities, finally closing in 1991. It too was sold to the University of Huddersfield in 1994. The building became Grade II Listed in June 1965. It is now again a private residence "The Mansion".
</p><p><b>Myers Wood</b>: recent archaeological excavations have uncovered the most complete iron working site in the North of England at <a href="/wiki/Myers_Wood" title="Myers Wood">Myers Wood</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Cistercian" class="mw-redirect" title="Cistercian">Cistercian</a> <a href="/wiki/Monastic" class="mw-redirect" title="Monastic">monastic</a> site was in operation from 12th–14th&#160;century, using advanced techniques and water power for smelting and smithing iron.
</p>
