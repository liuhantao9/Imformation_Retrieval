<p><i><b>Emeritus</b></i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ɛr/: &#39;err&#39; in &#39;merry&#39;">ɛr</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;s&#39; in &#39;sigh&#39;">s</span></span>/</a></span></span>),<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;Note 1&#93;</a></sup> in its current usage, is an adjective used to designate a retired <a href="/wiki/Chairperson" title="Chairperson">chairperson</a>, <a href="/wiki/Professor" title="Professor">professor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Priesthood_(Catholic_Church)" class="mw-redirect" title="Priesthood (Catholic Church)">pastor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">bishop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">pope</a>, <a href="/wiki/Company_director" class="mw-redirect" title="Company director">director</a>, <a href="/wiki/President_(government_title)" title="President (government title)">president</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prime_minister" title="Prime minister">prime minister</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rabbi" title="Rabbi">rabbi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emperor" title="Emperor">emperor</a>, or other person.
</p><p>In some cases, the term is conferred automatically upon all persons who retire at a given rank, but in others, it remains a mark of distinguished service, awarded only to a few on retirement. It is also used when a person of distinction in a profession retires or hands over the position, enabling their former rank to be retained in their title, e.g., "Professor Emeritus". The term <i>emeritus</i> does not necessarily signify that a person has relinquished all the duties of their former position, and they may continue to exercise some of them.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emeritus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Ēmĕ́rĕre</i> is a compound of the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> prefix <i>ē-</i> (a variant of <i>ex-</i>) meaning "out of, from" and <i>mĕ́rĕre</i> meaning "earn"; <i>ēmĕ́rĭtus</i> is the past participle of the verb.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The female equivalent, <i><b>ēmĕ́rǐta</b></i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ɛr/: &#39;err&#39; in &#39;merry&#39;">ɛr</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span></span>/</a></span></span><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>), is also sometimes used, but as is often true of <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">loanwords</a>, the use of the donor language's <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">inflectional</a> system faces limits in the recipient language; in English,  <i>emeritus</i> is often unmarked for gender.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_academia">In academia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emeritus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In academia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the United States and other countries, a fully <a href="/wiki/Tenure" class="mw-redirect" title="Tenure">tenured</a> professor who retires from an educational institution in good standing may be given the title "professor emeritus" regardless of gender. The title "professor emerita" is sometimes used for women. In most systems and institutions, the rank is bestowed on all professors who have retired in good standing, while at others, it needs a special act or vote. Professors emeriti may, depending on local circumstances, retain office space or other privileges. The word is used either as a postpositional adjective (e.g.,&#160;"professor emeritus", <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">Noam Chomsky</a>, the renowned scholar and MIT professor emeritus), or as a prepositional adjective (e.g.,&#160;"emeritus professor"). The concept has in some places been expanded to include tenured associate professors or non-tenure-track faculty.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> and most other parts of the world,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the term "emeritus professor" is given only to a person of outstanding merit who had full professorial status before he or she retired. The possession of a PhD or other higher degree, or even full professorial status, is not sufficient for calling oneself "emeritus professor" upon retirement. The term "Professor Emeritus" is also recognised in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a>. The word is capitalized when it forms part of a title which is capitalized. <a href="/wiki/Oxbridge" title="Oxbridge">Oxbridge</a> <a href="/wiki/Collegiate_university" title="Collegiate university">colleges</a> may appoint distinguished <a href="/wiki/Oxford_fellow" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford fellow">fellows</a> who have retired as "Emeritus Fellow".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emeritus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When a diocesan bishop or auxiliary bishop retires, the word <i>emeritus</i> is added to his former title, i.e., "Archbishop Emeritus of ...", "Bishop Emeritus of ...", or "Auxiliary Bishop Emeritus of ..."  Examples of usage are: "The Most Reverend (or Right Reverend) John Jones, Bishop Emeritus of Anytown"; and "His Eminence Cardinal James Smith, Archbishop Emeritus of Anycity". The term "Bishop Emeritus" of a particular see can apply to several people, if the first lives long enough. The sees listed in the 2007 Annuario Pontificio as having more than one (Arch)Bishop Emeritus included Zárate-Campana, Villavicencio, Versailles, and Uruguaiana. There were even three Archbishops Emeriti of Taipei. The same suffix was applied to the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Rome" class="mw-redirect" title="Bishop of Rome">Bishop of Rome</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Emeritus_Benedict_XVI" class="mw-redirect" title="Pope Emeritus Benedict XVI">Pope Emeritus Benedict XVI</a>, on his retirement. In the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> the word <i>emeritus</i> does not imply that the person in question is no longer a <a href="/wiki/Priesthood_in_the_Catholic_Church" title="Priesthood in the Catholic Church">priest</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Since 2001, the honorary title of <a href="/wiki/President_pro_tempore_emeritus_of_the_United_States_Senate" class="mw-redirect" title="President pro tempore emeritus of the United States Senate">president pro tempore emeritus</a> has been given to a Senator of the minority party who has previously served as president pro tempore of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Senate" title="United States Senate">United States Senate</a>. The position has been held by <a href="/wiki/Strom_Thurmond" title="Strom Thurmond">Strom Thurmond</a> (R-<a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_Senators_from_South_Carolina" class="mw-redirect" title="List of United States Senators from South Carolina">South Carolina</a>) (2001-2003), <a href="/wiki/Robert_Byrd" title="Robert Byrd">Robert Byrd</a> (D-<a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_Senators_from_West_Virginia" class="mw-redirect" title="List of United States Senators from West Virginia">West Virginia</a>) (2003-2007), <a href="/wiki/Ted_Stevens" title="Ted Stevens">Ted Stevens</a> (R-<a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_Senators_from_Alaska" class="mw-redirect" title="List of United States Senators from Alaska">Alaska</a>) (2007-2009), and <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Leahy" title="Patrick Leahy">Patrick Leahy</a> (D-<a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_Senators_from_Vermont" class="mw-redirect" title="List of United States Senators from Vermont">Vermont</a>) (2015-present).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is also commonly used in business and <a href="/wiki/Nonprofit_organization" title="Nonprofit organization">nonprofit organizations</a> to denote perpetual status of the founder of an organization or individuals who moved the organization to new heights as a former key member on the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_directors" title="Board of directors">board of directors</a> (e.g.,&#160;<a href="/wiki/Chairman" class="mw-redirect" title="Chairman">chairman</a> emeritus; director emeritus; president of the board emeritus).
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Community_of_Christ" title="Community of Christ">Community of Christ</a>, the status of emeritus is occasionally granted to senior officials upon retirement.  In 1938, Frederick A. Smith was given the title of "president emeritus" of the Order of Evangelists (one of the presiding councils of the church), though the president of that body at that time was more commonly known as Presiding Patriarch.  Roy A. Cheville became Presiding Patriarch Emeritus  in 1974.  W. Wallace Smith became the first person to retire as President of the Church (all prior presidents having served until death), and was accorded the title President Emeritus in 1978.  His successor, Wallace B. Smith, was also given the title of President Emeritus, upon his own retirement in 1996.  He continues to hold that status to this day.
</p>
