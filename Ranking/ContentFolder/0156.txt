<p><b>Poul Oluf Nyrup Rasmussen</b> (<small>Danish pronunciation:&#160;</small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Danish" title="Help:IPA/Danish">[pʰɒʊ̯l ˈnyːˀɔp ˈʁasmusn̩]</a></span>, informally <b>Poul Nyrup</b>, born 15 June 1943), was <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Denmark" title="Prime Minister of Denmark">Prime Minister of Denmark</a> from 25 January 1993 to 27 November 2001 and President of the <a href="/wiki/Party_of_European_Socialists" title="Party of European Socialists">Party of European Socialists</a> (PES) from 2004 to 2011. He was the leader of the governing <a href="/wiki/Social_Democrats_(Denmark)" title="Social Democrats (Denmark)">Social Democrats</a> from 1992 to 2002. He was a member of the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> from 2004 to 2009.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poul_Nyrup_Rasmussen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rasmussen was born to a working-class family in <a href="/wiki/Esbjerg" title="Esbjerg">Esbjerg</a> in 1943. His parents were Oluf Nyrup Rasmussen and Vera Eline Nyrup Rasmussen. He was educated at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Copenhagen" title="University of Copenhagen">University of Copenhagen</a>, earning a M.sc. degree in Economics in 1971. While studying he was active in the social democratic student union <a href="/wiki/Frit_Forum" title="Frit Forum">Frit Forum</a>, where he met some of his future political colleagues. He paid his way through university by doing several jobs, like counting traffic and being a part-time delivery boy.
</p>
<h2><span id="Prime_Minister_1993.E2.80.932001"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Prime_Minister_1993–2001">Prime Minister 1993–2001</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poul_Nyrup_Rasmussen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prime Minister 1993–2001">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rasmussen replaced <a href="/wiki/Svend_Auken" title="Svend Auken">Svend Auken</a>, the long serving leader of the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democrats_(Denmark)" title="Social Democrats (Denmark)">Social Democrats</a>, after his failure to form a government with the <a href="/wiki/Radikale_Venstre" class="mw-redirect" title="Radikale Venstre">Radikale Venstre</a> after the <a href="/wiki/1990_Danish_parliamentary_election" class="mw-redirect" title="1990 Danish parliamentary election">1990 election</a>, despite good results for both parties. Many in the party felt that Auken had stuck to a too left wing agenda, scuttling a possible deal with the more centrist Radikale Venstre.
</p><p>Rasmussen came to power in early 1993 when then-Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Poul_Schl%C3%BCter" title="Poul Schlüter">Poul Schlüter</a> resigned after an inquiry found that he had misinformed the <a href="/wiki/Folketing" title="Folketing">Folketing</a> about the so-called <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Case_(Denmark)" title="Tamil Case (Denmark)">Tamil Case</a>.
</p><p>A coalition of <a href="/wiki/Social_Democrats_(Denmark)" title="Social Democrats (Denmark)">Social Democrats</a>, <a href="/wiki/Det_Radikale_Venstre" class="mw-redirect" title="Det Radikale Venstre">Social Liberals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Centre_Democrats_(Denmark)" title="Centre Democrats (Denmark)">Centre Democrats</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democrats_(Denmark)" title="Christian Democrats (Denmark)">Christian Democrats</a>, Rasmussen's first cabinet made use of limited classical <a href="/wiki/Keynesianism" class="mw-redirect" title="Keynesianism">Keynesianism</a> in connection with the so-called kick-start of 1993–94 as its economic policy. Under Rasmussen, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a> experienced the most comprehensive privatisation of publicly owned assets in the 20th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The Christian Democrats left the coalition after their defeat in the <a href="/wiki/1994_Danish_parliamentary_election" class="mw-redirect" title="1994 Danish parliamentary election">1994 Folketing election</a>, as did the Centre Democrats in late 1996. Key ministers were <a href="/w/index.php?title=Minister_of_Economic_Affairs_(Denmark)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Minister of Economic Affairs (Denmark) (page does not exist)">Economy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deputy_Prime_Minister_(Denmark)" title="Deputy Prime Minister (Denmark)">Deputy Prime Minister</a>, Social Liberal leader <a href="/wiki/Marianne_Jelved" title="Marianne Jelved">Marianne Jelved</a>, Finance Minister <a href="/wiki/Mogens_Lykketoft" title="Mogens Lykketoft">Mogens Lykketoft</a> (Social Democrats) and <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Foreign_Affairs_(Denmark)" title="Minister of Foreign Affairs (Denmark)">Foreign Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Niels_Helveg_Petersen" title="Niels Helveg Petersen">Niels Helveg Petersen</a> (Social Liberals).
</p><p>The centre-left coalition only narrowly held on to its parliamentary majority in the <a href="/wiki/1998_Danish_parliamentary_election" class="mw-redirect" title="1998 Danish parliamentary election">1998 Folketing election</a>. After the election Prime Minister Rasmussen stated that the government's first order of business was to secure a "yes" vote in the upcoming referendum on ratification of the <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Treaty" class="mw-redirect" title="Amsterdam Treaty">Amsterdam Treaty</a> between the member states of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>. Eventually there were 55% "yes" votes in the <a href="/wiki/1998_Danish_Amsterdam_Treaty_referendum" title="1998 Danish Amsterdam Treaty referendum">Danish Amsterdam Treaty referendum</a>. Rasmussen's government later presided over the 2000 <a href="/w/index.php?title=2000_Danish_Euro_referendum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="2000 Danish Euro referendum (page does not exist)">referendum on Danish participation in the Euro</a>, in which participation was rejected by 53.2% of the vote.
</p><p>A 1998 initiative, dubbed the Whitsun Packet (Danish: <i>Pinsepakken</i>) from the season it was issued, increased taxes, limiting private consumption. It was not universally popular with the electorate, which may have been a factor in the Social Democrats' defeat in the <a href="/wiki/2001_Danish_parliamentary_election" class="mw-redirect" title="2001 Danish parliamentary election">2001 parliamentary election</a>.
</p><p>Rasmussen called an early election in 2001, saying this would give the next prime minister time to prepare for Denmark's upcoming presidency of the European Union in 2002. The patriarchal role Rasmussen had built for himself since the <a href="/wiki/11_September_attacks" class="mw-redirect" title="11 September attacks">11 September attacks</a> had gained him and the Social Liberals their highest poll ratings in years, a lead that would be eroded in the buildup to the <a href="/wiki/2001_Danish_parliamentary_election" class="mw-redirect" title="2001 Danish parliamentary election">election</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_RvR_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_RvR-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He was up against <a href="/wiki/Venstre_(Denmark)" title="Venstre (Denmark)">Liberal</a> leader <a href="/wiki/Anders_Fogh_Rasmussen" title="Anders Fogh Rasmussen">Anders Fogh Rasmussen</a>. The campaign focused mainly on immigration and refugees, which worked to the benefit of the anti-immigration <a href="/wiki/Danish_People%27s_Party" title="Danish People&#39;s Party">Danish People's Party</a>. Two in every three Danes now supported tighter immigration restrictions, compared to only one in two before 11 September. In the last few days of the campaign a number of predominantly left-leaning artists and intellectuals urged the Danish electorate not to vote for a rightwing government, warning that the Danish People's Party would then be likely to wield great influence on government policy.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_RvR_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_RvR-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other campaign focuses were on welfare and health care. Poul Nyrup Rasmussen stated the aim of creating a more robust economy to deal with the economic downturn.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> There was little debate about the European Union as the two leaders' opinions on that subject were largely the same.
</p><p>The loss of power in the 2001 election to Anders Fogh Rasmussen's <i><a href="/wiki/Venstre_(Denmark)" title="Venstre (Denmark)">Venstre</a></i> meant that the Social Democrats lost their position as the largest party in the <a href="/wiki/Folketing" title="Folketing">Folketing</a>, a position they had held without interruption since the <a href="/wiki/1924_Danish_Folketing_election" title="1924 Danish Folketing election">1924 Folketing election</a>. On election night Rasmussen vowed to stay on as party leader, famously declaring, "I will not run away with my tail between my legs."<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> He announced an effort of "renewal" within the Social Democrats, urging the promotion of centrist party members to leadership positions. Influential factions opposed Rasmussen's efforts, calling his leadership into question, and in late 2002 he announced that he would be stepping down as chairman.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="European_Parliament.2C_2004"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="European_Parliament,_2004">European Parliament, 2004</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poul_Nyrup_Rasmussen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: European Parliament, 2004">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rasmussen became a <a href="/wiki/Member_of_the_European_Parliament" title="Member of the European Parliament">MEP</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Party_of_European_Socialists" title="Party of European Socialists">Party of European Socialists</a> after winning a record number of 407,966 votes for an individual (from Denmark) in the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliamentary</a> <a href="/wiki/2004_European_Parliament_election_in_Denmark" title="2004 European Parliament election in Denmark">elections</a> in 2004. He sat on both the Foreign Affairs Committee and the Economic and Monetary Affairs Committee.
</p><p>A key issue tackled by Rasmussen in the European Parliament was the lack of regulation for private equity and hedge funds.  He worked to secure greater regulation in this area. starting long before the onset of the <a href="/wiki/Financial_crisis" title="Financial crisis">financial crisis</a>. His report, proposing binding rules for all players as well as greater transparency and accountability, was passed by the <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a> in September 2008. Rasmussen has since criticised the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a>, and in particular Commission President <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Manuel_Dur%C3%A3o_Barroso" class="mw-redirect" title="José Manuel Durão Barroso">José Manuel Durão Barroso</a> and Commissioner <a href="/wiki/Charlie_McCreevy" title="Charlie McCreevy">Charlie McCreevy</a> for failing to respond to the report with sufficient speed or dedication.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Rasmussen has also slammed the Commission's response to the economic crisis; in March 2009 he wrote: "A new, updated Recovery Plan is needed now, otherwise there will be 25 million unemployed in 2010. There must be real coordination focused on real investments. Europe also needs to do more for the countries of Central and Eastern Europe. It is in our common economic and political interest to prevent financial meltdown in those countries. Europe talks a lot about solidarity, now is the time it is really needed."<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span id="Party_of_European_Socialists.2C_2004.E2.80.9311"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Party_of_European_Socialists,_2004–11">Party of European Socialists, 2004–11</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poul_Nyrup_Rasmussen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Party of European Socialists, 2004–11">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2004 Rasmussen defeated <a href="/wiki/Giuliano_Amato" title="Giuliano Amato">Giuliano Amato</a> to be elected President of the <a href="/wiki/Party_of_European_Socialists" title="Party of European Socialists">PES</a>, succeeding <a href="/wiki/Robin_Cook" title="Robin Cook">Robin Cook</a> in the post. He was re-elected for a further 2.5 years at the PES Congress in Porto on 8 December 2006. The position involves coordinating the political vision of the party, ensuring unity, chairing the party presidency and representing the party on a regular basis. As PES President he is also President of the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Global_Progressive_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Global Progressive Forum (page does not exist)">Global Progressive Forum</a> and sits on committee of Transatlantic Dialogue, which fosters cooperation between progressives from the USA and Europe. Rasmussen has played a central role in making the party more inclusive and oversaw the launch of the network 'PES Activists', as well as a radically participative consultation process to construct the party's manifesto for the <a href="/wiki/2009_European_Parliament_election_in_Denmark" title="2009 European Parliament election in Denmark">2009 European election</a>.
</p><p>Rasmussen's influence in politicising the <a href="/wiki/Party_of_European_Socialists" title="Party of European Socialists">PES</a> can be seen in the party's headline political initiative, New Social Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Based on a report written by Rasmussen and former President of the European Commission <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Delors" title="Jacques Delors">Jacques Delors</a>, this aims at creating a "fairer, more inclusive, and more dynamic society".
</p><p>Currently, he is on the advisory board of OMFIF where he is regularly involved in meetings regarding the financial and monetary system.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Poul_Nyrup_Rasmussen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Rasmussen is married to <a href="/wiki/Lone_Dybkj%C3%A6r" title="Lone Dybkjær">Lone Dybkjær</a>, a member of the <a href="/wiki/Folketing" title="Folketing">Folketing</a> (and a former MEP) for the centrist <a href="/wiki/Det_Radikale_Venstre" class="mw-redirect" title="Det Radikale Venstre">Radikale Venstre</a>. He enjoys holidaying with his wife in their second house as well as swimming, walking, and reflecting with friends. He also likes listening to music.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> He is not related to his two immediate successors as Prime Minister, <a href="/wiki/Anders_Fogh_Rasmussen" title="Anders Fogh Rasmussen">Anders Fogh Rasmussen</a> or <a href="/wiki/Lars_L%C3%B8kke_Rasmussen" title="Lars Løkke Rasmussen">Lars Løkke Rasmussen</a>. His daughter committed suicide in 1993.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
