	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">English country house</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"><span class="mw-redirectedfrom">&#160;&#160;(Redirected from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stately_home&amp;redirect=no" class="mw-redirect" title="Stately home">Stately home</a>)</span></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>

<p class="mw-empty-elt">

</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Latimer_House_-_geograph.org.uk_-_139070.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Latimer_House_-_geograph.org.uk_-_139070.jpg/310px-Latimer_House_-_geograph.org.uk_-_139070.jpg" decoding="async" width="310" height="233" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Latimer_House_-_geograph.org.uk_-_139070.jpg/465px-Latimer_House_-_geograph.org.uk_-_139070.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Latimer_House_-_geograph.org.uk_-_139070.jpg/620px-Latimer_House_-_geograph.org.uk_-_139070.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Latimer_House_-_geograph.org.uk_-_139070.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Latimer_House" title="Latimer House">Latimer House</a> is an English country house in <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>An <b>English country house</b> is a large house or <a href="/wiki/Mansion" title="Mansion">mansion</a> in the English countryside. Such houses were often owned by individuals who also owned a <a href="/wiki/Townhouse_(Great_Britain)" title="Townhouse (Great Britain)">town house</a>. This allowed them to spend time in the country and in the city—hence, for these people, the term distinguished between town and country. However, the term also encompasses houses that were, and often still are, the full-time residence for the <a href="/wiki/Landed_gentry" title="Landed gentry">landed gentry</a> that ruled rural Britain until the <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_1832" title="Reform Act 1832">Reform Act 1832</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Frequently, the formal business of the <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">counties</a> was transacted in these country houses.
</p><p>With large numbers of indoor and outdoor staff, country houses were important as places of employment for many rural communities. In turn, until the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression_of_British_Agriculture" title="Great Depression of British Agriculture">agricultural depressions of the 1870s</a>, the estates, of which country houses were the hub, provided their owners with incomes. However, the late 19th and early 20th centuries were the swansong of the traditional English country house lifestyle. Increased taxation and the effects of World War I led to the <a href="/wiki/Destruction_of_country_houses_in_20th-century_Britain" title="Destruction of country houses in 20th-century Britain">demolition of hundreds of houses</a>; those that remained had to adapt to survive.
</p><p>While a <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau" title="Château">château</a> or a <i><a href="/wiki/Schloss" title="Schloss">Schloss</a></i> can be a fortified or unfortified building, a country house, similar to an <i><a href="/wiki/Ansitz" title="Ansitz">Ansitz</a></i>, is usually unfortified. If fortified, it is called a castle, but not all buildings with the name "castle" are fortified (for example <a href="/wiki/Highclere_Castle" title="Highclere Castle">Highclere Castle</a>).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Stately_homes_of_England">Stately homes of England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=English_country_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Stately homes of England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Longleat_House.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Longleat_House.jpg/220px-Longleat_House.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="167" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Longleat_House.jpg/330px-Longleat_House.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Longleat_House.jpg/440px-Longleat_House.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2522" data-file-height="1917" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Longleat_House.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Longleat_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Longleat House">Longleat House</a> was the first country house to open to the public, and also claims the first <a href="/wiki/Safari_park" title="Safari park">safari park</a> outside Africa.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It became the first property in what later was known as the Stately Home Industry.</div></div></div>
<p>The term <b>stately home</b> is subject to debate, and avoided by historians and other academics. As a description of a country house, the term was first used in a poem by <a href="/wiki/Felicia_Hemans" title="Felicia Hemans">Felicia Hemans</a>, <i>The Homes of England</i>, originally published in <i><a href="/wiki/Blackwood%27s_Magazine" title="Blackwood&#39;s Magazine">Blackwood's Magazine</a></i> in 1827. In the 20th century, the term was later popularised in a song by <a href="/wiki/No%C3%ABl_Coward" title="Noël Coward">Noël Coward</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> and in modern usage it often implies a country house that is open to visitors at least some of the time.
</p><p>In England, the terms "country house" and "stately home" are sometimes used vaguely and interchangeably; however, many country houses such as <a href="/wiki/Ascott_House" title="Ascott House">Ascott</a> in Buckinghamshire were deliberately designed not to be stately, and to harmonise with the landscape, while some of the great houses such as <a href="/wiki/Kedleston_Hall" title="Kedleston Hall">Kedleston Hall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holkham_Hall" title="Holkham Hall">Holkham Hall</a> were built as "power houses" to dominate the landscape, and were most certainly intended to be "stately" and impressive.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In his book <i>Historic Houses: Conversations in Stately Homes</i>, the author and journalist <a href="/wiki/Robert_Harling_(typographer)" title="Robert Harling (typographer)">Robert Harling</a> documents nineteen "stately homes"; these range in size from the vast Blenheim palace to the minuscule <a href="/wiki/Ebberston_Hall" title="Ebberston Hall">Ebberston Hall</a>, and in architecture from the Jacobean Renaissance of <a href="/wiki/Hatfield_House" title="Hatfield House">Hatfield House</a> to the eccentricities of <a href="/wiki/Sezincote" class="mw-redirect" title="Sezincote">Sezincote</a>. The book's collection of stately homes also includes <a href="/wiki/King_George_IV_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="King George IV of the United Kingdom">George IV's</a> Brighton town palace, the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Pavilion" title="Royal Pavilion">Royal Pavilion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">Evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=English_country_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Evolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Forde_Abbey_06.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Forde_Abbey_06.jpg/220px-Forde_Abbey_06.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Forde_Abbey_06.jpg/330px-Forde_Abbey_06.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Forde_Abbey_06.jpg/440px-Forde_Abbey_06.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="421" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Forde_Abbey_06.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Forde_Abbey" title="Forde Abbey">Forde Abbey</a> in Dorset. Many country houses have evolved and been extended over several centuries. Here, the architecture runs from Medieval ecclesiastical to <a href="/wiki/Palladian" class="mw-redirect" title="Palladian">Palladian</a> and on to <a href="/wiki/Strawberry_Hill_Gothic" class="mw-redirect" title="Strawberry Hill Gothic">Strawberry Hill Gothic</a>, while at sometime an attempt at unity has been made by the use of <a href="/wiki/Crenelation" class="mw-redirect" title="Crenelation">crenelation</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The country houses of England have evolved over the last five hundred years. Before this time, larger houses were usually fortified, reflecting the position of their owners as <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">feudal lords</a>, <i>de facto</i> overlords of their <a href="/wiki/Manorialism" title="Manorialism">manors</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Tudor_period" title="Tudor period">Tudor period</a> of stability in the country saw the building of the first of the unfortified great houses. <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Monasteries" title="Dissolution of the Monasteries">Dissolution of the Monasteries</a> saw many former <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiastical" class="mw-redirect" title="Ecclesiastical">ecclesiastical</a> properties granted to the King's favourites, who then converted them into private country houses. <a href="/wiki/Woburn_Abbey" title="Woburn Abbey">Woburn Abbey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forde_Abbey" title="Forde Abbey">Forde Abbey</a> and many other mansions with <i><a href="/wiki/Abbey" title="Abbey">abbey</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Priory" title="Priory">priory</a></i> in their name became private houses during this period. Other terms used in the names of houses to describe their origin or importance include <i><a href="/wiki/Palace" title="Palace">palace</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castle</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Court_(royal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Court (royal)">court</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hall" title="Hall">hall</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mansion" title="Mansion">mansion</a></i>, <i>park</i>, <i>house</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Manor_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Manor House">manor</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Location_(geography)" class="mw-redirect" title="Location (geography)">place</a></i>.
</p><p>It was during the second half of the reign of <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I</a>, and under her successor, <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="James I of England">James I</a>, that the first architect-designed mansions, thought of today as epitomising the English country house, began to make their appearance. <a href="/wiki/Burghley_House" title="Burghley House">Burghley House</a>, <a href="/wiki/Longleat" title="Longleat">Longleat House</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hatfield_House" title="Hatfield House">Hatfield House</a> are among the best known examples of the showy <a href="/wiki/Prodigy_house" title="Prodigy house">prodigy house</a>, often built with the intention of attracting the monarch to visit. By the reign of <a href="/wiki/Charles_I_of_England" title="Charles I of England">Charles I</a>, <a href="/wiki/Inigo_Jones" title="Inigo Jones">Inigo Jones</a> and his form of <a href="/wiki/Palladianism" class="mw-redirect" title="Palladianism">Palladianism</a> had changed the face of English domestic architecture completely, with the use of turrets and towers as an architectural reference to the earlier castles and fortified houses completely disappearing. The Palladian style, in various forms, interrupted briefly by <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">baroque</a>, was to predominate until the second half of the 18th century when, influenced by ancient Greek styles, it gradually evolved into the <a href="/wiki/Neoclassicism" title="Neoclassicism">neoclassicism</a> championed by such architects as <a href="/wiki/Robert_Adam" title="Robert Adam">Robert Adam</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Brympton_dEvercy_03.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Brympton_dEvercy_03.jpg/220px-Brympton_dEvercy_03.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="140" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Brympton_dEvercy_03.jpg/330px-Brympton_dEvercy_03.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Brympton_dEvercy_03.jpg/440px-Brympton_dEvercy_03.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="407" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Brympton_dEvercy_03.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Brympton_d%27Evercy" title="Brympton d&#39;Evercy">Brympton d'Evercy</a> in Somerset evolved from the Medieval period; its provincial architects are long forgotten. Yet, <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Hussey" title="Christopher Hussey">Christopher Hussey</a> described it as "The most incomparable house in Britain, the one which created the greatest impression and summarises so exquisitely English country life qualities".<sup id="cite_ref-Country_Life_1927_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Country_Life_1927-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup></div></div></div>
<p>Some of the best known of England's country houses were built by one architect at one particular time: <a href="/wiki/Montacute_House" title="Montacute House">Montacute House</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chatsworth_House" title="Chatsworth House">Chatsworth House</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Blenheim_Palace" title="Blenheim Palace">Blenheim Palace</a> are examples. While the latter two are <a href="/wiki/Duke" title="Duke">ducal</a> <a href="/wiki/Palace" title="Palace">palaces</a>, Montacute, although built by a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_the_Rolls" title="Master of the Rolls">Master of the Rolls</a> to Queen Elizabeth I, was occupied for the next 400 years by his descendants, who were gentry without a London <a href="/wiki/Townhouse" title="Townhouse">townhouse</a>, rather than aristocracy. They finally ran out of funds in the early 20th century.
</p><p>However, the vast majority of the lesser-known English country houses, often owned at different times by gentlemen and <a href="/wiki/Peerage" title="Peerage">peers</a>, are an evolution of one or more styles with facades and wings in different styles in a mixture of high architecture, often as interpreted by a local architect or surveyor, and determined by practicality as much as by the whims of architectural taste. An example of this is <a href="/wiki/Brympton_d%27Evercy" title="Brympton d&#39;Evercy">Brympton d'Evercy</a> in Somerset, a house of many periods that is unified architecturally by the continuing use of the same mellow, local <a href="/wiki/Hamstone" title="Hamstone">Ham Hill stone</a>.
</p><p>The fashionable <a href="/wiki/William_Kent" title="William Kent">William Kent</a> redesigned <a href="/wiki/Rousham_House" title="Rousham House">Rousham House</a> only to have it quickly and drastically altered to provide space for the owner's twelve children. <a href="/wiki/Canons_Ashby" title="Canons Ashby">Canons Ashby</a>, home to poet <a href="/wiki/John_Dryden" title="John Dryden">John Dryden</a>'s family, is another example of architectural evolution: a medieval farmhouse enlarged in the <a href="/wiki/Tudor_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Tudor era">Tudor era</a> around a courtyard, given grandiose plaster ceilings in the <a href="/wiki/Stuart_period" title="Stuart period">Stuart period</a>, and then having <a href="/wiki/Georgian_architecture" title="Georgian architecture">Georgian</a> façades added in the 18th century. The whole is a glorious mismatch of styles and fashions that seamlessly blend together. These could be called the true English country house. <a href="/wiki/Wilton_House" title="Wilton House">Wilton House</a>, one of England's grandest houses, is in a remarkably similar vein; although, while the Drydens, mere squires, at Canons Ashby employed a local architect, at Wilton the mighty <a href="/wiki/Earls_of_Pembroke" class="mw-redirect" title="Earls of Pembroke">Earls of Pembroke</a> employed the finest architects of the day: first <a href="/wiki/Hans_Holbein_the_Younger" title="Hans Holbein the Younger">Holbein</a>, 150 years later Inigo Jones, and then Wyatt followed by Chambers. Each employed a different style of architecture, seemingly unaware of the design of the wing around the next corner. These varying "improvements", often criticised at the time, today are the qualities that make English country houses unique.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sizes_and_types">Sizes and types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=English_country_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sizes and types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>Wealthy and influential people, often bored with their formal duties, go to the country in order to get out of London, the ugliest and most uncomfortable city in the world; they invented the long week-end to stay away as long as possible. Their <i lang="fr" title="French language text">métier</i> is politics; they talk politics; and they make politics, quite spontaneously.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite><a href="/wiki/John_Gunther" title="John Gunther">John Gunther</a>, 1940<sup id="cite_ref-gunther1940_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gunther1940-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<p>There are no written terms for distinguishing between vast country palaces and comparatively small country houses; the descriptive terms, which can include <i>castle</i>, <i>manor</i> and <i>court</i>, provide no firm clue and are often only used because of a historical connection with the site of such a building.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Therefore, for ease or explanation, Britain's country houses can be categorised according to the circumstances of their creation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_houses">Power houses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=English_country_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Power houses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Kedleston_Hall_20080730-04.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Kedleston_Hall_20080730-04.jpg/220px-Kedleston_Hall_20080730-04.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Kedleston_Hall_20080730-04.jpg/330px-Kedleston_Hall_20080730-04.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Kedleston_Hall_20080730-04.jpg/440px-Kedleston_Hall_20080730-04.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4284" data-file-height="2756" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Kedleston_Hall_20080730-04.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Kedleston_Hall" title="Kedleston Hall">Kedleston Hall</a> designed by <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Brettingham" title="Matthew Brettingham">Matthew Brettingham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Adam" title="Robert Adam">Robert Adam</a>, one of the great power houses.</div></div></div>
<p>The <b>great houses</b> are the largest of the country houses; in truth palaces, built by the country's most powerful – these were designed to display their owners' power or ambitions to power.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Really large unfortified or barely fortified houses began to take over from the traditional <a href="/wiki/Castle" title="Castle">castles</a> of the crown and magnates during the Tudor period, with vast houses such as <a href="/wiki/Hampton_Court_Palace" title="Hampton Court Palace">Hampton Court Palace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Burghley_House" title="Burghley House">Burghley House</a>, and continued until the 18th century with houses such as <a href="/wiki/Castle_Howard" title="Castle Howard">Castle Howard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kedleston_Hall" title="Kedleston Hall">Kedleston Hall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holkham_Hall" title="Holkham Hall">Holkham Hall</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Such building reached its zenith from the late 17th century until the mid-18th century; these houses were often completely built or rebuilt in their entirety by one eminent architect in the most fashionable architectural style of the day and often have a suite of Baroque state apartments, typically in <a href="/wiki/Enfilade_(architecture)" title="Enfilade (architecture)">enfilade</a>, reserved for the most eminent guests, the entertainment of whom was of paramount importance in establishing and maintaining the power of the owner. The common denominator of this category of English country houses is that they were designed to be lived in with a certain degree of ceremony and pomp. It was not unusual for the family to have a small suite of rooms for withdrawing in privacy away from the multitude that lived in the household. These houses were always an alternative residence to a London house.
</p><p>During the 18th and 19th centuries, for the highest echelons of English society, the country house served as a place for relaxing, hunting and running the country with one's equals at the end of the week, with some houses having their own <a href="/wiki/Country_House_Theatres" class="mw-redirect" title="Country House Theatres">theatre</a> where performances were staged.
</p><p>The <b>country house</b>, however, was not just an oasis of pleasure for a fortunate few; it was the centre of its own world, providing employment to hundreds of people in the vicinity of its <a href="/wiki/Estate_(house)" class="mw-redirect" title="Estate (house)">estate</a>. In previous eras, when state benefits were unheard of, those working on an estate were among the most fortunate, receiving secured employment and rent-free accommodation. At the summit of this category of people was the indoor staff of the country house. Unlike many of their contemporaries prior to the 20th century, they slept in proper beds, wore well-made adequate clothes and received three proper meals a day, plus a small wage. In an era when many still died from malnutrition or lack of medicine, the long working hours were a small price to pay.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>As a result of the aristocratic habit of only marrying within the aristocracy, and whenever possible to a sole heiress, many owners of country houses owned several country mansions,<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and would visit each according to the season: <a href="/wiki/Grouse" title="Grouse">Grouse</a> shooting in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pheasant" title="Pheasant">pheasant</a> shooting and <a href="/wiki/Fox_hunting" title="Fox hunting">fox hunting</a> in England. The <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Rosebery" title="Earl of Rosebery">Earl of Rosebery</a>, for instance, had <a href="/wiki/Dalmeny_House" title="Dalmeny House">Dalmeny House</a> in Scotland, <a href="/wiki/Mentmore_Towers" title="Mentmore Towers">Mentmore Towers</a> in Buckinghamshire, and another house near <a href="/wiki/Epsom" title="Epsom">Epsom</a> just for the racing season.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> For many, this way of life, which began a steady decline in 1914, continued well into the 20th century, and for a very few continues to this day.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Minor_country_houses">Minor country houses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=English_country_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Minor country houses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the second category of Britain's country houses are those that belonged to the squirearchy or <a href="/wiki/Landed_gentry" title="Landed gentry">landed gentry</a>. These tend either to have evolved from medieval hall houses, with rooms added as required, or were purpose-built by relatively unknown local architects. Smaller, and far greater in number than the "power houses", these were still the epicentre of their own estate, but were often the only residence of their owner.
</p><p>However, whether the owner of a "power house" or a small manor, the inhabitants of the English country house have become collectively referred to as the ruling class, because this is exactly what they did in varying degrees, whether by having high political influence and power in national government, or in the day-to-day running of their own localities or "county" in such offices as <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Lieutenant">lord/deputy lieutenant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Magistrates" class="mw-redirect" title="Magistrates">magistrates</a>, or occasionally even clergy.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Closed_circle_of_suspects#Country_house_mystery" title="Closed circle of suspects">Country house mystery</a> was a popular genre of English detective fiction in the 1920s and 1930s; set in the residence of the <a href="/wiki/Landed_gentry" title="Landed gentry">gentry</a> and often involving a murder in a country house temporarily isolated by a snowstorm or similar with the suspects all at a weekend house party.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Victorian_houses">Victorian houses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=English_country_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Victorian houses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:WaddesdonManor.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/WaddesdonManor.JPG/220px-WaddesdonManor.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/WaddesdonManor.JPG/330px-WaddesdonManor.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/WaddesdonManor.JPG/440px-WaddesdonManor.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3872" data-file-height="2592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:WaddesdonManor.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Waddesdon_Manor" title="Waddesdon Manor">Waddesdon Manor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a>. During the Victorian era, vast country houses were built in a variety of styles by wealthy industrialists and bankers.</div></div></div>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> of the 19th century, a third category of country houses was built as newly rich industrialists and bankers were eager to display their wealth and taste. By the 1850s, with the English economy booming, new mansions were built in one of the many <a href="/wiki/Revivalism_(architecture)" title="Revivalism (architecture)">revivalist</a> architectural styles popular throughout the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Hall,_p25_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hall,_p25-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The builders of these new houses were able to take advantage of the political unrest in Europe that gave rise to a large trade in architectural salvage.<sup id="cite_ref-Hall,_p25_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hall,_p25-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This new wave of country house building is exemplified by the <a href="/wiki/Rothschild_properties_in_the_Home_counties" class="mw-redirect" title="Rothschild properties in the Home counties">Rothschild properties in the Home counties</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bletchley_Park" title="Bletchley Park">Bletchley Park</a> (rebuilt in several styles, and famous for its code-breaking rôle in World War II).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline">Decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=English_country_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Destruction_of_country_houses_in_20th-century_Britain" title="Destruction of country houses in 20th-century Britain">Destruction of country houses in 20th-century Britain</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:TrenthamBalustrade.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/TrenthamBalustrade.jpg/220px-TrenthamBalustrade.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="110" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/TrenthamBalustrade.jpg/330px-TrenthamBalustrade.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/13/TrenthamBalustrade.jpg/440px-TrenthamBalustrade.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1335" data-file-height="670" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:TrenthamBalustrade.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Trentham_Gardens" class="mw-redirect" title="Trentham Gardens">Trentham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Staffordshire" title="Staffordshire">Staffordshire</a>. During the 20th century, thousands of country houses were demolished, their stone and fixtures sold. During this era, many fine architectural features were transported to the US.</div></div></div>
<p>The slow decline of the English country house coincided with the rise not just of taxation, but also of modern industry, along with the agricultural depression of the 1870s. By 1880, this had led some owners into financial shortfalls as they tried to balance maintenance of their estates with the income they provided. Some relied on funds from secondary sources such as banking and trade while others, like the severely impoverished <a href="/wiki/Charles_Spencer-Churchill,_9th_Duke_of_Marlborough" title="Charles Spencer-Churchill, 9th Duke of Marlborough">Duke of Marlborough</a>, sought to marry American heiresses to save their country houses and lifestyles.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The ultimate demise began immediately following <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. The members of the huge staff required to maintain large houses had either left to fight and never returned, departed to work in the munitions factories, or filled the void left by the fighting men in other workplaces. Of those who returned after the war, many left the countryside for better-paid jobs in towns. The final blow for many country houses came following <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>; having been requisitioned during the war, they were returned to the owners in poor repair. Many estate owners, having lost their heirs, if not in the immediately preceding war then in World War I, were now paying far higher rates of tax, and agricultural incomes had dropped. Thus, the solution for many was to hold <a href="/wiki/English_country_house_contents_auctions" class="mw-redirect" title="English country house contents auctions">contents auctions</a> and then demolish the house and sell its stone, <a href="/wiki/Fireplace" title="Fireplace">fireplaces</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Panelling" title="Panelling">panelling</a>. This is what happened to many of Britain's finest houses.
</p><p>Despite this slow decline, as late as 1920, so necessary was the country house for entertaining and prestige that, following the election of the first Labour Government in 1921, <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Lee,_1st_Viscount_Lee_of_Fareham" title="Arthur Lee, 1st Viscount Lee of Fareham">Viscount Lee of Fareham</a> donated his country house <a href="/wiki/Chequers" title="Chequers">Chequers</a> to the nation for the use of a prime minister who might not possess one of his own. Chequers still fulfills that need today as do both <a href="/wiki/Chevening_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Chevening House">Chevening House</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dorneywood" title="Dorneywood">Dorneywood</a>, donated for sole use of high-ranking ministers of the Crown.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Today">Today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=English_country_house&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=English_country_house&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p>Today, many country houses have become hotels<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>, schools, hospitals, museums and prisons, while others have survived as conserved ruins, but from the early 20th century until the early 1970s, <a href="/wiki/Destruction_of_country_houses_in_20th-century_Britain" title="Destruction of country houses in 20th-century Britain">hundreds of country houses were demolished</a>. Houses that survived destruction are now mostly Grade I or II listed as buildings of historic interest—and only the most faithful, most accurate, and most precise restoration and re-creation is permitted. Such work, however, is usually very expensive, although the system does ensure that everything is done correctly and authentically. The negative side is that many owners cannot afford the work, so a roof remains leaking for the sake of a cheap roof tile. The first stately home to open to the public in England in the modern style is said to be <a href="/wiki/Stoneleigh_Abbey" title="Stoneleigh Abbey">Stoneleigh Abbey</a> in <a href="/wiki/Warwickshire" title="Warwickshire">Warwickshire</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Although the ownership or management of some houses has been transferred to a <a href="/wiki/Trust_law" title="Trust law">private trust</a>, most notably at <a href="/wiki/Chatsworth_House" title="Chatsworth House">Chatsworth</a>, other houses have transferred art works and furnishings under the <a href="/wiki/Acceptance_in_Lieu" class="mw-redirect" title="Acceptance in Lieu">Acceptance in Lieu</a> scheme to ownership by various national or local museums, but are retained for display in the building. This enables the former owners to offset tax, the payment of which would otherwise have necessitated the sale of the art works. For example, tapestries and furniture at <a href="/wiki/Houghton_Hall" title="Houghton Hall">Houghton Hall</a> are now owned by the <a href="/wiki/Victoria_and_Albert_Museum" title="Victoria and Albert Museum">Victoria and Albert Museum</a>. In addition, increasing numbers of country houses hold licences for weddings and <a href="/wiki/Civil_ceremony" title="Civil ceremony">civil ceremonies</a>. Another source of income is to use the house as a venue for parties,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> a <a href="/wiki/Filming_location" title="Filming location">film location</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Corporate_entertainment" title="Corporate entertainment">corporate entertainment</a> venue. While many country houses are open to the public, they remain inhabited private houses, in some cases by the descendants of their original owners.
</p><p>The lifestyles of those living and working in a country house in the early 20th century were recreated in a <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> television programme, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Edwardian_Country_House" title="The Edwardian Country House">The Edwardian Country House</a></i>, which was filmed at <a href="/wiki/Manderston_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Manderston House">Manderston House</a> in Scotland.
</p>
