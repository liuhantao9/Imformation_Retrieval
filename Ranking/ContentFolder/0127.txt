<p><b>Sophia de Mello Breyner Andresen</b> (6 November 1919&#160;– 2 July 2004) was a Portuguese poet and writer. In 2014, she was unanimously chosen by the <a href="/wiki/Assembly_of_the_Republic_(Portugal)" title="Assembly of the Republic (Portugal)">Parliament</a> with <a href="/wiki/Church_of_Santa_Engr%C3%A1cia" title="Church of Santa Engrácia">National Pantheon</a> honours, the second woman to be so honored, after <a href="/wiki/Am%C3%A1lia_Rodrigues" title="Amália Rodrigues">Amália Rodrigues</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sophia_de_Mello_Breyner_Andresen&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Sophia, as she is often referred to in Portugal, was born in <a href="/wiki/Porto" title="Porto">Porto</a> to a wealthy aristocratic family. She inherited the surname 'Andresen' from her paternal great grandfather, a Danish merchant. She received a strict Catholic upbringing, and was to remain a fervent believer until the end of her life. After spending her childhood in Porto she moved to <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">Lisbon</a>, where she attended the Universidade de Lisboa. As a student, she was actively involved in Catholic movements. Politically, she defended constitutional monarchy and openly criticized <a href="/wiki/Ant%C3%B3nio_de_Oliveira_Salazar" title="António de Oliveira Salazar">Salazar</a>'s dictatorship.  In 1946 she married lawyer and politician <a href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_Sousa_Tavares&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Francisco Sousa Tavares (page does not exist)">Francisco Sousa Tavares</a>. They had five children, among whom is journalist and best-selling author <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Sousa_Tavares" title="Miguel Sousa Tavares">Miguel Sousa Tavares</a>. After the <a href="/wiki/Carnation_Revolution" title="Carnation Revolution">Carnation Revolution</a> in 1974, she made a brief incursion into politics as an MP for the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Party_(Portugal)" title="Socialist Party (Portugal)">Socialist Party</a> (centre-left).
</p><p>Andresen won acclaim as a storyteller with <i>Contos Exemplares</i> (Exemplary Tales),"Histórias da Terra e do Mar" (Stories of Land and Sea), and a number of <a href="/wiki/Children%27s_books" class="mw-redirect" title="Children&#39;s books">children's books</a> – <i>A Menina do Mar</i> (<a href="/wiki/The_Sea_Girl" title="The Sea Girl">The Sea Girl</a>), <i>O Cavaleiro da Dinamarca</i> (The Danish Knight), <i>A Floresta</i> (The Forest), <i>O Rapaz de Bronze</i> (The Bronze Boy), <i><a href="/wiki/A_Fada_Oriana" title="A Fada Oriana">A Fada Oriana</a></i> (The Fairy Oriana). She also published several poetry books and anthologies, including: <i>Poesia</i>, <i>Dual</i>, <i>Livro Sexto</i>, <i>Coral</i>, <i>Dia do Mar</i>, <i>No Tempo Dividido</i>, <i>Grades</i>, <i>O Nome das Coisas</i>, <i>As Ilhas</i>, <i>Antologia</i>, <i>Geografia</i>, <i>Navegações</i>, <i>O Búzio de Cós</i>. In 1999 she became the first woman to receive the highest Portuguese award for poetry, the <a href="/wiki/Pr%C3%A9mio_Cam%C3%B5es" class="mw-redirect" title="Prémio Camões">Prémio Camões</a>. She was also awarded the 
<a href="/w/index.php?title=Max_Jacob_Poetry_Prize&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Max Jacob Poetry Prize (page does not exist)">Max Jacob Poetry Prize</a>,in 2001, and the Spanish <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pr%C3%A9mio_Reinha_Sofia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Prémio Reinha Sofia (page does not exist)">Prémio Reinha Sofia</a> in 2003.
</p><p>"Poetry," she explained, "is my understanding of the universe, my way of relating to things, my participation in reality, my encounter with voices and images. This is why the poem speaks not of an ideal life but of a concrete one: the angle of a window, the resonance of streets, cities and rooms, the shadow cast by a wall, a sudden face, the silence, distance and brightness of the stars, the night’s breath, the scent of linden and of oregano." The sea is probably the most central theme in her poetical works. Other recurring themes are Ancient Greece and ideas of freedom and justice.
</p><p>Besides her work as a writer, she translated <a href="/wiki/Dante" class="mw-redirect" title="Dante">Dante</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shakespeare" class="mw-redirect" title="Shakespeare">Shakespeare</a> into Portuguese.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Sophia_de_Mello_Breyner_Andresen_(film)" title="Sophia de Mello Breyner Andresen (film)">documentary short film about her</a> was produced in 1969. It was the first completed film by director <a href="/wiki/Jo%C3%A3o_C%C3%A9sar_Monteiro" title="João César Monteiro">João César Monteiro</a> (then using the name João César Santos).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sophia de Mello Breyner Andresen died in Lisbon on July 2, 2004, aged 84.
</p><p>Her poetry has been translated into English by Ruth Fainlight, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Zenith" title="Richard Zenith">Richard Zenith</a> and most recently by Colin Rorrison with <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Jull_Costa" title="Margaret Jull Costa">Margaret Jull Costa</a>, as well as into many languages in the world.
</p>
