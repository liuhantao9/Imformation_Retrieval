<p><b>Slack Roman Fort</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Castra" title="Castra">castellum</a> near <a href="/wiki/Outlane" title="Outlane">Outlane</a>, to the west of <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a> in  <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, England. Its site is a <a href="/wiki/Scheduled_monument" title="Scheduled monument">scheduled monument</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-pastscape_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pastscape-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The ruins of the fort which lay alongside the <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennine</a> section of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_road" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman road">Roman road</a> from <a href="/wiki/Deva_Victrix" title="Deva Victrix">Deva Victrix</a> (<a href="/wiki/Chester" title="Chester">Chester</a>) to <a href="/wiki/Eboracum" title="Eboracum">Eboracum</a> (<a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a>) are no longer visible. The fort may have been the <b>Cambodunum</b> mentioned as a station on this route in the <a href="/wiki/Antonine_Itinerary" title="Antonine Itinerary">Antonine Itinerary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NHLECambodunum_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHLECambodunum-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Archaeological_dig" class="mw-redirect" title="Archaeological dig">Archaeological digs</a> indicate the fort was constructed of turf and wood to defend the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman</a> road in the time of <a href="/wiki/Gnaeus_Julius_Agricola" title="Gnaeus Julius Agricola">Agricola</a> in <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;79.<sup id="cite_ref-pastscape_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pastscape-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Outside the fort walls was a stone bath-house which was extended around <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;104 and <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;120. A <a href="/wiki/Vicus" title="Vicus">vicus</a> or small settlement of wooden huts grew outside the fort.<sup id="cite_ref-vicus_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vicus-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Slack_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Slack-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2016, a retired professor from <a href="/wiki/Bangor_University" title="Bangor University">Bangor University</a>, Peter Field, hypothesised that the fort's site was a potential location for the mythical <a href="/wiki/Camelot" title="Camelot">Camelot</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Field,_2016_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Field,_2016-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Location">Location</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slack_Roman_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The site of the fort is on gently sloping ground sheltered by a hill rising to 1,200 feet (366&#160;m) above sea level about 4 miles (6&#160;km) from Huddersfield. Observation posts on the surrounding hills commanded views towards <a href="/wiki/Blackstone_Edge" title="Blackstone Edge">Blackstone Edge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Standedge" title="Standedge">Standedge</a>, Huddersfield and the <a href="/wiki/Stainland" title="Stainland">Stainland</a> Valley in the <a href="/wiki/Halifax,_West_Yorkshire" title="Halifax, West Yorkshire">Halifax</a> direction.<sup id="cite_ref-GH_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GH-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Excavations">Excavations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Slack_Roman_Fort&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Excavations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Huddersfield Archaeological and Topographical Society, whose secretary was <a href="/wiki/George_Lloyd_(archaeologist)" title="George Lloyd (archaeologist)">George Lloyd</a>, excavated the site in 1865. It excavated the remains of a residence measuring 60 feet (18&#160;m) by 68 feet (21&#160;m) with a courtyard. Nearby was a cremation site and on the slope 80 yards (73&#160;m) below the house are the remains of a <a href="/wiki/Hypocaust" title="Hypocaust">hypocaust</a> for the hot air <a href="/wiki/Thermae" title="Thermae">bath</a>. Also found nearby were a British stone axe, pottery, part of a brooch and a quantity of <a href="/wiki/Galena" title="Galena">galena</a>, (lead ore).<sup id="cite_ref-GH_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GH-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> On another part of the site are the remains of a cold bath comprised a slab of concrete 13&#160;feet 6&#160;inches (4.1&#160;m) long by 6&#160;feet 3&#160;inches (1.9&#160;m) wide. The room it was in had a red tiled floor. Copper or bronze coins from the reigns of <a href="/wiki/Vespasian" title="Vespasian">Vespasian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nerva" title="Nerva">Nerva</a>, an earthenware jar and a tile stamped "COH IIII BRE" were also uncovered.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Excavations between 1913 and 1915 and 1958 to 1963 uncovered the fort's <a href="/wiki/Rampart_(fortification)" title="Rampart (fortification)">ramparts</a> and the foundations of its gateways and corner towers. The ramparts, constructed of turves on a stone footing, surrounded an area 356 feet (111 metres) square. They were surrounded by a ditch, in some parts a double ditch when the fort was built in about <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;79. Inside are the outlines of several wooden buildings including the military headquarters and barrack blocks. Another phase of development about 20 years later added two granaries roofed with tiles stamped COHIIIIBRE, the mark of the 4th cohort of <a href="/wiki/List_of_ancient_tribes_in_Illyria#Breuci" title="List of ancient tribes in Illyria">Breuci</a> who operated a tilery in the nearby Grimescar Valley. A phase of building in stone began in the early <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;120s. but by then the garrison had been reduced. The fort was occupied until about <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;140.<sup id="cite_ref-Slack_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Slack-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Outside the fort to the east was the bath-house and to the north was a small civilian settlement which may have continued until <span class="smallcaps"><span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">AD</span></span>&#160;200.<sup id="cite_ref-Slack_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Slack-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Tolson_Museum" title="Tolson Museum">Tolson Museum</a> in Huddersfield has a collection of artefacts including the reconstructed Grade II <a href="/wiki/Listed_building" title="Listed building">listed</a> remains of the <a href="/wiki/Hypocaust" title="Hypocaust">hypocaust</a>, comprising the rubble columns and tiled floor, that were moved to Ravensknowle Park from the fort.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
