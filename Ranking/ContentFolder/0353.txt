<p>An <b>order</b> is a visible <a href="/wiki/Honour" title="Honour">honour</a> awarded by a <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">sovereign state</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dynasty" title="Dynasty">dynastic royal house</a> or <a href="/wiki/Organisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Organisation">organisation</a> to a person, typically in recognition of individual <a href="/wiki/Meritocracy" title="Meritocracy">merit</a>, that often comes with distinctive insignia such as <a href="/wiki/Collar_(order)" title="Collar (order)">collars</a>, <a href="/wiki/Medal" title="Medal">medals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Badge" title="Badge">badges</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sash" title="Sash">sashes</a> worn by recipients.
</p><p>Modern <a href="/wiki/Honour_system" class="mw-redirect" title="Honour system">honour systems</a> of state orders and <a href="/wiki/Dynastic_order" title="Dynastic order">dynastic orders</a> emerged from the culture of <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_chivalry" class="mw-redirect" title="Orders of chivalry">orders of chivalry</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, which in turn emerged from the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_religious_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic religious order">Catholic religious orders</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">Terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Order_(distinction)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word order (<a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>: <i lang="la">ordo</i>), in the case referred to in this article, can be traced back to the <a href="/wiki/Chivalric_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Chivalric order">chivalric orders</a>, including the <a href="/wiki/Military_order_(society)" class="mw-redirect" title="Military order (society)">military orders</a>, which in turn trace the name of their organisation back to that of the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_religious_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic religious order">Catholic religious orders</a>.
</p><p>Orders began to be created <i><a href="/wiki/Ad_hoc" title="Ad hoc">ad hoc</a></i> and in a more courtly nature. Some were merely honorary and gradually the <i>badges</i> of these orders (i.e. the association) began to be known informally as <i>orders</i>. As a result, the modern distinction between <i>orders</i> and <i>decorations</i> or <i>insignia</i> has become somewhat blurred. While some <i>orders</i> today retain the original notion of being an association or society of individuals, others make no distinction and an "order" may even be the name of a decoration.
</p><p>Most historic <a href="/wiki/Chivalric_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Chivalric order">chivalric orders</a> imply a membership in a group, typically a <a href="/wiki/Confraternity" title="Confraternity">confraternity</a>. In a few exclusive European orders, membership is or was also limited in number. Decorations seldom have such limitations. Orders often come in multiple classes, including <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knights</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dame" title="Dame">dames</a> in imitation of the original chivalric orders.<sup id="cite_ref-decorations_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-decorations-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Order_(distinction)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Modern national orders, orders of merit, and decorations, emerged from the culture of <a href="/wiki/Chivalric_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Chivalric order">chivalric orders</a> established in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, originally the <a href="/wiki/Military_order_(society)" class="mw-redirect" title="Military order (society)">military orders</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">crusades</a>, who in turn grew out of the original <a href="/wiki/Catholic_religious_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic religious order">Catholic religious orders</a>.
</p><p>While these chivalric orders were "societies, fellowships and colleges of <a href="/wiki/Knight" title="Knight">knights</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-SGC_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SGC-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> founded by the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a> or European monarchs in imitation of the <a href="/wiki/Military_order_(society)" class="mw-redirect" title="Military order (society)">military orders</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusades</a>, granting membership in such societies gradually developed into an honour that could be bestowed in recognition of service or to ensure the loyalty of a certain <a href="/wiki/Clientele" class="mw-redirect" title="Clientele">clientele</a>. Some of modern Europe's highest honours, such as the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Golden_Fleece" title="Order of the Golden Fleece">Order of the Golden Fleece</a>, <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Garter" title="Order of the Garter">Order of the Garter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Elephant" title="Order of the Elephant">Order of the Elephant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Thistle" title="Order of the Thistle">Order of the Thistle</a>, were created during that era. They were essentially courtly in nature, characterised by close personal relations between the orders' members and the orders' sovereign.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orders_by_fount_of_honour">Orders by fount of honour</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Order_(distinction)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orders by fount of honour">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Fount_of_honour" title="Fount of honour">Fount of honour</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="State_orders">State orders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Order_(distinction)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: State orders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/State_order" title="State order">State order</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynastic_orders">Dynastic orders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Order_(distinction)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dynastic orders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Dynastic_order" title="Dynastic order">Dynastic order</a></div>
<p>By the time of the <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">Renaissance</a>, most European monarchs had either acquired an existing order of chivalry, or created new ones of their own, to reward loyal civilian and especially military officials. Such orders remained out of reach to the general public, however, as being of noble rank or birth was usually a prerequisite to being admitted.
</p><p>In the 18th century, these ideas gradually changed and the orders developed from "honourable societies" to visible honours. An example of this gradual development can be seen in two orders founded by <a href="/wiki/Maria_Theresa_of_Austria" class="mw-redirect" title="Maria Theresa of Austria">Maria Theresa of Austria</a>. While the <a href="/wiki/Military_Order_of_Maria_Theresa" title="Military Order of Maria Theresa">Military Order of Maria Theresa</a> (1757) was open to any deserving military officer regardless of social origin, and would grant titles of nobility to those who did not already have them, the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Stephen_of_Hungary" title="Order of Saint Stephen of Hungary">Order of Saint Stephen of Hungary</a> (1764) still required that one had to have at least four generations of noble ancestors.
</p><p>Still today many dynastic orders are granted by royal families to worthy individuals for service and achievements.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Orders_of_merit">Orders of merit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Order_(distinction)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Orders of merit">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Order_of_merit" title="Order of merit">Order of merit</a></div>
<p>In 1802 <a href="/wiki/Napoleon" title="Napoleon">Napoleon</a> created the <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9gion_d%27honneur" class="mw-redirect" title="Légion d&#39;honneur">Légion d'honneur</a> (Legion of Honour), which could be awarded to any person, regardless of status, for bravery in combat or for 20 years of distinguished service. While still retaining many trappings of an <a href="/wiki/Order_of_chivalry" title="Order of chivalry">order of chivalry</a>, it was the first modern national order of merit and is still <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>'s highest award today. The French Legion of Honour served as the model for numerous modern orders of merit in the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Leopold_(Belgium)" title="Order of Leopold (Belgium)">Order of Leopold</a> in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> (1832) and the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">Order of the British Empire</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">United Kingdom</a> (1917). Curiously, orders of merit based on the French Legion of Honour typically retain five classes in accordance with habits of <a href="/wiki/Chivalric_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Chivalric order">chivalric orders</a>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Communist" class="mw-redirect" title="Communist">communist</a> countries, orders of merit usually come in one to three grades, with only a <a href="/wiki/Badge" title="Badge">badge</a> worn with or without a <a href="/wiki/Ribbon" title="Ribbon">ribbon</a> on the chest. An example of a communist order of merit was the one-class <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Lenin" title="Order of Lenin">Order of Lenin</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> (1930). Unlike Western orders, however, communist orders could be awarded more than once to an individual. After the collapse of the Soviet bloc, most Eastern European countries reverted to the Western-style orders originally established before the rise of communism.
</p><p>Today many countries have some form of order of merit or national decorations. Both <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">Thailand</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_White_Elephant" title="Order of the White Elephant">Order of the White Elephant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Rising_Sun" title="Order of the Rising Sun">Order of the Rising Sun</a> are over 100 years old. In Canada and some Commonwealth Realms, the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Merit" title="Order of Merit">Order of Merit</a> is the highest civilian honour. <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> has the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Canada" title="Order of Canada">Order of Canada</a> and provincial orders such as the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Nova_Scotia" title="Order of Nova Scotia">Order of Nova Scotia</a>. <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>  has the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Australia" title="Order of Australia">Order of Australia</a>, and <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> awards the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_New_Zealand" title="Order of New Zealand">Order of New Zealand</a> and the <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Order_of_Merit" title="New Zealand Order of Merit">New Zealand Order of Merit</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Mapungubwe" title="Order of Mapungubwe">Order of Mapungubwe</a> is the highest honour in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>, while the Orders of <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Luthuli" title="Order of Luthuli">Luthuli</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Baobab" title="Order of the Baobab">Baobab</a> exist alongside other decorations. The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> awards the <a href="/wiki/Medal_of_Honor" title="Medal of Honor">Medal of Honor</a> to members of its military for acts of valour, and the <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Medal_of_Freedom" title="Presidential Medal of Freedom">Presidential Medal of Freedom</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Congressional_Gold_Medal" title="Congressional Gold Medal">Congressional Gold Medal</a> to civilians. The <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Merit" title="Legion of Merit">Legion of Merit</a> is the only United States decoration which may be issued in award degrees (much like an Order of chivalry or certain Orders of Merit), but award degrees are only made to foreign nationals, typically senior military officers or government officials.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> does not award any orders. Article 12 of the 1848 Swiss Constitution prohibited the acceptance of honours and titles by Swiss citizens.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Swiss_Federal_Constitution" title="Swiss Federal Constitution">The current Constitution of 1999</a> has no specific prohibition, but a federal statute effectively continues the prohibition by barring holders of foreign orders from holding public office.
</p><p>In 1974 the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_Sweden" class="mw-redirect" title="Cabinet of Sweden">Cabinet of Sweden</a> passed a <a href="/wiki/Delegated_legislation" class="mw-redirect" title="Delegated legislation">regulation</a> forbidding the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Sweden" title="Monarchy of Sweden">Monarch of Sweden</a> from awarding membership in orders to Swedish citizens. The orders themselves were not abolished, but only the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Order_of_the_Seraphim" title="Royal Order of the Seraphim">Royal Orders of the Seraphim</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Polar_Star" title="Order of the Polar Star">Polar Star</a> (both established in 1748) continue to be awarded, and only to foreign citizens and stateless individuals. In 1995 the regulation was altered, allowing the Monarch to bestow the two remaining active Orders to members of the <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Royal_Family" class="mw-redirect" title="Swedish Royal Family">Swedish Royal Family</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Modern orders are usually open to all citizens of a particular country, regardless of status, sex, race or creed; there may be a minimum age for eligibility. Nominations are made either by private citizens or by government officials, depending on the country. An order may be revoked if the holder is convicted of a <a href="/wiki/Crime" title="Crime">crime</a> or renounces <a href="/wiki/Citizenship" title="Citizenship">citizenship</a>. Some people nominated for an award refuse it.
</p>
