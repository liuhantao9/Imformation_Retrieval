<p>A <b>name suffix</b>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (February 2016)">dubious</span></a>&#32;<span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Suffix_(name)#Rename_and_reorganize_as_a_CONCEPTDAB_page" title="Talk:Suffix (name)">discuss</a></span></i>&#93;</sup> in the Western <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English-language</a> naming tradition, follows a person's full <a href="/wiki/Name" title="Name">name</a> and provides additional information about the person. <a href="/wiki/Post-nominal_letters" title="Post-nominal letters">Post-nominal letters</a> indicate that the individual holds a position, educational degree, accreditation, office, or honor (e.g. "PhD", "CCNA", "OBE").  Other examples include generational designations like "Sr." and "Jr." (or often "Snr" and "Jnr" in <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>) and "III", and legal ones such as "Estate" and (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>) <i><i lang="fr" title="French language text"><a href="/wiki/Coverture" title="Coverture">Feme Covert</a></i></i>. Another used is Sñr (Spanish for Mr.).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-nominal_letters">Post-nominal letters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffix_(name)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Post-nominal letters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Post-nominal_letters" title="Post-nominal letters">Post-nominal letters</a></div>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic">Academic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffix_(name)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Academic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Academic suffixes indicate the degree earned at a <a href="/wiki/College" title="College">college</a> or <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a>. These include the <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor&#39;s degree">bachelor's degree</a> (A.B, B.A., B.A., Hons,  B.S., B.E., B.F.A., B.Tech.,
L.L.B, B.Sc., etc.), the <a href="/wiki/Master%27s_degree" title="Master&#39;s degree">master's degree</a> (<a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts" title="Master of Arts">M.A.</a>, M.S., M.F.A., LL.M, <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Landscape_Architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Master of Landscape Architecture">M.L.A.</a>, M.B.A., M.Sc., M.Eng etc.), the professional <a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">doctorate</a> (<a href="/wiki/Juris_Doctor" title="Juris Doctor">J.D</a>., <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Medicine" title="Doctor of Medicine">M.D.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Osteopathic_Medicine" title="Doctor of Osteopathic Medicine">D.O.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Pharmacy" title="Doctor of Pharmacy">Pharm.D.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Ministry" title="Doctor of Ministry">D.Min.</a>, etc.), and the academic <a href="/wiki/Doctorate" title="Doctorate">doctorate</a> (Ph.D., Ed.D., D.Phil., D.B.A., LL.D, Eng.D., etc.).
</p><p>In the case of doctorates, normally either the prefix (e.g. "Dr." or "Atty.") or the suffix (e.g. "J.D.", "M.D.", "D.O.", "D.C.", or "Ph.D.") is used, but both can be used at the same time due to preferences of different countries. In the United States, the suffix is the preferred format (thus allowing differentiation between types of doctorate) in written documentation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Honorary">Honorary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffix_(name)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Honorary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Such titles may be given by:
</p>
<ul><li>a <a href="/wiki/Monarch" title="Monarch">monarch</a> (for example, K.B.E., a suffix granted to Knights Commander of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_British_Empire" title="Order of the British Empire">Order of the British Empire</a>);</li>
<li>a <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> (as in a <a href="/wiki/LL.D." class="mw-redirect" title="LL.D.">LL.D.</a> (Doctor of Laws) given in recognition of a person's life achievements rather than their academic standing);</li>
<li>a <a href="/wiki/Church_body" class="mw-redirect" title="Church body">church</a> or seminary, who may offer an honorary <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Divinity" title="Doctor of Divinity">Doctor of Divinity</a> (D.D.) to outstanding ministers or teachers.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Esquire">Esquire</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffix_(name)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Esquire">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The style <i><a href="/wiki/Esq." class="mw-redirect" title="Esq.">Esq.</a></i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Esquire" title="Esquire">Esquire</a></i> was once used to distinguish a man who was an apprentice to a knight and is used for a man of socially high ranking. In the United States, <i>Esq.</i> is used as a professional styling for a licensed attorney. In the United Kingdom, it is used by untitled males in social and business contexts, or occasionally by an untitled male heir to a <a href="/wiki/Hereditary_peer" title="Hereditary peer">hereditary peer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional">Professional</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffix_(name)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Professional">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="box-Globalize plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-globalize" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><img alt="Globe icon." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_globe_content.svg/48px-Ambox_globe_content.svg.png" decoding="async" width="48" height="40" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_globe_content.svg/73px-Ambox_globe_content.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Ambox_globe_content.svg/97px-Ambox_globe_content.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="350" data-file-height="290" /></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">The examples and perspective in this section <b>may not represent a <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:WikiProject_Countering_systemic_bias" title="Wikipedia:WikiProject Countering systemic bias">worldwide view</a> of the subject</b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> You may <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Suffix_(name)&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a>, discuss the issue on the <a href="/wiki/Talk:Suffix_(name)#Globalize" title="Talk:Suffix (name)">talk page</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Article_wizard" title="Wikipedia:Article wizard">create a new article</a>, as appropriate.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">July 2011</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>Professional titles include <a href="/wiki/Esquire" title="Esquire">Esq.</a>, often used for an <a href="/wiki/Lawyer" title="Lawyer">attorney</a> (but not necessarily) in the United States who has passed a state bar examination, and CSA (casting) and ASCAP, which indicate membership in professional societies.  The suffix CA is used for individuals who have completed the requirements to become a <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Accountant" class="mw-redirect" title="Chartered Accountant">Chartered Accountant</a>. The suffix CPA is also used for individuals who have completed the requirements to become a Certified Public Accountant. Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Financial_Analyst" title="Chartered Financial Analyst">Chartered Financial Analysts</a> use the suffix CFA. Sommeliers (restaurant wine professionals) who have passed the <a href="/wiki/Master_Sommelier" class="mw-redirect" title="Master Sommelier">Master Sommelier</a> exam use the MS suffix. Engineers that are certified as a <a href="/wiki/Professional_Engineer" class="mw-redirect" title="Professional Engineer">Professional Engineer</a> in his or her state will use the suffix P.E., Certified Professional Geologists use P.G., Certified Professional Logisticians use CPL, and <a href="/wiki/Chartered_Engineer" class="mw-redirect" title="Chartered Engineer">Chartered Engineers</a> use CEng. Likewise, <a href="/wiki/Registered_Architect" class="mw-redirect" title="Registered Architect">Registered Architects</a> sometimes use the suffix R.A., or more often a suffix such as AIA or RIBA that refers to their professional society. Examination Office personnel within the United Kingdom who are registered with the Examination Officers' Association use MEOA. In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, professional archaeologists registered with the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Register_of_Professional_Archaeologists&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Register of Professional Archaeologists (page does not exist)">Register of Professional Archaeologists</a> use the suffix RPA.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Project_manager" title="Project manager">Project managers</a> that have obtained certification as <a href="/wiki/Project_Management_Professional" title="Project Management Professional">Project Management Professionals</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Project_Management_Institute" title="Project Management Institute">Project Management Institute</a> may use the suffix PMP after their name. Similarly, individuals who hold certifications in the field of <a href="/wiki/Information_security" title="Information security">information security</a> &#8211; e.g.&#160;<a href="/wiki/Certified_Information_Systems_Auditor" class="mw-redirect" title="Certified Information Systems Auditor">CISA</a>, <a href="/wiki/CISSP" class="mw-redirect" title="CISSP">CISSP</a>, and/or <a href="/wiki/Certified_Information_Security_Manager" class="mw-redirect" title="Certified Information Security Manager">CISM</a> &#8211; may use them as suffixes.
</p><p>The suffix PT is used by Physical Therapists to denote their state certification, but not to be confused with DPT (Doctor of Physical Therapy) which is a qualifying degree.  UK physiotherapists prefer to use MCSP or SRP to denote membership to professional bodies.  RN is used by qualified nurses as a suffix.
</p><p>Officers and enlisted in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Military" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Military">United States Military</a> will add an abbreviation of the service frequently to disambiguate seniority, and reserve status.  For example, Captain Smith, USN (O-6), outranks Captain Jones, USMC (O-3).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Religious_orders">Religious orders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffix_(name)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Religious orders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Members of <a href="/wiki/Religious_institute" title="Religious institute">religious institutes</a> commonly use their institute's initials as a suffix. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" class="mw-redirect" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> <a href="/wiki/Friar" title="Friar">friar</a> uses the post-nominal initials <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Friars_Minor" title="Order of Friars Minor">"O.F.M."</a>, derived from the Order's name in <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a>, "Ordo Fratrum Minorum" (Order of Friars Minor). Equally, a <a href="/wiki/Viatorian" class="mw-redirect" title="Viatorian">Viatorian</a> <a href="/wiki/Priesthood_(Catholic_Church)" class="mw-redirect" title="Priesthood (Catholic Church)">priest</a> uses the suffix "C.S.V." from the name of his religious institute, "Clerici Santi Viatori", the (<a href="/wiki/Clerics_of_Saint_Viator" title="Clerics of Saint Viator">Clerics of Saint Viator</a>). These initials are not considered by members of religious institutes as an equivalent to academic or honorary post-nominial initials, but rather as a sign of membership in a particular religious lineage, similar to the use of "Senior" or "Junior".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ordering">Ordering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffix_(name)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ordering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In some <a href="/wiki/English-speaking" class="mw-redirect" title="English-speaking">English-speaking</a> countries, the arrangement of post-nominal letters is governed by rules of precedence, and this list is sometimes called the "Order of Wear" (for the wearing of medals).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Generational_titles">Generational titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Suffix_(name)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Generational titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For a more global view of generational naming, see <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">Patronymic</a>.</div>
<p>Generational suffixes are used to distinguish persons who share the same name within a family. A generational suffix can be used informally (for disambiguation purposes, or as nicknames) and is often incorporated in legal documents.
</p><p>In the United States the most common name suffixes are senior and junior, which are written with a capital first letter (<i>Sr.</i> and <i>Jr.</i>) with or without an interceding comma. In Britain these are rarer, but when they are used the abbreviations are <i>Snr</i> and <i>Jnr</i>, respectively. The terms <i>Jr.</i> and <i>II</i> can both be correctly used if a child's first, middle, and last names are identical to his or her parent's names. When the suffixes are spelled out in full, they are always written with the first letter in lower case. Social name suffixes are far more frequently applied to men than to women (due to the common practice of women taking their husbands' surnames). In <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, the designations for a father and son with the same name are <i>père</i> (<i>father</i>) and <i>fils</i> (<i>son</i>). In <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>, common designations are <i>Júnior</i> (junior), <i>Filho</i> (son), <i>Neto</i> (grandson), and <i>Sobrinho</i> (nephew). In many other nations, it is considered highly unusual or even inauspicious to give a son the same first name(s) as his father, removing the need for such suffixes. Sons with a different middle name or initial may also be informally known as Jr. (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Will_Rogers_Jr." title="Will Rogers Jr.">William Vann Rogers Jr.</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Will_Rogers" title="Will Rogers">William Penn Adair Rogers</a>), in which case the <i>Jr.</i> is not part of the legal name.<sup id="cite_ref-Emily294_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emily294-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Jr.</i> or <i>II</i> can be used when being named after the father, however they are both pronounced differently; <i>Jr.</i> is pronounced as <i>junior</i> and <i>II</i> is pronounced as <i>the second</i>. Roman numeral suffixes can be used to name after other family members like an uncle, cousin, or ancestor (including grandfather). The suffix <i>III</i> is used after either <i>Jr</i> or <i>II</i> and, like subsequent numeric suffixes, does not need to be restricted to one family line. For example, if Randall and Patrick Dudley are brothers and if Randall has a son before Patrick, he will call his son <i>Patrick II</i>. If Patrick now has a son, his son is <i>Patrick Jr.</i> or <i>Patrick II</i> if Randall doesn't have a son named Patrick II. As time passes, the <i>III</i> suffix goes to the son of either Patrick Jr or Patrick II, whoever is first to have a son named Patrick. This is one way it is possible and correct for a Junior to father a IV. Another example involves President <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_S._Grant" title="Ulysses S. Grant">Ulysses S. Grant</a> and his sons <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Dent_Grant" title="Frederick Dent Grant">Frederick</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_S._Grant_Jr." title="Ulysses S. Grant Jr.">Ulysses Jr.</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jesse_Root_Grant_(politician)" title="Jesse Root Grant (politician)">Jesse</a>. When Frederick's son <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_S._Grant_III" title="Ulysses S. Grant III">Ulysses</a> was born in 1881, Ulysses Jr did not yet have a son named after himself. Therefore, Frederick's son was Ulysses III. Ulysses Jr's son, born afterwards in 1893, was <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_S._Grant_IV" title="Ulysses S. Grant IV">Ulysses IV</a>. Jesse's son <a href="/wiki/Chapman_Grant" title="Chapman Grant">Chapman</a> was the father of Ulysses V, as neither Ulysses III nor Ulysses IV had sons named for themselves.
</p><p>There is no hard-and-fast rule over what happens to suffixes when the most senior of the name dies. Etiquette expert and humorist <a href="/wiki/Judith_Martin" title="Judith Martin">Judith Martin</a>, for example, believes they should all move up,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> but most agree that this is up to the individual families.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although there are instances of daughters being named after their mothers and also using the suffix <i>Jr.</i> (such as <a href="/wiki/Anna_Roosevelt_Halsted" title="Anna Roosevelt Halsted">Anna Eleanor Roosevelt Jr.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winifred_Sackville_Stoner_Jr." title="Winifred Sackville Stoner Jr.">Winifred Sackville Stoner Jr.</a>, and Carolina Herrera Jr.), or after their grandmothers or aunts with the suffix <i>II</i>, this is not common. Usually, the namesake is given a different middle name and so would not need a suffix for differentiation. Furthermore, once the woman marries, she would most commonly take the surname of her husband and thus do away with the generational suffix. The title <i>Jr.</i> is sometimes used in legal documents, particularly those pertaining to wills and estates, to distinguish among female family members of the same name.
</p><p>A wife who uses the title <a href="/wiki/Mrs." title="Mrs.">Mrs.</a> would also use her husband's full name, including the suffix. In less formal situations, the suffix may be omitted. Mrs. Lon Chaney Jr. on a wedding invitation, but Mrs. L. Chaney or simply Shannon Chaney for a friendly note. Widows are entitled to retain their late husband's full names and suffixes, but divorcees may not continue to style themselves with a former husband's full name and suffix, even if they retain the surname.<sup id="cite_ref-Emily294_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Emily294-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Juniors sometimes go by their first initials and "J" for Jr., regardless of middle initial. Examples include <a href="/wiki/American_football" title="American football">American football</a> players Terrell Ray Ward Jr. (who goes by <a href="/wiki/T._J._Ward" title="T. J. Ward">T.J. Ward</a>) and Erick R. Manuel Jr., who is better known as <a href="/wiki/EJ_Manuel" title="EJ Manuel">E.J. Manuel</a>.
</p><p>Common nicknames for a junior or II include "Chip" (as in "chip off the old block"); e.g., President <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Carter" title="Jimmy Carter">James Earl "Jimmy" Carter Jr.</a>'s second son James Earl Carter III goes by "Chip".<sup id="cite_ref-ChipCarter_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ChipCarter-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Another is "Bud" (predominantly in the American South). Common nicknames for a III are "<a href="/wiki/Tripp_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Tripp (disambiguation)">Trip(p)</a>", "Trace", and "<a href="/wiki/Trey_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Trey (disambiguation)">Trey</a>" which denote that the name carrier is the third person to carry the name. Notable examples include <a href="/wiki/Green_Day" title="Green Day">Green Day</a> drummer <a href="/wiki/Tr%C3%A9_Cool" title="Tré Cool">Frank Edwin "Tré Cool" Wright III</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/South_Park" title="South Park">South Park</a></i> co-creator <a href="/wiki/Trey_Parker" title="Trey Parker">Randolph Severn "Trey" Parker III</a>, and Willard Carroll "Trey" Smith III, elder son of actor <a href="/wiki/Will_Smith" title="Will Smith">Willard Carroll "Will" Smith Jr.</a>.
</p><p>Another convention is for the elder member of a family to go by their first name, and the younger one to go by their middle name. Examples include <a href="/wiki/George_Osmond" title="George Osmond">George Virl Osmond</a>, who went by George while his eponymous son goes by the name Virl. Another example, although father and son had different middle names, is the case of Cuban evangelist <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Cruz" title="Rafael Cruz">Rafael Cruz</a>, whose son, also with the given name Rafael, is a U.S. Senator who is known by a variant of his middle name, <a href="/wiki/Ted_Cruz" title="Ted Cruz">Edward "Ted" Cruz</a>.
</p>
