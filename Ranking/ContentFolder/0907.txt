<p><b>Pontefract</b> is a historic market town in <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, England, near the <a href="/wiki/A1_road_(Great_Britain)" title="A1 road (Great Britain)">A1</a> (or Great North Road) and the <a href="/wiki/M62_motorway" title="M62 motorway">M62 motorway</a>. <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">Historically</a> part of the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>, it is one of the five towns in the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan borough</a> of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Wakefield" title="City of Wakefield">City of Wakefield</a> and has a population of 28,250,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> increasing to 30,881 at the 2011 Census.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Pontefract's motto is <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">Post mortem patris pro filio</i>, <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> for "After the death of the father, support the son", a reference to the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a> Royalist sympathies.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pontefract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the end of the 11th century, the modern <a href="/wiki/Township#United_Kingdom" title="Township">township</a> of Pontefract consisted of two distinct and separate localities known as Tanshelf and Kirkby.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  The 11th-century historian, Orderic Vitalis, recorded that, in 1069, William the Conqueror travelled across Yorkshire to put down an uprising which had sacked York, but that, upon his journey to the city, he discovered that the crossing of the <a href="/wiki/River_Aire" title="River Aire">River Aire</a> at what is modern-day Pontefract had been blockaded by a group of local <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Scandinavian" title="Anglo-Scandinavian">Anglo-Scandinavian</a> <a href="/wiki/Insurgent" class="mw-redirect" title="Insurgent">insurgents</a>, who had broken the bridge and held the opposite bank in force.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Such a crossing point would have been important in the town's early days, providing access between Pontefract and other settlements to the north and east, such as York.<sup id="cite_ref-Ayto_&amp;_Crofton_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ayto_&amp;_Crofton-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Historians believe that, in all probability, it is this historical event which gives the township of Pontefract its modern name. The name "Pontefract" originates from the Latin for "broken bridge", formed of the elements pons ('bridge') and fractus ('broken'). Pontefract was not recorded in the 1086 <i>Domesday Book</i>, but it was noted as Pontefracto in 1090, four years after the Domesday survey.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pontefract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Neolithic">Neolithic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pontefract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Neolithic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2007 a suspected extension of <a href="/wiki/Ferrybridge_Henge" title="Ferrybridge Henge">Ferrybridge Henge</a>&#160;– a <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> <a href="/wiki/Henge" title="Henge">henge</a>&#160;– was discovered near Pontefract during a survey in preparation for the construction of a row of houses. Once the survey was complete, the construction continued.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roman">Roman</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pontefract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Roman">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The modern town is situated on an old <a href="/wiki/Roman_road" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman road">Roman road</a> (now the A639), described as the "Roman Ridge", which passes south towards <a href="/wiki/Doncaster" title="Doncaster">Doncaster</a>. The Roman Ridge is believed to form part of an alternative route from Doncaster to York via Castleford and Tadcaster, as a diversion of the major Roman road <a href="/wiki/Ermine_Street" title="Ermine Street">Ermine Street</a>, which may have been used to avoid having to cross the <a href="/wiki/Humber" title="Humber">River Humber</a> near <a href="/wiki/North_Ferriby" title="North Ferriby">North Ferriby</a> during rough weather conditions over the Humber.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anglo-Scandinavian_history">Anglo-Scandinavian history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pontefract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Anglo-Scandinavian history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The period of Yorkshire's history between the demise of the Viking king Eric Bloodaxe in 954 and the arrival of the Normans in 1068 is known as the Anglo-Scandinavian age.  The modern township of Pontefract consisted of two Anglo-Scandinavian settlements, known as Tanshelf and Kirkby.  In Yorkshire, place-name locations often contain the distinctive Danish '-by' i.e. Kirkby.  And even today, the major streets in Pontefract are designated by the Danish word 'gate' e.g. Bailygate.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tanshelf_and_Kirkby">Tanshelf and Kirkby</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pontefract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tanshelf and Kirkby">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Anglo-Scandinavian township of Tanshelf recorded variously as <i>Tateshale</i>, <i>Tateshalla</i>, <i>Tateshalle</i> or <i>Tatessella</i> in the 1086 <i>Domesday Book</i> existed in the region that is today occupied by the town of Pontefract.  The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle makes its first reference to Tanshelf in the year 947 when King Eadred of England met with the ruling council of Northumbria to accept its submission. King Eadred did not enjoy Northumbria's support for long, and a year later the kingdom voted Eric Bloodaxe King of York.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When the <i>Doomsday Book</i> was commissioned by William the Conqueror in 1086 Tanshelf was still a sizeable settlement for the period. The town had a priest, 60 petty burgesses, 16 cottagers, 16 villagers and 8 smallholders, amounting to a total of 101 people.  But the actual size of the population might be as much as four or five times larger than this as the people listed are landholders, and therefore the Doomsday Book does not take their families into account.  Tanshelf also had a church, a fishery and three mills.  Archaeologists have discovered the remains of the church on The Booths in Pontefract, off North Baileygate, below the castle. The oldest grave dates from around 690. The church is likely to be at Tanshelf and may have been similar to the church at <a href="/wiki/Ledsham,_West_Yorkshire" title="Ledsham, West Yorkshire">Ledsham</a>.  The area which is now the town market place was the original meeting place of the <a href="/wiki/Osgoldcross_(wapentake)" class="mw-redirect" title="Osgoldcross (wapentake)">Osgoldcross</a> <a href="/wiki/Wapentake" class="mw-redirect" title="Wapentake">wapentake</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>  
In the Anglo-Saxon period a part of the modern township of Pontefract was known by the Anglo-Scandinavian name of Kirkby.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval">Medieval</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pontefract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Medieval">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Norman_conquest">Norman conquest</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pontefract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Norman conquest">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Pontefract_Castle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Pontefract_Castle.jpg/300px-Pontefract_Castle.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="173" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Pontefract_Castle.jpg/450px-Pontefract_Castle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Pontefract_Castle.jpg/600px-Pontefract_Castle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="944" data-file-height="544" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Pontefract_Castle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Painting of <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Castle" title="Pontefract Castle">Pontefract Castle</a> in the early 17th century by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Keirincx" title="Alexander Keirincx">Alexander Keirincx</a></div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/De_Lacy" title="De Lacy">de Lacy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_de_Lacys%27_family_tree" title="Pontefract de Lacys&#39; family tree">Pontefract de Lacys' family tree</a></div>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman conquest</a> in 1066 almost all of Yorkshire came under the ownership of followers of <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Ilbert_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ilbert-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> one of whom was <a href="/wiki/De_Lacy" title="De Lacy">Ilbert de Lacy</a> who became the owner of Tateshale (Tanshelf) where he began to build a castle.<sup id="cite_ref-Ilbert_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ilbert-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
<a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Castle" title="Pontefract Castle">Pontefract Castle</a> began as a wooden <a href="/wiki/Motte_and_bailey" class="mw-redirect" title="Motte and bailey">motte and bailey</a> castle, built before 1086 and later rebuilt in stone. The de Lacys lived in the castle for more than two centuries<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and were holders of the castle and the <a href="/wiki/Honour_(feudal_barony)" class="mw-redirect" title="Honour (feudal barony)">Honour</a> of Pontefract from 1067<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> until the death of Alice de Lacy in 1348.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Richard_II_of_England" title="Richard II of England">King Richard II</a> was murdered at the castle in 1400.<sup id="cite_ref-H2G2_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H2G2-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Little is known of the precise nature of his demise; in particular Shakespeare may have "adjusted" the facts for his own purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> There are at least three theories which attempt to explain his death:<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> He was starved to death by his keepers, he starved himself to death or he was murdered by Sir Piers (Peter) Exton on 14 February 1399 or 1400.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Robin_Hood">Robin Hood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pontefract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Robin Hood">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Fifteenth century <a href="/wiki/Robin_Hood" title="Robin Hood">Robin Hood</a> ballads place the outlaw's activities in the forest of <a href="/wiki/Barnsdale" title="Barnsdale">Barnsdale</a>, the southern edge of which borders Pontefract. In particular, the medieval ballads make specific reference to the village of <a href="/wiki/Wentbridge" title="Wentbridge">Wentbridge</a>, which is an outlying village of the modern town of Pontefract.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In addition, Michael Drayton's <i>Poly-Olbion</i> Song 28 (67–70) composed in 1622 speaks of Robin Hood's death and clearly states that the outlaw died at Saxon 'Kirkby', modern Pontefract.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  The historian S. A. La' Chance proposes that Robin Hood died at Saint Nicholas's hospital, Kirkby, which was attached to All Saints' Church.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  In commemoration of Wakefield's connections to Robin Hood, a plaque has been placed on the bridge that crosses the River Went.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_history">Early modern history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pontefract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Early modern history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Tudor">Tudor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pontefract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tudor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In Elizabethan times the castle, and Pontefract itself, was referred to as "Pomfret".<sup id="cite_ref-H2G2_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H2G2-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a>'s play <i><a href="/wiki/Richard_III_(play)" title="Richard III (play)">Richard III</a></i> mentions the castle:<br />
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Pomfret, Pomfret! O thou bloody prison,<br />
Fatal and ominous to noble peers!<br />
Within the guilty closure of thy walls<br />
Richard the second here was hack'd to death;<br />
And, for more slander to thy dismal seat,<br />
We give thee up our guiltless blood to drink.<sup id="cite_ref-H2G2_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H2G2-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Pomfret is referred to in Shakespeare's <a href="/wiki/Richard_II_(play)" title="Richard II (play)">Richard II</a> also<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<blockquote>
<p>My lord, the mind of Bolingbroke is changed:<br />
You must to Pomfret, not unto the Tower.<br />
And, madam, there is order ta'en for you;<br />
With all swift speed you must away to France
</p>
</blockquote>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Stuart_history">Stuart history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pontefract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Stuart history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_war">Civil war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pontefract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Civil war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_New_Church_within_the_Old,_All_Saints,_Bondgate,_Pontefract._-_geograph.org.uk_-_239265.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/The_New_Church_within_the_Old%2C_All_Saints%2C_Bondgate%2C_Pontefract._-_geograph.org.uk_-_239265.jpg/220px-The_New_Church_within_the_Old%2C_All_Saints%2C_Bondgate%2C_Pontefract._-_geograph.org.uk_-_239265.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="145" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/The_New_Church_within_the_Old%2C_All_Saints%2C_Bondgate%2C_Pontefract._-_geograph.org.uk_-_239265.jpg/330px-The_New_Church_within_the_Old%2C_All_Saints%2C_Bondgate%2C_Pontefract._-_geograph.org.uk_-_239265.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/The_New_Church_within_the_Old%2C_All_Saints%2C_Bondgate%2C_Pontefract._-_geograph.org.uk_-_239265.jpg/440px-The_New_Church_within_the_Old%2C_All_Saints%2C_Bondgate%2C_Pontefract._-_geograph.org.uk_-_239265.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="423" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_New_Church_within_the_Old,_All_Saints,_Bondgate,_Pontefract._-_geograph.org.uk_-_239265.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The new church within the old. After <a href="/wiki/All_Saints%27_Church,_Pontefract" title="All Saints&#39; Church, Pontefract">All Saints' Church</a> was damaged during the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">civil war</a> a new one was built within.</div></div></div>
<p>Pontefract suffered throughout the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>. In 1648–49 the castle was laid under siege by <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cromwell" title="Oliver Cromwell">Oliver Cromwell</a>, who said it was "... one of the strongest inland garrisons in the kingdom."<sup id="cite_ref-H2G2_16-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-H2G2-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Three sieges by the Parliamentarians left the town "impoverished and depopulated".<sup id="cite_ref-petition_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-petition-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In March 1649, after the third siege, Pontefract inhabitants, fearing a fourth, petitioned Parliament for the castle to be <a href="/wiki/Slighted" class="mw-redirect" title="Slighted">slighted</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-petition_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-petition-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In their view, the castle was a magnet for trouble,<sup id="cite_ref-petition_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-petition-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and in April 1649 demolition began.<sup id="cite_ref-petition_25-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-petition-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>  The ruins of the castle remain and are publicly accessible.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Pontefract_Priory_history">Pontefract Priory history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pontefract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Pontefract Priory history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Pontefract was the site of <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Priory" title="Pontefract Priory">Pontefract Priory</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Cluniac" class="mw-redirect" title="Cluniac">Cluniac</a> priory founded in 1090 by Robert de Lacy dedicated to <a href="/wiki/John_the_Evangelist" title="John the Evangelist">St John the Evangelist</a>. The priory was dissolved by royal authority in 1539. The abbey maintained the Chartularies of St John, a collection of historic documents later discovered among family papers by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Levett" title="Thomas Levett">Thomas Levett</a>, the <a href="/wiki/High_Sheriff_of_Rutland" title="High Sheriff of Rutland">High Sheriff of Rutland</a> and a native of Yorkshire, who later gave them to <a href="/wiki/Roger_Dodsworth" title="Roger Dodsworth">Roger Dodsworth</a>, an <a href="/wiki/Antiquary" class="mw-redirect" title="Antiquary">antiquary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> They were later published by the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Archaeological_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="Yorkshire Archaeological Society">Yorkshire Archaeological Society</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Pontefract has been a <a href="/wiki/Market_town" title="Market town">market town</a> since the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>; market days are Wednesday and Saturday, with a smaller market on Fridays. The covered market is open all week, except Thursday afternoons and Sundays. Thursday afternoon is half-day closing in the town. The town is called 'Ponte'/'Ponty' by its citizens and sometimes jokingly referred to as Ponte Carlo, in reference to <a href="/wiki/Monte_Carlo" title="Monte Carlo">Monte Carlo</a>. This theme is continued in the name of bars in the <a href="/wiki/Xscape_(building)" title="Xscape (building)">Xscape</a> complex, <a href="/wiki/Glasshoughton" title="Glasshoughton">Glasshoughton</a> between Pontefract and <a href="/wiki/Castleford" title="Castleford">Castleford</a>, referred to locally as 'Cas Vegas'. It is reported that Pontefract once held the British record for the town with the most pubs per square mile:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2013)">by whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> indeed, numerous pubs still survive in the town centre in particular, for example Beastfair Vaults, the Liquorice Bush, the Red Lion, the Malt Shovel and the Blackmoor Head. A Wetherspoon public house opened on Horsefair in 2010.
</p><p>Pontefract's deep, sandy soil makes it one of the few British places in which <a href="/wiki/Liquorice" title="Liquorice">liquorice</a> can successfully be grown.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The town has a liquorice-sweet industry; and the famous <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_cake" title="Pontefract cake">Pontefract cakes</a> are produced, though the liquorice plant itself is no longer grown there. The town's two liquorice factories are owned by <a href="/wiki/Haribo" title="Haribo">Haribo</a> (formerly known as Dunhills) and Tangerine Confectionery (formerly part of the Cadbury's Group as Monkhill Confectionery, and before that Wilkinson's), respectively. A Liquorice Festival is held annually. <a href="/wiki/Poet_laureate" title="Poet laureate">Poet laureate</a> <a href="/wiki/Sir_John_Betjeman" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir John Betjeman">Sir John Betjeman</a> wrote a poem entitled "The Licorice Fields at Pontefract". In 2012 local farmer Robert Copley announced that he would be re-introducing a liquorice crop to Pontefract.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Close by is the coal-fired <a href="/wiki/Ferrybridge_power_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferrybridge power station">power station</a> at <a href="/wiki/Ferrybridge" title="Ferrybridge">Ferrybridge</a>, although the local <a href="/wiki/Coal_mining" title="Coal mining">coal mines</a> largely closed in the 1990s, contributing to high unemployment in the local area. The final colliery, Prince of Wales Colliery, closed in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are a number of supermarkets in Pontefract which include a <a href="/wiki/Tesco" title="Tesco">Tesco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morrisons" title="Morrisons">Morrisons</a> which are located opposite each other, and an <a href="/wiki/Asda" title="Asda">Asda</a>, which was originally a Kwik Save store, a short distance outside the town centre. The secondary schools in the town are Carleton Community High School in Carleton, and the King's School on Mill Hill Lane, both for pupils aged 11–16. A sixth-form college, <a href="/wiki/NEW_College,_Pontefract" title="NEW College, Pontefract">NEW College, Pontefract</a>, is located on Park Lane.
</p><p>The old Pontefract General Infirmary on Southgate (pictured) was a general hospital; it is the place at which serial killer <a href="/wiki/Harold_Shipman" title="Harold Shipman">Harold Shipman</a> began to murder his elderly patients. Beneath this building is an old <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Hermitage" title="Pontefract Hermitage">hermitage</a>, open to the public on certain days. <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Museum" title="Pontefract Museum">Pontefract Museum</a>, from which the hermitage schedule can be obtained, is in the town centre, housed in the former <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_library" title="Carnegie library">Carnegie library</a>. A new hospital was built on Friarwood and opened in 2010 with the new name of <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Hospital" title="Pontefract Hospital">Pontefract Hospital</a> and there is now a modern library building.
</p><p>Near to the hospital is Friarwood Valley Gardens, a rose garden, a sensory garden, a pinhole camera (formerly an aviary and earlier a Georgian gambling den), and an avenue of cherry trees. Although the trees continue to attract admiration, the gardens had become quite depleted though Friends of Friarwood Valley Gardens now work to improve this hidden gem.
</p><p>Pontefract has three railway stations: <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Baghill_railway_station" title="Pontefract Baghill railway station">Pontefract Baghill</a>, on the <a href="/wiki/Dearne_Valley_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Dearne Valley Line">Dearne Valley Line</a>, which connects <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Monkhill_railway_station" title="Pontefract Monkhill railway station">Pontefract Monkhill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Tanshelf_railway_station" title="Pontefract Tanshelf railway station">Pontefract Tanshelf</a>, which connects with <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a>. There are also rail services from Bradford to London that stop at <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Monkhill" class="mw-redirect" title="Pontefract Monkhill">Pontefract Monkhill</a>.
</p><p>The local police force is West Yorkshire Police, with the town's neighbourhood policing team being situated at the new fire station on Stumpcross Lane. The original police station situated in Sessions House yard is due for closure since the new divisional headquarters for the Wakefield District opened in Normanton and the neighbouring magistrates' court has moved over to Wakefield.
</p><p>Fire cover is provided by West Yorkshire Fire and Rescue with one pump (sometimes two) based at Pontefract Fire Station. Formerly located on Stuart Road in the town centre, the station has now moved to a new site at Stumpcross Lane, by the A645 road at the town's eastern edge. The new fire station also provides cover for Knottingley, that town's fire station having been closed as part of the merging of fire cover for Pontefract and Knottingley.
</p><p>The Territorial Army, Army Cadets and Air Training Corps all have a presence within the town and are based at the historic <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Barracks" title="Pontefract Barracks">Pontefract Barracks</a> building on Wakefield Road. It now houses a Rifles Regiment Recruitment team.
</p>
<h2><span id="Media.2C_arts_and_entertainment"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Media,_arts_and_entertainment">Media, arts and entertainment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pontefract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Media, arts and entertainment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The local newspaper is the <i>Pontefract and Castleford Express</i>. Pontefract is known for its 'down-to-earth' nightlife.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Venues include The Red Lion, the Green Dragon, the Tap and Barrel, The Broken Bridge (<a href="/wiki/Wetherspoons" title="Wetherspoons">Wetherspoons</a>), the Malt Shovel, and the Ponty Tavern (formally The Blackmoor Head) or <i>Blacky</i> in local terms. In August 2012 one of Pontefract's oldest nightclubs, Kiko's, re-opened its doors to the public after being closed since 2007, then closed again in 2013 due to the building being too far away from the town &amp; other nightlife spots. Kiko's once hosted many notable performances back in the 1970s and 80s from notable bands such as The <a href="/wiki/Bay_City_Rollers" title="Bay City Rollers">Bay City Rollers</a>. As of summer 2019 the building is now vacant &amp; damaged inside awaiting revival, venue change or demolition.
</p><p>Novelist <a href="/wiki/Jack_Vance" title="Jack Vance">Jack Vance</a>, in the "<a href="/wiki/Demon_Princes" title="Demon Princes">Demon Princes</a>" cycle has named the capital of Aloysius, the main planet in the Vega system, after Pontefract. The hero of the series, Kirth Gersen, has his residence there.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ridings_FM" title="Ridings FM">Ridings FM</a> is the town's local commercial radio station, Launched in October 1999, <a href="/wiki/Ridings_FM" title="Ridings FM">Ridings FM</a> can be heard across Pontefract on 106.8 FM. The station serves the wider <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a> District. The station has a close relationship with the community and is actively involved in local charities' fundraising efforts.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pontefract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For local government purposes the town lies in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Wakefield" title="City of Wakefield">City of Wakefield</a>, coming under the governance of <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Council" title="Wakefield Council">Wakefield Council</a>. For this purpose it is divided into two electoral wards, Pontefract North and Pontefract South. Pontefract South is currently represented by three Labour councillors with North ward represented by three Labour councillors [as of September 2015].<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> South ward is a marginal ward, containing relatively affluent suburbs of Pontefract and outlying villages such as Darrington, combined with less wealthy areas such as Chequerfield, whilst North Ward includes parts of Monkhill, Ladybalk, Nevison and Myson Chair.
</p><p>From 1978 to 1997 the local ex-miner and former local <a href="/wiki/National_Union_of_Mineworkers_(Great_Britain)" title="National Union of Mineworkers (Great Britain)">NUM</a> branch leader <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Lofthouse,_Baron_Lofthouse_of_Pontefract" class="mw-redirect" title="Geoffrey Lofthouse, Baron Lofthouse of Pontefract">Geoff Lofthouse</a> (18 December 1925&#160;– 1 November 2012) was MP for the former constituency of Pontefract and Castleford. During this time he rose to the position of Deputy Speaker of the House of Commons. When the general election of 1997 was called he stood down to allow <a href="/wiki/Yvette_Cooper" title="Yvette Cooper">Yvette Cooper</a> to be selected as the Labour Party candidate for that election. He was made a peer on 11 June 1997.
</p><p>Yvette Cooper <a href="/wiki/Member_of_Parliament" class="mw-redirect" title="Member of Parliament">MP</a>, was elected MP for the <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_and_Castleford_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Pontefract and Castleford (UK Parliament constituency)">Pontefract and Castleford constituency</a> at the <a href="/wiki/1997_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="1997 United Kingdom general election">1997 general election</a>. Cooper held a number of positions in the Labour governments up to 2010, followed by Shadow Cabinet roles (most notably <a href="/wiki/Shadow_Home_Secretary" title="Shadow Home Secretary">Shadow Home Secretary</a>) after the election of that year, but returned to the back benches following the <a href="/wiki/2015_Labour_Party_leadership_election_(UK)" title="2015 Labour Party leadership election (UK)">Labour leadership election of 2015</a>. Pontefract and Castleford was merged with the <a href="/wiki/Normanton_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Normanton (UK Parliament constituency)">Normanton constituency</a> in a boundary change before the <a href="/wiki/2010_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="2010 United Kingdom general election">2010 general election</a>.
</p><p>
In her maiden speech to the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Commons_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="House of Commons of the United Kingdom">House of Commons</a>, Cooper said:</p><blockquote><p>It is true that my constituency is plagued by unemployment, but I represent hard-working people who are proud of their strong communities and who have fought hard across generations to defend them. They are proud of their socialist traditions, and have fought for a better future for their children and their grandchildren. In the Middle Ages, that early egalitarian, the real <a href="/wiki/Robin_Hood" title="Robin Hood">Robin Hood</a>, lived, so we maintain, in the <a href="/wiki/Wentbridge" title="Wentbridge">Vale of Wentbridge</a> to the south of Pontefract. It was a great base from which to hassle the travelling <a href="/wiki/Fat_cat_(term)" title="Fat cat (term)">fat cats</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Great_North_Road_(Great_Britain)" title="Great North Road (Great Britain)">Great North Road</a>.</p></blockquote>
<p>The seat, which has a history of mining and industry, has consistently returned Labour MPs at <a href="/wiki/General_elections" class="mw-redirect" title="General elections">general elections</a>. <a href="/wiki/Yvette_Cooper" title="Yvette Cooper">Yvette Cooper</a> polled 59.5% of the vote in the <a href="/wiki/2017_United_Kingdom_general_election" title="2017 United Kingdom general election">2017 general election</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pontefract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town is home to many sports including cricket, football and squash. Prominent squash players <a href="/wiki/Lee_Beachill" title="Lee Beachill">Lee Beachill</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Willstrop" title="James Willstrop">James Willstrop</a> both train at Pontefract Squash Centre. Notable institutions are horse racing at <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Racecourse" title="Pontefract Racecourse">Pontefract Racecourse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Featherstone_Rovers" title="Featherstone Rovers">Featherstone Rovers</a>, the area's professional rugby league club.
</p><p>Pontefract Racecourse is the longest continuous horse racing circuit in Europe at 2&#160;miles 125&#160;yards (3,333&#160;m; 16.57 furlongs).<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> It stages flat racing between the end of March and the end of October. Pontefract swimming pool is on Stuart Road.
</p><p>Pontefract has its own non-league football club, <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Collieries_F.C." title="Pontefract Collieries F.C.">Pontefract Collieries F.C.</a>, which was founded in 1958 and plays adjacent to the former Prince of Wales Colliery off Beechnut Lane. The team, known locally as "Ponte Colls" play in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Premier_League" title="Northern Premier League">Northern Premier League</a> Division One North West (correct as of the start of the 2019/20 season).
Pontefract is also home to the Pontefract Knights rugby league football club.
</p><p>Pontefract RUFC is based at Moor Lane Carleton.  It runs three senior sides as well as a number of junior and girls teams.  Rugby Union has been played in the town since the 19th Century when Pontefract won the Yorkshire Cup.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pontefract&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Pontefract lies in close proximity to the <a href="/wiki/A1_road_(Great_Britain)" title="A1 road (Great Britain)">A1</a> and the <a href="/wiki/M62_motorway" title="M62 motorway">M62</a>.  Bus transport is provided by <a href="/wiki/Arriva_Yorkshire" title="Arriva Yorkshire">Arriva Yorkshire</a>, the main hub in the town for this being <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_bus_station" title="Pontefract bus station">Pontefract bus station</a>.  The town has direct rail connections with <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goole" title="Goole">Goole</a>, <a href="/wiki/Castleford,_West_Yorkshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Castleford, West Yorkshire">Castleford</a>, <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a> from the town's three railway stations; <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Monkhill_railway_station" title="Pontefract Monkhill railway station">Pontefract Monkhill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Tanshelf_railway_station" title="Pontefract Tanshelf railway station">Pontefract Tanshelf</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Baghill_railway_station" title="Pontefract Baghill railway station">Pontefract Baghill</a>.  The closest airports are <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Bradford_Airport" title="Leeds Bradford Airport">Leeds Bradford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_Sheffield_Airport" title="Doncaster Sheffield Airport">Doncaster Sheffield</a>
</p>
