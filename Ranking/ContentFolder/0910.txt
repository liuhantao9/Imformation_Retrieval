<p><b>Shipley</b> is a commuter suburb and small town in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Bradford" title="City of Bradford">City of Bradford</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, England, by the <a href="/wiki/River_Aire" title="River Aire">River Aire</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Leeds_and_Liverpool_Canal" title="Leeds and Liverpool Canal">Leeds and Liverpool Canal</a>, north of <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a>. The population of the Shipley ward on Bradford City Council taken at the 2011 Census was 15,483.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Before 1974 Shipley was an <a href="/wiki/Urban_district_(Great_Britain_and_Ireland)" title="Urban district (Great Britain and Ireland)">urban district</a> in the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>. The town forms a continuous urban area with Bradford.  It has a population of approximately 28,162.<sup id="cite_ref-ONS_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ONS-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">place-name</a> 'Shipley' derives from two words - the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>scīp</i> ('sheep', a <a href="/wiki/Northumbrian_dialect" title="Northumbrian dialect">Northumbrian dialect</a> form, contrasting with the <a href="/wiki/Anglian_dialects#Dialects" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglian dialects">Anglian dialect</a> form <i>scēp</i> which underlies modern English <i>sheep</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-CamDict_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CamDict-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and <i>lēah</i> meaning either "a forest, wood, glade, clearing" or, later, "a pasture, meadow".<sup id="cite_ref-Nott_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nott-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>  It has therefore been variously defined as "forest clearing used for sheep"<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows1_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> or "sheep field".<sup id="cite_ref-T&amp;A1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-T&amp;A1-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shipley appears to have first been settled in the late <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows1_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows1-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and is mentioned in the <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> of 1086, in the form <i>Scipelei(a)</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-CamDict_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CamDict-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Its early history relies on the records of a succession of <a href="/wiki/Lord_of_the_Manor" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord of the Manor">Lords of the Manor</a>, not all of whom were in permanent residence. The rolls of the <a href="/wiki/Manor_court" class="mw-redirect" title="Manor court">manor court</a> have been missing since the 18th&#160;century,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran19845_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran19845-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> leaving the records incomplete. In the 12th&#160;century, 'Adam, son of Peter', an early Lord of the Manor, granted <a href="/wiki/Grazing" title="Grazing">grazing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iron_ore" title="Iron ore">iron ore</a> mining rights to the monks of <a href="/wiki/Rievaulx_Abbey" title="Rievaulx Abbey">Rievaulx Abbey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran19845_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran19845-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>  Through the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> the Lords were the 'Earls of Ormande' (sic), possibly the Irish <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Ormond_(Ireland)" title="Earl of Ormond (Ireland)">Earls of Ormond</a>, followed by the <a href="/wiki/William_Gascoigne" title="William Gascoigne">Gascoigne family</a>. In 1495, Rosamund Gascoigne, a daughter of one of the William Gascoignes who held the title, married Robert Rawson, thought to be related to the Rawson family of <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a>, after whom one of the city's markets is named. Their son, William, married a cousin, Agnes Gascoigne, and through the marriage the Rawson family inherited the manor in 1570.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran19845–7_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran19845–7-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Rawsons lived at Over Hall known as the Manor House, on the site of the current <a href="/wiki/Town_hall" class="mw-redirect" title="Town hall">town hall</a>.  The manor estates extended to Northcliff.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran198412_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran198412-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The family had interests in <a href="/wiki/Halifax,_West_Yorkshire" title="Halifax, West Yorkshire">Halifax</a> and moved there in the early 18th&#160;century, retaining their Shipley estates until the last male heir died in 1745.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran19848_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran19848-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the 19th&#160;century the Rawson estates and those of the Fields, another prominent land-owning family, had become the property of the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Rosse" title="Earl of Rosse">Earl of Rosse</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran198437_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran198437-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> who had extensive holdings in <a href="/wiki/Heaton,_West_Yorkshire" title="Heaton, West Yorkshire">Heaton</a>.  His legacy has endured in the name of a public house on the main Bradford to Keighley road, and Rossefield School in Heaton. Of the lower orders at this time not much is known, but there was relief housing offered at the town's expense near Crowghyll.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran198410_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran198410-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_Revolution">Industrial Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Industrial Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Canal_side,_Shipley_-_geograph.org.uk_-_347005.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Mill buildings alongside the Leeds and Liverpool Canal in Shipley" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Canal_side%2C_Shipley_-_geograph.org.uk_-_347005.jpg/220px-Canal_side%2C_Shipley_-_geograph.org.uk_-_347005.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Canal_side%2C_Shipley_-_geograph.org.uk_-_347005.jpg/330px-Canal_side%2C_Shipley_-_geograph.org.uk_-_347005.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Canal_side%2C_Shipley_-_geograph.org.uk_-_347005.jpg/440px-Canal_side%2C_Shipley_-_geograph.org.uk_-_347005.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="479" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Canal_side,_Shipley_-_geograph.org.uk_-_347005.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Canal-side mills, Shipley</div></div></div>
<p>Shipley was shaped largely by the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> and, in particular, the growth of the <a href="/wiki/Textile_industry" title="Textile industry">textile industry</a>. Textile manufacture dates from pre-industrial times.  As the place name indicates, Shipley had a history as sheep grazing land, so wool was plentiful, and the River Aire was a ready source of water for powering water mills and cleaning processes.  There was a <a href="/wiki/Fulling" title="Fulling">fulling</a> mill in Shipley by 1500 and two more by 1559.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran198413_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran198413-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>  Another mill was built by the Dixon family on the banks of the Aire in 1635.  New Mill on the far side of Hirst Wood was built in the 1740s and by the late 18th&#160;century between 9,000 and 10,000 pieces of broadcloth were being fulled annually at Shipley's mills.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran198415_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran198415-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  Much work was undertaken in workers' cottages which had 'loomshops' for spinning yarn.  Home workshops were once a common sight along the River Aire and often had external flights of steps. Examples can be seen in the cottages at Jane Hills along the canal in <a href="/wiki/Saltaire" title="Saltaire">Saltaire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran198414_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran198414-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The industrial era ended <a href="/wiki/Cottage_industry" class="mw-redirect" title="Cottage industry">cottage industry</a>. Providence Mill, one of the first steam-driven mills was built for Denby Bros. in 1796.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows2_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows2-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  Other spinning mills followed, including Ashley Mill, Prospect Mill, Red Beck Mill on Heaton Beck (c. 1815), Well Croft Mill (c. 1840s) and Whiting Mill on Briggate.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran198421–22_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran198421–22-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The smaller mills gave way to larger premises which could combine all the processes of <a href="/wiki/Worsted" title="Worsted">worsted</a> production on one site. The first was Joseph Hargreaves' Airedale Mills (demolished 1970s), <a href="/wiki/Salts_Mill" title="Salts Mill">Salts Mill</a> (built 1853 and now a gallery and restaurant complex), an enlarged Well Croft Mill (demolished 1950s) and Victoria Mills near the canal...<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran198423_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran198423-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  Hargreaves employed 1,250, Salt initially 2,500 and by 1876 total employment in the mills was 6,900.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran198423_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran198423-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The growth in textile production stimulated the growth of associated supply industries. Other local employers included <a href="/wiki/Loom" title="Loom">loom</a> makers, Lee and Crabtree, WP Butterfield's galvanised containers and J. Parkinson and Sons machine tool makers.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth19967_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth19967-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The other major effect of industrialisation was the vast expansion in housing stock.  <a href="/wiki/Titus_Salt" title="Titus Salt">Titus Salt</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Saltaire" title="Saltaire">Saltaire</a> is an example of a <a href="/wiki/Model_village" title="Model village">model village</a>, and Hargreaves had cottages built for his workers around the town centre and his mill.  He built 92 <a href="/wiki/Back-to-back_houses" class="mw-redirect" title="Back-to-back houses">back-to-back houses</a> along Market Street and Central Avenue in an area which came to be called <i>Hargreaves Square</i> or <i>The Square</i>.  The houses were built by filling in the old courtyards.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran198432_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran198432-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>  The population of the township grew from 1,214 in 1822<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEThe_Edinburgh_Gazetteer1822572_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThe_Edinburgh_Gazetteer1822572-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> to just over 3,000 in 1851 to 10,000 by 1869.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWatson19894_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWatson19894-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was then the landowning families—the Rosses, the Crompton-Stansfields and the Wainmans—took advantage of the demand for housing by selling their less productive land on Low Moor and High Moor.  Houses for the better off were built in Sunny Bank and Hall Royd in the 1840s, 1850s and 1860s.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran198435_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran198435-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>  Kirkgate was lined with villas from the 1860s, some of which still stand.  Middle-class houses were built in the Nab Wood and Moorhead districts.  In 1870 a tranche of land in Moorhead was sold by the Countess of Rosse to build five streets of terraces.  The public house on Saltaire Roundabout that bears her name dates from that time.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWatson19894–5_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWatson19894–5-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-war_redevelopment">Post-war redevelopment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Post-war redevelopment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shipley_Market_Place.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Shipley_Market_Place.jpg/220px-Shipley_Market_Place.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Shipley_Market_Place.jpg/330px-Shipley_Market_Place.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Shipley_Market_Place.jpg/440px-Shipley_Market_Place.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shipley_Market_Place.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Shipley Market Place</div></div></div>
<p>The decline of the textile industry saw the demolition of many mills, only Salts Mill and Victoria Mills remain and have been converted to other uses.
</p><p>Of more concern in the immediate <a href="/wiki/Post-war" title="Post-war">post-war</a> period was the deteriorating housing stock. In the 1950s, the back-to-backs of Hargreaves' Square were condemned as slums and the site redeveloped. The redevelopment removed several historic buildings – Shipley Old Hall (1593), at the junction of Kirkgate and Manor Lane<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199615_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199615-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and of which a few fragments of roof drainage and a roof truss survive in Crowghyll Park,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows6_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows6-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Shipley Hall (1734), which stood at the junction of Market Street and Otley Road became the headquarters of Windhill <a href="/wiki/Cooperative_society" class="mw-redirect" title="Cooperative society">Cooperative Society</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199615_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199615-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> and possibly Hudson Fold House (1629).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran198438_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran198438-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>  Of the major <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian</a> town centre buildings, only the Old Bradford Bank (now Barclays) and Sun Hotel remain.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199637_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199637-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
The slums were replaced with low-rise modern retail outlets, a central square serves as an outdoor market and an underground indoor market is situated beneath a tall, <a href="/wiki/Brutalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Brutalist">brutalist</a> market hall tower which is a visible landmark for many miles around. Until recently the tower had a 'man' striking a bell to mark the hours.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows15_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows15-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A second phase of clearance in 1978 saw the construction of Asda, a library, swimming pool and health centre. Croft House (1729), a stone built farmhouse which was converted to a school and then subsequently used as the <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)" title="Labour Party (UK)">Labour Party</a> headquarters was a casualty of this development. By 1970 2,900 slum houses had been demolished.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth1996104–105_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth1996104–105-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Otley and Leeds Roads were widened in the early 1970s, at the expense of the Fox and Hounds Hotel after which Shipley's main road junction, Fox Corner, was named.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows2_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows2-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Shipley is located at an important crossing of the <a href="/wiki/River_Aire" title="River Aire">River Aire</a>, where the route from <a href="/wiki/Otley" title="Otley">Otley</a> to <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a> crosses the route from <a href="/wiki/Skipton" title="Skipton">Skipton</a> to <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>. It is sheltered by the millstone crags of <a href="/wiki/Wrose" title="Wrose">Wrose</a> and <a href="/wiki/Windhill" title="Windhill">Windhill</a> to the east, and to the north by <a href="/wiki/Baildon" title="Baildon">Baildon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guiseley" title="Guiseley">Hawksworth</a> Moors.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth19966_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth19966-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Early development in Shipley was centred on the <a href="/wiki/Intersection_(road)" title="Intersection (road)">crossroads</a>, locally known today as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fox_Corner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fox Corner (page does not exist)">Fox Corner</a> after the former Fox and Hounds <a href="/wiki/Public_house" class="mw-redirect" title="Public house">pub</a> that stood there.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199610_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199610-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran1984_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran1984-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (October 2017)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Here, as today, the route from <a href="/wiki/Otley" title="Otley">Otley</a> to <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a> crossed the route from <a href="/wiki/Skipton" title="Skipton">Skipton</a> to <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> at an important crossing of the <a href="/wiki/River_Aire" title="River Aire">River Aire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran1984_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran1984-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="This citation requires a reference to the specific page or range of pages in which the material appears. (October 2017)">page&#160;needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The present Kirkgate and Manor Lane (then known as Sower Lane) were probably no more than tracks.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran19845_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran19845-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval times</a>, Shipley consisted of the settlement around the crossroads, and the <a href="/wiki/Enclosure" title="Enclosure">unenclosed</a> fields at Shipley Fields and the Hirst which were collectively farmed. Beyond these lay the Low Moor, which ran from the Crowghyll to the former Saltaire roundabout (now a junction on the Bradford to Keighley road), in the approximate area of modern day Wycliffe,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth19966_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth19966-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Barren_vegetation" title="Barren vegetation">wasteland</a> of High Moor (from Saltaire roundabout, through Moorhead, as far as New Brighton and Noon Nick).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth19966_31-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth19966-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> These areas were steep, rocky land, unsuitable for farming.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWatson19891_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWatson19891-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> By 1600 at the latest, the open fields had been enclosed and given way to individual farms.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran198411_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran198411-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The town was bounded to the north by the <a href="/wiki/River_Aire" title="River Aire">River Aire</a>, to the east by <a href="/wiki/Bradford_Beck" title="Bradford Beck">Bradford Beck</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Cottingley,_Bradford" title="Cottingley, Bradford">Cottingley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heaton,_West_Yorkshire" title="Heaton, West Yorkshire">Heaton</a> lying beyond its western and southern boundaries.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran19845_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran19845-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Outlying districts, such as Windhill, were not part of Shipley until the 19th&#160;century. <a href="/wiki/Saltaire" title="Saltaire">Saltaire</a> became part of Shipley after its foundation in the 1860s, while Windhill, which had previously been part of <a href="/wiki/Idle" class="mw-redirect" title="Idle">Idle</a>, became part of the Shipley Urban District in 1894.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran198437_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran198437-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Shipley_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Shipley (UK Parliament constituency)">Shipley (UK Parliament constituency)</a></div>
<p>Shipley was historically a <a href="/wiki/Township_(England)" title="Township (England)">township</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chapelry" title="Chapelry">chapelry</a> in the large ancient parish of Bradford.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>  Shipley Local Board was formed in 1853.  Originally the board met at the Sun Hotel near the market.  In 1880, it moved to the old Manor House, until it was demolished in 1915.  Shipley became a separate <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> in 1866.  In 1894, Shipley Urban District Council was constituted with 15 councillors,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199619–100_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199619–100-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> and Shipley incorporated the Windhill district, formerly part of Idle.  An attempt was made to gain borough status in 1898, but failed.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows11_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows11-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>  Shipley Town Hall was built in 1932, as part of a scheme to relieve high unemployment during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a> and was opened by the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Harewood" title="Earl of Harewood">Earl of Harewood</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199615_25-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199615-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>  It became the seat of Shipley's administration for the next four decades.
</p><p>For many years, Shipley opposed joining Bradford for local government purposes whenever it was proposed.  A large protest march marked the third attempt in 1937.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199647_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199647-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>  In 1974 after local government reorganisation, merger into Bradford seemed inevitable, and no resistance was offered.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth1996101_40-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth1996101-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Shipley_parliamentary_constituency" class="mw-redirect" title="Shipley parliamentary constituency">Shipley parliamentary constituency</a> was created in 1885, and its first MP was <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Craven_(politician)" title="Joseph Craven (politician)">Joseph Craven</a>.
</p><p>A campaign for Shipley to have a Town Council was launched in 2018 and is currently collecting signatures to trigger a Governance Review by Bradford Metropolitan District Council.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_and_retail">Commercial and retail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Commercial and retail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Shipley_Market,_rear1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Shipley_Market%2C_rear1.jpg/220px-Shipley_Market%2C_rear1.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Shipley_Market%2C_rear1.jpg/330px-Shipley_Market%2C_rear1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Shipley_Market%2C_rear1.jpg/440px-Shipley_Market%2C_rear1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Shipley_Market,_rear1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Shipley market hall</div></div></div>
<p>Shipley is dominantly residential in character serving as a commuter suburb of larger urban employment centres in Bradford and Leeds. Manufacturing activity includes information technology specialist <a href="/wiki/Arris_International" title="Arris International">ARRIS</a> located in the Salts Mill complex.  Marlin Windows, HC Slingsby and the offices of the Bradford Health Authority also feature among the larger employers in the town.
</p><p>The town has one large scale supermarket, <a href="/wiki/Asda" title="Asda">Asda</a> in the town centre, but also supports smaller scale supermarkets and convenience shops. An open air market is a feature of the main commercial centre of the town as well as a covered market hall known for its landmark clock tower and 1960s <a href="/wiki/Brutalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Brutalist">brutalist</a> architecture. Other shops in the same precinct include an <a href="/wiki/Arndale_Centre" title="Arndale Centre">Arndale Centre</a>, retailers such as <a href="/wiki/Laura_Ashley_plc" title="Laura Ashley plc">Laura Ashley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argos_(shop)" class="mw-redirect" title="Argos (shop)">Argos</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Boots_the_Chemist" class="mw-redirect" title="Boots the Chemist">Boots</a>. A pedestrian precinct with some shops and leisure businesses links Asda and its multi-storey free car park with Market Square. This area also includes the Shipley Library and the <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.kirkgatecentre.org.uk">Kirkgate Centre</a>, the town's main cultural focus offering regular a range of community activities in addition to holding cultural events such as live music, a regular alternative market and world cinema.
</p><p>The town's secondary commercial centre, Gordon Terrace, part of the historic Saltaire Village development, features independent food and fashion retailers, as well as numerous restaurants and cafes.  The town has a large volume of through vehicle traffic as it is on two of the main routes between Bradford, Leeds and the Aire Valley towns of <a href="/wiki/Bingley" title="Bingley">Bingley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Keighley" title="Keighley">Keighley</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Skipton" title="Skipton">Skipton</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Visitor_attractions">Visitor attractions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Visitor attractions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Saltaire_from_Leeds_and_Liverpool_Canal.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Saltaire_from_Leeds_and_Liverpool_Canal.jpg/220px-Saltaire_from_Leeds_and_Liverpool_Canal.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Saltaire_from_Leeds_and_Liverpool_Canal.jpg/330px-Saltaire_from_Leeds_and_Liverpool_Canal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Saltaire_from_Leeds_and_Liverpool_Canal.jpg/440px-Saltaire_from_Leeds_and_Liverpool_Canal.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Saltaire_from_Leeds_and_Liverpool_Canal.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Salts_Mill" title="Salts Mill">Salts Mill</a> alongside the Leeds and Liverpool Canal</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Saltaire" title="Saltaire">Saltaire</a></div>
<p>The village of <a href="/wiki/Saltaire" title="Saltaire">Saltaire</a> located in Shipley is a <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> designated <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a> incorporating the Victorian era <a href="/wiki/Salts_Mill" title="Salts Mill">Salts Mill</a> and associated residential district.  Located by the <a href="/wiki/River_Aire" title="River Aire">River Aire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leeds_and_Liverpool_Canal" title="Leeds and Liverpool Canal">Leeds and Liverpool Canal</a> the model village was planned by industrialist Sir <a href="/wiki/Titus_Salt" title="Titus Salt">Titus Salt</a> as a processing facility for <a href="/wiki/Alpaca" title="Alpaca">alpaca</a> woollen cloth and as residential accommodation for his workforce.  <a href="/wiki/Salts_Mill" title="Salts Mill">Salts Mill</a> is no longer used for textile production, but now contains the 1853 Gallery, housing many works by the artist <a href="/wiki/David_Hockney" title="David Hockney">David Hockney</a>, a variety of shops, restaurants and local businesses, including <a href="/wiki/Pace_Micro_Technology" class="mw-redirect" title="Pace Micro Technology">Pace Micro Technology</a>. Salts Mill is accessed via the nearby <a href="/wiki/Saltaire_railway_station" title="Saltaire railway station">Saltaire railway station</a> and together with the stone built terraced houses, ornate Victorian era civic buildings and Roberts Park, draws significant numbers of tourists to the area.
</p><p>To the north across the River Aire, is <i>Shipley Glen</i> ( "glen" refers to the little valley beneath a ridge).  It has long been a popular beauty spot, and in 1895 the <a href="/wiki/Shipley_Glen_Tramway" title="Shipley Glen Tramway">Shipley Glen Tramway</a> was built to carry visitors up to the top.  The tramway has weathered periods of neglect and closure, but in 2012 it ran most weekends through the summer, staffed by volunteers.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Overview_of_Shipley,_West_Yorkshire.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Overview_of_Shipley%2C_West_Yorkshire.jpg/220px-Overview_of_Shipley%2C_West_Yorkshire.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Overview_of_Shipley%2C_West_Yorkshire.jpg/330px-Overview_of_Shipley%2C_West_Yorkshire.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Overview_of_Shipley%2C_West_Yorkshire.jpg/440px-Overview_of_Shipley%2C_West_Yorkshire.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1943" data-file-height="1295" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Overview_of_Shipley,_West_Yorkshire.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Looking over Shipley from Northcliffe</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parks_and_gardens">Parks and gardens</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Parks and gardens">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Crowghyll Park was once a quarry and the town's refuse dump.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199666_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199666-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>  The land was given to church wardens in lieu of common rights when Shipley Common was enclosed and in 1889 it was landscaped. A public playground was opened by Mrs Titus Salt in 1890.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199625_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199625-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> A larger recreation area with playing fields, allotments, woods, and a private golf club is situated on the hill at <a href="/wiki/Northcliffe_Park" title="Northcliffe Park">Northcliffe</a>. The woods and playing fields were opened to the public by <a href="/wiki/Henry_Norman_Rae" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Norman Rae">Norman Rae</a> MP and the playing fields are named after him.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199649_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199649-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> In the village of Saltaire is <a href="/wiki/Roberts_Park,_Saltaire" title="Roberts Park, Saltaire">Roberts Park</a>, built by Sir Titus Salt for his workers' recreation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatre_and_cinema">Theatre and cinema</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Theatre and cinema">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Hall,_Saltaire" title="Victoria Hall, Saltaire">Victoria Hall</a> in Saltaire is a concert venue, hosting bands such as <a href="/wiki/Fairport_Convention" title="Fairport Convention">Fairport Convention</a>. However, there were once a number of entertainment establishments within the district:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Queen's Palace</i> – Formerly a temperance coffee house, an institution called Queens' Palace Theatre was sited on Briggate around the turn of the 20th&#160;century.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199610_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199610-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>  It held twice nightly variety shows at 7&#160;pm and 9&#160;pm.  In December 1915 it became Shipley Picture House and remained as a cinema until August 1932. .<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199628_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199628-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>  The building was demolished following a fire in 1960.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows8_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows8-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Glen Royal</i> – The same fate met its successor, the Glen Royal Cinema, which was sited slightly further along Briggate.  This 1,200 seat "showpiece super-cinema"<sup id="cite_ref-Greenhalf1_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenhalf1-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> opened with a showing of <a href="/wiki/Emma_(1932_film)" title="Emma (1932 film)">Emma</a> on 5 September 1932 and was Shipley's premier cinema during the <a href="/wiki/Golden_Age_of_Hollywood" class="mw-redirect" title="Golden Age of Hollywood">Golden Age of Hollywood</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199628_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199628-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>  A <a href="/wiki/Hammond_organ" title="Hammond organ">Hammond organ</a> was installed in 1936 and it became the first cinema in the area after the Ritz in Bradford to show <a href="/wiki/3D_film" title="3D film">3D film</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Greenhalf1_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Greenhalf1-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>  In 1963 it followed the path of many former cinemas, by becoming a <a href="/wiki/Casino" title="Casino">casino</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bingo_hall" class="mw-redirect" title="Bingo hall">bingo hall</a>, and later part of it became a <a href="/wiki/Snooker" title="Snooker">snooker</a> club.  In time, it became derelict, before being destroyed by fire like its predecessor in 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-Postles1_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Postles1-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Saltaire Picture House</i> – Saltaire Picture House was on a site opposite the Old Tramshed and opened in 1922, seated 1,500.  It later became the <a href="/wiki/Gaumont_British" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaumont British">Gaumont</a>.  The cinema closed in 1957 and the building was demolished soon after.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199627_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199627-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Pavilion</i> – The Pavilion Cinema or Pavilion de Luxe on Commercial Street was built in 1912 and known as the 'Bug Ole' or 'Bug Run'.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199628_48-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199628-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>  It opened on 2 April 1914.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows21_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows21-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>  The small seating capacity, 630, gave rise to the motto 'the little theatre with the big reputation'.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows21_53-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows21-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>  It closed in November 1956.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows6_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows6-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Prince's Hall</i> – Prince's Hall Cinema opened on 24 June 1911<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows13_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows13-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> and like the rest of Shipley's cinemas had an organ.  It survived for many years, called Unit Four, with half the capacity of the old Prince's Hall<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows13_54-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows13-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>  It was the last remaining cinema in Shipley until it closed at the turn of the 21st&#160;century.</li></ul>
<p><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.shipleyfilmsociety.org.uk">Shipley Film Society</a> was established in 2010 to bring cinema back to Shipley, and runs a programme of independent and world cinema between September and May each year.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Libraries">Libraries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Libraries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The library on Well Croft in the town centre is a branch library of Bradford Central Library.  A Carnegie Library on Briggate built with a £3,000 donation by <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Carnegie" title="Andrew Carnegie">Andrew Carnegie</a> now stands empty<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows4_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows4-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> but the name persists in Carnegie Drive and the Carnegie Clinic.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industry">Industry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Industry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Shipley houses the  Naylor Cars, Ltd., that produced the <a href="/wiki/Naylor_TF_1700" title="Naylor TF 1700">Naylor TF 1700</a>, an MG TF replica.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">Religion</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Religion">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Traditionally, <a href="/wiki/Non-conformist" class="mw-redirect" title="Non-conformist">non-conformist</a> churches have predominated in Shipley and this is still the case to some extent today.  There are four <a href="/wiki/Methodist" class="mw-redirect" title="Methodist">Methodist</a> churches, which feature <a href="/wiki/Victorian_architecture" title="Victorian architecture">Victorian architecture</a>.  These are: Northcliffe on the site of a 'tin chapel',<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows13_54-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows13-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Crag Road, Saltaire and Christ Church at Windhill.<sup id="cite_ref-ShipChurch_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ShipChurch-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> Saltaire United Reformed Church was built in the <a href="/wiki/Italianate" class="mw-redirect" title="Italianate">Italianate</a> style at the behest of <a href="/wiki/Sir_Titus_Salt" class="mw-redirect" title="Sir Titus Salt">Sir Titus Salt</a> in 1859.  It is a <a href="/wiki/Grade_I_listed" class="mw-redirect" title="Grade I listed">Grade I listed</a> building.<sup id="cite_ref-SURC_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SURC-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Pauls,_Shipley,_West_Yorkshire.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/St_Pauls%2C_Shipley%2C_West_Yorkshire.jpg/220px-St_Pauls%2C_Shipley%2C_West_Yorkshire.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/St_Pauls%2C_Shipley%2C_West_Yorkshire.jpg/330px-St_Pauls%2C_Shipley%2C_West_Yorkshire.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/St_Pauls%2C_Shipley%2C_West_Yorkshire.jpg/440px-St_Pauls%2C_Shipley%2C_West_Yorkshire.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2288" data-file-height="1712" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:St_Pauls,_Shipley,_West_Yorkshire.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>St Paul's, Shipley</div></div></div>
<p>The first place of worship in Shipley was the Bethel <a href="/wiki/Baptist" class="mw-redirect" title="Baptist">Baptist</a> Chapel in 1758, it was rebuilt in 1836 and demolished in the early 1970s and only part of the graveyard survives.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran198421_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran198421-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>  A second Baptist chapel was built at Rosse Street near the town centre in 1865 and is still in use.  There is a Victorian <a href="/wiki/Salvation_Army" class="mw-redirect" title="Salvation Army">Salvation Army</a> Citadel on Rhodes Place.
</p><p>Historically, Shipley was part of the <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parish</a> of Bradford and did not have a church until well into the 19th&#160;century.  The first <a href="/wiki/Anglican" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglican">Anglican</a> church was the <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">Gothic</a> St Paul's on Kirkgate, consecrated by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Venables-Vernon-Harcourt" title="Edward Venables-Vernon-Harcourt">Edward Harcourt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_of_York" title="Archbishop of York">Archbishop of York</a> in 1826.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199620_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199620-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>  It was built at a cost of <a href="/wiki/Pre-decimal_British_Coinage#Pre-decimal_coinage" class="mw-redirect" title="Pre-decimal British Coinage">£7,687.19s.3d</a>, a gift of the nation under the <a href="/wiki/Million_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Million Act">Million Act</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows12_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows12-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> on land donated by John Wilmer Field, from the Shipley land-owning family.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran198421_58-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran198421-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>  The parish of Shipley cum Heaton was created on 30 May 1828 by an <a href="/wiki/Order_in_council" class="mw-redirect" title="Order in council">order in council</a> of <a href="/wiki/George_IV" class="mw-redirect" title="George IV">King George IV</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows12_60-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows12-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>  St Paul's is one of an identical pair of churches with Wilsden Church.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199620_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199620-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>  A graveyard was added in 1860, but by 1895 was full and additional land at Hirst Wood was consecrated.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199620_59-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199620-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>  The church seated 1488 and has an organ built by Binns of Bramley in 1892.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows6_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows6-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other Anglican churches in the town are St Margaret's, Frizinghall and St Peter's in Moorhead Lane. The later was commissioned in 1888 as a daughter church for St Paul's and consecrated in 1909 by the <a href="/wiki/William_Boyd_Carpenter" title="William Boyd Carpenter">Bishop of Ripon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWatson198911_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWatson198911-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" class="mw-redirect" title="Roman Catholic Church">Roman Catholic Church</a> of St Theresa Benedicta and St Walburga, usually referred to as St Walburga's is situated on Kirkgate.<sup id="cite_ref-ShipChurch_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ShipChurch-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Road">Road</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Road">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Bradford to Bingley Road was constructed in the 1820s<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheeran198421_58-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheeran198421-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> and with Otley Road and Saltaire Road form a triangle framing Shipley centre.  They connect the town to Bradford, Leeds and the Airedale towns.
</p><p>There is a small bus station in Shipley Market Place.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rail">Rail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Rail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Shipley_railway_station" title="Shipley railway station">Shipley railway station</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saltaire_railway_station" title="Saltaire railway station">Saltaire railway station</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Midland_Railway" title="Midland Railway">Midland Railway</a>'s Leeds and Bradford line opened on 2 July 1846 and was extended to <a href="/wiki/Keighley" title="Keighley">Keighley</a> by March 1847.  The <a href="/wiki/Guiseley" title="Guiseley">Guiseley</a> branch opened on 4 December 1876 and in the same year completion of the <a href="/wiki/Settle-Carlisle_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Settle-Carlisle Line">Settle-Carlisle Line</a> put Shipley on the London to Scotland route. In 1885, the old Midland Railway station was replaced, and by 1900, 400 trains were passing through Shipley each month, carrying 50,000 passengers.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows6–7_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows6–7-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Shipley_railway_station" title="Shipley railway station">Shipley railway station</a> has an unusual triangular layout, serving trains on the <a href="/wiki/Skipton" title="Skipton">Skipton</a> to Leeds line, the Leeds to <a href="/wiki/Bradford_Forster_Square_railway_station" title="Bradford Forster Square railway station">Bradford Forster Square</a> line, and the Bradford to Skipton/Ilkley lines.  <a href="/wiki/Saltaire_railway_station" title="Saltaire railway station">Saltaire railway station</a>, opened in 1984 on the Setttle-Carlisle Line, serves the heritage village of Saltaire.  Long-distance trains run south to London <a href="/wiki/London_King%27s_Cross_railway_station" title="London King&#39;s Cross railway station">King's Cross</a> and north to <a href="/wiki/Carlisle,_Cumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Carlisle, Cumbria">Carlisle</a>, while local trains connect the town with Leeds, Bradford and Skipton.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canals">Canals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Canals">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Leeds_and_Liverpool_Canal" title="Leeds and Liverpool Canal">Leeds and Liverpool Canal</a> was once an important navigation linking Shipley to the wider world.  The Skipton to Shipley section was completed in 1773 and in 1774 a branch was extended to Bradford. <a href="/wiki/Wharf" title="Wharf">Wharves</a> were established on the north side of Briggate.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurrows6_26-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurrows6-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>  The Bradford branch was filled in during the 1920s. The canal is used for pleasure cruising.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trams">Trams</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Trams">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Tram" title="Tram">Trams</a> ran along Bradford Road to the south and Saltaire Road to the north<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199610–18_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199610–18-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> and between <a href="/wiki/Baildon" title="Baildon">Baildon</a> Bridge and the Branch.  The intersection of these lines led to the main road junction of Fox's Corner being given the alternative name of <i>Cobweb Square</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199637_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199637-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>  The legacy of trams is the terminal building on Saltaire Roundabout, now a public house named The Hop. There was a second tram shed off the roundabout at the foot of Moorhead Land.
</p><p>Saltaire shed was converted for trolleybuses in 1939 until Bradford scrapped trolleybuses in the 1970s
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Newspapers">Newspapers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Shipley,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Newspapers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town's first newspaper was the <i>Shipley Times &amp; Express</i> run by stationer and printer, Johnny Walker.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFirth199610_32-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFirth199610-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>  The paper was based in premises at Shipley crossroads, and the junction was sometimes called Johnny Walker's Corner as well as Fox Corner.  In 1922, Walker sold out to printer/stationer, Osbaldiston, and the building still stands under his name. The building is now a scuba diving centre called Duck and Dive.  The paper closed in 1981.<sup id="cite_ref-LocalPaper_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LocalPaper-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Shipley is in the distribution area of the Bradford-based <i><a href="/wiki/Telegraph_%26_Argus" title="Telegraph &amp; Argus">Telegraph &amp; Argus</a></i>. The <i>Telegraph &amp; Argus</i> produced a free newspaper for the district, the <i>Aire Valley (or Shipley) Target</i>, which was then produced as one of four local editions of the <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bradford_%26_District_Advertiser&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bradford &amp; District Advertiser (page does not exist)">Bradford &amp; District Advertiser</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-BritPaper_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BritPaper-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> This no longer prints.
</p><p>Shipley is also well-served by a local community magazine, The Local Leader, which distributes bi-monthly and is produced by local teenager James Slater and is based on Commercial Street.
</p><p>The Saltaire Review was launched by Festival Publications in October 2014. It is published bi-monthly and covers community issues and events, with an estimated readership of over 18,000.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
