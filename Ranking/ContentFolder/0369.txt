	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Calendar of saints</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Christian liturgical calendar celebrating saints</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Calendar_of_saints.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Calendar_of_saints.jpg/220px-Calendar_of_saints.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="287" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Calendar_of_saints.jpg/330px-Calendar_of_saints.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Calendar_of_saints.jpg/440px-Calendar_of_saints.jpg 2x" data-file-width="564" data-file-height="736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Calendar_of_saints.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> manuscript fragment of <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finnish</a> origin, about 1340–1360, utilized by the <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominican</a> convent at <a href="/wiki/Turku" title="Turku">Turku</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <b>calendar of saints</b> is a traditional <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> method of organizing a <a href="/wiki/Liturgical_year" title="Liturgical year">liturgical year</a> by associating each day with one or more <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saints</a> and referring to the day as the <b>feast day</b> or <b>feast</b> of said saint. The word "feast" in this context does not mean "a large meal, typically a celebratory one", but instead "an annual religious celebration, a day dedicated to a particular saint".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> 
</p><p>The system arose from the early Christian custom of commemorating each <a href="/wiki/Martyr" title="Martyr">martyr</a> annually on the date of their death, or birth into heaven, a date therefore referred to in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> as the martyr's <i>dies natalis</i> ('day of birth'). In the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a>, a calendar of saints is called a <i><a href="/wiki/Menologion" class="mw-redirect" title="Menologion">Menologion</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> "Menologion" may also mean a set of icons on which saints are depicted in the order of the dates of their feasts, often made in two panels.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar_of_saints&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:P._13_a_calendar_of_saint_days.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/P._13_a_calendar_of_saint_days.jpg/220px-P._13_a_calendar_of_saint_days.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/P._13_a_calendar_of_saint_days.jpg/330px-P._13_a_calendar_of_saint_days.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/P._13_a_calendar_of_saint_days.jpg/440px-P._13_a_calendar_of_saint_days.jpg 2x" data-file-width="617" data-file-height="823" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:P._13_a_calendar_of_saint_days.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a> calendar of saint days c.&#160;1488–1498</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:National_Library_of_Ireland_MS_G10_p24.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/National_Library_of_Ireland_MS_G10_p24.jpg/220px-National_Library_of_Ireland_MS_G10_p24.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="309" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/National_Library_of_Ireland_MS_G10_p24.jpg/330px-National_Library_of_Ireland_MS_G10_p24.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/National_Library_of_Ireland_MS_G10_p24.jpg/440px-National_Library_of_Ireland_MS_G10_p24.jpg 2x" data-file-width="771" data-file-height="1084" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:National_Library_of_Ireland_MS_G10_p24.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Excerpt from the Irish <a href="/wiki/Martyrology_of_Oengus" class="mw-redirect" title="Martyrology of Oengus">Feastology of Oengus</a>, presenting the entries for 1 and 2 January in the form of quatrains of four six-syllabic lines for each day. In this 16th-century copy (MS G10 at the <a href="/wiki/National_Library_of_Ireland" title="National Library of Ireland">National Library of Ireland</a>) we find pairs of two six-syllabic lines combined into bold lines, amended by glosses and notes that were added by later authors.</div></div></div>
<p>As the number of recognized saints increased during <a href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" class="mw-redirect" title="Late Antiquity">Late Antiquity</a> and the first half of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>, eventually every day of the year had at least one saint who was commemorated on that date. To deal with this increase, some saints were moved to alternate days in some traditions or completely removed, with the result that some saints have different feast days in different calendars. For example, St. <a href="/wiki/Perpetua_and_Felicity" class="mw-redirect" title="Perpetua and Felicity">Perpetua and Felicity</a> died on 7 March, but this date was later assigned to St. <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Aquinas" title="Thomas Aquinas">Thomas Aquinas</a>, allowing them only a commemoration (see <a href="/wiki/Tridentine_Calendar" title="Tridentine Calendar">Tridentine Calendar</a>), so in 1908 they were moved one day earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> When the 1969 reform of the Catholic calendar moved him to 28 January, they were moved back to 7 March (see <a href="/wiki/General_Roman_Calendar" title="General Roman Calendar">General Roman Calendar</a>). Both days can thus be said to be their feast day, in different traditions. The Roman Catholic calendars of saints in their various forms, which list those saints celebrated in the entire church, contains only a selection of the saints for each of its days. A fuller list is found in the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Martyrology" title="Roman Martyrology">Roman Martyrology</a>, and some of the saints there may be celebrated locally.
</p><p>The earliest feast days of saints were those of martyrs, venerated as having shown for Christ the greatest form of love, in accordance with the teaching: "Greater love has no one than this, that someone lay down his life for his friends."<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Saint <a href="/wiki/Martin_of_Tours" title="Martin of Tours">Martin of Tours</a> is said to be the first<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> or at least one of the first non-martyrs to be venerated as a saint. The title "<a href="/wiki/Confessor" title="Confessor">confessor</a>" was used for such saints, who had confessed their faith in Christ by their lives rather than by their deaths. Martyrs are regarded as dying in the service of the Lord, and confessors are people who died natural deaths. A broader range of titles was used later, such as: <a href="/wiki/Virgin_(title)" title="Virgin (title)">Virgin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pastor" title="Pastor">Pastor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bishop" title="Bishop">Bishop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">Monk</a>, <a href="/wiki/Priest" title="Priest">Priest</a>, Founder, <a href="/wiki/Abbot" title="Abbot">Abbot</a>, Apostle, <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_the_Church" title="Doctor of the Church">Doctor of the Church</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Tridentine_Mass" title="Tridentine Mass">Tridentine Missal</a> has <a href="/wiki/Common_(liturgy)" title="Common (liturgy)">common formulæ</a> for Masses of Martyrs, Confessors who were bishops, Doctors of the Church, Confessors who were not Bishops, Abbots, Virgins, Non-Virgins, Dedication of Churches, and Feast Days of the Blessed Virgin Mary. <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XII" title="Pope Pius XII">Pope Pius XII</a> added a common formula for Popes. The <a href="/wiki/General_Roman_Calendar_of_1960" title="General Roman Calendar of 1960">1962 Roman Missal of Pope John XXIII</a> omitted the common of Apostles, assigning a proper Mass to every feast day of an Apostle. The present <a href="/wiki/Roman_Missal" title="Roman Missal">Roman Missal</a> has common formulas for the Dedication of Churches, the Blessed Virgin Mary, Martyrs (with special formulas for missionary martyrs and virgin martyrs), Pastors (subdivided into bishops, generic pastors, founders of churches, and missionaries), Doctors of the Church, Virgins, and (generic) Saints (with special formulas for abbots, monks, nuns, religious, those noted for works of mercy, educators, and [generically] women saints).
</p><p>This <a href="/wiki/Calendar" title="Calendar">calendar</a> system, when combined with major <a href="/wiki/Church_(building)" title="Church (building)">church</a> festivals and movable and immovable feasts, constructs a very human and personalised yet often localized way of organizing the year and identifying dates. Some Christians continue the tradition of dating by saints' days: their works may appear "dated" as "The Feast of <a href="/wiki/Martin_of_Tours" title="Martin of Tours">Saint Martin</a>". Poets such as <a href="/wiki/John_Keats" title="John Keats">John Keats</a> commemorate the importance of <i>The Eve of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Agnes" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Agnes">Saint Agnes</a></i>.
</p><p>As different Christian jurisdictions parted ways theologically, differing lists of saints began to develop. This happened because the same individual may be considered differently by one church; in extreme examples, one church's saint may be another church's heretic, as in the cases of <a href="/wiki/Nestorius" title="Nestorius">Nestorius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pope_Dioscorus_I_of_Alexandria" title="Pope Dioscorus I of Alexandria">Pope Dioscorus I of Alexandria</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Archbishop_Flavian_of_Constantinople" class="mw-redirect" title="Archbishop Flavian of Constantinople">Archbishop Flavian of Constantinople</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ranking_of_feast_days">Ranking of feast days</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar_of_saints&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ranking of feast days">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ranking_of_liturgical_days_in_the_Roman_Rite" title="Ranking of liturgical days in the Roman Rite">Ranking of liturgical days in the Roman Rite</a></div>
<p>Feast days are ranked in accordance with their importance. In the post-Vatican II form of the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Rite" title="Roman Rite">Roman Rite</a>, feast days are ranked (in descending order of importance) as <a href="/wiki/Solemnity" title="Solemnity">solemnities</a>, feasts or <a href="/wiki/Memorial_(liturgy)" title="Memorial (liturgy)">memorials</a> (obligatory or optional).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_XXIII" title="Pope John XXIII">Pope John XXIII</a>'s 1960 <a href="/wiki/Code_of_Rubrics" title="Code of Rubrics">Code of Rubrics</a>, whose use remains authorized by the <a href="/wiki/Motu_proprio" title="Motu proprio">motu proprio</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Summorum_Pontificum" title="Summorum Pontificum">Summorum Pontificum</a></i>, divides liturgical days into I, II, III, and IV class days. Those who use even earlier forms of the Roman Rite rank feast days as Doubles (of three or four kinds), Semidoubles, and Simples. See <a href="/wiki/Ranking_of_liturgical_days_in_the_Roman_Rite#Feast_days" title="Ranking of liturgical days in the Roman Rite">Ranking of liturgical days in the Roman Rite</a>.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Orthodox Church</a> the ranking of feasts varies from church to church. In the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Orthodox_Church" title="Russian Orthodox Church">Russian Orthodox Church</a> they are: <a href="/wiki/Great_Feasts" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Feasts">Great Feasts</a>, middle, and minor feasts. Each portion of such feasts may also be called feasts as follows: <a href="/wiki/All-Night_Vigil" class="mw-redirect" title="All-Night Vigil">All-Night Vigils</a>, <a href="/wiki/Polyeleos" title="Polyeleos">Polyeleos</a>, <a href="/wiki/Great_Doxology" class="mw-redirect" title="Great Doxology">Great Doxology</a>, Sextuple ("sixfold", having six <a href="/wiki/Stichera" class="mw-redirect" title="Stichera">stichera</a> at <a href="/wiki/Vespers" title="Vespers">Vespers</a> and six <a href="/wiki/Troparia" class="mw-redirect" title="Troparia">troparia</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Canon_(hymnography)" title="Canon (hymnography)">Canon</a> of <a href="/wiki/Matins" title="Matins">Matins</a>). There are also distinctions between Simple feasts and Double (i.e., two simple feasts celebrated together). In Double Feasts the order of hymns and readings for each feast are rigidly instructed in <a href="/wiki/Typikon" title="Typikon">Typikon</a>, the liturgy book.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">Church of England</a>, there are <a href="/wiki/Principal_Feast" title="Principal Feast">Principal Feasts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Principal_Holy_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Principal Holy Day">Principal Holy Days</a>, <a href="/wiki/Festival_(Church_of_England)" class="mw-redirect" title="Festival (Church of England)">Festivals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lesser_Festival" class="mw-redirect" title="Lesser Festival">Lesser Festivals</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Commemoration_(observance)" class="mw-redirect" title="Commemoration (observance)">Commemorations</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Connection_to_tropical_cyclones">Connection to tropical cyclones</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Calendar_of_saints&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Connection to tropical cyclones">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before the advent of <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_naming" title="Tropical cyclone naming">standardized naming of tropical storms and hurricanes</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Tropical_cyclone_basins" title="Tropical cyclone basins">North Atlantic basin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tropical_storm" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical storm">tropical storms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_hurricane" title="Atlantic hurricane">hurricanes</a> that affected the island of <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">Puerto Rico</a> were informally named after the Catholic saints corresponding to the feast days when the cyclones either made landfall or started to seriously affect the island. Examples are: the 1780 San Calixto hurricane a.k.a. the <a href="/wiki/Great_Hurricane_of_1780" title="Great Hurricane of 1780">Great Hurricane of 1780</a> (the deadliest in the North Atlantic basin's recorded history; named after <a href="/wiki/Pope_Callixtus_I" title="Pope Callixtus I">Pope Callixtus I</a> a.k.a. Saint Callixtus, whose feast day is October&#160;14),<sup id="cite_ref-Mujica_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mujica-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/1867_San_Narciso_hurricane" title="1867 San Narciso hurricane">1867 San Narciso hurricane</a> (named after Saint <a href="/wiki/Narcissus_of_Jerusalem" title="Narcissus of Jerusalem">Narcissus of Jerusalem</a>, feast day October&#160;29),<sup id="cite_ref-Mujica_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mujica-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/1899_San_Ciriaco_hurricane" title="1899 San Ciriaco hurricane">1899 San Ciriaco hurricane</a> (the deadliest in the island's recorded history; <a href="/wiki/Saint_Cyriacus" class="mw-redirect" title="Saint Cyriacus">Saint Cyriacus</a>, August&#160;8),<sup id="cite_ref-Mujica_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mujica-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/1928_San_Felipe_hurricane" class="mw-redirect" title="1928 San Felipe hurricane">1928 San Felipe hurricane</a> (the strongest in terms of measured wind speed; <a href="/wiki/September_13_(Eastern_Orthodox_liturgics)#Pre-Schism_Western_Saints" title="September 13 (Eastern Orthodox liturgics)">Saint Philip</a>, father of Saint <a href="/wiki/Eugenia_of_Rome" title="Eugenia of Rome">Eugenia of Rome</a>, September&#160;13),<sup id="cite_ref-Mujica_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mujica-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/1932_San_Ciprian_hurricane" title="1932 San Ciprian hurricane">1932 San Ciprian hurricane</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cyprian_and_Justina" title="Cyprian and Justina">Saint Cyprian</a>, September&#160;26).<sup id="cite_ref-Mujica_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mujica-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This practice continued until quite some time after the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Weather_Bureau" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Weather Bureau">United States Weather Bureau</a> (now called the National Weather Service) started <a href="/wiki/History_of_tropical_cyclone_naming" title="History of tropical cyclone naming">publishing and using official female human names</a> (initially; male names were added starting in 1979 after the NWS relinquished control over naming to the <a href="/wiki/World_Meteorological_Organization" title="World Meteorological Organization">World Meteorological Organization</a>). The last two usages of this informal naming scheme in P.R. were in 1956 (<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Betsy_(1956)" title="Hurricane Betsy (1956)">Hurricane Betsy</a>, locally nicknamed Santa Clara after Saint <a href="/wiki/Clare_of_Assisi" title="Clare of Assisi">Clare of Assisi</a>, feast day August&#160;12 back then; her feast day was advanced one day in 1970) and 1960 (<a href="/wiki/Hurricane_Donna" title="Hurricane Donna">Hurricane Donna</a>, nicknamed San Lorenzo after Saint <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Giustiniani" class="mw-redirect" title="Lawrence Giustiniani">Lawrence Justinian</a>, September&#160;5 back then; feast day now observed January&#160;8 by <a href="/wiki/Canons_regular" title="Canons regular">Canons regular</a> of St. <a href="/wiki/Augustine" class="mw-redirect" title="Augustine">Augustine</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-Mujica_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mujica-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
