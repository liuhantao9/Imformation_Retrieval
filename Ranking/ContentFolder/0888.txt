<p><b>Bingley</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Market_town" title="Market town">market town</a> and <a href="/wiki/Civil_parish" title="Civil parish">civil parish</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan borough</a> of the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Bradford" title="City of Bradford">City of Bradford</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, England, on the <a href="/wiki/River_Aire" title="River Aire">River Aire</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Leeds_and_Liverpool_Canal" title="Leeds and Liverpool Canal">Leeds and Liverpool Canal</a>, which had a population of 18,294 at the 2011 Census.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bingley_railway_station" title="Bingley railway station">Bingley railway station</a> is in the town centre and <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Bradford_International_Airport" class="mw-redirect" title="Leeds Bradford International Airport">Leeds Bradford International Airport</a> 10 miles (16&#160;km) away. The B6265 connects Bingley to <a href="/wiki/Keighley" title="Keighley">Keighley</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_England" title="Historic counties of England">Historically</a> part of the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>, Bingley appears in the <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> of 1086 as "Bingheleia".
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Founding">Founding</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Founding">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bingley was probably founded by the <a href="/wiki/Saxon_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Saxon people">Saxons</a>, by a ford on the <a href="/wiki/River_Aire" title="River Aire">River Aire</a>. This crossing gave access to <a href="/wiki/Harden,_West_Yorkshire" title="Harden, West Yorkshire">Harden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cullingworth" title="Cullingworth">Cullingworth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wilsden" title="Wilsden">Wilsden</a> on the south side of the river.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1086-Bingley-detail.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/1086-Bingley-detail.jpg/200px-1086-Bingley-detail.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="129" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/1086-Bingley-detail.jpg/300px-1086-Bingley-detail.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/1086-Bingley-detail.jpg/400px-1086-Bingley-detail.jpg 2x" data-file-width="455" data-file-height="294" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1086-Bingley-detail.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bingley's entry in the Domesday Book. 1086 AD</div></div></div>
<p>The origins of the name are from the Old English personal name <i>Bynna</i> + <i>ingas</i> ("descendants of") + <i>lēah</i> ("clearing in a forest"). This would mean altogether the "wood or clearing of the Bynningas, the people called after Bynna".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Normans">Normans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Normans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a> of 1086, Bingley is listed as "Bingheleia":
</p>
<dl><dd><i>m In Bingheleia hb. Gospatric iiij car' tra e' ad gld. tra ad ii car' Ernegis de burun h't. &amp; Wast' e'. T.R.E. val, iiij lib'. Silva past' ii leu' lg' &amp; i lat'. Tot' m' e iiij leu' lg' &amp; ii lat'</i><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>which roughly translated reads:
</p>
<dl><dd><i>In Bingheleia, Gospatric has a manor of four carucate of land to be taxed, land for two ploughs.  Ernegis de Burun has it and it is waste. In the time of King Edward the Confessor it was valued at four pounds.  Woodland pasture two leagues long and one broad. All the manor is four long and two broad.</i></dd></dl>
<div style="clear:both;"></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval">Medieval</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Medieval">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ford was superseded by <a href="/wiki/Bingley_Ireland_Bridge" class="mw-redirect" title="Bingley Ireland Bridge">Ireland Bridge</a>. Bingley was a <a href="/wiki/Manorialism" title="Manorialism">manor</a> which extended several miles up and down the Aire valley, extending upstream to Marley on the outskirts of <a href="/wiki/Keighley" title="Keighley">Keighley</a> and downstream to <a href="/wiki/Cottingley,_Bradford" title="Cottingley, Bradford">Cottingley</a>. Bingley became a <a href="/wiki/Market_town" title="Market town">market town</a> with the grant of a Market Charter in 1212 by <a href="/wiki/John_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="John of England">King John</a>.
</p><p>According to the poll tax returns of 1379, Bingley had 130 households, probably around 500 people.  The nearby towns of Bradford, Leeds and Halifax had about half this population.  At this time Bingley was the largest town in the area.
</p><p>No records tell of how Bingley fared in the <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">Black Death</a> that swept Europe in the 14th century.  Approximately one third of all the people in Europe died of this plague, sometimes wiping out whole towns and villages.  According to the 1379 Poll tax records, the nearby town of Boulton had no survivors worth taxing. It seems Bingley may have got off relatively lightly.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tudors">Tudors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tudors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1592, Bingley was shown on a map by Yorkshire map-maker <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Saxton" title="Christopher Saxton">Christopher Saxton</a> as a single street with about 20 houses on each side.  The church sits at the west end of the street opposite a single large house, possibly a <a href="/wiki/Manor_house" title="Manor house">manor house</a>. Since Bingley was a market town, the market stalls would have been set up on either side of the main street. One of the oldest buildings in Bingley is a <a href="/wiki/Coaching_inn" title="Coaching inn">coaching inn</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Old_White_Horse_Inn" title="Old White Horse Inn">Old White Horse Inn</a>, on the flatter north bank of the River Aire by Ireland Bridge.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_Revolution">Industrial Revolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Industrial Revolution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bingley_Five_Rise_Locks_1.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Bingley_Five_Rise_Locks_1.JPG/220px-Bingley_Five_Rise_Locks_1.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Bingley_Five_Rise_Locks_1.JPG/330px-Bingley_Five_Rise_Locks_1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Bingley_Five_Rise_Locks_1.JPG/440px-Bingley_Five_Rise_Locks_1.JPG 2x" data-file-width="1600" data-file-height="1200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bingley_Five_Rise_Locks_1.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Bingley Five Rise Locks.</div></div></div>
<p>Like many towns in the West Riding, Bingley prospered during the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a>. The Bingley section of the <a href="/wiki/Leeds_and_Liverpool_Canal" title="Leeds and Liverpool Canal">Leeds and Liverpool Canal</a> was completed in 1774, linking the town with <a href="/wiki/Skipton" title="Skipton">Skipton</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bradford" title="Bradford">Bradford</a> via the <a href="/wiki/Bradford_Canal" title="Bradford Canal">Bradford Canal</a>. The canal passes through the town centre and ascends the side of the valley via the <a href="/wiki/Bingley_Five_Rise_Locks" title="Bingley Five Rise Locks">Bingley Five Rise</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bingley_Three_Rise_Locks" title="Bingley Three Rise Locks">Bingley Three Rise Locks</a>. Several woollen and worsted  mills were built and people migrated from the surrounding countryside to work in them. Many came from further afield such as Ireland in the wake of the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Potato_Famine_(1845-1849)" class="mw-redirect" title="Irish Potato Famine (1845-1849)">Irish Potato Famine</a>. A railway and line goods yard were constructed bringing further trade. The villages of <a href="/wiki/Gilstead" title="Gilstead">Gilstead</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eldwick" title="Eldwick">Eldwick</a> became <a href="/wiki/Conurbation" title="Conurbation">conurbated</a> with Bingley. The Bingley Building Society was also founded in this period.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Damart_buildings,_Bingley.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Damart_buildings%2C_Bingley.jpg/220px-Damart_buildings%2C_Bingley.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Damart_buildings%2C_Bingley.jpg/330px-Damart_buildings%2C_Bingley.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Damart_buildings%2C_Bingley.jpg/440px-Damart_buildings%2C_Bingley.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Damart_buildings,_Bingley.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Damart buildings</div></div></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-industrial">Post-industrial</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Post-industrial">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bingley Teacher Training College opened in 1911 with Helen Wodehouse as principal. The first intake of students was 102 women from in and around the then <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>. Before its closure in 1979, the college produced approximately 16,000 teachers.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Beeching_Axe" class="mw-redirect" title="Beeching Axe">Beeching Axe</a> demolished the goods yard, although the station still has trains to Leeds, Bradford, Skipton, <a href="/wiki/Morecambe" title="Morecambe">Morecambe</a> and <a href="/wiki/Carlisle,_Cumbria" class="mw-redirect" title="Carlisle, Cumbria">Carlisle</a>. The textile mills have largely been closed. The <a href="/wiki/Damart" title="Damart">Damart</a> mill still stands and trades in textiles. Since 1995 the tannery, Bingley Mill &amp; Andertons, have been converted into flats. The most cramped and outdated <a href="/wiki/Terraced_house" title="Terraced house">terraced housing</a> was partly replaced with <a href="/wiki/Council_house" title="Council house">council housing</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingley_Arts_Centre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Bingley Arts Centre (page does not exist)">Bingley Arts Centre</a> and the headquarters of the Bradford &amp; Bingley Building Society. Further council housing was built up the hill towards Gilstead including three <a href="/wiki/Tower_block" class="mw-redirect" title="Tower block">tower blocks</a>. In the wake of <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a>'s reforms of council housing much of the council estate was sold and a substantial portion has been knocked down and rebuilt as private housing. The Bingley Permanent Building Society merged with the Bradford Equitable Building Society to form the <a href="/wiki/Bradford_%26_Bingley" title="Bradford &amp; Bingley">Bradford &amp; Bingley Building Society</a> in 1964. It was decided to site the <a href="/wiki/Bradford_and_Bingley_head_office" class="mw-redirect" title="Bradford and Bingley head office">corporate headquarters</a> in Bingley. This brought several thousand jobs to the town, but the building itself did not meet with universal acclaim and was demolished in 2015. The Bradford and Bingley collapsed in the 2008 credit crunch.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criminal">Criminal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Criminal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bingley's most infamous son is <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sutcliffe" title="Peter Sutcliffe">Peter Sutcliffe</a>, the Yorkshire Ripper,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> who was convicted of the murders of 13 women and the attempted murder of seven others.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Mark_Rowntree" title="Mark Rowntree">Mark Rowntree</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Spree_killer" title="Spree killer">spree killer</a>, started his murderous campaign in Bingley before moving on to <a href="/wiki/Eastburn,_West_Yorkshire" title="Eastburn, West Yorkshire">Eastburn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>. He was convicted in 1976 of four murders.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1966, bookie Fred Craven was brutally murdered in his betting shop on Wellington Street, Bingley. Craven, who was well known in the area because of his short stature (he was 4&#160;ft 7in tall), had gone into his shop to collect papers despite there being no racing that day. It is believed that he had £200 in his wallet (£3,200.00 in 2015 prices)<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> which went missing. Mr Craven suffered extensive injuries at the hands of the killer. Because the local constabulary had a very narrow time frame for the murder, and the fact the killer must have been covered in blood, they anticipated a quick arrest.As of August 2017<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bingley&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, his murder remains unsolved,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> but rumours that <a href="/wiki/Peter_Sutcliffe" title="Peter Sutcliffe">Peter Sutcliffe</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Ripper" class="mw-redirect" title="Yorkshire Ripper">Yorkshire Ripper</a>, was responsible for his murder resurfaced in 2017. <a href="/wiki/ITV_Calendar" class="mw-redirect" title="ITV Calendar">ITV Calendar News</a> asked Sutcliffe about Craven's death and also that of a taxi driver on Bingley Moor. In what was described as a "bizarre letter", Sutcliffe responded that he was not responsible for either crime to presenter <a href="/wiki/Christine_Talbot" title="Christine Talbot">Christine Talbot</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Road_history">Road history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Road history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main road through Bingley has been re-sited twice in its history. In 1904, a new cut of the road heading north west out of the town created a mini-bypass between All Saints Parish Church and the Railway. The cobbled road around the front of the church became Old Main Street. A huge section of the graveyard was taken out to lay the new road and the gravestones were used as flags on the paths around the church. A small section of the graveyard still exists on the east side of the new road bounded by the old path to Treacle Cock Alley and the railway.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Plans had been on the table for a trunk road through Bingley and the Aire valley since at least 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> In 1992 preparatory work was undertaken to lengthen Park Road bridge so that the Leeds Liverpool Canal could be moved slightly to the east. This work would allow the formation of the new road to be sandwiched between the railway and the canal. In 1998, the Government, gave the go ahead for the trunk road to be constructed.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Post-relief_road_Bingley">Post-relief road Bingley</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Post-relief road Bingley">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bingley_Panorama_001.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Bingley_Panorama_001.jpg/500px-Bingley_Panorama_001.jpg" decoding="async" width="500" height="133" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Bingley_Panorama_001.jpg/750px-Bingley_Panorama_001.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Bingley_Panorama_001.jpg/1000px-Bingley_Panorama_001.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2272" data-file-height="603" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bingley_Panorama_001.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Panoramic view over Bingley</div></div></div>
<p>In 2004, the Bingley Relief Road opened. The £47.9&#160;million road stretches from <a href="/wiki/Crossflatts" title="Crossflatts">Crossflatts</a> to <a href="/wiki/Cottingley,_Bradford" title="Cottingley, Bradford">Cottingley</a>, threading through Bingley between the railway and the canal. One of the most expensive parts of the construction was moving a 160-yard (150&#160;m) stretch of the canal.  The construction involved the removal of Treacle Cock Alley pedestrian tunnel and the Tin Bridge, which have been replaced by the Three Rise Bridge, and the Britannia Bridge
</p><p>In 2004, the average home price in Bingley rose 30% to £196,850 - the second fastest appreciating area in the UK (after the nearby <a href="/wiki/Hebden_Bridge" title="Hebden Bridge">Hebden Bridge</a><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>).
</p><p>The road was a target of <a href="/wiki/Road_protest_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Road protest (UK)">road protest camp</a> where protesters occupied tree houses for nearly two years.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bingley was part of the <a href="/wiki/Wapentake" class="mw-redirect" title="Wapentake">Wapentake</a> of <a href="/wiki/Skyrack" title="Skyrack">Skyrack</a>, which was in turn part of the <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a>. The ancient parish of Bingley included the <a href="/wiki/Chapelry" title="Chapelry">chapelry</a> of <a href="/wiki/Morton,_West_Yorkshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Morton, West Yorkshire">Morton</a> and several other hamlets, including <a href="/wiki/Cullingworth" title="Cullingworth">Cullingworth</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>  In 1866, the parish was divided into the new civil parishes of Bingley and Morton.  In 1898, Bingley <a href="/wiki/Urban_district_(Great_Britain_and_Ireland)" title="Urban district (Great Britain and Ireland)">Urban District</a> was formed.  In 1974 the urban district and civil parish were abolished and absorbed into the City of Bradford Metropolitan District in the new county of West Yorkshire.  Bingley became a <a href="/wiki/Ward_(politics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ward (politics)">ward</a> in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Bradford" title="City of Bradford">Bradford metropolitan district</a>. A civil parish called Bingley Town Council was established on 1 April 2016 following a petition to Bradford Council in October 2015. This followed a two-year campaign by the Bingley Community Council Group. Elections were held in May 2016 for the first councillors for the new Bingley Town Council.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> It has 16 councillors representing eight wards: Bingley Central, Crossflatts, Lady Lane and Oakwood, Eldwick, Gilstead, Priestthorpe, Crownest and Cottingley.
</p>
<dl><dt>Councillors</dt></dl>
<p>Bingley ward is represented on <a href="/wiki/City_of_Bradford_Metropolitan_District_Council" title="City of Bradford Metropolitan District Council">Bradford Council</a> by three <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">Conservative</a> councillors; David Heseltine (Conservative),<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Mark Shaw (Conservative)<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> and John Pennington (Conservative).<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Bingley_Railway_Station.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Bingley_Railway_Station.jpg/220px-Bingley_Railway_Station.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Bingley_Railway_Station.jpg/330px-Bingley_Railway_Station.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Bingley_Railway_Station.jpg/440px-Bingley_Railway_Station.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Bingley_Railway_Station.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Bingley_railway_station" title="Bingley railway station">Bingley railway station</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/A650_road" title="A650 road">A650</a> trunk road passes through Bingley. Through traffic has been diverted onto the new <a href="/wiki/Dual_carriageway" title="Dual carriageway">dual carriageway</a> avoiding the town centre. The road passes north-west to <a href="/wiki/Keighley" title="Keighley">Keighley</a> and south-east to <a href="/wiki/Shipley,_West_Yorkshire" title="Shipley, West Yorkshire">Shipley</a> and Bradford. Bingley is served by a number of bus services including the 680 between Bradford and Bingley, the 616 / 619 services between Bradford and Eldwick and the 622 service between Bingley and Bradford operated by <a href="/wiki/First_West_Yorkshire" title="First West Yorkshire">First West Yorkshire</a>, the 662 between Bradford and Keighley, the 60 between Leeds and Keighley and the K17 / K19 services between Keighley and Cullingworth operated by <a href="/wiki/Keighley_Bus_Company" title="Keighley Bus Company">Keighley Bus Company</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Airedale_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Airedale Line">Airedale Line</a>, part of the <a href="/wiki/Metro_(West_Yorkshire)" class="mw-redirect" title="Metro (West Yorkshire)">Metro (West Yorkshire)</a> railway network passes through Bingley. It is operated by <a href="/wiki/Northern_(train_operating_company)" title="Northern (train operating company)">Northern</a>. <a href="/wiki/Bingley_railway_station" title="Bingley railway station">Bingley railway station</a> still has many historical features.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Leeds_and_Liverpool_Canal" title="Leeds and Liverpool Canal">Leeds and Liverpool Canal</a> passes through Bingley. There are several flights of locks in the Bingley section, the famous <a href="/wiki/Bingley_Five_Rise_Locks" title="Bingley Five Rise Locks">Five Rise Locks</a>, the smaller <a href="/wiki/Bingley_Three_Rise_Locks" title="Bingley Three Rise Locks">Three Rise</a> and a further two lock flight at Dowley Gap. The canal climbs steeply up the side of the Aire valley through this section.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">Climate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Climate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As with all of the United Kingdom, Bingley experiences a <a href="/wiki/Maritime_climate" class="mw-redirect" title="Maritime climate">maritime climate</a>, characterised cool to mild weather, with often cloudy and damp conditions. This is amplified by Bingley's hilly northern situation. The nearest official met office weather station for which data is available is 'Bingley No. 2", actually located a couple of miles south of the town centre and at a relatively high 262 metres above sea level - More elevated than all of the urbanized area of Bingley. It is therefore likely that temperatures in central Bingley, at as little as 80 metres above sea level, tend to be 1 to 1.5&#160;°C milder year round - though on cold clear nights this may be reversed as a temperature inversion takes hold.
</p><p>The highest temperature recorded since 1980 at Bingley was 31.6&#160;°C (88.9&#160;°F)<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> during August 1990. More recently the temperature fell to -10.8&#160;°C (12.6&#160;°F)<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> on 20 December 2010.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bingley provides a range of primary and secondary schools.  The secondary schools are <a href="/wiki/Beckfoot_School" title="Beckfoot School">Beckfoot School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bingley_Grammar_School" title="Bingley Grammar School">Bingley Grammar School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Lister_Academy" class="mw-redirect" title="Samuel Lister Academy">Samuel Lister Academy</a>. Bingley Grammar School was founded in the 16th century and is one of the oldest schools in the country. Heather Bank School was a private preparatory school which closed in the 1970s.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bingley&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Bradford City Council organises an annual music festival, <a href="/wiki/Bingley_Music_Live" title="Bingley Music Live">Bingley Music Live</a> in Myrtle Park. The <a href="/wiki/Festival" title="Festival">festival</a> evolved from  Music At Myrtle and features a range of musical genres including <a href="/wiki/Rock_music" title="Rock music">rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alternative_rock" title="Alternative rock">alternative rock</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indie_rock" title="Indie rock">indie rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop music</a>. It is held at the end of August. First held in 2007, it was shortlisted in the ‘Best New Festival’ category of the Virtual Festival Awards.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> The ethos of the event is to present high quality music at an affordable price and give a platform for local bands from across West Yorkshire to a large audience.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>  Artists such as <a href="/wiki/The_Charlatans_(English_band)" title="The Charlatans (English band)">The Charlatans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Happy_Mondays" title="Happy Mondays">Happy Mondays</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scouting_for_Girls" title="Scouting for Girls">Scouting for Girls</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Automatic" title="The Automatic">The Automatic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Echo_%26_The_Bunnymen" class="mw-redirect" title="Echo &amp; The Bunnymen">Echo &amp; The Bunnymen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Doves_(band)" title="Doves (band)">Doves</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Zutons" title="The Zutons">The Zutons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Editors_(band)" title="Editors (band)">Editors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calvin_Harris" title="Calvin Harris">Calvin Harris</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_(band)" title="James (band)">James</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Enemy_(UK_rock_band)" title="The Enemy (UK rock band)">The Enemy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Seasick_Steve" title="Seasick Steve">Seasick Steve</a> and <a href="/wiki/Professor_Green" title="Professor Green">Professor Green</a> have performed at Bingley Music Live since 2007.
</p><p>Each year Bingley Show is held in Myrtle Park and is one of the largest one day shows in Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Its focus is on the horticultural and agricultural nature that reflects Bingley and its environs. The Bingley show has been held every year since 1867 apart from in 2012 when the event was cancelled due to flooding.
</p><p><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.bingleylittletheatre.co.uk">Bingley Little Theatre</a> is both a venue and a major amateur group, with eight productions a year as well as studio pieces.
</p><p>The first <a href="/wiki/Airedale_Terrier" title="Airedale Terrier">Airedale Terrier</a> was bred in Bingley.
</p><p>Since January 2015, Bingley is part of the <a href="/wiki/Walkers_are_Welcome" title="Walkers are Welcome">Walkers are Welcome</a> town network making Yorkshire the friendliest place for walkers!<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.bingleywalkersarewelcome.org.uk">Bingley Walkers are Welcome</a> now works with the local community and businesses to increase footfall into the area and boosting the local economy.
</p>
