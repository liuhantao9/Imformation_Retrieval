	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Chinese name</h1>

<p><b>Chinese personal names</b> are names used by those from <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">mainland China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_diaspora" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese diaspora">Chinese diaspora</a> overseas. Due to China's historical dominance of East Asian culture, many names used in <a href="/wiki/Korean_name" title="Korean name">Korea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_name" title="Vietnamese name">Vietnam</a> are adaptations of Chinese names, or have historical roots in Chinese, with appropriate adaptation to accommodate linguistic differences.
</p><p>Modern Chinese names consist of a surname known as <b>xing</b> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A7%93" class="extiw" title="wikt:姓">姓</a></span>, <i>xìng</i>), which comes first and is usually but <a href="/wiki/Chinese_compound_surname" title="Chinese compound surname">not always</a> monosyllabic, followed by a personal name called <b>ming</b> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%90%8D" class="extiw" title="wikt:名">名</a></span>, <i>míng</i>), which is nearly always mono- or disyllabic.  Prior to the 20th century, educated Chinese also utilized a "<a href="/wiki/Courtesy_name" title="Courtesy name">courtesy name</a>" or "style name" called <b>zi</b> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%AD%97" class="extiw" title="wikt:字">字</a></span>, <i>zì</i>) by which they were known among those outside their family and closest friends.
</p><p>From at least the time of the <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang dynasty</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Han_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Han people">Chinese</a> observed a number of <a href="/wiki/Naming_taboo" title="Naming taboo">naming taboos</a> regulating who may or may not use a person's given name (without being disrespectful). In general, using the given name connoted the speaker's authority and superior position to the addressee. Peers and younger relatives were barred from speaking it. Owing to this, many historical Chinese figures—particularly emperors—used a half-dozen or more different names in different contexts and for different speakers. Those possessing names (sometimes even mere homophones) identical to the emperor's were frequently forced to change them. The normalization of personal names after the <a href="/wiki/May_Fourth_Movement" title="May Fourth Movement">May Fourth Movement</a> has generally eradicated aliases such as the school name and courtesy name but traces of the old taboos remain, particularly within families.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although some terms in the ancient Chinese naming system, such as <i>xìng</i> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">姓</span>) and <i>míng</i> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">名</span>), are still used today, in ancient times they were used in different and more complex ways than in modern China.
</p><p>In the first half of the 1st millennium BC, during  the <a href="/wiki/Zhou_dynasty" title="Zhou dynasty">Zhou dynasty</a>, members of the Chinese nobility could possess up to four different names—personal names (<i>míng</i> <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">名</span>), clan names (<i>xìng</i> <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">姓</span>), lineage names (<i>shì</i> <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">氏</span>), and "style" or "courtesy" names (<i>zì</i> <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">字</span>)— as well as up to two titles: standard titles (<i>jué</i> <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">爵</span>), and posthumous titles (<i>shì</i> <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">諡</span> or <i>shìhào</i> <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">諡號</span>).<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkinson2012113_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkinson2012113-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  Commoners possessed only a personal name (<i>ming</i>), and the modern concept of a "surname" or "family name" did not yet exist at any level of society.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkinson2012113_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkinson2012113-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  The old lineage (<i>shi</i>) and clan names (<i>xing</i>) began to become "family names" in the modern sense and trickle down to commoners around 500 BC, during the late <a href="/wiki/Spring_and_Autumn_period" title="Spring and Autumn period">Spring and Autumn period</a>, but the process took several centuries to complete, and it was not until the late <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a> (1st and 2nd centuries AD) that all Chinese commoners had surnames.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilkinson2012113-115_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilkinson2012113-115-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Family_names">Family names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Family names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_surname" title="Chinese surname">Chinese surname</a></div>
<p>Although there are currently over 4,000 <a href="/wiki/Chinese_surname" title="Chinese surname">Chinese surnames</a> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A7%93" class="extiw" title="wikt:姓">姓</a></span>, <i>xìng</i>) in use in China,<sup id="cite_ref-Xinhua_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Xinhua-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> the colloquial expression for the "Chinese people" is  Bǎixìng (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%99%BE%E5%A7%93" class="extiw" title="wikt:百姓">百姓</a></span>) "<a href="/wiki/Hundred_Family_Surnames" title="Hundred Family Surnames">Hundred Surnames</a>", and a mere hundred surnames still make up over 85% of China's 1.3 billion citizens.<sup id="cite_ref-PD_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PD-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In fact, just the top three—<a href="/wiki/Wang_(surname)" title="Wang (surname)">Wang</a> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">王</span>), <a href="/wiki/Li_(%E6%9D%8E)" class="mw-redirect" title="Li (李)">Li</a> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%9D%8E" class="extiw" title="wikt:李">李</a></span>), and <a href="/wiki/Zhang_(surname)" title="Zhang (surname)">Zhang</a> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">張</span>)—cover more than 20% of the population.<sup id="cite_ref-PD_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PD-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> This homogeneity results from the great majority of <a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han</a> family names having only one character, while the small number of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_compound_surname" title="Chinese compound surname">compound surnames</a> is mostly restricted to minority groups.<sup id="cite_ref-Xinhua_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Xinhua-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chinese surnames arose from two separate <a href="/wiki/Prehistoric_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Prehistoric China">prehistoric</a> traditions: the <i><a href="/wiki/Chinese_family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese family name">xìng</a></i> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A7%93" class="extiw" title="wikt:姓">姓</a></span>) and the <i><a href="/wiki/Chinese_clan_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese clan name">shì</a></i> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%B0%8F" class="extiw" title="wikt:氏">氏</a></span>). The original <i>xìng</i> were clans of royalty at the <a href="/wiki/Shang_dynasty" title="Shang dynasty">Shang</a> court and always included the 'woman' <a href="/wiki/Radical_(Chinese_character)" class="mw-redirect" title="Radical (Chinese character)">radical</a> <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A5%B3" class="extiw" title="wikt:女">女</a></span>. The <i>shì</i> did not originate from families, but denoted fiefs, states, and titles granted or recognized by the Shang court. Apart from the <a href="/wiki/Jiang_(surname)" title="Jiang (surname)">Jiang</a> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A7%9C" class="extiw" title="wikt:姜">姜</a></span>) and <a href="/wiki/Yao_(surname)" title="Yao (surname)">Yao</a> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A7%9A" class="extiw" title="wikt:姚">姚</a></span>) families, the original <i>xìng</i> have nearly disappeared but the terms ironically reversed their meaning. <i>Xìng</i> is now used to describe the <i>shì</i> surnames which replaced them, while <i>shì</i> is used to refer to <a href="/wiki/Maiden_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Maiden name">maiden names</a>.
</p><p>The enormous modern clans sometimes share <a href="/wiki/Tanghao" class="mw-redirect" title="Tanghao">ancestral halls</a> with one another, but actually consist of many different lineages gathered under a single name. As an example, the surname <a href="/wiki/Ma_(surname)" title="Ma (surname)">Ma</a> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%A6%AC" class="extiw" title="wikt:馬">馬</a></span>) includes descendants of the <a href="/wiki/Warring_States_period" title="Warring States period">Warring States</a>–era bureaucrat <a href="/wiki/Zhao_She" title="Zhao She">Zhao She</a>, descendants of his subjects in his fief of <a href="/wiki/Mafu" class="mw-redirect" title="Mafu">Mafu</a>, Koreans from an <a href="/wiki/Mahan_confederacy" title="Mahan confederacy">unrelated confederation</a>, and Muslims from all over western China who chose it to honor <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Nonetheless, however tenuous these bonds sometimes are, it remains a minor taboo to marry someone with the same family name.
</p><p>Traditionally, a married woman keeps her name unchanged, without adopting her husband's surname.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> A child would inherit her father's surname. This is still the norm in <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">mainland China</a>, though the marriage law explicitly states that a child may use either parent's surname. It is also possible, though far less common, for a child to combine both parents' surnames. Due to Western influence, some areas of <a href="/wiki/Greater_China" title="Greater China">greater China</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> and <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a>, have also adopted the tradition of a woman changing her last name, or prepending her husband's to her own.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Given_names">Given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_given_name" title="Chinese given name">Chinese given name</a></div>
<p>Chinese given names (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">名字</span>, <i>míngzi</i>) show much greater diversity than the surnames, while still being restricted almost universally to one or two syllables. Including <a href="/wiki/Variant_Chinese_characters" class="mw-redirect" title="Variant Chinese characters">variant forms</a>, there are at least 106,000 individual Chinese characters,<sup id="cite_ref-TME_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TME-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> but as of 2006, in the People's Republic of China Public Security Bureau only approximately 32,000 are supported for computer input<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and even fewer are in common use. Given names are chosen based on a range of factors, including possession of pleasing sound and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_tones" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese tones">tonal qualities</a>, as well as bearing positive associations or a beautiful shape. Two-character <i>ming</i> may be chosen for each character's separate meaning and qualities, but the name remains a single unit which is almost always said together even when the combination no longer 'means' anything.
</p><p>Today, two-character names are more common and make up more than 80% of Chinese names.<sup id="cite_ref-ABC_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> However, this custom has been consistent only since the <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming dynasty</a>. About 70% of all names were only one character long during the <a href="/wiki/Western_Han_dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Western Han dynasty">early Han</a> and that rose beyond 98% after the usurping <a href="/wiki/Wang_Mang" title="Wang Mang">Wang Mang</a> banned all two-character names outright. Although his <a href="/wiki/Xin_dynasty" title="Xin dynasty">Xin dynasty</a> was short-lived, the law was not repealed until 400 years later, when northern invasions and interest in establishing lineages revived interest in such longer names.<sup id="cite_ref-ABC_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Song_dynasty" title="Song dynasty">Song</a> saw populations with a majority of two-character names for the first time, but the <a href="/wiki/Five_Dynasties_and_Ten_Kingdoms_period" title="Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms period">Liao</a> between them and the <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan</a> afterward both preferred single character names. The restoration of <a href="/wiki/Han_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Han people">Han</a> dominance under the Ming, promotion of <a href="/wiki/Han_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Han culture">Han culture</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Qing" class="mw-redirect" title="Qing">Qing</a>, and development of <a href="/wiki/Generation_name" title="Generation name">generation names</a> established the current traditions.<sup id="cite_ref-ABC_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Given names resonant of qualities which are perceived to be either masculine or feminine are frequently given, with males being linked with strength and firmness and females with beauty and flowers. It is also more common for female names to employ diminutives like <i>Xiǎo</i> or doubled characters in their formal names, although there are famous male examples such as <a href="/wiki/Li_Xiaoping" title="Li Xiaoping">Li Xiaoping</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yo-Yo_Ma" title="Yo-Yo Ma">Yo-Yo Ma</a>. People from the countryside previously often bore names that reflect rural life—for example, Daniu (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A4%A7" class="extiw" title="wikt:大">大</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%89%9B" class="extiw" title="wikt:牛">牛</a></span>, <small>lit.</small> "Big Ox") and Dazhu (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A4%A7" class="extiw" title="wikt:大">大</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%9F%B1" class="extiw" title="wikt:柱">柱</a></span>, <small>lit.</small> "Big Pole")—but such names are becoming less common.
</p><p>It is also considered bad form to name a child after a famous person, although tens of thousands might happen to share a common name such as "<a href="/wiki/Liu_Xiang_(hurdler)" title="Liu Xiang (hurdler)">Liu Xiang</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, owing to the traditional <a href="/wiki/Naming_taboo" title="Naming taboo">naming taboos</a>, it is very uncommon in China to name a child directly after a relative, since such children would permit junior family members to inappropriately use the personal names of senior ones. Ancestors can leave a different kind of mark: Chinese naming schemes often employ a <a href="/wiki/Generation_name" title="Generation name">generation name</a>. Every child recorded into the family records in each generation would share an identical character in their names. Sixteen, thirty-two, or more generations would be worked out in advance to form a <a href="/wiki/Generation_poem" class="mw-redirect" title="Generation poem">generation poem</a>. For example, the one selected in 1737 for the family of <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">Mao Zedong</a> read:<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd><dl><dd><span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%AB%8B" class="extiw" title="wikt:立">立</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%98%BE" class="extiw" title="wikt:显">显</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%8D%A3" class="extiw" title="wikt:荣">荣</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%9C%9D" class="extiw" title="wikt:朝">朝</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A3%AB" class="extiw" title="wikt:士">士</a></span>，<i>Stand tall &amp; display unstintingly before gentlemen,</i></dd>

<p>This scheme was in its fourteenth generation when Mao rejected it for the naming of his own children, preferring to give his sons the generational name An (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%B2%B8" class="extiw" title="wikt:岸">岸</a></span>, <small>lit.</small> "Lofty", "Proud") instead.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> A similar practice was observed regarding the <a href="/wiki/Stage_name" title="Stage name">stage names</a> of <a href="/wiki/Chinese_opera" title="Chinese opera">Chinese opera</a> performers: all the students entering a training academy in the same year would adopt the same first character in their new "given name". For example, as part of the class entering the National Drama School in 1933, <a href="/wiki/Li_Yuru" title="Li Yuru">Li Yuru</a> adopted a name with the central character "jade" (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%8E%89" class="extiw" title="wikt:玉">玉</a></span>).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Depending on the region and particular family, daughters were not entered into the family records and thus did not share the boys' generation name, although they may have borne a separate one among themselves. Even where generation names are not used, siblings' names are frequently related, so that a boy named Song (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%9D%BE" class="extiw" title="wikt:松">松</a></span>, <small>lit.</small> "Pine") might have a sister named Mei (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%A2%85" class="extiw" title="wikt:梅">梅</a></span>, <small>lit.</small> "<a href="/wiki/Plum_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Plum tree">Plum</a>"). In some families, the siblings' names have the same <a href="/wiki/Radical_(Chinese_characters)" title="Radical (Chinese characters)">radical</a>. For example, in the Jia (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">贾</span>) clan in <i><a href="/wiki/Dream_of_the_Red_Chamber" title="Dream of the Red Chamber">Dream of the Red Chamber</a></i>, a novel mirroring the rise and decline of the Qing Dynasty, there is Zheng (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">政</span>), She (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">赦</span>), and Min (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">敏</span>) in the first generation, Lian (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">琏</span>), Zhen (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">珍</span>), and Huan (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">环</span>) in the second, and Yun (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">芸</span>), Qin (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">芹</span>), and Lan (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">兰</span>(<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">蘭</span> at the time)) in the third.
</p><p>More recently, although generation names have become less common, many personal names reflect periods of <a href="/wiki/History_of_China" title="History of China">Chinese history</a>. For example, following the victory of the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese Communist Party">Communists</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Civil_War" title="Chinese Civil War">Civil War</a>, many Chinese bore "revolutionary names" such as Qiangguo (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%BC%B7" class="extiw" title="wikt:強">強</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%9C%8B" class="extiw" title="wikt:國">國</a></span>, <small>lit.</small> "Strong Nation" or "Strengthening the Nation") or Dongfeng (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%9D%B1" class="extiw" title="wikt:東">東</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%A2%A8" class="extiw" title="wikt:風">風</a></span>, <small>lit.</small> "Eastern Wind"). Similarly, on <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, it used to be common to incorporate one of the four characters of the name "<a href="/wiki/Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Republic of China">Republic of China</a>" (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E4%B8%AD%E8%8F%AF" class="extiw" title="wikt:中華">中華</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%B0%91%E5%9C%8B" class="extiw" title="wikt:民國">民國</a></span>, <i>Zhōnghuá Mínguó</i>) into masculine names. Periodic fad names like Aoyun ("Olympics") also appear. Owing to both effects, there has also been a recent trend in China to hire fortune tellers to change people's names to new ones more in accordance with traditional Taoist and <a href="/wiki/Five_elements_(Chinese_philosophy)" class="mw-redirect" title="Five elements (Chinese philosophy)">five element</a> practices. In creating a new Chinese name, it is sometimes the practice to analyze the number of strokes in the characters used in the potential name and attempt to use characters that produce specific totals of strokes.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spelling">Spelling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Spelling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">Pinyin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Chinese" title="Romanization of Chinese">Romanization of Chinese</a></div>
<p>The process of converting Chinese names into a phonetic alphabet is called <a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Chinese" title="Romanization of Chinese">romanization</a>.
</p><p>In mainland China, Chinese names have been romanized using the <a href="/wiki/Hanyu_Pinyin" class="mw-redirect" title="Hanyu Pinyin">Hanyu Pinyin</a> system since 1958. Although experiments with the complete conversion of Chinese to the Pinyin alphabet failed,<sup id="cite_ref-F&amp;F_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-F&amp;F-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> it remains in common use and has become the transcription system of the government of <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">International Organization for Standardization</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> After many decades of avoiding its use, Taiwan formally adopted Pinyin as its "New Phonetic System" in 2009,<sup id="cite_ref-pinyin_tt_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pinyin_tt-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pinyin_cp_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pinyin_cp-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> although it continues to allow its citizens to use other romanizations on official documents such as passports. The system is easily identified by its frequent use of letters uncommon in English, such as "q", "x", and "z"; when tones are included, they are noted via <a href="/wiki/Pinyin#Tones" title="Pinyin">tone marks</a>. In Pinyin, <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">毛澤東</span> is written as Máo Zédōng.
</p><p>Proper use of <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">Pinyin</a> means treating the surname and given name as precisely two separate words with no spaces between the letters of multiple Chinese characters. For example, "<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">王秀英</span>" is properly rendered either with its <a href="/wiki/Pinyin#Tones" title="Pinyin">tone marks</a> as "Wáng Xiùyīng" or without as "Wang Xiuying", but should not be written as "Wang Xiu Ying", "Wang XiuYing", "Wangxiuying", and so on. In the rare cases where a surname consists of more than one character, it too should be written as a unit: "<a href="/wiki/Sima_Qian" title="Sima Qian">Sima Qian</a>", not "Si Ma Qian" or "Si Maqian". However, as the Chinese language makes almost no use of spaces, native speakers often do not know these rules and simply put a space between each Chinese character of their name, causing those used to alphabetical languages to think of the <i>xing</i> and <i>ming</i> as three words instead of two.
</p><p>The switch to Pinyin is still quite new for Taiwan and many non-standard spellings continue to be found, including "Lee" and "Soong". Similarly, many Taiwanese and historic names still employ the older <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade–Giles">Wade–Giles</a> system. This <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English</a>-influenced system is identified by its use of the <a href="/wiki/Digraph_(orthography)" title="Digraph (orthography)">digraphs</a> "hs" (for the pinyin <i>x</i>) and "ts" (for the pinyin <i>z</i> and <i>c</i>) and by its use of hyphens to connect the syllables of multi-character words. Correct reading depends on the inclusion of superscript numbers and the use of apostrophes to distinguish between different consonants, but in practice both of these are commonly omitted. In Wade–Giles, <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">毛澤東</span> is written as <a href="/wiki/Mao_Tse-tung" class="mw-redirect" title="Mao Tse-tung">Mao Tse-tung</a>, as the system hyphenates names between the characters. For example, Wang Xiuying and Sima Qian are written in Wade as "Wang<sup>2</sup> Hsiu<sup>4</sup>-Ying<sup>1</sup>" and "Ssu<sup>1</sup>Ma<sup>3</sup> Ch'ien<sup>1</sup>".
</p><p>Pinyin and Wade–Giles both represent the pronunciation of <a href="/wiki/Standard_Chinese" title="Standard Chinese">Mandarin</a>, based on the <a href="/wiki/Beijing_dialect" title="Beijing dialect">Beijing dialect</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_diaspora" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese diaspora">diaspora</a> communities in southeast Asia and abroad, the Chinese often romanize according to the sounds of their own local Chinese dialect or lingua franca or language mostly spoken in the area or country they settled into, particularly <a href="/wiki/Cantonese_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Cantonese language">Cantonese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hokkien" title="Hokkien">Hokkien</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hakka_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Hakka language">Hakka</a>. This occurs amid a plethora of competing romanization systems. In Hong Kong, many Chinese who grew up during British colonial rule adopted English spelling conventions for their names: "Lee" for <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">李</span>, "Shaw" for <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">邵</span>, and so forth. In Macau, Chinese names are similarly sometimes still transliterated based on <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_orthography" title="Portuguese orthography">Portuguese orthography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jyutping" title="Jyutping">Jyutping</a>. The Chinese from <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a>, <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">Macau</a> and the diaspora communities in Malaysia and Singapore fully divide the characters in their names with spaces as a matter of course.
</p><p>A final point is that—although characters have remained roughly the same since the Han dynasty and some <a href="/wiki/Classical_Chinese" title="Classical Chinese">classical grammar</a> is still part of the core curriculum of Chinese secondary schools—vocabulary, grammar, and pronunciation have all changed from <a href="/wiki/Old_Chinese" title="Old Chinese">Old</a> to <a href="/wiki/Middle_Chinese" title="Middle Chinese">Middle</a> to <a href="/wiki/Written_Vernacular_Chinese" class="mw-redirect" title="Written Vernacular Chinese">Modern Chinese</a> even within the <a href="/wiki/Prestige_dialect" class="mw-redirect" title="Prestige dialect">prestige dialects</a>. Thus, although modern Chinese read "Confucius" (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%AD%94" class="extiw" title="wikt:孔">孔</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A4%AB%E5%AD%90" class="extiw" title="wikt:夫子">夫子</a></span>) as <i>Kǒng Fūzǐ</i>, the same name would have been <i>Khuwng Pjutsi</i> in the Tang dynasty and was probably something like an atonal <i>Khongʔ Patsəʔ</i> during his lifetime.<sup id="cite_ref-BaxSag_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BaxSag-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Of course, at the time, he was addressed by his <a href="/wiki/Courtesy_name" title="Courtesy name">courtesy name</a> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E4%BB%B2" class="extiw" title="wikt:仲">仲</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%B0%BC" class="extiw" title="wikt:尼">尼</a></span>, <i>Zhòngní</i>) instead.<sup id="cite_ref-BaxSag_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BaxSag-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_names">Alternative names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Alternative names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Naming_taboo" title="Naming taboo">Naming taboo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taboo_on_rulers" title="Taboo on rulers">Taboo on rulers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Table_of_Chinese_monarchs" class="mw-redirect" title="Table of Chinese monarchs">Table of Chinese monarchs</a></div>
<p>From their earliest recorded history, the Chinese observed a number of <a href="/wiki/Naming_taboo" title="Naming taboo">naming taboos</a>, avoiding the names of their elders, <a href="/wiki/Taboo_against_naming_the_dead" class="mw-redirect" title="Taboo against naming the dead">ancestors</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Taboo_on_rulers" title="Taboo on rulers">rulers</a> out of respect and fear. As a result, the upper classes of traditional Chinese culture typically employed a variety of names over the course of their lives, and the <a href="/wiki/Table_of_Chinese_monarchs" class="mw-redirect" title="Table of Chinese monarchs">emperors</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ancestor_veneration_in_China" title="Ancestor veneration in China">sanctified deceased</a> had still others.
</p><p>Current naming practices are more straightforward and consistent, but a few pseudonyms or alternate names remain common.
</p><p>When discussing Chinese writers, Chinese and Japanese scholars do not consistently use particular names, whether they are private names or alternative names.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Milk">Milk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Milk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traditionally, babies were named a hundred days after their birth; modern <a href="/wiki/Naming_laws_in_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Naming laws in the People&#39;s Republic of China">naming laws in the People's Republic of China</a> grant the parents a month before requiring the baby to be registered.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Upon birth, the parents often use a "milk name" (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E4%B9%B3%E5%90%8D" class="extiw" title="wikt:乳名">乳名</a></span>, <i>rǔmíng</i>; <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%B0%8F%E5%90%8D" class="extiw" title="wikt:小名">小名</a></span>)—typically employing <a href="/wiki/Chinese_diminutives" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese diminutives">diminutives</a> like <i>xiǎo</i> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%B0%8F" class="extiw" title="wikt:小">小</a></span>, <small>lit.</small> "little") or doubled characters—before a formal name is settled upon, often in consultation with the grandparents. The milk name may be abandoned but is often continued as a form of familial nickname. A tradition sometimes attached to the milk name is to select an unpleasant name, in order to ward off demons who might wish to harm the child.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nickname">Nickname</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nickname">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">Nicknames</a> (<span lang="zh-Hans"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%BB%B0%E5%8F%B7" class="extiw" title="wikt:绰号">绰号</a></span>; <span lang="zh-Hant"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%B6%BD%E8%99%9F" class="extiw" title="wikt:綽號">綽號</a></span>; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">chuòhào</span></i>, or <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%A4%96%E8%99%9F" class="extiw" title="wikt:外號">外號</a></span>, <i>wàihào</i>) are acquired in China in much the same way they are in other countries. Not everyone has one. Most that do received theirs in childhood or adolescence from family or friends. Common Chinese nicknames are those based on a person's physical attributes, speaking style, or behavior. Names involving animals are common, although those animals may be associated with different attributes than they are in English: for example, Chinese cows are strong, not stupid; foxes are devious, not clever; pigs are ugly, lazy, stupid, or content, but not dirty. Similarly, nicknames that might seem especially insulting in English—such as "Little Fatty" (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%B0%8F%E8%83%96" class="extiw" title="wikt:小胖">小胖</a></span>)—are more acceptable in Chinese. One especially common method of creating nicknames is prefixing <i>Ā-</i> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%98%BF" class="extiw" title="wikt:阿">阿</a></span>) or <i>Xiǎo</i> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%B0%8F" class="extiw" title="wikt:小">小</a></span>) to the surname or the second character of the given name. <i>Ā-</i> is more common in southern China and abroad, while <i>Xiǎo</i> is common throughout China. Both <i>Ā-</i> and <i>Xiǎo</i> are distinguished from <i>Lǎo</i> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%80%81" class="extiw" title="wikt:老">老</a></span>, "old" but <a href="#Forms_of_address">see below</a> for usage). Nicknames are rarely used in formal or semi-formal settings, although a famous exception is <a href="/wiki/Chen_Shui-bian" title="Chen Shui-bian">A-bian</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_name">Western name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Western name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>English is taught throughout China's secondary schools and the English language section is a required component of the <i><a href="/wiki/Gaokao" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaokao">Gaokao</a></i>, China's college entrance examination. Many Chinese teenagers thus acquire Western names, commonly of English origin, which they may keep and use as nicknames even in Chinese-language contexts. Chinese may adopt English names for a variety of reasons, including foreigners' difficulty with <a href="/wiki/Chinese_tones" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese tones">Chinese tones</a> and better integration of people working in foreign enterprises. The freedom associated with choosing a Chinese given name sometimes leads to choosing English names which seem bizarre to native English speakers. Names taken from nouns are relatively common, such as Chlorophyll, Candy, Devil or Whale<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. Established English names chosen by Chinese may also be those rarely used by native English speakers.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Creative names made and adopted by <a href="/wiki/Hongkongers" title="Hongkongers">Hongkongers</a> are generated by modifying normal English names either by deleting, inserting or substituting specific letters (e.g. Kith, Sonija, Garbie) or by mimicking the phonetic sounds of the Chinese name (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Hacken_Lee" title="Hacken Lee">Hacken Lee</a> from Lee Hak-kan (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">李克勤</span>)).<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Hong Kong, because of its one and a half century <a href="/wiki/British_Hong_Kong" title="British Hong Kong">British rule</a>, many Hong Kong people will pick English names as early as attending English classes in kindergarten, or even have the English alias embedded in official documentation. The English aliases are widely used at schools and at work. Similar to <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> people of Chinese descent, which shares a similar historical development, it is very common among <a href="/wiki/Hongkongers" title="Hongkongers">Hongkongers</a> to address each other with an English alias. An English alias can be accepted as part of the name in official documentation, but whether to include such is an option to individuals.
</p><p>It is also becoming more popular for parents to give their child a middle name in between their given name and family name, similar to many Western traditions.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="School_name">School name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: School name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The school name (<span lang="zh-Hans"><span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%AD%A6%E5%90%8D" class="extiw" title="wikt:学名">学名</a></span></span>; <span lang="zh-Hant"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%AD%B8%E5%90%8D" class="extiw" title="wikt:學名">學名</a></span>) was a separate formal name used by the child while they were at school.
</p><p>As <a href="/wiki/Binomial_nomenclature" title="Binomial nomenclature">binomial nomenclature</a> is also called <i>xuémíng</i> in Chinese, the school name is also sometimes now referenced as the <i>xùnmíng</i> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%A8%93%E5%90%8D" class="extiw" title="wikt:訓名">訓名</a></span>) to avoid confusion.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Courtesy_name">Courtesy name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Courtesy name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Courtesy_name" title="Courtesy name">Courtesy name</a></div>
<p>Upon maturity, it was common for educated males to acquire a <a href="/wiki/Courtesy_name" title="Courtesy name">courtesy name</a> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%AD%97" class="extiw" title="wikt:字">字</a></span>, <i>zì</i> or <span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%A1%A8%E5%AD%97" class="extiw" title="wikt:表字">表字</a></span>, <i>biǎozì</i>) either from one's parents, a teacher, or self-selection. The name commonly mirrored the meaning of one's given name or displayed his birth order within his family.
</p><p>The practice was a consequence of admonitions in the <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Rites" title="Book of Rites">Book of Rites</a></i> that among adults it is disrespectful to be addressed by one's given name by others within the same generation. The true given name was reserved for the use of one's elders, while the courtesy name was employed by peers on formal occasions and in writing. The practice was decried by the <a href="/wiki/May_Fourth_Movement" title="May Fourth Movement">May Fourth Movement</a> and has been largely abandoned.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pseudonym">Pseudonym</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Pseudonym">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Art_name" title="Art name">Art name</a></div>
<p>Pseudonyms or aliases (<span lang="zh-Hans"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%8F%B7" class="extiw" title="wikt:号">号</a></span>; <span lang="zh-Hant"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%99%9F" class="extiw" title="wikt:號">號</a></span>; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">hào</span></i>) or pen names (<span lang="zh-Hans"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%AC%94%E5%90%8D" class="extiw" title="wikt:笔名">笔名</a></span>; <span lang="zh-Hant"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%AD%86%E5%90%8D" class="extiw" title="wikt:筆名">筆名</a></span>; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">bǐmíng</span></i>) were self-selected alternative courtesy names, most commonly three or four characters long. They may have originated from too many people having the same courtesy name.
</p><p>Some—but by no means most—authors do continue to employ stylized pen names. A noted<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute"><span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (May 2012)">dubious</span></a>&#32;<span class="metadata"> &#8211; <a href="/wiki/Talk:Chinese_name#Dubious" title="Talk:Chinese name">discuss</a></span></i>&#93;</sup> example is the exile and dissident poet <a href="/wiki/Zhao_Zhenkai" class="mw-redirect" title="Zhao Zhenkai">Zhao Zhenkai</a>, whose pen name is "Bei Dao" (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%8C%97" class="extiw" title="wikt:北">北</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%B2%9B" class="extiw" title="wikt:岛">岛</a></span>, <small>lit.</small> "North Island").
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Posthumous_name">Posthumous name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Posthumous name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Posthumous_name" title="Posthumous name">Posthumous name</a></div>
<p>Posthumous names (<span lang="zh-Hans"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%B0%A5%E5%8F%B7" class="extiw" title="wikt:谥号">谥号</a></span>; <span lang="zh-Hant"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%AB%A1%E8%99%9F" class="extiw" title="wikt:諡號">諡號</a></span>; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">shìhào</span></i>) were honorary names selected after a person's death, used extensively for royalty. The common "names" of most Chinese emperors before the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a>—with the pointed exception of <a href="/wiki/Shi_Huangdi" class="mw-redirect" title="Shi Huangdi">Shi Huangdi</a>—are their posthumous ones. In addition to emperors, successful courtiers and politicians such as <a href="/wiki/Sun_Yat-sen" title="Sun Yat-sen">Sun Yat-sen</a> also occasionally received posthumous titles.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Temple_name">Temple name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Temple name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Temple_name" title="Temple name">Temple name</a></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Ancestor_veneration_in_China" title="Ancestor veneration in China">Ancestor veneration in China</a></div>
<p>The temple name (<span lang="zh-Hans"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%BA%99%E5%8F%B7" class="extiw" title="wikt:庙号">庙号</a></span>; <span lang="zh-Hant"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%BB%9F%E8%99%9F" class="extiw" title="wikt:廟號">廟號</a></span>; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">'miàohào</span></i>) of the emperor inscribed on the <a href="/wiki/Spiritual_tablet" class="mw-redirect" title="Spiritual tablet">spiritual tablets</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_ancestral_temple" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial ancestral temple">imperial ancestral temple</a> often differed from his posthumous name. The structure eventually became highly restricted, consisting of a single adjective and either <i>zǔ</i> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%A5%96" class="extiw" title="wikt:祖">祖</a></span>) or <i>zōng</i> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%AE%97" class="extiw" title="wikt:宗">宗</a></span>). These common "names" of the emperors between the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Yuan_dynasty" title="Yuan dynasty">Yuan</a> are their temple ones.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Era_name">Era name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Era name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Chinese_era_name" title="Chinese era name">Chinese era name</a> and <a href="/wiki/Table_of_Chinese_monarchs" class="mw-redirect" title="Table of Chinese monarchs">Table of Chinese monarchs</a></div>
<p>The era name (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%B9%B4%E8%99%9F" class="extiw" title="wikt:年號">年號</a></span>, <i>niánhào</i>) arose from the custom of dating years by the reigns of the ruling emperors. Under the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han</a>, the practice began of changing <a href="/wiki/Regnal_name" title="Regnal name">regnal names</a> as means of dispensing with bad luck and attracting better. Almost all era names were literary and employed exactly two characters. By the <a href="/wiki/Ming_dynasty" title="Ming dynasty">Ming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">Qing</a> dynasties, emperors had largely dispensed with the practice and kept a single era name during their reign, such that it is customary to refer to Ming and Qing emperors by their era names.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forms_of_address">Forms of address</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Forms of address">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Within families, it is often considered inappropriate or even offensive to use the given names of relatives who are senior to the speaker. Instead, it is more customary to identify each family member by abstract hierarchical connections: among siblings, gender and birth order (big sister, second sister, and so on); for the extended family, the manner of relationship (by birth or marriage; from the maternal or paternal side).
</p><p>The hierarchical titles of junior relatives are seldom used except in formal situations, or as indirect reference when speaking to family members who are even younger than the person in question. Children can be called by their given names, or their parents may use their nicknames.
</p><p>When speaking of non-family social acquaintances, people are generally referred to by a title, for example Mother Li (<a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">simplified Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hans">李妈妈</span>; <a href="/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_characters" title="Traditional Chinese characters">traditional Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh-Hant">李媽媽</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">lǐ māma</span></i>) or Mrs. Zhu (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">朱太太</span>, <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">zhū tàitai</span></i>). Personal names are used when referring to adult friends or to children, although, unlike in the west, referring to somebody by their full name (including surname) is common even among friends, especially if the person's full name is only two syllables.  It is common to refer to a person as <i>lǎo</i> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">老</span>, old) or <i>xiǎo</i> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">小</span>, young) followed by their family name, thus Lǎo Wáng (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">老王</span>) or Xiǎo Zhāng (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">小張, 小张</span>).  Xiǎo is also frequently used as a diminutive, when it is typically paired with the second or only character in a person's name, rather than the surname.  Note that because old people are well respected in Chinese society, <i>lǎo</i> (old) does not carry disrespect, offense or any negative implications even if it is used to refer to an older woman.  Despite this, it is advisable for non-Chinese to avoid calling a person xiǎo-something or lǎo-something unless they are so-called by other Chinese people and it is clear that the appellation is acceptable and widely used.  Otherwise, the use of the person's full name, or alternatively, their surname followed by xiānsheng (<a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh">先生</span>, mister) or nǚshì (<span lang="zh">女士</span>, madam) is relatively neutral and unlikely to cause offense.
</p><p>Within school settings and when addressing former classmates, it is common to refer to them as older siblings, e.g. elder brother Zhao (<span lang="zh-Hans">赵哥</span>; <span lang="zh-Hant">趙哥</span>; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Zhào Gé</span></i>) or e.g. elder sister Zhang (<span lang="zh-Hans">张姐</span>; <span lang="zh-Hant">張姐</span>; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Zhāng Jǐe</span></i>) if they were of senior classes, or simply to show respect or closeness. The opposite (e.g. younger brother Zhao) is rarely used. This custom spawns from traditional forms of respectful address, where it was considered rude to directly address your seniors.
</p><p>Whereas <a href="/wiki/Title" title="Title">titles</a> in many cultures are commonly solely determined by gender and, in some cases, marital status, the occupation or even work title of a person can be used as a title as a sign of respect in common address in Chinese culture. Because of the prestigious position of a teacher in traditional culture, a teacher is invariably addressed as such by his or her students (e.g. <span lang="zh">李老師</span>; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Lǐ Lǎoshī</span></i>; 'Teacher Li'), and commonly by others as a mark of respect. Where applicable, "Teacher Surname" is considered more respectful than "Mr/Mrs/Miss Surname" in Chinese. A professor is also commonly addressed as "teacher", though "professor" is also accepted as a respectful title. By extension, a junior or less experienced member of a work place or profession would address a more senior member as "Teacher".
</p><p>Similarly, engineers are often addressed as such, though often shortened to simply the first character of the word "engineer" -- <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh">工</span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Gōng</span></i>. Should the person being addressed be the head of a company (or simply the middle manager of another company to whom you would like to show respect), one might equally address them by the title "zŏng" (<span lang="zh-Hans">总</span>; <span lang="zh-Hant">總</span>), which means "general" or "overall", and is the first character of titles such as "Director General" or "General Manager" (e.g. <span lang="zh-Hans">李总</span>; <span lang="zh-Hant">李總</span>; <i><span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">Lĭ zŏng</span></i>), or, if they are slightly lower down on the corporate hierarchy but nonetheless a manager, by affixing Jīnglĭ (<span lang="zh-Hans">经理</span>; <span lang="zh-Hant">經理</span>, manager).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">Variations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Variations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unusual_names">Unusual names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Unusual names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Naming_laws_in_the_People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Naming laws in the People&#39;s Republic of China">Naming laws in the People's Republic of China</a></div>
<p>Because the small number of Chinese surnames leads to confusion in social environments, and because some Chinese parents have a desire to give individuality, some Chinese have received unusual given names. As of April 2009, about 60 million Chinese people have unusual characters in their names. A 2006 report by the <a href="/wiki/Chinese_public_security_bureau" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese public security bureau">Chinese public security bureau</a> stated that of about 55,000 Chinese characters used in the People's Republic of China, only 32,232 of those are supported by the ministry's computers. The PRC government has asked individuals with unusual names to change them so they can get new computer-readable public identity cards, and the diversity prevents them from receiving new identity cards if they do not change their names.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Beginning in at least 2003, the PRC government has been writing a list of standardized characters for everyday usage in life, which would be the pool of characters to select from when a child is given his or her name. Originally the limits were to go in place in 2005. In April 2009, the list had been revised 70 times, and it still has not been put into effect.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Wang Daliang, a <a href="/wiki/China_Youth_University_for_Political_Sciences" class="mw-redirect" title="China Youth University for Political Sciences">China Youth University for Political Sciences</a> linguistics scholar, said that "Using obscure names to avoid duplication of names or to be unique is not good. Now a lot of people are perplexed by their names. The computer cannot even recognize them and people cannot read them. This has become an obstacle in communication."<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Zhou Youyong, the dean of the <a href="/wiki/Southeast_University" title="Southeast University">Southeast University</a> law school, argued that the ability to choose the name of one's children is a fundamental right, so the PRC government should be careful when making new naming laws.<sup id="cite_ref-Xinhua_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Xinhua-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While the vast majority of Han Chinese names consist of two or three characters, there are some Han Chinese with longer names, up to 15 characters.<sup id="cite_ref-Xinhua_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Xinhua-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> In addition, <a href="/wiki/Transcription_into_Chinese_characters" title="Transcription into Chinese characters">transliteration of ethnic languages into Chinese characters</a> often results in long names.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Taiwan">Taiwan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Taiwan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Han_Chinese" title="Han Chinese">Han</a> family names in Taiwan are similar to those in southeast China, as most families trace their origins to places such as <a href="/wiki/Fujian" title="Fujian">Fujian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Guangdong" title="Guangdong">Guangdong</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Taiwanese" class="mw-redirect" title="Indigenous Taiwanese">Indigenous Taiwanese</a> have also been forced <a href="/wiki/Naming_customs_of_Taiwanese_aborigines" title="Naming customs of Taiwanese aborigines">to adopt Chinese names</a> as part of enforced <a href="/wiki/Sinicization" title="Sinicization">Sinicization</a>. The popularity distribution of family names in Taiwan as a whole differs somewhat from the distribution of names among all Han Chinese, with the family name <a href="/wiki/Chen_(surname)" title="Chen (surname)">Chen</a> (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%99%B3" class="extiw" title="wikt:陳">陳</a></span>) being particularly more common (about 11% on Taiwan, compared with about 3% on the Mainland). Local variations also exist.
</p><p>Given names that consist of one character are much less common on Taiwan than on the mainland.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>A traditional practice, now largely supplanted, was choosing deliberately unpleasant given names to ward off bad omens and evil spirits. For example, a boy facing a serious illness might be renamed Ti-sái (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%B1%AC" class="extiw" title="wikt:豬">豬</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%B1%8E" class="extiw" title="wikt:屎">屎</a></span>, <small>lit.</small> "Pig Shit") to indicate to the evil spirits that he was not worth their trouble. Similarly, a girl from a poor family might have the name Bóng-chī (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%BD%94" class="extiw" title="wikt:罔">罔</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%B8%82" class="extiw" title="wikt:市">市</a></span>, <small>lit.</small> "No Takers").
</p><p>Nicknames (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%9B%9D" class="extiw" title="wikt:囝">囝</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E4%BB%94" class="extiw" title="wikt:仔">仔</a><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%90%8D" class="extiw" title="wikt:名">名</a></span>, <i>gín-á-miâ</i>, "child names") are common and generally adopt the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanfangren&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Nanfangren (page does not exist)">Southern Chinese</a> practice of affixing the prefix "A-" (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%98%BF" class="extiw" title="wikt:阿">阿</a></span>) to the last syllable of a person's name. Although these names are rarely used in formal contexts, there are a few public figures who are well known by their nicknames, including former president <a href="/wiki/Chen_Shui-bian" title="Chen Shui-bian">A-bian</a> and the singer <a href="/wiki/A-mei" class="mw-redirect" title="A-mei">A-mei</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diaspora">Diaspora</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Diaspora">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Among <a href="/wiki/Chinese_American" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese American">Chinese Americans</a>, it is common practice to be referred to primarily by the Western name and to use the Chinese given name as an often-omitted <a href="/wiki/Middle_name" title="Middle name">middle name</a>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_Chinese" title="Malaysian Chinese">Malaysia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Singaporean" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese Singaporean">Singapore</a>, it is equally acceptable for Western names to appear before or after the Chinese given name, in Latin characters. Thus, the <a href="/wiki/President_of_Singapore" title="President of Singapore">Singaporean President</a> <a href="/wiki/Tony_Tan_Keng_Yam" class="mw-redirect" title="Tony Tan Keng Yam">Tony Tan</a> might see his name written as "Tony Tan Keng Yam" or "Tan Keng Yam Tony".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Individuals are free to register their <a href="/wiki/Legal_name" title="Legal name">legal names</a> in either format on their <a href="/wiki/National_Registration_Identity_Card" title="National Registration Identity Card">identity cards</a>. In general use, the English name first version is typically preferred as it keeps the correct order for both systems; however, for administrative purposes, the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Singapore" title="Government of Singapore">government</a> <a href="/wiki/Organisations_of_the_Singapore_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Organisations of the Singapore Government">agencies</a> tend to place the English name last to organize lists of names and databases more easily, similar to the Western practice of organizing names with the last name first followed by a comma ("Smith, John").
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> printed media tend to adopt the hybrid name style—for example, <a href="/wiki/Andy_Lau_Tak-wah" class="mw-redirect" title="Andy Lau Tak-wah">Andy Lau Tak-wah</a>—although some people prefer American-style middle names, such as <a href="/wiki/Steven_N._S._Cheung" title="Steven N. S. Cheung">Steven N. S. Cheung</a>, or simply use English names like <a href="/wiki/Henry_Lee_(actor)" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Lee (actor)">Henry Lee</a>. On official records such as the Hong Kong <a href="/wiki/Identity_document" title="Identity document">Identity Cards</a>, family names are always printed first in all-caps Latin characters and followed by a comma for all names, including Chinese ones. Thus, the examples above would have identity cards that read "LAU, Tak-wah Andy" or "CHEUNG, Steven Ng-sheong", with the position of the given names determined at the time of application. Non-Chinese names are printed in similar style: "DOE, Jane".
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, one of the country with the most <a href="/wiki/Chinese_diaspora" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese diaspora">Chinese diaspora</a> population, the <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_Chinese" class="mw-redirect" title="Indonesian Chinese">Indonesian Chinese</a> in Indonesia and in diaspora has mostly adopted <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Indonesian_surname" title="Chinese Indonesian surname">Indonesian-sounding Chinese names</a> due to centuries of regulation and acculturation. Conversely, the usage of these Indonesian-sounding Chinese names are not restricted for surnames, and many are used liberally between other surnames since many Indonesian Chinese didn't keep track their Chinese (sur)names anymore, and even used by non-Chinese people (with some names being borrowing from regional languages and names).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_names_in_English">Chinese names in English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Chinese names in English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:171px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sun_Yat_Sen_Signature.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/77/Sun_Yat_Sen_Signature.png" decoding="async" width="169" height="41" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="169" data-file-height="41" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sun_Yat_Sen_Signature.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The signature of <a href="/wiki/Sun_Yat-sen" title="Sun Yat-sen">Sun Yat-sen</a>; in English Chinese people usually keep their names in Chinese order unless they live or travel abroad</div></div></div>
<p>Chinese people, except for those traveling or living outside China, rarely reverse their names to the western naming order (given name, then family name). Western publications usually preserve the Chinese naming order, with the family name first, followed by the given name. Beginning in the early 1980s, in regards to people from <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">Mainland China</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> western publications began using the <a href="/wiki/Hanyu_Pinyin" class="mw-redirect" title="Hanyu Pinyin">Hanyu Pinyin</a> romanization system instead of earlier romanization systems; this resulted from the normalization of diplomatic relations between the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> and the <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" class="mw-redirect" title="People&#39;s Republic of China">People's Republic of China</a> in 1979.<sup id="cite_ref-Terryp633_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Terryp633-25">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The usual presentation of Chinese names in English differs from the usual presentations of modern <a href="/wiki/Japanese_name" title="Japanese name">Japanese names</a>, since modern Japanese names are usually reversed to fit the western order in English. In English the presentation of Chinese names is similar to those of <a href="/wiki/Korean_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Korean names">Korean names</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPower2008C4-2_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPower2008C4-2-26">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> As of 1989, Pinyin became the preferred romanization system in works discussing contemporary China, while English-language books relevant to Japanese history still used the <a href="/wiki/Wade%E2%80%93Giles" title="Wade–Giles">Wade–Giles</a> system to romanize Chinese names more often than other romanization systems.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> As of 1993, Wade–Giles was still used in Taiwan.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Unlike mainland Chinese, Taiwanese people usually place a dash between the two characters of the given name, similar to Korean names. This is also the case for the standard styling of Hong Kong Chinese names, where the given name is hyphenated.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Names of <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_Chinese" title="Malaysian Chinese">Malaysian Chinese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Singaporeans" title="Chinese Singaporeans">Singaporean Chinese</a> people are often <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language_romanisation_in_Singapore" title="Chinese language romanisation in Singapore">romanized</a> inconsistently, but are usually expressed in three parts (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Goh_Chok_Tong" title="Goh Chok Tong">Goh Chok Tong</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For people with just a single given name or with <a href="/wiki/Chinese_compound_surnames" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese compound surnames">compound surnames</a> and single given name, the western name order may add to the complication of confusing the surname and given name.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_names_in_Japanese">Chinese names in Japanese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Chinese names in Japanese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the Japanese language, Chinese names can be pronounced either approximating the original Chinese, the <b>Local reading</b> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">現地読み</span>) of the characters, or using a Sino-Japanese <b>On'yomi reading</b> (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">音読み</span>) to pronounce the Chinese characters. Local readings are often written in katakana rather than kanji, but not always. For example, <span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">毛泽东</span> (Mao Zedong) is pronounced Mō Takutō using an On'yomi reading, whereas Beijing (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">北京</span>) is spelled with kanji but pronounced Pekin (<span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">ペキン</span>), with a local reading (which may also be considered a post-Tōsō-on reading), rather than Hokkyō (which would be the Kan-on reading).
</p>
