<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The earliest surnames found in Scotland occur during the reign of <a href="/wiki/David_I_of_Scotland" title="David I of Scotland">David I</a>, <a href="/wiki/King_of_Scots" class="mw-redirect" title="King of Scots">King of Scots</a> (1124–53). These were <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Normans" title="Anglo-Normans">Anglo-Norman</a> names which had become <a href="/wiki/Hereditary" class="mw-redirect" title="Hereditary">hereditary</a> in England before arriving in Scotland (for example, the contemporary surnames <i>de Brus</i>, <i>de Umfraville</i>, and <i>Ridel</i>). During the reigns of kings David I, <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_IV_of_Scotland" title="Malcolm IV of Scotland">Malcolm IV</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_the_Lion" title="William the Lion">William the Lion</a>, some inhabitants of Scottish towns were <a href="/wiki/English_people" title="English people">English</a> and <a href="/wiki/Flemish_people" title="Flemish people">Flemish</a> settlers, who bore English and continental <a href="/wiki/Personal_name" title="Personal name">personal names</a>, with trade names and sometimes nicknames.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> One of the earliest sources for surnames in Scotland is the <a href="/wiki/Ragman_Roll" class="mw-redirect" title="Ragman Roll">Ragman Roll</a>. This document records the deeds of <a href="/wiki/Homage_(feudal)" title="Homage (feudal)">homage</a> pledged by Scots nobles to <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England" title="Edward I of England">Edward I</a>, <a href="/wiki/King_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="King of England">King of England</a> in 1296. The surnames recorded within are for the most part very similar to those found in England at around the same date, consisting of local, patronymic and occupational names, and nicknames. Some of the local surnames with the roll are derived from places within Scotland; there are very few Gaelic surnames recorded in the roll.<sup id="cite_ref-Reaney_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reaney-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Categorisation">Categorisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Categorisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Patronymics">Patronymics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Patronymics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many Scottish surnames originate from names that were originally <a href="/wiki/Patronym" class="mw-redirect" title="Patronym">patronyms</a>. Patronyms are derived from the <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">forename</a> of the bearer's father (for example, the full name of a man named <i>John Donaldson</i> indicates that the father's name was <i>Donald</i>). Patronyms change with every successive generation<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xxivxxv_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xxivxxv-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> (for example, the patronyms of a grandson, father, and grandfather may be <i>John Donaldson</i>, son of <i>Donald Robertson</i>, son of <i>Robert Williamson</i>).
</p><p>The earliest patronyms recorded in Scotland are written in several different languages. In early <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> documents, such names were formed by the <a href="/wiki/Genitive" class="mw-redirect" title="Genitive">genitive</a> case of the father's name preceded by forms of <i>filius</i>, meaning "son" (for example <i>Dugaldus filius Nigelli</i>); later the <i>filius</i> was only implied (for example <i>Dugaldus Nigelli</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xxivxxv_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xxivxxv-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;note 1&#93;</a></sup> Other early records show patronyms using forms of the Welsh <i>ap</i>, meaning "son"; and the Gaelic <i>mac</i>, meaning son (for example, the names of <i>Macrath ap Molegan</i>, and <i>Gilmychel Mac Eth</i> appear in the same document).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;note 2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are several <a href="/wiki/Prefix" title="Prefix">prefixes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffixes</a> that may indicate whether a modern surname originated from a patronym: the English suffixes <i>-son</i>, and <i>-s</i>; and the Gaelic prefix <i>Mac-</i>. In some cases, the <i>-son</i> was dropped from such surnames, and just the forename of an ancestor was used (for example <i><a href="/wiki/Martin_(surname)" class="mw-redirect" title="Martin (surname)">Martin</a></i>). In some cases, the suffix <i>-s</i> was used, and according to Black, such names appear to have originated in England (for example <i><a href="/wiki/Adams_(surname)" title="Adams (surname)">Adams</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xxivxxv_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xxivxxv-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The use of patronyms died out in the Lowlands after the 15th century, as they became solidified as surnames.<sup id="cite_ref-scotlandspeople-560_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scotlandspeople-560-8">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> It was not until the 18th century that they were given up in the Gaelic-speaking Highlands. As late as the first part of the 18th century, some men were distinguished not only by their father's name, but their grandfather's and great-grandfather's (for example, <i>John Roy M'Ean Vc Ewin Vc Dougall Vc Ean</i>, a man from <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Lismore" class="mw-redirect" title="Isle of Lismore">Lismore</a> recorded in 1585).<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xlii_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xlii-9">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;note 3&#93;</a></sup> Patronyms were still common in Shetland in the first half of the 19th century. One of the most common surnames in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> is <a href="/wiki/Simpson_(surname)" class="mw-redirect" title="Simpson (surname)">Simpson</a>, which means the son of "Simon", in Gaelic the equivalent names are McSymon, and MacSymon.
</p>
<h3><span id="Territorial_names.2C_topographical_names"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Territorial_names,_topographical_names">Territorial names, topographical names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Territorial names, topographical names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many of the first surnames recorded in Scotland were those of nobles, or great landowners, whose surnames derived from the lands they possessed.<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xxvxxvii_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xxvxxvii-11">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> These names are sometimes called territorial names,<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xxvxxvii_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xxvxxvii-11">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> or habitation names.<sup id="cite_ref-askoxford_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-askoxford-12">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Many of these surnames were brought to Scotland by Anglo-Normans, whose surnames were derived from either lands in  Normandy or in England (for example, <i><a href="/wiki/Bruce" title="Bruce">Bruce</a></i> is derived from <a href="/wiki/Brix,_Manche" title="Brix, Manche">Brix</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manche" title="Manche">Manche</a>, France,<sup id="cite_ref-B3-5_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B3-5-13">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Crawford_(name)" title="Crawford (name)">Crawford</a></i> is derived from <a href="/wiki/Crawford,_South_Lanarkshire" title="Crawford, South Lanarkshire">Crawford, South Lanarkshire</a>, in the south of Scotland, <i><a href="/wiki/Barton_(surname)" title="Barton (surname)">Barton</a></i> is devired from <a href="/wiki/Dumbarton" title="Dumbarton">Dumbarton</a>, or the several villages and towns in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Graham_(surname)" title="Graham (surname)">Graham</a></i> is derived from <a href="/wiki/Grantham" title="Grantham">Grantham</a>, in <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>, England).<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Not all territorial surnames are derived from lands owned by their bearers.<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xxvxxvii_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xxvxxvii-11">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In some cases such names were borne by tenants, or followers, of the owners of the lands they lived on.  In this way the bearers of these surnames may not have had any kinship with the landowners (the surname <i><a href="/wiki/Gordon_(surname)" title="Gordon (surname)">Gordon</a></i> is an historical example of such a name).<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xxvxxvii_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xxvxxvii-11">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some Scottish surnames are derived from vague geographical locations rather than specific places.<sup id="cite_ref-N1-96_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-N1-96-15">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> These names are sometimes called "topographic names".<sup id="cite_ref-askoxford_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-askoxford-12">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> These names refer to physical features, like forests, streams, and marshes; such names may also refer to man-made structures, such as castles and churches<sup id="cite_ref-askoxford_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-askoxford-12">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> (for example the surnames <i><a href="/wiki/Wood_(surname)" title="Wood (surname)">Wood</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Milne_(surname)" title="Milne (surname)">Milne</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Shaw_(name)" title="Shaw (name)">Shaw</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-N1-96_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-N1-96-15">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Sometimes names derived from proper names of geographical features can be classified as topographic names rather than habitational names. This is because these names refer to a location rather than a specific settlement.<sup id="cite_ref-askoxford_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-askoxford-12">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Occupational_names">Occupational names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Occupational names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many surnames are derived from the occupations, or <a href="/wiki/Trade_(occupation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Trade (occupation)">trades</a>, of their original bearers (for example, <i><a href="/wiki/Stewart_(surname)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stewart (surname)">Stewart</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Shepherd_(surname)" class="mw-redirect" title="Shepherd (surname)">Shepherd</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mason_(surname)" title="Mason (surname)">Mason</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Kemp_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Kemp (disambiguation)">Kemp</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Webb_(surname)" title="Webb (surname)">Webb</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Fletcher_(surname)" title="Fletcher (surname)">Fletcher</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-NRS--BowieJackson_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NRS--BowieJackson-16">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> In time, true occupational surnames became hereditary and were passed down through families (for example, in 1525 there is a record of a woman named <i>Agnes Beltmakar</i>, who is described as a <i>kaikbakstar</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-N1-96_15-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-N1-96-15">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;note 4&#93;</a></sup> Occupational names were rare amongst Gaelic speakers<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Examples of such surnames derived from Gaelic occupational name is <i><a href="/wiki/Gow_(surname)" title="Gow (surname)">Gow</a></i>, from the Scottish Gaelic <i>Gobha</i> (smith),<sup id="cite_ref-m1_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-m1-19">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/MacIntyre" title="MacIntyre">MacIntyre</a></i> from <i>Mac an t-Saoir</i> (<small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Help:IPA/Scottish Gaelic">[ˈmaxk ən̪ˠ ˈt̪ʰɯːɾʲ]</a></span> "son of the carpenter."<sup id="cite_ref-m1_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-m1-19">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/McPherson_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="McPherson (disambiguation)">Macpherson</a></i> means "son of the <a href="/wiki/Parson" title="Parson">parson</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> from the Gaelic surname <i>Mac a' Phearsain</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-m1_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-m1-19">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bynames">Bynames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Bynames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Bynames, to-names, or other names,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;note 5&#93;</a></sup> were once very common in Scotland.<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xxxxxxii_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xxxxxxii-21">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> These names were used in areas where there were few names in circulation, and the bynames were added onto the name of person, in order to distinguish them from others who bore the same name. Bynames were particularly prevalent in fishing communities in the northeastern part of Scotland,<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xxxxxxii_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xxxxxxii-21">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> but were also used in the Borders and the West Highlands.<sup id="cite_ref-scotlandspeople-560_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scotlandspeople-560-8">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> In some cases within fishing communities, the names of fishing boats were tacked onto the names people in order to differentiate them from others.<sup id="cite_ref-NRS--BowieJackson_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NRS--BowieJackson-16">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Examples of Scottish surnames derived from nicknames are: <i><a href="/wiki/Little_(surname)" title="Little (surname)">Little</a></i>; <i><a href="/wiki/White_(surname)" title="White (surname)">White</a></i>; and <i><a href="/wiki/Meikle_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Meikle (disambiguation)">Meikle</a></i> (which means "big"). One of the most common Scottish surnames is <i><a href="/wiki/Campbell_(surname)" title="Campbell (surname)">Campbell</a></i>, which is derived from the Gaelic <i>Caimbeul</i>, meaning "crooked-mouth".<sup id="cite_ref-NRS--BowieJackson_16-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NRS--BowieJackson-16">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>. Another common Scottish surname is <i><a href="/wiki/Armstrong_(surname)" title="Armstrong (surname)">Armstrong</a></i>, which means the son of a strong man.
</p>
<h3><span id="Regional_names.2C_or_ethnic_names"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_names,_or_ethnic_names">Regional names, or ethnic names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Regional names, or ethnic names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some Scottish surnames can be classified as either "regional names" or "ethnic names".<sup id="cite_ref-askoxford_12-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-askoxford-12">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> These names originally referred to the origin of the bearer and tended to have been acquired by people who migrated a considerable distance for their original homes. In other cases, such names were sometimes borne by people were connected with a foreign place (such as a trader).<sup id="cite_ref-askoxford_12-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-askoxford-12">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Examples of ethnic surnames are <i><a href="/wiki/Fleming_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Fleming (disambiguation)">Fleming</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Galbraith" title="Galbraith">Galbraith</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Scott_(name)" title="Scott (name)">Scott</a></i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scottish_clans">Scottish clans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Scottish clans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many Scottish surnames are the names of <a href="/wiki/Scottish_clans" class="mw-redirect" title="Scottish clans">Scottish clans</a> that were once powerful families dominating large swaths of territory.<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xxxviixxxviii_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xxxviixxxviii-23">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> However, it is a common misconception that every person who bears a clan's name is a lineal descendant of the chiefs of that particular clan.<sup id="cite_ref-scotlandspeople-560_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scotlandspeople-560-8">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;note 6&#93;</a></sup> There are several reasons for this. In many cases, the families that originally lived on the lands acquired by powerful clans (such as the <a href="/wiki/Clan_Campbell" title="Clan Campbell">Campbells</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clan_Gordon" title="Clan Gordon">Gordons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clan_Donald" title="Clan Donald">Macdonalds</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Clan_Mackenzie" title="Clan Mackenzie">Mackenzies</a>) adopted the names of their new lords.<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xxxviixxxviii_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xxxviixxxviii-23">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;note 7&#93;</a></sup> The leadership of large clans increased their power by increasing the number of their followers by both <a href="/wiki/Conciliation" title="Conciliation">conciliation</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coercion" title="Coercion">coercion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Reaney_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reaney-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The memory of such renaming is sometimes preserved in tradition. One old Gaelic saying reads: <i>Frisealach am boll a mine</i> ("the Frasers of the boll of meal"), which explains that some of name <i><a href="/wiki/Fraser_(surname)" title="Fraser (surname)">Fraser</a></i> are actually <a href="/wiki/Clan_Bissett" title="Clan Bissett">Bissets</a>, who originally controlled the lands taken by the <a href="/wiki/Clan_Fraser" title="Clan Fraser">Frasers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;note 8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 17th and 18th centuries, the clan name of the <a href="/wiki/Clan_Gregor" title="Clan Gregor">MacGregors</a> was outlawed, and members of the clan were forced to assume other names. When the bans were lifted once and for all, some of the clan resumed using forms of <i><a href="/wiki/MacGregor_(surname)" title="MacGregor (surname)">MacGregor</a></i>—but not all.<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xxxviixxxviii_23-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xxxviixxxviii-23">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;note 9&#93;</a></sup> In some cases, the name of a clan may be identical to the surname of another family, yet there is no etymological link between the employed surname, and there is no historical connection between the different families (for example, the Hebridean/Kintyre surname <i><a href="/wiki/Brodie" title="Brodie">Brodie</a></i> is not connected to surname of the <a href="/wiki/Clan_Brodie" title="Clan Brodie">Brodies of Brodie</a>, who were centred in <a href="/wiki/Moray" title="Moray">Moray</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-B1-104105_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-104105-32">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;note 10&#93;</a></sup> Similarly, a surname derived from a patronym, may be used by numerous unconnected families descended from a like-named individual (for example, the bardic family of the surname <i><a href="/wiki/MacEwen" title="MacEwen">MacEwan</a></i> employed by the Campbells are not connected to the <a href="/wiki/Clan_Ewen_of_Otter" title="Clan Ewen of Otter">MacEwens of Otter</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-C1-7_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C1-7-36">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;note 11&#93;</a></sup> Historian C.I Fraser of Reelig stated in his history of the <a href="/wiki/Clan_Munro" title="Clan Munro">Clan Munro</a> that the bond between clansman and chief cannot in every instance have been that of a common blood.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scottish_heraldry">Scottish heraldry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Scottish heraldry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Scottish_heraldry" title="Scottish heraldry">Scottish heraldry</a> operates under the implication that everyone who shares the same surname might be related.<sup id="cite_ref-B2_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B2-39">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> The position of the standing court of <a href="/wiki/Heraldry" title="Heraldry">heraldry</a> in Scotland, the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_the_Lord_Lyon" title="Court of the Lord Lyon">Court of the Lord Lyon</a>, considers that everyone who shares the same surname as a recognised chief, is a member of that chief's clan.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> In consequence, where a <a href="/wiki/Coat_of_arms" title="Coat of arms">coat of arms</a> already exists for the chief of a clan, or head of a family, when new grants of arms to individuals with the same surname are being discussed with the client, then the suggestions put forward are generally variations of those arms<sup id="cite_ref-B2_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B2-39">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> (for example, one of the oldest families of <a href="/wiki/Argyll" title="Argyll">Argyll</a> are the Fergussons of Glensellich; this clan is not related or historically connected in any way to the <a href="/wiki/Fergusson_baronets" title="Fergusson baronets">Fergussons of Kilkerran</a> who the Lord Lyon King of Arms considers to be the <i><a href="/wiki/Chief_of_the_Name_and_Arms" class="mw-redirect" title="Chief of the Name and Arms">Chief of the Name and Arms</a> of Fergusson</i>; consequently, the arms of Fergusson of Glensellich are based upon the arms of Fergusson of Kilkerran).<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Influences">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gaelic">Gaelic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Gaelic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Not all surnames that begin with the prefix <i>Mac-</i> are truly derived from patronyms. Forms of the surnames <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=MacBeth_(surname)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="MacBeth (surname) (page does not exist)">MacBeth</a></i> and <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=MacRae_(surname)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="MacRae (surname) (page does not exist)">MacRae</a></i> are derived from the Gaelic personal names <i>Mac-bethad</i> and <i>Mac-raith</i>. The prefix in such cases means "pupil", "devotee", "disciple".<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> (Other examples can be found under the section Occupational Surnames). 
Also, not all names beginning with the prefix are derived from Gaelic personal names; in areas along the Lowland border, the prefix was added to the diminutives of non-Gaelic personal names ending with the suffix <i>-ie</i> (for example, <i>McRitchie</i>, <i>MacWillie</i>). Other Lowland, or English, diminutives of personal names ending with the suffixes <i>-on</i>, and <i>-in</i>, were borrowed by Gaelic speakers (for example <i><a href="/wiki/Rankin_(name)" title="Rankin (name)">Rankin</a></i> to <i>Macrankin</i>, <i>Gibbon</i> to <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Macgibbon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Macgibbon (page does not exist)">Macgibbon</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xxxviiixl_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xxxviiixl-44">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Norse">Norse</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Norse">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the northern Hebrides, many of the indigenous surnames are derived from <a href="/wiki/Gaelicisation" title="Gaelicisation">Gaelicised</a> Norse personal names (for example, MacAmhlaigh -&gt; <i><a href="/wiki/Macaulay_(surname)" title="Macaulay (surname)">Macaulay</a></i>, MacAsgaill -&gt; <i><a href="/wiki/MacAskill_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="MacAskill (disambiguation)">Macaskill</a></i>, and MacLeòid -&gt; <i><a href="/wiki/Macleod" class="mw-redirect" title="Macleod">Macleod</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-m1_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-m1-19">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Such 'Norse' names in these areas are in complete contrast to the indigenous surnames of the adjacent islands and lands to the south.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Some surnames brought to Scotland in the Middle Ages by English, or Anglo-Normans, are also derived from Norse personal names.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Anglo-Normans">Anglo-Normans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Anglo-Normans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nearly all the surnames brought to Britain by the Normans were territorial names derived from lands on the continent. In some families where patronyms were used, once a man acquired lands in Britain he would assume his surname from these lands. Norman patronyms were made up of mainly three types of names: <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">Germanic</a> names derived from <a href="/wiki/Frankish_language" title="Frankish language">Frankish</a> names; other Germanic names derived from Norse names; and Latin and Greek names, many of which were religious names. Many diminutive suffixes were introduced with the Normans (for example, <i>-el</i>, <i>-et</i>, <i>-ett</i>, <i>-ot</i>, <i>-at</i>, <i>-en</i>, <i>-in</i>, <i>-oc</i>, <i>-on</i>, <i>-uc</i>, and <i>-cock</i>). These suffixes were added to pet-forms of names (for example, <i>Adkin</i> diminutive of <i><a href="/wiki/Adam_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Adam (name)">Adam</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Patton_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Patton (disambiguation)">Paton</a></i> from <i><a href="/wiki/Patrick_(given_name)" title="Patrick (given name)">Patrick</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xlivxlv_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xlivxlv-46">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The name <i><a href="/wiki/Fraser_(surname)" title="Fraser (surname)">Fraser</a></i> (Gaelic <i>Friseal</i>) has a unique etymology; it originally derives from the French <i>fraise</i>, meaning <i>strawberry</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Extinction">Extinction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Extinction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While the total bearers of specific surnames have grown in number over the years, such as the names of the most dominant clans, some Scottish surnames have <a href="/wiki/Extinction_of_surnames" class="mw-redirect" title="Extinction of surnames">completely died out</a> in the country (for example, the family with the Hebridean surname <i>MacUspaig</i>, derived from a Gaelicised Norse personal name, died out in the male line shortly after the turn of the 20th century).<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surname_changes">Surname changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Surname changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anyone whose <a href="/wiki/Birth_registration" class="mw-redirect" title="Birth registration">birth is registered</a> in Scotland, or who was legally adopted in Scotland, may apply to register a surname <a href="/wiki/Name_change" title="Name change">change</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Children under the age of 16 are only allowed to change their surname once. For people over the age of 16, a total of three surname changes are allowed, however a period of five years must elapse between each successive name change. In some cases, surname changes are allowed if the person receives a decree, or certificate of change, from the <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lyon_King_of_Arms" title="Lord Lyon King of Arms">Lord Lyon King of Arms</a>; or if certain certified <a href="/wiki/Will_(law)" class="mw-redirect" title="Will (law)">wills</a>, settlements, or deeds of trust, contain conditions where a person must take a surname different from the one which they were registered at birth; or when a male has married and changed his surname following his marriage, and has received a decree or certificate from the Lord Lyon King of Arms. Surname changes can consist of substituting one surname for another (for example, from <i>Brown</i> to <i>Johnson</i>); changing the spelling of the surname (for example, from <i>Brown</i> to <i>Broun</i>); or changing surname by <a href="/wiki/Hyphen" title="Hyphen">hyphenation</a> (for example, from <i>Brown</i> to <i>Brown-Johnson</i>) (see also: <a href="/wiki/Double-barrelled_name" title="Double-barrelled name">double-barrelled name</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Scottish_surnames_as_given_names">Scottish surnames as given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Scottish_surnames&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Scottish surnames as given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In recent years, names that have been traditionally surnames have been used as <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given names</a>, particularly in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>. These names are sometimes given to both males and females (for example, the given name <i><a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_(given_name)" title="Mackenzie (given name)">Mackenzie</a></i>, taken from the Scottish surname <i><a href="/wiki/Mackenzie_(surname)" title="Mackenzie (surname)">Mackenzie</a></i>, is given to girls more than boys in North America;<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> another North American given name used for both genders is <i><a href="/wiki/Cameron_(given_name)" title="Cameron (given name)">Cameron</a></i> derived from the surname <i><a href="/wiki/Cameron_(surname)" title="Cameron (surname)">Cameron</a></i>).<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<li id="cite_note-4"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-4">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In names where <i>filius</i> is implied, it is sometimes difficult to know whether the name refers to a Dugald who is actually a <i>son</i> of Nigel, or if he is just a Dugald who is descended in several generations from a Nigel.<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xxivxxv_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xxivxxv-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-7"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-7">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Both Macrath, and Gilmychel, were recorded as being from <a href="/wiki/Dumfriesshire" title="Dumfriesshire">Dumfriesshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-10"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-10">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">This names equates to Modern Scottish Gaelic: <i>Iain Ruadh mac Iain mhic Eòghainn mhic Dhùghaill mhic Iain</i>; or in English: <i>John the Red, son of John, son of Ewen, son of Dougal, son of John</i>.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-17"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-17">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Several hundred years before she would have been known as Agnes 'the maker of belts and girdles', rather than Agnes 'the baker of cakes'.<sup id="cite_ref-N1-96_15-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-N1-96-15">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-22"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-22">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">From the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> <i>tō-nama</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xxxxxxii_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xxxxxxii-21">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-25"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-25">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">For example, a recent and ongoing <a href="/wiki/Surname_DNA_project" title="Surname DNA project">DNA study</a> shows that about 13% of men taking part in the Clan Donald DNA project,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> most of whom bear forms of the surname <i><a href="/wiki/Macdonald" title="Macdonald">Macdonald</a></i>, are lineal descendants of one man, possibly <a href="/wiki/Somerled" title="Somerled">Somhairle</a> the grandfather of <a href="/wiki/Clan_Donald" title="Clan Donald">Clan Donald</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Eponymous" class="mw-redirect" title="Eponymous">eponymous</a> ancestor, <a href="/wiki/Domhnall_mac_Raghnaill" title="Domhnall mac Raghnaill">Domhnall mac Raghnaill</a>.</span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-28"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-28">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">From the beginning of the 16th century, as tenants of the <a href="/wiki/Earl_of_Seaforth" title="Earl of Seaforth">Earl of Seaforth</a>, the native clans of Lewis followed the Mackenzies. An account of <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Lewis" title="Isle of Lewis">Lewis</a>, written in about 1750, states: "the common inhabitants of Lewis are Morisons, McAulays and McKivers, but when they go from home, all who live under Seaforth call themselves Mackenzies".<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The Campbells are known to have swallowed the names of several smaller clans that they dominated over, such as in the case of the MacIvers of Lergachonzie sometime after the mid 16th century.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-29"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-29">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">There are similar traditions associated with the <a href="/wiki/Clan_Farquharson" title="Clan Farquharson">Farquharsons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clan_Forbes" title="Clan Forbes">Forbeses</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Clan_Gordon" title="Clan Gordon">Gordons</a>. One associated with the <a href="/wiki/Clan_Comyn" class="mw-redirect" title="Clan Comyn">Cumins</a> reads: <i>Cuminich clach nan cearc</i> ("Cumins of the hen-trough"), to distinguish certain renamed families from the lineal Cumins of the clan.<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xxxviixxxviii_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xxxviixxxviii-23">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-31"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-31">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The name was first proscribed by an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Parliament" title="Act of Parliament">Act of Parliament</a> in 1603. The proscription was rescinded in 1661, but revived in 1693; it wasn't abolished once and for all until 1784.<sup id="cite_ref-B1-505506_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-505506-30">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-35"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-35">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The Brodies from the Hebrides and Kintyre are thought to be a branch of the O'Brologhan family who were centred in <a href="/wiki/County_Londonderry" title="County Londonderry">County Londonderry</a>, Ireland. In modern times their Gaelic surname has been <a href="/wiki/Anglicised" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglicised">Anglicised</a> into the form used by Brodies of <a href="/wiki/Clan_Brodie" title="Clan Brodie">Clan Brodie</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
<li id="cite_note-37"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-37">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The bardic MacEwans are actually <a href="/wiki/Clan_MacDougall" title="Clan MacDougall">MacDougalls</a>, or possibly O'Hoseys.<sup id="cite_ref-B1-xlii_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B1-xlii-9">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-C1-7_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C1-7-36">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup></span>
</li>
</ol></div></div>
