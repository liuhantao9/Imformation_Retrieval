<p><b>Gonzalo Fernández de Córdoba y Enríquez de Aguilar</b> (1 September 1453&#160;– 2 December 1515) was a Spanish general and statesman who led successful military campaigns during the <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Granada" class="mw-redirect" title="Conquest of Granada">Conquest of Granada</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a>. His military victories and widespread popularity earned him the distinction of being called "<i>El Gran Capitán</i>" ("The Great Captain"). He also negotiated the final surrender of Granada and later served as <a href="/wiki/Viceroy_of_Naples" class="mw-redirect" title="Viceroy of Naples">Viceroy of Naples</a>. Gonzalo was a masterful military strategist and tactician. He was the first to introduce the successful use of firearms on the battlefield and he reorganized his infantry to include pikes and firearms in effective defensive and offensive formations. The changes implemented by Gonzalo were instrumental in making the Spanish army a dominant force in Europe for more than a hundred years.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Córdoba was born on 1 September 1453 at <a href="/wiki/Montilla" title="Montilla">Montilla</a> in the <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba_Province_(Spain)" class="mw-redirect" title="Córdoba Province (Spain)">province of Córdoba</a>. He was the younger son of Pedro Fernández de Córdoba, Count of <a href="/wiki/Aguilar_de_la_Frontera" title="Aguilar de la Frontera">Aguilar</a> (himself the son of 
Pedro Fernández de Córdoba, 1390&#8211;1424 and of Leonor de Arellano)
and of Elvira de Herrera (daughter of Pedro Núñez de Herrera y Guzmán, d. 1430, and of  Blanca <a href="/wiki/House_of_Enr%C3%ADquez" title="House of Enríquez">Enríquez</a> de Mendoza). In 1455 when Gonzalo was two years old, his father died. His older brother, Alonso, inherited all of their father's estates, leaving Gonzalo to seek his own fortune. In 1467 Gonzalo was first attached to the household of <a href="/wiki/Alfonso,_Prince_of_Asturias_(1453%E2%80%931468)" title="Alfonso, Prince of Asturias (1453–1468)">Alfonso, Prince of Asturias</a>, the half-brother of King <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_Castile" title="Henry IV of Castile">Henry IV of Castile</a>. After Alfonso's death in 1468 Gonzalo devoted himself to Alfonso's sister, <a href="/wiki/Isabella_I_of_Castile" title="Isabella I of Castile">Isabella of Castile</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Purcell_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purcell-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When King Henry IV died in 1474 Isabella proclaimed herself successor queen, disputing the right of <a href="/wiki/Juana_la_Beltraneja" class="mw-redirect" title="Juana la Beltraneja">Juana la Beltraneja</a> (the king's 13-year-old daughter and her niece) to ascend the throne. During the ensuing <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Castilian_Succession" title="War of the Castilian Succession">civil war</a> between the followers of Isabella and Juana, there was also conflict with Portugal since King <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_V_of_Portugal" class="mw-redirect" title="Alfonso V of Portugal">Alfonso V of Portugal</a> sided with his niece Juana. Gonzalo fought for Isabella under <a href="/wiki/Alonso_de_C%C3%A1rdenas" title="Alonso de Cárdenas">Alonso de Cárdenas</a>, grand master of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Santiago" title="Order of Santiago">Order of Santiago</a>. In 1479 he fought in the final battle against the Portuguese leading 120 lancers. Cárdenas praised him for his service. When the war ended Isabella and her husband <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferdinand</a> were the rulers of Castile and Aragon.<sup id="cite_ref-Purcell_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purcell-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Tucker_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tucker-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reconquista">Reconquista</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reconquista">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Once the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">Catholic Monarchs</a> had consolidated their rule, they embarked in 1481 on a ten-year campaign to <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Granada" class="mw-redirect" title="Conquest of Granada">conquer Granada</a>, the last remaining Muslim stronghold on the Iberian peninsula. Córdoba was an active participant in the fighting and distinguished himself as a brave and competent military leader. He gained renown for participation in the sieges of several walled towns including Loja, Tajara, <a href="/wiki/Illora" class="mw-redirect" title="Illora">Illora</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Montefr%C3%ADo" title="Montefrío">Montefrío</a>. At Montefrío he was reported to be the first attacker over the walls. In 1492, Córdoba captured the city of Granada, bringing an end to the war. The skills of a military engineer and a <a href="/wiki/Guerilla_fighter" class="mw-redirect" title="Guerilla fighter">guerilla fighter</a> were equally useful. Because of his knowledge of Arabic and his familiarity with <a href="/wiki/Boabdil" class="mw-redirect" title="Boabdil">Boabdil</a>, Gonzalo was chosen as one of the officers to arrange the surrender.<sup id="cite_ref-Purcell_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purcell-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Tucker_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tucker-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GALE_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GALE-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Italian_campaigns"><span id="Italian_Campaigns"></span>Italian campaigns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Italian campaigns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gonzalo was an important military commander during the <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a>, holding command twice and earning the name "The Great Captain".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="First_Italian_War">First Italian War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: First Italian War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Italy_1494_AD.png" class="image"><img alt="Multicolored map of Italy, showing duchies and kingdoms" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Italy_1494_AD.png/220px-Italy_1494_AD.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="315" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Italy_1494_AD.png/330px-Italy_1494_AD.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Italy_1494_AD.png/440px-Italy_1494_AD.png 2x" data-file-width="1261" data-file-height="1805" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Italy_1494_AD.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Italy in 1494, when <a href="/wiki/Frederick_IV_of_Naples" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick IV of Naples">Frederick IV of Naples</a> took power as the second inheriting son of <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I_of_Naples" title="Ferdinand I of Naples">Ferdinand I of Naples</a></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Italian_Wars" title="Italian Wars">Italian Wars</a> began in 1494 when <a href="/wiki/Charles_VIII_of_France" title="Charles VIII of France">Charles VIII of France</a> marched into Italy with 25,000 men to make good his claim to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">Kingdom of Naples</a> ruled by  <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Naples" title="Ferdinand II of Naples">Ferdinand II</a>, a cousin to Ferdinand of Aragon. The French easily overwhelmed the Neapolitan defenses and on 12 May 1495 Charles had himself crowned Emperor of Naples. The Catholic Monarchs were anxious to reverse French success in Naples and selected Córdoba to lead an expeditionary force against Charles. Córdoba landed in Naples shortly after Charles' coronation with a force of about 5,000 infantry and 600 light cavalry. Fearful of being trapped in Italy, Charles installed <a href="/wiki/Gilbert,_Count_of_Montpensier" title="Gilbert, Count of Montpensier">Gilbert de Bourbon</a> as Viceroy of Naples and returned to France with about half of the French forces.<sup id="cite_ref-Tucker_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Tucker-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Rubin_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rubin-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Initially, Córdoba's light infantry and cavalry were no match against the heavily-armed French. A lack of training and poor coordination between Spanish and Italian forces compounded the problem. In their first major engagement on 28 June 1495, Córdoba was defeated at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Seminara" title="Battle of Seminara">Battle of Seminara</a> against French forces led by <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Stewart_d%27Aubigny" class="mw-redirect" title="Bernard Stewart d&#39;Aubigny">Bernard Stewart d'Aubigny</a>. After the defeat, Córdoba withdrew to implement a rigorous training program and reorganize his army.  The Spanish employed effective guerrilla tactics, striking quickly to disrupt French supply lines and avoiding large-scale battles. Gradually Córdoba regained a foothold in the country and then assaulted the French-occupied Italian cities. Within a year, Córdoba achieved a decisive victory at Atella, capturing the French viceroy and expelling the remaining 
French forces from Naples. He also recovered the Roman port of <a href="/wiki/Ostia_(Rome)" title="Ostia (Rome)">Ostia</a> and returned the captured territories to the Italians by 1498.<sup id="cite_ref-GALE_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GALE-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Military_restructuring">Military restructuring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Military restructuring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When Córdoba returned to Spain he drew on the lessons from the Italian campaign to restructure the Spanish forces and military strategy. In the open field, the loose formation and short swords of the Spanish infantry were unable to withstand a charge of heavy cavalry and infantry armed with pikes. To overcome this weakness, Córdoba introduced a new infantry formation armed with pikes and a heavy, shoulder-fired gun called an <a href="/wiki/Arquebus" title="Arquebus">arquebus</a>. To increase tactical flexibility he assigned different sections of his forces to specific roles, rather than using them as one general force. These new sections could maneuver more independently and act with greater flexibility.<sup id="cite_ref-GALE_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GALE-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Second_Italian_War">Second Italian War</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Second Italian War">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Louis_XII_of_France" title="Louis XII of France">Louis XII</a> succeeded Charles as king of France in 1498, he quickly declared his intention to re-invade Italy and once again seize Naples. To buy time, Spain negotiated the Treaty of Granada with France in 1500, agreeing to partition Naples between the two countries. Córdoba returned to Italy leading a large force on the pretext of joining with France and Venice to attack the Ottomans in the Ionian Sea. For a time Córdoba did fight the Turks, seizing the strongly held island of <a href="/wiki/Cephalonia" title="Cephalonia">Cephalonia</a> in December 1500 after a <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_the_Castle_of_Saint_George" title="Siege of the Castle of Saint George">two-month siege</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:El_gran_capitan_gonzalo_de_cordoba.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Bronze bust of man with long hair and a hat" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/El_gran_capitan_gonzalo_de_cordoba.jpg/220px-El_gran_capitan_gonzalo_de_cordoba.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="331" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/El_gran_capitan_gonzalo_de_cordoba.jpg/330px-El_gran_capitan_gonzalo_de_cordoba.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/El_gran_capitan_gonzalo_de_cordoba.jpg/440px-El_gran_capitan_gonzalo_de_cordoba.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3056" data-file-height="4592" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:El_gran_capitan_gonzalo_de_cordoba.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i>El Gran Capitán</i></div></div></div>
<p>Córdoba returned to Naples and after <a href="/wiki/Frederick_IV_of_Naples" class="mw-redirect" title="Frederick IV of Naples">Frederick IV</a> abdicated, the French and Spanish fought a guerilla war while negotiating the partition of the kingdom. Spain was outnumbered and besieged in <a href="/wiki/Barletta" title="Barletta">Barletta</a> by the French. Gonzalo refused to be drawn into a full-scale battle until he received sufficient reinforcements. 
</p><p>When his army was adequately reinforced, Córdoba engaged the French on 28 April 1503 at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cerignola" title="Battle of Cerignola">Battle of Cerignola</a> where 6,000 Spanish troops faced a French army of 10,000. Gonzalo formed his infantry into units called <i>coronelías</i> with pikemen tightly packed in the center and arquebusiers and swordsmen on the flanks. The French unsuccessfully attacked the front and were assailed by gunfire coming from the flanks. The French commander, the <a href="/wiki/Louis_d%27Armagnac,_Duke_of_Nemours" title="Louis d&#39;Armagnac, Duke of Nemours">Duke of Nemours</a>, was killed early in the battle. After withstanding two French charges, Córdoba went on the offensive and drove the French off the field. This was the first time in history that a battle had been won largely through the strength of firearms.<sup id="cite_ref-Lanning_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lanning-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Córdoba occupied the city of Naples and pushed the French forces back across the <a href="/wiki/Garigliano_River" class="mw-redirect" title="Garigliano River">Garigliano River</a>. Separated by the river, a stalemate ensued with neither side able to make progress. But Córdoba strung together a pontoon bridge and stole across the river on the night of December 29, 1503. The French, commanded by <a href="/wiki/Ludovico_II_of_Saluzzo" class="mw-redirect" title="Ludovico II of Saluzzo">Ludovico II of Saluzzo</a>, had assumed the rain-swollen river was impassable and were taken by complete surprise. Córdoba and his army decisively defeated the French with their formations of pikes and arquebuses. Córdoba continued to pursue the French and captured the Italian city of Gaeta in January 1504. Unable to mount a defense after these losses, the French were allowed to evacuate Italy by sea and forced to sign the Treaty of Blois in 1505, relinquishing their hold on Naples.<sup id="cite_ref-Lanning_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lanning-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GALE_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GALE-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Viceroy_of_Naples">Viceroy of Naples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Viceroy of Naples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Monumento_a_Isabel_la_Cat%C3%B3lica_(Madrid)_04a.jpg" class="image"><img alt="Outdoor statue of man in long cape holding a horse&#39;s reins" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Monumento_a_Isabel_la_Cat%C3%B3lica_%28Madrid%29_04a.jpg/160px-Monumento_a_Isabel_la_Cat%C3%B3lica_%28Madrid%29_04a.jpg" decoding="async" width="160" height="270" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Monumento_a_Isabel_la_Cat%C3%B3lica_%28Madrid%29_04a.jpg/240px-Monumento_a_Isabel_la_Cat%C3%B3lica_%28Madrid%29_04a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Monumento_a_Isabel_la_Cat%C3%B3lica_%28Madrid%29_04a.jpg/320px-Monumento_a_Isabel_la_Cat%C3%B3lica_%28Madrid%29_04a.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1415" data-file-height="2386" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Monumento_a_Isabel_la_Cat%C3%B3lica_(Madrid)_04a.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Statue of Gonzalo de Córdoba in <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">Madrid</a> (<a href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manuel_Oms_y_Canet" class="extiw" title="es:Manuel Oms y Canet">Manuel Oms</a>, 1883)</div></div></div>
<p>When the French were driven out of Naples, Gonzalo was made Duke of Terranova and appointed <a href="/wiki/Viceroy_of_Naples" class="mw-redirect" title="Viceroy of Naples">Viceroy of Naples</a> in 1504. Later that same year Queen <a href="/wiki/Isabel_I_of_Castile" class="mw-redirect" title="Isabel I of Castile">Isabel I of Castile</a> died, depriving him of his most ardent supporter. Isabel's death also effectively pushed her husband, <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">Ferdinand II of Aragon</a>, out of power temporarily in Castile and forced him to defend his interests in Aragon. Naples was an Aragonese kingdom but Gonzalo was a Castilian and widely popular. As a result, Ferdinand suspected his loyalty and also felt that Gonzalo spent too freely from the treasury. In 1507 Ferdinand traveled to Naples, removed him from office and ordered him to return to Spain with a promise that he would be installed as master of the <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Santiago" title="Order of Santiago">Order of Santiago</a>, a powerful and prestigious position.<sup id="cite_ref-Purcell_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purcell-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Although Gonzalo was awarded the additional title, Duke of Sessa, he never received the promised appointment to lead the Santiago military order.  Ferdinand continued to praise him but gave him nothing else to do; he eventually retired to one of his country estates. Gonzalo Fernández de Córdoba died of <a href="/wiki/Malaria" title="Malaria">malaria</a> on 2 December 1515 at his villa near Granada at age 62.<sup id="cite_ref-Purcell_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purcell-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage_and_family">Marriage and family</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Marriage and family">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Gonzalo Fernández de Córdoba first married in 1474 to his cousin María de Sotomayor; about a year later she died giving birth to a stillborn son. On 14 February 1489 he married María <a href="/wiki/House_of_Lara" title="House of Lara">Manrique de Lara</a> y Figueroa (also known as María Manrique de Lara y Espinosa, d. 1527)  from a powerful and wealthy noble family. His only surviving daughter, <a href="/wiki/Elvira_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba_y_Manrique" title="Elvira Fernández de Córdoba y Manrique">Elvira Fernández de Córdoba y Manrique</a>, would inherit all his titles upon his death in 1515.<sup id="cite_ref-Purcell_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purcell-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy"><span id="Renown"></span>Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gonzalo_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Granada_san_jeronimo_detalle.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Granada_san_jeronimo_detalle.jpg/220px-Granada_san_jeronimo_detalle.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Granada_san_jeronimo_detalle.jpg/330px-Granada_san_jeronimo_detalle.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8d/Granada_san_jeronimo_detalle.jpg/440px-Granada_san_jeronimo_detalle.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="525" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Granada_san_jeronimo_detalle.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Coat of arms on the wall of the monastery church of San Jerónimo in Granada.</div></div></div>
<p>The "Gran Capitán" was a pioneer of <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_warfare" title="Early modern warfare">modern warfare</a>.  He revolutionized 16th-century military strategy by integrating firearms into the Spanish infantry and directed the first battle in history won by <a href="/wiki/Gunpowder" title="Gunpowder">gunpowder</a> <a href="/wiki/Small_arms" class="mw-redirect" title="Small arms">small arms</a> (in this case, <a href="/wiki/Arquebus" title="Arquebus">arquebuses</a>),  the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cerignola" title="Battle of Cerignola">Battle of Cerignola</a> of 1503. 
He helped found the first modern standing army (the nearly invincible <a href="/wiki/Tercio" title="Tercio">Spanish infantry</a> which dominated European battlefields for most of the 16th and 17th centuries), and he pioneered <a href="/wiki/Combined_arms" title="Combined arms">combined arms</a> warfare by combining the use of infantry, cavalry and artillery with naval support.
</p><p>He left no sons, and was succeeded in his dukedoms by daughter <a href="/wiki/Elvira_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_C%C3%B3rdoba_y_Manrique" title="Elvira Fernández de Córdoba y Manrique">Elvira Fernández de Córdoba y Manrique</a>. 
His  burial place in  the <a href="/wiki/Monastery_of_Saint_Jerome_(Granada)" class="mw-redirect" title="Monastery of Saint Jerome (Granada)">Monastery of San Jerónimo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Granada" title="Province of Granada">Granada</a>, was built in <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_Architecture" class="mw-redirect" title="Renaissance Architecture">Renaissance style</a>. His remains were transferred there in 1552, together with some 700 war trophies (captured banners). His wife and daughter (died in 1527 and 1524, respectively) were also buried there, as were a number of other family members. 
</p><p>The tomb was desecrated by Napoleonic troops under the Corsican <a href="/wiki/Horace_Fran%C3%A7ois_Bastien_S%C3%A9bastiani_de_La_Porta" title="Horace François Bastien Sébastiani de La Porta">General Sebastiani</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">Peninsular War</a>, in 1810/11. Gonzalo's remains were exhumed and mutilated, and the 700 banners were burned. 
Stone from the tower was used to build the Puente Verde bridge over the <a href="/wiki/Genil" title="Genil">Genil</a>. The monastery was fully restored at the end of the 19th century.
</p>
