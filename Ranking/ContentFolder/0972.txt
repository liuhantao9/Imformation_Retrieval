<p><b>David John Wheeler</b> <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">FRS</a> (9 February 1927 – 13 December 2004)<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> was a <a href="/wiki/Computer_scientist" title="Computer scientist">computer scientist</a> and professor of computer science at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-microsoft_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-microsoft-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Wheeler_(computer_scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wheeler was born in <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a> and gained a scholarship at <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College, Cambridge</a> to read the <a href="/wiki/Cambridge_Mathematical_Tripos" class="mw-redirect" title="Cambridge Mathematical Tripos">Cambridge Mathematical Tripos</a>, graduating in 1948.<sup id="cite_ref-dblp_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dblp-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> He completed the world's first<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> PhD in computer science in 1951.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Wheeler_(computer_scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wheeler's contributions to the field included work on the <a href="/wiki/Electronic_delay_storage_automatic_calculator" class="mw-redirect" title="Electronic delay storage automatic calculator">Electronic delay storage automatic calculator</a> (EDSAC)<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Burrows%E2%80%93Wheeler_transform" title="Burrows–Wheeler transform">Burrows–Wheeler transform</a> (BWT). Along with <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Wilkes" title="Maurice Wilkes">Maurice Wilkes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Gill" title="Stanley Gill">Stanley Gill</a> he is credited with the invention of the <a href="/wiki/Subroutine" title="Subroutine">subroutine</a> (which they referred to as the <i>closed subroutine</i>), and gave the first explanation of how to design software libraries;<sup id="cite_ref-goto_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-goto-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> as a result, the <i>jump to subroutine</i> instruction was often called a <a href="/wiki/Wheeler_Jump" class="mw-redirect" title="Wheeler Jump">Wheeler Jump</a>. Wilkes published a paper in 1953 discussing relative <a href="/wiki/Addressing_mode" title="Addressing mode">addressing</a> to facilitate the use of subroutines.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He was responsible for the implementation of the <a href="/wiki/CAP_computer" title="CAP computer">CAP computer</a>, the first to be based on security capabilities. In <a href="/wiki/Cryptography" title="Cryptography">cryptography</a>, he was the designer of <a href="/wiki/WAKE_(cipher)" title="WAKE (cipher)">WAKE</a> and the co-designer of the <a href="/wiki/Tiny_Encryption_Algorithm" title="Tiny Encryption Algorithm">TEA</a> and <a href="/wiki/XTEA" title="XTEA">XTEA</a> encryption algorithms together with <a href="/wiki/Roger_Needham" title="Roger Needham">Roger Needham</a>. In 1950, along with Maurice Wilkes, he used EDSAC to solve a <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">differential equation</a> relating to <a href="/wiki/Allele_frequency" title="Allele frequency">gene frequencies</a> in a paper by <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Fisher" title="Ronald Fisher">Ronald Fisher</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> This represents the first use of a computer for a problem in the field of <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>.
</p><p>Wheeler married Joyce Blackler in August 1957, who herself used EDSAC for her own mathematical investigations as a research student from 1955. He became a <a href="/wiki/Fellow" title="Fellow">Fellow</a> of <a href="/wiki/Darwin_College,_Cambridge" title="Darwin College, Cambridge">Darwin College, Cambridge</a> in 1964 and formally retired in 1994, although he continued to be an active member of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a> <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge_Computer_Laboratory" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Cambridge Computer Laboratory">Computer Laboratory</a> until his death. In 1994 he was inducted as a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Computing_Machinery" title="Association for Computing Machinery">Association for Computing Machinery</a>. In 2003, he was named a <a href="/wiki/Computer_History_Museum" title="Computer History Museum">Computer History Museum</a> Fellow Award recipient "for his invention of the closed subroutine, and for his architectural contributions to ILLIAC, the Cambridge Ring, and computer testing."<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The Computer Laboratory at the University of Cambridge annually holds the "Wheeler Lecture", a series of distinguished lectures named after him.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Quotes">Quotes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Wheeler_(computer_scientist)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Quotes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Wheeler is often quoted as saying "All problems in computer science can be solved by another level of <a href="/wiki/Indirection" title="Indirection">indirection</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Another quotation attributed to him is "Compatibility means deliberately repeating other people's mistakes."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Another quote of him saying at the famous Cambridge Computer Lab Coffee Room - "Published Papers in the Shelves Collecting Dust" to Ph.D. students - signifying that research must have impact, and not about papers or number of publications.
</p>

<ul><li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://purl.umn.edu/107711">Oral history interview with David Wheeler, 14 May 1987</a>. <a href="/wiki/Charles_Babbage_Institute" title="Charles Babbage Institute">Charles Babbage Institute</a>, University of Minnesota.  Wheeler was a research student at the University Mathematical Laboratory at Cambridge from 1948–51, and a pioneer programmer on the <a href="/wiki/EDSAC" title="EDSAC">EDSAC</a> project. Wheeler discusses projects that were run on EDSAC, user-oriented programming methods, and the influence of EDSAC on the <a href="/wiki/ILLIAC" title="ILLIAC">ILLIAC</a>, the <a href="/wiki/ORDVAC" title="ORDVAC">ORDVAC</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/IBM_701" title="IBM 701">IBM 701</a>.  Wheeler also notes visits by <a href="/wiki/Douglas_Hartree" title="Douglas Hartree">Douglas Hartree</a>, Nelson Blackman (of ONR), <a href="/wiki/Peter_Naur" title="Peter Naur">Peter Naur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Adriaan_van_Wijngaarden" title="Adriaan van Wijngaarden">Aad van Wijngarden</a>, Arthur van der Poel, <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_L._Bauer" title="Friedrich L. Bauer">Friedrich Bauer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Louis_Couffignal" title="Louis Couffignal">Louis Couffignal</a>.</li>
<li><a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://purl.umn.edu/107335">Oral history interview with Gene H. Golub</a>. <a href="/wiki/Charles_Babbage_Institute" title="Charles Babbage Institute">Charles Babbage Institute</a>, University of Minnesota.  Golub discusses the construction of the <a href="/wiki/ILLIAC" title="ILLIAC">ILLIAC</a> computer, the work of Ralph Meager and David Wheeler on the ILLIAC design, British computer science, programming, and the early users of the ILLIAC at the University of Illinois.</li>
<li>In 2003, he was made a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Computer_History_Museum" title="Computer History Museum">Computer History Museum</a> "for his invention of the closed subroutine, his architectural contributions to the ILLIAC, the Cambridge Ring, and computer testing."</li></ul>
