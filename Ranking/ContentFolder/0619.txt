<p><b>Wakefield Westgate railway station</b> is a mainline railway station in <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, England. It is 10 miles (16&#160;km) south of <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> to the west of the city centre, on the <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Line" class="mw-redirect" title="Wakefield Line">Wakefield Line</a> and Leeds branch of the <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_Main_Line" title="East Coast Main Line">East Coast Main Line</a>.
</p><p>The first Westgate station opened in 1856 a few years after the town's first station, <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Kirkgate_railway_station" title="Wakefield Kirkgate railway station">Wakefield Kirkgate</a>. In 1867, the station was rebuilt on the opposite side of Westgate on the main line between <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> and <a href="/wiki/Doncaster" title="Doncaster">Doncaster</a>. <a href="/wiki/British_Rail" title="British Rail">British Rail</a> modernised the station in 1967 when large parts of the 19th-century station were demolished and replaced with austere but functional facilities. By the 21st century, there was pressure to modernise the station and between 2009 and 2013 the station was rebuilt and modernised as a result of regeneration efforts focused upon the wider area. On 3 February 2014, the rebuilt station was officially opened.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">Early history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wakefield_Westgate_railway_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>During 1856, shortly after the spur line from <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a>'s first station, <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Kirkgate_railway_station" title="Wakefield Kirkgate railway station">Wakefield Kirkgate</a> was built, the first Westgate station opened.<sup id="cite_ref-appear_13_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appear_13-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Its southern side was built for the <a href="/wiki/Bradford,_Wakefield_and_Leeds_Railway" title="Bradford, Wakefield and Leeds Railway">Bradford, Wakefield and Leeds Railway</a> (GNR) on part of the private estate belonging to wealthy cloth merchant John Milnes and his mid-18th century <a href="/wiki/Mansion" title="Mansion">mansion</a> was partially demolished and its remains were incorporated into the station.<sup id="cite_ref-WWS_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WWS-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The station was used for ten years before further developments necessitated its demolition and rebuilding. No traces remain of the original station as the site was levelled and premises for Wakefield School were built on the site. The school has since been demolished.<sup id="cite_ref-WWS_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WWS-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A new station opened on the opposite side of Westgate in 1867.  It was constructed for the GNR, the <a href="/wiki/Manchester,_Sheffield_and_Lincolnshire_Railway" title="Manchester, Sheffield and Lincolnshire Railway">Manchester, Sheffield and Lincolnshire</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Lancashire_and_Yorkshire_Railway" title="Lancashire and Yorkshire Railway">Lancashire and Yorkshire Railways</a> on the main line from <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> to <a href="/wiki/Doncaster" title="Doncaster">Doncaster</a> which approached Westgate from the north on an embankment before passing through the station and over the bridge in Westgate which is at the start of a 95-arch viaduct. The station was designed by Leeds engineer, J.B. Fraser.<sup id="cite_ref-WWS_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WWS-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  Built at a cost of £60,000, it was described by a local <a href="/wiki/Newspaper" title="Newspaper">newspaper</a> as "one of the most perfect stations in England – special care in designing the works having been taken…in order that every facility might be given for the easy and expeditious working of the goods and passenger traffic".<sup id="cite_ref-appear_13_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appear_13-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> A prominent feature was a <a href="/wiki/Turret" title="Turret">turreted</a> tower, which, as a result of lobbying by the Wakefield Tradesmen's Association, was converted into a four-faced <a href="/wiki/Clock_tower" title="Clock tower">clock tower</a> in June 1880. The clock's mechanism was designed by the <a href="/wiki/Horology" title="Horology">horologist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lawyer" title="Lawyer">lawyer</a> <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Beckett,_1st_Baron_Grimthorpe" title="Edmund Beckett, 1st Baron Grimthorpe">Sir Edmund Beckett</a> and made by <a href="/wiki/Potts_of_Leeds" title="Potts of Leeds">Potts of Leeds</a>.
</p><p>Starting in 1967,  <a href="/wiki/British_Rail" title="British Rail">British Rail</a> embarked on an extensive rebuild of the second station, resulting in the removal of the clock tower and most of the station buildings.<sup id="cite_ref-WWS_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WWS-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WWS_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WWS-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The station's decorative and elegant frontage and pavilion roof were demolished and replaced with an austere counterpart. The rebuilt station was "aesthetically inferior to its earlier incarnation, soon proved to be too cramped to cope with a rise in passenger numbers". It was poorly laid out with few opportunities for retail and other services.<sup id="cite_ref-appear_13_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appear_13-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Infilling the station forecourt to the level of the first-storey platform provided direct access to the Up platform level but there was no level access to the Down platform except via a <a href="/wiki/Level_crossings_in_the_United_Kingdom#Barrow_Crossing" title="Level crossings in the United Kingdom">barrow crossing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-appear_13_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appear_13-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Westgate became the main station serving Wakefield because of its location on the main line from Leeds to London. Until the mid-1960s, it had regular services to Bradford Exchange via <a href="/wiki/Batley_railway_station" title="Batley railway station">Batley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ossett" title="Ossett">Ossett</a> and via Morley Top and to <a href="/wiki/Castleford" title="Castleford">Castleford</a> via the Methley Joint Railway, but these services fell victim to the <a href="/wiki/Beeching_cuts" title="Beeching cuts">Beeching cuts</a> between 1964 and 1966.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regeneration">Regeneration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wakefield_Westgate_railway_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Regeneration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Improvements to Westgate Station were constrained by a lack of funding.<sup id="cite_ref-appear_13_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appear_13-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In early 2007 Network Rail announced that a £1.4&#160;million redevelopment scheme was planned for the station to take place by the end of 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The scheme was a part of the <i>Westgate Key Development Area</i> authorised by <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Council" title="Wakefield Council">Wakefield Council</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-WakefieldKDA_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WakefieldKDA-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The scheme involved constructing offices, leisure, small-scale retail, hotel, restaurants and housing on the site of an old dairy and disused railway goods yards. In 2009, work on the Merchant Gate development commenced and work on the first phase was completed by September 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Four years later,  Westgate Station was rebuilt at the northern end of the platforms and the former overflow car park.<sup id="cite_ref-appear_13_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appear_13-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Key aims of the project were nearly doubling station's retail facilities, an improved forecourt area, station management centre, staff offices, a customer information point, a first class waiting room and standard class waiting facilities  and the installation of new passenger information technologies and automated ticket barriers to reduce fare evasion.<sup id="cite_ref-appear_13_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appear_13-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Better pedestrian access to the <a href="/wiki/Multi-storey_car_park" class="mw-redirect" title="Multi-storey car park">multi-storey car park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Taxi" class="mw-redirect" title="Taxi">taxi</a> ranks were provided.<sup id="cite_ref-appear_13_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appear_13-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wakefield_Westgate_stn_p2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Wakefield_Westgate_stn_p2.jpg/220px-Wakefield_Westgate_stn_p2.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Wakefield_Westgate_stn_p2.jpg/330px-Wakefield_Westgate_stn_p2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Wakefield_Westgate_stn_p2.jpg/440px-Wakefield_Westgate_stn_p2.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="1728" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wakefield_Westgate_stn_p2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Platform 2 - For northbound trains</div></div></div>
<p>Few elements of the modernisation programme interfered with the operational railway except for the installation of additional canopies and the replacement <a href="/wiki/Footbridge" title="Footbridge">footbridge</a>. The old footbridge and some 1960's station buildings were removed.<sup id="cite_ref-appear_13_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appear_13-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Wakefield Westgate was  the first newly-built station on the East Coast Main Line in decades. The programme was an element of the third phase of <i>Merchant Gate</i> redevelopment scheme and promoted by its backers as being a key part of the area's renewal.<sup id="cite_ref-minopen_14_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-minopen_14-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Network Rail was appointed by <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_(train_operating_company)" title="East Coast (train operating company)">East Coast</a> as the principal contractor for the programme and the <a href="/wiki/Buckingham_Group" title="Buckingham Group">Buckingham Group</a> was the design-and-build subcontractor.<sup id="cite_ref-appear_13_1-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appear_13-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In January 2013, after planning permission was granted onsite preparations began<sup id="cite_ref-appear_13_1-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appear_13-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> and in March rebuilding commenced at a cost of £8.8 million, a large portion was provided via the Station Commercial Project Facility (SCPF), and 1 million each from the <i>Access for All</i> programme and the <i>English Cities Fund</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-appear_13_1-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appear_13-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> On 23 December 2013, the station opened to service and on 3 February 2014, it was officially opened by <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Transport" title="Secretary of State for Transport">Secretary of State for Transport</a> <a href="/wiki/Patrick_McLoughlin" title="Patrick McLoughlin">Patrick McLoughlin</a> and blessed by the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Wakefield" title="Bishop of Wakefield">Bishop of Wakefield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Platten" title="Stephen Platten">Stephen Platten</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-minopen_14_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-minopen_14-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In total, there are 6 tph to <a href="/wiki/Leeds_railway_station" title="Leeds railway station">Leeds</a> with additional peak services. All northbound services call at Leeds.
</p><p>The station is managed by <a href="/wiki/London_North_Eastern_Railway" title="London North Eastern Railway">London North Eastern Railway</a>, whose services run south from platform 1 to Doncaster and stations to <a href="/wiki/London_King%27s_Cross_railway_station" title="London King&#39;s Cross railway station">London King's Cross</a> and north from platform 2 to <a href="/wiki/Leeds_railway_station" title="Leeds railway station">Leeds</a>. A half-hourly weekday service from Wakefield to London takes approximately just over 2&#160;hours for the 175 miles (282&#160;km) journey.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-appear_13_1-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-appear_13-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/CrossCountry" title="CrossCountry">CrossCountry</a> operate train services north to <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_railway_station" title="Newcastle railway station">Newcastle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_Waverley_railway_station" title="Edinburgh Waverley railway station">Edinburgh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glasgow_Central_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Glasgow Central railway station">Glasgow</a> and services south to <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_New_Street_railway_station" title="Birmingham New Street railway station">Birmingham New Street</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exeter_St_Davids_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Exeter St Davids railway station">Exeter</a> and the <a href="/wiki/South_West_England" title="South West England">South West of England</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> These call every hour with some additional peak services.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Northern_(train_operating_company)" title="Northern (train operating company)">Northern</a> operate stopping services between Leeds and <a href="/wiki/Doncaster_railway_station" title="Doncaster railway station">Doncaster</a>, calling every hour on weekdays and Saturdays. An hourly stopping service also operates from <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Sheffield railway station">Sheffield</a> to Leeds via <a href="/wiki/Rotherham_Central_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Rotherham Central railway station">Rotherham</a>. Introduced at the May 2018 Timetable change, there is now another hourly service to Leeds. This train originates at <a href="/wiki/Knottingley_railway_station" title="Knottingley railway station">Knottingley</a> and runs via <a href="/wiki/Pontefract_Monkhill_railway_station" title="Pontefract Monkhill railway station">Pontefract Monkhill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wakefield_Kirkgate_railway_station" title="Wakefield Kirkgate railway station">Wakefield Kirkgate</a>. This gives a 3tph <a href="/wiki/Northern_(train_operating_company)" title="Northern (train operating company)">Northern</a> service to Leeds (of which, 2tph call at <a href="/wiki/Outwood_railway_station" title="Outwood railway station">Outwood</a>). 
</p><p><a href="/wiki/East_Midlands_Railway" title="East Midlands Railway">East Midlands Railway</a> services from Leeds to <a href="/wiki/St_Pancras_railway_station" title="St Pancras railway station">London St Pancras</a> via <a href="/wiki/Derby_railway_station" title="Derby railway station">Derby</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nottingham_railway_station" class="mw-redirect" title="Nottingham railway station">Nottingham</a> call at Westgate. They are three early morning departures from Leeds and up to four late afternoon/evening return trips from St Pancras.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> This is because the maintenance depot for former Midland Mainline HST <a href="/wiki/Power_car" title="Power car">power cars</a> is the Maintrain depot at <a href="/wiki/Neville_Hill_depot" class="mw-redirect" title="Neville Hill depot">Neville Hill</a> in Leeds. This provides a limited but useful service between West Yorkshire and the East Midlands. The former operator Midland Mainline had plans for a regular service between St Pancras and Leeds via the <a href="/wiki/Erewash_Valley" title="Erewash Valley">Erewash Valley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leicester_railway_station" title="Leicester railway station">Leicester</a> but these ambitions were ultimately rejected by the <a href="/wiki/Strategic_Rail_Authority" title="Strategic Rail Authority">Strategic Rail Authority</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">Facilities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wakefield_Westgate_railway_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Facilities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:New_Wakefield_Westgate_Railway_Station_-_Concourse_(geograph_3939108).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/New_Wakefield_Westgate_Railway_Station_-_Concourse_%28geograph_3939108%29.jpg/220px-New_Wakefield_Westgate_Railway_Station_-_Concourse_%28geograph_3939108%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/New_Wakefield_Westgate_Railway_Station_-_Concourse_%28geograph_3939108%29.jpg/330px-New_Wakefield_Westgate_Railway_Station_-_Concourse_%28geograph_3939108%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/New_Wakefield_Westgate_Railway_Station_-_Concourse_%28geograph_3939108%29.jpg/440px-New_Wakefield_Westgate_Railway_Station_-_Concourse_%28geograph_3939108%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:New_Wakefield_Westgate_Railway_Station_-_Concourse_(geograph_3939108).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The station concourse in 2014.</div></div></div>
<p>The station is manned and has an information kiosk, ticket office and self-service ticket machines, refreshments and a newsagent. Outside is a taxi rank, a <a href="/wiki/Cashpoint" class="mw-redirect" title="Cashpoint">cashpoint</a> and a bus stop. Train running information is via digital display screens, timetable posters and automatic announcements.  Lifts and a footbridge connect the platforms, so step-free access is available throughout the station.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">Culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wakefield_Westgate_railway_station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Between 1988 and 2009, a <a href="/wiki/Modern_art" title="Modern art">modern art</a> sculpture, titled 'A Light Wave', created by the <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>-based artist Charles Quick, was located on the wall behind the old bay platform on the northbound side of the station. The installation comprised a series of wooden planks laid up against a wall, in the form of waves, and illuminated from behind by a succession of lamps.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> The sculpture gradually fell into a state of disrepair, which prompted its removal.
</p><p>Wakefield Westgate appeared in the TV series <i><a href="/wiki/A_Touch_of_Frost_(TV_series)" class="mw-redirect" title="A Touch of Frost (TV series)">A Touch of Frost</a></i> as Denton Station.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
