<p>The <b>historic counties of England</b> are areas that were established for administration by the <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Normans</a>, in many cases based on earlier <a href="/wiki/Heptarchy" title="Heptarchy">kingdoms</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shire" title="Shire">shires</a> created by the <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxons" title="Anglo-Saxons">Anglo-Saxons</a> and others. They are alternatively known as <b>ancient counties</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-1891census_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1891census-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-youngsdef_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-youngsdef-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <b>traditional counties</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-guide_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guide-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> <b>former counties</b><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> or simply as <b>counties</b>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> In the centuries that followed their establishment, as well as their administrative function, the counties also helped define local culture and identity.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> This role continued even after the counties ceased to be used for administration after the creation of <a href="/wiki/Administrative_counties_of_England" title="Administrative counties of England">administrative counties</a> in 1889,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> which were themselves amended by further local government reforms in the years following.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike the partly self-governing <a href="/wiki/Ancient_borough" title="Ancient borough">boroughs</a> that covered urban areas, the counties of medieval England existed primarily as a means of enforcing central government power, enabling monarchs to exercise control over local areas through their chosen representatives – originally <a href="/wiki/High_sheriff" title="High sheriff">sheriffs</a> and later the <a href="/wiki/Lord-Lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord-Lieutenant">Lord-Lieutenants</a> – and their subordinate <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_peace" title="Justice of the peace">justices of the peace</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Counties were used initially for the <a href="/wiki/Administration_of_justice" title="Administration of justice">administration of justice</a>, collection of taxes and organisation of the military, and later for local government and electing parliamentary representation.<sup id="cite_ref-Hackwood_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hackwood-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bryne_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bryne-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> They continue to form the basis of <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_England" title="Local government in England">modern local government</a> in many parts of the country away from the main urban areas, although sometimes with considerably altered boundaries.<sup id="cite_ref-guide_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guide-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-aspects_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aspects-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hampton_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hampton-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name of a county often gives a clue to how it was formed, either as a division that took its name from a centre of administration, an ancient kingdom, or an area occupied by an ethnic group.<sup id="cite_ref-Hackwood_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hackwood-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The majority of English counties are in the first category, with the name formed by combining the central town with the suffix "-shire", for example <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>. Former kingdoms, which became <a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">earldoms</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">united England</a> did not feature this formulation; so for Kent, the former kingdom of the <a href="/wiki/Jutes" title="Jutes">Jutes</a>, "Kentshire" was not used. Counties ending in the suffix "-sex", the former Saxon kingdoms, are also in this category. Many of these names are formed from compass directions. The third category includes counties such as <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> and <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> where the name corresponds to the tribes who inhabited the area.<sup id="cite_ref-Hackwood_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hackwood-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a> is anomalous in terms of naming and origin, not falling into any of the three categories. Instead, it was a <a href="/wiki/Diocese" title="Diocese">diocese</a> that was turned into the <a href="/wiki/County_Palatine_of_Durham" title="County Palatine of Durham">County Palatine of Durham</a>, ruled by the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Durham" title="Bishop of Durham">Bishop of Durham</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hackwood_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hackwood-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>  The expected form would otherwise be "Durhamshire", but it was rarely used.
</p><p>There are customary abbreviations for many of the counties. In most cases, these consist of simple truncation, usually with an "s" at the end signifying "shire", such as "Berks" for <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a> or "Bucks" for <a href="/wiki/Buckinghamshire" title="Buckinghamshire">Buckinghamshire</a>. Some abbreviations are not obvious, such as "Salop" for <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a>, from the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">Norman</a>-derived word for its county town Shrewsbury; "Oxon" for <a href="/wiki/Oxfordshire" title="Oxfordshire">Oxfordshire</a>, from Latin <i>Oxonium</i> (referring to both the county and the city of Oxford); "Hants" for <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>; and "Northants" for <a href="/wiki/Northamptonshire" title="Northamptonshire">Northamptonshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hackwood_14-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hackwood-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Counties were often prefixed with "County of" in official contexts, such as "County of Kent". Those counties named after central towns lost the -"shire" suffix, for example Yorkshire would be known as "County of York". This usage was sometimes followed even where there was no town by that name, such as the "County of Berks". The "-shire" suffix was also appended for some counties, such as "Devonshire", "Dorsetshire" and "Somersetshire", despite their origin.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> There is still a <a href="/wiki/Duke_of_Devonshire" title="Duke of Devonshire">Duke of Devonshire</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> was first divided into administrative areas by the Romans, most likely following major geographical features such as rivers.<sup id="cite_ref-Hackwood_14-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hackwood-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Before their arrival there were distinct tribal areas, but they were in a constant state of flux as territory was gained and lost. After the demise of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">Roman Britain</a> around 410 these first divisions of land were generally abandoned, although traditional divisions taking the form of petty kingdoms such as <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Powys" title="Kingdom of Powys">Powys</a>,  <a href="/wiki/Dumnonia" title="Dumnonia">Dumnonia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Elmet" title="Elmet">Elmet</a>, remained in those areas which remained <a href="/wiki/Britons_(historical)" class="mw-redirect" title="Britons (historical)">British</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/South_West_England" title="South West England">south west England</a>. The areas that would later form the English counties started to take shape soon afterwards, with the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kent" title="Kingdom of Kent">Kingdom of Kent</a> founded by settlers around 445. In southern England more widely, <a href="/wiki/Shire" title="Shire">shires</a> emerged from earlier sub-kingdoms as part of the administrative structure of <a href="/wiki/Wessex" title="Wessex">Wessex</a>, which then imposed its system of shires, <a href="/wiki/Boroughs" class="mw-redirect" title="Boroughs">boroughs</a> (or <i><a href="/wiki/Burh" title="Burh">burhs</a></i>) and <i><a href="/wiki/Ealdormen" class="mw-redirect" title="Ealdormen">ealdormen</a></i> on <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">Mercia</a> after it came under West Saxon control during the 9th century.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>  Once the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">Kingdom of England</a> was united as a whole in 927 it became necessary to subdivide it for administrative convenience and to this end, <a href="/wiki/Earl" title="Earl">earldoms</a> were created out of the earlier kingdoms. The whole kingdom was divided into shires by the time of the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman conquest">Norman conquest</a>. <a href="/wiki/Robert_of_Gloucester_(historian)" title="Robert of Gloucester (historian)">Robert of Gloucester</a> accounts for thirty-five shires and <a href="/wiki/William_of_Malmesbury" title="William of Malmesbury">William of Malmesbury</a> thirty-two,<sup id="cite_ref-Hackwood_14-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hackwood-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Henry of Huntingdon, thirty-seven.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In most cases the counties or shires in medieval times were administered by a <a href="/wiki/Sheriff" title="Sheriff">sheriff</a> (derived from "shire-<a href="/wiki/Reeve_(England)" title="Reeve (England)">reeve</a>") on behalf of the monarch. After the Norman conquest the sheriff was replaced and the shires became counties, or "areas under the control of a count",<sup id="cite_ref-Hackwood_14-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hackwood-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> in the French manner.
</p><p>Although all of England was divided into shires by the time of the Norman conquest, some counties<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (August 2011)">which?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> were formed considerably later, up to the 16th century. Because of their differing origins the counties <a href="/wiki/List_of_counties_of_England_by_area_in_1831" title="List of counties of England by area in 1831">varied considerably in size</a>. The county boundaries were fairly static between the 16th century <a href="/wiki/Laws_in_Wales_Acts_1535-1542" class="mw-redirect" title="Laws in Wales Acts 1535-1542">Laws in Wales acts</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1888" title="Local Government Act 1888">Local Government Act 1888</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-vob_census_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vob_census-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Each shire was responsible for gathering taxes for the central government; for local defence; and for justice, through <a href="/wiki/Assize_courts" class="mw-redirect" title="Assize courts">assize courts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-winchester_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-winchester-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Southern_England">Southern England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Southern England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In southern England the counties were mostly subdivisions of the Kingdom of <a href="/wiki/Wessex" title="Wessex">Wessex</a>, and in many areas represented annexed, previously independent, kingdoms or other tribal territories.  <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a> derives from the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Kent" title="Kingdom of Kent">Kingdom of Kent</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sussex" title="Sussex">Sussex</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">Middlesex</a> come from the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Essex" title="Kingdom of Essex">East Saxons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Sussex" title="Kingdom of Sussex">South Saxons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Middle_Saxons" title="Middle Saxons">Middle Saxons</a>. Norfolk and Suffolk were subdivisions representing the "North Folk" and "South Folk" of the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_East_Anglia" title="Kingdom of East Anglia">Kingdom of East Anglia</a>. Only one county on the south coast of England now usually takes the suffix "-shire": <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>, named after the former town of "Hamwic" (sic), the site of which is now a part of the city of <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a>.  A "lost" Saxon county was <a href="/wiki/Winchcombeshire" title="Winchcombeshire">Winchcombeshire</a> which lasted from 1007 to 1017 before being incorporated into <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a> and <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a> derive their names from the <i>saete</i> or inhabitants of the areas around the towns of <a href="/wiki/Dorchester,_Dorset" title="Dorchester, Dorset">Dorchester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Somerton,_Somerset" title="Somerton, Somerset">Somerton</a> respectively; the names were first used by the Saxons in the 9th century.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a> were based on the pre-Saxon <a href="/wiki/Celtic_Britain" class="mw-redirect" title="Celtic Britain">Celtic</a> tribes known in Latin as the <a href="/wiki/Dumnonii" title="Dumnonii">Dumnonii</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cornovii_(Cornwall)" title="Cornovii (Cornwall)">Cornovii</a>, in the latter case with the suffix <i>wealas</i>, meaning foreigners, added by the Saxons.
</p>

<p>When Wessex annexed <a href="/wiki/Mercia" title="Mercia">Mercia</a> in the 10th century, it subdivided the area into various shires of roughly equal size and tax-raising potential or <a href="/wiki/Hide_(unit)" title="Hide (unit)">hidage</a>. These generally took the name of the main town (the <a href="/wiki/County_town" title="County town">county town</a>) of the county, along with "-shire". Examples of these include <a href="/wiki/Northamptonshire" title="Northamptonshire">Northamptonshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warwickshire" title="Warwickshire">Warwickshire</a>.  In some cases the original names have been worn down — for example, <a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a> was originally "Chestershire".<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the east Midlands, it is thought that county boundaries may represent a 9th-century division of the <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">Danelaw</a> between units of the Danish army.<sup id="cite_ref-winchester_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-winchester-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Rutland was an anomalous territory or <a href="/wiki/Soke_(legal)" title="Soke (legal)">soke</a>, associated with <a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire" title="Nottinghamshire">Nottinghamshire</a>, but it eventually became considered the smallest county. <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a> was the successor to the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Lindsey" title="Kingdom of Lindsey">Kingdom of Lindsey</a>, and took on the territories of <a href="/wiki/Kesteven" title="Kesteven">Kesteven</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holland,_Lincolnshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Holland, Lincolnshire">Holland</a> when <a href="/wiki/Stamford,_Lincolnshire" title="Stamford, Lincolnshire">Stamford</a> became the only <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">Danelaw</a> borough to fail to become a <a href="/wiki/County_town" title="County town">county town</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_England">Northern England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Northern England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Much of Northumbria was also shired, the best known of these counties being <a href="/wiki/Hallamshire" title="Hallamshire">Hallamshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cravenshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Cravenshire">Cravenshire</a>. The Normans did not use these divisions, and so they are not generally regarded as ancient counties. The huge county of Yorkshire was a successor to the Viking <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_York" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingdom of York">Kingdom of York</a>, and at the time of the Domesday Book in 1086 it was considered to include what was to become northern <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a>, as well as parts of <a href="/wiki/Cumberland" title="Cumberland">Cumberland</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Westmorland" title="Westmorland">Westmorland</a>.  Most of the later Cumberland and Westmorland were under <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scottish</a> rule until 1092. After the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman Conquest">Norman Conquest</a> in 1066 and the <a href="/wiki/Harrying_of_the_North" title="Harrying of the North">harrying of the North</a>, much of the North of England was left depopulated and was included in the returns for <a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a> in the Domesday Book.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>  However, there is some disagreement about the status of some of this land. The area in between the <a href="/wiki/River_Ribble" title="River Ribble">River Ribble</a> and the River <a href="/wiki/Mersey" class="mw-redirect" title="Mersey">Mersey</a>, referred to as <i lang="la" title="Latin language text">"Inter Ripam et Mersam"</i> in the Domesday Book,<sup id="cite_ref-sylvesterp14_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sylvesterp14-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> was included in the returns for Cheshire.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> Whether this meant that this land was actually part of Cheshire is however not clear.<sup id="cite_ref-sylvesterp14_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sylvesterp14-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-handt_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-handt-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-crosby_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-crosby-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>  The Northeast land that later became <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">County Durham</a> and <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>, was left unrecorded.
</p><p>Cumberland, Westmorland, Lancashire, County Durham and Northumberland were established as counties in the 12th century. Lancashire can be firmly dated to 1182.<sup id="cite_ref-George_D_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-George_D-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Part of the domain of the <a href="/wiki/Bishop_of_Durham" title="Bishop of Durham">Bishops of Durham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hexhamshire" title="Hexhamshire">Hexhamshire</a> was split off and was considered an independent county until 1572, when it became part of Northumberland.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Welsh_border">Welsh border</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Welsh border">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the time of the <a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a>, some parts of what later became Wales were accounted as parts of English counties; <a href="/wiki/Monmouth" title="Monmouth">Monmouth</a>, for example, was included in <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>  Additionally, the Domesday book included as part of Cheshire, areas that later became part of Wales, including the two hundreds of <a href="/wiki/Hundreds_of_Cheshire" title="Hundreds of Cheshire">Atiscross and Exestan</a>, and the southern part of Duddestan Hundred (as it was known as the time), which later became known as <a href="/wiki/Maelor_Saesneg" class="mw-redirect" title="Maelor Saesneg">Maelor Saesneg</a>, and (later still) "<a href="/wiki/Flintshire_(historic)" title="Flintshire (historic)">Flintshire</a> Detached".<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>  Parts of the <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Marches" title="Welsh Marches">March of Wales</a>, which after the <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest" class="mw-redirect" title="Norman conquest">Norman conquest</a> had been administered by <a href="/wiki/Marcher_Lord" title="Marcher Lord">Marcher Lords</a> largely independently of the English monarch, were incorporated into the English counties of Shropshire, Herefordshire and Gloucestershire in 1535.
</p><p>There was historic ambiguity as to the status of the county of <a href="/wiki/Monmouthshire_(historic)" title="Monmouthshire (historic)">Monmouthshire</a>. It was created out of ‘the said Country or Dominion of Wales’ by the <a href="/wiki/Laws_in_Wales_Acts_1535_and_1542" title="Laws in Wales Acts 1535 and 1542">Laws in Wales Act</a> of 1535 (though neither this Act nor any other specifically separates it from Wales and adds it to England) and later added to the <a href="/wiki/Oxford_circuit" class="mw-redirect" title="Oxford circuit">Oxford circuit</a> of the English Assizes. Laws since 1536 relating to Wales alone applied to "<a href="/wiki/Wales_and_Monmouthshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Wales and Monmouthshire">Wales and Monmouthshire</a>"<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> and for most purposes it was regarded as part of Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> It was listed among the English counties for parliamentary purposes until 1950 and for local government until 1974, with the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a> unambiguously including the area as part of Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Counties_corporate">Counties corporate</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Counties corporate">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/County_corporate" title="County corporate">County corporate</a></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Charter" title="Charter">charter</a> of <a href="/wiki/Henry_I_of_England" title="Henry I of England">Henry I</a> in about 1130 gave the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> its own <a href="/wiki/Sheriffs_of_the_City_of_London" title="Sheriffs of the City of London">Sheriff</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The Sheriff of London also had jurisdiction over the county of Middlesex, so that "London and Middlesex were from that time regarded as one from an administrative point of view",<sup id="cite_ref-vch_middlesex_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vch_middlesex-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> although they retained their separate identities.  This relationship continued until the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1888" title="Local Government Act 1888">Local Government Act 1888</a> created a new office of <a href="/wiki/High_Sheriff_of_Middlesex" title="High Sheriff of Middlesex">High Sheriff of Middlesex</a> appointed in the same manner as other English and Welsh counties, created the <a href="/wiki/County_of_London" title="County of London">County of London</a> with its own high sheriff, and restricted  the jurisdiction of the sheriffs of London to the City.<sup id="cite_ref-vch_middlesex_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vch_middlesex-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the Middle Ages a number of other large cities and towns were granted the status of self-governing counties separate from adjacent counties.  Such a county became known as a <a href="/wiki/County_corporate" title="County corporate">county corporate</a> or "county of itself".  For most practical purposes this separate status was replaced in the late 19th century when <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county boroughs</a> were introduced.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a> developed as a major port in the medieval period, straddling both sides of the <a href="/wiki/River_Avon_(Bristol)" class="mw-redirect" title="River Avon (Bristol)">River Avon</a> which formed the ancient boundary between <a href="/wiki/Gloucestershire" title="Gloucestershire">Gloucestershire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Somerset" title="Somerset">Somerset</a>.  In 1373 <a href="/wiki/Edward_III_of_England" title="Edward III of England">Edward III</a> decreed 
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>…that the said town of Bristol with its suburbs and their precinct, as the boundaries now exist, henceforward shall be separated and exempt in every way from the said counties of Gloucester and Somerset, on land and by water; that it shall be a county in itself and be called the county of Bristol for ever…<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p>Similar arrangements were later applied to <a href="/wiki/Norwich" title="Norwich">Norwich</a> (1404), <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a> (1447), <a href="/wiki/Canterbury" title="Canterbury">Canterbury</a> (1471), <a href="/wiki/Gloucester" title="Gloucester">Gloucester</a> (1483), <a href="/wiki/Exeter" title="Exeter">Exeter</a> (1537), and <a href="/wiki/Poole" title="Poole">Poole</a> (1571).<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Charters were granted constituting the boroughs or cities of <a href="/wiki/Lincoln,_Lincolnshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Lincoln, Lincolnshire">Lincoln</a> (1409), <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a> (1448), <a href="/wiki/Lichfield" title="Lichfield">Lichfield</a> (1556) and <a href="/wiki/Worcester" title="Worcester">Worcester</a> (1622) as counties.  The <a href="/wiki/County_of_the_City_of_Coventry" title="County of the City of Coventry">County of the City of Coventry</a> was separated from Warwickshire in 1451, and included an extensive area of countryside surrounding the city.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Charters granting separate county status to the cities and boroughs of <a href="/wiki/Chester" title="Chester">Chester</a> (1238/9), <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> (1396), <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a> (1400) and <a href="/wiki/Kingston-upon-Hull" class="mw-redirect" title="Kingston-upon-Hull">Kingston-upon-Hull</a> (with the surrounding area of <a href="/wiki/Hullshire" title="Hullshire">Hullshire</a>) (1440).  In 1551 <a href="/wiki/Berwick_upon_Tweed" class="mw-redirect" title="Berwick upon Tweed">Berwick upon Tweed</a>, on the border with <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>, was created a county corporate.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Detached_parts">Detached parts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Detached parts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:235px;"><a href="/wiki/File:DudleyTraditionalDetail.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/68/DudleyTraditionalDetail.jpg" decoding="async" width="233" height="331" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="233" data-file-height="331" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption">This (rather inaccurate) 1814 map shows <a href="/wiki/Dudley" title="Dudley">Dudley</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Exclave" class="mw-redirect" title="Exclave">detached part</a> of <a href="/wiki/Worcestershire" title="Worcestershire">Worcestershire</a> surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Staffordshire" title="Staffordshire">Staffordshire</a>. Note the detached portion of <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a> (the parish of <a href="/wiki/Halesowen" title="Halesowen">Halesowen</a>), just to the south-east and part of Staffordshire (<a href="/wiki/Broome,_Worcestershire" title="Broome, Worcestershire">Broome</a> and <a href="/wiki/Clent" title="Clent">Clent</a>) to the south-west as well.</div></div></div>
<p>The ancient counties had many anomalies, and many small <a href="/wiki/Exclave" class="mw-redirect" title="Exclave">exclaves</a>, where a parcel of land was politically part of one county despite not being physically connected to the rest of the county. The <a href="/wiki/Counties_(Detached_Parts)_Act_1844" title="Counties (Detached Parts) Act 1844">Counties (Detached Parts) Act 1844</a> modified the counties by abolishing the many <a href="/wiki/Enclave" class="mw-redirect" title="Enclave">enclaves</a> of counties within others, which had already been done for Parliamentary purposes by the Great Reform Act.
</p><p>Large exclaves affected by the 1844 Act included the County Durham exclaves of <a href="/wiki/Islandshire" title="Islandshire">Islandshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bedlingtonshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Bedlingtonshire">Bedlingtonshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norhamshire" title="Norhamshire">Norhamshire</a>, which were incorporated into <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Halesowen_(ancient_parish)" class="mw-redirect" title="Halesowen (ancient parish)">Halesowen</a> exclave of Shropshire, which was incorporated into <a href="/wiki/Worcestershire" title="Worcestershire">Worcestershire</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/List_of_county_exclaves_in_England_and_Wales_1844_-_1974" class="mw-redirect" title="List of county exclaves in England and Wales 1844 - 1974">Exclaves that the 1844 Act did not touch</a> include the part of <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a> around <a href="/wiki/Donisthorpe" title="Donisthorpe">Donisthorpe</a>, locally in <a href="/wiki/Leicestershire" title="Leicestershire">Leicestershire</a>; and most of the larger exclaves of Worcestershire, including the town of <a href="/wiki/Dudley" title="Dudley">Dudley</a>, which remained surrounded by <a href="/wiki/Staffordshire" title="Staffordshire">Staffordshire</a>. Additionally, the <a href="/wiki/Furness" title="Furness">Furness</a> portion of <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a> remained separated from the rest of Lancashire by a narrow strip of Westmorland — though it was accessible by way of the <a href="/wiki/Morecambe_Bay" title="Morecambe Bay">Morecambe Bay</a> <a href="/wiki/Mudflat" title="Mudflat">tidal flats</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1889">1889</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 1889">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When the first county councils were set up in 1889, they covered newly created entities known as <a href="/wiki/Administrative_counties_of_England" title="Administrative counties of England">administrative counties</a>. Several historic subdivisions with separate county administrations were also created administrative counties, particularly the separate ridings of <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>, the separate parts of Lincolnshire, and East and West Sussex.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1888" title="Local Government Act 1888">Local Government Act 1888</a> also contained wording to create both a new "administrative county" and "county" of <a href="/wiki/County_of_London" title="County of London">London</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> and to ensure that the <a href="/wiki/County_borough" title="County borough">county boroughs</a> which were created at the same time continued for non-administrative purposes to be part of the county which they had previously formed part of.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> These counties were to be used "for all purposes, whether sheriff, lieutenant, custos rotulorum, justices, militia, coroner, or other". The effect was that new county boroughs which were counties corporate retained their status as separate counties.  In retrospect, these "statutory" counties can be identified as the predecessors of the ceremonial counties of England.   The censuses of 1891, 1901 and 1911 provided figures for the "ancient counties".
</p><p>Several towns were historically divided between counties, including <a href="/wiki/Banbury" title="Banbury">Banbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Burton_upon_Trent" title="Burton upon Trent">Burton upon Trent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Newmarket,_Suffolk" title="Newmarket, Suffolk">Newmarket</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peterborough" title="Peterborough">Peterborough</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royston,_Hertfordshire" title="Royston, Hertfordshire">Royston</a>, Stamford, <a href="/wiki/Tamworth,_Staffordshire" title="Tamworth, Staffordshire">Tamworth</a>, <a href="/wiki/Todmorden" title="Todmorden">Todmorden</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warrington" title="Warrington">Warrington</a>. In Newmarket and Tamworth the county boundary ran right up the middle of the high street, and in Todmorden, the historically fractious border between Lancashire and Yorkshire (the <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a> known as Walsden Water) had had Todmorden Town Hall built right on top of it on a <a href="/wiki/Culvert" title="Culvert">culvert</a> tunnel, dividing the hall down the middle between the two counties &#8211; a division reflected in its architecture. The 1888 Act ensured that every urban sanitary district would be considered to be part of a single county. This principle was maintained in the 20th century: when county boroughs such as <a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reading,_Berkshire" title="Reading, Berkshire">Reading</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stockport" title="Stockport">Stockport</a> expanded into neighbouring counties, the area added became associated with another county.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1965_and_1974">1965 and 1974</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: 1965 and 1974">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Warwickshiremap_700.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Warwickshiremap_700.jpg/220px-Warwickshiremap_700.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="183" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Warwickshiremap_700.jpg/330px-Warwickshiremap_700.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Warwickshiremap_700.jpg/440px-Warwickshiremap_700.jpg 2x" data-file-width="700" data-file-height="583" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Warwickshiremap_700.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The ancient county boundaries of <a href="/wiki/Warwickshire" title="Warwickshire">Warwickshire</a> covered a larger area than the county in 1974 (in green).</div></div></div>
<p>On 1 April 1965 a number of changes came into effect. The new administrative area of <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a> was created, resulting in the abolition of the administrative counties of London and Middlesex, at the same time taking in areas from surrounding counties. On the same date the new counties of <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire_and_Isle_of_Ely" title="Cambridgeshire and Isle of Ely">Cambridgeshire and Isle of Ely</a> and of <a href="/wiki/Huntingdon_and_Peterborough" title="Huntingdon and Peterborough">Huntingdon and Peterborough</a> were formed by the merger of pairs of administrative counties. The new areas were also adopted for lieutenancy and shrievalty purposes.
</p><p>In 1974 a major local government reform took place under the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1972" title="Local Government Act 1972">Local Government Act 1972</a>. The Act abolished administrative counties and county boroughs, and divided England (except Greater London and the Isles of Scilly) into counties. These were of two types:  "metropolitan" and "non-metropolitan" counties.<sup id="cite_ref-guide_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guide-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> Apart from local government, the new counties were "substituted for counties of any other description" for judicial, shrievalty, lieutenancy and other purposes.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Several counties, such as <a href="/wiki/Cumberland" title="Cumberland">Cumberland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rutland" title="Rutland">Rutland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Westmorland" title="Westmorland">Westmorland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Worcestershire" title="Worcestershire">Worcestershire</a>, vanished from the administrative map, while new entities such as <a href="/wiki/Avon_(county)" title="Avon (county)">Avon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cleveland,_England" title="Cleveland, England">Cleveland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cumbria" title="Cumbria">Cumbria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Humberside" title="Humberside">Humberside</a> appeared, in addition to the six new <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_county" title="Metropolitan county">metropolitan counties</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-guide_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guide-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The built-up areas of conurbations tend to cross historic county boundaries freely.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> Examples here include <a href="/wiki/Bournemouth" title="Bournemouth">Bournemouth</a>–<a href="/wiki/Poole" title="Poole">Poole</a>–<a href="/wiki/Christchurch,_Dorset" title="Christchurch, Dorset">Christchurch</a> (<a href="/wiki/Dorset" title="Dorset">Dorset</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a>) <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester" title="Greater Manchester">Greater Manchester</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cheshire" title="Cheshire">Cheshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lancashire" title="Lancashire">Lancashire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>), <a href="/wiki/Merseyside" title="Merseyside">Merseyside</a> (Cheshire and Lancashire), <a href="/wiki/Teesside" title="Teesside">Teesside</a> (Yorkshire and County Durham), <a href="/wiki/South_Yorkshire" title="South Yorkshire">South Yorkshire</a> (Yorkshire, <a href="/wiki/Nottinghamshire" title="Nottinghamshire">Nottinghamshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a>), <a href="/wiki/Tyneside" title="Tyneside">Tyneside</a> (County Durham and <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>) and <a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_(county)" title="West Midlands (county)">West Midlands</a> (<a href="/wiki/Staffordshire" title="Staffordshire">Staffordshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Warwickshire" title="Warwickshire">Warwickshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Worcestershire" title="Worcestershire">Worcestershire</a>). <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a> itself straddles five ancient counties — <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hertfordshire" title="Hertfordshire">Hertfordshire</a>, Kent, <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">Middlesex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a> — and the London urban area sprawls into Buckinghamshire and Berkshire.  The Local Government Act 1972 sought generally to unite conurbations within a single county, while retaining the historic county boundaries as far as was practicable.<sup id="cite_ref-guide_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guide-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-hampton_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hampton-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postal_counties">Postal counties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Postal counties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Postal_counties_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Postal counties of the United Kingdom">Postal counties of the United Kingdom</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Former_postal_counties_of_England.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Former_postal_counties_of_England.svg/220px-Former_postal_counties_of_England.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Former_postal_counties_of_England.svg/330px-Former_postal_counties_of_England.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Former_postal_counties_of_England.svg/440px-Former_postal_counties_of_England.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="1108" data-file-height="1345" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Former_postal_counties_of_England.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Former postal counties of England from 1974 to 1996</div></div></div>
<p>In a period of financial crisis,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Mail" title="Royal Mail">Post Office</a> was able to alter many of its <a href="/wiki/Postal_counties_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Postal counties of the United Kingdom">postal counties</a> in accordance with the 1965 and 1974 reforms, but not all. The two major exceptions were <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester" title="Greater Manchester">Greater Manchester</a>. Greater London was not adopted in 1965, since, according to the Post Office at the time, it would have been too expensive to do so, while it gave as its reason for not adopting Greater Manchester the ambiguity of the name with the <a href="/wiki/M_postal_area" class="mw-redirect" title="M postal area">Manchester post town</a>. Perhaps as a result of this, the ancient counties appear not to have fallen completely out of use for locating places in Greater Manchester, along with areas of Greater London that are not part of the <a href="/wiki/London_postal_district" title="London postal district">London</a> post town. It is common for people to speak of "<a href="/wiki/Uxbridge" title="Uxbridge">Uxbridge</a>, Middlesex", "<a href="/wiki/Dagenham" title="Dagenham">Dagenham</a>, Essex" or "<a href="/wiki/Bromley" title="Bromley">Bromley</a>, Kent" (which are outside the London postal district), but much less so to speak of "<a href="/wiki/Brixton" title="Brixton">Brixton</a>, Surrey", "<a href="/wiki/Greenwich" title="Greenwich">Greenwich</a>, Kent", or "<a href="/wiki/West_Ham" title="West Ham">West Ham</a>, Essex" (which are inside it).
</p><p>In 1996, following further local government reform and the modernisation of its sorting equipment, the Royal Mail ceased to use counties at all in the direction of mail.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> Instead it now uses the outward code (first half) of the postcode. The former postal counties were removed in 2000 from its <a href="/wiki/Postcode_Address_File" title="Postcode Address File">Postcode Address File</a> database and included in an "alias file",<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> which is used to cross-reference details that may be added by users but are no longer required, such as former street names or historic, administrative and former postal counties.
</p><p>During a public consultation in 2009 <a href="/wiki/Postcomm" class="mw-redirect" title="Postcomm">Postcomm</a> found that many respondents objected to the use of counties in the alias file.  In May 2010 Postcomm announced that it was encouraging Royal Mail to discontinue the use of counties in its alias file at the earliest opportunity.  However because some existing software included the use of counties, Royal Mail was advised not to implement the change before 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="County_cricket">County cricket</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: County cricket">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The historic counties of England continue to be used as the basis for <a href="/wiki/County_cricket" title="County cricket">county cricket</a> teams<sup id="cite_ref-bbc_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> and the governance of <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a> in England through the <a href="/wiki/England_and_Wales_Cricket_Board#County_Boards" title="England and Wales Cricket Board">ECB County Boards</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> There are exceptions in that Rutland is integrated with Leicestershire; the Isle of Wight has its own board outside the Hampshire one; there is a board for the ceremonial county of Cumbria which is representative of both Cumberland and Westmorland. In addition, the ECB County Boards include one for the country of Wales.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Recognition_of_historic_county_boundaries">Recognition of historic county boundaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Recognition of historic county boundaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A review of the structure of local government in England by the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Commission_for_England_(1992)" title="Local Government Commission for England (1992)">Local Government Commission for England</a> led to the restoration of the <a href="/wiki/East_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="East Riding of Yorkshire">East Riding of Yorkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rutland" title="Rutland">Rutland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Worcestershire" title="Worcestershire">Worcestershire</a> as administrative areas in the 1990s; the abolition of Avon, Cleveland and Humberside within 25 years of their creation; and the restoration of the traditional borders between Somerset and Gloucestershire (except at <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>), County Durham and Yorkshire (towards the mouth of the <a href="/wiki/River_Tees" title="River Tees">River Tees</a>; not in <a href="/wiki/Teesdale" title="Teesdale">Teesdale</a>), and Yorkshire and Lincolnshire for ceremonial purposes in these areas. The case of Huntingdonshire was considered twice, but the Commission found that "there was no exceptional county allegiance to Huntingdonshire, as had been perceived in Rutland and Herefordshire".<sup id="cite_ref-cooksey_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cooksey-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Association_of_British_Counties" title="Association of British Counties">Association of British Counties</a>, and its regional affiliates, such as the <a href="/wiki/Friends_of_Real_Lancashire" title="Friends of Real Lancashire">Friends of Real Lancashire</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Ridings_Society" title="Yorkshire Ridings Society">Yorkshire Ridings Society</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> are pressure groups that assert that, on the basis that they were not formally abolished, the counties continue to exist with their ancient boundaries. These groups seek to promote greater public awareness of what they term "traditional counties" and broadly wish to see counties realigned to the historic boundaries.
</p><p>In 2013, <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Communities_and_Local_Government" class="mw-redirect" title="Secretary of State for Communities and Local Government">Secretary of State for Communities and Local Government</a> <a href="/wiki/Eric_Pickles" title="Eric Pickles">Eric Pickles</a> formally recognised and acknowledged the continued existence of England's 39 historic counties.<sup id="cite_ref-CLG-EP_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CLG-EP-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ind-EP_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ind-EP-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ABC-EP_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ABC-EP-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>  On 23 April 2014 a new initiative was announced to support the ‘tapestry’ of traditional English counties, including the removal of a restriction preventing the names of traditional counties being displayed on street and road signs.<sup id="cite_ref-CLG-BG_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CLG-BG-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>  In August 2014, the first road sign was erected to mark the boundary of the historic county of Yorkshire.<sup id="cite_ref-YP=YB_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YP=YB-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>  The Government is also publishing a new online interactive map  of England’s county boundaries.<sup id="cite_ref-CLG-BG_64-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CLG-BG-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup>  The Government has previously changed rules to allow local and county flags to be flown without planning permission, and supported the <a href="/wiki/Flag_Institute" title="Flag Institute">Flag Institute</a>  in encouraging a new wave of county and community flags to be designed and flown by local communities.  The flags of England's historic counties have been flown from Government offices in support of these identities. Of the 39 historic counties in England, 37 now have registered flags, many of them recently adopted.  In July 2019 the UK Government published official guidance on Celebrating the Historic Counties of England, stating that 
"the tapestry of England's historic counties is one of the bonds which draws our nation together". <sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sussex" title="Sussex">Sussex</a><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-special_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-special-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> both historic counties and long abandoned as units for administrative purposes, have continued to be widely recognised as cultural regions, significant in sport and used by many organisations as regional units.  These counties, and several others, have a <a href="/wiki/List_of_county_days_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="List of county days in England">county day</a> in which the culture and history of the historic county is celebrated; many of these county days were created in the 21st century.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Direct_action" title="Direct action">direct action</a> group, <a href="/wiki/CountyWatch" title="CountyWatch">CountyWatch</a>, was formed in 2004 to remove what its members consider to be wrongly placed county boundary signs that do not mark the historic or traditional county boundaries of England and <a href="/wiki/Historic_counties_of_Wales" title="Historic counties of Wales">Wales</a>. They have removed, resorted or erected a number of what they claim to be "wrongly sited" county boundary signs in various parts of England. For instance, in Lancashire 30 signs were removed.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_News4439228_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_News4439228-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> CountyWatch has been criticised for such actions by the councils that erected the signs:<sup id="cite_ref-YP1199795_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-YP1199795-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Lancashire_County_Council" title="Lancashire County Council">Lancashire County Council</a> pointed out that the taxpayers would have to pay for the signs to be re-erected.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_News4436170_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_News4436170-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The only political party with a manifesto commitment to restore the boundaries and political functions of all ancient counties, including <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">Middlesex</a> and <a href="/wiki/Monmouthshire_(historic)" title="Monmouthshire (historic)">Monmouthshire</a>, is the <a href="/wiki/English_Democrats_Party" class="mw-redirect" title="English Democrats Party">English Democrats Party</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-edcounties_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-edcounties-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Functions">Functions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Functions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>By the late Middle Ages the county was being used as the basis of a number of functions.<sup id="cite_ref-bryne_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bryne-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Administration_of_justice_and_law_enforcement">Administration of justice and law enforcement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Administration of justice and law enforcement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Assize_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="Assize Court">Assize Courts</a> used counties, or their major divisions, as a basis for their organisation.<sup id="cite_ref-aspects_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-aspects-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Justice_of_the_peace" title="Justice of the peace">Justices of the peace</a> originating in Norman times as Knights of the Peace,<sup id="cite_ref-elcock_73-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-elcock-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> were appointed in each county. At the head of the legal hierarchy were the <a href="/wiki/High_Sheriff" class="mw-redirect" title="High Sheriff">High Sheriff</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Custos_rotulorum" title="Custos rotulorum">Custos rotulorum</a> (keeper of the rolls) for each county.
</p><p>The justices had responsibility for maintaining county <a href="/wiki/Gaol" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaol">gaols</a> and <a href="/wiki/House_of_correction" title="House of correction">houses of correction</a>. During the 19th century <a href="/wiki/Prison_reform" title="Prison reform">penal reformers</a> campaigned against the often primitive conditions in gaols, and under the <a href="/wiki/Prison_Act_1877" title="Prison Act 1877">Prison Act 1877</a> they came under <a href="/wiki/Home_Office" title="Home Office">Home Office</a> control.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Until the 19th century law enforcement was mostly carried out at the <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parish</a> level. With an increasingly mobile population, however, the system became outdated. Following the successful establishment of the <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Police" class="mw-redirect" title="Metropolitan Police">Metropolitan Police</a> in London, the <a href="/wiki/County_Police_Act_1839" title="County Police Act 1839">County Police Act 1839</a> empowered justices of the peace to form county constabularies outside boroughs. The formation of county police forces was made compulsory by the <a href="/wiki/County_and_Borough_Police_Act_1856" title="County and Borough Police Act 1856">County and Borough Police Act 1856</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Defence">Defence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Defence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the 1540s the office of <a href="/wiki/Lord_Lieutenant" class="mw-redirect" title="Lord Lieutenant">Lord Lieutenant</a> was instituted. The lieutenants had a military role, previously exercised by the sheriffs, and were made responsible for raising and organising the <a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a> in each county. The lieutenancies were subsequently given responsibility for the <a href="/wiki/Volunteer_Force_(Great_Britain)" class="mw-redirect" title="Volunteer Force (Great Britain)">Volunteer Force</a>. In 1871 the lieutenants lost their positions as heads of the militia, and their office became largely ceremonial.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Cardwell_Reforms" title="Cardwell Reforms">Cardwell</a> and <a href="/wiki/Childers_Reforms" title="Childers Reforms">Childers Reforms</a> of the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a> linked the recruiting areas of infantry regiments to the counties.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parliamentary_representation">Parliamentary representation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Parliamentary representation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Each English county sent two <a href="/wiki/Knights_of_the_Shire" class="mw-redirect" title="Knights of the Shire">Knights of the Shire</a> to the <a href="/wiki/British_House_of_Commons" class="mw-redirect" title="British House of Commons">House of Commons</a> (in addition to the burgesses sent by boroughs). Yorkshire gained two members in 1821 when <a href="/wiki/Grampound_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Grampound (UK Parliament constituency)">Grampound</a> was disenfranchised. The <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_1832" title="Reform Act 1832">Great Reform Act</a> of 1832 reapportioned members throughout the counties, many of which were also split into parliamentary divisions. Constituencies based on the ancient county boundaries remained in use until 1918.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_government">Local government</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Local government">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From the 16th century onwards the county was increasingly used as a unit of <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">local government</a> as the justices of the peace took on various administrative functions known as "county business". This was transacted at the <a href="/wiki/Quarter_sessions" class="mw-redirect" title="Quarter sessions">quarter sessions</a>, summoned four times a year by the lord lieutenant. By the 19th century the county magistrates were exercising powers over the licensing of alehouses, the construction of bridges, prisons and asylums, the superintendence of main roads, public buildings and charitable institutions, and the regulation of weights and measures.<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> The justices were empowered to levy local taxes to support these activities, and in 1739 these were unified as a single "county rate", under the control of a county treasurer.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> In order to build and maintain roads and bridges, a salaried county surveyor was to be appointed.<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By the 1880s it was being suggested that it would be more efficient if a wider variety of functions were provided on a county-wide basis.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Subdivisions">Subdivisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Subdivisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Yorkshire_Ridings.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Yorkshire_Ridings.png/200px-Yorkshire_Ridings.png" decoding="async" width="200" height="169" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Yorkshire_Ridings.png/300px-Yorkshire_Ridings.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Yorkshire_Ridings.png/400px-Yorkshire_Ridings.png 2x" data-file-width="572" data-file-height="484" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Yorkshire_Ridings.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Yorkshire had three major subdivisions known as the <a href="/wiki/Riding_(division)" class="mw-redirect" title="Riding (division)">ridings</a> of Yorkshire: <div><ol><li><a href="/wiki/North_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="North Riding of Yorkshire">North Riding</a></li><li><a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding</a></li><li><a href="/wiki/East_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="East Riding of Yorkshire">East Riding</a></li></ol></div></div></div></div>
<p>Some of the counties had major subdivisions. Of these, the most significant were the divisions of Yorkshire: the <a href="/wiki/East_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="East Riding of Yorkshire">East Riding</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding</a>, <a href="/wiki/North_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="North Riding of Yorkshire">North Riding</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ainsty" title="Ainsty">ainsty</a> of York. Since Yorkshire is so big, its <a href="/wiki/Riding_(division)" class="mw-redirect" title="Riding (division)">ridings</a> became established as geographical terms quite apart from their original role as administrative divisions. The second largest county, <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>, was divided into three historic "<a href="/wiki/Parts_of_Lincolnshire" title="Parts of Lincolnshire">parts</a>": <a href="/wiki/Parts_of_Lindsey" class="mw-redirect" title="Parts of Lindsey">Parts of Lindsey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holland,_Lincolnshire" class="mw-redirect" title="Holland, Lincolnshire">Holland</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kesteven" title="Kesteven">Kesteven</a>. Other divisions include the special status of <a href="/wiki/Tower_division" title="Tower division">Tower Hamlets</a> within <a href="/wiki/Middlesex" title="Middlesex">Middlesex</a>, those of <a href="/wiki/Sussex" title="Sussex">Sussex</a> into <a href="/wiki/East_Sussex" title="East Sussex">East Sussex</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Sussex" title="West Sussex">West Sussex</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a> into <a href="/wiki/East_Suffolk_(county)" title="East Suffolk (county)">East Suffolk</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Suffolk_(county)" title="West Suffolk (county)">West Suffolk</a>, and, more informally and hence more vaguely, of Kent into <a href="/wiki/East_Kent" class="mw-redirect" title="East Kent">East Kent</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Kent" class="mw-redirect" title="West Kent">West Kent</a>.
</p><p>Several counties had <a href="/wiki/Liberty_(division)" title="Liberty (division)">liberties</a> or <a href="/wiki/Soke_(legal)" title="Soke (legal)">sokes</a> within them that were administered separately. <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a> had the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Ely" title="Isle of Ely">Isle of Ely</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Northamptonshire" title="Northamptonshire">Northamptonshire</a> had the <a href="/wiki/Soke_of_Peterborough" title="Soke of Peterborough">Soke of Peterborough</a>. Such divisions were used by such entities as the <a href="/wiki/Quarter_Sessions" class="mw-redirect" title="Quarter Sessions">Quarter Sessions</a> courts and were inherited by the later <a href="/wiki/Administrative_county" title="Administrative county">administrative county</a> areas under the control of <a href="/wiki/County_council" title="County council">county councils</a>.
</p><p>Most English counties were subdivided into smaller subdivisions called <a href="/wiki/Hundred_(country_subdivision)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hundred (country subdivision)">hundreds</a>. Nottinghamshire, Yorkshire and Lincolnshire were divided into wapentakes (a unit of Danish origin), while Durham, Northumberland, Cumberland and Westmorland were divided into <a href="/wiki/Wards_of_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Wards of the United Kingdom">wards</a>, areas originally organised for military purposes, each centred on a castle.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> Kent and Sussex had an intermediate level between their major subdivisions and their hundreds, known as <a href="/wiki/Lathe_(county_subdivision)" title="Lathe (county subdivision)">lathes</a> in Kent and <a href="/wiki/Rape_(county_subdivision)" title="Rape (county subdivision)">rapes</a> in Sussex. Hundreds or their equivalents were divided into <a href="/wiki/Tithing_(country_subdivision)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tithing (country subdivision)">tithings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parish" title="Parish">parishes</a> (the only class of these divisions still used administratively), which in turn were divided into <a href="/wiki/Township_(England)" title="Township (England)">townships</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manorialism" title="Manorialism">manors</a>. In the 17th century the <a href="/wiki/Ossulstone" title="Ossulstone">Ossulstone</a> hundred of Middlesex was further divided into four divisions, which replaced the functions of the hundred. The borough and parish were the principal providers of local services throughout England until the creation of ad-hoc boards and, later, local government districts.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_counties">List of counties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Historic_counties_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: List of counties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the time of the <a href="/wiki/2009_structural_changes_to_local_government_in_England" title="2009 structural changes to local government in England">2009 structural changes to local government in England</a>, the ancient counties continue to form, with considerably altered boundaries, many of the <a href="/wiki/Ceremonial_counties_of_England" title="Ceremonial counties of England">ceremonial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Non-metropolitan_county" title="Non-metropolitan county">non-metropolitan counties</a> in England. Some ancient counties have their names preserved in multiple contemporary counties, such as Yorkshire in <a href="/wiki/North_Yorkshire" title="North Yorkshire">North Yorkshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a> or now correspond to another type of subdivision, such as the <a href="/wiki/Huntingdonshire" title="Huntingdonshire">Huntingdonshire</a> district. In some areas ancient counties have been abandoned for local government use and then later revived. The ancient counties as they existed in the 19th century were as follows:

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Watsonian_vice-counties" class="mw-redirect" title="Watsonian vice-counties">vice counties</a>, used for biological recording since 1852, are largely based on historic county boundaries. They ignore all exclaves and are modified by subdividing large counties and merging smaller areas into neighbouring counties; such as Rutland with Leicestershire and Furness with Westmorland. The static boundaries make <a href="/wiki/Longitudinal_study" title="Longitudinal study">longitudinal study</a> of biodiversity easier. They also cover the rest of Great Britain and Ireland.
</p><p>The historic counties of England are included in the <a href="/wiki/Index_of_Place_Names" title="Index of Place Names">Index of Place Names</a> (IPN) published by the <a href="/wiki/Office_for_National_Statistics" title="Office for National Statistics">Office for National Statistics</a>. Each "place" included in the IPN is related to the historic county it lies within, as well as to a set of administrative areas.
</p>
