	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Light cruiser</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p>A <b>light cruiser</b> is a type of small- or medium-sized <a href="/wiki/Warship" title="Warship">warship</a>. The term is a shortening of the phrase "light <a href="/wiki/Armored_cruiser" title="Armored cruiser">armored cruiser</a>", describing a small ship that carried armor in the same way as an armored cruiser: a protective belt and deck. Prior to this smaller cruisers had been of the <a href="/wiki/Protected_cruiser" title="Protected cruiser">protected cruiser</a> model, possessing armored decks only. While lighter and smaller than other contemporary ships they were still true cruisers, retaining the extended radius of action and self-sufficiency to act independently across the world. Through their history they served in a variety of roles, primarily as convoy escorts and destroyer command ships, but also as scouts and fleet support vessels for battle fleets.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_development">Origins and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light_cruiser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HMS_Mercury_(1878).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/HMS_Mercury_%281878%29.jpg/220px-HMS_Mercury_%281878%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="112" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/HMS_Mercury_%281878%29.jpg/330px-HMS_Mercury_%281878%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/HMS_Mercury_%281878%29.jpg/440px-HMS_Mercury_%281878%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="673" data-file-height="343" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HMS_Mercury_(1878).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>HMS <i>Mercury</i></div></div></div>
<p>The first small steam-powered cruisers were built for the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Royal_Navy" title="Royal Navy">Royal Navy</a> with <a href="/wiki/HMS_Mercury_(1878)" title="HMS Mercury (1878)">HMS <i>Mercury</i></a> launched in 1878.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Such second and third class protected cruisers evolved, gradually becoming faster, better armed and better protected. Germany took a lead in small cruiser design in the 1890s, building a class of fast cruisers—the <a href="/wiki/Gazelle-class_cruiser" title="Gazelle-class cruiser"><i>Gazelle</i>&#x20;class</a>—copied by other nations. Such vessels were powered by coal-fired boilers and <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">reciprocating steam engines</a> and relied in part on the arrangement of coal bunkers for their protection. The adoption of oil-fired <a href="/wiki/Water-tube_boiler" title="Water-tube boiler">water-tube boilers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">steam turbine</a> engines meant that older small cruisers rapidly became obsolete. Furthermore, new construction could not rely on the protection of coal bunkers and would therefore have to adopt some form of side armoring. The British <i>Bristol</i> group of <a href="/wiki/Town-class_cruiser_(1910)" title="Town-class cruiser (1910)"><i>Town</i>-class</a> cruisers (1909) were a departure from previous designs; with turbine propulsion, mixed coal and oil firing and a 2-inch protective armoured belt as well as deck. Thus, by definition, they were armoured cruisers, despite displacing only 4,800&#160;tons; the light armored cruiser had arrived. The first true modern light cruisers were the <a href="/wiki/Arethusa-class_cruiser_(1913)" title="Arethusa-class cruiser (1913)"><i>Arethusa</i>&#x20;class</a> (1911) which had all oil-firing and used lightweight <a href="/wiki/Destroyer" title="Destroyer">destroyer</a>-type machinery to make 29 knots (54&#160;km/h).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light_cruiser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_I">World War I</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light_cruiser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: World War I">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HMS_Gloucester_at_anchor_at_Brindisi,_Italy,_1917_-_IWM_SP_459.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/HMS_Gloucester_at_anchor_at_Brindisi%2C_Italy%2C_1917_-_IWM_SP_459.jpg/220px-HMS_Gloucester_at_anchor_at_Brindisi%2C_Italy%2C_1917_-_IWM_SP_459.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="134" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/HMS_Gloucester_at_anchor_at_Brindisi%2C_Italy%2C_1917_-_IWM_SP_459.jpg/330px-HMS_Gloucester_at_anchor_at_Brindisi%2C_Italy%2C_1917_-_IWM_SP_459.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/HMS_Gloucester_at_anchor_at_Brindisi%2C_Italy%2C_1917_-_IWM_SP_459.jpg/440px-HMS_Gloucester_at_anchor_at_Brindisi%2C_Italy%2C_1917_-_IWM_SP_459.jpg 2x" data-file-width="800" data-file-height="486" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HMS_Gloucester_at_anchor_at_Brindisi,_Italy,_1917_-_IWM_SP_459.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>HMS <i>Gloucester</i>, one of the Town class, in 1917</div></div></div>
<p>By <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">British</a> light cruisers often had either two 6-inch (152&#160;mm) and perhaps eight 4-inch (102&#160;mm) <a href="/wiki/Naval_gun" class="mw-redirect" title="Naval gun">guns</a>, or a uniform armament of 6-inch guns on a ship of around 5,000 tons, while German light cruisers progressed during the war from 4.1-inch (104&#160;mm) to 5.9-inch (150&#160;mm) guns. Cruiser construction in Britain continued uninterrupted until Admiral <a href="/wiki/John_Fisher,_1st_Baron_Fisher" title="John Fisher, 1st Baron Fisher">"Jacky" Fisher</a>'s appointment as <a href="/wiki/First_Sea_Lord" title="First Sea Lord">First Sea Lord</a> in 1904. Due in part to the desire to curtail excess expenditures in light of the increasing cost of keeping up with German naval production and in part because he felt the type to be outdated, Fisher authorized few new cruisers and scrapped 70 older ones. Fisher's belief that <a href="/wiki/Battlecruiser" title="Battlecruiser">battlecruisers</a> would take the place of light cruisers to protect <a href="/wiki/Cargo_ship" title="Cargo ship">commercial shipping</a> soon proved impractical, as their high construction cost precluded their availability in sufficient numbers to do so, and destroyers were too small for scouting duties. The group of 21 <a href="/wiki/Town-class_cruiser_(1910)" title="Town-class cruiser (1910)"><i>Town</i>-class</a> cruisers begun in 1910 proved excellent in scouting in all types of weather and could carry enough fuel and ammunition to guard the <a href="/wiki/Shipping_lane" class="mw-redirect" title="Shipping lane">shipping lanes</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Arethusa-class_cruiser_(1913)" title="Arethusa-class cruiser (1913)"><i>Arethusa</i>&#x20;class</a>, launched three years later, was also successful. British designers continued enlarging and refining subsequent cruiser designs throughout the war.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:SMS_Bremen_1907.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/SMS_Bremen_1907.jpg/220px-SMS_Bremen_1907.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="142" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/SMS_Bremen_1907.jpg/330px-SMS_Bremen_1907.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/SMS_Bremen_1907.jpg/440px-SMS_Bremen_1907.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4367" data-file-height="2827" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:SMS_Bremen_1907.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>SMS <i>Bremen</i></div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/German_Empire" title="German Empire">Germans</a> built a number of light cruisers in the belief that they were good multi-purpose vessels. Unlike the British, who built both long-range cruisers like the Town class for commerce protection and short-range "scout" cruisers for fleet support, the Germans built a single series of light cruisers for both functions. Compared to the British "scout" type the German ships were bigger, slower and less manoeuvrable but, through a successive series of classes, improved consistently in seagoing qualities. However, the Germans were very late in adapting 5.9-inch guns (not doing so until the <a href="/wiki/Pillau-class_cruiser" title="Pillau-class cruiser"><i>Pillau</i>&#x20;class</a> of 1913); <a href="/wiki/Grand_Admiral" class="mw-redirect" title="Grand Admiral">Grand Admiral</a> <a href="/wiki/Alfred_von_Tirpitz" title="Alfred von Tirpitz">Alfred von Tirpitz</a>'s recalcitrance over the issue overrode the desires of others in the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_German_Navy" title="Imperial German Navy">German Navy</a>. For about a three-year period after the British <i>Weymouth</i> class of the Town series, completed with a uniform armament of 6-inch guns, and before the German <i>Pillau</i> class, German light cruisers (such as the <a href="/wiki/Magdeburg-class_cruiser" title="Magdeburg-class cruiser"><i>Magdeburg</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe-class_cruiser" title="Karlsruhe-class cruiser"><i>Karlsruhe</i>-class&#x20;cruisers</a>) were faster but maintained a lighter 104&#160;mm main armament compared to their British Town-class counterparts. With the <i>Pillau</i> and <a href="/wiki/Wiesbaden-class_cruiser" title="Wiesbaden-class cruiser"><i>Wiesbaden</i>-class</a> cruisers the Germans followed the British example of heavier guns.
</p><p>Earlier German light cruisers were in competition with a series of British <a href="/wiki/Scout_cruiser" title="Scout cruiser">scout cruisers</a> which had a higher speed of 25 knots, but smaller 3-inch <a href="/wiki/12_pounder_12_cwt_naval_gun" class="mw-redirect" title="12 pounder 12 cwt naval gun">12 pounder guns</a> or 4-inch guns. The Germans completed the last two of their <a href="/wiki/Bremen-class_cruiser" title="Bremen-class cruiser"><i>Bremen</i>-class</a> cruisers in 1906 and 1907 and followed them up with four <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg-class_cruiser_(1905)" title="Königsberg-class cruiser (1905)"><i>Königsberg</i>-class</a> and two <a href="/wiki/Dresden-class_cruiser" title="Dresden-class cruiser"><i>Dresden</i>-class&#x20;cruisers</a> between 1905 and 1908. These last two classes, larger and faster than the <i>Bremen</i>s, were armed the same (ten 4.1-inch guns) and carried less deck armor. Other major powers concentrated on <a href="/wiki/Battleship" title="Battleship">battleship</a> construction and built few cruisers.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy" title="Kingdom of Italy">Italy</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a> each built only a handful of scout cruisers while <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Japan">Japan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> added a few examples based on British designs; <a href="/wiki/French_Third_Republic" title="French Third Republic">France</a> built none at all.
</p><p>During World War I, the Germans <a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg-class_cruiser_(1915)" title="Königsberg-class cruiser (1915)">continued building larger cruisers</a> with 150&#160;mm guns while the British <i>Arethusa</i> class and early <a href="/wiki/C-class_cruiser" title="C-class cruiser">C-class&#x20;cruisers</a> reverted to an emphasis on superior speed with a more lightly-armed design for fleet support.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Between_the_wars">Between the wars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light_cruiser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Between the wars">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:USS_Raleigh_(CL-7)_off_the_Mare_Island_Naval_Shipyard_on_6_July_1942_(19-N-30916).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/USS_Raleigh_%28CL-7%29_off_the_Mare_Island_Naval_Shipyard_on_6_July_1942_%2819-N-30916%29.jpg/220px-USS_Raleigh_%28CL-7%29_off_the_Mare_Island_Naval_Shipyard_on_6_July_1942_%2819-N-30916%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="141" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/USS_Raleigh_%28CL-7%29_off_the_Mare_Island_Naval_Shipyard_on_6_July_1942_%2819-N-30916%29.jpg/330px-USS_Raleigh_%28CL-7%29_off_the_Mare_Island_Naval_Shipyard_on_6_July_1942_%2819-N-30916%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/USS_Raleigh_%28CL-7%29_off_the_Mare_Island_Naval_Shipyard_on_6_July_1942_%2819-N-30916%29.jpg/440px-USS_Raleigh_%28CL-7%29_off_the_Mare_Island_Naval_Shipyard_on_6_July_1942_%2819-N-30916%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="710" data-file-height="455" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:USS_Raleigh_(CL-7)_off_the_Mare_Island_Naval_Shipyard_on_6_July_1942_(19-N-30916).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>USS <i>Raleigh</i>, an <i>Omaha</i>-class cruiser, in 1942. <div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:ARA_General_Belgrano_underway.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/ARA_General_Belgrano_underway.jpg/220px-ARA_General_Belgrano_underway.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="155" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/ARA_General_Belgrano_underway.jpg/330px-ARA_General_Belgrano_underway.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/ARA_General_Belgrano_underway.jpg/440px-ARA_General_Belgrano_underway.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1033" data-file-height="730" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:ARA_General_Belgrano_underway.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Argentine cruiser <a href="/wiki/ARA_General_Belgrano" title="ARA General Belgrano">ARA&#160;<i>General Belgrano</i></a></div></div></div>
<p>The United States resumed building light cruisers in 1918, largely because the ships it then had in service had become obsolete. The first of these, the ten <a href="/wiki/Omaha-class_cruiser" title="Omaha-class cruiser"><i>Omaha</i>-class</a> ships, displaced 7,050 tons and were armed with twelve 6-inch (152&#160;mm) guns. Eight of these guns were mounted in double-story <a href="/wiki/Casemate" title="Casemate">casemates</a> at the bow and stern, a reflection of the US prewar preference for heavy end-on fire. Fast and maneuverable, they were well-liked as seaboats despite being very wet in rough weather.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term light cruiser was given a definition by the <a href="/wiki/London_Naval_Treaty" title="London Naval Treaty">London Naval Treaty</a> of 1930. Light cruisers were defined as cruisers having guns of 6.1-inch (155&#160;mm) or smaller, with <a href="/wiki/Heavy_cruiser" title="Heavy cruiser">heavy cruisers</a> defined as cruisers having guns of up to 8-inch (203&#160;mm). In both cases, the ships could not be greater than 10,000 tons.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:USS_Brooklyn_(CL-40)_in_the_Hudson_River,_in_1939_(80-G-1023215).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/USS_Brooklyn_%28CL-40%29_in_the_Hudson_River%2C_in_1939_%2880-G-1023215%29.jpg/220px-USS_Brooklyn_%28CL-40%29_in_the_Hudson_River%2C_in_1939_%2880-G-1023215%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/USS_Brooklyn_%28CL-40%29_in_the_Hudson_River%2C_in_1939_%2880-G-1023215%29.jpg/330px-USS_Brooklyn_%28CL-40%29_in_the_Hudson_River%2C_in_1939_%2880-G-1023215%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/USS_Brooklyn_%28CL-40%29_in_the_Hudson_River%2C_in_1939_%2880-G-1023215%29.jpg/440px-USS_Brooklyn_%28CL-40%29_in_the_Hudson_River%2C_in_1939_%2880-G-1023215%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="740" data-file-height="576" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:USS_Brooklyn_(CL-40)_in_the_Hudson_River,_in_1939_(80-G-1023215).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/USS_Brooklyn_(CL-40)" title="USS Brooklyn (CL-40)">USS&#160;<i>Brooklyn</i></a></div></div></div>
<p>After 1930, most naval powers concentrated on building light cruisers since they had already built up to the maximum limitations for heavy cruisers allowed under the Washington treaty. Japan laid down its four <a href="/wiki/Mogami-class_cruiser" title="Mogami-class cruiser"><i>Mogami</i>-class&#x20;cruisers</a> between 1931 and 1934.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The political climate from 1936 to 1939 gave the renewed building of light cruisers an added urgency. The British built 11 during this period, which culminated in the two <a href="/wiki/Town-class_cruiser_(1936)" title="Town-class cruiser (1936)">Town-class</a> ships, armed with 12 6-inch (152&#160;mm) guns. The new ships were larger and better armored than other British treaty cruisers, with a 4.5-inch (114&#160;mm) belt in the Towns and were capable of 32.5 knots, but for the most part tried to stay within past treaty limitations. The US also attempted to follow treaty limitations as it completed seven of its nine <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn-class_cruiser" title="Brooklyn-class cruiser"><i>Brooklyn</i>-class&#x20;cruisers</a> between 1938 and September 1939. These ships were an answer to Japan's <i>Mogami</i>s and were an indication of rising tensions in the Pacific theater. Japan, now considering itself under no restrictions, began rearming its <i>Mogami</i>s with 10 8-inch (203&#160;mm) guns.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="World_War_II">World War II</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light_cruiser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: World War II">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_cruisers_of_the_Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="List of cruisers of the Second World War">List of cruisers of the Second World War</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:USS_Atlanta_(CL-51)_steaming_at_high_speed,_circa_in_November_1941_(NH_57455).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/USS_Atlanta_%28CL-51%29_steaming_at_high_speed%2C_circa_in_November_1941_%28NH_57455%29.jpg/220px-USS_Atlanta_%28CL-51%29_steaming_at_high_speed%2C_circa_in_November_1941_%28NH_57455%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="164" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/USS_Atlanta_%28CL-51%29_steaming_at_high_speed%2C_circa_in_November_1941_%28NH_57455%29.jpg/330px-USS_Atlanta_%28CL-51%29_steaming_at_high_speed%2C_circa_in_November_1941_%28NH_57455%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/USS_Atlanta_%28CL-51%29_steaming_at_high_speed%2C_circa_in_November_1941_%28NH_57455%29.jpg/440px-USS_Atlanta_%28CL-51%29_steaming_at_high_speed%2C_circa_in_November_1941_%28NH_57455%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5173" data-file-height="3858" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:USS_Atlanta_(CL-51)_steaming_at_high_speed,_circa_in_November_1941_(NH_57455).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/USS_Atlanta_(CL-51)" title="USS Atlanta (CL-51)">USS&#160;<i>Atlanta</i></a></div></div></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, light cruisers had guns ranging from 5&#160;inch (127&#160;mm), as seen in the US <a href="/wiki/Atlanta-class_cruiser" title="Atlanta-class cruiser"><i>Atlanta</i>-class</a> and British <a href="/wiki/Dido-class_cruiser" title="Dido-class cruiser"><i>Dido</i>-class</a> anti-aircraft cruisers, to 6.1&#160;inch, though the most common size by far was 6&#160;inch. The <i>Atlanta</i>s and <i>Dido</i>s were borne out of the tactical need for vessels to protect aircraft carriers, battleships and convoys from air attack.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The United States would move into full wartime production of the light cruisers of the <a href="/wiki/Cleveland-class_cruiser" title="Cleveland-class cruiser"><i>Cleveland</i>-class</a> of which 27 would be produced. Unwilling to do anything to slow production, the ships of the class would be seriously overweight. They provided AA screening for the fast carriers, shore bombardment, and anti-destroyer screening for the US fleet. They traded a main gun turret for additional AA, fire control, and radar installations, over the Brooklyn Class.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Light_cruisers_today">Light cruisers today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light_cruiser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Light cruisers today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:HMS_Belfast_1_db.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/HMS_Belfast_1_db.jpg/220px-HMS_Belfast_1_db.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="131" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/HMS_Belfast_1_db.jpg/330px-HMS_Belfast_1_db.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/HMS_Belfast_1_db.jpg/440px-HMS_Belfast_1_db.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2270" data-file-height="1347" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:HMS_Belfast_1_db.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/HMS_Belfast_(C35)" class="mw-redirect" title="HMS Belfast (C35)">HMS&#160;<i>Belfast</i></a> as she is today. She carries 12 x <a href="/wiki/BL_6_inch_Mk_XXIII_naval_gun" class="mw-redirect" title="BL 6 inch Mk XXIII naval gun">6-inch guns</a> and displaced 11,553 tons – "light" in World War II referred to gun size, not displacement.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/BAP_Almirante_Grau_(CLM-81)" title="BAP Almirante Grau (CLM-81)">BAP&#160;<i>Almirante Grau</i></a> of the <a href="/wiki/Peruvian_Navy" title="Peruvian Navy">Peruvian Navy</a> was the last light cruiser in service, being retired in 2017, and will become a museum ship in <a href="/wiki/Lima" title="Lima">Lima</a>. Another four are preserved as <a href="/wiki/Museum_ship" title="Museum ship">museum ships</a>: <a href="/wiki/HMS_Belfast_(C35)" class="mw-redirect" title="HMS Belfast (C35)">HMS&#160;<i>Belfast</i></a> in London, <a href="/wiki/HMS_Caroline_(1914)" title="HMS Caroline (1914)">HMS&#160;<i>Caroline</i></a> in <a href="/wiki/Belfast" title="Belfast">Belfast</a>, <a href="/wiki/USS_Little_Rock_(CL-92)" title="USS Little Rock (CL-92)">USS&#160;<i>Little Rock</i></a> in <a href="/wiki/Buffalo,_New_York" title="Buffalo, New York">Buffalo, New York</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sverdlov-class_cruiser" title="Sverdlov-class cruiser"><i>Mikhail Kutuzov</i></a> at <a href="/wiki/Novorossiysk" title="Novorossiysk">Novorossiysk</a>. Similar ships include the protected cruisers <a href="/wiki/Russian_cruiser_Aurora" title="Russian cruiser Aurora"><i>Aurora</i></a> (<a href="/wiki/St._Petersburg" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Petersburg">St. Petersburg</a>) and <a href="/wiki/USS_Olympia_(C-6)" title="USS Olympia (C-6)">USS&#160;<i>Olympia</i></a> (<a href="/wiki/Philadelphia,_Pennsylvania" class="mw-redirect" title="Philadelphia, Pennsylvania">Philadelphia, Pennsylvania</a>), and the bow of the <i>Puglia</i> (Italy).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_Navy_classification">United States Navy classification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Light_cruiser&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: United States Navy classification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a>, light cruisers have the <a href="/wiki/Hull_classification_symbol" title="Hull classification symbol">hull classification symbol</a> <b>CL</b>. Both <a href="/wiki/Heavy_cruiser" title="Heavy cruiser">heavy cruisers</a> and light cruisers were classified under a common CL/CA sequence after 1931, hence there are some missing hull numbers, see <a href="/wiki/List_of_United_States_Navy_cruisers" class="mw-redirect" title="List of United States Navy cruisers">List of United States Navy cruisers</a>.  After the development of seaborne guided missiles in the 1950s, all remaining cruisers armed solely with guns, regardless of caliber were redesignated as "Gun Cruisers" (hull classification symbol <b>CA</b>), with <a href="/wiki/Guided_missile_cruiser" class="mw-redirect" title="Guided missile cruiser">guided missile cruisers</a> (which generally carry some gun armament) gaining the new hull classification symbol <b>CG</b>.  By the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy_1975_ship_reclassification" title="United States Navy 1975 ship reclassification">1975 fleet realignment</a>, all gun cruisers were out of the fleet.
</p>
