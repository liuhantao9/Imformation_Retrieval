	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Familiar spirit</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Familiar" redirects here. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Familiar_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Familiar (disambiguation)">Familiar (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Familiar Spirits" redirects here. For the book, see <a href="/wiki/Familiar_Spirits_(memoir)" title="Familiar Spirits (memoir)">Familiar Spirits (memoir)</a>.</div>
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Witches%27Familiars1579.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Witches%27Familiars1579.jpg/220px-Witches%27Familiars1579.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Witches%27Familiars1579.jpg/330px-Witches%27Familiars1579.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Witches%27Familiars1579.jpg/440px-Witches%27Familiars1579.jpg 2x" data-file-width="600" data-file-height="400" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Witches%27Familiars1579.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A late 16th-century English illustration of a witch feeding her familiars.</div></div></div>
<p>In European <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a> and folk-belief of the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Medieval</a> and <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern" class="mw-redirect" title="Early Modern">Early Modern</a> periods, <b>familiar spirits</b> (sometimes referred to simply as "<b>familiars</b>" or "<b>animal guides</b>") were believed to be supernatural entities that would assist <a href="/wiki/Witches" class="mw-redirect" title="Witches">witches</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cunning_folk" title="Cunning folk">cunning folk</a> in their practice of <a href="/wiki/Magic_(paranormal)" class="mw-redirect" title="Magic (paranormal)">magic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> According to the records of the time, they would appear in numerous guises, often as an animal, but also at times as a human or humanoid figure, and were described as "clearly defined, three-dimensional… forms, vivid with colour and animated with movement and sound" by those alleging to have come into contact with them, unlike later descriptions of <a href="/wiki/Ghosts" class="mw-redirect" title="Ghosts">ghosts</a> with their "smoky, undefined form[s]".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When they served witches, they were often thought to be <a href="/wiki/Evil" title="Evil">malevolent</a>, while when working for cunning folk they were often thought of as <a href="/wiki/Good_and_evil" title="Good and evil">benevolent</a> (although there was some ambiguity in both cases). The former were often categorised as <a href="/wiki/Demon" title="Demon">demons</a>, while the latter were more commonly thought of and described as <a href="/wiki/Fairy" title="Fairy">fairies</a>. The main purpose of familiars is to serve the witch or young witch, providing protection for them as they come into their new powers.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Since the 20th century a number of magical practitioners, including adherents of the <a href="/wiki/Neopaganism" class="mw-redirect" title="Neopaganism">Neopagan</a> religion of <a href="/wiki/Wicca" title="Wicca">Wicca</a>, have begun to use the concept of familiars, due to their association with older forms of magic. These contemporary practitioners utilize pets, wildlife or believe that invisible spirit versions of familiars act as magical aids.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">Definitions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Familiar_spirit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Histoires_prodigieuses;_Histoire_d%27un_prestre..._WMS_136_Wellcome_L0025557.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Histoires_prodigieuses%3B_Histoire_d%27un_prestre..._WMS_136_Wellcome_L0025557.jpg/170px-Histoires_prodigieuses%3B_Histoire_d%27un_prestre..._WMS_136_Wellcome_L0025557.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="213" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Histoires_prodigieuses%3B_Histoire_d%27un_prestre..._WMS_136_Wellcome_L0025557.jpg/255px-Histoires_prodigieuses%3B_Histoire_d%27un_prestre..._WMS_136_Wellcome_L0025557.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Histoires_prodigieuses%3B_Histoire_d%27un_prestre..._WMS_136_Wellcome_L0025557.jpg/340px-Histoires_prodigieuses%3B_Histoire_d%27un_prestre..._WMS_136_Wellcome_L0025557.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1240" data-file-height="1551" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Histoires_prodigieuses;_Histoire_d%27un_prestre..._WMS_136_Wellcome_L0025557.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A story of "a priest who for the space of 40 years employed a familiar spirit", illustrated in <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I of England</a>'s copy of the <i>Histoires Prodigieuses</i> by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Boaistuau" title="Pierre Boaistuau">Pierre Boaistuau</a>.</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pierre_A._Riffard" title="Pierre A. Riffard">Pierre A. Riffard</a> proposed this definition and quotations<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>A familiar spirit –(alter ego, <a href="/wiki/Doppelg%C3%A4nger" title="Doppelgänger">doppelgänger</a>, personal demon, personal totem, spirit companion) is the double, the alter-ego, of an individual. It does not look like the individual concerned. Even though it may have an independent life of its own, it remains closely linked to the individual. The familiar spirit can be an animal (animal companion).
</p></blockquote>
<p>The French poet <a href="/wiki/Charles_Baudelaire" title="Charles Baudelaire">Charles Baudelaire</a>, a cat fancier, believed in familiar spirits.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>It is the familiar spirit of the place;
</p><p>It judges, presides, inspires
Everything in its empire;
It is perhaps a fairy or a god?
When my eyes, drawn like a magnet
To this cat that I love…
</p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/A._P._Elkin" title="A. P. Elkin">A. P. Elkin</a> studied the belief in familiar spirits among the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Aborigines" class="mw-redirect" title="Australian Aborigines">Australian Aborigines</a>:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>A usual method, or explanation, is that the medicine man sends his familiar spirit (his assistant totem, spirit-dog, spirit-child or whatever the form may be) to gather the information. While this is occurring, the man himself is in a state of receptivity, in sleep or trance. In modern phraseology [spiritism], his familiar spirit would be the control [control spirit].<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p></blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Mircea_Eliade" title="Mircea Eliade">Mircea Eliade</a>:
</p>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r886047036"/><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>The Goldi [Nanai people in Siberia] clearly distinguish between the tutelary spirit (<i>ayami</i>), which chooses the shaman, and the helping spirits (<i>syven</i>), which are subordinate to it and are granted to the shaman by the <i>ayami</i> itself. According to Sternberg the Goldi explain the relations between the shaman and his <i>ayami</i> by a complex sexual emotion. Here is the report of a Goldi shaman.
</p><p>"Once I was asleep on my sick-bed, when a spirit approached me. It was a very beautiful woman. Her figure was very slight, she was no more than half an arshin (71 cm.) tall. Her face and attire were quite as those of one of our Gold women… She said: 'I am the <i>ayami</i> of your ancestors, the Shamans. I taught them shamaning. Now I am going to teach you… I love you, I have no husband now, you will be my husband and I shall be a wife unto you. I shall give you assistant spirits. You are to heal with their aid, and I shall teach and help you myself…' Sometimes she comes under the aspect of an old woman, and sometimes under that of a wolf, so she is terrible to look at. Sometimes she comes as a winged tiger… She has given me three assistants-the <i>jarga</i> (the panther), the <i>doonto</i> (the bear) and the <i>amba</i> (the tiger). They come to me in my dreams, and appear whenever I summon them while shamaning. If one of them refuses to come, the <i>ayami</i> makes them obey, but, they say, there are some who do not obey even the <i>ayami</i>. When I am shamaning, the <i>ayami</i> and the assistant spirits are possessing me; whether big or small, they penetrate me, as smoke or vapour would. When the <i>ayami</i> is within me, it is she who speaks through my mouth, and she does everything herself."<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Descriptions">Descriptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Familiar_spirit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Descriptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Among those accused witches and cunning-folk who described their familiar spirits, there were commonly certain unifying features. The historian <a href="/wiki/Emma_Wilby" title="Emma Wilby">Emma Wilby</a> noted how the accounts of such familiars were striking for their "ordinariness" and "naturalism", despite the fact that they were dealing with <a href="/wiki/Supernatural" title="Supernatural">supernatural</a> entities.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Familiar spirits were most commonly small animals, such as cats, rats, dogs, ferrets, birds, frogs, toads, and hares. There were also cases of wasps and butterflies, as well as pigs, sheep, and horses. Familiar spirits were usually kept in pots or baskets lined with sheep’s wool and fed a variety of things including, milk, bread, meat, and blood.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Familiar spirits usually had names and "were often given down-to-earth, and frequently affectionate, nicknames."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> One example of this was Tom Reid, who was the familiar of the cunning-woman and accused witch <a href="/wiki/Bessie_Dunlop_of_Lynn" title="Bessie Dunlop of Lynn">Bessie Dunlop</a>, while other examples included Grizell and Gridigut, who were the familiars of 17th century <a href="/wiki/Huntingdonshire" title="Huntingdonshire">Huntingdonshire</a> witch Jane Wallis.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An <b>Agathion</b> is a familiar spirit which appears in the shape of a human or an animal, or even within a <a href="/wiki/Amulet" title="Amulet">talisman</a>, bottle or magic ring. It is strongest at midday.<sup id="cite_ref-Bane_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bane-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_between_magical_practitioner_and_familiar">Relationship between magical practitioner and familiar</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Familiar_spirit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relationship between magical practitioner and familiar">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Matthewhopkins.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Matthewhopkins.png/170px-Matthewhopkins.png" decoding="async" width="170" height="219" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Matthewhopkins.png/255px-Matthewhopkins.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5b/Matthewhopkins.png/340px-Matthewhopkins.png 2x" data-file-width="409" data-file-height="528" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Matthewhopkins.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Frontispiece from the witch hunter <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Hopkins" title="Matthew Hopkins">Matthew Hopkins</a>' <i>The Discovery of Witches</i> (1647), showing witches identifying their familiar spirits.</div></div></div>
<p>Using her studies into the role of witchcraft and magic in Britain during the Early Modern period as a starting point, the historian <a href="/wiki/Emma_Wilby" title="Emma Wilby">Emma Wilby</a> examined the relationship that familiar spirits allegedly had with the witches and cunning-folk in this period.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Meeting">Meeting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Familiar_spirit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Meeting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the British accounts from the Early Modern period at least, there were three main types of encounter narrative related to how a witch or cunning person first met their familiar. The first of these was that the spirit spontaneously appeared in front of the individual while they were going about their daily activities, either in their home or outdoors somewhere. Various examples for this are attested in the sources of the time, for instance, Joan Prentice from <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>, England, gave an account when she was interrogated for witchcraft in 1589 claiming that she was "alone in her chamber, and sitting upon a low stool preparing herself to bedward" when her familiar first appeared to her, while the Cornish cunning-woman Anne Jeffries related in 1645 that hers first appeared to her when she was "knitting in an arbour in our garden".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The second manner in which the familiar spirit commonly appeared to magical practitioners in Britain was that they would be given to a person by a pre-existing individual, who was sometimes a family member and at other times a more powerful spirit. For instance, the alleged witch Margaret Ley from <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> claimed, in 1667, that she had been given her familiar spirit by her mother when she died, while the <a href="/wiki/Leicestershire" title="Leicestershire">Leicestershire</a> cunning-woman Joan Willimot related, in 1618, that a mysterious figure whom she only referred to as her "master", "willed her to open her mouth and he would blow into her a fairy which should do her good. And that she open her mouth, and that presently after blowing, there came out of her mouth a spirit which stood upon the ground in the shape and form of a woman."<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In a number of accounts, the cunning person or witch was experiencing difficulty prior to the appearance of the familiar, who offered to aid them. As historian <a href="/wiki/Emma_Wilby" title="Emma Wilby">Emma Wilby</a> noted, "their problems… were primarily rooted in the struggle for physical survival—the lack of food or money, bereavement, sickness, loss of livelihood and so on", and the familiar offered them a way out of this by giving them magical powers.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Working_relationship">Working relationship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Familiar_spirit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Working relationship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In some cases, the magical practitioner then made an agreement or entered a pact with their familiar spirit. The length of time that the witch or cunning person worked with their familiar spirit varied between a few weeks through to a number of decades.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In most cases, the magical practitioner would conjure their familiar spirit when they needed their assistance, although there are many different ways that they did this: the Essex witch Joan Cunny claimed, in 1589, that she had to kneel down within a circle and pray to <a href="/wiki/Satan" title="Satan">Satan</a> for her familiar to appear while the Wiltshire cunning woman Anne Bodenham described, in 1653, that she conjured her familiars by reading books. In some rarer cases there were accounts where the familiars would appear at times when they were unwanted and not called upon, for instance the Huntingdonshire witch Elizabeth Chandler noted, in 1646, that she could not control when her two familiars, named Beelzebub and Trullibub, appeared to her, and had prayed for a god to "deliver her therefrom".<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Types">Types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Familiar_spirit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Familiars are most common in western European mythology, with some scholars arguing that familiars are only present in the traditions of Great Britain and France. In these areas, three categories of familiars are believed to exist:<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>human familiars, throughout Western Europe</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Divinatory" class="mw-redirect" title="Divinatory">divinatory</a> animals, Great Britain and France</li>
<li>malevolent animals, only in <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a></li></ul>
<h2><span id="Prince_Rupert.27s_dog"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Prince_Rupert's_dog">Prince Rupert's dog</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Familiar_spirit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Prince Rupert&#039;s dog">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Prince_Rupert_-_1st_English_Civil_War.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Prince_Rupert_-_1st_English_Civil_War.jpg/220px-Prince_Rupert_-_1st_English_Civil_War.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="239" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Prince_Rupert_-_1st_English_Civil_War.jpg/330px-Prince_Rupert_-_1st_English_Civil_War.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/24/Prince_Rupert_-_1st_English_Civil_War.jpg 2x" data-file-width="398" data-file-height="432" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Prince_Rupert_-_1st_English_Civil_War.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Prince Rupert and his "familiar" dog in a pamphlet titled "The Cruel Practices of Prince Rupert" (1643).</div></div></div>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/English_Civil_War" title="English Civil War">English Civil War</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Cavalier" title="Cavalier">Royalist</a> general <a href="/wiki/Prince_Rupert_of_the_Rhine" title="Prince Rupert of the Rhine">Prince Rupert</a> was in the habit of taking his large <a href="/wiki/Poodle" title="Poodle">poodle</a> dog named <a href="/wiki/Boye_(dog)" class="mw-redirect" title="Boye (dog)">Boye</a> into battle with him. Throughout the war the dog was greatly feared among the <a href="/wiki/Roundhead" title="Roundhead">Parliamentarian</a> forces and credited with supernatural powers. As noted by Morgan,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> the dog was apparently considered a kind of familiar. At the end of the war the dog was shot, allegedly with a <a href="/wiki/Silver_bullet" title="Silver bullet">silver bullet</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Witch_trials">Witch trials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Familiar_spirit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Witch trials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Most data regarding familiars comes from the transcripts of English and Scottish witch trials held during the 16th-17th centuries. The court system that labeled and tried witches was known as the <a href="/wiki/Essex" title="Essex">Essex</a>. The Essex trial of <a href="/wiki/Agnes_Sampson" title="Agnes Sampson">Agnes Sampson</a> of Nether Keith, <a href="/wiki/East_Lothian" title="East Lothian">East Lothian</a> in <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a> in 1590, presents prosecution testimony regarding a divinatory familiar. This case is fundamentally political, trying Sampson for high treason, and accusing Sampson for employing witchcraft against <a href="/wiki/King_James_VI" class="mw-redirect" title="King James VI">King James VI</a>. The prosecution asserts Sampson called familiar spirits and resolved her doubtful matter. Another Essex trial is that of Hellen Clark, tried in 1645, in which Hellen was compelled to state that <a href="/wiki/The_Devil" class="mw-redirect" title="The Devil">The Devil</a> appeared as a "familiar" in the form of a dog.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The English court cases reflect a strong relationship between State's accusations of witchcraft against those who practiced ancient indigenous traditions, including the familiar animal or spirit.
</p><p>In some cases familiars replace children in the favour of their mothers. (See <a href="/wiki/Witchcraft_and_children" class="mw-redirect" title="Witchcraft and children">witchcraft and children</a>.)
</p><p>In colonial America animal familiars can be seen in the witch hunts that took place in Salem, Massachusetts in 1692. Familiar spirits often appear in the visions of the afflicted girls. Although the 1648 law that defined a witch as one who "hath or consulteth with a familiar spirit" had been suspended ten years earlier, association with a familiar spirit was used in the <a href="/wiki/Salem_witch_trials" title="Salem witch trials">Salem trials</a> as evidence to convict suspected witches. Sarah Good was said to have a yellow bird who sucked between her fingers. Ann Putnam in particular frequently saw the yellow bird in her afflictions. <a href="/wiki/Tituba" title="Tituba">Tituba</a> was known to have seen strange animals that urged her to hurt children, these included, a hog, a black dog, a red cat, and a black cat.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Witches%27_mark" title="Witches&#39; mark">witch’s mark</a> added a sexual component to the familiar spirit and is often found in trial records as a way to convict a suspected witch. The mark was most commonly an extra teat found somewhere on the body and was suspected to be used to suckle the familiar spirits. An example of this can be seen in the Salem witch trials of 1692. For example, Ann Putnam told Martha Corey that, "ther is a yellow burd a sucking between your fore finger and midel finger I see it"<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Love_Potion.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/The_Love_Potion.jpg/170px-The_Love_Potion.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="266" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/The_Love_Potion.jpg/255px-The_Love_Potion.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/The_Love_Potion.jpg/340px-The_Love_Potion.jpg 2x" data-file-width="750" data-file-height="1174" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_Love_Potion.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>"<a href="/wiki/The_Love_Potion" title="The Love Potion">The Love Potion</a>" by <a href="/wiki/Evelyn_De_Morgan" title="Evelyn De Morgan">Evelyn De Morgan</a>: a witch with a <a href="/wiki/Black_cat" title="Black cat">black cat</a> familiar at her feet.</div></div></div>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Familiar_spirit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Folk_tales">Folk tales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Familiar_spirit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Folk tales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Historian Emma Wilby identified recurring motifs in various European <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folk tales</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fairy_tales" class="mw-redirect" title="Fairy tales">fairy tales</a> that she believed displayed a belief in familiar spirits. She noted that in such tales as <a href="/wiki/Rumpelstiltskin" title="Rumpelstiltskin">Rumpelstiltskin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Puss-in-Boots" class="mw-redirect" title="Puss-in-Boots">Puss-in-Boots</a> and the <a href="/wiki/The_Frog_Prince_(story)" class="mw-redirect" title="The Frog Prince (story)">Frog Prince</a>, the protagonist is approached by a supernatural being when they are in need of aid, something that she connected to the appearance of familiar spirits in the Early Modern accounts of them.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilby_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilby-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> She believed there to be a direct connection between the belief in and accounts of familiar spirits with these folk tales because "These fairy stories and myths originate from the same reservoir of folk belief as the descriptions of familiar-encounters given by cunning-folk and witches".<sup id="cite_ref-Wilby_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilby-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historiography">Historiography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Familiar_spirit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Historiography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Recent scholarship on familiars exhibits the depth and respectability absent from earlier demonological approaches. The study of familiars has grown from an academic topic in folkloric journals to a general topic in popular books and journals incorporating anthropology, history and other disciplines. James Sharpe, in <i>The Encyclopedia of Witchcraft: the Western Tradition</i>, states: "Folklorists began their investigations in the 19th Century [and] found that familiars figured prominently in ideas about witchcraft."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 19th century, folklorists fired the imagination of scholars who would, in decades to come, write descriptive volumes on witches and familiars. Examples of the growth and development of familiar scholarship are found in <i>Folklore</i>, which consistently contributes articles on traditional beliefs in England and early modern Europe.
</p><p>In the first decades of the 20th century, familiars are identified as "niggets", which are "creepy-crawly things that witches kept all over them".<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Margaret_Murray" title="Margaret Murray">Margaret Murray</a> delves into variations of the familiar found in witchcraft practices. Many of the sources she employs are trial records and demonological texts from early to modern England. These include the 1556 Essex Witchcraft Trials of the Witches of Hatfield Perevil, the 1582 Trial of the Witches of St. Osyth, and the 1645 Essex Trials with <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Hopkins" title="Matthew Hopkins">Matthew Hopkins acting as a Witch-finder</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> In 1921, Murray published <i>The Witch Cult in Western Europe</i>. Her information concerning familiars comes from witchcraft trials in Essex in the 16th and 17th centuries.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Within this book Murray dedicates an entire chapter to the familiar spirit. Her detailed contribution to the topic included several court cases and accounts from Europe in which she finds mention of familiars.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Mary Beth Norton's <i>In the Devils Snare</i> published in 2002, discusses the <a href="/wiki/Salem_witch_trials" title="Salem witch trials">Salem witchcraft</a> crisis of 1692. She frequently references familiar spirits as she explores the trials of the Salem witches.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Familiar_spirit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Charmed" title="Charmed">Charmed</a></i>, the Halliwell sisters are gifted a familiar cat. Other witches have also been shown throughout the series with familiars in the form of cats.
</p><p>Familiars are featured in the <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/Chilling_Adventures_of_Sabrina" title="Chilling Adventures of Sabrina">Chilling Adventures of Sabrina</a></i>. Sabrina’s  familiar is a goblin, which takes the form of black stray cat.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Fantastic_Beasts:_The_Crimes_of_Grindelwald" title="Fantastic Beasts: The Crimes of Grindelwald">Fantastic Beasts: The Crimes of Grindelwald</a></i> features <a href="/wiki/Matagot" title="Matagot">matagots</a>, explained to be spirit familiars.
</p><p>In the manga <i><a href="/wiki/Sugar_Sugar_Rune" title="Sugar Sugar Rune">Sugar Sugar Rune</a></i> and its anime adaptation of the same name, the two main characters have each a familiar to help them become a Queen. Chocolat has a frog named Duke, and Vanilla has a mouse named Blanca.
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Witch_(2015_film)" title="The Witch (2015 film)">The Witch</a></i> features small animals (a goat) as familiars.
</p><p>In a <a href="/wiki/The_X-Files_(season_11)" title="The X-Files (season 11)">season 11</a> episode of <i><a href="/wiki/The_X-Files" title="The X-Files">The X-Files</a></i>, titled "<a href="/wiki/Familiar_(The_X-Files)" title="Familiar (The X-Files)">Familiar</a>", Mulder and Scully encounter a familiar demon which takes the form of creepy children's television show characters.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/FX_(TV_channel)" title="FX (TV channel)">FX</a> series <a href="/wiki/What_We_Do_in_the_Shadows_(TV_series)" title="What We Do in the Shadows (TV series)">What We Do in the Shadows (TV series)</a> depicts familiars as human servants to vampires, either working under hypnosis or the promise of being transformed into a vampire.
</p><p>The tabletop <a href="/wiki/Role-playing_game" title="Role-playing game">role-playing game</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i> features familiars for <a href="/wiki/Wizard_(Dungeons_%26_Dragons)" title="Wizard (Dungeons &amp; Dragons)">wizards</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sorcerer_(Dungeons_%26_Dragons)" title="Sorcerer (Dungeons &amp; Dragons)">sorcerers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Warlock_(Dungeons_%26_Dragons)" title="Warlock (Dungeons &amp; Dragons)">warlocks</a>.
</p>
