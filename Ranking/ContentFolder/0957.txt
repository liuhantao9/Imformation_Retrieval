<p><b>Over</b> is a large village near the <a href="/wiki/River_Great_Ouse" title="River Great Ouse">River Great Ouse</a> in the <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">English</a> county of <a href="/wiki/Cambridgeshire" title="Cambridgeshire">Cambridgeshire</a>, just east of the <a href="/wiki/Prime_Meridian" class="mw-redirect" title="Prime Meridian">Prime Meridian</a>.
</p><p>The parish covers an area of approximately 2,535 acres (1,026&#160;ha). It is ten miles (16&#160;km) east of the town of <a href="/wiki/Huntingdon" title="Huntingdon">Huntingdon</a> and is also ten miles (16&#160;km) northwest from the city of <a href="/wiki/Cambridge" title="Cambridge">Cambridge</a>.
</p><p>Over contains the basic village facilities, including a primary school, shop, one public house (the Admiral Vernon) and St. Mary's Church. In recent years, the village has expanded rapidly, with the inclusion of several housing estates, a community and conference centre and modern sporting facilities. An Over day centre was set up in 1989 by Dr. Pamela Cressey. The Over Community Centre was set up with National Lottery funding of almost £1 million in 1999.
</p><p>Over is mentioned in the poem "The Old Vicarage, Grantchester", by <a href="/wiki/Rupert_Brooke" title="Rupert Brooke">Rupert Brooke</a>. 
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Over,_Cambridgeshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:UK_Over_(Cambridgeshire)_(SideA).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/UK_Over_%28Cambridgeshire%29_%28SideA%29.jpg/220px-UK_Over_%28Cambridgeshire%29_%28SideA%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="382" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/UK_Over_%28Cambridgeshire%29_%28SideA%29.jpg/330px-UK_Over_%28Cambridgeshire%29_%28SideA%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/UK_Over_%28Cambridgeshire%29_%28SideA%29.jpg/440px-UK_Over_%28Cambridgeshire%29_%28SideA%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="789" data-file-height="1369" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:UK_Over_(Cambridgeshire)_(SideA).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Signpost in Over</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:UK_Over_(Cambridgeshire)_(SideB).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/UK_Over_%28Cambridgeshire%29_%28SideB%29.jpg/220px-UK_Over_%28Cambridgeshire%29_%28SideB%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="383" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/UK_Over_%28Cambridgeshire%29_%28SideB%29.jpg/330px-UK_Over_%28Cambridgeshire%29_%28SideB%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/UK_Over_%28Cambridgeshire%29_%28SideB%29.jpg/440px-UK_Over_%28Cambridgeshire%29_%28SideB%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="861" data-file-height="1497" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:UK_Over_(Cambridgeshire)_(SideB).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Signpost in Over</div></div></div>
<p>Some archaeologists<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (August 2009)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> believe that the ridge of slightly higher land upon which the village stands was the furthest intrusion inland of the sea — unlike the villages in the fens, which were often surrounded by watery land after the sea receded. Over was an edge-of-fen settlement.
</p><p>Some say<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (August 2009)">who?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> its name derives from the fact that to get to it travellers had to go <i>over</i> the fens to reach it, while others believe it may come from an old English word meaning "on the banks of the river".
</p><p>By 1628 the fens and meres to the north of the settlement were enclosed, as was the rest of the village land by 1837.
</p><p>Originally, there were two distinct settlements. One was around St Mary's Parish Church, the other at Over End &#8211; the south eastern part of today's High Street running towards the village green.
</p><p>When it comes to buildings, Over is a village full of contrast both in terms of age and designs. Although the exact date is unknown, a row of old thatched cottages on the north side of the High Street were burned to the ground during a fire started by an arsonist. These were replaced by the large Victorian houses which feature balconies to the front.
</p><p>Much of the antiquated property in the village is Victorian, as the majority of the older clunch and wattle cottages have been demolished.
</p><p>The influence of the Dutch who, under the leadership of the engineer Vermuyden, came to drain the Fens, can be seen in several of Over's older houses &#8211; The Old Black Horse in the High Street and the Ivy House in Fen End are the most obvious, with their rounded end-walls and angled brick-ties.
</p><p>The town hall, in the High Street opposite Overcote Road, has the date MDCCCXLIX (1849) carved over the door and some marks made to show where flood waters reached in past years.
</p><p>Apart from the parish church, the oldest remaining structure in Over now is believed to be the wall running down Fen End from the Willingham Road corner. The small patch of wall, now incorporated into newer brickwork, was believed by the late Ernie Papworth to have stood for over 500 years.
</p>
<h2><span id="St_Mary.27s_Church"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="St_Mary's_Church">St Mary's Church</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Over,_Cambridgeshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: St Mary&#039;s Church">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Cmglee_Over_St_Marys_Church.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Cmglee_Over_St_Marys_Church.jpg/170px-Cmglee_Over_St_Marys_Church.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Cmglee_Over_St_Marys_Church.jpg/255px-Cmglee_Over_St_Marys_Church.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Cmglee_Over_St_Marys_Church.jpg/340px-Cmglee_Over_St_Marys_Church.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2048" data-file-height="3072" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Cmglee_Over_St_Marys_Church.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>St Mary's Church, Over in July 2014</div></div></div>
<p>St Mary's Church is made almost entirely of stone from <a href="/wiki/Barnack" title="Barnack">Barnack</a>, in the extreme north of Cambridgeshire. It has traces of 14th century work, including flower-ball carvings, gargoyles representing birds and beasts, and a south porch of perfect proportions.
</p><p>Its size reflects the significant income the village received from, among other things, the <a href="/wiki/Suffolk" title="Suffolk">Suffolk</a> wool trade and goods sold in the market at St Ives.
</p><p>Unlike many Fenland and edge-of-fen churches, it is highly ornamented, which is evidence of the amount of money that was available at the time of construction and decoration: the more complicated the work, the more it cost.
</p><p>There are also two other churches in Over: a Methodist church in the Lanes, which was built in 1848 and features a typical one-storey design found in Methodist churches; and a Baptist church in New Road, built directly onto a house whose owner gave the land for the church to be built in 1737.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Over_today">Over today</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Over,_Cambridgeshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Over today">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Today's Over is within a part of Cambridgeshire where there is enormous pressure for expansion with new housing and industrial and commercial buildings. The village has seen a number of estates built over the past 30 years. Plans have been approved for limited further growth, most of which is likely to be on small sites dotted around the village. One such development is a new estate, Hazel Green, which was completed by Camstead Homes in 2011.
</p><p>The village's population in 1951 was 910, rising to 2,420 by 1991. Today, according to the most recent official figures,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (September 2011)">when?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> there are 1,200 households with a total population of 3,200.
</p>
