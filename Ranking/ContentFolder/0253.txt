<p><b>Burmese names</b> lack the serial structure of most modern names. The <a href="/wiki/Bamar_people" title="Bamar people">Bamars</a> have no customary <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Matronymic" title="Matronymic">matronymic</a> system and thus there is no <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> at all. In the <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Myanmar" title="Culture of Myanmar">culture of Myanmar</a>, people can change their name at will, often with no government oversight, to reflect a change in the course of their lives. Also, many Burmese names use an <a href="/wiki/Honorific" title="Honorific">honorific</a>, given at some point in life, as an integral part of the name.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_and_Western-style_names">Traditional and Western-style names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burmese_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Traditional and Western-style names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Burmese names were originally one syllable, as in the cases of <a href="/wiki/U_Nu" title="U Nu">U Nu</a> and <a href="/wiki/U_Thant" title="U Thant">U Thant</a> ("U" being an honorific). In the mid-20th century, many Burmese started using two syllables, albeit without any formal structure. In the late 1890s, British scholars observed that <a href="/wiki/Rakhine_people" title="Rakhine people">Rakhines</a> commonly adopted three-syllable names whereas Bamars were still using one or two at most.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> As they become more familiar with Western culture, Burmese people are gradually increasing the number of syllables in their children's names, by use of various structures. Today, names with up to four syllables are common for males and up to five for females.
</p><p>Scholars such as <a href="/wiki/Thant_Myint-U" title="Thant Myint-U">Thant Myint-U</a> have argued that the rise of complex Burmese personal names resulted from the collapse of the Burmese monarchy, which ended the sophisticated system of Pali-Burmese styles, crown service and gentry titles, leaving the majority of Burmese with single-syllable names.<sup id="cite_ref-tmu_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tmu-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Former titles, such as <i>min</i> (<span lang="my" title="Burmese language text">မင်း</span>; "leader") were re-appropriated as part of personal names.<sup id="cite_ref-tmu_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tmu-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For example, Burmese nationalist <a href="/wiki/Aung_San" title="Aung San">Aung San</a>'s parents were named Pha (<span lang="my" title="Burmese language text">ဖာ</span>) and Suu (<span lang="my" title="Burmese language text">စု</span>), both of which are single-syllable names. His birth name was Htain Lin (<span lang="my" title="Burmese language text">ထိန်လင်း</span>), but he changed his name to Aung San (<span lang="my" title="Burmese language text">အောင်ဆန်း</span>) later in life. His child is named <a href="/wiki/Aung_San_Suu_Kyi" title="Aung San Suu Kyi">Aung San Suu Kyi</a> (<span lang="my" title="Burmese language text">အောင်ဆန်းစုကြည်</span>). The first part of her name, "Aung San", is from her father's name at the time of her birth. "Suu" comes from her grandmother. "Kyi" comes from her mother, Khin Kyi (<span lang="my" title="Burmese language text">ခင်ကြည်</span>). The addition of the father or mother's name in a person's name is now quite frequent, although it does not denote the development of a family name. Other nomenclature systems are used as well.
</p><p>The use of the names of one's parents and relatives in personal names has been criticized as an un-Burmese adoption of <a href="/wiki/Seriality" title="Seriality">seriality</a>, although it differs from historical Western practices.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bamar_people" title="Bamar people">Bamar</a> names commonly include <a href="/wiki/Pali" title="Pali">Pali</a>-derived words combined with native <a href="/wiki/Burmese_language" title="Burmese language">Burmese</a> words, including:
</p>
<div class="div-col columns column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em;">
<ul><li>Male:
<ul><li><i>thura</i> (<span lang="my" title="Burmese language text">သူရ</span> "brave, gallant" from <i>sūra</i>)</li>
<li><i>thiha</i> (<span lang="my" title="Burmese language text">သီဟ</span> "lion", from <i>sīha</i>)</li>
<li><i>zeya</i> (<span lang="my" title="Burmese language text">ဇေယျာ</span> "victory", from <i>jāya</i>)</li>
<li><i>wunna</i> (<span lang="my" title="Burmese language text">ဝဏ္ဏ</span> "gold", from <i>vaṇṇa</i>)</li></ul></li>
<li>Female:
<ul><li><i>sanda</i> (<span lang="my" title="Burmese language text">စန္ဒာ</span> "moon", from <i>canda</i>)</li>
<li><i>thanda</i> (<span lang="my" title="Burmese language text">သန္တာ</span> "coral", from <i>santa</i>)</li>
<li><i>thiri</i> (<span lang="my" title="Burmese language text">သီရိ</span> "splendour", from <i>siri</i>)</li>
<li><i>hayma</i> (<span lang="my" title="Burmese language text">ဟေမာ</span>, "forest", compare <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">Himalayas</a>)</li></ul></li></ul>
</div>
<p>Burmese people who marry foreigners or move to countries that use surnames may use their name as if part of it represented a <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a>. For example, Tun Myint's wife changed her last name to Myint, but Myint is part of his personal name.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honorifics">Honorifics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burmese_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Honorifics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Burmese_Buddhist_titles" title="Burmese Buddhist titles">Burmese Buddhist titles</a></div>
<p>As above, honorifics supplement a given name, and can be the normal form of address used both in writing and in speech, especially with a name of one or two syllables. Widespread use of honorifics is found within all cultures in the Burmese region. Although some ethnic groups have special honorifics, these words are recognized and applied by other groups (rather than being translated).
</p><p>For example, Aung San's parents are more generally known as U Pha and Daw Suu. These can be translated as "Mr. Pha" and "Ms. Suu" but are often used more informally.

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indexing">Indexing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burmese_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Indexing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chicago_Manual_of_Style" title="The Chicago Manual of Style">The Chicago Manual of Style</a></i>, Burmese names are <a href="/wiki/Subject_indexing" title="Subject indexing">indexed</a> by the first element unless this element is an honorific. Honorifics are mentioned after the other elements of the name, separated by a comma, or are not stated at all.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
