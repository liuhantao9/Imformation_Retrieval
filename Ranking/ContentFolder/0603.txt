<p>A <b>ring road</b> (also known as <b>beltline</b>, <b>beltway</b>, <b>circumferential</b> (<b>high</b>)<b>way</b>, <b>loop</b> or <b>orbital</b>) is a road or a series of connected roads encircling a <a href="/wiki/Town" title="Town">town</a>, <a href="/wiki/City" title="City">city</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">country</a>. The most common purpose of a ring road is to assist in reducing traffic volumes in the urban centre, such as by offering an alternate route around the city for drivers who do not need to stop in the city core.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">Nomenclature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name "ring road" is used for the majority of metropolitan circumferential routes in the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Bundesautobahn_10" title="Bundesautobahn 10">Berliner Ring</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Brussels_Ring" title="Brussels Ring">Brussels Ring</a>, the <a href="/wiki/A10_motorway_(Netherlands)" title="A10 motorway (Netherlands)">Amsterdam Ring</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Boulevard_P%C3%A9riph%C3%A9rique" title="Boulevard Périphérique">Boulevard Périphérique</a> around <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a> <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Inner_Ring_Road" title="Leeds Inner Ring Road">Inner</a> and <a href="/wiki/Leeds_Outer_Ring_Road" title="Leeds Outer Ring Road">Outer</a> ring roads. <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">Pakistan</a> and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> also use the term ring road, as in <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>'s <a href="/wiki/M80_Ring_Road,_Melbourne" title="M80 Ring Road, Melbourne">Western Ring Road</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lahore" title="Lahore">Lahore</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Lahore_Ring_Road" title="Lahore Ring Road">Lahore Ring Road</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hyderabad,_India" class="mw-redirect" title="Hyderabad, India">Hyderabad</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Outer_Ring_Road_(Hyderabad)" class="mw-redirect" title="Outer Ring Road (Hyderabad)">Outer Ring Road</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> the term is the most commonly used, with "orbital" also used, but to a much lesser extent.
</p><p>In Europe, some ring roads, particularly those of <a href="/wiki/Motorway" class="mw-redirect" title="Motorway">motorway</a> standard which are longer in length, are often known as "orbital motorways". Examples include the <a href="/wiki/London_Orbital" class="mw-redirect" title="London Orbital">London Orbital</a> (188 <a href="/wiki/Kilometres" class="mw-redirect" title="Kilometres">km</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Grande_Raccordo_Anulare" title="Grande Raccordo Anulare">Rome Orbital</a> (68&#160;km).
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, many ring roads are called <b>beltlines</b>, <b>beltways</b>, or <b>loops</b>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Capital_Beltway" class="mw-redirect" title="Capital Beltway">Capital Beltway</a> around <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a> Some ring roads, such as Washington's Capital Beltway, use "Inner Loop" and "Outer Loop" <a href="/wiki/Inner/outer_directions" class="mw-redirect" title="Inner/outer directions">terminology</a> for directions of travel, since <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_directions" class="mw-redirect" title="Cardinal directions">cardinal (compass) directions</a> cannot be signed uniformly around the entire loop.  The term 'ring road' is occasionally – and inaccurately – used interchangeably with the term '<a href="/wiki/Bypass_(road)" title="Bypass (road)">bypass</a>'.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sydney-orbital-black-line.png" class="image"><img alt="Map of the Sydney orbital network" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Sydney-orbital-black-line.png/220px-Sydney-orbital-black-line.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="161" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Sydney-orbital-black-line.png/330px-Sydney-orbital-black-line.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Sydney-orbital-black-line.png/440px-Sydney-orbital-black-line.png 2x" data-file-width="2333" data-file-height="1711" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sydney-orbital-black-line.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Sydney_Orbital_Network" title="Sydney Orbital Network">Sydney Orbital Network</a>, New South Wales, Australia</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Bypass_(road)" title="Bypass (road)">Bypasses</a> around many large and small towns were built in many areas when many old roads were upgraded to four-lane status in the 1930s to 1950s, such as those along the <a href="/wiki/National_Road" title="National Road">Old National Road</a> (now generally <a href="/wiki/U.S._Route_40" title="U.S. Route 40">U.S. 40</a> or <a href="/wiki/Interstate_70" title="Interstate 70">Interstate 70</a>) in the United States, leaving the old road in place to serve the town or city, but allowing through travelers to continue on a wider, faster, and safer route.
</p><p>Construction of fully circumferential ring roads has generally occurred more recently, beginning in the 1960s in many areas, when the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Interstate_Highway_System" title="Interstate Highway System">Interstate Highway System</a> and similar-quality roads elsewhere were designed.  Ring roads have now been built around numerous cities and metropolitan areas, including cities with multiple ring roads, irregularly shaped ring roads, and ring roads made up of various other long-distance roads.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> has three ringroads (the <a href="/wiki/M25_motorway" title="M25 motorway">London Orbital</a>, the <a href="/wiki/A406_road" class="mw-redirect" title="A406 road">North</a> and <a href="/wiki/A205" class="mw-redirect" title="A205">South</a> Circular routes, and the <a href="/wiki/London_Inner_Ring_Road" title="London Inner Ring Road">Inner Ring Road</a>). Other British cities have two (<a href="/wiki/Birmingham" title="Birmingham">Birmingham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Norwich" title="Norwich">Norwich</a> and <a href="/wiki/Glasgow" title="Glasgow">Glasgow</a>). <a href="/wiki/Columbus,_Ohio" title="Columbus, Ohio">Columbus, Ohio</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, also has two, while <a href="/wiki/Houston,_Texas" class="mw-redirect" title="Houston, Texas">Houston, Texas</a> will have three official ring roads (not including the downtown freeway loop).  Some cities have far more&#160;&#8211;&#32; <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">Beijing</a>, for example, has <a href="/wiki/Ring_roads_of_Beijing" title="Ring roads of Beijing">six ring roads</a>, simply numbered in increasing order from the city center (though skipping #1).
</p><p>Geographical constraints can complicate or prohibit the construction of a complete ring road.  For example, the <a href="/wiki/Interstate_695_(Maryland)" title="Interstate 695 (Maryland)">Baltimore Beltway</a> in <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">Maryland</a> crosses <a href="/wiki/Baltimore" title="Baltimore">Baltimore Harbor</a> on a high arch <a href="/wiki/Francis_Scott_Key_Bridge_(Baltimore)" title="Francis Scott Key Bridge (Baltimore)">bridge</a>, and much of the partially completed <a href="/wiki/Stockholm_Ring_Road" title="Stockholm Ring Road">Stockholm Ring Road</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Sweden</a> runs through tunnels or over long bridges. However, some towns or cities on seacoasts or near rugged mountains cannot have a full ring road, such as Dublin's <a href="/wiki/M50_motorway_(Ireland)" title="M50 motorway (Ireland)">ring road</a>; or, in the US, <a href="/wiki/Interstate_287" title="Interstate 287">Interstate 287</a>, mostly in <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">New Jersey</a> (bypassing New York City), or <a href="/wiki/Interstate_495_(Massachusetts)" title="Interstate 495 (Massachusetts)">Interstate 495</a> around <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">Boston</a>, none of which completely circles these seaport cities.
</p><p>Adjacency of international boundaries may also prohibit ring road completion in other cases. Construction of a true ring road around <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a> is effectively blocked by its location on the <a href="/wiki/Canada%E2%80%93United_States_border" title="Canada–United States border">border with Canada</a>; although constructing a route mostly or entirely outside city limits is technically feasible, a true ring around Detroit would necessarily pass through Canada, and so <a href="/wiki/Interstate_275_(Michigan)" title="Interstate 275 (Michigan)">Interstate 275</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interstate_696" title="Interstate 696">Interstate 696</a>  together bypass but do not encircle the city. Sometimes, the presence of significant <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">natural</a> or <a href="/wiki/Historic_site" title="Historic site">historical</a> areas limits route options, as for the long-proposed <a href="/wiki/Washington_Outer_Beltway" title="Washington Outer Beltway">Outer Beltway</a> around Washington, D.C., where options for a new western <a href="/wiki/Potomac_River" title="Potomac River">Potomac River</a> crossing are limited by a nearly continuous corridor of heavily visited scenic, natural, and historical landscapes in the <a href="/wiki/Mather_Gorge" title="Mather Gorge">Potomac River Gorge</a> and adjacent areas.
</p><p>When referring to a road encircling a capital city, the term "beltway" can also have a political connotation, as in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> term "<a href="/wiki/Inside_the_Beltway_(terminology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Inside the Beltway (terminology)">Inside the Beltway</a>", derived <a href="/wiki/Metonymy" title="Metonymy">metonymically</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Interstate_495_(Capital_Beltway)" title="Interstate 495 (Capital Beltway)">Capital Beltway</a> encircling Washington, D.C.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_of_ring_roads">Examples of ring roads</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Examples of ring roads">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Most orbital motorways (or beltways) are purpose-built major highways around a town or city, typically without either signals or road or railroad crossings.  In the United States, beltways are commonly parts of the Interstate Highway System.  Similar roads in the United Kingdom are often called "orbital motorways".  Although the terms "ring road" and "orbital motorway" are sometimes used interchangeably, "ring road" often indicates a circumferential route formed from one or more existing roads within a city or town, with the standard of road being anything from an ordinary city street up to motorway level.  An excellent example of this is London's North Circular/South Circular ring road.
</p><p>In some cases, a circumferential route is formed by the combination of a major through highway and a similar-quality loop route that extends out from the parent road, later reconnecting with the same highway.  Such loops not only function as a <a href="/wiki/Bypass_route" class="mw-redirect" title="Bypass route">bypass</a> for through traffic, but also to serve outlying <a href="/wiki/Suburb" title="Suburb">suburbs</a>.  In the United States, an Interstate highway loop is usually designated by a three-digit number beginning with an even digit before the two-digit number of its parent interstate. Interstate spurs, on the other hand, generally have three-digit numbers beginning with an odd digit.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="mw-kartographer-container thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width: 290px;"><a class="mw-kartographer-map" data-mw="interface" data-style="osm-intl" data-width="290" data-height="290" data-zoom="10" data-lat="38.906" data-lon="-77.033" data-overlays="[&quot;_3b75881863c1fb271eb39c4b61bfa85409e072ca&quot;]" style="background-image: url(https://maps.wikimedia.org/img/osm-intl,10,38.906,-77.033,290x290.png?lang=en&amp;domain=en.wikipedia.org&amp;title=Ring+road&amp;groups=_3b75881863c1fb271eb39c4b61bfa85409e072ca); height: 290px;" href="/wiki/Special:Map/10/38.906/-77.033/en"></a><div class="thumbcaption">The <a href="/wiki/Capital_Beltway" class="mw-redirect" title="Capital Beltway">Capital Beltway</a> around <a href="/wiki/Washington,_DC" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington, DC">Washington, DC</a></div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:I275_in_Sharonville.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/I275_in_Sharonville.jpg/220px-I275_in_Sharonville.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/I275_in_Sharonville.jpg/330px-I275_in_Sharonville.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/I275_in_Sharonville.jpg/440px-I275_in_Sharonville.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2272" data-file-height="1704" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:I275_in_Sharonville.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>I-275 passing through Sharonville (suburb of Cincinnati, OH)</div></div></div>
<p>Circumferential highways are prominent features in or near many large cities in the United States. In many cases, such as <a href="/wiki/Interstate_285_(Georgia)" title="Interstate 285 (Georgia)">Interstate 285</a> (also known as the Perimeter) in <a href="/wiki/Atlanta,_Georgia" class="mw-redirect" title="Atlanta, Georgia">Atlanta, Georgia</a>, circumferential highways serve as a bypass while other highways pass directly through the city center. In other cases, a primary Interstate highway passes around a city on one side, with a connecting loop Interstate bypassing the city on the other side, together forming a circumferential route, as with <a href="/wiki/I-93" class="mw-redirect" title="I-93">I-93</a> and <a href="/wiki/Interstate_495_(Massachusetts)" title="Interstate 495 (Massachusetts)">I-495</a> in the area of <a href="/wiki/Lawrence,_Massachusetts" title="Lawrence, Massachusetts">Lawrence, Massachusetts</a>.  However, if a primary Interstate passes through a city and a loop bypasses it on only one side (as in the <a href="/wiki/Wilmington,_Delaware" title="Wilmington, Delaware">Wilmington, Delaware</a>, area), no fully circumferential route is provided.
</p><p>Route numbering is challenging when a through highway and a loop bypass together form a circumferential ring road.  Since neither of the highways involved is circumferential itself, either dual signage or two (or more) route numbers is needed. The history of signage on the <a href="/wiki/Interstate_495_(Capital_Beltway)" title="Interstate 495 (Capital Beltway)">Capital Beltway</a> around Washington, D.C., is instructive here. <a href="/wiki/Interstate_95" title="Interstate 95">Interstate 95</a>, a major through highway along the U.S. <a href="/wiki/East_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="East Coast of the United States">East Coast</a>, was originally planned as a through-the-city route there, with the Beltway encircling the city as I-495.  The portion of I-95 entering the city from the south was soon completed (and so signed), primarily by adapting an existing major highway, but the planned extension of I-95 through residential areas northward to the Beltway was long delayed, and eventually abandoned, leaving the eastern portion of the Beltway as the best Interstate-quality route for through traffic.
</p><p>This eastern portion of the Beltway was then redesignated from I-495 to I-95, leaving the I-495 designation only on the western portion, and the completed part of the planned Interstate inside the Beltway was redesignated as a spur, <a href="/wiki/Interstate_395_(District_of_Columbia%E2%80%93Virginia)" class="mw-redirect" title="Interstate 395 (District of Columbia–Virginia)">I-395</a>.  A few years later, the resulting confusion from different route numbers on the circumferential Beltway was resolved by restoring I-495 signage for the entire Beltway, with dual signage for I-95 for the highway's concurrent use as a through Interstate on its eastern portion.
</p><p>The longest complete beltway in the United States is <a href="/wiki/Interstate_275_(Ohio%E2%80%93Indiana%E2%80%93Kentucky)" title="Interstate 275 (Ohio–Indiana–Kentucky)">Interstate 275</a>, an 83.71-mile (134.72&#160;km) loop in <a href="/wiki/Ohio" title="Ohio">Ohio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Indiana" title="Indiana">Indiana</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">Kentucky</a> that forms a complete beltway around the <a href="/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" class="mw-redirect" title="Cincinnati, Ohio">Cincinnati, Ohio</a> area. It encloses an area of over 250,000 acres.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:AnthonyHendayDrive.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/AnthonyHendayDrive.JPG/220px-AnthonyHendayDrive.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="134" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/AnthonyHendayDrive.JPG/330px-AnthonyHendayDrive.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/AnthonyHendayDrive.JPG/440px-AnthonyHendayDrive.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4896" data-file-height="2981" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:AnthonyHendayDrive.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Anthony Henday Drive ring road in Edmonton</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Edmonton" title="Edmonton">Edmonton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a> has two ring roads.  The first is a loose conglomeration of four major <a href="/wiki/Arterial_roads" class="mw-redirect" title="Arterial roads">arterial roads</a> with an average distance of 6&#160;km (4&#160;mi) from the downtown core. <a href="/wiki/Yellowhead_Trail" title="Yellowhead Trail">Yellowhead Trail</a> forms the northern section, <a href="/wiki/Wayne_Gretzky_Drive" title="Wayne Gretzky Drive">Wayne Gretzky Drive</a>/75 &#160;Street forms the eastern section, <a href="/wiki/Whitemud_Drive" title="Whitemud Drive">Whitemud Drive</a> forms the southern and longest section, and <a href="/wiki/170_Street,_Edmonton" title="170 Street, Edmonton">170&#160;Street</a> forms the western and shortest section.  Whitemud Drive is the only section that is a true <a href="/wiki/Controlled-access_highway" title="Controlled-access highway">controlled-access highway</a>, while Yellowhead Trail and Wayne Gretzky Drive have interchanges and intersections and are therefore both <a href="/wiki/Limited-access_road" title="Limited-access road">limited-access roads</a>. 170 Street and 75&#160;Street are merely large arterial roads with intersections only.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>  The second and more prominent ring road is named <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Henday_Drive" title="Anthony Henday Drive">Anthony Henday Drive</a>; it circles the city at an average distance of 12&#160;km (7.5&#160;mi) from the downtown core.  It is a freeway for its entire 78-kilometre (48&#160;mi) length, and was built to reduce inner-city traffic congestion, created a bypass of Yellowhead Trail, and has improved the movement of goods and services across Edmonton and the surrounding areas. It was completed in October 2016 as the first free-flowing orbital road in Canada.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NEdone_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NEdone-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Stoney_Trail" title="Stoney Trail">Stoney Trail</a> is a ring road that circles the majority of <a href="/wiki/Calgary" title="Calgary">Calgary</a>. The southwest portion of the freeway is currently under construction.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Winnipeg" title="Winnipeg">Winnipeg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a> has a ring road which is called the <a href="/wiki/Perimeter_Highway" class="mw-redirect" title="Perimeter Highway">Perimeter Highway</a>. It is designated as <a href="/wiki/Manitoba_Highway_101" class="mw-redirect" title="Manitoba Highway 101">Manitoba Highway 101</a> on the north, northwest and east sides and as <a href="/wiki/Manitoba_Highway_100" class="mw-redirect" title="Manitoba Highway 100">Manitoba Highway 100</a> on the south and southwest sides. The majority of it is a four-lane divided <a href="/wiki/Limited-access_road" title="Limited-access road">expressway</a>. It has a second ring road, planned since the 1950s and not yet completed, called the Suburban Beltway. It consists of several roads -- <a href="/wiki/Lagimodiere_Boulevard" class="mw-redirect" title="Lagimodiere Boulevard">Lagimodiere Blvd.</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg_Route_165" title="Winnipeg Route 165">Bishop Grandin Blvd.</a>, the Fort Garry Bridge, the Moray Bridge, <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg_Route_96" title="Winnipeg Route 96">William R Clement Parkway</a>, <a href="/wiki/Winnipeg_Route_17" title="Winnipeg Route 17">Chief Peguis Trail</a> and the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kildonan_Bridge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kildonan Bridge (page does not exist)">Kildonan Bridge</a>.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Saskatoon" title="Saskatoon">Saskatoon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a> has a ring road named Circle Drive. It is designated as <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan_Highway_16" title="Saskatchewan Highway 16">Saskatchewan Highway 16</a> on the east and north, and as <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan_Highway_11" title="Saskatchewan Highway 11">Saskatchewan Highway 11</a> on the south, and simply as Circle Drive on the west.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Regina,_Saskatchewan" title="Regina, Saskatchewan">Regina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">Saskatchewan</a> has a partial ring road which is named <a href="/wiki/Ring_Road_(Regina,_Saskatchewan)" title="Ring Road (Regina, Saskatchewan)">Ring Road</a>; however, due to the city's urban growth since the road was originally constructed, it no longer functions as a true ring road and has instead come to be used partially for local arterial traffic. A new outer ring, the <a href="/wiki/Regina_Bypass" title="Regina Bypass">Regina Bypass</a>, is under construction.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Greater_Sudbury" title="Greater Sudbury">Sudbury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> has a partial ring road consisting of the <a href="/wiki/Southwest_and_Southeast_Bypasses" title="Southwest and Southeast Bypasses">Southwest and Southeast Bypasses</a> segment of <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Highway_17" title="Ontario Highway 17">Ontario Highway 17</a>, and the Northwest Bypass segment of <a href="/wiki/Ontario_Highway_144" title="Ontario Highway 144">Ontario Highway 144</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ring_road&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:S_65_Abb_15_Opernring.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/S_65_Abb_15_Opernring.jpg/220px-S_65_Abb_15_Opernring.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="128" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/S_65_Abb_15_Opernring.jpg/330px-S_65_Abb_15_Opernring.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/S_65_Abb_15_Opernring.jpg/440px-S_65_Abb_15_Opernring.jpg 2x" data-file-width="878" data-file-height="509" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:S_65_Abb_15_Opernring.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Vienna's Ring Road with the opera house, in 1905</div></div></div>
<p>Most major cities in Europe are served by a ring road that either circles the inner core of their metropolitan areas or the outer borders of the city proper or both. In major transit hubs, such as the <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele-de-France" title="Île-de-France">Île-de-France</a> region surrounding Paris and the <a href="/wiki/Frankfurt" title="Frankfurt">Frankfurt</a> area, major national highways converge just outside city limits before forming one of several routes of an urban network of roads circling the city. Unlike in United States, route numbering is not a challenge on European ring roads as routes merge to form the single designated road. However, exit and road junction access can be challenging due to the complexity of other routes branching from or into the ring road.
</p><p>One of the most renowned ring roads is the <a href="/wiki/Vienna_Ring_Road" title="Vienna Ring Road">Vienna Ring Road</a> (<i>Ringstraße</i>), a grand boulevard constructed in the mid-19th century and filled with representative buildings. Due to its unique architectural beauty and history, it has also been called the "Lord of the ring roads", and is declared by UNESCO as part of Vienna's <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">World Heritage Site</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Major European cities that are served by a ring road or ring road system:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a> – <a href="/wiki/A10_motorway_(Netherlands)" title="A10 motorway (Netherlands)">A10 motorway</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Antwerp" title="Antwerp">Antwerp</a> – <a href="/wiki/R1_ring_road_(Belgium)" title="R1 ring road (Belgium)">R1</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a> – <a href="/wiki/Attiki_Odos" title="Attiki Odos">Attiki Odos</a> (Motorway A6)</li>
