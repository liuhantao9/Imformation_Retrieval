<p><b>Ross John Anderson</b>, <a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society" title="Fellow of the Royal Society">FRS</a>, <a href="/wiki/Royal_Academy_of_Engineering" title="Royal Academy of Engineering">FREng</a><sup id="cite_ref-List_of_Fellows_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-List_of_Fellows-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> (born 15 September 1956)<sup id="cite_ref-whoswho_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whoswho-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is a researcher, author, and industry consultant in <a href="/wiki/Security_engineering" title="Security engineering">security engineering</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-securityengineering_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-securityengineering-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He is <a href="/wiki/Security_engineering" title="Security engineering">Professor of Security Engineering</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Computer_Laboratory,_University_of_Cambridge" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer Laboratory, University of Cambridge">Computer Laboratory, University of Cambridge</a><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> where he is part of the University's security group.<sup id="cite_ref-scopus_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scopus-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-acm_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-acm-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Education">Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ross_J._Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anderson was educated at the <a href="/wiki/High_School_of_Glasgow" title="High School of Glasgow">High School of Glasgow</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-whoswho_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whoswho-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 1978, he graduated with a Bachelor of Arts in mathematics and <a href="/wiki/Natural_Sciences_(Cambridge)" title="Natural Sciences (Cambridge)">natural science</a> from <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College, Cambridge</a>, and subsequently received a qualification in <a href="/wiki/Computer_engineering" title="Computer engineering">computer engineering</a>. Anderson worked in the <a href="/wiki/Avionics" title="Avionics">avionics</a> and banking industry before moving back to the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">University of Cambridge</a> in 1992, to work on his doctorate under the supervision of <a href="/wiki/Roger_Needham" title="Roger Needham">Roger Needham</a><sup id="cite_ref-needphd_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-needphd-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and start his career as an academic researcher.<sup id="cite_ref-cv_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cv-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He received his PhD in 1995, and became a lecturer in the same year.<sup id="cite_ref-whoswho_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-whoswho-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ross_J._Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anderson's research interests<sup id="cite_ref-googlescholar_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-googlescholar-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mathgene_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mathgene-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-scopus_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scopus-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> are in security, <a href="/wiki/Cryptography" title="Cryptography">cryptology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dependability" title="Dependability">dependability</a> and <a href="/wiki/Technology_policy" title="Technology policy">technology policy</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-googlescholar_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-googlescholar-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Cryptography" title="Cryptography">cryptography</a>, he designed with <a href="/wiki/Eli_Biham" title="Eli Biham">Eli Biham</a> the <a href="/wiki/BEAR_(cipher)" class="mw-redirect" title="BEAR (cipher)">BEAR</a>, <a href="/wiki/LION_(cipher)" class="mw-redirect" title="LION (cipher)">LION</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tiger_(hash)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tiger (hash)">Tiger</a> <a href="/wiki/Cryptographic" class="mw-redirect" title="Cryptographic">cryptographic</a> primitives, and co-wrote with Biham and <a href="/wiki/Lars_Knudsen" class="mw-redirect" title="Lars Knudsen">Lars Knudsen</a> the <a href="/wiki/Block_cipher" title="Block cipher">block cipher</a> <a href="/wiki/Serpent_(cipher)" title="Serpent (cipher)">Serpent</a>, one of the finalists in the <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Encryption_Standard" title="Advanced Encryption Standard">Advanced Encryption Standard (AES)</a> competition. He has also discovered weaknesses in the <a href="/wiki/FISH_(cipher)" title="FISH (cipher)">FISH</a> cipher and designed the stream cipher <a href="/wiki/Pike_(cipher)" title="Pike (cipher)">Pike</a>.
</p><p>In 1998, Anderson founded the <a href="/wiki/Foundation_for_Information_Policy_Research" title="Foundation for Information Policy Research">Foundation for Information Policy Research</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Think_tank" title="Think tank">think tank</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lobbying" title="Lobbying">lobbying</a> group on information-technology policy.
</p><p>Anderson is also a founder of the UK-Crypto mailing list and the <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_security" title="Economics of security">economics of security</a> research domain.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He is well-known among Cambridge academics as an outspoken defender of academic freedoms, intellectual property and other matters of university politics. He is engaged in the ″Campaign for Cambridge Freedoms″<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> and has been an elected member of Cambridge University Council since 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In January 2004, the student newspaper <i><a href="/wiki/Varsity_(Cambridge)" title="Varsity (Cambridge)">Varsity</a></i> declared Anderson to be Cambridge University's "<i>most powerful person</i>".<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2002, he became an outspoken critic of <a href="/wiki/Trusted_computing" class="mw-redirect" title="Trusted computing">trusted computing</a> proposals, in particular <a href="/wiki/Microsoft" title="Microsoft">Microsoft</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Next-Generation_Secure_Computing_Base" title="Next-Generation Secure Computing Base">Palladium</a> operating system vision.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Anderson's TCPA FAQ has been characterised by IBM TC researcher David R. Safford as "full of technical errors" and of "presenting speculation as fact."<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For years Anderson has been arguing that by their nature large <a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">databases</a> will never be free of abuse by breaches of security. He has said that if a large system is designed for ease of access it becomes insecure; if made watertight it becomes impossible to use. This is sometimes known as <i>Anderson's Rule</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Anderson is the author of <i>Security Engineering</i>, published by Wiley in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-securityengineering_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-securityengineering-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> He was the founder and editor of <i>Computer and Communications Security Reviews</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-cv_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cv-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
After the vast <a href="/wiki/Global_surveillance_disclosure" class="mw-redirect" title="Global surveillance disclosure">Global surveillance disclosure</a> leaked by <a href="/wiki/Edward_Snowden" title="Edward Snowden">Edward Snowden</a> beginning in June 2013 Anderson suggested one way to begin stamping out the British state's unaccountable involvement in this NSA spying scandal is to entirely end the domestic secret services. Anderson: “Were I a legislator, I would simply abolish <a href="/wiki/MI5" title="MI5">MI5</a>." Anderson notes the only way this kind of systemic data collection has been made possible was through the <a href="/wiki/Business_model" title="Business model">business models</a> of private industry. The value of information-driven web companies such as Facebook and <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">Google</a> is built around their ability to gather vast tracts of data. It was something the intelligence agencies would have struggled with alone.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Anderson is a critic of <a href="/wiki/Smart_meter" title="Smart meter">smart meters</a>, writing that there are various privacy and energy security concerns.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_honours">Awards and honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ross_J._Anderson&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Awards and honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anderson was elected a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Fellows_of_the_Royal_Society_elected_in_2009" title="List of Fellows of the Royal Society elected in 2009">Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS) in 2009</a>. His nomination reads: 
</p>
<table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
<tbody><tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“
</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">Professor Ross Anderson, Personal Chair in Security Engineering, Computer Laboratory, University of Cambridge.
<p>Ross Anderson is a pioneer and world leader in security engineering, and is distinguished for starting a number of new areas of research in hardware, software and systems.
</p><p>His early work on how systems fail established a base of empirical evidence for building <a href="/wiki/Threat_model" title="Threat model">threat models</a> for a wide range of applications from banking to healthcare.
</p><p>He has made trailblazing contributions that helped establish a number of new research topics, including security usability, hardware <a href="/wiki/Tamper_resistance" class="mw-redirect" title="Tamper resistance">tamper-resistance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Information_hiding" title="Information hiding">information hiding</a>, and the analysis of <a href="/wiki/Application_programming_interface" title="Application programming interface">application programming interfaces</a>.
</p><p>He is also one of the founders of the study of information <a href="/wiki/Economics_of_security" title="Economics of security">security economics</a>, which not only illuminates where the most effective attacks and defences may be found, but is also of fundamental importance to making policy for the <a href="/wiki/Information_society" title="Information society">information society</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-royal_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-royal-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
</td>

</td></tr>
