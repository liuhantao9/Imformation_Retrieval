<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="First_names">First names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armenian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: First names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Armenia's National Statistical Service, the  most commonly used names for newborn boys in <a href="/wiki/Armenia" title="Armenia">Armenia</a>, as of early 2012<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Armenian_name&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, were <a href="/wiki/David_(name)" title="David (name)">Davit</a>, Narek, Gor, <a href="/wiki/Hayk" title="Hayk">Hayk</a>, Alex, <a href="/wiki/Eric" title="Eric">Erik</a>, Arman, <a href="/wiki/Samuel_(name)" title="Samuel (name)">Samvel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tigran" class="mw-redirect" title="Tigran">Tigran</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aram_(given_name)" title="Aram (given name)">Aram</a>. For girls, the most popular names were <a href="/wiki/Nareh_(given_name)" title="Nareh (given name)">Nare</a>, <a href="/wiki/Milena_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Milena (name)">Milena</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maria_(given_name)" title="Maria (given name)">Maria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Maneh_(given_name)" title="Maneh (given name)">Mane</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ani_(given_name)" title="Ani (given name)">Ani</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anna_(given_name)" title="Anna (given name)">Anna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anahit" title="Anahit">Anahit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mariam" class="mw-redirect" title="Mariam">Mariam</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ellen" title="Ellen">Elen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mary_(name)" title="Mary (name)">Mary</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Traditionally, Armenians have used <a href="/wiki/Biblical_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Biblical name">Biblical names</a> of Greek, Latin and Hebrew origin, such as <a href="/wiki/Abraham_(given_name)" title="Abraham (given name)">Abraham</a>, Hakob (<a href="/wiki/Jacob_(name)" title="Jacob (name)">Jacob</a>), Hovhannes (<a href="/wiki/John_(given_name)" title="John (given name)">John</a>), Petros (<a href="/wiki/Peter_(given_name)" title="Peter (given name)">Peter</a>), Poghos (<a href="/wiki/Paul_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Paul (name)">Paul</a>), Madlene (<a href="/wiki/Magdalene_(given_name)" title="Magdalene (given name)">Magdalene</a>), Yeghisabet (<a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_(given_name)" title="Elizabeth (given name)">Elizabeth</a>), <a href="/wiki/Tamar_(name)" title="Tamar (name)">Tamar</a>, etc.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surnames">Surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armenian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Typical modern Armenian last names (<a href="/wiki/Family_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Family names">family names</a>) end with the originally <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> suffix <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-%D5%B5%D5%A1%D5%B6" class="extiw" title="wikt:-յան">-յան</a> (<a href="/wiki/Reformed_Armenian_orthography" class="mw-redirect" title="Reformed Armenian orthography">reformed orthography</a>) or <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-%D5%A5%D5%A1%D5%B6" class="extiw" title="wikt:-եան">-եան</a> (<a href="/wiki/Classical_Armenian_orthography" title="Classical Armenian orthography">classical orthography</a>), transliterated as <b>-yan</b>, <b>-ian</b>,  or less often '-jan'. Example: Petros<i>yan</i>, meaning "issued from Petros", akin to the English name Peterson. Some Armenian last names bear the suffix -նց ([nʦʰ]), transliterated as -nc, -nts or -ntz (as in <a href="/wiki/Axel_Bakunts" title="Axel Bakunts">Bakunts</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Adontz" title="Nicholas Adontz">Adontz</a>), or in addition to -yan/-ian (as in Vardanyants), although that is not common.<sup id="cite_ref-Seferian_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Seferian-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some Armenian surnames have a suffix -լի ([li]) preceding the -յան/-եան suffix.  For example, Sivaslian and Vanlian refer to the cities of <a href="/wiki/Sivas" title="Sivas">Sivas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Van,_Turkey" title="Van, Turkey">Van</a>, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Other Armenian surnames have a suffix -ճի ([ʤi] or [ʧi]), transliterated as -ji/dji or -chi/tchi, preceding the -յան/-եան suffix. Names such as Kebabjian and Kahvejian refer to kebab and coffee merchants, respectively. While the -եան suffix already existed in the <a href="/wiki/Classical_Armenian" title="Classical Armenian">Classical Armenian</a> period (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Artaxiad_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Artaxiad Dynasty">Արտաշեսեան Artashesian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mamikonian" title="Mamikonian">Մամիկոնեան Mamikonian</a>), other suffixes, now rarer, also existed. The suffix <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-%D5%B8%D6%82%D5%B6%D5%AB" class="extiw" title="wikt:-ունի">-ունի</a> (as in <a href="/wiki/Bagratuni_Dynasty" class="mw-redirect" title="Bagratuni Dynasty">Բագրատունի Bagratuni</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arsacid_Dynasty_of_Armenia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arsacid Dynasty of Armenia">Արշակունի Arshakuni</a>, [uni]) had a similar meaning to -եան.
</p><p>The suffix <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-%D6%81%D5%AB" class="extiw" title="wikt:-ցի">-ցի</a> ([tsʰi]) denoted a geographical provenance, e.g. <a href="/wiki/Movses_Khorenatsi" title="Movses Khorenatsi">Movses Khorenatsi</a> (from Khoren) and <a href="/wiki/Anania_Shirakatsi" title="Anania Shirakatsi">Anania Shirakatsi</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Shirak,_Armenia" title="Shirak, Armenia">Shirak</a>).
</p><p>The prefix <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D5%BF%D5%A5%D6%80" class="extiw" title="wikt:տեր">Տեր</a> ([ter]), which comes from how one addresses a priest, is typically Armenian. It might be followed by a space or attached directly to the root. If someone possesses a surname containing "Der" or "Ter" it usually signifies that this person has a <a href="/wiki/Patrilineality" title="Patrilineality">patrilineal ancestor</a> who was a priest.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Roots_of_names">Roots of names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Armenian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Roots of names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The roots of names ending with -ian/-yan are typically first names, such as Petrosian "issued from Petros", Simonian "issued from Simon", etc. When the name ends in -lian or -(d)jian, the root is expected to refer to a location or a profession, respectively. Thus, the etymology of Vanlian is literally "issued from the man from Van" and that of Kebabjian is "issued from the kebab merchant". However, an ancestor's occupation will not necessarily contain the suffix -(d)ji, thus Vardapetian/Vartabedian (Վարդապետյան/-եան) translates as "issued from the <a href="/wiki/Vardapet" title="Vardapet">vardapet</a>", Bjishkian (Բժիշկյան/-եան) as "issued from the doctor" and Keshishian (Քեշիշյան/-եան) as "issued from the priest".
</p><p>The -ian/-yan suffix may also, but more rarely, attach to an adjective denoting a quality. Thus, Bzdigian/Pztikian (Պզտիկյան/-եան) translates as "issued from the small (man)", Medzian/Metsian (Մեծյան/-եան) as "issued from the tall (man)", Ganantchian/Kanantchian (Կանանչյան/-եան) as "issued from the green, i.e. young (man)".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some former <a href="/wiki/Armenian_nobility" title="Armenian nobility">noble Armenian names</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Artsruni" class="mw-redirect" title="Artsruni">Artsruni/Ardzruni</a> (Արծրունի) are still in use today. When they moved from Armenia or from the Middle East, some changed their last names to adapt better to their new societies. Sometimes the -ian or -yan ending was dropped and the root kept, such as <a href="/wiki/Charles_Aznavour" title="Charles Aznavour">Charles Aznavour</a> (was Aznavourian), or <a href="/wiki/Andy_Serkis" title="Andy Serkis">Andy Serkis</a> (originally Sarkissian). Other times the name was translated, as "Tashjian" (which is derived from Turkish word 'taş' meaning 'stone') becoming (approximately) "Stone".
</p>
