<p><b>Normanton</b> is a town and <a href="/wiki/Civil_parishes_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil parishes in England">civil parish</a> in the <a href="/wiki/City_of_Wakefield" title="City of Wakefield">City of Wakefield</a> in <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, England. It is north-east of <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a> and south-west of <a href="/wiki/Castleford" title="Castleford">Castleford</a>, and at the time of the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_Census_2011" class="mw-redirect" title="United Kingdom Census 2011">2011 Census</a>, the population of the civil parish was 20,872,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> whilst the Normanton ward of the Wakefield City Council had a population of 16,220.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Normanton,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Domesday_Book" title="Domesday Book">Domesday Book</a></i> gives information on 'Norman-tune'<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> as:
</p>
<blockquote><p>
In Normantune there are 10 carucates for geld, which 5 plows can plough. 2 thegns had 2 manors there T.R.E. Now, in the King's hand there are 6 villeins there, and 3 bordars, a priest and a church, with 3 ploughs, 3 acres (12,000&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) of meadow. Pasturable wood (land) 6 furlongs in length and 1 in breadth. The whole of this land lies in the soc of Wachefelt, except the Church. T.R.E. it was worth 12s: now (it is worth) 10s.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Normanton was originally surrounded by a <a href="/wiki/Moat" title="Moat">moat</a>, and in <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">Norman</a> times was the site of an enclosed settlement (chosen for its strategic view points across the surrounding area), and became known as 'Norman – tune', or 'Norman – ton'.
</p><p>At the time the <i>Domesday Book</i> was compiled in 1086, the colonisation of Yorkshire by the Normans was well underway. Archaeological evidence at today's Normanton points to Haw Hill (or How Hill), an eminence that was probably a Norman defensive strategic mound once reinforced by a wooden palisade.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The evidence of a Norman motte-and-bailey fortification at the town, and the name, is likely evidence that Normanton's name derived from the substantial <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Normans" title="Anglo-Normans">Anglo-Norman</a> presence in the area. Nearby, after all, were <a href="/wiki/Tickhill" title="Tickhill">Tickhill</a>, the fortress of Norman magnate <a href="/wiki/Roger_de_Busli" title="Roger de Busli">Roger de Busli</a>, as well as other new Norman power centres. Although the area had once been part of the <a href="/wiki/Danelaw" title="Danelaw">Scandinavian Danelaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">William the Conqueror</a>'s scorched earth northern campaign had left the area ripe for exploitation by his Lords. By all measures, Normanton likely owes its name to these new aggressive warriors.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hanson_House_circa_1890_Normanton_West_Yorkshire.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ec/Hanson_House_circa_1890_Normanton_West_Yorkshire.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="138" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="200" data-file-height="138" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hanson_House_circa_1890_Normanton_West_Yorkshire.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Hanson House, Normanton, ca. 1890</div></div></div>
<p>One of the earliest buildings to survive within the village is Hanson House.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> This is set back from the church and has a timber frame construction. The building was constructed in the mid-15th century, with a later wing added in the 18th century. A fire has damaged the building, which is being restored and modernised.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growth">Growth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Normanton,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Growth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Normanton_old.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d7/Normanton_old.jpg/200px-Normanton_old.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="113" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d7/Normanton_old.jpg/300px-Normanton_old.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d7/Normanton_old.jpg/400px-Normanton_old.jpg 2x" data-file-width="957" data-file-height="539" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Normanton_old.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>All Saints' Parish Church rooms <a href="/wiki/Circa" title="Circa">circa</a> 1880</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Normanton_community_centre_today.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/Normanton_community_centre_today.jpg/200px-Normanton_community_centre_today.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="150" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/Normanton_community_centre_today.jpg/300px-Normanton_community_centre_today.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/Normanton_community_centre_today.jpg/400px-Normanton_community_centre_today.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Normanton_community_centre_today.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>All Saints' Parish Church rooms in 2008</div></div></div>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Grammar_school" title="Grammar school">grammar school</a> was founded in Normanton by <a href="/wiki/John_Freeston" title="John Freeston">John Freeston</a> (now the 'Parish Rooms'..later moved to the site of the, Normanton Junior School on Church Lane) in 1592,<sup id="cite_ref-auto_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> but the town remained very small until it became the focus of several <a href="/wiki/Railway_line" class="mw-redirect" title="Railway line">railway lines</a> in the mid-19th century. Work began in 1837 under the supervision of <a href="/wiki/George_Stephenson" title="George Stephenson">George Stephenson</a> on the construction of the <a href="/wiki/North_Midland_Railway" title="North Midland Railway">North Midland Railway</a> from <a href="/wiki/Derby" title="Derby">Derby</a> to <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>. This was soon followed by an addition to the <a href="/wiki/York_and_North_Midland_Railway" title="York and North Midland Railway">York and North Midland Railway</a> from <a href="/wiki/York" title="York">York</a> and then the <a href="/wiki/Manchester_and_Leeds_Railway" title="Manchester and Leeds Railway">Manchester and Leeds Railway</a> from <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> which all extended to Normanton thereby giving the town access to much of the country. The Leeds and Manchester lines crossed a 51-mile (82&#160;km) stretch across the <a href="/wiki/Pennines" title="Pennines">Pennines</a> and at the time had the world's longest railway station platform at Normanton, a quarter of a mile long. The station was, for the next ten years or so, the most important in England, employing over 700 people who looked after the station's 700,000 passengers a year.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian times</a> Normanton station was one of the most important stations in <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">Northern England</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a> stopped over in the Station Hotel. The town also served as an important part of the transport infrastructure for national and local industries including coal and bricks, although most of this was lost during the 1950s and 1960s with the last remaining operational brickworks eventually closing in the mid-1990s. There were three brickworks in town which were all built within the small area of Newland, taking advantage of the abundance of clay from the area. A fourth works was founded in the 1890s by a Thomas Kirk from <a href="/wiki/Nottingham" title="Nottingham">Nottingham</a>, who had heard rumours that Normanton was rapidly turning into an important junction on the railways. Both Kirk and his sons used their life savings and formed the Normanton Brick Company at nearby <a href="/wiki/Altofts" title="Altofts">Altofts</a> which finally closed in 2011
</p><p>The coming of the railways enabled the locally mined coal to be sent across the country. Demand soon outstripped supply and many more shafts were sunk to reach the coal seams under the town. In 1871 <a href="/wiki/Dom_Pedro" title="Dom Pedro">Dom Pedro</a>, the Emperor of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, visited Normanton with his Empress and gave his name (in a slightly altered form) to the Don Pedro <a href="/wiki/Colliery" class="mw-redirect" title="Colliery">Colliery</a> at Hopetown. Today roads in the area of the former colliery retain the name. At their peak the collieries employed over 10,000 men, most of whom wanted to move themselves and their families to Normanton. The town enjoyed a boom period with more mines opening and more shafts being sunk in order to meet the increasing demand for coal until, by the mid-1970s, most coal seams in the Normanton area were worked-out.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Decline">Decline</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Normanton,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Decline">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The disputes surrounding the <a href="/wiki/UK_Miners%27_Strike_(1984-1985)" class="mw-redirect" title="UK Miners&#39; Strike (1984-1985)">miners' strike of 1984–85</a> meant that many mines across the country were to close although by this stage there were no collieries still in production in Normanton. However, the strike still affected many families in the area, as Normanton colliers still worked in pits in neighbouring towns. The area once occupied by the St John's or Newland Colliery is now part of the controversial Welbeck landfill site which has been the subject of both local and national media attention since its development as a toxic tip. The site attracted so much negative attention that the group Residents Against Toxic Scheme (RATS) was established to halt planning permission for the disposal of toxic chemicals at the site, claiming the scheme was an extreme health hazard.
</p><p>Normanton suffered decline in the years following the miners' strike. The railway station was in such a state of neglect and disrepair that the decision was made to demolish it in 1986; the section of railway line between Goosehill Junction and Crofton Interchange were lifted the year after. Very little now remains of Normanton's railway and mining heritage.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern">Modern</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Normanton,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Normanton has become a growing commuter suburb of the <a href="/wiki/Leeds_City_Region" title="Leeds City Region">Leeds City Region</a>, with relatively cheap housing and efficient transport links. The town is still accessible via <a href="/wiki/Normanton_railway_station" title="Normanton railway station">Normanton railway station</a>, and is currently served by an unmanned island platform station with regular trains to <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Castleford" title="Castleford">Castleford</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wakefield" title="Wakefield">Wakefield</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a> <a href="/wiki/Meadowhall_Interchange" title="Meadowhall Interchange">Meadowhall Interchange</a>.
</p><p>The addition and expansion of the Eurolink <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Estate" class="mw-redirect" title="Industrial Estate">Industrial Estate</a> at Junction 31 of the <a href="/wiki/M62_motorway" title="M62 motorway">M62</a> attracted national and multi-national corporations to locate their distribution depots to Normanton. Being centrally located within the UK, the town is served by three major motorway networks: the M62 linking Liverpool to <a href="/wiki/Kingston_upon_Hull" title="Kingston upon Hull">Hull</a> (west to east); the <a href="/wiki/M1_motorway" title="M1 motorway">M1</a> linking Leeds to <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> (North to South); and the North of England via a new link between the M62 and the A1(M) at nearby <a href="/wiki/Ferrybridge" title="Ferrybridge">Ferrybridge</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Newland">Newland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Normanton,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Newland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Newland_with_Woodhouse_Moor" title="Newland with Woodhouse Moor">Newland estate</a> was a small township on the outskirts of Normanton and lies on the north bank of the River Calder. Existing since 1213 when it was established by <a href="/wiki/John,_King_of_England" title="John, King of England">King John</a> as a preceptory of the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Templar" title="Knights Templar">Knights Templar</a>, it was later transferred to the similar organisation, the "<a href="/wiki/Knights_Hospitaller" title="Knights Hospitaller">Knights Hospitaller</a>" in 1256.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The property belonging to the Hospitallers was dissolved by King <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a> who bestowed the property upon himself. The earliest known preceptor of the Knights Hospitaller was Simon Paccable in 1313. Newland was once held by the <a href="/wiki/Levett" title="Levett">Levett</a> family, and William Levett, who was lord of the manor, was admitted tenant of the Knights Hospitaller on 2 October 1447.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The estate changed hands of ownership a number of times since King Henry VIII sold the land in 1544, with final ownership landing on the Warmfield Company Limited in 1926, who ran brickworks until the mid-20th century.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Newland today is kept as a nature reserve, with the remains of the estate overgrown with shrubs and plant life, the remains of Newland Hall still stand in the woodlands as a derelict building that last saw occupancy in 1972.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> A small fishing area just outside the Newland Estate is used by local fishermen.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Normanton,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Normanton is just off of Junction 31 of the M62. It has a railway station, <a href="/wiki/Normanton_railway_station" title="Normanton railway station">Normanton Railway Station</a>. The town is on bus routes<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and is served by taxi and minibus companies.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Normanton,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The town is home to a cricket club – Normanton St John's CC, and 2 football teams – A.S. Normanton F.C. and Normanton Athletic Juniors. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Filmography">Filmography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Normanton,_West_Yorkshire&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Filmography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The old Normanton brickyard, situated just off of the A655 Wakefield Road, was used in the late-1990s as the fictional setting of a murder in the <a href="/wiki/ITV_Network" class="mw-redirect" title="ITV Network">ITV</a> series <i><a href="/wiki/A_Touch_Of_Frost" class="mw-redirect" title="A Touch Of Frost">A Touch Of Frost</a></i>. The structure was intact but abandoned at the time of filming and was the ideal location favoured by the ITV production team.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2013)">according to whom?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In the series a body was found by the <a href="/wiki/Police" title="Police">Police</a> in one of the factory's old kilns. Within 18 months of filming, the structure had been declared unsafe, and was partially demolished, leaving only the chimney stack standing.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
