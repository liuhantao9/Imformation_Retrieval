	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Irish name</h1>

<p>A formal <b>Irish-language personal name</b> consists of a <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a>. Surnames in <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a> are generally <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> in etymology, although they are no longer literal patronyms, as most <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_name" title="Icelandic name">Icelandic names</a> are. The form of a surname varies according to whether its bearer is <a href="/wiki/Male" title="Male">male</a> or <a href="/wiki/Female" title="Female">female</a> and in the case of a <a href="/wiki/Married" class="mw-redirect" title="Married">married</a> woman, whether she chooses to adopt her husband's surname.
</p><p>An alternative traditional naming convention consists of the first name followed by a double patronym, usually with the father and grandfather's names. This convention is not used for official purposes but is generalized in <i><a href="/wiki/Gaeltachta%C3%AD" class="mw-redirect" title="Gaeltachtaí">Gaeltachtaí</a></i>, or Irish-speaking areas, and also survives in some rural non-Gaeltacht areas. Sometimes the name of the mother or grandmother may be used instead of that of the father or grandfather.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Epithets">Epithets</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Epithets">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A first name may be modified by an adjective to distinguish its bearer from other people with the same name. <i><b>Mór</b></i> ("big") and <i><b>Óg</b></i> ("young") are used to distinguish father and son, like English "<a href="/wiki/Suffix_(name)" title="Suffix (name)">senior</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Junior_(suffix)" class="mw-redirect" title="Junior (suffix)">junior</a>", but are placed between the given name and the surname: <i>Seán Óg Ó Súilleabháin</i> corresponds to "John O'Sullivan Jr." (although anglicised versions of the name often drop the "O'" from the name).
</p><p>The word <i>Beag</i>/<i>Beg</i>, meaning "little", can be used in place of <i>Óg</i>. This did not necessarily indicate that the younger person was small in stature, merely younger than his father. Sometimes <i>beag</i> would be used to imply a baby was small at birth, possibly <a href="/wiki/Preterm_birth" title="Preterm birth">premature</a>.
</p><p>Adjectives denoting hair colour may also be used, especially informally: <i>Pádraig Rua</i> ("red-haired Patrick"), <i>Máire Bhán</i> ("fair-haired Mary").
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surnames_and_prefixes">Surnames and prefixes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Surnames and prefixes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A male's surname generally takes the form <i><b>Ó</b></i>/<i><b>Ua</b></i> (meaning "descendant") or <i><b>Mac</b></i> ("son") followed by the <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">genitive case</a> of a name, as in <i>Ó Dónaill</i> ("descendant of Dónall") or <i>Mac Lochlainn</i> ("son of Lochlann").
</p><p>A son has the same surname as his father. A female's surname replaces <i>Ó</i> with <i><b>Ní</b></i> (reduced from <i>Iníon Uí</i> – "daughter of descendant of") and <i>Mac</i> with <i><b>Nic</b></i> (reduced from <i>Iníon Mhic</i> – "daughter of the son of"); in both cases the following name undergoes <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">lenition</a>. However, if the second part of the surname begins with the letter <i>C</i> or <i>G</i>, it is not lenited after <i>Nic</i>. Thus the daughter of a man named Ó Dónaill has the surname <i>Ní Dhónaill</i>; the daughter of a man named Mac Lochlainn has the surname <i>Nic Lochlainn</i>. When anglicised, the name can remain O' or Mac, regardless of gender.
</p><p>If a woman marries, she may choose to take her husband's surname. In this case, <i>Ó</i> is replaced by <i><b>Bean Uí</b></i> ("wife of descendant of") and <i>Mac</i> is replaced by <i><b>Bean Mhic</b></i> ("wife of the son of"). In both cases <i>bean</i> may be omitted, in which case the woman uses simply <i><b>Uí</b></i> or <i><b>Mhic</b></i>. Again, the second part of the surname is lenited (unless it begins with <i>C</i> or "G", in which case it is only lenited after <i>Uí</i>). Thus a woman marrying a man named Ó Dónaill may choose to be use <i>Bean Uí Dhónaill</i> (Mrs. <a href="/wiki/O%27Donnell" class="mw-redirect" title="O&#39;Donnell">O'Donnell</a> in English) or <i>Uí Dhónaill</i> as her surname; a woman marrying a man named Mac Lochlainn may choose to be use <i>Bean Mhic Lochlainn</i> (Mrs. <a href="/wiki/McLaughlin_(surname)" title="McLaughlin (surname)">McLaughlin</a> in English) or <i>Mhic Lochlainn</i> as her surname.
</p><p>If the second part of the surname begins with a <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">vowel</a>, the form <i>Ó</i> attaches an <i>h</i> to it, as in <i>Ó hUiginn</i> (<a href="/wiki/O%27Higgins_(surname)" title="O&#39;Higgins (surname)">O'Higgins</a>) or <i>Ó hAodha</i> (<a href="/wiki/Hughes_(surname)" title="Hughes (surname)">Hughes</a>). The other forms effect no change: <i>Ní Uiginn</i>, <i>(Bean) Uí Uiginn</i>; <i>Mac Aodha</i>, <i>Nic Aodha</i>, <i>Mhic Aodha</i>, and so forth.
</p><p><i>Mag</i> is often used instead of <i>Mac</i> before a vowel or (sometimes) the silent <i>fh</i>. The single female form of "Mag" is "Nig". <i>Ua</i> is an alternative form of <i>Ó</i>.
</p><p>Some names of <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">Norman</a> origin have the prefix <i>Fitz</i>, from <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin language</a> <i>filius</i> "son", such as Fitzwilliam, Fitzgerald, and so forth. Other Norman surnames may have the prefix "de", such as de Búrca, de Paor, or de Róiste.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Patronyms_and_other_additives">Patronyms and other additives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Patronyms and other additives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many Irish surnames are concentrated in particular parts of the country and there are areas where a single surname may account for a large proportion of the population. Examples include O'Reilly in County Cavan, Ryan in County Tipperary and East County Limerick, or O'Sullivan in the Beara peninsula of West Cork; or areas, such as <a href="/wiki/Glenullin" title="Glenullin">Glenullin</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Sperrins" title="Sperrins">Sperrins</a>, where there are several dominant surnames (in that instance O'Kane, Mullan, McNicholl and some others). In such cases, the surname may also acquire an additive in popular usage to differentiate one group bearing the same surname from another.
</p><p>This sometimes originates as a simple <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronym</a>&#160;– that is, a James whose father was Harry might be referred to as Harry's James&#160;– but may be passed to later generations, so that James' son Pat might be Harry's Pat. This can also occur if a person becomes well known by a nickname: his children may take his nickname as an additive. For example, if Seán O'Brien was often referred to as "Badger", his son Patrick might be referred to orally as Pat Badger and written as Patrick O'Brien (B).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In Tipperary, additives are particularly common among those bearing the Ryan surname. Examples include Ryan Lacken, Ryan Luke and Ryan Doc. A man christened Thomas Ryan might be known as Tommy Doc and his family might be referred as the Docs. While the additive is not part of a person's official name, it may be used in a postal address, on an election register or in newspaper reports. In this case, Tommy Doc might be written as Thomas Ryan (D).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Traditional_Gaeltacht_names">Traditional Gaeltacht names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Irish_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Traditional Gaeltacht names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In Gaeltacht (Irish-speaking) areas, it remains customary to use a name composed of the first name, followed by the father's name in the <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">genitive case</a>, followed by the name of the paternal grandfather, also in the genitive. Thus Seán Ó Cathasaigh (Seán O'Casey), son of Pól, son of Séamus, would be known to his neighbours as Seán Phóil Shéamuis. Occasionally, if the mother or grandmother was a well-known person locally, her name may be used instead of that of the father or grandfather. If the mother's name is used, then that of the maternal grandfather (or potentially grandmother) follows it, for example, Máire Sally Eoghain.
</p><p>These names are not used for official purposes. Often a nickname or English version of a name is used in their composition where the person would use a standard Irish form in formal circumstances. For example, the prominent <a href="/wiki/Sean-n%C3%B3s_song" title="Sean-nós song">sean-nós</a> singer <a href="/w/index.php?title=Se%C3%A1n_Mac_Dhonnchadha&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Seán Mac Dhonnchadha (page does not exist)">Seán Mac Dhonnchadha</a> is perhaps better known as Johnny Mhairtín Learaí.
</p><p>This naming system also survives to a certain extent in rural areas outside the existing Gaeltacht. The system can be particularly useful for distinguishing individuals who live in the same locale and who share a common surname but are not closely related. For example, two individuals named John McEldowney might be known as "John Patsy Den" and "John Mary Philip" respectively. Even the Irish forms sometimes survive in parts of the <a href="/wiki/Sperrins" title="Sperrins">Sperrins</a>, so that among the principal families of <a href="/wiki/Glenullin" title="Glenullin">Glenullin</a> some branches are known by father/grandfather forms such as Pháidí Shéamais or Bhrian Dhónaill.
</p>
