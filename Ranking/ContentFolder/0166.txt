<p><b>Melih Cevdet Anday</b> (13 March 1915 – 28 November 2002), was a <a href="/wiki/Turkish_people" title="Turkish people">Turkish</a> writer whose unique poetry stands outside the traditional literary movements. He also wrote in many other genres which, over six and a half decades, included eleven collections of poems, eight plays, eight novels, fifteen collections of essays, several of which won major literary awards. He also translated several books from diverse languages into Turkish.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melih_Cevdet_Anday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Melih Cevdet Anday was born in <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a> in 1915 and lived there until his parents moved to <a href="/wiki/Ankara" title="Ankara">Ankara</a> in 1931. He graduated from Gazi High School and for a while began studying sociology in <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">Belgium</a> on a State Railways scholarship but had to return home in 1940 after the German invasion. Between 1942–51 he worked as a publication consultant for the Ministry of Education in Ankara and then as a city librarian. During this time he began his career as a journalist for several newspapers. After 1954, he worked as a teacher for the Istanbul Municipal Conservatory. Between 1964 and 1969, Anday served on Turkish Radio Television’s Board of Directors. After he retired from his position in the Conservatory in 1977, Anday was assigned to <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">UNESCO</a> Headquarters in Paris as Cultural Attaché until recalled after a change of Government.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_career">Literary career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melih_Cevdet_Anday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Literary career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As a poet, Anday was one of the leaders of the <a href="/wiki/Garip" title="Garip">Garip</a> movement, which also included <a href="/wiki/Orhan_Veli" class="mw-redirect" title="Orhan Veli">Orhan Veli</a> and <a href="/wiki/Oktay_Rifat" class="mw-redirect" title="Oktay Rifat">Oktay Rifat</a>. According to the preface of their joint collection, published in 1941, poetry should abandon the formalism and rhetorical classical style of previous centuries, making itself simple, colloquial, and matter of fact—an artless art designed to serve the common people.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>However, present there even then was an uneasy acknowledgement of French <a href="/wiki/Surrealism" title="Surrealism">Surrealism</a>, and Anday was eventually to change his engaged style to a cerebral neo-surrealism as he cautiously navigated beyond the difficult political waters of his country. This culminated in what was regarded at the time as his masterwork, the four-sectioned long poem "Ulysses Bound" (<i>Kollari Bagli Odysseus</i>) of 1963.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In this he deploys an original rhetoric of his own:
</p>
<dl><dd>A slow world, in progress, with no memory</dd>
<dd>Visible only to the eye before there was an eye</dd>
<dd>Where nameless beings were advancing among other beings</dd>
<dd>Trees grew before trees were</dd>
<dd>And a star in the temple of the clouds</dd>
<dd>Opened wide the unharvested sky</dd>
<dd>To the bloody dawn of the epochs before there was reason.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>Other sectioned poems of some length were to follow, including "On the Nomad Sea" (<i>Göçebe Denizin Üstünde</i>, 1970)<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> and "A poem in the manner of Karacaoglan" (<i>Karacaoğlan’ın Bir Şiiri Üzerine Çeşitlemeler’de</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> But there were also many short poems of disarming simplicity such as "Sun" (I was just about to speak/ When suddenly the sun came out) and "Seagull" (Seagull, capital letter/ Scribbled by a child) whose thoughtful qualities journey beyond his earlier manner.
</p><p>From henceforth his varied work began to earn Anday official recognition. In particular his play <i>Mikado’nun Çöpleri</i> (The Mikado Game) earned him several awards: Most Successful Playwright of the 1967–1968 Drama Season; the İlhan İskender Prize; Ankara Art Lovers Foundation for the Best Playwright in 1971–1972.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Another play, <i>Ölümsüzler ya da Bir Cinayetin Söylencesi</i> (The Immortals or the Legend of a Murder) won the Enka Art Prize in 1980. His poetry collection <i>Teknenin Ölümü</i> (Death of the Boat) won the 1978 Sedat Simavi Foundation Literature Prize, and <i>Ölümsüzlük Ardinda Gilgamis</i> (Gilgamesh Beyond Death) gained the 1981 Türkiye İş Bankası Prize. In 1971 UNESCO honoured among him other outstanding European authors. He also received the TÜYAP Honour Prize for 1991 and the 2000 Aydin Dogan Foundation’s Literature Award.
</p><p>In 1994 the sculptor <a href="/wiki/Metin_Yurdanur" title="Metin Yurdanur">Metin Yurdanur</a> cast a seated statue of him in bronze which is now sited in the park named after him at Ören on the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_G%C3%B6kova" title="Gulf of Gökova">Gulf of Gökova</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Translations_into_European_languages">Translations into European languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Melih_Cevdet_Anday&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Translations into European languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anday’s works have been translated into Russian, German, Hungarian, Romanian, French and English. Book-length translations include the novel <i>Aylaklar</i> into Bulgarian (Sofia 1966) and poetry selections into French: <i>Ulysse Bras Attachés et autres poems</i>, (Poésie-Club UNESCO, Paris, 1970) and <i>Offrandes</i> 1946–1989 (Editions UNESCO, 1998). US selections of poetry include <i>On The Nomad Sea</i>, (Geronimo Books, New York, 1974); <i>Rain One Step Away</i>, (Charioteer Press, Washington, DC, 1980); <i>Silent Stones: Selected Poems of Melih Cevdet Anday</i> (Northfield: Talisman House, 2017).<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  The last of these, translated by poets <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Wade" title="Sidney Wade">Sidney Wade</a> and  <a href="/wiki/Efe_Murad" title="Efe Murad">Efe Murad</a>, was winner of the 2015 Meral Divitci Prize.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><b>Sources</b>
</p><p>1.<i>M.C.ANDAY</i>. "EI" Magazine of European Art Center (EUARCE) of Greece, 8st issue 1994, p.11 &amp; 38-39
</p>
