<p><b>Woolworths Group</b> is a listed British company that owned the <a href="/wiki/High_Street" title="High Street">high-street</a> retail chain Woolworths. It also owned other companies such as the entertainment distributor <a href="/wiki/Entertainment_UK" title="Entertainment UK">Entertainment UK</a>, and book and resource distributor <a href="/wiki/Bertram_Books" class="mw-redirect" title="Bertram Books">Bertram Books</a>.
</p><p>The Woolworths store chain is the main enterprise of the group. Originally a division of the American <a href="/wiki/F._W._Woolworth_Company" title="F. W. Woolworth Company">F. W. Woolworth Company</a> until its sale in the early 1980s,<sup id="cite_ref-teleg_history_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-teleg_history-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bbc_cert_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bbc_cert-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> it had more than 800 stores in the UK prior to closure. Woolworths sold many goods and had its own <a href="/wiki/Ladybird_(clothing)" title="Ladybird (clothing)">Ladybird</a> children's clothing range, <a href="/wiki/Chad_Valley_(toy_brand)" title="Chad Valley (toy brand)">Chad Valley</a> toys, and WorthIt! value range. The chain was the UK's largest buyer of <a href="/w/index.php?title=Candyking&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Candyking (page does not exist)">Candyking</a> "<a href="/wiki/Pick_%27n%27_mix" class="mw-redirect" title="Pick &#39;n&#39; mix">pick 'n' mix</a>" sweets. It was sometimes referred to as "Woolies" by the UK media, the general public, and occasionally in its own television commercials.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The British company also owned and ran <a href="/wiki/F._W._Woolworth_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="F. W. Woolworth Ireland">F. W. Woolworth Ireland</a> until 1984 and <a href="/wiki/Woolworths_(Cyprus)" title="Woolworths (Cyprus)">Woolworths (Cyprus)</a> until 2003.
</p><p>On 26 November 2008, trading of shares in Woolworths Group was suspended, and its Woolworths and Entertainment UK subsidiaries entered <a href="/wiki/Administration_(insolvency)" class="mw-redirect" title="Administration (insolvency)">administration</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC26Nov08er_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC26Nov08er-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Deloitte" title="Deloitte">Deloitte</a> closed all 807 Woolworths stores between 27 December 2008 and 6 January 2009, resulting in 27,000 job losses.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Woolworths Group plc entered administration on 27 January 2009,<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and it was officially dissolved on 13 October 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 2009, <a href="/wiki/Shop_Direct_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Shop Direct Group">Shop Direct Group</a> purchased the Woolworths trademark and internet address, which continued as a retail website until its closure in June 2015. As of April 2017, there are talks of Woolworths making a comeback to British high streets, as former director Tony Page wants to buy the Woolworths name from Shop Direct.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>.
</p><p>On 21 September 2019, it was announced that Woolworths would be making a comeback to the UK, and that a number of stores would re-open in 2020.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inauguration">Inauguration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Inauguration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Woolworths_head_office_on_Marylebone_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_586681.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Woolworths_head_office_on_Marylebone_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_586681.jpg/220px-Woolworths_head_office_on_Marylebone_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_586681.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Woolworths_head_office_on_Marylebone_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_586681.jpg/330px-Woolworths_head_office_on_Marylebone_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_586681.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Woolworths_head_office_on_Marylebone_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_586681.jpg/440px-Woolworths_head_office_on_Marylebone_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_586681.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Woolworths_head_office_on_Marylebone_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_586681.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Woolworths head office on <a href="/wiki/Marylebone_Road" title="Marylebone Road">Marylebone Road</a>, <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a></div></div></div>
<p>The British branch of the <a href="/wiki/F._W._Woolworth_Company" title="F. W. Woolworth Company">F. W. Woolworth Company</a>, which had been founded in Pennsylvania, <b>F. W. Woolworth &amp; Co. Ltd</b> was founded by <a href="/wiki/Franklin_Winfield_Woolworth" class="mw-redirect" title="Franklin Winfield Woolworth">Frank Woolworth</a> in <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>, England on 5 November 1909.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Frank Woolworth had ancestry in <a href="/wiki/Woolley,_Cambridgeshire" title="Woolley, Cambridgeshire">Woolley, Cambridgeshire</a>— Frank claimed he had traced his ancestry through the Founding Fathers of the district to a small farm in middle-England.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> When Frank eventually travelled to England in 1890,<sup id="cite_ref-1800s_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1800s-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> he docked in <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> and travelled by train to <a href="/wiki/Stoke-on-Trent" title="Stoke-on-Trent">Stoke-on-Trent</a> for the purchase of china and glassware for Woolworth's ranges, but also noted his love of England in his diary and his aspirations for bringing the Woolworth name to England:
</p>

</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”

<p>During the buying trip, Woolworth met a young clerk, William Lawrence Stephenson, who was recommended to him by <a href="/wiki/John_Wanamaker" title="John Wanamaker">John Wanamaker</a>. Wanamaker had established a large chain of department stores across the United States and was one of Woolworth's heroes. Stephenson was invited to London to meet Woolworth again, and was offered the job as director of the new company, which he accepted.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_concerns_with_British_stores">Internal concerns with British stores</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Internal concerns with British stores">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the idea for the creation of British stores, Frank Woolworth had offered invitations to store managers in the United States to open up stores in the UK and had only received offers to take positions at the time of his illness in March 1909 from Fred Woolworth of the <a href="/wiki/Sixth_Avenue_(Manhattan)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sixth Avenue (Manhattan)">Sixth Avenue</a> and Samuel Balfour of the <a href="/wiki/14th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="14th Street (Manhattan)">14th Street</a> stores in New York City.<sup id="cite_ref-woolworth_eng_volun_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-woolworth_eng_volun-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> After these initial offers, Byron Miller, a superintendent in a Boston store, also offered his assistance and set sail with the other volunteers on the steam boat <i><a href="/wiki/RMS_Empress_of_Scotland_(1906)" class="mw-redirect" title="RMS Empress of Scotland (1906)">Kaiserin Auguste Victoria</a></i> on 29 May 1909 for England from Hoboken.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Frank Woolworth expected other members of staff to admire the volunteers in establishing FW Woolworth &amp; Co in Britain.  However, Carson C. Peck, vice president and general manager of the company, had reservations with enlisting staff members to travel to Britain, questioning whether Woolworth had indeed created the new business adventure following a dream, or due to his dissatisfaction with the current condition of the American branch.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Peck also asked those who were willing to volunteer to reconsider their decision, claiming that those who had volunteered were unaware of the uncertainty and risks involved and that some were only tentatively willing to engage in Woolworth's new endeavour:<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> His concerns mainly entered on the fact that the majority of the managers who followed the decision did so out of loyalty to Woolworth,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and that moving such a valuable resource already established in the United States to what was a financially unproven "Little Infant" in the UK would have a detrimental effect upon the "Bread and Butter" of the Company.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Inauguration_of_British_stores">Inauguration of British stores</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Inauguration of British stores">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Woolworths_head_office_on_Marylebone_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_586681.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Woolworths_head_office_on_Marylebone_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_586681.jpg/220px-Woolworths_head_office_on_Marylebone_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_586681.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Woolworths_head_office_on_Marylebone_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_586681.jpg/330px-Woolworths_head_office_on_Marylebone_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_586681.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Woolworths_head_office_on_Marylebone_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_586681.jpg/440px-Woolworths_head_office_on_Marylebone_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_586681.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Woolworths_head_office_on_Marylebone_Road_-_geograph.org.uk_-_586681.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Woolworths Group PLC headquarters on <a href="/wiki/Marylebone_Road" title="Marylebone Road">Marylebone Road</a>, London</div></div></div>
<p>Despite reservations such as Peck's, the decision to launch stores in the United Kingdom went ahead as previously planned by Woolworth. He considered several locations for the first stores, together with future possible sites.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The chosen location for the first store was 25 - 25A, Church Street and 8, Williamson Street <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a> <sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> (the street addresses of the different entrances). It opened on 5 November 1909 with a performance by a full orchestra, circus acts and fireworks.<sup id="cite_ref-Coslett_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Coslett-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As a means of adherence to American trading tradition, only viewing of items was allowed on the first day of the shop's opening. This included guests being given complementary tea while being entertained by a traditional brass band in the refreshment room. The event was reported positively by the local newspaper, <i>the <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_Courier" title="Liverpool Courier">Liverpool Courier</a></i>, which praised the decor of the stores along with the value and range of items on sale.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite local press praise, British national newspaper the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Mail" title="Daily Mail">Daily Mail</a></i> likened Frank Woolworth to <a href="/wiki/P._T._Barnum" title="P. T. Barnum">Phineas Taylor Barnum</a> and claimed that the store location had been decided as part of a contingency plan in the event of failure so as to facilitate escape from any financial liability.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Despite these reservations, the store proved to be a success; large queues outside and low priced <a href="/wiki/%C2%A3sd" title="£sd">3d.</a> and 6d. ("threepenny and sixpenny") items leading to it being almost stripped bare of goods before the end of the first day of trading and being attributed to mass purchased mass-produced foreign and local goods.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At the onset of the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a>, F.W. Woolworth &amp; Co. had 40 stores in Great Britain and Ireland located in most major cities - from which a total of 57 staff including store managers had enlisted; the majority of whom did not return after the end of the war in 1918.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Despite American staff again offering their services to the Woolworths branches in Britain, remaining staff increased their efforts to cope with the lack of staff members  throughout the war with several staff members being promoted to managerial positions.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Stores in the United States, which were then stocking ranges also present in British stores were dependent upon European manufacturers which had adopted newer production methods than their American counterparts.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
<h3><span id="1918.E2.80.931982"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1918–1982">1918–1982</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1918–1982">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:A_Picture_of_a_Southern_Town-_Life_in_Wartime_Reading,_Berkshire,_England,_UK,_1945_D25360.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/A_Picture_of_a_Southern_Town-_Life_in_Wartime_Reading%2C_Berkshire%2C_England%2C_UK%2C_1945_D25360.jpg/220px-A_Picture_of_a_Southern_Town-_Life_in_Wartime_Reading%2C_Berkshire%2C_England%2C_UK%2C_1945_D25360.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="209" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/A_Picture_of_a_Southern_Town-_Life_in_Wartime_Reading%2C_Berkshire%2C_England%2C_UK%2C_1945_D25360.jpg/330px-A_Picture_of_a_Southern_Town-_Life_in_Wartime_Reading%2C_Berkshire%2C_England%2C_UK%2C_1945_D25360.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/A_Picture_of_a_Southern_Town-_Life_in_Wartime_Reading%2C_Berkshire%2C_England%2C_UK%2C_1945_D25360.jpg/440px-A_Picture_of_a_Southern_Town-_Life_in_Wartime_Reading%2C_Berkshire%2C_England%2C_UK%2C_1945_D25360.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1868" data-file-height="1772" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:A_Picture_of_a_Southern_Town-_Life_in_Wartime_Reading,_Berkshire,_England,_UK,_1945_D25360.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Interior of a Woolworths store in Reading in 1945</div></div></div>
<p>After the First World War, the company continued to expand with the opening of further branches. By 1923 there were 130 branches, and William Lawrence Stephenson (1880–1963) became managing director. He implemented a strategy of major expansion, with the company buying or building freehold properties. Many of the stores had distinctive <a href="/wiki/Glazed_architectural_terra-cotta" title="Glazed architectural terra-cotta">faience</a> tiled <a href="/wiki/Art_deco" class="mw-redirect" title="Art deco">art deco</a> frontages. The expansion was funded entirely out of earnings and without any borrowing or further capitalisation. The 400th branch, at Southport, Lancashire, opened on 12 July 1930, and the company was floated on the London Stock Exchange in 1931. The US parent company reduced its holding in the company to 51.7% at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> In 1934, the 600th store was opened, in <a href="/wiki/Wallington,_London" title="Wallington, London">Wallington</a> in <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">Surrey</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Expansion was effectively suspended between 1940 and 1950, owing to the Second World War and post-war restrictions, but then resumed. The 800th branch, at Wilton Road, Victoria, London, opened in September 1953. On 22 May 1958, the 1000th branch (known as "Portslade" to distinguish it from the existing Hove branch) opened in Boundary Road, Hove. The peak of 1,141 branches was reached in the late 1960s. From then until the US parent sold out in 1982, a number of branches were closed and sold, and at the time that ownership shifted to the UK, there were about 1,000 branches.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Woolworths tried the large out-of-town store or <a href="/wiki/Hypermarket" title="Hypermarket">hypermarket</a> format in the 1960s with the <a href="/wiki/Woolco" title="Woolco">Woolco</a> stores.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> While some of these stores were closed, the majority were sold to the Dee Corporation in the early 1980s and re-opened as Gateway hypermarkets, later being taken over by <a href="/wiki/Asda" title="Asda">Asda</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One of Woolworths' flagship stores, on <a href="/wiki/Briggate" title="Briggate">Briggate</a> in <a href="/wiki/Leeds" title="Leeds">Leeds</a>, suffered a major fire in 1969. The store, which opened in 1913, was spread over four floors and sustained extensive damage, requiring a total refit. The store was not open to the public at the time and all staff were evacuated, avoiding fatalities, although some staff suffered minor injuries. It took several hours for the fire to be fully extinguished.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Split_from_parent_company_and_after">Split from parent company and after</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Split from parent company and after">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Woolworth_(horizontal).svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/Woolworth_%28horizontal%29.svg/220px-Woolworth_%28horizontal%29.svg.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="23" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/Woolworth_%28horizontal%29.svg/330px-Woolworth_%28horizontal%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/4a/Woolworth_%28horizontal%29.svg/440px-Woolworth_%28horizontal%29.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="12482" data-file-height="1282" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Woolworth_(horizontal).svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Woolworth logo used from the 1970s until around 1985</div></div></div>
<p>In 1982, the British Woolworths was acquired by Paternoster Stores Ltd, the forerunner of <a href="/wiki/Kingfisher_plc" title="Kingfisher plc">Kingfisher plc</a>. Woolworths Group plc was formed by the demerger of Kingfisher's general merchandise business,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> and began trading as a listed company on the <a href="/wiki/London_Stock_Exchange" title="London Stock Exchange">London Stock Exchange</a> on 28 August 2001, using the symbol WLW.
</p><p>In October 1984, the <a href="/wiki/Woolworths_(Ireland)" title="Woolworths (Ireland)">Woolworths stores in the Republic of Ireland</a> were closed. In August 1996, market research was undertaken by Woolworths investigating opportunities to re-enter the Republic of Ireland market. About 32 potential locations were identified that could support a Woolworths store. However, the project did not proceed beyond the market research phase.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Johnstone_High_Street_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1255431.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Johnstone_High_Street_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1255431.jpg/220px-Johnstone_High_Street_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1255431.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Johnstone_High_Street_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1255431.jpg/330px-Johnstone_High_Street_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1255431.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Johnstone_High_Street_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1255431.jpg/440px-Johnstone_High_Street_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1255431.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Johnstone_High_Street_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1255431.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Woolworths store in Johnstone, April 1989</div></div></div>
<p>During the 1980s, management rationalised merchandise lines into clearly defined categories: entertainment, home, kids (toys and clothing) and confectionery. Many Woolworths branches were downsized during this time. Older branches in major cities were sometimes almost as large as the major department stores nearby. In 1987, for example, Woolworths left its five-floor branch on Briggate in Leeds (now occupied by <a href="/wiki/House_of_Fraser" title="House of Fraser">House of Fraser</a>), which it had occupied since 1913, and kept only its smaller single-level branch in the <a href="/wiki/Merrion_Centre_(Leeds)" class="mw-redirect" title="Merrion Centre (Leeds)">Merrion Centre</a>. This was in an area of the <a href="/wiki/Leeds_city_centre" title="Leeds city centre">city centre</a> which had less pedestrian traffic, of shoppers who were generally on a lower budget.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the late 1990s, the management extended the Woolworths brand into other retail formats and alternative channels to accelerate growth by taking advantage of changing retail trends. Some larger-format stores were opened under the <a href="/wiki/Big_W_(UK_Chain)" class="mw-redirect" title="Big W (UK Chain)">Big W</a> brand, similar to <a href="/wiki/Wal-Mart" class="mw-redirect" title="Wal-Mart">Wal-Mart</a> in the US. Although it was successful at the beginning, the format failed to catch on; the original plan had relied upon leveraging the involvement of other Kingfisher group retailers, but following the de-merger this was no longer possible.<sup id="cite_ref-BigW_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BigW-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Following a period of losses, Woolworths confirmed in 2004 it would abandon the Big W concept.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> The group sold 7 of the 21 Big W stores in 2005 to Tesco and Asda.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The gross internal floor area of the remaining sites was reduced to an optimum trading size of around 40,000 to 50,000 square feet (4,600&#160;m<sup>2</sup>). Following this, they were rebranded as Woolworths Out of Town stores.<sup id="cite_ref-BigW_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BigW-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_years">Later years</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Later years">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The newly independent Woolworths faced severe competitive and financial pressures. The market for physical copies of music, one of Woolworths' main money spinners, shrank in the early 21st century; specialist music chains such as <a href="/wiki/Our_Price" title="Our Price">Our Price</a> collapsed. The major supermarket chains expanded into many of Woolworths' product areas, and the fast-expanding <a href="/wiki/Wilkinson_(retailer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Wilkinson (retailer)">Wilkinson</a> challenged it directly on the high street.
</p><p>Woolworths did not generally follow the trend started in the 1980s of opening stores at out-of-town relocations. One of its few out-of-town stores opened at the <a href="/wiki/Merry_Hill_Shopping_Centre" title="Merry Hill Shopping Centre">Merry Hill Shopping Centre</a> in 1989, but this closed within a few years due to disappointing trade.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Interior_of_Woolworths,_Leigh_Road,_Eastleigh_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1060629.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Interior_of_Woolworths%2C_Leigh_Road%2C_Eastleigh_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1060629.jpg/200px-Interior_of_Woolworths%2C_Leigh_Road%2C_Eastleigh_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1060629.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="252" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Interior_of_Woolworths%2C_Leigh_Road%2C_Eastleigh_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1060629.jpg/300px-Interior_of_Woolworths%2C_Leigh_Road%2C_Eastleigh_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1060629.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Interior_of_Woolworths%2C_Leigh_Road%2C_Eastleigh_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1060629.jpg/400px-Interior_of_Woolworths%2C_Leigh_Road%2C_Eastleigh_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1060629.jpg 2x" data-file-width="508" data-file-height="640" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Interior_of_Woolworths,_Leigh_Road,_Eastleigh_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1060629.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Woolworths became well known for its "pick 'n' mix" sweet selections.</div></div></div>
<p>In an attempt to raise the group's corporate profile, under the chairmanship of <a href="/wiki/Gerald_Corbett" title="Gerald Corbett">Gerald Corbett</a>, Woolworths sponsored a show garden at the <a href="/wiki/The_Royal_Horticultural_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="The Royal Horticultural Society">RHS</a> <a href="/wiki/Chelsea_Flower_Show" title="Chelsea Flower Show">Chelsea Flower Show</a> in 2004. Designed by a group of graduate students from Pickard School of Garden Design, including Karl Rushen, Catherine Gamble, Bella Montgomery, Darryl Moore and Rob Whitehead, the contemporary-formal style garden was awarded a silver medal.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Woolworths_Group_share_price_2006-2008.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f4/Woolworths_Group_share_price_2006-2008.png/220px-Woolworths_Group_share_price_2006-2008.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="128" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f4/Woolworths_Group_share_price_2006-2008.png/330px-Woolworths_Group_share_price_2006-2008.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f4/Woolworths_Group_share_price_2006-2008.png/440px-Woolworths_Group_share_price_2006-2008.png 2x" data-file-width="1630" data-file-height="952" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Woolworths_Group_share_price_2006-2008.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Woolworths Group plc share price dropped gradually between 2006 and 2008</div></div></div>
<p>In the middle of 2006 the business launched an in-store collection service for items ordered on their website or in-store, to complement the already established in-store ordering system. In late September 2006, the "Big Red Book" was launched. This was designed to be a direct competitor of the <a href="/wiki/Argos_(retailer)" title="Argos (retailer)">Argos</a> catalogue.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Woolworths launched the <i>WorthIt!</i> brand as a value range in 2007.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> The first advertising campaign for the brand aired on 15 June 2007 and introduced the characters of Wooly the sheep and Worth the sheepdog.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Further advertising campaigns featured celebrities such as <a href="/wiki/Rolf_Harris" title="Rolf Harris">Rolf Harris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jackie_Chan" title="Jackie Chan">Jackie Chan</a><sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Kelly_Osbourne" title="Kelly Osbourne">Kelly Osbourne</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> The brand covered a wide variety of products including confectionery, electricals, alcohol, jewellery, perfumes and clothing.
</p><p>Woolworths had a strongly <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">unionised</a> workforce, with shop stewards such as Paul Thompson of <a href="/wiki/Unite_the_Union" title="Unite the Union">Unite</a> (formerly the <a href="/wiki/Transport_and_General_Workers%27_Union" title="Transport and General Workers&#39; Union">Transport and General Workers' Union</a>) particularly active in the north of the UK.
</p><p>In July 2008, the board rejected a bid from Iceland founder Malcolm Walker to buy Woolworths' 819 stores for £50m. Walker's bid did not include Entertainment UK or the stake in 2 Entertain, and also avoided taking on Woolworths' debt and pension liabilities.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 12 August 2008, Woolworths Group announced the appointment of Steve Johnson, former <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">chief executive officer</a> of <a href="/wiki/Focus_DIY" title="Focus DIY">Focus DIY</a>, to the post of chief executive.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> He replaced Trevor Bish-Jones, who had left in June.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> Woolworths scrapped its interim dividend in September 2008, after it announced a pre-tax loss of £99.7m for the six months to 2 August. At the same time, Johnson outlined a possible turnaround plan to sell 120 stores, axe a quarter of its products, reduce web operations and cut jobs.<sup id="cite_ref-sugar_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sugar-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>At that time, the retailer's largest shareholder was Iranian property developer Ardeshir Naghshineh, with a 10.2% stake. A consortium led by Icelandic investor <a href="/wiki/Baugur_Group" title="Baugur Group">Baugur</a>, called Unity owned a 10% stake in Woolworths.<sup id="cite_ref-sugar_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sugar-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> In October 2008 <a href="/wiki/Alan_Sugar" title="Alan Sugar">Sir Alan Sugar</a>, founder of electronics firm <a href="/wiki/Amstrad" title="Amstrad">Amstrad</a>, increased his stake in Woolworths to around 4%.<sup id="cite_ref-sugar_47-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sugar-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Theo_Paphitis" title="Theo Paphitis">Theo Paphitis</a>, owner of stationery retailer <a href="/wiki/Ryman" title="Ryman">Ryman</a>, also stated his interest in the company.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Administration_and_closure">Administration and closure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Administration and closure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><b>2008–2009</b><br />
From September 2008, the world entered into a <a href="/wiki/Global_financial_crisis_of_2008" class="mw-redirect" title="Global financial crisis of 2008">severe financial crisis</a> with reducing availability of <a href="/wiki/Credit_(finance)" class="mw-redirect" title="Credit (finance)">credit</a> and reduced consumer spending.
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Woolworths_Hounslow_-_Closing_Down_Sale_-_Exterior.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Woolworths_Hounslow_-_Closing_Down_Sale_-_Exterior.jpg/220px-Woolworths_Hounslow_-_Closing_Down_Sale_-_Exterior.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Woolworths_Hounslow_-_Closing_Down_Sale_-_Exterior.jpg/330px-Woolworths_Hounslow_-_Closing_Down_Sale_-_Exterior.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Woolworths_Hounslow_-_Closing_Down_Sale_-_Exterior.jpg/440px-Woolworths_Hounslow_-_Closing_Down_Sale_-_Exterior.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2304" data-file-height="1728" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Woolworths_Hounslow_-_Closing_Down_Sale_-_Exterior.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Queues form in <a href="/wiki/Hounslow" title="Hounslow">Hounslow</a> for the final sale</div></div></div>
<p>On 19 November 2008, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i> reported that the Woolworths' retail business was a target for restructuring specialist <a href="/wiki/Hilco_UK" class="mw-redirect" title="Hilco UK">Hilco UK</a>, who would buy the retail arm for a nominal £1;<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> this was confirmed the same day.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> The deal would have left Woolworths Group with its profitable distribution and publishing businesses and a reduced debt load. Ardeshir Naghshineh criticised the plan, recommending instead that the company sell some of the stores to raise more funds.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The group's banks, <a href="/wiki/Ally_Financial" title="Ally Financial">GMAC</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_Ireland" title="Bank of Ireland">Burdale</a>, rejected the deal<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> and recalled their loans, forcing the group to place the retail business and Entertainment UK into <a href="/wiki/Administration_(law)" title="Administration (law)">administration</a>. On 26 November 2008, the trading of shares in Woolworths PLC was suspended, and Neville Kahn, Dan Butters and Nick Dargan of <a href="/wiki/Deloitte" title="Deloitte">Deloitte</a> were appointed joint administrators.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC26Nov08er_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC26Nov08er-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> When the company entered administration it had a debt of £385 million. The administrators announced that they were aiming to keep the company as a going concern over the crucial <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> period, although analysts feared that any heavy discounting would create a <a href="/wiki/Domino_effect" title="Domino effect">domino effect</a> and drag down other high street retailers. Deloitte later announced they had received "substantial interest" in Woolworths.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>When news of Woolworths' entry into administration was widely publicised, <a href="/wiki/National_Lottery_(United_Kingdom)" title="National Lottery (United Kingdom)">National Lottery</a> operator <a href="/wiki/Camelot_Group" title="Camelot Group">Camelot Group</a> immediately suspended Woolworths from selling their lottery tickets and scratch cards, as well as preventing claimants from redeeming prizes at the stores.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 5 December 2008, Woolworths recorded their greatest single day takings of £27 million, and axed 450 head office and support staff jobs.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> A <i>closing-down sale</i> started on 11 December.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Woolies_croydon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Woolies_croydon.jpg/200px-Woolies_croydon.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Woolies_croydon.jpg/300px-Woolies_croydon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Woolies_croydon.jpg/400px-Woolies_croydon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="1600" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Woolies_croydon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Woolworths in <a href="/wiki/Croydon" title="Croydon">Croydon</a>, shelves bare, on last day of Final Clearance Sale</div></div></div>
<p>On 17 December 2008, administrators announced that all 807 Woolworths stores would close by 5 January 2009 (later changed to 6 January), with 27,000 job losses.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> Deloitte's Neville Kahn also said that it was unclear how much of Woolworths' debt would be paid. In the last few days of trading discounts of up to 90% were offered, and a number of stores sold all of their stock, many selling all of their fixtures and fittings too.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The stores were closed in phases, and the final two closing days were moved back a day to try to sell more of the remaining stock and to ease logistics of closing.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:The_last_days_of_Bangor%27s_Woolworths_store_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1089037.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/The_last_days_of_Bangor%27s_Woolworths_store_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1089037.jpg/220px-The_last_days_of_Bangor%27s_Woolworths_store_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1089037.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/The_last_days_of_Bangor%27s_Woolworths_store_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1089037.jpg/330px-The_last_days_of_Bangor%27s_Woolworths_store_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1089037.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/The_last_days_of_Bangor%27s_Woolworths_store_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1089037.jpg/440px-The_last_days_of_Bangor%27s_Woolworths_store_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1089037.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="480" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:The_last_days_of_Bangor%27s_Woolworths_store_-_geograph.org.uk_-_1089037.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Woolworths in <a href="/wiki/Bangor,_Gwynedd" title="Bangor, Gwynedd">Bangor, Gwynedd</a> days before closing. It was replaced by a <a href="/wiki/Boots_UK" title="Boots UK">Boots</a> store.</div></div></div>207 stores closed on 27 December 2008</li>
<li>37 closed on 29 December</li>
<li>164 closed on 30 December</li>
<li>200 closed on 3 January 2009</li>
<li>remaining stores (199) closed on 6 January 2009</li></ul>
<p>The former chief executive of Kingfisher, Woolworths' former parent company, and Ardeshir Naghshineh, a major shareholder of Woolworths, criticised the closures.<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 19 January 2009, the parent company, Woolworths Group, announced its intention to also enter administration, as it could no longer pay its debts.<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> The application was heard by the High Court on 27 January, and Woolworths Group PLC entered administration.<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The trade unions complained of the collective redundancies and they started various legal actions before the UK tribunals based on the absence of proper consultation of the employee representatives. The UK Court of Appeal referred the case to the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Justice_of_the_European_Union" title="Court of Justice of the European Union">Court of Justice of the European Union</a> which partially disagreed with the unions by an <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.lagbd.org/index.php/Woolworths_CJEU_decision:_how_to_dismiss_4,440_employees_without_consulting_staff_representatives_%28eu%29">important decision of 30 April 2015</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The administrators announced on 10 December 2008, that they were having difficulty selling the company as a going concern, and as a result some stores might close before the end of the month. Talks were still progressing to sell individual stores and leases to a number of retailers, said to include the supermarket chains <a href="/wiki/Morrisons" title="Morrisons">Morrisons</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tesco" title="Tesco">Tesco</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asda" title="Asda">Asda</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sainsbury%27s" title="Sainsbury&#39;s">Sainsbury's</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Co-operative_Group" title="The Co-operative Group">The Co-operative Group</a> and <a href="/wiki/Poundland" title="Poundland">Poundland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In December 2008, Woolworths executive Tony Page was trying to raise around £30,000,000 to relaunch the brand after closure with a chain of 125 stores.<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> However, a deal could not be met in time. The government were also asked in a final attempt to make the deal but without success.
</p><p>Then-Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Brown" title="Gordon Brown">Gordon Brown</a> noted that the Government had considered saving Woolworths, but they concluded that it was a "financially unviable" business.<sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The supermarket chain <a href="/wiki/Iceland_(supermarket)" title="Iceland (supermarket)">Iceland</a> bought 51 of the stores on 9 January 2009 for an undisclosed sum.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> Baugur, one of the major shareholders of Woolworths, partly owns Iceland, and Baugur UK itself entered administration in February.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> A further five former stores in Wales were bought by <a href="/wiki/The_Original_Factory_Shop" title="The Original Factory Shop">The Original Factory Shop</a> in April 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup> Across the UK stores were sporadically replaced by other retailers such as <a href="/wiki/Next_plc" title="Next plc">Next</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/W_Boyes_%26_Co" class="mw-redirect" title="W Boyes &amp; Co">Boyes</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Boots_UK" title="Boots UK">Boots</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-76" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Primark" title="Primark">Primark</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/TK_Maxx" title="TK Maxx">TK Maxx</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Home_Bargains" title="Home Bargains">Home Bargains</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/W_H_Smith" class="mw-redirect" title="W H Smith">W H Smith</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In August 2010, the BBC reported that over 300 (i.e., approximately 40%) of former Woolworths stores remained empty, and that the largest group now using former Woolworths stores were discount retailers- such as Poundland.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> By January 2012, it was reported that there were still 105 empty former Woolworths stores, and a further 68 had been demolished.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alworths">Alworths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Alworths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Alworths" title="Alworths">Alworths</a></div>
<p>According to press reports on 17 February 2009, Tony Page planned to open a chain of shops adopting the Woolworths format, but under a different name, with an initial 50 stores under the Alworths fascia.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> A store opened under the Alworths name on 5 November 2009 in <a href="/wiki/Didcot" title="Didcot">Didcot</a>, on the site of a former Woolworths store, without the involvement of Page.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> By January 2011, 18 stores had been opened, although in April the company also entered administration and closed.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wellworths">Wellworths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Wellworths">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wellchester" title="Wellchester">Wellchester</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Wellworths.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8e/Wellworths.svg/250px-Wellworths.svg.png" decoding="async" width="250" height="60" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8e/Wellworths.svg/375px-Wellworths.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8e/Wellworths.svg/500px-Wellworths.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="125" data-file-height="30" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Wellworths.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Wellworths' logo from 2009–2010</div></div></div>
<p>The Woolworths store in <a href="/wiki/Dorchester,_Dorset" title="Dorchester, Dorset">Dorchester, Dorset</a> was reopened as an independent business named <i>Wellworths</i> by the store manager Claire Robertson. The store was officially opened by <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_2" title="BBC Radio 2">BBC Radio 2</a> DJ <a href="/wiki/Chris_Evans_(presenter)" title="Chris Evans (presenter)">Chris Evans</a> on 11 March 2009. It was later renamed <i>Wellchester</i>.
</p><p>Wellchester ceased trading in August 2012, after store manager Claire Robertson left to set up her own retail consultancy business.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Woolworths.co.uk">Woolworths.co.uk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Woolworths.co.uk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Woolworths.co.uk" title="Woolworths.co.uk">Woolworths.co.uk</a></div>
<p>The Woolworths brand and domain was bought by <a href="/wiki/Shop_Direct_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Shop Direct Group">Shop Direct Group</a>, owned by <a href="/wiki/David_and_Frederick_Barclay" title="David and Frederick Barclay">Sir David and Sir Frederick Barclay</a>, on 2 February 2009. The company announced it would relaunch Woolworths as an <a href="/wiki/Electronic_commerce" class="mw-redirect" title="Electronic commerce">online retail store</a>. The website was launched on 26 June 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-87" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> It was entirely independent of the former Woolworths Group.  It has been closed until further notice since June 2015. On 13 October 2015, the company finally dissolved and the website went defunct. 
In April 2017, multiple news sources reported that Woolworths may possibly be re-opening, nearly nine years since it closed, after former director Tony Page confirmed interest in acquiring the Woolworths name from Shop Direct.  <sup id="cite_ref-88" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup>
As of August 2018, the 'woolworths.co.uk' URL redirects to <a href="/wiki/Very_(online_retailer)" title="Very (online retailer)">Very.co.uk</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Incidents">Incidents</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Incidents">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Disasters">Disasters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Disasters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span id="New_Cross.2C_London"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Cross,_London">New Cross, London</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: New Cross, London">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Many branches of Woolworths suffered severe bomb damage and even destruction during the <a href="/wiki/Luftwaffe" title="Luftwaffe">Luftwaffe</a> attacks in the early part of the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>. However it was towards the end of the war that the largest civilian loss of life due to direct enemy fire in Britain during the conflict occurred when, at lunchtime on 25<sup id="cite_ref-89" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> November 1944, a German <a href="/wiki/V-2_rocket" title="V-2 rocket">V-2 rocket</a> fell on a packed Woolworths store in <a href="/wiki/New_Cross" title="New Cross">New Cross Road</a>, killing 168 people (including 15 children), injuring 122 others and razing the building to the ground. The neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Co-operative_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Co-operative Group">London Co-operative Society</a> store was also demolished in the attack.
</p><p>The store was especially busy as news of a delivery of hard-to-obtain <a href="/wiki/Saucepan" class="mw-redirect" title="Saucepan">saucepans</a> generated huge crowds, many of whom were queueing outside the store at the time of the rocket's impact.
</p><p>Planning and economic restrictions after the war meant Woolworths did not build a replacement store on the site until 1960; this closed in 1984. It was reported that some employees there felt the building was haunted.<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lewisham_Council" class="mw-redirect" title="Lewisham Council">Lewisham Council</a> and Woolworths erected a plaque on the site commemorating those who died that day. There is now an <a href="/wiki/Iceland_(supermarket)" title="Iceland (supermarket)">Iceland</a> store on the site.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_safety">Fire safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Fire safety">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the period from 1971 to 1981 a series of fires occurred in Woolworth's premises which brought into sharp focus the company's complacent attitude towards fire safety, a policy which culminated in a number of fatalities.
</p>
<h4><span id="Central_Warehouse.2C_Rochdale"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_Warehouse,_Rochdale">Central Warehouse, Rochdale</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Central Warehouse, Rochdale">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In May 1971 a fire broke out at the main distribution depot at Rochdale, Lancashire. The site was split into three sections, along with an administration block which housed the company’s new mainframe computer system and all stock and accounting records. The fire soon spread due to the failure of the sprinkler system and led to administration staff frantically dis-assembling the computer and passing parts of it, along with stock cards and other records out of windows onto waiting lorries.
</p><p>Over 100 fire fighters attended the blaze and they managed to save two thirds of the building with no loss of life. The resultant insurance payout of £3m was the highest of its kind in the north-west of England at that time.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_91-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="High_Street.2C_Colchester"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="High_Street,_Colchester">High Street, Colchester</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: High Street, Colchester">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In the Autumn of 1973 a fire broke out in the stockroom of the store at 40-50 High Street, Colchester. The blaze soon spread to the rest of the store and the building was totally destroyed. Although all customers and staff were evacuated, subsequent findings blamed a lack of sprinkler system (which was not and still isn't a legal requirement) and poor procedures in place for staff to deal with fires and evacuations.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_91-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>  
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Central_Manchester">Central Manchester</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Central Manchester">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Woolworth%27s,_Manchester_-_geograph-2182393-by-David-Dixon.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Woolworth%27s%2C_Manchester_-_geograph-2182393-by-David-Dixon.jpg/220px-Woolworth%27s%2C_Manchester_-_geograph-2182393-by-David-Dixon.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Woolworth%27s%2C_Manchester_-_geograph-2182393-by-David-Dixon.jpg/330px-Woolworth%27s%2C_Manchester_-_geograph-2182393-by-David-Dixon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Woolworth%27s%2C_Manchester_-_geograph-2182393-by-David-Dixon.jpg/440px-Woolworth%27s%2C_Manchester_-_geograph-2182393-by-David-Dixon.jpg 2x" data-file-width="897" data-file-height="597" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Woolworth%27s,_Manchester_-_geograph-2182393-by-David-Dixon.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Image taken a few days after the fire at Woolworth's Manchester store</div></div></div> 
<p>A serious fire erupted just after 1<span class="nowrap">&#160;</span>pm on 8 May 1979 at the <a href="/wiki/Manchester" title="Manchester">Manchester</a> store opposite <a href="/wiki/Piccadilly_Gardens" title="Piccadilly Gardens">Piccadilly Gardens</a>, said at the time to be the largest Woolworths in Europe, with six floors plus two basement levels. The fire, which started in the second floor furnishing department, killed ten shoppers and one member of staff;<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> of whom three were found just six feet away from an exit with another three bodies nearby. Of the 12 calls made to the fire service that day, none came from the store itself. It is believed that the fire was started by a damaged electrical cable, which had furniture stacked in front of it.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> An inquiry showed that, although the store's fire precautions met all legal requirements, the spread of the fire and the high number of casualties were in part due to the absence of measures such as a <a href="/wiki/Fire_sprinkler_system" title="Fire sprinkler system">fire sprinkler system</a> to stop the spread of the fire from the furniture department, and the use of <a href="/wiki/Polyurethane" title="Polyurethane">polyurethane foam</a> in the furnishings, a material which is highly inflammable and highly toxic but cheap and at that time legal in furniture; however, this would have consequences for later legislation.
</p><p>The second floor was gutted by the fire, while the third floor suffered severe smoke damage; the ground, first and second floors all received extensive water damage when the fire was extinguished.  Due to the loss of life and devastation to the Manchester store, the Fire Research Station conducted a number of tests to develop sprinkler systems that could handle a similar large department store fire.  However, there is still no requirement for United Kingdom retailers to have a sprinkler system in place, with many preferring to focus on evacuation procedures rather than fire containment. The catastrophe also resulted in modifications to the Fire Precautions Act and was among the factors that led to the ban on the use of polyurethane foam in home furnishings (a long-time concern of the Fire Service) forcing furniture manufacturers to develop new fabrics and materials for sofas and other items.
</p><p>The fire brought graphic images into the public consciousness (including footage of office girls trapped behind barred windows on the top floor) due to the store's location next to the then studios of <a href="/wiki/BBC_North_West" title="BBC North West">BBC Manchester</a> (above the National Westminster Bank) and near to those of <a href="/wiki/Granada_Television" class="mw-redirect" title="Granada Television">Granada Television</a>, the offices of the <i><a href="/wiki/Manchester_Evening_News" title="Manchester Evening News">Manchester Evening News</a></i> and the northern offices of several national newspapers.
</p><p>The disaster has become a significant object of study for academics interested in the behaviour of people in emergency situations, after research showed a number of customers (predominately in the public restaurant area) refused to leave despite the sounding of alarms, requests from staff and even the smell and visibility of <a href="/wiki/Smoke" title="Smoke">smoke</a>; some even continued to queue at an abandoned check-out.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup> The majority of those who perished were in this area.
</p><p>The store was re-opened, but closed in 1986.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> The site became an <a href="/wiki/Amusement_arcade" title="Amusement arcade">amusement arcade</a> for many years, until the building owners evicted the tenants in favour of a building renovation to house a 157-room Travelodge, a Morrisons convenience store, and Zizzi and Nandos restaurants.<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-98" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Woolworths did have several smaller outlets during the 1990s which sold <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a> and <a href="/wiki/Confectionery" title="Confectionery">confectionery</a>. Smaller outlets with a similar format were also tried at the <a href="/wiki/Sheffield" title="Sheffield">Sheffield</a> <a href="/wiki/Meadowhall_Shopping_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Meadowhall Shopping Centre">Meadowhall Shopping Centre</a>, but closed in 2003; the Manchester Music and Video store was superseded by a larger <a href="/wiki/Music_and_Video_Club" title="Music and Video Club">MVC</a> store, owned by Woolworths Group.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Worcester">Worcester</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Worcester">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>On October 1st 1979, the Woolworths store located in Worcester's High Street was severely damaged in a large fire. There were no casualties but the store remained closed for several months until it re-opened in 1980. It was later discovered that the fire was purposely started by a disgruntled staff member, and was worsened by the fact there was no sprinkler system in the store.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Wimbledon_Broadway">Wimbledon Broadway</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Wimbledon Broadway">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1981 a fire was discovered in a storeroom at the store in Wimbledon. Better staff training as a result of the Manchester fire led to a successful evacuation but the building was totally destroyed by the blaze. A fireman was killed when he and two colleagues became trapped when the upper levels of the building collapsed.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_91-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>  
</p><p>As a result of the damage caused to the reputation of the business by these events, the American parent owners decided to sell the British operation to Paternoster Stores, which eventually was renamed Kingfisher. The company actively promoted the use of smoke detectors after these fires, a policy which helped increase their usage and subsequently bring down the cost of such items.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_91-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Terrorism">Terrorism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Terrorism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As a result of the targeting of high-profile British businesses in <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Republican_Army" title="Irish Republican Army">Irish Republican Army</a> during <a href="/wiki/The_Troubles" title="The Troubles">The Troubles</a>, a number of Woolworth stores suffered significant damage.
</p>
<h4><span id="High_Street.2C_Belfast"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="High_Street,_Belfast">High Street, Belfast</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: High Street, Belfast">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Being the flagship Woolworths' store in Ulster this suffered a series of incendiary attacks, with one such attack in 1972 gutting the store. Due to telephoned warnings no-one was killed in any of these attacks.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_91-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span id="Bangor.2C_County_Down"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Bangor,_County_Down">Bangor, County Down</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Bangor, County Down">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Woolworths' store at 18/22 Main Street, Bangor, County Down, Northern Ireland (Store No. 380) was targeted by terrorists on 30 March 1974 as part of co-ordinated incendiary bomb attack on the town centre.
</p><p>At 5pm, a telephone warning was received that fifteen incendiary devices had been placed in the town centre and were due to explode in 30 minutes. Immediate and successful efforts were made by the Police to evacuate the commercial centre of the town, however there was inadequate time to prevent the devices from exploding. The Woolworths store was badly damaged after a device exploded on the sales-floor. A policeman suffered concussion after he was blown off his feet by the resultant blast from the device and a female civilian was cut by flying debris.<sup id="cite_ref-99" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other stores targeted in the attack included the town's Co-operative Department Store and <a href="/wiki/Wellworths" title="Wellworths">FA Wellworth Department Store</a>. The town's Woolworths' store was demolished after the attack. A new 8,000 square feet (740&#160;m<sup>2</sup>) store was built on the same site, which reopened in the mid 1970s.
</p><p>The rebuilt store suffered minor damage after a 200&#160;lb car bomb exploded a short distance away, near the town's FA Wellworth's store on the evening of 21 October 1992. Nobody was injured in the explosion, which occurred after most stores in the town centre had closed for the day. However, significant damage was caused to the entrance area of the Woolworths' store, with windows being blown out, the porch roof being destroyed and a small quantity of stock toward the front of the store being damaged. Additionally, minor structural damage was caused to the store's stockroom with two internal portioning walls adjacent to the Generator Room and Fixtures' Store having to be rebuilt. The store recommenced trading on 23 October 1992.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Woolworths, for many years, was a leader in the UK music industry. In the 1950s and well into the 1960s, Woolworths issued recordings available only via their stores on their own label <a href="/wiki/Embassy_Records" title="Embassy Records">Embassy Records</a>, produced and manufactured by <a href="/wiki/Oriole_Records_(UK)" title="Oriole Records (UK)">Oriole Records</a>. These releases were double-sided singles featuring two cover versions of current hit singles sold at a much cheaper price. This venture was very successful at the time, but was eventually killed off when other record companies started to issue compilation albums. However, Woolworths remained in the music business selling a wide range of singles and albums, and remained the UK's Number 1 music retailer well into the 1990s. Even successful nationwide music specialists stores such as <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Megastores" title="Virgin Megastores">Virgin Megastores</a> and <a href="/wiki/HMV" title="HMV">HMV</a> did not overtake Woolworths during this time. They later suffered from strong competition in this field from the large supermarket chains <a href="/wiki/Tesco" title="Tesco">Tesco</a> and <a href="/wiki/Asda" title="Asda">Asda</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cafes">Cafes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Cafes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In early days many Woolworths stores had cafes in them. However, as the years went on, and many larger stores were either closed or downscaled, fewer stores had cafes in them. When the stores finally ceased trading in 2008/9, only around 10% of the stores had cafes in them. These were usually located at the back of the stores or, when a store had a second sales floor, they were located either in the basement or upstairs on the first floor. They sold the usual range of hot and cold drinks, with hot food available, including breakfasts and lunch-time meals.
</p><p>Stores with cafes in them included<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Aylesbury, Basingstoke, Milton Keynes, Blackpool, Brighton, Bromley, Exeter, Coventry, Cambridge, Dereham, Doncaster, Grantham, Hounslow, High Wycombe, Ipswich, Paignton, Gloucester, Huntingdon, Worcester, Windsor, Plymouth, Portsmouth, Leigh, Lancaster, Leicester, Wrexham, Leamington Spa, Maidstone, Newquay, Peterborough, Salisbury, Scarborough, Sheffield, Southport, St Helens, Wolverhampton and Yeovil. The Big W stores also had cafes.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Subsidiaries">Subsidiaries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Subsidiaries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Entertainment_UK">Entertainment UK</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Entertainment UK">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Entertainment_UK" title="Entertainment UK">Entertainment UK</a></div>
<p>Entertainment UK (EUK) was founded as Record Merchandisers Limited in 1966 by EMI Records to distribute music to non-specialist retailers. It later became a joint venture between a number of record companies. Woolworths became Entertainment UK's largest customer and in 1986 Record Merchandisers Limited was acquired by Kingfisher plc. Record Merchandisers was renamed Entertainment UK in 1988.
</p><p>EUK became the property of Woolworths Group plc after the de-merger from Kingfisher in 2001. In 2006, Woolworths Group acquired Total Home Entertainment Distribution Limited (THE) and combined it with EUK.<sup id="cite_ref-100" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup> In November 2007 the company acquired the book wholesaler and distributor <a href="/wiki/Bertram_Books" class="mw-redirect" title="Bertram Books">Bertram Books</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>EUK was the main supplier of <a href="/wiki/Zavvi_(retailer)" title="Zavvi (retailer)">Zavvi</a> under an exclusive supply deal.<sup id="cite_ref-102" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup> As a result of EUK entering into administration, on 24 December the music retailer was also forced into administration as it was unable to source stock on favourable terms direct from suppliers.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup> Zavvi later closed entirely.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Streets_Online">Streets Online</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Streets Online">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Streets Online, founded in 1996 by Stephen Cole, was one of the pioneers of online retailing in the UK.  The company was the name behind the online bookseller Alphabetstreet and music site Audiostreet. 85% of the company was bought out by the Kingfisher Group in 2000 for £15.7 million,<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup> and then became part of the Woolworths Group with its de-merger in 2001. It then became responsible for the web operations of <a href="/wiki/Music_and_Video_Club" title="Music and Video Club">MVC</a> and Tesco. When Kingfisher bought this 85%, the remaining 15% was owned by Sky New Media Ventures (part of <a href="/wiki/BSkyB" class="mw-redirect" title="BSkyB">BSkyB</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup> In 2003 the company headquarters was moved to the EUK site in <a href="/wiki/Hayes,_Hillingdon" title="Hayes, Hillingdon">Hayes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-106" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2_Entertain">2 Entertain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: 2 Entertain">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/2_Entertain" title="2 Entertain">2 Entertain</a></div>
<p>2 Entertain was established as a joint venture between the Woolworths Group and <a href="/wiki/BBC_Worldwide" title="BBC Worldwide">BBC Worldwide</a>. It combined the group's former video and music publishing and television and video production business, <a href="/wiki/Video_Collection_International" class="mw-redirect" title="Video Collection International">VCI</a>, with BBC Worldwide's video publishing business. After negotiations with Woolworth Group's administrators, BBC Worldwide purchased Woolworths' 40% stake in 2 Entertain in March 2010 for £17 million, taking full ownership of the company.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Brands">Brands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Brands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Winfield">Winfield</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Winfield">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <b>Winfield</b> brand was launched by Woolworths in 1963 and continued until the 1980s. Goods sold under the brand included household cleaners, groceries, kitchenware, perfumes and other ranges e.g. <a href="/wiki/Fishing_tackle" title="Fishing tackle">fishing tackle</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-winfield_108-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-winfield-108">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chad_Valley">Chad Valley</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Chad Valley">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Chad_Valley_(toy_brand)" title="Chad Valley (toy brand)">Chad Valley (toy brand)</a></div>
<p><b>Chad Valley</b> was launched in 1991 to create an own label range of merchandise. The Chad Valley brand name, which has been in existence since 1860, is used on a range of toys and games suitable for children under 8 years old. <a href="/wiki/Home_Retail_Group" title="Home Retail Group">Home Retail Group</a>, the parent company of <a href="/wiki/Argos_(retailer)" title="Argos (retailer)">Argos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Homebase" title="Homebase">Homebase</a>, purchased the brand for £5 million on 20 January 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup> Chad Valley first appeared exclusively in the Autumn/Winter 2009 Argos catalogue.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ladybird">Ladybird</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Woolworths_Group&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: Ladybird">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Ladybird_(clothing)" title="Ladybird (clothing)">Ladybird (clothing)</a></div>
<p><b>Ladybird</b> is a brand of children's wear for children aged 0–10 years which was sold exclusively in Woolworths stores. Before the collapse of the Woolworths chain it was ranked third overall in the childrenswear market, with a market share of 5%. Woolworths purchased rights to the Ladybird brand in 1984, purchasing it outright from <a href="/wiki/Coats_plc" class="mw-redirect" title="Coats plc">Coats</a> <a href="/wiki/Viyella" title="Viyella">Viyella</a> in 2001. The brand has a history which dates back to a trading partnership beginning in 1934 between the original firm <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Pasold_%26_Son" class="mw-redirect" title="Adolf Pasold &amp; Son">Adolf Pasold &amp; Son</a><sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup> and Woolworths. On 1 February 2009, Shop Direct Group purchased the brand and whole rights from the administrators.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
