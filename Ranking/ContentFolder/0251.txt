<p>A modern <b>Belarusian name</b> of a person consists of three parts: <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>, <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">family name</a> (surname), according to the <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Slavic_naming_customs" title="Eastern Slavic naming customs">Eastern Slavic naming customs</a>, similar to <a href="/wiki/Russian_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian name">Russian names</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_name" title="Ukrainian name">Ukrainian names</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Belarusian_given_names">Belarusian given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belarusian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Belarusian given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As with most cultures, a person has a given name chosen by the parents. First names in East-<a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic languages</a> mostly originate from three sources: <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Orthodox</a> church tradition (which is itself of <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> origin), <a href="/wiki/Catholic" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholic">Catholic</a> church tradition (which is itself of <a href="/wiki/Latin_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Latin language">Latin</a> origin) and <a href="/wiki/Slavic_names" title="Slavic names">native pre-Christian Slavic origin lexicons</a>. Most names have several diminutive forms.
</p>

<h2><span id="Belarusian_family_names_.28surnames.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Belarusian_family_names_(surnames)">Belarusian family names (surnames)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Belarusian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Belarusian family names (surnames)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a> and most of the former <a href="/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Lithuanian_Commonwealth" title="Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth">Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth</a>, surnames first appeared during the late Middle Ages. They initially denoted the differences between various people living in the same town or village and bearing the same name. The conventions were similar to those of <a href="/wiki/English_surnames" class="mw-redirect" title="English surnames">English surnames</a>, using occupations, patronymic descent, geographic origins, or personal characteristics.
</p><p>Belarusian surnames, like those in most of Europe, are hereditary and generally patrilineal, i.e., passed from the father on to his children.
</p><p>Depending on the region, Belarusian surnames could have a different form and different ending.
</p><p>One very large group of surnames end with the common Slavonic suffixes -vič (wicz) and -ič (icz) (Daškievič, Šuškievič, Vajciuškievič, Mackievič, Mickievič, Misilievič) or -cki and -ski (feminine form -ckaja and -skaja: Navicki, Kalinoŭski, Piliecki, Rusiecki, Sadoŭski, Caŭloŭski, Bialiaŭski).
</p><p>One common suffix in surnames is <i>-čuk</i> (Ramančuk, Kačuk, Kavaĺčuk) or its simplified versions <i>-iuk</i> and <i>-juk</i> (Maliuk, Masiuk).
</p><p>Other group includes surnames with the suffix <i>-ka</i>, corresponding to the suffix <i>-ko</i> <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Ukrainian names">Ukrainian names</a> (Lukašenka, Jakavienka, Haponienka), <i>-onak, -jonak (-ionak)</i> (Malašonak, Manionak).
</p><p>Another suffix is <i>-jenia (-ienia)</i> (Majsienia, Astapienia, Jurčenia, Hierasimienia).
</p>
