<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a>, varying <a href="/wiki/Naming_convention" title="Naming convention">naming customs</a> are observed, whether it is given name first, family name last, a mixture of native conventions with those of neighbouring territories, etc. The most common iteration amongst <a href="/wiki/Filipino_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Filipino people">Filipinos</a> is a blend of the older Spanish system and Anglo-American conventions, where there is a distinction between the "Christian name" from "surname". The construct of having several names in the middle name convention is common to all systems, but to have multiple "first" names and only one middle and last name is a result of the blending of American and Spanish naming customs. The <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">Tagalog language</a> is one of the few national languages in Asia to use the Western name order while formally uses the eastern name order. Thus, the Philippine naming custom is coincidentally identical to the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish name">Spanish</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_name" title="Portuguese name">Portuguese name</a> customs and to an extent <a href="/wiki/Chinese_name" title="Chinese name">Chinese</a> naming customs.
</p><p>For the most part, most Filipinos abide by the Spanish system of using both paternal and maternal surnames, the latter constituting the "middle name". An example would be <i>Jose Cuyegkeng y Mangahas</i> becoming <i>Jose Mangahas Cuyegkeng</i>, where the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_naming_customs#The_particle_&quot;y&quot;_(and)" title="Spanish naming customs">particle <i><b>y</b></i></a> is used only for legal purposes and is otherwise dropped. The middle name in its natural sense would have been the second name if the person had one, but is never counted as an individual's given name.
</p><p>Filipinos tend to use middle names and surnames always.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Given_names">Given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Filipinos may have one or more official given names (as registered in their birth certificates and baptismal certificates) and various types of temporary or permanent nicknames. Filipinos have a penchant for giving themselves or each other various sorts of nicknames and monikers. Some nicknames are carried for life while others are used only with certain groups so a person can have multiple nicknames at different ages or among different groups of people.
</p>
<h3><span id="Abbreviations.2C_combinations.2C_and_elisions"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Abbreviations,_combinations,_and_elisions">Abbreviations, combinations, and elisions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Abbreviations, combinations, and elisions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Long given names can be shortened in various ways. <i>Emmanuel</i> can become <i>Eman</i>, <i>Manuel</i>, <i>Manolo</i>, <i>Manny</i>, or <i>Manoy</i>. <i>Consolación</i> has been converted to <i>Connie</i>, <i>Cons</i>, <i>Sol</i>, or <i>Chona</i>.
</p><p>Filipino women with two given names such as <i>María Cristina</i> or <i>María Victoria</i> may choose to abbreviate the very common <i>María</i> (in honour of the <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Mary" class="mw-redirect" title="Virgin Mary">Virgin Mary</a>) as <i>Ma</i>. (with a <a href="/wiki/Full_stop" title="Full stop">full stop</a>), thus rendering these given names as <i>Ma. Cristina</i> or <i>Ma. Victoria</i>. Filipino males with two given names such as <i>José Mariano</i> or <i>José Gerardo</i> could follow the same practice of abbreviating <i>Josés</i> as <i>Jo.</i>, but this is not as consistent. Some Muslims would follow conventions found in neighbouring <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>, abbreviating "<i>Muhammad</i>" (and its variants, <i><a href="/wiki/Viz." title="Viz.">viz.</a></i> "<i>Mohammed</i>", etc.) as "<i>Muhd.</i>" or "<i>Mohd.</i>"
</p><p>Another common practice rarely seen in other cultures (but common with <a href="/wiki/Spanish_name#María_and_José" class="mw-redirect" title="Spanish name">Spanish conventions</a>) is to <a href="/wiki/Elision" title="Elision">elide</a> or combine multiple given names into one nickname. The aforementioned <i>María Cristina</i> and <i>María Victoria</i> may thus acquire the nicknames <i>Maricris</i> and <i>Marivic</i>. Thus the Filipino names <i>Maricel</i>, <i>Maritoni</i>, <i>Marijo</i>, <i>Maritess</i>, and <i>Maricon</i> come from  <i>Maria Celia (or Celeste)</i>, <i>Marie Antoinette</i>, <i>María Josefa</i> (or <i>Josefina)</i>, <i>María Teresa</i>, and <i>María Concepción</i> (or <i>Consolación)</i>. The popular male nicknames <i>Joma</i>, <i>Jomar</i>, and <i>Jomari</i> are derived from <a href="/wiki/Concatenation" title="Concatenation">concatenating</a> <i>José Mariano</i>. <i>Jestoni</i> was derived from <i>Jesús Antonio</i>. These types of nicknames have become so common that they have also been registered as a child's official given name by the parents (e.g., <i>Maricris Llamador Gunigundo</i> or <i>Maricris Ll. Gunigundo</i>). The child <i>Sidperl</i> got his name when his parents combined their given names <i>Isidro</i> and <i>Perlita</i>.
</p><p>Sometimes, this practice results in a completely new, unprecedented given name. Former <a href="/wiki/Vice_President_of_the_Philippines" title="Vice President of the Philippines">Vice-President</a> <a href="/wiki/Jejomar_Binay" title="Jejomar Binay">Jejomar Binay</a>'s given name is a combination of <i><a href="/wiki/Holy_Family" title="Holy Family">Jesus-Joseph-Mary</a></i>. A former senator's first name was <i>Heherson</i>, derived from <i>He-Her-Son</i> (from "He, Her [Virgin Mary]'s Son", referring to <a href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" class="mw-redirect" title="Jesus Christ">Jesus Christ</a>). The unique, patriotic female names <i><a href="/wiki/Luzviminda" title="Luzviminda">Luzviminda</a></i> and its variants <i>Minvilu</i> and <i>Vizminda</i> come from concatenating the names of the country's three main island groups: <a href="/wiki/Luzon" title="Luzon">Luzon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Visayas" title="Visayas">Visayas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mindanao" title="Mindanao">Mindanao</a>.
</p><p>Some first names like <i>Lodegrano</i> or <i>Lorimer</i> may have been invented on the spot by the parents or derived from some partially remembered foreign term. Other coined first names have unusual spellings or <a href="/wiki/Spelling_pronunciation" title="Spelling pronunciation">spellings which are pronounced differently</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Honorifics_and_titles">Honorifics and titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Honorifics and titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Honorifics and titles are sometimes used in place of a person's actual name. Thus, the titles for family elders are often used by the younger persons and then adopted by the wider community: <i>Apo</i> (grandson/granddaughter). <i>Lolo</i> (grandfather) and <i>Lola</i> (grandmother) are used for senior elders; <i>Tatay/Itay/Ama</i> (father) or <i>Tito/Tiyo/Tsong</i> (uncle) and <i>Nanay/Inay/Ina</i> (mother) or <i>Tita/Tiya/Tsang</i> (aunt) for middle-aged elders; <i>Manong</i> or <i>Kuya</i> (elder brother) and <i>Manang</i> or <i>Ate</i> (elder sister) for anyone slightly older than the person speaking.
</p><p>People in the community are often addressed by their military or police rank, professional titles or job descriptions, either with or without their names. <i>Architect</i>, <i>Attorney</i>, <i>Engineer</i>, <i>Dok/Doctor</i>, <i>Direk/Director</i>, <i>Manager</i>, <i>Bisor</i> (supervisor), <i>Boss</i>, <i>Tsip/Chief</i>, are used in the same way as <i>Mister</i>, <i>Miss</i>, <i>Ms</i>., or <i>Mrs</i>. especially when the addressee's name is not yet known by the speaker. This is often done as a sign of respect and to avoid giving offence. Even foreigners who work in the Philippines or are naturalized Filipino citizens, including foreign spouses of Filipinos, who hold some of these titles and descriptions are addressed in the same way as their Filipino counterparts, although it may sound awkward or unnatural to some of them or language purists who argue that the basic titles that precede surnames and either <i>Sir</i> or <i>Ma'am/Madam</i> are to be employed for simplicity. Also, <i>Sir</i> and <i>Madam/Ma'am</i> are not to be used precede a nickname.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Numerals_and_birth_order_patterns">Numerals and birth order patterns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Numerals and birth order patterns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>People with the same name as their father are registered as <i>Junior</i> (abbreviated to <i>Jr</i>.) or numbered with Roman numerals (III, IV, V, etc.); their father adds <i>Senior</i> (<i>Sr</i>.) after his surname or suffix. Inevitably, the younger person tends to be nicknamed <i>Junior</i> or <i>Jun</i> permanently. One person's nickname became <i>Third</i> because his full name was <i>Alfonso Ma. Roxas Cuyegkeng III</i> (this is a fictional name for example purposes). Thus a family will necessarily bestow a variety of unofficial nicknames to distinguished the various people having with nearly identical official given names.
</p><p>The names of children in some families may follow a certain pattern, such as beginning with a certain letter of the alphabet, e.g. <i>Diego Arnel</i>, <i>Diamond Amelia</i>, <i>Danford Arman</i>, <i>Dolores Allison</i>, such that all their initials will be the same, i.e., <i>DAZL</i> if the middle name is <i>Zulueta</i> and the surname is <i>Lim</i>. One group of siblings was named after countries (<i>Arabia</i>, <i>Australia</i>, <i>Aruba</i>, <i>Albania</i>) while another was named after car trademarks (<i>Ford</i>, <i>Mercedes</i>, <i>Bentley</i>, <i>Maserati</i>). Other names seemed to have been taken from popular brand names, food, fruits, and flowers: <i>Ramcar</i>, <i>Cherry Pie</i>, <i>Apple</i>, <i>Peachy</i>, <i>Pepsi</i>, <i>Brandy</i>. World Champion boxer Manny Pacquiao named his two daughters <i>Queen Elizabeth</i> and <i>Princess</i><sup id="cite_ref-McGeown_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGeown-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> while his wife is named <i>Jinky</i>. Former Philippine Senator Joker Arroyo (legal name) has a brother named Jack.<sup id="cite_ref-McGeown_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGeown-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many nicknames are bestowed by parents or other elders on children while they are still toddlers. Examples are the numerous <i>Boy</i>, <i>Toto/Totoy</i> (young boy), <i>Girlie</i>, <i>Nene</i> (young girl), <i>Baby</i> and similar types of pet names given to people who received them as kids and carried them into adult life and seniority. They've carried the nickname all their lives and see no incongruity in being called <i>Boy</i> or <i>Baby</i> even when in their sixth decade. Some are diminutives of the actual name, such as <i>Pepito</i> for <i>Pepe</i>, <i>Juanito</i> for <i>Juan</i> (or the English form <i>Johnny</i> for <i>John</i>), and <i>Nenita</i> for <i>Nena</i>. Thus, a person used to being called <i>Joselito</i> (<i>Little Joseph</i>) as a child may retain the nickname as an adult even if he could already be called <i>José</i> or <i>Joseph</i>.
</p>
<h3><span id="Reversals.2C_indigenised_names_and_Anglicisation"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Reversals,_indigenised_names_and_Anglicisation">Reversals, indigenised names and Anglicisation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reversals, indigenised names and Anglicisation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Filipino given name <i>Dranreb</i> was invented by reversing the spelling of the English name <i>Bernard</i>, while someone calling himself <i>Nosrac</i> bears the legal name <i>Carson</i>. <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Estrada" title="Joseph Estrada">Joseph Ejército Estrada</a>, <a href="/wiki/List_of_Presidents_of_the_Philippines" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Presidents of the Philippines">Thirteenth</a> <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Philippines" title="President of the Philippines">President of the Philippines</a>, began as a movie actor and received his nickname <i>Erap</i> as an adult; it comes from <i>Pare</i> spelled backwards (from Spanish <i>compadre</i>, <i>fellow godparent</i>) but now means <i>mate</i> or <i>buddy</i> in Filipino.<sup id="cite_ref-McGeown_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGeown-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>An old custom is to replace or insert Filipino phonemes into a Spanish or English name: <i>Mariano</i> becomes <i>Nano</i>, <i>Edwin</i> becomes <i>Aweng</i>, <i>Eduardo</i> becomes <i>Dwarding</i>, <i>Roberto</i> becomes <i>Berting</i>, <i>Ponciano</i> becomes either <i>Popoy</i>, <i>Onse</i>, or <i>Syano</i>. Sometimes there is a tendency to convert a grand-sounding given name into something very ordinary, such as when <i>John Paul</i> becomes <i>JayPee</i>, <i>Peter John</i> becomes <i>Peejong</i>, <i>Anthony</i> becomes <i>Tonyo</i>, <i>Ronald</i> becomes <i>Onad</i>, <i>María Elena</i> becomes <i>Ineng</i> or <i>Inyang</i>, or <i>Ambrosia</i> becomes <i>Brosya</i>.
</p><p>Complementary to this is the practise of Anglicising (with the implication of "modernising") a Spanish given name. Thus <i>José Roberto</i> becomes <i>Joseph Robert</i> (further shortened to <i>Joebert</i>). <i>Eduardo</i> becomes <i>Edward</i> and then <i>Eddie</i> or <i>Eddieboy</i> (sometimes further shortened to <i>Daboy</i>). <i>Consolación</i> becomes <i>Connie</i>; <i>Corazón</i> becomes <i>Cora</i> or <i>Cory</i>; <i>Juan</i> becomes <i>John</i> or <i>Johnny</i>; <i>Teresita</i> or <i>Teresa</i> becomes <i>Terê</i>, <i>Tessa</i>, or <i>Tessie</i>; and <i>Gracia</i> becomes <i>Grace</i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Monikers_and_progressional_names">Monikers and progressional names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Monikers and progressional names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The variety of Filipino names, some of them with negative connotations in Anglicised form, often take foreigners by surprise.<sup id="cite_ref-McGeown_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGeown-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  Most Filipinos don't notice any negative English connotations, however, unless somebody points it out.<sup id="cite_ref-McGeown_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGeown-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Many Filipino celebrities and high-status personalities, such as actors and politicians, don't mind having such types of nicknames;<sup id="cite_ref-McGeown_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-McGeown-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> in fact, their nicknames are often more well-known than their actual given names. Film and television celebrity <a href="/wiki/German_Moreno" title="German Moreno">German Moreno</a> didn't mind using the nickname <i>Kuya Germs</i> (<i>kuya</i> = elder brother). National Artist of the Philippines for Fashion Design, José <i>Pitoy</i> Moreno, would never be recognized anywhere under his official given name, but so far, he is the only prominent <i>Pitoy</i> in the world.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Combined_names">Combined names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Combined names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is very common for parents to combine their given names to create a name for their child. Examples:
</p>
<ul><li>Maria + Carlos = Maricar</li>
<li>Elvin + Liza = Elliza</li>
<li>Marino + Erlinda = Marinerl</li>
<li>Pearlisita + Ian = Pearlianne</li>
<li>Mark + Melissa = Marissa</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_names">Middle names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Middle names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maternal_names">Maternal names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Maternal names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Christians (as well as certain Muslims, Chinese Filipinos, and others) in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> formerly followed <a href="/wiki/Spanish_naming_customs" title="Spanish naming customs">naming patterns</a> practiced throughout the Spanish-speaking world (the practice of having the father's surname followed by the mother's surname, the two being connected by the particle "y", which means "and", such as <i>Guillermo Cu-Unjieng y Araullo</i>). If the second surname starts with <i>i</i>, <i>y</i>, <i>hi</i> or <i>hy</i>, the particle becomes <i>e</i>, following Spanish rules of euphony, as in <i><a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Dato_e_Iradier" class="mw-redirect" title="Eduardo Dato e Iradier">Eduardo Dato e Iradier</a></i>. Sometimed this second rule is overlooked.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Janet_Lim-Napoles_mugshot.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Janet_Lim-Napoles_mugshot.jpg/220px-Janet_Lim-Napoles_mugshot.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="293" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Janet_Lim-Napoles_mugshot.jpg/330px-Janet_Lim-Napoles_mugshot.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Janet_Lim-Napoles_mugshot.jpg/440px-Janet_Lim-Napoles_mugshot.jpg 2x" data-file-width="545" data-file-height="725" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Janet_Lim-Napoles_mugshot.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Mug shot of <a href="/wiki/Janet_Lim-Napoles" title="Janet Lim-Napoles">Janet Lim-Napoles</a> with her name on a placard showing "Janet Napoles y Lim". Notice the use of Spanish order of paternal and maternal surnames, which are Napoles and Lim, respectively, separated by the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_naming_customs#The_particle_y_(and)" title="Spanish naming customs">particle <i>y</i></a></div></div></div>
<p>This practice changed when the Philippines became a <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> colony in the early 20th century. The order was reversed to follow the conventional American form "Christian name - <a href="/wiki/Middle_name" title="Middle name">Middle name</a> -
Surname," which in this case is actually "Christian name - Mother's surname - Father's surname" (Francisco Concepcion Casas or simply Francisco C. Casas). The conjunction <i>y</i> was dropped, although it is still used in certain contexts today (most notably names in <a href="/wiki/Criminal_record" title="Criminal record">criminal records</a>, like the names used in placards used in <a href="/wiki/Mug_shot" title="Mug shot">mug shots</a>, such as shown in the image on the right).
</p><p>Currently, the middle name is usually, though not always, the mother's maiden name (followed by the last name which is the father's surname). This is the opposite of what is done in Spanish-speaking countries and is similar to the way surnames are done in <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>. The blending of American and Spanish naming customs results in the way Filipinos write their names today.
</p><p>Furthermore, application forms for various legal documents define the first name as the "Christian name(s)," the middle name as the "mother's maiden surname" (this becomes the basis for the middle initial), and the surname as the "father's surname."
</p><p>Bearing the mother's maiden surname as the middle name or middle initial is more important to a majority of Filipinos than to use one of the given names as a middle name or middle initial. Filipino culture usually allocates equal value to the lineage from both mother and father except in some prominent families who practice a strictly patriarchal system (usually of Spanish or Chinese heritage).
</p><p>Exceptions apply in the case of children with single parents. Children born out of wedlock are registered under the mother's maiden name (if still unmarried), applying her middle name (maternal surname) for the child's last name, respectively. The unmarried father must resort to legal and administrative procedures if he desires to acknowledge the child as his own and for the child to be registered with his own surname (in which case the child will use the mother's surname as his/her middle name). Likewise, children raised by single fathers take the current surname (paternal surname) with no middle name, whether or not the mother's identity is known. These exceptions also apply to Filipino children who have non-Filipino descent.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maiden_and_married_names">Maiden and married names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Maiden and married names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When a woman marries, she may: use her maiden first name and surname and add her husband's surname; use her maiden first name and her husband's surname; or use her husband's full name, but prefixing a word indicating that she is his wife, such as “Mrs.”<sup id="cite_ref-SNCivilCode_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SNCivilCode-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> She may also decline to adopt her husband's surname and continue to use her maiden name since there is no law in the Philippines which obligates a married woman to use the surname of her husband.<sup id="cite_ref-PAOOp_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PAOOp-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li>A woman may use her birth surname after marriage. However, once she has opted to use the surname of her husband, she shall continue using it until her marriage with her husband is validly terminated, such as through <a href="/wiki/Annulment" title="Annulment">annulment</a>, subject to certain conditions.<sup id="cite_ref-SNCivilCode_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SNCivilCode-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PAOOp_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PAOOp-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>All children from this marriage will automatically have the mother's birth surname as their middle name, and the father's surname as their surname.<sup id="cite_ref-SNCivilCode_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-SNCivilCode-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>Until the middle of the 20th century, it was common for married Filipino women to insert the particle "de" ("of") between her maiden surname and husband's surname (as in <i>Margarita Mangahas de Cuyegkeng</i> or <i>Margarita M. de Cuyegkeng</i>), another common Spanish naming custom. However, this practice is no longer common.
</p><p>Married Filipino women who are professionals may choose to hyphenate their surnames (such as <i>Margarita Margarita - Cuyegkeng</i>, instead of simply <i>Margarita Cuyegkeng</i> or <i>Margarita M. Cuyegkeng</i>), at least in professional use, and use it socially even if legal documents follow a different naming pattern. This practice allows others to identify them after their marriage and helps others keep track of their professional achievements; otherwise, her unmarried and married names would seem to refer to two different persons (<i>Margarita Gomez Mangahas</i> as compared to <i>Margarita Mangahas-Cuyegkeng</i>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_initials">Middle initials</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Middle initials">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Before digitization of records, middle initials and sorting of surnames follow the first letter of the name after Hispanic <i>de</i>, <i>dela</i>, <i>del</i>, <i>delos</i>. For example, the name <i>Jose delos Santos dela Cruz</i> is shortened as <i>Jose S. dela Cruz</i> and surname sorted on the letter C. Today, the middle initial must be the letter D (<i>Jose D. dela Cruz</i>) and surname sorted in the letter D.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surnames">Surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indigenous_languages">Indigenous languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Indigenous languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Though most Filipinos adopted Malaysian/Indonesian, Chinese and European (especially Spanish and English) surnames, some chose surnames that derive from words in autochthonous languages, like Tagalog, Visayan (Cebuano and Hiligaynon), Ilocano, Kapampangan and Pangasinan. Many indigenous surnames derive from words displaying qualities of people, especially those related to strength (e.g. Tagalog <i>Macaraeg</i> and <i>Panganiban</i>), defiance (e.g. Tagalog <i>Dimayuga</i>) or settlement (e.g. Cebuano/Hiligaynon <i>Magbanua</i>).
</p><p>Most indigenous surnames are spelled closely following the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_orthography" title="Spanish orthography">Spanish-derived</a> orthographic conventions of the time. Many of these words are spelled differently today in the various Philippine languages (following spelling reforms since the late 19th century).<sup id="cite_ref-PilipinoExpress_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PilipinoExpress-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cordilleran">Cordilleran</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Cordilleran">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Unlike their lowlander counterparts, <a href="/wiki/Igorot_people" title="Igorot people">Igorots</a> living in the <a href="/wiki/Cordillera_Central_(Luzon)" title="Cordillera Central (Luzon)">Cordillera Central</a> in northern <a href="/wiki/Luzon" title="Luzon">Luzon</a> were not conquered by the Spaniards, thereby preserving their naming customs from foreign influence. Each group had their own naming customs, but generally, like <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_names" title="Indonesian names">Indonesian names</a>, there is only one given name and no surname to speak of. The given name's meaning is usually connected to natural phenomena or objects, such as <i>danum</i> for water. It was only the Igorots who have had interacted with Spaniards and lowlanders for trade who were given a name that follows the binomial "first name"-"surname" system, such as Mateo Cariño and Mateo Carantes.
</p><p>It was only at the turn of the 20th century and the advent of the <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Philippines_(1898%E2%80%931946)" title="History of the Philippines (1898–1946)">American occupation of the Philippines</a> that the naming customs of the Igorots slowly conformed with the national legal naming system used to today, aided by the evangelization efforts of American Protestant missionaries. Most older people, however, still keep the singular given name given to them by their parents while also using the so-called "Christian names" to conform to Philippine law. The singular given names of some individuals living in the early 20th century have since been adopted as a surname by their descendants.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish">Spanish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Spanish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cat%C3%A1logo_alfab%C3%A9tico_de_apellidos" title="Catálogo alfabético de apellidos">Catálogo alfabético de apellidos</a></div>
<p>Almost all Filipinos had <a href="/wiki/Spanish_naming_customs" title="Spanish naming customs">Spanish</a> or Spanish-sounding surnames imposed on them for taxation purposes, but a number of them have indigenous Filipino surnames. On 21 November 1849, Governor General Narciso Clavería y Zaldúa issued a decree stating that Filipinos should adopt Spanish surnames to make census counting easier. Some Filipinos retained their native pre-colonial names, especially those who were exempted from the Clavería decree such as the descendants of rulers of the <i>Maharlika</i> or noble class. These surnames of the native nobility include Lacandola, <a href="/wiki/Macapagal" title="Macapagal">Macapagal</a>, Macabulos, and Tupas whom each descended from different <a href="/wiki/Datu" title="Datu">Datus</a>. They were allowed to keep the name to claim tax exemptions.
</p><p>The Spanish surname category provides the <a href="/wiki/List_of_common_Filipino_surnames" class="mw-redirect" title="List of common Filipino surnames">most common surnames</a> in the Philippines.<sup id="cite_ref-geocities.com_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-geocities.com-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> At the course of time, some Spanish surnames were altered (with some eventually diverged/displaced their original spelling), as resulted from illiteracy among the poor and farming class bearing such surnames, creating confusion in the civil registry and a sense of detachment from their better-off relatives. Except for the "Ñ", Filipino surnames from Spanish are written without accents from American influence.
</p><p>Below is the list of common Spanish surnames, categorized by Spanish regional origins, with variation or altered renderings enclosed in the parentheses.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese">Chinese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Chinese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/List_of_common_Chinese_surnames" title="List of common Chinese surnames">Filipino spelling of Chinese names</a>.
Many modern-day <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Filipino" title="Chinese Filipino">Chinese Filipinos</a>, mostly <a href="/wiki/Hoklo_people" title="Hoklo people">Hokkien</a> and some few <a href="/wiki/Cantonese_people" title="Cantonese people">Cantonese</a>, or Hokkien people that initially immigrated from Hong kong, have last names with one syllable like Lim, Cheng, Lao, Ang, Lo, Chua, Ong, Chiu, Yan, Uy, Ching, Sin, Go, Tan, Yap, Cu, Ke, Wu, So, Yu, Dy, Khu and Sy. However, early <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Filipino" title="Chinese Filipino">Chinese Filipino</a> families took on the complete name of their <a href="/wiki/Patriarch" title="Patriarch">patriarch</a>, thus their names had three syllables or sometimes they would also append "-co" at the end from 哥("ko"), which means brother, such as Cojuangco, Gotamco, Yuchengco, Lichauco, Limjoco, Tanseco, Tancinco, Ongkiko, etc. Some took their single syllable chinese family names, spelt in a hispanicized latin spelling, then appended -son or -zon from 孫 ("sun"), which means grandchild, such as those like Quezon, Singson, Jocson, Joson, Henson, Pecson, Unson, Dyson, Hizon, Syson, Quison, etc. There are also those that used this style but instead of a family name, they used hokkien transcriptions of birth order numbers appended with -son or -zon or even -co, like Tuazon, Tiongson, Sioson, Dizon, Samson, Sison, Goson/Gozon/Gozum, Lacson, Quitson, Pueson, Causon, Tuaco, Tiongco, Sioco, etc.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> These were adopted into the mainstream <a href="/wiki/Surnames_by_country#The_Philippines" title="Surnames by country">Filipino surnames</a> and do not exist anywhere else in the world. Their names were transcribed using the <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Spanish#Orthography" title="Philippine Spanish">Spanish-derived orthography</a> used during the 19th century.<sup id="cite_ref-PilipinoExpress_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PilipinoExpress-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese">Japanese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Japanese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some Filipinos bear Japanese surnames. They most likely indicate Japanese ancestry either from the many Japanese who settled during pre-colonial Philippines when it was separated among different nations (kingdoms, rajahnates, sultanates, tribes, etc.), colonial times, or more recently from World War II.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/Diosdado_Macapagal" title="Diosdado Macapagal">Macapagal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Marcos" title="Ferdinand Marcos">Marcos</a> administrations, only few Japanese Filipinos have entered military services and this was very limited due to the post-war prejudice and distrust of the Filipinos to the Japanese who were naturalized or to people with Japanese heritage. These people in military service during that time are descendants of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> Japanese soldiers who were captured, pardoned and settled in the Philippines, or descended from pre-war immigrants who became economically successful in their ventures in the country, and married local Filipino women. Japanese migrants in the Philippines in the early 19th Century are categorically <a href="/wiki/Issei" title="Issei">Issei</a>, or "first generation", who have been born in Japan and migrated to the country or to other countries primarily to <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">U.S</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a> and <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>; their descendants are either full-blooded Japanese who extend their descent categorically to <a href="/wiki/Nisei" title="Nisei">Nisei</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sansei" title="Sansei">Sansei</a>, or a generation of Filipino-Japanese with combined or Filipinized cultural practices and lifestyles less often categorized to both Nisei or Sansei. However, there's no official categorization of the generation of Japanese migrants unlike in U.S. and Brazil since post-war developments in the Filipino social landscape enabled them to be socially and economically integrated to the Filipino society, making their cultural practices and lifestyles fully Filipino and ignoring or obscuring their Japanese heritage.
</p><p>More recent economic developments in both countries enabled exchanges of both peoples for various economic reasons, whether for employment, higher career opportunities, business expansions, or philanthropic ventures, thus a few Japanese expatriates making such ventures settled in the country today, marrying local Filipino women and raising a generation of Filipino-Japanese children taking their education in the Philippines and living in a mix of Filipino and Japanese cultural lifestyles.
</p><p>Below is a list of a few Japanese surnames borne by some Filipino-Japanese:
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Moro">Moro</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Moro">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The use of <a href="/wiki/Arabic_name" title="Arabic name">Arabic names</a> is prominent among the <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_the_Philippines" title="Islam in the Philippines">Filipino Muslims</a>. The country has Islamic influence from what are now Arabs, Persians, Malays, Indonesians, and Indians, who have traded with ancestors of Filipinos, and introduced <a href="/wiki/Islam_in_the_Philippines" title="Islam in the Philippines">Islam</a> to the southern parts of the archipelago beginning in the 13th century. Some names, including Fátima, Omar, and Soraya, bear direct influence from Arab sources and Spanish ones, given the latter's period <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">under Moorish rule</a>. Filipino Muslims, or called "Moros" as how the Spaniards called them, also bear and maintain unique blend of indigenous Moro and Arabic surnames, some are registered with Spanish renderings. Among Maranao families, some Maranao surnames are often used as a given name to their children. Others may or might have borne surnames from their mothers, manifesting scant matrilineal practice.
</p><p>Below a list of known Moro surnames, combining the surnames borne by Maranaos, Maguindanaos, Iranuns, and Tausugs. Alternate or Hispanized spellings are enclosed in the parentheses.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_surnames_of_foreign_origins">Other surnames of foreign origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Other surnames of foreign origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Filipinos who hold middle or family names from other non-English-speaking nations also follow the conventions mentioned in this article, despite the proper naming customs that are followed in those nations.
