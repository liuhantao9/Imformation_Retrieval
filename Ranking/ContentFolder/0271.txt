	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">German name</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Germanic_name" title="Germanic name">Germanic name</a>.</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Personal_name" title="Personal name">Personal names</a> in <a href="/wiki/German-speaking_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="German-speaking Europe">German-speaking Europe</a> consist of one or several <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given names</a> (<i>Vorname</i>, plural <i>Vornamen</i>) and a <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a> (<i>Nachname, Familienname</i>).
The <i>Vorname</i> is usually gender-specific. A name is usually cited in the "<a href="/wiki/Name_order" class="mw-redirect" title="Name order">Western order</a>" of "given name, surname", unless it occurs in an alphabetized list of surnames, e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Johann_Sebastian_Bach" title="Johann Sebastian Bach">Bach, Johann Sebastian</a>".
</p><p>In this, the German conventions parallel the naming conventions in most of Western and Central Europe, including <a href="/wiki/English_name" title="English name">English</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_name" title="Dutch name">Dutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Italian names">Italian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/French_name" title="French name">French</a>. There are some vestiges of a <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> system as they survive in parts of Eastern Europe and Scandinavia, but these do not form part of the official name.
</p><p>Women traditionally adopted their husband's name upon marriage and would occasionally retain their maiden name by <a href="/wiki/Hyphen" title="Hyphen">hyphenation</a>, in a so-called <i><a href="/wiki/Doppelname" class="mw-redirect" title="Doppelname">Doppelname</a></i>, e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Else_Lasker-Sch%C3%BCler" title="Else Lasker-Schüler">Else Lasker-Schüler</a>". Recent legislation motivated by <a href="/wiki/Gender_equality" title="Gender equality">gender equality</a> now allows a married couple to choose the surname they want to use, including an option for men to keep their birthname hyphenated to the common family name in the same way. It is also possible for the spouses to do without a common surname altogether and to keep their birthnames. 
</p><p>The most common given names are either <a href="/wiki/Biblical_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Biblical name">Biblical</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Christian_name" title="Christian name">Christian</a>", derived from names of Biblical characters or saints; <i>Johann/Hans</i> "John", <i>Georg/Jörg</i> "George", <i>Jakob</i> "Jacob" and "James"; <i>Anna</i>, <i>Maria</i>, <i>Barbara</i>, <i>Christina</i>) or from <a href="/wiki/Germanic_name" title="Germanic name">Germanic names</a> (<i>Friedrich</i> "Frederick", <i>Ludwig</i> "Louis", etc.) Since the 1990s, there has however been a trend of parents picking non-German forms of names, either for originality, or influenced by international celebrities, e.g. <i>Liam</i> (Gaelic form of <i>William</i>) rather than the German equivalent <i>Wilhelm.</i><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Most surnames are derived either from occupations, or from geographical origin, less often from bodily attributes. They became heritable with the beginning of central demographic records in the <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_period" title="Early modern period">early modern period</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Forenames">Forenames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Forenames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="Vorname"></span><span id="Rufname"></span>
The <i>Vorname</i> (in English <i><a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">forename</a></i>) is usually given to a child by the parents shortly after birth. It is common to give a child several <i>Vornamen</i>  (forenames), one of them intended for everyday use and known as the <i>Rufname</i> ("appellation name" or "call name"). 
This <i>Rufname</i> is often underlined on official documents, as it is sometimes the second or third name in the sequence of given names on official record, even though it is the given name in daily use from childhood.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> For example, in the resume submitted by mathematician <a href="/wiki/Emmy_Noether" title="Emmy Noether">Emmy Noether</a> to Erlangen University in 1907,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dd><i>Ich, Amalie <u>Emmy</u> Noether, bayerischer Staatsangehörigkeit und israelitischer Konfession, bin geboren zu Erlangen am 23. März 1882 ...</i></dd>
<dd>"I, Amalie <u>Emmy</u> Noether, of <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria" title="Kingdom of Bavaria">Bavarian nationality</a> and of Israelite confession, born in Erlangen on 23 March 1882 ..."</dd></dl>
<p>the underlining of <i>Emmy</i> communicates that this is the <i>Rufname</i>, even though it is the second of two official given names.
</p><p>In Germany, the chosen name must be approved by the local <a href="/wiki/Standesamt" title="Standesamt">Standesamt</a>  (civil registry office). The name must indicate the gender of the child and not negatively affect the well being of the child. Last names or the names of objects and products are not acceptable. For example, "Matti" was rejected for a boy's name because it did not indicate gender (however, these types of names are permissible if combined with a second name which clarifies the gender, for example: "Matti Oliver" or "Matti Julia").<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Among <a href="/wiki/German_nobility" title="German nobility">German nobility</a>, a fashion arose in the early modern period<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> to give a large number of forenames, often six or more. This fashion was to some extent copied by the <a href="/wiki/Bourgeoisie" title="Bourgeoisie">bourgeois class</a>, but subsided again after the end of the 19th century, so that while two or three forenames remain common, a larger number is now rare. 
The practice persists among German nobility, e.g. 
<a href="/wiki/Konrad,_Prince_of_Saxe-Meiningen" title="Konrad, Prince of Saxe-Meiningen">Johann Friedrich <u><i>Konrad</i></u> Carl Eduard Horst Arnold Matthias, Prince of Saxe-Meiningen, Duke of Saxony</a> (b. 1952), <a href="/wiki/Prince_Ernst_August_of_Hanover_(born_1954)" title="Prince Ernst August of Hanover (born 1954)"><u><i>Ernst August</i></u> Albert Paul Otto Rupprecht Oskar Berthold Friedrich-Ferdinand Christian-Ludwig, Prince of Hanover</a> (b. 1954), <a href="/wiki/Prince_Christian_of_Hanover_(born_1985)" title="Prince Christian of Hanover (born 1985)"><i>Christian</i> Heinrich Clemens Paul Frank Peter Welf Wilhelm-Ernst Friedrich Franz Prince of Hanover, Duke of Brunswick and Lüneburg</a> (b. 1985).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Surnames">Surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="Nachname"></span>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r889293853/mw-parser-output/.tmulti">.mw-parser-output .tmulti .thumbinner{display:flex;flex-direction:column}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .trow{display:flex;flex-direction:row;clear:left;flex-wrap:wrap;width:100%;box-sizing:border-box}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .tsingle{margin:1px;float:left}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .theader{clear:both;font-weight:bold;text-align:center;align-self:center;background-color:transparent;width:100%}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .thumbcaption{text-align:left;background-color:transparent}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .text-align-left{text-align:left}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .text-align-right{text-align:right}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .text-align-center{text-align:center}@media all and (max-width:720px){.mw-parser-output .tmulti .thumbinner{width:100%!important;box-sizing:border-box;max-width:none!important;align-items:center}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .trow{justify-content:center}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .tsingle{float:none!important;max-width:100%!important;box-sizing:border-box;text-align:center}.mw-parser-output .tmulti .thumbcaption{text-align:center}}</style><div class="thumb tmulti tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:208px;max-width:208px"><div class="trow"><div class="tsingle" style="width:102px;max-width:102px"><div class="thumbimage"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Verteilung_Nachname_Bachhuber_DE.png/100px-Verteilung_Nachname_Bachhuber_DE.png" decoding="async" width="100" height="130" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Verteilung_Nachname_Bachhuber_DE.png/150px-Verteilung_Nachname_Bachhuber_DE.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Verteilung_Nachname_Bachhuber_DE.png/200px-Verteilung_Nachname_Bachhuber_DE.png 2x" data-file-width="460" data-file-height="600" /></div><div class="thumbcaption">Distribution of the surname <i>Bachhuber</i> (lit. "peasant of an estate near a stream") in Germany (2005). Its Bavarian origin remains clearly visible.</div></div><div class="tsingle" style="width:102px;max-width:102px"><div class="thumbimage"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Verteilung_Nachname_Hoffmann_DE.png/100px-Verteilung_Nachname_Hoffmann_DE.png" decoding="async" width="100" height="130" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Verteilung_Nachname_Hoffmann_DE.png/150px-Verteilung_Nachname_Hoffmann_DE.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Verteilung_Nachname_Hoffmann_DE.png/200px-Verteilung_Nachname_Hoffmann_DE.png 2x" data-file-width="460" data-file-height="600" /></div><div class="thumbcaption">Distribution of the surname <i>Hoffmann</i> in Germany (2005). <p><a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">Surnames</a> (<i>family name</i>; <i>Nachname</i>, <i>Familienname</i>) were gradually introduced in <a href="/wiki/German-speaking_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="German-speaking Europe">German-speaking Europe</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">Late Middle Ages</a>. Many of such surnames are derived from <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nicknames</a>. They are generally classified into four groups by <a href="/wiki/Family_name_etymology" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name etymology">derivation</a>: 
given names, occupational designations, bodily attributes, and toponyms (including references to named buildings). Also, many family names display characteristic features of the <a href="/wiki/German_dialects" title="German dialects">dialect</a> of the region they originated in.
</p>
<ul><li><b>Given names</b> often turned into family names when people were identified <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">by their father's name</a>. For example, the first name <i><a href="/wiki/Ahrend" class="mw-redirect" title="Ahrend">Ahrend</a></i> developed into the family name <i>Ahrends</i> by adding a genitive s-ending, as in <i>Ahrend's son</i>. <br /> Examples: <i><a href="/wiki/Ahrends_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Ahrends (disambiguation)">Ahrends</a>/<a href="/wiki/Ahrens_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Ahrens (disambiguation)">Ahrens</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Burchard_(name)" title="Burchard (name)">Burkhard</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Wulff_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Wulff (disambiguation)">Wulff</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Friedrich_(surname)" title="Friedrich (surname)">Friedrich</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Benz_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Benz (disambiguation)">Benz</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Fritz_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Fritz (disambiguation)">Fritz</a></i>. With many of the early city records written in <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a>, occasionally the Latin genitive singular -i was used such as in <i>Jakobi</i> or <i>Alberti</i> or (written as -'y') in <a href="/wiki/Mendelssohn_Bartholdy" class="mw-redirect" title="Mendelssohn Bartholdy">Mendelssohn Bartholdy</a>.</li>
<li><b>Occupational name</b> are the most common form of family names; anybody who had an unusual job would have been bound to be identified by it. Examples: <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gaschler_(surname)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Gaschler (surname) (page does not exist)">Gaschler</a></i> (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Glass_(glass_blower)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Glass (glass blower) (page does not exist)">glass</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Schmidt_(surname)" title="Schmidt (surname)">Schmidt</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Smith_(metalwork)" class="mw-redirect" title="Smith (metalwork)">smith</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCller_(surname)" title="Müller (surname)">Müller</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Miller" title="Miller">miller</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Meier" title="Meier">Meier</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Farm" title="Farm">farm</a> administrator; akin to tenant, steward, sometimes also a bailiff), <i><a href="/wiki/Schulz" title="Schulz">Schulze</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Constable" title="Constable">constable</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Fischer" title="Fischer">Fischer</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Fisherman" title="Fisherman">fisherman</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Schneider_(surname)" title="Schneider (surname)">Schneider</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Tailor" title="Tailor">tailor</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Maurer_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Maurer (disambiguation)">Maurer</a></i> (mason), <i><a href="/wiki/Bauer_(surname)" title="Bauer (surname)">Bauer</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Farmer" title="Farmer">farmer</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Zimmermann_(surname)" class="mw-redirect" title="Zimmermann (surname)">Zimmermann</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Carpenter" class="mw-redirect" title="Carpenter">carpenter</a>), <a href="/wiki/Metzger" title="Metzger">Metzger</a> or <a href="/wiki/Fleischer" title="Fleischer">Fleischer</a> (butcher), <a href="/wiki/T%C3%B6pfer" title="Töpfer">Töpfer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toepfer" class="mw-redirect" title="Toepfer">Toepfer</a> (potter) or <a href="/wiki/Klingemann_(surname)" class="mw-redirect" title="Klingemann (surname)">Klingemann</a> (weapons smith). Also, names referring to nobility such as <i><a href="/wiki/Kaiser" title="Kaiser">Kaiser</a></i> (emperor), <i><a href="/wiki/K%C3%B6nig_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="König (disambiguation)">König</a></i> (king), <i><a href="/wiki/Graf" title="Graf">Graf</a></i> (count) are common, with the name bearers probably only a minor functionary of a monarch.</li>
<li><b>Bodily attribute names</b> are family names such as <i><a href="/wiki/Krause" title="Krause">Krause</a></i> (curly), <i><a href="/wiki/Schwarzkopf" title="Schwarzkopf">Schwarzkopf</a></i> (black head), <i><a href="/wiki/Klein_(surname)" title="Klein (surname)">Klein</a></i> (small), <a href="/wiki/Gro%C3%9F" class="mw-redirect" title="Groß">Groß</a> (big).</li>
<li><b>Geographical names</b> are derived from the name of a city or village, or the location of someone's home. They often have the '-er' postfix that signifies origin (as in English <i>New Yorker</i>). Examples: <i>Kissinger</i> (from <a href="/wiki/Kissingen" class="mw-redirect" title="Kissingen">Kissingen</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Schwarzenegger" class="mw-redirect" title="Schwarzenegger">Schwarzenegger</a></i> (from <a href="/w/index.php?title=Schwarzenegg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Schwarzenegg (page does not exist)">Schwarzenegg</a><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> or <a href="/w/index.php?title=Schwarzeneck&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Schwarzeneck (page does not exist)">Schwarzeneck</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Bayer" title="Bayer">Bayer</a></i> (from <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, German <i>Bayern</i>). Böhm indicates that a family originated in <a href="/wiki/Bohemia" title="Bohemia">Bohemia</a>. A special case of geographical names were those derived from a building or landmark, e.g. a <i><a href="/wiki/Busch_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Busch (disambiguation)">Busch</a></i> (bush) or <i><a href="/wiki/Springborn_(surname)" class="mw-redirect" title="Springborn (surname)">Springborn</a></i> (spring/well). Before the advent of street names and numbers, even for long times afterwards, many important buildings like inns, mills and farmsteads were given house names or <i><a href="/wiki/Hofname" class="mw-redirect" title="Hofname">Hofnamen</a></i> "estate names", e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Rothschild" title="Rothschild">Rothschild</a></i> "red sign/escutcheon", <i><a href="/wiki/Der_Lachs_zu_Danzig" class="mw-redirect" title="Der Lachs zu Danzig">Lachs</a></i> "(sign of the) salmon" , <i>Bär</i> "bear", <i><a href="/wiki/Engels" class="mw-redirect" title="Engels">Engels</a></i> from <i>Engel</i> "angel", etc.   A house or estate name could be combined with a profession, e.g.  <i>Rosenbauer</i> (rose-farmer, from a farmstead called 'the rose'); <i>Kindlmüller</i> (child's miller, from a mill named 'the Christmas child', 'the prodigal child' or 'the king's child').</li></ul>
<p>The preposition <i><a href="/wiki/Von" title="Von">von</a></i> ("of") was used to distinguish <a href="/wiki/Nobility" title="Nobility">Nobility</a>; for example, if someone was baron of the village of Veltheim, his family name would be <i>von Veltheim</i>. In modern times, people who were elevated to nobility often had a 'von' added to their name. For example, <a href="/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_von_Goethe" title="Johann Wolfgang von Goethe">Johann Wolfgang Goethe</a> had his name changed to <i>Johann Wolfgang von Goethe</i>. This practice ended with the abolition of the monarchy in Germany and Austria in 1919. Sometimes <i>von</i> is also used in geographical names that are not noble, as in <i>von Däniken</i>.
</p><p>With family names originating locally, many names display particular characteristics of the local dialects, such as the south German, Austrian and Swiss <a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">diminutive</a> endings <i>-l</i> <i>-el</i>, <i>-erl</i>, <i>-le</i> or <i>-li</i> as in <i>Kleibl</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Sch%C3%A4uble" class="mw-redirect" title="Schäuble">Schäuble</a></i> or <i>Nägeli</i> (from 'Nagel', nail). The same is true for regional variants in the naming of professions. While a barrel-maker from <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a> may have been called "Böttcher", a <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavarian</a> could easily have been called "Schäffler".
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Jewish_surname" title="Jewish surname">surnames</a> of the <a href="/wiki/German_Jews" class="mw-redirect" title="German Jews">German Jews</a> are a special case, as they were introduced later, in the late 18th to early 19th century, per <i>fiat</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The Prussian authorities imposed made-up and sometimes derogatory names. For instance, the name "Waldlieferant" (lit.: forest supplier) was "created" to ridicule a Jewish timber trader<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. Even way more offensive expressions ("Afterduft"; lit.: anus odor) were in use<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. This is by no means the rule, though; on the contrary, those surnames most quickly recognized as probably Jewish in origin are distinctly poetical ones, probably as they were made-up choices by the people themselves (e.g. <i><a href="/wiki/Rosenzweig" title="Rosenzweig">Rosenzweig</a></i>).
</p><p>Immigration, often sponsored by local authorities, also brought foreign family names into the German-speaking regions. Depending on regional history, geography and economics, many family names have <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">Hungarian</a> or <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic</a> (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>) origins. Sometimes they survived in their original form; in other cases, the spelling would be adapted to German (the <a href="/wiki/Slavic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Slavic language">Slavic</a> ending <i>ic</i> becoming the German <i lang="de" title="German language text">-itz</i><span class="lang-comment" style="font-style:normal; display:none; color:#33aa33; margin-left:0.3em">code: deu promoted to code: de </span> or <i lang="de" title="German language text">-itsch</i><span class="lang-comment" style="font-style:normal; display:none; color:#33aa33; margin-left:0.3em">code: deu promoted to code: de </span> or <a href="/wiki/Baltic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltic language">Baltic</a> "-kis" becoming "-ke"). Over time, the spelling often changed to reflect native German pronunciation (<i>Sloothaak</i> for the Dutch <i>Sloothaag</i>); but some names, such as those of French <a href="/wiki/Huguenots" title="Huguenots">Huguenots</a> settling in <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">Prussia</a>, retained their spelling but with the pronunciation that would come naturally to a German reading the name: <i><a href="/wiki/Marquard_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Marquard (disambiguation)">Marquard</a></i>, pronounced <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">[maʁkaʁ]</a></span> in French, ended up being pronounced <small></small><span title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)" class="IPA"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German" title="Help:IPA/Standard German">[ˈmaʁkvaʁt]</a></span> much like the German <i>Markwart</i> from which it was originally derived.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage">Marriage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Marriage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traditionally, the wife adopts her husband's <i>Nachname</i> on marriage and drops her own. However, due to the legal equality of sexes, the opposite is possible as well, though rare.
</p><p>A few examples of the practice under German law, if "Herr Schmidt" and "Frau Meyer" marry:<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>They can keep their former <i>Nachnamen</i>. (Herr Schmidt and Frau Meyer). In the 1990s, the law was thus changed. They can later change to variant 2, though the inverse is not possible.</li>
<li>They can declare one name as a "marriage name" (<i>Ehename</i>). In doing so, they can either both adopt the husband's name, or both adopt the wife's name as an <i>Ehename</i>. (Herr Meyer and Frau Meyer; Herr Schmidt and Frau Schmidt)</li>
<li>There is the possibility that one partner can combine both names by a hyphen. Thus, one of them then bears a double name (<i>Doppelname</i>). (Herr Schmidt and Frau Meyer-Schmidt (or Frau Schmidt-Meyer); the children have to be called Schmidt). Only one partner can take this option, making it impossible for both partners to have <i>Doppelnamen</i> (Thus, there would be no Herr Meyer-Schmidt and Frau Meyer-Schmidt)</li></ol>
<p>All children of a family have to receive the same non-hyphenated <i>Nachname</i> at birth, which may be either the mother's or the father's <i>Nachname</i> (traditionally it was the father's). If the parents adopted an <i>Ehename</i> this is the <i>Nachname</i> of the child. It is strictly forbidden to give children <i>Doppelnamen</i> if it is not the <i>Ehename</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The latter case can arise with traditional aristocratic <i>Doppelnamen</i> (e.g. Faber-Castell).
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austria</a> (§ 93 ABGB), a couple can choose either of their surnames as married name. In the default case, this is the surname of the groom. The partner who is changing surnames (usually the bride) has the possibility to use their unmarried name alongside the married name with hyphenation.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> (Art. 160 ZGB), the couple can opt to both retain their unmarried name, or the couple can choose to use either surname as their married name. If both retain their name, they need to declare which will be the surname of any future children.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nobility">Nobility</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nobility">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Titles of former aristocrats (like <i>Graf</i> for "Count") have become parts of the <i>Nachname</i> in Germany, giving longer names of several words, usually including the <a href="/wiki/Nobiliary_particle" title="Nobiliary particle">nobiliary particle</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Von" title="Von">von</a></i> (meaning "of") or <i>zu</i> (meaning "to", sometimes "at"), often <i>von und zu</i> are also found together (meaning "of and to/at").<sup id="cite_ref-NM-KTG_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NM-KTG-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NM-WKG_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NM-WKG-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The legal rules for these names are the same as those for other <i>Nachnamen</i>, which gives rise to a number of cases where people legally bear such names but are not recognized by the associations of formerly <a href="/wiki/German_nobility" title="German nobility">noble families in Germany</a>, which continue to apply the old rules of the German Empire in their publications. Most of these cases come about when a woman of noble descent marries a man with no title, and the two adopt the woman's name as their common <i>Nachname</i>, which was impossible under imperial law.
</p><p>In Austria, titles of nobility including certain other orders and honours held by Austrian citizens have since 3 April 1919 been abolished, including nobiliary particles such as <i>von</i>, the use of such titles by Austrian citizens is an offence punishable with a financial enforcement penalty.<sup id="cite_ref-GN-NRA_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GN-NRA-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> For example, <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Habsburg" title="Otto von Habsburg">Otto von Habsburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">Austria-Hungary</a>'s last crown prince, was referred to as Otto Habsburg(-Lothringen) in Austria. In Switzerland, where titles of nobility have been rare for several centuries, they can be used in private conversation, but are not officially recognised.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_surnames">Common surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Common surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most common surnames in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> are reportedly:<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li><a href="/wiki/M%C3%BCller_(surname)" title="Müller (surname)">Müller</a>, Möller ("miller")</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Schmidt_(surname)" title="Schmidt (surname)">Schmidt</a>, Schmitt, Schmitz, <a href="/wiki/Schmied" title="Schmied">Schmied</a> ("smith, blacksmith")</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Schneider_(surname)" title="Schneider (surname)">Schneider</a> ("tailor")</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fischer" title="Fischer">Fischer</a> ("fisherman")</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Weber" title="Weber">Weber</a> ("weaver")</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Meyer_(surname)" title="Meyer (surname)">Meyer</a>, Meier, Meir, Meyr, Mayer, Maier, Mair, or Mayr (often the senior farmer in a given settlement [running a "full range" farmstead in terms of area, workforce and harvest, compared to various partitions of lesser size], often as the leaseholder of a landlord like an abbey, a worldly ruler, etc.; "tenant, steward, sometimes also a bailiff, but <i>not</i> a mayor"<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag relies on an unreliable source. (May 2014)">unreliable source</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wagner_(surname)" title="Wagner (surname)">Wagner</a> ("carter, cartwright")</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Schulz" title="Schulz">Schulz</a>, Schulze, Schultze, Schulte, Schultheiß ("constable")</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Becker_(surname)" class="mw-redirect" title="Becker (surname)">Becker</a>, Bäcker ("baker")</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hoffmann" title="Hoffmann">Hoffmann</a>, Hofmann ("steward; tenant/leaseholder; courtier")</li></ol>
<p>These are all occupational names, designating common occupations around 1600 when surnames became heritable, so that these names arose independently across Germany.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gender-specific_surname_variants">Gender-specific surname variants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Gender-specific surname variants">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traditionally, there was a differentiation of surnames of women from those of their male siblings (as is still a rule in <a href="/wiki/Czech_name#Female_surnames" title="Czech name">Czech</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Slavic_naming_customs" title="Eastern Slavic naming customs">Eastern Slavic</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Polish_name#Feminine_forms" title="Polish name">Polish female surnames</a>), widespread in Germany until the 18th century. Thus, in old records, especially <a href="/wiki/Church_register" class="mw-redirect" title="Church register">church registers</a> on <a href="/wiki/Rites_de_passage" class="mw-redirect" title="Rites de passage">rites de passage</a>, such as baptisms, deaths and marriages etc., women may appear bearing regionally typical female surname variants (like, in South Germany: Peter Huber, but Anna Huberin). With the establishment of general official registration of <a href="/wiki/Legal_name" title="Legal name">legal names</a>, this practice was abolished in the 18th and the 19th centuries, depending on the legislation of the <a href="/wiki/States_of_the_Holy_Roman_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="States of the Holy Roman Empire">respective states</a>.
</p><p>Also, the spelling of given and surnames, varying previously from author to author, or even entry to entry, was then mostly fixed according to the official recorded form. Former noble titles appearing in male and female variants were transformed by the Weimar Constitution, article 109, into parts of the surnames in Germany, but a new tradition of gender-specific variants, for official registration, was established for these surnames. This practice was confirmed in a judgement by the <a href="/wiki/Reichsgericht" title="Reichsgericht">Reichsgericht</a> on 10 March 1926.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Colloquially, surname variants for women continue to appear in some German dialects. In <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_language" title="Bavarian language">Bavarian dialect</a> surnames of women sometimes are formed by adding the ending "-in", used in standard High German to indicate noun variants for women or items of grammatical feminine gender, such as Näher<b>in</b> (seamstr<b>ess</b>), with Näher (seamster) being the male form. In <a href="/wiki/West_Low_German" title="West Low German">West Low German</a> parlance the ending "…sch(e)" is sometimes added to surnames of women, related to the standard High German adjective ending "…isch" (cognitive to English "<a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-ish" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:-ish">…ish</a>"), <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">suffixed</a> to nouns or adjectives indicating belonging / pertaining to, being of the kind described by the suffixed word: for example, <i>de Smidtsche</i>, is Ms Schmidt (Smith), but literally about <i>the Smithian</i> (the woman pertaining to a man/family named Schmidt).<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another form, indicating a female bearer of a surname, was the addition of a genitive "s" (like the <a href="/wiki/Saxon_genitive" class="mw-redirect" title="Saxon genitive">Saxon genitive</a>), the daughter or wife of Mr. Bäcker (literally Baker) would appear as Ms Bäckers (in German without an apostrophe), as being Bäcker's daughter or wife.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pseudonyms">Pseudonyms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pseudonyms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">Pseudonyms</a> can be used by artists (<i>Künstlername</i>, "artist's name") and members of religious orders (<i>Ordensname</i>); If a pseudonym is widely known in public it can be added to the passport of that person (under the weaker legal status of <i>Künstlername</i>) and be used instead of the original name in most situations. The same field in the passport also serves to show religious names, i.e. the new name somebody takes on when becoming a <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">monk</a> or <a href="/wiki/Nun" title="Nun">nun</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_degrees_and_titles">Academic degrees and titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Academic degrees and titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The academic degree of <i>Doktor</i> (<i>Dr.</i>) and the academic title of <i>Professor</i> (<i>Prof.</i>) are not part of the name in Germany but can be entered into an <a href="/wiki/Identity_card" class="mw-redirect" title="Identity card">identity card</a> or <a href="/wiki/Passport" title="Passport">passport</a> and are frequently used in documents and addresses.
</p><p>They are, however, often used in a written address (e.g., Dr. Meier, Prof. Dr. Müller), and will often be used in formal speech or sometimes by lower-ranked persons such as students, though many academics prefer being addressed just like anyone else, i.e. by <i>Herr</i> or <i>Frau</i> alone (see below).
</p>
<h2><span id="Hofname_.28estate_name.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Hofname_(estate_name)">Hofname (estate name)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Hofname (estate name)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="Hofname"></span>
In rural areas it is common that farmers are known by the traditional name of their <i><a href="/wiki/Hof_(farm)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hof (farm)">Hof</a></i> (farm or estate). Because of the long-standing tradition of impartible inheritance in <a href="/wiki/German-speaking_Europe" class="mw-redirect" title="German-speaking Europe">German-speaking Europe</a>, ownership of a <i>Hof</i> had often been tied to direct patrilineal descent over centuries.
Thus, farmers were traditionally known by their <i>Hofname</i> even before the development of the <i>Nachname</i> in the early modern period, and the two systems came to overlap. Many <i>Nachnamen</i> are in fact derived from such <i>Hofnamen</i>, but in some instances, the <i>Hofname</i> tradition survived alongside the official <i>Nachname</i>
</p><p>Historically, the <i>Hofname</i> was the first type of commoners' family name to become heritable. This process began still in the <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">Late Middle Ages</a> (14th to 15th century); e.g. <a href="/wiki/Ulrich_Zwingli" class="mw-redirect" title="Ulrich Zwingli">Ulrich Zwingli</a> (b. 1484) inherited his father's surname, in origin a <i>Hofname</i> (from the term <i>Twing</i>, denoting a type of walled-in estate) even though he did not inherit his estate.
</p><p>In cases where <i>Nachname</i> and <i>Hofname</i> are not identical (usually because there was no male heir at some point in the family history) they are joined in official documents by <i>genannt</i> (abbr. <i>gen.</i>), e.g. <i>Amann gen. Behmann</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" class="mw-redirect" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words"><span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (October 2014)">which?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> In Austria the term <i>vulgo</i> (abbr. <i>vlg.</i>) is used instead of <i>genannt</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_changes">Name changes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Name changes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are only a few circumstances in which one is allowed to <a href="/wiki/Name_change" title="Name change">change one's name</a>:
</p>
<ol><li>On marriage: the couple can choose the name of either partner, they can both keep their original names, or (provided the original family name of neither partner contains a hyphen), one partner can modify their own name, appending the partner's family name to their own, creating a hyphenated name ("Mr. Schmid and Ms. Meier-Schmid" or "Mr. Schmid-Meier and Ms. Meier").</li>
<li>Correction of a name: if the state has made an error with the name and this can be proven, the original name can be restored. Example: "Maſs" became "Mahs" and is corrected to "Mass".</li>
<li>Gender reassignment, in the case of <a href="/wiki/Transsexualism" class="mw-redirect" title="Transsexualism">transsexuals</a>.</li>
<li>Naturalisation of foreigners (per Article 47 of the EGBGB). In this case, the foreigners may choose to adopt German forms of their first and last names, or adopt new first names if their old first names cannot be adapted into German.</li>
<li>Changing a name that is too complicated (too long or difficult spelling because of origin), too common (like Müller or Schmidt), or causes ridicule (which can be because of the name itself, like "Fick" ("Fuck", even though of different etymologic origin), or because of association, like "Adolf").</li></ol>
<p>Though the <i>Namensänderungsgesetz</i> (Law concerning the changing of names) allows changing because of any "important reason", in practice this will very rarely be allowed.
</p><p>Adding <i>Doktor</i> (Ph.D.) in Germany, and adding any academic degree in Austria, into one's identity card or passport is not considered a name change.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Order_of_names_and_use_of_articles">Order of names and use of articles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=German_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Order of names and use of articles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i>Nachname</i> is put after the <i>Vorname</i>. In the rural use of several regions where heavy <a href="/wiki/German_dialects" title="German dialects">dialect</a> is spoken (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saxony" title="Saxony">Saxony</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Palatinate_(region)" title="Palatinate (region)">Palatinate</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Saarland" title="Saarland">Saarland</a>), the order is reversed, e.g. "der Mühlbach Klaus" instead of "Klaus Mühlbach". The definite article is always added in this style of naming. Especially in these regions, it is also the usual administrative way, but with a comma; the said person would appear in documents as "Mühlbach, Klaus" or even, with a title or profession "Mühlbach, Klaus, Dr./OLt<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>/Bäcker".
</p><p>Except for <a href="/wiki/Southern_Germany" title="Southern Germany">Southern Germany</a>, usage of the definite article with the name outside of dialect is uncommon, and considered a mistake in Standard German. It is considered familiar language, but not as a mark of rough, rural manners as in <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>. It is used especially when talking of and/or with children, but also in some other situations. E.g., <i>Ich bin der Nils</i> ('I am the Nils'); <i>Ich gab der Eva eine Süßigkeit</i> ('I gave a sweet to the Eva').  Once again, such usage varies and is optional, and is often used in clarification or in emphasis.
</p><p>In Austria, the definite article is always used in informal spoken language, but most of the time not in very formal or written language.
</p><p>In some dialects (such as those spoken in the Western Palatinate, the Saarland and parts of the Rhineland), the article used with women's and girls' names is not the feminine, but the neuter article. This is because<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (October 2014)">verification needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> the German word for "girl", <i>Mädchen</i>, is a neuter noun, due to the diminutive suffix <i>-chen</i>.
</p>
