<p><i><b>Narcos</b></i> is an American <a href="/wiki/Crime_film" title="Crime film">crime</a> <a href="/wiki/Drama_(film_and_television)" title="Drama (film and television)">drama</a> <a href="/wiki/Web_television" title="Web television">web television</a> series created and <a href="/wiki/Executive_producer" title="Executive producer">produced</a> by <a href="/wiki/Chris_Brancato" title="Chris Brancato">Chris Brancato</a>, Carlo Bernard, and <a href="/wiki/Doug_Miro" title="Doug Miro">Doug Miro</a>.
</p><p>Set and filmed in <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">Colombia</a>, seasons 1 and 2 are based on the story of <a href="/wiki/Drug_kingpin" class="mw-redirect" title="Drug kingpin">drug kingpin</a> <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Escobar" title="Pablo Escobar">Pablo Escobar</a>, who became a billionaire through the production and distribution of <a href="/wiki/Cocaine" title="Cocaine">cocaine</a>. The series also focuses on Escobar's interactions with drug lords, <a href="/wiki/Drug_Enforcement_Administration" title="Drug Enforcement Administration">Drug Enforcement Administration</a> (DEA) agents, and various opposition entities.<sup id="cite_ref-Premiere_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Premiere-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-huffpostapril_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-huffpostapril-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Season 3 picks up after the fall of Escobar and continues to follow the DEA as they try to shutdown the rise of the infamous <a href="/wiki/Cali_Cartel" title="Cali Cartel">Cali Cartel</a>.
</p><p>Season 1, comprising 10 episodes, originally aired on August 28, 2015, as a <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a> exclusive.<sup id="cite_ref-hrapril_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hrapril-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>  The series was renewed for a second season, which premiered on September 2, 2016, with 10 episodes.<sup id="cite_ref-ign2_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ign2-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> On September 6, 2016, Netflix renewed the series for its third and fourth seasons.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Season 3 premiered on September 1, 2017, but<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> on July 18, 2018, the directors announced that season 4 would instead "reset" as season 1 of a new Netflix original series, titled <i><a href="/wiki/Narcos:_Mexico" title="Narcos: Mexico">Narcos: Mexico</a></i>. The new series, released on November 16, 2018,<sup id="cite_ref-Netflix_&#39;Narcos:_Mexico&#39;_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Netflix_&#39;Narcos:_Mexico&#39;-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> is set in Mexico in the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span id="Season_1_.282015.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_1_(2015)">Season 1 (2015)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Season 1 (2015)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Narcos_(season_1)" title="Narcos (season 1)">Narcos (season 1)</a></div>
<p>Season 1 chronicles the life of Pablo Escobar from the late 1970s, when he first began manufacturing cocaine, to July 1992. The show relates the main events that happened in Colombia during this period and Escobar’s relationship to them. It is told through the perspective of <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Murphy_(civil_servant)" title="Stephen Murphy (civil servant)">Steve Murphy</a>, an American DEA agent working in Colombia. The series depicts how Escobar first became involved in the cocaine trade in Colombia. He was an established <a href="/wiki/Black_market" title="Black market">black marketeer</a> in Medellín, moving trucks worth of illegal goods (alcohol, cigarettes, and household appliances) into Colombia during a time when this was strictly forbidden, when introduced to Mateo "Cockroach" Moreno, a <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chilean</a> exile and underground chemist, who pitched the idea that they go into business together, with Moreno producing and Escobar distributing a new, profitable drug—cocaine. They expand beyond Moreno's small cocaine processing lab by building additional, larger labs in the rainforest and, using the expertise of <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Lehder" title="Carlos Lehder">Carlos Lehder</a>, transport their product in bulk to Miami, where it gains notoriety amongst the rich and famous. Soon enough, Pablo develops larger labs and more extensive distribution routes into the United States to supply growing demand. With cocaine's growth into a drug of importance in the American market, one that accounts for a large flow of U.S. dollars to Colombia and escalating drug-related violence in the United States, the Americans send a task force from the DEA to Colombia to address the issue. Murphy is partnered with <a href="/wiki/Javier_Pe%C3%B1a" title="Javier Peña">Javier Peña</a>. The purpose of Murphy's task force is to work with the Colombian authorities, led by Colonel Carrillo, to put an end to the flow of cocaine into the United States. The season ends with Escobar's escape from prison.
</p>
<h3><span id="Season_2_.282016.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_2_(2016)">Season 2 (2016)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Season 2 (2016)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Narcos_(season_2)" title="Narcos (season 2)">Narcos (season 2)</a></div>
<p>Season 2 continues where season 1 ended. Soldiers find Escobar and his entourage right outside the perimeter of La Catedral but are too petrified of Escobar to make an arrest. At the embassy, the United States sends a new ambassador who brings the CIA into play. In the beginning, little change occurs for Escobar, as he still has the loyalty of his cartel. This loyalty, however, starts to slip as Escobar needs more time and resources to hide from the government. Among the tricks he uses to avoid being seen are riding around town in the trunk of a taxi cab and using young lookouts to report police movements to him.
</p><p>Initially, Escobar easily adapts to his new life, giving money to the community while ruthlessly killing those who try to break away from his empire. The Colombian police and Escobar engage in massive battles, resulting in high tension and unrest in Colombia. Escobar's rivals in the Cali cartel form an unlikely alliance with ousted members of his own cartel, as well as with a CIA-backed anticommunist paramilitary group. Agent Peña secretly works with this group, who kill members of Pablo's organization and claim responsibility as "Los Pepes". After two of Escobar's top cartel members are caught and betray him, Escobar goes on the run. His bodyguard and he hide in a safehouse, where he celebrates his 44th birthday. When Pablo tries to make contact with his family, the DEA and military track him down via radio triangulation and corner him on the rooftops. Pablo is hit twice in the ensuing shootout, and although he might have survived his injuries, a Colombian policeman named Trujillo executes him, amidst shouts of "Viva Colombia!" Escobar's wife Tata goes to the Cali cartel for their help in leaving the country. Peña returns to the United States, in expectation of being reprimanded by the disciplinary committee for his associations with Los Pepes, but is surprised when asked to provide intelligence against the Cali cartel, implying his future involvement with the DEA.
</p>
<h3><span id="Season_3_.282017.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_3_(2017)">Season 3 (2017)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Season 3 (2017)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Narcos_(season_3)" title="Narcos (season 3)">Narcos (season 3)</a></div>
<p>Season 3 was released on September 1, 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The story continues after Pablo Escobar's death and shows the DEA's fight against the Cali cartel. With Escobar out of the way, business for the cartel is booming, with new markets in the United States and elsewhere. To everyone's surprise, Gilberto Rodríguez Orejuela, the leader of the Cali cartel, announces that within 6 months, the cartel will leave the cocaine business entirely to focus on legal business interests. The decision is met with mixed reactions within the cartel.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The series was announced in April 2014, through a partnership deal struck between <a href="/wiki/Netflix" title="Netflix">Netflix</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gaumont_International_Television" title="Gaumont International Television">Gaumont International Television</a>. The series was primarily written by Chris Brancato and directed by Brazilian filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Padilha" title="José Padilha">José Padilha</a>, who also directed the critically and commercially successful <i><a href="/wiki/Elite_Squad" title="Elite Squad">Elite Squad</a></i> (2007) and <a href="/wiki/Elite_Squad:_The_Enemy_Within" title="Elite Squad: The Enemy Within">its sequel</a> in 2010, which became the highest-grossing film ever in Brazil.<sup id="cite_ref-ign1_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ign1-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> On September 15, 2017, one of the show's location scouts, Carlos Muñoz Portal, was reported as having been found murdered with multiple gunshot wounds, in his car on a dirt road in central Mexico, near the town of <a href="/wiki/Temascalapa" title="Temascalapa">Temascalapa</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> A spokesman for the attorney general in Mexico state said there were no witnesses due to the remote location, and the authorities will continue to investigate.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> The possibility of narco gangs' being involved is being considered.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opening_theme_and_title_sequence">Opening theme and title sequence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Opening theme and title sequence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<dl><dt>Title card</dt></dl>
<p><i>Narcos</i> opens with a title card, from which the narrator reads: "<a href="/wiki/Magical_realism" class="mw-redirect" title="Magical realism">Magical realism</a> is defined as what happens when a highly detailed, realistic setting is invaded by something too strange to believe. There is a reason magical realism was born in Colombia".<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Opening theme</dt></dl>
<p><i>Narcos</i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span> opening theme, "Tuyo", is a <i><a href="/wiki/Bolero" title="Bolero">bolero</a></i> written and composed for the show by Brazilian singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Rodrigo_Amarante" title="Rodrigo Amarante">Rodrigo Amarante</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<dl><dt>Visual montage</dt></dl>
<p>The theme scores the visual montage comprising the title sequence, created by DK Studios under artistic director Tom O’Neill. The 1980s-themed images address Colombian drug trafficking in general, the United States’ attempt to control it, the era’s glamour, footage from the mountainous regions of Bogota and surrounding underprivileged neighbourhoods, shots of local residents, archival news coverage, and violence. The montage excludes some people who were unwilling to appear in the credits, but it does include some news clips and images "of Pablo Escobar and his entourage, like those at the zoo, [which] came directly from the drug baron’s personal photographer, who goes by the name El Chino." According to O'Neill, "the production team took inspiration from James Mollison’s photo book <i>The Memory of Pablo Escobar</i>."<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Narcotics is a late Middle English word derived  from Old French <i>narcotique</i>, in turn derived via medieval Latin from the Greek <i>narkōtikos</i>, from <i>narkoun</i> ("make numb" or "produce <a href="/wiki/Stupor" title="Stupor">stupor</a>").<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In Spanish, the term <i>narco</i> (or its plural, <i>narcos</i>) is an abbreviation of the word <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/narcotraficante" class="extiw" title="wikt:narcotraficante">narcotraficante</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Drug_trafficker" class="mw-redirect" title="Drug trafficker">drug trafficker</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> In the United States, the <a href="/wiki/Epithet" title="Epithet">epithet</a> "narc" (or "narco") refers to a specialist officer of a narcotics police force, such as a DEA agent.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_1">Season 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Season 1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first season received generally favorable reviews from critics. <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Review_aggregator" title="Review aggregator">review aggregator</a>, surveyed 45 reviews and judged 79% to be positive. The site reads, "<i>Narcos</i> lacks sympathetic characters, but pulls in the viewer with solid acting and a story that's fast-paced enough to distract from its familiar outline."<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> On <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>, season one holds a weighted average score of 77 out of 100, based on 19 critics, indicating "generally favorable reviews".<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> gave the first season a 7.8 out of 10 score, saying: "It's a true-to-life account, sometimes to a fault, of the rise of Pablo Escobar and the hunt that brought him down laced with stellar performances and tension-filled stand-offs. Its blend of archival footage reminds us that the horrors depicted really happened, but also manage to present an Escobar that is indefensible but frighteningly sympathetic."<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> Writing for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Philadelphia_Inquirer" title="The Philadelphia Inquirer">The Philadelphia Inquirer</a></i>, Tirdad Derakhshani reviewed the season positively, calling it, "Intense, enlightening, brilliant, unnerving, and addictive, <i>Narcos</i> is high-concept drama at its finest."<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Television critic Tim Goodman of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hollywood_Reporter" title="The Hollywood Reporter">The Hollywood Reporter</a></i> also reviewed the series positively, saying, "The series begins to find its pacing not long after, and we see the strength of Moura’s acting, which to his credit never races, in the early going, toward over-the-top menace or the drug-lord cliches we're all used to at this point. Credit also the fact that Padilha brings a documentary feel to Narcos."<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Nancy_deWolf_Smith" class="mw-redirect" title="Nancy deWolf Smith">Nancy deWolf Smith</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Wall_Street_Journal" title="The Wall Street Journal">The Wall Street Journal</a></i> wrote, "The omniscient-narrator device works very well for a complex story spanning many years and varied sets of players."<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Critic <a href="/wiki/Neil_Genzlinger" title="Neil Genzlinger">Neil Genzlinger</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> said, "It’s built on sharp writing and equally sharp acting, as any good series needs to be."<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> However, chief television critic <a href="/wiki/Mary_McNamara" title="Mary McNamara">Mary McNamara</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> wrote, "It's a grand if inconsistent experiment that, from the moment it opens with a definition of magic realism, wears its considerable ambitions on its sleeve."<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Writing for <i><a href="/wiki/IndieWire" title="IndieWire">IndieWire</a></i>, Liz Shannon Miller said, "An unlikeable character, no matter the circumstances, remains unlikeable, but an unlikeable character trumps a bland blonde man whose position of authority appears to be his only really interesting character trait, no matter how much voice-over he utters."<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The show received criticism for the quality of the Spanish spoken. Dr. Alister Ramírez-Márquez, a member of the <a href="/wiki/North_American_Academy_of_the_Spanish_Language" title="North American Academy of the Spanish Language">North American Academy of the Spanish Language</a>, faulted the accents, pronunciation, intonation, and incorrect use of <a href="/wiki/Paisa_Region" title="Paisa Region">Paisa</a> colloquialisms.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Speaking of the show's reception in Colombia, Sibylla Brodzinsky of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> stated, "audiences have been bemused by the stars’ ropey accents, irritated by its portrayal of the country's recent history, and&#160;– in some cases&#160;– simply bored by yet another narco-drama."<sup id="cite_ref-Brodzinsky_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brodzinsky-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> The Brazilian accent of Wagner Moura was particularly criticized for being incongruent with Escobar's Paisa background.<sup id="cite_ref-Brodzinsky_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brodzinsky-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-GOrozco_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GOrozco-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Gisela Orozco of the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> said the show would not engross Latinos due to the mishmash of accents and contrasted <i>Narcos</i> with <i><a href="/wiki/Pablo_Escobar:_El_Patr%C3%B3n_del_Mal" class="mw-redirect" title="Pablo Escobar: El Patrón del Mal">Pablo Escobar: El Patrón del Mal</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-GOrozco_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-GOrozco-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> In his review of the show, Colombian TV critic Omar Rincón wrote in <i><a href="/wiki/El_Tiempo_(Colombia)" title="El Tiempo (Colombia)">El Tiempo</a></i>, "<i>Narcos</i> is the Miami and US vision of NarColombia&#160;– something like <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Trump</a>’s idea of us: the good guys are the gringos&#160;... and the narcos are comically dysfunctional or primitives with bad taste&#160;... <i>Narcos</i> may do well outside Colombia, but here it produces anger and laughter."<sup id="cite_ref-Brodzinsky_48-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brodzinsky-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_2">Season 2</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Season 2">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The second season generated better reviews compared with those for the <a href="/wiki/Narcos_(season_1)" title="Narcos (season 1)">previous season</a>. Rotten Tomatoes gives the second season an approval rating of 92% based on 22 reviews, with an average rating of 7.6/10. The site's critical consensus reads, "<i>Narcos'</i> sophomore season manages to elevate the stakes to a gut-wrenching degree in what continues to be a magnificent account of Pablo Escobar's life."<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> On Metacritic, season two holds a score of 76 out of 100, based on 13 critics, indicating "generally favorable reviews".<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/IGN" title="IGN">IGN</a> gave the second season a score of 7.4 out of 10, calling it "Good" and wrote "It may go overboard with its love of Pablo Escobar, but I can't truly fault the show for taking advantage of its best performer and character – or for scrambling to find an emotional core on a show that can feel rather clinical."<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup> Joshua Alston of <i><a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">The A.V. Club</a></i> lauded the performance of Moura's and said, "While the show never soft-pedals the havoc Escobar created, it makes him surprisingly sympathetic, thanks in part to Moura’s shrewd, affecting performance."<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup>  Critic <a href="/wiki/Neil_Genzlinger" title="Neil Genzlinger">Neil Genzlinger</a> of <i>The New York Times</i> said, "Mr. Moura is inscrutably brilliant at the center of it all."<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i><span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">&#39;</span>s <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Jensen" title="Jeff Jensen">Jeff Jensen</a> also reviewed the series positively, saying, "Where season 1 spanned 10 years, season 2 captures Escobar's last days on the loose. Each tightly packed episode moves quickly without sacrificing richness, chronicling the uneasy alliances and gross tactics employed to snare Escobar."<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> Tim Goodman of <i>The Hollywood Reporter</i> said,  "What works in the early going of season two is that the fall is almost always more thrilling, if not engaging, than the buildup. Escobar senses the loss of power and Moura does some of his best work as viewers read the worry and interior thinking on his face."<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Season_3">Season 3</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Season 3">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On Rotten Tomatoes, the third season holds an approval rating of 96% based on 22 reviews, with an average rating of 7.46/10. The site's critical consensus reads, "<i>Narcos</i> continues to evolve in its third season, drawing on historical details to take viewers on a thoroughly gripping – and unsettlingly timely – journey into darkness."<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> On Metacritic, season 3 holds a weighted average score of 78 out of 100, based on 9 critics, indicating "generally favorable reviews".<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>IGN gave the third season a score of 8.0 out of 10, calling it “a flawed-but-thrilling improvement over its first two seasons”.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> Critics praised the show for its ability to still be engaging after moving on from the Pablo Escobar storyline. Liz Shannon Miller of <i>IndieWire</i> said, “Outside of the overhanging threat of [Pablo] Escobar, “Narcos” proves its ability to excel as an ongoing drama by not just introducing new members of these syndicates, but giving them real, fleshed-out lives beyond basic confrontations.”<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup> Writing for <i>The Telegraph</i>, Ed Power called the show “A sweltering thrill ride you’ll want to stay with all the way until the end.”<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> Critics praised Pedro Pascal’s performance of DEA agent Javier Peña, who became the main protagonist after the departure of Boyd Holbrook, who played Steve Murphy. Kevin Yeoman of <i>ScreenRant</i> wrote, “The charismatic actor is finally offered a chance to lead the semi-anthological series.”<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Writing for <i>Collider</i>, Chris Cabin said, “Pascal carries the series admirably, expanding the scope of his character through Peña’s relationship with his father.”<sup id="cite_ref-63" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup> Despite the positive reviews, some critics said the show got off to a slow start in the first few episodes. Critic Scott Tobias of <i>The New York Times</i> wrote, “Setting aside the “Goodfellas” narration, the earliest episodes of “Narcos” were distinguished by a meat-and-potatoes approach to the crime genre that rebuffed the slickness that usually comes with the territory.”<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> Some critics acknowledged the expository flaws while praising the last sequence of episodes. Daniel Fienberg of <i>The Hollywood Reporter</i> wrote, “After a slow start to its third season, Netflix's cartel drama proves that it's able to be gripping TV even without Wagner Moura's Pablo Escobar.”<sup id="cite_ref-65" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Writing for <i>Entertainment Weekly</i>, James Hibberd said, “The narrative picks up its pace as the season progresses into a final run of episodes that feature the most suspenseful sequences Narcos has ever had.”<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spin-off_series">Spin-off series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Narcos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Spin-off series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Narcos:_Mexico" title="Narcos: Mexico">Narcos: Mexico</a></div>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Narcos:_Mexico" title="Narcos: Mexico">Narcos: Mexico</a></i> was released on November 16, 2018. The story goes back to the 1980s, in Mexico, where it sets a <a href="/wiki/Shared_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Shared world">shared world</a> with the previous seasons of <i>Narcos</i> (which focus on Colombia), bringing back characters like <a href="/wiki/Amado_Carrillo_Fuentes" title="Amado Carrillo Fuentes">Amado Carrillo</a> (<a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mar%C3%ADa_Yazpik" title="José María Yazpik">José María Yazpik</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Escobar" title="Pablo Escobar">Pablo Escobar</a> (<a href="/wiki/Wagner_Moura" title="Wagner Moura">Wagner Moura</a>), for small roles, and introducing new ones like the famous DEA Agent <a href="/wiki/Kiki_Camarena" title="Kiki Camarena">Kiki Camarena</a> (<a href="/wiki/Michael_Pe%C3%B1a" title="Michael Peña">Michael Peña</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Miguel_%C3%81ngel_F%C3%A9lix_Gallardo" title="Miguel Ángel Félix Gallardo">Miguel Felix-Gallardo</a> (<a href="/wiki/Diego_Luna" title="Diego Luna">Diego Luna</a>), creator of the first Mexican drug cartel, depicting the clash between them and the eve of the catastrophic Mexican war on drugs.<sup id="cite_ref-Netflix_&#39;Narcos:_Mexico&#39;_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Netflix_&#39;Narcos:_Mexico&#39;-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
