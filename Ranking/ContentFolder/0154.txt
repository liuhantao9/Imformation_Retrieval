<p>The <b>Prime Minister of Denmark</b> (<a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">Danish</a>: <i lang="da">Danmarks statsminister</i>) is the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_government" title="Head of government">head of government</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Kingdom of Denmark</a> comprising the three <a href="/wiki/Unity_of_the_Realm" class="mw-redirect" title="Unity of the Realm">constituent countries</a>: <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">Denmark</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a>. Before the creation of the modern office, the kingdom did not initially have a head of government separate from its head of state, namely the <a href="/wiki/Monarch_of_Denmark" class="mw-redirect" title="Monarch of Denmark">Monarch</a>, in whom the <a href="/wiki/Executive_(government)" title="Executive (government)">executive</a> authority was vested. The <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Denmark" title="Constitution of Denmark">Constitution of 1849</a> established a <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a> by limiting the powers of the Monarch and creating the office of <i lang="da" title="Danish language text">premierminister</i>. The inaugural holder of the office was <a href="/wiki/Adam_Wilhelm_Moltke" title="Adam Wilhelm Moltke">Adam Wilhelm Moltke</a>.
</p><p>The Prime Minister presides over a <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_Denmark" title="Cabinet of Denmark">cabinet</a> that is formally appointed by the Monarch. In practice, the appointment of the Prime Minister is determined by their support in the <a href="/wiki/Folketing" title="Folketing">Folketing</a> (The National Parliament). Since the beginning of the 20th century no single party has held a majority in the <a href="/wiki/Folketing" title="Folketing">Folketing</a>, so the Prime Minister must head a coalition of political parties, as well as their own party. Additionally, only four coalition governments since World War II have enjoyed a majority in the Folketing, so the coalitions (and the Prime Minister) must also gain loose support from other minor parties.
</p><p>The current Prime Minister of Denmark is <a href="/wiki/Mette_Frederiksen" title="Mette Frederiksen">Mette Frederiksen</a> who since 27 June 2019 has led a one-party government consisting of the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democrats_(Denmark)" title="Social Democrats (Denmark)">Social Democrats</a> with parliamentary support from the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Social_Liberal_Party" title="Danish Social Liberal Party">Social Liberals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Socialist_People%27s_Party_(Denmark)" title="Socialist People&#39;s Party (Denmark)">Socialist People's Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Red%E2%80%93Green_Alliance_(Denmark)" title="Red–Green Alliance (Denmark)">Red–Green Alliance</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroese</a> <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_(Faroe_Islands)" title="Social Democratic Party (Faroe Islands)">Social Democratic Party</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Inuit_Ataqatigiit" title="Inuit Ataqatigiit">Inuit Ataqatigiit</a> and <a href="/wiki/Siumut" title="Siumut">Siumut</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prime_Minister_of_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Establishment">Establishment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prime_Minister_of_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Establishment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From approximately 1699 to 1730, the highest-ranking non-monarchial government official was titled the "Grand Chancellor" (<i lang="da" title="Danish language text">storkansler</i>) and from 1730 until 1848, this office was titled "Minister of State" (<i lang="da" title="Danish language text">Statsminister</i>). These titles foreshadowed the modern office of Prime Minister, however, unlike the current office, the Grand Chancellor and State Minister were not formal heads of government. The King held <a href="/wiki/Executive_(government)" title="Executive (government)">executive</a> authority as <a href="/wiki/Absolute_monarchy" title="Absolute monarchy">absolute</a> ruler from 1661 until the enactment of a liberal Constitution in the early nineteenth century.
</p><p>The office of Prime Minister was introduced as a part of the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">constitutional monarchy</a> outlined in 1848 and signed as the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Denmark" title="Constitution of Denmark">Danish Constitution</a> on <a href="/wiki/Constitution_Day_(Denmark)" title="Constitution Day (Denmark)">5 June 1849</a>. The new Constitution established a <a href="/wiki/Parliamentary_system" title="Parliamentary system">parliamentary system</a> by creating a new bicameral parliament (<i lang="da" title="Danish language text"><a href="/wiki/Rigsdagen" title="Rigsdagen">Rigsdagen</a></i>) and a Council Presidium, headed by a Council President.<sup id="cite_ref-stm_history_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stm_history-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The Council Presidium is regarded as the predecessor of the modern <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_the_State_of_Denmark" class="mw-redirect" title="Ministry of the State of Denmark">Prime Minister's Office</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-stm_history_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stm_history-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The first Council President was <a href="/wiki/Adam_Wilhelm_Moltke" title="Adam Wilhelm Moltke">Adam Wilhelm Moltke</a>, who came to power on 22 March 1848.  Molte and his next two successors also held the title of <i lang="da" title="Danish language text">premierminister</i>, which translates as "prime minister".
</p><p>From 1855 onwards the Prime Minister was known simply as the "Council President" (<i lang="da" title="Danish language text">Konseilspræsident</i>). <a href="/wiki/Carl_Christian_Hall" title="Carl Christian Hall">Carl Christian Hall</a> became the first Prime Minister/Council President to lead a political party (the <a href="/wiki/National_Liberal_Party_(Denmark)" title="National Liberal Party (Denmark)">National Liberal Party</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_office">Modern office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prime_Minister_of_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Modern office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The modern Prime Minister's Office was founded on 1 January 1914, when the Council Presidium was established as a department under the Prime Minister,<sup id="cite_ref-stm_history_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stm_history-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> when it had previously existed as an informal council gathered by the Prime Minister. The title of the Prime Minister changed again in 1918 under the Premiership of <a href="/wiki/Carl_Theodor_Zahle" title="Carl Theodor Zahle">Carl Theodor Zahle</a>, becoming titled the "Minister of State" (in-line with its Scandinavian neighbours, Norway and Sweden), which it remains to this day.
</p><p>By the mid-nineteenth century a strong party-system had developed, with most Prime Ministers being the leader of either <a href="/wiki/Venstre_(Denmark)" title="Venstre (Denmark)">Venstre</a> (<i lang="da" title="Danish language text">left</i>) or <a href="/wiki/H%C3%B8jre" title="Højre">Højre</a> (<i lang="da" title="Danish language text">right</i>). However, by <a href="/wiki/1924_Danish_Folketing_election" title="1924 Danish Folketing election">1924</a> the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democrats_(Denmark)" title="Social Democrats (Denmark)">Social Democrats</a> had become the largest party and Højre had disappeared.
</p><p>During the first years of <a href="/wiki/Occupation_of_Denmark" class="mw-redirect" title="Occupation of Denmark">Occupation of Denmark</a>, the governments of Prime Ministers <a href="/wiki/Vilhelm_Buhl" title="Vilhelm Buhl">Vilhelm Buhl</a> and then <a href="/wiki/Erik_Scavenius" title="Erik Scavenius">Erik Scavenius</a> cooperated with the Nazi occupiers. On 29 August 1943, the Danish government resigned, refusing to grant further concessions to Nazi Germany. All government operations were assumed by the permanent secretaries of the individual departments, and this arrangement lasted until the Liberation of Denmark on 5 May 1945. Since King Christian X never accepted the resignation of the government, it existed de jure until a new cabinet was formed on 5 May 1945.
</p><p>The twentieth century was dominated by Social Democratic Prime Ministers leading left-wing coalitions; Social Democratic Prime Ministers were in power nearly continuously from 1924 until 1982. The first Prime Minister from the <a href="/wiki/Conservative_People%27s_Party_(Denmark)" title="Conservative People&#39;s Party (Denmark)">Conservative People's Party</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poul_Schl%C3%BCter" title="Poul Schlüter">Poul Schlüter</a>, came to power as the head of a broad centre-right coalition in 1982. The centre-right coalition ruled in 1993, last for eleven years, made it became the longest centre-right government in Denmark history since the 1920s.
</p><p>In November 2001 the left-wing coalition in the Folketing lost seats to the right-wing coalition led by Venstre, ending their eight years rule. Venstre became the largest party since 1924. <a href="/wiki/Anders_Fogh_Rasmussen" title="Anders Fogh Rasmussen">Anders Fogh Rasmussen</a>, leader of Venstre, served as the Prime Minister from 2001 to April 2009. His coalition government consisted of Venstre and the Conservative People's Party, with parliamentary support from the national-conservative <a href="/wiki/Danish_People%27s_Party" title="Danish People&#39;s Party">Danish People's Party</a> (<i lang="da" title="Danish language text">Dansk Folkeparti</i>). On 5 April 2009, Rasmussen resigned to become <a href="/wiki/Secretary_General_of_NATO" title="Secretary General of NATO">Secretary General of NATO</a>, leaving <a href="/wiki/Finance_minister" title="Finance minister">minister of finance</a> and vice president of Venstre <a href="/wiki/Lars_L%C3%B8kke_Rasmussen" title="Lars Løkke Rasmussen">Lars Løkke Rasmussen</a> to be the Prime Minister.
</p><p>Following the <a href="/wiki/2011_Danish_general_election" title="2011 Danish general election">September 2011 election</a> the right-wing lost by a small margin to the opposing centre-left coalition, led by <a href="/wiki/Helle_Thorning-Schmidt" title="Helle Thorning-Schmidt">Helle Thorning-Schmidt</a> who on 3 October 2011 formed a <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_Helle_Thorning-Schmidt" class="mw-redirect" title="Cabinet of Helle Thorning-Schmidt">new government</a> initially consisting of the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democrats_(Denmark)" title="Social Democrats (Denmark)">Social Democrats</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Danish_Social_Liberal_Party" title="Danish Social Liberal Party">Danish Social Liberal Party</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Socialist_People%27s_Party_(Denmark)" title="Socialist People&#39;s Party (Denmark)">Socialist People's Party</a>. Following a <a href="/wiki/2015_Danish_general_election" title="2015 Danish general election">general election defeat</a>, in June 2015 Thorning-Schmidt resigned as Prime Minister and was succeeded by Lars Løkke Rasmussen, who heads a minority government consisting entirely of ministers from Venstre.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_and_authority">Role and authority</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prime_Minister_of_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Role and authority">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Denmark" title="Politics of Denmark">Politics of Denmark</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Denmark" title="Constitution of Denmark">Constitution of Denmark</a> states that the <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_Denmark" title="Monarchy of Denmark">Monarch</a>, who is the <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">head of state</a>, has supreme authority and acts out this power through their ministers.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The Monarch formally appoints and dismisses ministers, including the Prime Minister.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In a sense, the Prime Minister only has the power that is given by the Monarch, according to the Constitution.
</p><p>Although the country's leading politician, the Prime Minister is not nearly as powerful as other prime ministers in Europe. This is mainly because it is nearly impossible for one party to get a majority of seats in the <a href="/wiki/Folketing" title="Folketing">Folketing</a> (Parliament), so the government is always a coalition between two or more parties. No Danish party has won a majority since 1901, and for much of that time there has not even been a majority coalition.<sup id="cite_ref-DAA_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAA-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Because of his limited powers, the Prime Minister is <i><a href="/wiki/Primus_inter_pares" title="Primus inter pares">primus inter pares</a></i> (first among equals).<sup id="cite_ref-DAA_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DAA-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, unlike most of his counterparts, the Prime Minister can never be certain that his agenda will pass, and must cobble together a majority for each piece of legislation.
</p><p>Although, as stated, the Monarch formally appoints all ministers of the <a href="/wiki/Cabinet_of_Denmark" title="Cabinet of Denmark">cabinet</a> freely, in practice Monarchs only conventionally select the Prime Minister after a leader has gathered support from a majority in the Folketing. With no party having held a majority in over a century, parties form alliances. Usually, the Social Democrats join with centre-left parties, and Venstre with centre-right parties. Following elections when there is no clear leader, the Monarch will hold a "King/Queen's meeting" where, after a series of discussions and agreements, the leader of the largest alliance and the largest party within that alliance—usually the Social Democrats or Venstre- is appointed as Prime Minister-<i>elect</i> (<i lang="da" title="Danish language text">kongelig undersøger</i>) . The new Prime Minister-elect, together with the leaders of the junior parties, select ministers to form a new coalition cabinet, which is the presented to the Monarch. The government is allowed to take office without a vote of confidence, and is allowed to stay in office as long as it does not lose a <a href="/wiki/Motion_of_no_confidence" title="Motion of no confidence">vote of no confidence</a>.
</p><p>The Prime Minister chairs the weekly meetings of the council of ministers and has the power to set the agenda of these meetings. The Prime Minister traditionally gathers together a government ministry known as the "Ministry of the State of Denmark" (<i lang="da" title="Danish language text">Statsministeriet</i>) or Prime Minister's Office.<sup id="cite_ref-stm_history_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stm_history-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Atypical of a Danish ministry it does not have any councils, boards or committees associated with it and its near sole responsibility is to act as the secretariat of the Prime Minister. There is a small department under the ministry that takes care of special legal issues not covered under other ministries, among others <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>'s and <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a> relation to the Monarchy, the mass media's contact to the State, number of ministers in the government, or <a href="/wiki/Margrethe_II_of_Denmark" title="Margrethe II of Denmark">Queen Margrethe II</a> legal status as a civilian.
</p><p>The Prime Minister, by convention, chooses to dissolve the Folketing and call a new election (although this is formally undertaken by the Monarch), which the Prime Minister is obligated to do within four years of the previous election. In spite of this, the Prime Minister has no say with respect to Denmark's autonomous regions, the <a href="/wiki/Faroe_Islands" title="Faroe Islands">Faroe Islands</a> and <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">Greenland</a>, while the Folketing on the other hand does, as all laws passed by the <a href="/wiki/L%C3%B8gting" title="Løgting">Faroese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Greenland" title="Parliament of Greenland">Greenlandic</a> parliaments must be ratified by the Folketing.
</p><p>There exist checks on the Prime Minister's power. If the Folkleting revokes its confidence in an incumbent Prime Minister he must either resign along with the entire cabinet or ask the Monarch to dissolve the Folketing and call a new election. Whenever a Prime Minister resigns, dies, or is forced from office, the Monarch asks them (or, in the case of death, the next available leader in a coalition) to keep the government as a <a href="/wiki/Caretaker_government" title="Caretaker government">caretaker government</a> until a successor has been elected.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Amenities">Amenities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Prime_Minister_of_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Amenities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Expand_section plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><a href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg" class="image"><img alt="[icon]" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" decoding="async" width="20" height="14" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="44" data-file-height="31" /></a></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs expansion</b>&#32;with: information on salary. <small>You can help by <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Prime_Minister_of_Denmark&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">adding to it</a>.</small>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">September 2012</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>The government offices, including the Ministry of the State of Denmark (<i lang="da" title="Danish language text">Statsministeriet</i>; The Prime Minister's Office), is located inside <a href="/wiki/Christiansborg_Palace" title="Christiansborg Palace">Christiansborg Palace</a>, along with the Folketing and the <a href="/wiki/Courts_of_Denmark" title="Courts of Denmark">courts</a>.
</p><p>The official summer residence of the Prime Minister is <a href="/wiki/Marienborg" title="Marienborg">Marienborg</a>, an eighteenth-century estate that was acquired by the State. It is situated on the shore of <a href="/wiki/Lake_Bagsv%C3%A6rd" title="Lake Bagsværd">Lake Bagsværd</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kongens_Lyngby" title="Kongens Lyngby">Kgs. Lyngby</a>, 15 kilometres (9.3&#160;mi) north of <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">Copenhagen</a>. It has served as an official summer residence for ten Prime Ministers since 1960. Marienborg is frequently used for governmental conferences and informal summits between the government, industry and organisations in Denmark.
</p>
