	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Matronymic</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For a family name inherited from one's mother (and maternal grandmother, etc.), see <a href="/wiki/Matriname" title="Matriname">Matriname</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>matronymic</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Personal_name" title="Personal name">personal name</a> based on the <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> of one's mother, grandmother, or any female ancestor. It is the female equivalent of a <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a>. Around the world, <b>matronymic surnames</b> are far less common than <a href="/wiki/Patronymic_surname" title="Patronymic surname">patronymic surnames</a><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>. In some cultures in the past, matronymic last names were often given to children of unwed mothers. Or if a woman was especially well known or powerful, her descendants might adopt a matronym based on her name.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology_of_English">Terminology of English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology of English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word <i>matronymic</i> is first attested in English in 1794 and originates in the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> μήτηρ <i>mētēr</i> "mother" (<small><a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">GEN</a></small> μητρός <i>mētros</i> whence the <a href="/wiki/Combining_form" class="mw-redirect" title="Combining form">combining form</a> μητρo- <i>mētro</i>-),<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  ὄνυμα <i>onyma</i>, a variant form of ὄνομα <i>onoma</i> "name",<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> and the suffix <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-%CE%B9%CE%BA%CF%8C%CF%82" class="extiw" title="wikt:-ικός">-ικός</a> -<i>ikos</i>, which was originally used to form adjectives with the sense "pertaining to" (thus "pertaining to the mother's name"). The Greek word μητρωνυμικός <i>mētrōnymikos</i><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> was then borrowed into Latin in a partially Latinised form (Greek <i>mētēr</i>, dialectally <i>mātēr</i>, corresponds to Latin <i>mater</i>),<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> as <i>matronomicus</i>. These words were a source for coining the English <i>matronymic</i> as the female counterpart to <i>patronymic</i> (first attested in English in 1612).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Whereas the <i><a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a></i> records an English noun <i>patronym</i> in <a href="/wiki/Free_variation" title="Free variation">free variation</a> with the noun <i>patronymic</i>, it does not, however, record a corresponding noun <i>matronym</i>.
</p><p>More rarely, English speakers use forms based wholly on Greek: the noun <i>metronym</i> (first attested in 1904); and the noun and adjective <i>metronymic</i> (first attested in 1868).<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> These are, for example, the forms used in the 2016 <i>The Oxford Dictionary of Family Names in Britain and Ireland</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The matrilineal communities in South and North-East India, like the <a href="/wiki/Nairs" class="mw-redirect" title="Nairs">Nairs</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bunt_(community)" title="Bunt (community)">Bunts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Khasi_people" title="Khasi people">Khasi</a>, have family names which are inherited from their mother. Matronymic names are common in Kerala.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Daughters take the names of their mothers as the second part of their name.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Indonesia">Indonesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Indonesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Minangkabau_people" title="Minangkabau people">Minangkabau</a> of <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> are the largest group of people who use this naming system. People of <a href="/wiki/Enggano_Island" title="Enggano Island">Enggano Island</a> also use a matronymic system. They also have family name/surname (marga).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines">Philippines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Philippines">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Filipino_name" title="Filipino name">Filipino names</a> legally have the <a href="/wiki/Maiden_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Maiden name">maiden name</a> of the child's mother as a <a href="/wiki/Middle_name" title="Middle name">middle name</a> as opposed to Anglo-American use of secondary or tertiary given names. Filipino children born to <a href="/wiki/Unwed_mother" class="mw-redirect" title="Unwed mother">unwed mothers</a>, if not claimed by the father nor adopted by anyone else, automatically bear their mother's maiden name and sometimes middle name.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vietnam">Vietnam</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vietnam">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Vietnamese names">Vietnamese names</a> also function this way, as less of a "tradition" than a style or trend, in which the mother's maiden name is the child's middle name.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="England">England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although many English matronyms were given to children of unwed mothers, it was not unusual for children of married women to also use a matronymic surname. For instance, it was traditional during the Middle Ages for <a href="/wiki/Posthumous_birth" title="Posthumous birth">children whose fathers died before their births</a> to use a matronym, and it was not unheard of for children to be given a matronym if the father's name was foreign, difficult to pronounce, or had an unfortunate meaning. A child of a strong-minded woman might also take a matronym, as might a child whose name would otherwise be confused with that of a cousin or neighbour. There are even instances where royal houses used matronymics to strengthen claims to the English throne  – for example, Princess Matilda's eldest son was known as <a href="/wiki/Henry_FitzEmpress" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry FitzEmpress">Henry FitzEmpress</a> (-<a href="/wiki/Fitz" title="Fitz">fitz</a> meaning "son of" from Latin <i>filius</i>). Common English matronyms include Beaton, Custer, Tiffany, Parnell, Hilliard, Marriott, Ibbetson, Babbs, and Megson.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finland">Finland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Finland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the old Finnish system, women were standardly given matronyms, while men were given patronyms, for example, Ainontytär (female) or Pekanpoika (male). Since the 19th century the system of inherited family names has been used, however, and today nearly all Finns have inherited surnames.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Family names derived from matronyms are found in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a>, especially in <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">Normandy</a>: Catherine, Marie, Jeanne, Adeline. In medieval Normandy (<a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Normandy" title="Duchy of Normandy">Duchy of Normandy</a>), a matronym might be used when the mother was of greater prominence than the father or the basis for a claim of inheritance, such as in the cases of <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_England" title="Henry II of England">Henry FitzEmpress</a> and <a href="/wiki/Robert_FitzWimarc" title="Robert FitzWimarc">Robert FitzWimarc</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Iceland">Iceland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Iceland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_name" title="Icelandic name">Icelandic name</a></div>
<p>Some <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">Icelandic</a> people, like <a href="/wiki/Hei%C3%B0ar_Helguson" title="Heiðar Helguson">Heiðar Helguson</a>, have matronyms.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland_and_Wales">Ireland and Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ireland and Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Matronymics appear in medieval Celtic tales such as <i><a href="/wiki/Cath_Maige_Tuired" title="Cath Maige Tuired">Cath Maige Tuired</a></i> and the Fourth Branch of the <a href="/wiki/Mabinogi" class="mw-redirect" title="Mabinogi">Mabinogi</a> (the children of <a href="/wiki/D%C3%B4n" title="Dôn">Dôn</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Netherlands">Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Matronymics are accepted in the Netherlands but are generally written as given names on identity cards.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Romania">Romania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Romania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Family names derived from matronyms are also found in <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">Romania</a>, especially in the region of <a href="/wiki/Moldavia" title="Moldavia">Moldavia</a>. Examples include: Aioanei, Ababei, Acatrinei, Ailincăi.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Serbia">Serbia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Serbia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although far less common than patronymic surnames, matronymic surnames are widespread both in Serbia and in neighboring countries. Examples include surnames such as Katić, Sinđelić, Nedić, Marić, Višnjić, Janjić, Sarić, Miličić, Milenić, Natalić, Zorić, Smiljić, Anđelić and many others. Sometimes it is difficult to ascertain if name of a specific family is patronymic or matronymic considering many Serbian names have both male and female version (for example, surname Miljanić could come from both m.- Miljan and f.- Miljana). Cases where widows had to become heads of households were not uncommon during 18th and 19th century and when surnames were first standardized in Serbia in 1851 it was decided they would be based on the names of eldest living heads of households which in some cases were women. People who didn't know their father well would also take matronymic surnames, with notable cases being hero of the First Serbian Uprising Stevan Sinđelić, who took that surname in honor of his mother Sinđelija.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ukraine">Ukraine</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Ukraine">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Family names derived from matronyms are also found in <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">Ukraine</a>. Examples include: Katin, Mashkov, Annushkin. <a href="/wiki/Oleg_Yaroslavich" title="Oleg Yaroslavich">Oleg Yaroslavich</a>, 12th century prince of Galich, was known as Oleg Nastasyich during his life to distinguish his claim from that of his half-brother Vladimir.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_East">Middle East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Middle East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arabic">Arabic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Arabic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An example of an <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a> matronymic is the name of <a href="/wiki/Jesus_in_Islam" title="Jesus in Islam">Jesus</a> in the Qur'an, ‘Īsá ibn Maryam, which means Jesus the son of Mary. The book <i>Kitāb man nusiba ilá ummihi min al-shu‘arā’</i> (The book of poets who are named with the lineage of their mothers) by the 9th-century author Muḥammad ibn Ḥabīb is a study of the matronymics of Arabic poets.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> There exist other examples of matronymics in historical Arabic.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Jewish">Jewish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Matronymic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Jewish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most characters in the <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">Bible</a> are referred to with a patronymic. However, <a href="/wiki/Abishai_(Bible)" class="mw-redirect" title="Abishai (Bible)">Abishai</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joab" title="Joab">Joab</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Asahel" title="Asahel">Asahel</a> – the sons of <a href="/wiki/Zeruiah" title="Zeruiah">Zeruiah</a>, sister or stepsister of <a href="/wiki/King_David" class="mw-redirect" title="King David">King David</a> – are invariably referred to as "Sons of Zeruiah" and the name of their father remains unknown. Also the Biblical Judge <a href="/wiki/Shamgar" title="Shamgar">Shamgar</a> is referred to with the matronymic "Son of <a href="/wiki/Anat" title="Anat">Anat</a>".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>There are indications of a Jewish history of matronymic names. Specifically, in East European Jewish society, there appeared various matronymic family names such as <a href="/wiki/Rivlin" title="Rivlin">Rivlin</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Rivka" class="mw-redirect" title="Rivka">Rivka</a>/<a href="/wiki/Rebecca" title="Rebecca">Rebecca</a>), <a href="/wiki/Sorkin" title="Sorkin">Sorkin</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Sarah" title="Sarah">Sarah</a>), <a href="/wiki/Zeitlin" title="Zeitlin">Zeitlin</a> (from Zeitl), <a href="/wiki/Rochlin" class="mw-redirect" title="Rochlin">Rochlin</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Rachel" title="Rachel">Rachel</a>), <a href="/wiki/Feiglin" class="mw-redirect" title="Feiglin">Feiglin</a> (from <a href="/wiki/Feige" title="Feige">Feige</a>), Dworkin (from Dvora), and others.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> In certain Jewish prayers and blessings, matronyms are used, e.g., "Joseph <i>ben</i> (son of) Miriam".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Specifically, when people are asked to pray urgently for the recovery of a person whose life is in danger, the endangered person mother is named, the normal formula being "We call upon you to pray for the recovery of "[person's name], son of (or daughter of), [mother's name]".
</p>
