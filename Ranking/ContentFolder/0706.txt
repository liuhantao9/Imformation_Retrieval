<p><b>Thrash metal</b> (or simply <b>thrash</b>) is an <a href="/wiki/Extreme_metal" title="Extreme metal">extreme</a> subgenre of <a href="/wiki/Heavy_metal_music" title="Heavy metal music">heavy metal music</a> characterized by its overall aggression and often fast tempo. The songs usually use fast percussive beats and low-register guitar <a href="/wiki/Riffs" class="mw-redirect" title="Riffs">riffs</a>, overlaid with <a href="/wiki/Shred_guitar" title="Shred guitar">shredding</a>-style lead guitar work. The lyrical subject matter often deals with criticisms of <a href="/wiki/The_Establishment" title="The Establishment">The Establishment</a>, and at times shares a disdain for <a href="/wiki/Christian_dogma" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian dogma">Christian dogma</a> resembling that of their <a href="/wiki/Black_metal" title="Black metal">black metal</a> counterparts. The language is typically quite direct and denunciatory, an approach borrowed from <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_punk" title="Hardcore punk">hardcore punk</a>.
</p><p>The genre emerged in the early 1980s as musicians began fusing the double bass drumming and complex guitar stylings of the <a href="/wiki/New_wave_of_British_heavy_metal" title="New wave of British heavy metal">new wave of British heavy metal</a> (NWOBHM) with the speed and aggression of <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_punk" title="Hardcore punk">hardcore punk</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Philosophically, thrash metal developed as a backlash against both the <a href="/wiki/Conservatism" title="Conservatism">Conservatism</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Reaganomics" title="Reaganomics">Reagan era</a> and the much more moderate, <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a>-influenced and widely accessible heavy metal sub-genre of <a href="/wiki/Glam_metal" title="Glam metal">glam metal</a> which also developed concurrently in the 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinstein200048_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinstein200048-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Thrash metal was an inspiration for subsequent extreme genres such as <a href="/wiki/Death_metal" title="Death metal">death metal</a> and black metal.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrash_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Metallica_London_2008-09-15_Kirk_and_James.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Metallica_London_2008-09-15_Kirk_and_James.jpg/220px-Metallica_London_2008-09-15_Kirk_and_James.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Metallica_London_2008-09-15_Kirk_and_James.jpg/330px-Metallica_London_2008-09-15_Kirk_and_James.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Metallica_London_2008-09-15_Kirk_and_James.jpg/440px-Metallica_London_2008-09-15_Kirk_and_James.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2085" data-file-height="1545" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Metallica_London_2008-09-15_Kirk_and_James.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Kirk_Hammett" title="Kirk Hammett">Kirk Hammett</a> and <a href="/wiki/James_Hetfield" title="James Hetfield">James Hetfield</a> of <a href="/wiki/Metallica" title="Metallica">Metallica</a> (pictured in 2008). Metallica's early work is regarded as essential to the development of the genre in the 1980s.</div></div></div>
<p>Thrash metal generally features fast <a href="/wiki/Tempos" class="mw-redirect" title="Tempos">tempos</a>, low-register, complex guitar <a href="/wiki/Riffs" class="mw-redirect" title="Riffs">riffs</a>, high-register guitar <a href="/wiki/Guitar_solo" title="Guitar solo">solos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Double_bass_drumming" class="mw-redirect" title="Double bass drumming">double bass drumming</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Bowar_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bowar-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The rhythm guitar parts are played with heavy distortion and often <a href="/wiki/Palm_mute" title="Palm mute">palm muted</a> to create a tighter and more precise sound.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPillsbury200620_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPillsbury200620-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Vocally, thrash metal can employ anything from melodic singing to shouted vocals. Most guitar solos are played at high speed and technically demanding, as they are usually characterized by <a href="/wiki/Shred_guitar" title="Shred guitar">shredding</a>, and use advanced techniques such as <a href="/wiki/Sweep_picking" title="Sweep picking">sweep picking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Legato" title="Legato">legato phrasing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alternate_picking" title="Alternate picking">alternate picking</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tremolo_picking" class="mw-redirect" title="Tremolo picking">tremolo picking</a>, <a href="/wiki/String_skipping" title="String skipping">string skipping</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Tapping" title="Tapping">two-hand tapping</a>.
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Anthrax_Rockavaria_2016_(12_von_12).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Anthrax_Rockavaria_2016_%2812_von_12%29.jpg/200px-Anthrax_Rockavaria_2016_%2812_von_12%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="134" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Anthrax_Rockavaria_2016_%2812_von_12%29.jpg/300px-Anthrax_Rockavaria_2016_%2812_von_12%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Anthrax_Rockavaria_2016_%2812_von_12%29.jpg/400px-Anthrax_Rockavaria_2016_%2812_von_12%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3074" data-file-height="2052" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Anthrax_Rockavaria_2016_(12_von_12).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a> band <a href="/wiki/Anthrax_(American_band)" title="Anthrax (American band)">Anthrax</a> were among the earliest and most successful thrash acts</div></div></div>
<p>The guitar riffs often use <a href="/wiki/Chromatic_scale" title="Chromatic scale">chromatic scales</a> and emphasize the tritone and diminished intervals, instead of using conventional single scale based riffing. For example, the intro riff of Metallica's "<a href="/wiki/Master_of_Puppets_(song)" title="Master of Puppets (song)">Master of Puppets</a>" (the title track of the <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Puppets" title="Master of Puppets">namesake album</a>) is a chromatic descent, followed by a chromatic ascent based on the <a href="/wiki/Tritone" title="Tritone">tritone</a>.
</p><p>Speed, pacing and time-changes also define thrash metal. Thrash tends to have an accelerating feel which may be due in large part to its aggressive drumming style. For example, drummers often use two bass drums, or a double-bass pedal, in order to create a relentless, driving beat. Cymbal stops/<a href="/wiki/Cymbal_choke" title="Cymbal choke">chokes</a> are often used to transition from one riff to another or to precede an acceleration in tempo. Some common characteristics of the genre are fast guitar riffs with aggressive picking styles and fast guitar solos, and extensive use of two bass drums as opposed to the conventional use of only one, typical of most rock music.
</p><p>To keep up with the other instruments, many bassists use a <a href="/wiki/Plectrum" title="Plectrum">plectrum</a>. However, some prominent thrash metal bassists have used their fingers, such as <a href="/wiki/Frank_Bello" title="Frank Bello">Frank Bello</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greg_Christian" title="Greg Christian">Greg Christian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Steve_DiGiorgio" class="mw-redirect" title="Steve DiGiorgio">Steve DiGiorgio</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Trujillo" title="Robert Trujillo">Robert Trujillo</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cliff_Burton" title="Cliff Burton">Cliff Burton</a>. Several bassists use a <a href="/wiki/Fuzz_bass" title="Fuzz bass">distorted bass tone</a>, an approach popularized by Burton and Motörhead's <a href="/wiki/Lemmy" title="Lemmy">Lemmy</a>. Lyrical themes in thrash metal include warfare, corruption, injustice, murder, suicide, isolation, alienation, addiction, and other maladies that afflict the individual and society. In addition, politics, particularly pessimism and dissatisfaction towards politics, are common themes among thrash metal bands. Humor and irony can occasionally be found (<a href="/wiki/Anthrax_(American_band)" title="Anthrax (American band)">Anthrax</a> for example), but they are limited, and are exception rather than a rule.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinstein200050-51_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinstein200050-51-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrash_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrash_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Venom_live_at_hellfest.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Venom_live_at_hellfest.jpg/220px-Venom_live_at_hellfest.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Venom_live_at_hellfest.jpg/330px-Venom_live_at_hellfest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/Venom_live_at_hellfest.jpg/440px-Venom_live_at_hellfest.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="333" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Venom_live_at_hellfest.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Venom_(band)" title="Venom (band)">Venom</a>'s early work is considered a major influence on thrash metal.</div></div></div>
<p>Among the earliest songs credited with influencing future thrash musicians was <a href="/wiki/Queen_(band)" title="Queen (band)">Queen</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Stone_Cold_Crazy" title="Stone Cold Crazy">Stone Cold Crazy</a>", recorded and released in 1974. The song was described as being thrash metal "before the term had been invented".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Black_Sabbath" title="Black Sabbath">Black Sabbath</a>'s "<a href="/wiki/Symptom_of_the_Universe_(song)" title="Symptom of the Universe (song)">Symptom of the Universe</a>", released in 1975, is often referred to as a compelling early influence on thrash, and was a direct inspiration for <a href="/wiki/Diamond_Head_(English_band)" title="Diamond Head (English band)">Diamond Head</a>'s pioneering song "<a href="/wiki/Am_I_Evil%3F" title="Am I Evil?">Am I Evil?</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The NWOBHM bands emerging from Britain in the late 1970s further influenced the development of early thrash. The early work of artists such as Diamond Head, <a href="/wiki/Iron_Maiden" title="Iron Maiden">Iron Maiden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Judas_Priest" title="Judas Priest">Judas Priest</a>, <a href="/wiki/Venom_(band)" title="Venom (band)">Venom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mot%C3%B6rhead" title="Motörhead">Motörhead</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tygers_of_Pan_Tang" title="Tygers of Pan Tang">Tygers of Pan Tang</a>, <a href="/wiki/Raven_(band)" class="mw-redirect" title="Raven (band)">Raven</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Angel_Witch" title="Angel Witch">Angel Witch</a>, among others, introduced the fast-pace and intricate musicianship that became core aspects of thrash. <a href="/wiki/Phil_Taylor_(musician)" title="Phil Taylor (musician)">Phil Taylor</a>'s double-bass drumming featured in Motörhead's 1979 song "<a href="/wiki/Overkill_(Mot%C3%B6rhead_song)" title="Overkill (Motörhead song)">Overkill</a>" has been acknowledged by many thrash drummers, most notably <a href="/wiki/Lars_Ulrich" title="Lars Ulrich">Lars Ulrich</a>, as a primary influence on their playing. <a href="/wiki/Metal_Blade_Records" title="Metal Blade Records">Metal Blade Records</a> executive <a href="/wiki/Brian_Slagel" title="Brian Slagel">Brian Slagel</a> played a key role in bringing the emerging genre to a larger audience, as he was responsible for discovering  both <a href="/wiki/Metallica" title="Metallica">Metallica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Slayer" title="Slayer">Slayer</a> and producing their earliest studio recordings.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Void_(band)" title="Void (band)">Void</a> is hailed as one of the earliest examples of hardcore/heavy metal crossover, whose chaotic musical approach is often cited as particularly influential.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Their 1982 <a href="/wiki/Faith/Void_Split" class="mw-redirect" title="Faith/Void Split">split LP</a> with fellow Washington band <a href="/wiki/The_Faith_(American_band)" title="The Faith (American band)">The Faith</a> showed both bands exhibiting quick, fiery, high-speed <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a>. It has been argued that those recordings laid the foundation for early thrash metal, at least in terms of selected tempos.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In Europe, the earliest band of the emerging thrash movement was <a href="/wiki/Venom_(band)" title="Venom (band)">Venom</a> from <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_upon_Tyne" title="Newcastle upon Tyne">Newcastle upon Tyne</a>, formed in 1979. Their 1982 album <i><a href="/wiki/Black_Metal_(album)" class="mw-redirect" title="Black Metal (album)">Black Metal</a></i> has been cited as a major influence on many subsequent genres and bands in the extreme metal world, such as <a href="/wiki/Bathory_(band)" title="Bathory (band)">Bathory</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hellhammer" title="Hellhammer">Hellhammer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slayer" title="Slayer">Slayer</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mayhem_(band)" title="Mayhem (band)">Mayhem</a>. The European scene was almost exclusively influenced by the most aggressive music Germany and England were producing at the time. British bands such as <a href="/wiki/Tank_(band)" title="Tank (band)">Tank</a> and <a href="/wiki/Raven_(band)" class="mw-redirect" title="Raven (band)">Raven</a>, along with German band <a href="/wiki/Accept_(band)" title="Accept (band)">Accept</a>, motivated musicians from central Europe to start bands of their own, eventually producing groups such as <a href="/wiki/Sodom_(band)" title="Sodom (band)">Sodom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kreator" title="Kreator">Kreator</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Destruction_(band)" title="Destruction (band)">Destruction</a> from Germany, as well as Switzerland's <a href="/wiki/Coroner_(band)" title="Coroner (band)">Coroner</a>. The Swedish punk band <a href="/wiki/Warheads_(band)" title="Warheads (band)">Warheads</a> have also been described as a proto-thrash band.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_1980s">Early 1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrash_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1981, <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">Southern California</a> band <a href="/wiki/Leather_Charm" title="Leather Charm">Leather Charm</a> wrote a song entitled "Hit the Lights". Leather Charm soon disbanded and the band's primary songwriter, vocalist/rhythm guitarist <a href="/wiki/James_Hetfield" title="James Hetfield">James Hetfield</a> met drummer <a href="/wiki/Lars_Ulrich" title="Lars Ulrich">Lars Ulrich</a> through a classified advertisement. Together, Hetfield and Ulrich formed <a href="/wiki/Metallica" title="Metallica">Metallica</a>, the first of the "Big Four" thrash bands, with lead guitarist <a href="/wiki/Dave_Mustaine" title="Dave Mustaine">Dave Mustaine</a>, who would later form <a href="/wiki/Megadeth" title="Megadeth">Megadeth</a>, another of the "Big Four" originators of thrash, and bassist <a href="/wiki/Ron_McGovney" title="Ron McGovney">Ron McGovney</a>. McGovney would be replaced by Cliff Burton, and Mustaine was later replaced by <a href="/wiki/Kirk_Hammett" title="Kirk Hammett">Kirk Hammett</a>, and at Burton's insistence the band relocated to the <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">San Francisco Bay Area</a>. Before Metallica had even settled on a definitive lineup, <a href="/wiki/Metal_Blade_Records" title="Metal Blade Records">Metal Blade Records</a> executive <a href="/wiki/Brian_Slagel" title="Brian Slagel">Brian Slagel</a> asked Hetfield and Ulrich (credited as "Mettallica") to record "Hit the Lights" for the first edition of his <i><a href="/wiki/Metal_Massacre" title="Metal Massacre">Metal Massacre</a></i> compilation in 1982. An updated version of "Hit the Lights" would later open their first studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Kill_%27Em_All" title="Kill &#39;Em All">Kill 'Em All</a></i>, released in mid–1983.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The term "thrash metal" was first used in the music press by <i><a href="/wiki/Kerrang!" title="Kerrang!">Kerrang!</a></i> magazine's journalist <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Dome" title="Malcolm Dome">Malcolm Dome</a> while making a reference to the Anthrax song "Metal Thrashing Mad".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Prior to this, <a href="/wiki/Metallica" title="Metallica">Metallica</a> frontman James Hetfield referred to his band's sound as <a href="/wiki/Speed_metal" title="Speed metal">speed metal</a> or <a href="/wiki/Power_metal" title="Power metal">power metal</a>.
</p><p>Another "Big Four" thrash band formed in Southern California in 1981, when guitarists <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Hanneman" title="Jeff Hanneman">Jeff Hanneman</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kerry_King" title="Kerry King">Kerry King</a> met while auditioning for the same band and subsequently decided to form a band of their own. Hanneman and King recruited vocalist/bassist <a href="/wiki/Tom_Araya" title="Tom Araya">Tom Araya</a>, a former <a href="/wiki/Respiratory_therapist" title="Respiratory therapist">respiratory therapist</a>, and drummer <a href="/wiki/Dave_Lombardo" title="Dave Lombardo">Dave Lombardo</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Pizza_delivery" title="Pizza delivery">pizza delivery</a> driver, and <a href="/wiki/Slayer" title="Slayer">Slayer</a> was formed. Slayer was discovered by Metal Blade Records executive Brian Slagel; the band's live performance of Iron Maiden's "Phantom of the Opera" so impressed him that he promptly signed them to his label. In December 1983, less than six months after the release of <i>Kill 'Em All</i>, Slayer released their debut album, <i><a href="/wiki/Show_No_Mercy_(Slayer_album)" class="mw-redirect" title="Show No Mercy (Slayer album)">Show No Mercy</a></i>.
</p><p>In 1982, <a href="/wiki/Stress_(Brazil_band)" class="mw-redirect" title="Stress (Brazil band)">Stress</a> recorded what is considered to be the first Brazilian heavy metal album.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> Roosevelt "Bala" (bass and vocals) claimed this to be the first thrash metal album, since it was recorded before Metallica's <i><a href="/wiki/Kill_%27Em_All" title="Kill &#39;Em All">Kill 'Em All</a></i>. However, later he stated that some compositions have elements of thrash, like the speed, fast alternate picking, and the aggressive vocals and sound. Canada also produced influential <a href="/wiki/Speed_metal" title="Speed metal">speed metal</a> bands such as <a href="/wiki/Annihilator_(band)" title="Annihilator (band)">Annihilator</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anvil_(band)" title="Anvil (band)">Anvil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exciter_(band)" title="Exciter (band)">Exciter</a>, <a href="/wiki/Razor_(band)" title="Razor (band)">Razor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Voivod_(band)" title="Voivod (band)">Voivod</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mid-1980s">Mid-1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrash_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mid-1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The popularity of thrash metal increased in 1984 with the release of Metallica's sophomore record <i><a href="/wiki/Ride_the_Lightning" title="Ride the Lightning">Ride the Lightning</a></i>, as well as Anthrax's debut <i><a href="/wiki/Fistful_of_Metal" title="Fistful of Metal">Fistful of Metal</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Overkill_(band)" title="Overkill (band)">Overkill</a> and Slayer released extended plays on independent labels the same year. This led to a heavier sounding form of thrash, which was reflected in Exodus' <i><a href="/wiki/Bonded_by_Blood" title="Bonded by Blood">Bonded by Blood</a></i> and Slayer's <i><a href="/wiki/Hell_Awaits" title="Hell Awaits">Hell Awaits</a></i>. In 1985, the German band <a href="/wiki/Kreator" title="Kreator">Kreator</a> released their debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Endless_Pain" title="Endless Pain">Endless Pain</a></i> and the Brazilian band <a href="/wiki/Sepultura" title="Sepultura">Sepultura</a> released their EP <i><a href="/wiki/Bestial_Devastation" title="Bestial Devastation">Bestial Devastation</a></i>. Megadeth, which was formed by former Metallica guitarist Dave Mustaine, released their debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Killing_Is_My_Business..._and_Business_Is_Good!" title="Killing Is My Business... and Business Is Good!">Killing Is My Business... and Business Is Good!</a></i>, and Anthrax released the critically acclaimed <i><a href="/wiki/Spreading_the_Disease" title="Spreading the Disease">Spreading the Disease</a></i> in 1985.
</p><p>From a creative standpoint, the year 1986 was perhaps the pinnacle of thrash metal, as a number of critically acclaimed and genre defining albums were released. Metallica's major label debut <i><a href="/wiki/Master_of_Puppets" title="Master of Puppets">Master of Puppets</a></i> was released in March, becoming the first thrash album to be certified platinum, being certified 6× platinum by the Recording Industry Association of America (RIAA). Kreator released <i><a href="/wiki/Pleasure_to_Kill" title="Pleasure to Kill">Pleasure to Kill</a></i> in April, which would later be a major influence on the death metal scene.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Megadeth released <i><a href="/wiki/Peace_Sells..._but_Who%27s_Buying%3F" title="Peace Sells... but Who&#39;s Buying?">Peace Sells... but Who's Buying?</a></i> in September, an album which proved to be the band's commercial and critical breakthrough and which <a href="/wiki/AllMusic" title="AllMusic">AllMusic</a> later cited as "a classic of early thrash".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Slayer" title="Slayer">Slayer</a>, regarded as one of the most sinister thrash metal bands of the early 1980s,<sup id="cite_ref-Rockd_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rockd-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Reliable sources"><span title="The material near this tag may rely on an unreliable source. (March 2016)">unreliable source?</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> released <i><a href="/wiki/Reign_in_Blood" title="Reign in Blood">Reign in Blood</a></i> in October, an album considered by some to have single-handedly inspired the <a href="/wiki/Death_metal" title="Death metal">death metal</a> genre.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Also in October, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Assault" title="Nuclear Assault">Nuclear Assault</a> released their debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Game_Over_(Nuclear_Assault_album)" title="Game Over (Nuclear Assault album)">Game Over</a></i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Late_1980s">Late 1980s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrash_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Late 1980s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Slayer,_The_Fields_of_Rock,_2007.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Slayer%2C_The_Fields_of_Rock%2C_2007.jpg/200px-Slayer%2C_The_Fields_of_Rock%2C_2007.jpg" decoding="async" width="200" height="96" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Slayer%2C_The_Fields_of_Rock%2C_2007.jpg/300px-Slayer%2C_The_Fields_of_Rock%2C_2007.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Slayer%2C_The_Fields_of_Rock%2C_2007.jpg/400px-Slayer%2C_The_Fields_of_Rock%2C_2007.jpg 2x" data-file-width="760" data-file-height="364" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Slayer,_The_Fields_of_Rock,_2007.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Slayer (pictured in 2007) released <i><a href="/wiki/Reign_in_Blood" title="Reign in Blood">Reign in Blood</a></i> in 1986, considered a landmark achievement in the genre's history.</div></div></div>
<p>In 1987, <a href="/wiki/Anthrax_(American_band)" title="Anthrax (American band)">Anthrax</a> released their third album <i><a href="/wiki/Among_the_Living" title="Among the Living">Among the Living</a></i>, which borrowed elements from their two previous releases, with fast guitar riffs and pounding drums. <a href="/wiki/Death_Angel" title="Death Angel">Death Angel</a> took a similar approach with their 1987 debut, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ultra-Violence" title="The Ultra-Violence">The Ultra-Violence</a></i>. In 1988, <a href="/wiki/Suicidal_Tendencies" title="Suicidal Tendencies">Suicidal Tendencies</a>, who had previously been a straightforward <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_punk" title="Hardcore punk">hardcore punk</a> band, released their major label debut <i><a href="/wiki/How_Will_I_Laugh_Tomorrow_When_I_Can%27t_Even_Smile_Today" title="How Will I Laugh Tomorrow When I Can&#39;t Even Smile Today">How Will I Laugh Tomorrow When I Can't Even Smile Today</a>,</i> which helped pioneer the <a href="/wiki/Crossover_thrash" title="Crossover thrash">crossover thrash</a> genre.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sepultura" title="Sepultura">Sepultura</a>'s third album, <i><a href="/wiki/Beneath_the_Remains" title="Beneath the Remains">Beneath the Remains</a></i>, earned them some mainstream appeal as it was released by <a href="/wiki/Roadrunner_Records" title="Roadrunner Records">Roadrunner Records</a>. <a href="/wiki/Testament_(band)" title="Testament (band)">Testament</a> continued through the late 1980s with <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Order_(album)" title="The New Order (album)">The New Order</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Practice_What_You_Preach" title="Practice What You Preach">Practice What You Preach</a></i>, both albums showing the band's musical growth and gaining Testament nearly the same level of popularity as the "Big Four".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Vio-lence" title="Vio-lence">Vio-lence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forbidden_(band)" title="Forbidden (band)">Forbidden</a>, two relative latecomers to the Bay Area thrash metal scene, released their debut albums <i><a href="/wiki/Eternal_Nightmare_(Vio-lence_album)" title="Eternal Nightmare (Vio-lence album)">Eternal Nightmare</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Forbidden_Evil_(album)" title="Forbidden Evil (album)">Forbidden Evil</a></i> in 1988. Canadian thrashers <a href="/wiki/Annihilator_(band)" title="Annihilator (band)">Annihilator</a> released their highly technical debut <i><a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Hell" title="Alice in Hell">Alice in Hell</a></i> in 1989, which was praised for its fast riffs and extended guitar solos. <a href="/wiki/Sadus" title="Sadus">Sadus</a> appeared later, demonstrating a sound which was primarily driven by the fretless bass of Steve DiGiorgio. In Germany, Sodom released <i><a href="/wiki/Agent_Orange_(album)" title="Agent Orange (album)">Agent Orange</a></i>, and Kreator would release <i><a href="/wiki/Extreme_Aggression" title="Extreme Aggression">Extreme Aggression</a></i>.
</p><p>From 1987 to 1989, Overkill released <i><a href="/wiki/Taking_Over_(Overkill_album)" title="Taking Over (Overkill album)">Taking Over</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Under_the_Influence_(Overkill_album)" title="Under the Influence (Overkill album)">Under the Influence</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Years_of_Decay" title="The Years of Decay">The Years of Decay</a></i>, three albums considered their best. In 1988, Slayer released <i><a href="/wiki/South_of_Heaven" title="South of Heaven">South of Heaven</a></i>, Megadeth released <i><a href="/wiki/So_Far,_So_Good..._So_What!" title="So Far, So Good... So What!">So Far, So Good... So What!</a></i>, Anthrax released <i><a href="/wiki/State_of_Euphoria" title="State of Euphoria">State of Euphoria</a></i> while Metallica's <i><a href="/wiki/...And_Justice_for_All_(album)" title="...And Justice for All (album)">...And Justice for All</a></i> spawned the band's first video, the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>-themed song "<a href="/wiki/One_(Metallica_song)" title="One (Metallica song)">One</a>".
</p><p>A substantial number of thrash metal groups pay tribute to <a href="/wiki/Punk_rock" title="Punk rock">punk rock</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_punk" title="Hardcore punk">hardcore punk</a>. Metallica has covered <a href="/wiki/Discharge_(band)" title="Discharge (band)">Discharge</a> ("Free Speech for the Dumb"), <a href="/wiki/Anti-Nowhere_League" title="Anti-Nowhere League">Anti-Nowhere League</a> ("So What?"), <a href="/wiki/Killing_Joke" title="Killing Joke">Killing Joke</a> ("the Wait"), <a href="/wiki/Ramones" title="Ramones">Ramones</a> ("53rd &amp; 3rd", among others), and <a href="/wiki/Misfits_(band)" title="Misfits (band)">The Misfits</a> ("Die Die my Darling", "<a href="/wiki/Last_Caress" title="Last Caress">Last Caress</a>/Green Hell"),<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> and Slayer recorded <i><a href="/wiki/Undisputed_Attitude" title="Undisputed Attitude">Undisputed Attitude</a></i>, an album of punk rock covers, including <a href="/wiki/Minor_Threat" title="Minor Threat">Minor Threat</a>, early <a href="/wiki/Dirty_Rotten_Imbeciles" title="Dirty Rotten Imbeciles">DRI</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Iggy_and_the_Stooges" class="mw-redirect" title="Iggy and the Stooges">Iggy and the Stooges</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Megadeth have covered two <a href="/wiki/Sex_Pistols" title="Sex Pistols">Sex Pistols</a> songs ("<a href="/wiki/Anarchy_in_the_UK" class="mw-redirect" title="Anarchy in the UK">Anarchy in the UK</a>" and "Problems"), as have Anthrax ("<a href="/wiki/God_Save_the_Queen_(Sex_Pistols_song)" title="God Save the Queen (Sex Pistols song)">God Save the Queen</a>" and "Friggin' in the Riggin"). Anthrax have also covered "Protest and Survive" by Discharge on their album <i><a href="/wiki/Attack_of_the_Killer_B%27s" title="Attack of the Killer B&#39;s">Attack of the Killer B's</a></i>, "<a href="/wiki/Rocket_to_Russia" title="Rocket to Russia">We're a Happy Family</a>" by Ramones, and "New Noise" by the Swedish band <a href="/wiki/Refused" title="Refused">Refused</a> as a hidden track on <i><a href="/wiki/Worship_Music_(album)" title="Worship Music (album)">Worship Music</a></i>. Overkill have covered the Sex Pistols ("<a href="/wiki/Never_Mind_the_Bollocks" class="mw-redirect" title="Never Mind the Bollocks">No Feelings</a>"), Ramones ("<a href="/wiki/Road_to_Ruin_(Ramones_album)" title="Road to Ruin (Ramones album)">I'm Against it</a>"), and <a href="/wiki/Dead_Boys" title="Dead Boys">Dead Boys</a> ("<a href="/wiki/Sonic_Reducer" title="Sonic Reducer">Sonic Reducer</a>", and "Ain't Nothing to Do"). In addition, <a href="/wiki/Pantera" title="Pantera">Pantera</a> covered <a href="/wiki/Poison_Idea" title="Poison Idea">Poison Idea</a> ("the Badge").<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="1990s">1990s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrash_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1990s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A number of more typical but technically sophisticated albums were released in 1990, including Megadeth's <i><a href="/wiki/Rust_in_Peace" title="Rust in Peace">Rust in Peace</a></i>, Anthrax's <i><a href="/wiki/Persistence_of_Time" title="Persistence of Time">Persistence of Time</a></i>, Slayer's <i><a href="/wiki/Seasons_in_the_Abyss" title="Seasons in the Abyss">Seasons in the Abyss</a></i>, Suicidal Tendencies' <i><a href="/wiki/Lights...Camera...Revolution!" title="Lights...Camera...Revolution!">Lights...Camera...Revolution!</a></i>, Testament's <i><a href="/wiki/Souls_of_Black" title="Souls of Black">Souls of Black</a></i>, Kreator's <i><a href="/wiki/Coma_of_Souls" title="Coma of Souls">Coma of Souls</a></i>, Destruction's <i><a href="/wiki/Cracked_Brain" title="Cracked Brain">Cracked Brain</a></i>, Forbidden's <i><a href="/wiki/Twisted_into_Form" title="Twisted into Form">Twisted into Form</a></i>, Exodus' <i><a href="/wiki/Impact_Is_Imminent" title="Impact Is Imminent">Impact Is Imminent</a></i>, and the more groove-oriented <a href="/wiki/Pantera" title="Pantera">Pantera</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Cowboys_from_Hell" title="Cowboys from Hell">Cowboys from Hell</a></i>. All of those albums were commercial high points for the aforementioned artists. Many of these bands embarked on a group tour called the <a href="/wiki/Clash_of_the_Titans_tour" class="mw-redirect" title="Clash of the Titans tour">"Clash of the Titans"</a> the same year. Several albums that continued this style, which had come to be known as technical thrash metal, were released in 1991, such as Overkill's <i><a href="/wiki/Horrorscope_(Overkill_album)" title="Horrorscope (Overkill album)">Horrorscope</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Heathen_(band)" title="Heathen (band)">Heathen</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Victims_of_Deception" title="Victims of Deception">Victims of Deception</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Dark_Angel_(band)" title="Dark Angel (band)">Dark Angel</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Time_Does_Not_Heal" title="Time Does Not Heal">Time Does Not Heal</a></i>, Sepultura's <i><a href="/wiki/Arise_(Sepultura_album)" title="Arise (Sepultura album)">Arise</a></i>, and Coroner's <i><a href="/wiki/Mental_Vortex" title="Mental Vortex">Mental Vortex</a></i>.
</p><p>Later in 1991, Metallica released their <a href="/wiki/Metallica_(album)" title="Metallica (album)">eponymous album</a>, known as "The Black Album". The album marked a stylistic change in the band, eliminating much of the speed and longer song structures of the band's previous work, and instead focusing on more concise and slower songs. It would go on to become the band's best selling album, and began a wave of thrash metal bands releasing more commercially oriented albums.
</p><p>After the commercial and artistic climax for the genre, the energy of the thrash metal was exhausted and it was overtaken by the rising <a href="/wiki/Alternative_metal" title="Alternative metal">alternative metal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grunge" title="Grunge">grunge</a> movements.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> In the 1990s many veteran thrash metal bands began changing to more accessible, radio-friendly styles.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Metallica was a notable example of this shift, particularly with their mid–to–late 1990s albums <i><a href="/wiki/Load_(album)" title="Load (album)">Load</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Reload_(Metallica_album)" title="Reload (Metallica album)">ReLoad</a></i>, which displayed minor <a href="/wiki/Blues" title="Blues">blues</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southern_rock" title="Southern rock">southern rock</a> influences, and were seen as a major departure from the band's earlier sound.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESharpe-Young2007256_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESharpe-Young2007256-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Megadeth took a more accessible heavy metal route starting with their 1992 album <i><a href="/wiki/Countdown_to_Extinction" title="Countdown to Extinction">Countdown to Extinction</a></i>,<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESharpe-Young2007241_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESharpe-Young2007241-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> and Testament released the melodic <i><a href="/wiki/The_Ritual_(Testament_album)" title="The Ritual (Testament album)">The Ritual</a></i> in 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As further <a href="/wiki/Extreme_metal" title="Extreme metal">extreme metal</a> genres came to prominence in the 1990s (<a href="/wiki/Industrial_metal" title="Industrial metal">industrial metal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Death_metal" title="Death metal">death metal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Black_metal" title="Black metal">black metal</a> each finding their own fanbase), the heavy metal "family tree" soon found itself blending aesthetics and styles.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> For example, bands with all the musical traits of thrash metal began using <a href="/wiki/Death_growls" class="mw-redirect" title="Death growls">death growls</a>, a vocal style borrowed from death metal, while black metal bands often utilized the airy feel of <a href="/wiki/Synthesizers" class="mw-redirect" title="Synthesizers">synthesizers</a>, popularized in industrial metal. Today the placing of bands within distinct subgenres remains a source of contention for heavy metal fans, however, little debate resides over the fact that thrash metal is the sole proprietor of its respective spinoffs.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="2000s_and_2010s">2000s and 2010s</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrash_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2000s and 2010s">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many 1980s-era thrash bands who split or were inactive during the 1990s – such as <a href="/wiki/Dark_Angel_(band)" title="Dark Angel (band)">Dark Angel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Death_Angel" title="Death Angel">Death Angel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Assault" title="Nuclear Assault">Nuclear Assault</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Forbidden_(band)" title="Forbidden (band)">Forbidden</a> – reunited in the 2000s. Notable bands returned to their roots with releases such as Metallica's <i><a href="/wiki/Death_Magnetic" title="Death Magnetic">Death Magnetic</a></i> (2008), Megadeth's <i><a href="/wiki/Endgame_(Megadeth_album)" title="Endgame (Megadeth album)">Endgame</a></i> (2009), Slayer's <i><a href="/wiki/World_Painted_Blood" title="World Painted Blood">World Painted Blood</a></i> (2009), Exodus' <i><a href="/wiki/Exhibit_B:_The_Human_Condition" title="Exhibit B: The Human Condition">Exhibit B: The Human Condition</a></i> (2010), Anthrax's <i><a href="/wiki/Worship_Music_(album)" title="Worship Music (album)">Worship Music</a></i> (2011), Overkill's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Electric_Age" title="The Electric Age">The Electric Age</a></i> (2012), Testament's <i><a href="/wiki/Dark_Roots_of_Earth" title="Dark Roots of Earth">Dark Roots of Earth</a></i> (2012), and Flotsam and Jetsam's <i><a href="/wiki/Ugly_Noise" title="Ugly Noise">Ugly Noise</a></i> (2012).
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Genre_spinoffs">Genre spinoffs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrash_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Genre spinoffs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thrash metal is directly responsible for the development of underground metal genres, such as <a href="/wiki/Death_metal" title="Death metal">death metal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Black_metal" title="Black metal">black metal</a><sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWeinstein2000288_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWeinstein2000288-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Groove_metal" title="Groove metal">groove metal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BestPanteraCDs_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BestPanteraCDs-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> In addition to this, <a href="/wiki/Metalcore" title="Metalcore">metalcore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Grindcore" title="Grindcore">grindcore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deathcore" title="Deathcore">deathcore</a> employ similar riffs in their composition, the former with more focus on melody rather than chromaticism. The blending of punk ethos and metal's brutal nature led to even more extreme, underground styles after thrash metal began gaining mild commercial success in the late 1980s.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESharpe-Young2007162_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESharpe-Young2007162-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> With gorier subject matter, heavier downtuning of guitars, more consistent use of <a href="/wiki/Blast_beats" class="mw-redirect" title="Blast beats">blast beat drumming</a>, and darker, atonal <a href="/wiki/Death_growls" class="mw-redirect" title="Death growls">death growls</a>, death metal was established in the mid-1980s. Black metal, also related to thrash metal, has emerged at the same time, with many black metal bands taking influence from thrash metal bands such as <a href="/wiki/Venom_(band)" title="Venom (band)">Venom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESharpe-Young2007208_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESharpe-Young2007208-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Black metal continued deviating from thrash metal, often providing more orchestral overtones, open <a href="/wiki/Tremolo_picking" class="mw-redirect" title="Tremolo picking">tremolo picking</a>, blast beat drumming, <a href="/wiki/Screaming_(music)#Black_metal" title="Screaming (music)">shrieked</a> or raspy vocals and <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">pagan</a> or <a href="/wiki/Occultism" class="mw-redirect" title="Occultism">occult</a>-based aesthetics to distinguish itself from thrash metal. Thrash metal would later combine with its spinoffs, thus giving rise to genres like <a href="/wiki/Blackened_thrash_metal" class="mw-redirect" title="Blackened thrash metal">blackened thrash metal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Deathrash" class="mw-redirect" title="Deathrash">deathrash</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-loudwire.com_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-loudwire.com-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Deathrash_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Deathrash-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ekeroth_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ekeroth-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Groove metal takes the intensity and sonic qualities of thrash metal and plays them at mid-<a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">tempo</a>, with most bands making only occasional forays into fast tempo,<sup id="cite_ref-BestPanteraCDs_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BestPanteraCDs-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> but since the early 1990s it started to favor a more <a href="/wiki/Death_metal" title="Death metal">death metal</a>–derived sound.<sup id="cite_ref-Sound_of_the_Beast_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sound_of_the_Beast-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> Thrash metal with stronger punk elements is called <a href="/wiki/Crossover_thrash" title="Crossover thrash">crossover thrash</a>. Its overall sound is more punk-influenced than traditional thrash metal, but has more heavy metal elements than <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_punk" title="Hardcore punk">hardcore punk</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thrashcore" title="Thrashcore">thrashcore</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_scenes">Regional scenes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thrash_metal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Regional scenes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Thrash metal emerged predominantly from a handful of regional scenes, each of which was generally distinguished by the unique characteristics of its bands.
</p>
<ul><li><i><b><a href="/wiki/Bay_Area_thrash_metal" title="Bay Area thrash metal">Bay Area thrash metal</a></b></i>: In addition to being the most commercially successful, <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Bay_Area" title="San Francisco Bay Area">San Francisco Bay Area</a> thrash tended to be the most progressive and technical of the major regional thrash scenes, being strongly <a href="/wiki/New_wave_of_British_heavy_metal" title="New wave of British heavy metal">NWOBHM</a> influenced. <a href="/wiki/Metallica" title="Metallica">Metallica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Testament_(band)" title="Testament (band)">Testament</a>, <a href="/wiki/Exodus_(American_band)" title="Exodus (American band)">Exodus</a>, <a href="/wiki/Death_Angel" title="Death Angel">Death Angel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vio-lence" title="Vio-lence">Vio-lence</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Forbidden_(band)" title="Forbidden (band)">Forbidden</a> are prominent examples of bands to emerge from this region.</li>
<li><i><b>East Coast thrash metal</b></i>: Centered in <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, the East Coast's thrash metal tended to display a sound that incorporated a strong <a href="/wiki/Hardcore_punk" title="Hardcore punk">hardcore punk</a> influence. An emphasis was placed on aggression and speed rather than technicality. <a href="/wiki/Anthrax_(American_band)" title="Anthrax (American band)">Anthrax</a>, <a href="/wiki/Overkill_(band)" title="Overkill (band)">Overkill</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Assault" title="Nuclear Assault">Nuclear Assault</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toxik" title="Toxik">Toxik</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Whiplash_(band)" title="Whiplash (band)">Whiplash</a> exemplified the style to emerge from this regional scene.</li>
<li><i><b>British thrash metal</b></i>: The British thrash bands leaned towards a more <a href="/wiki/Traditional_heavy_metal" class="mw-redirect" title="Traditional heavy metal">traditional heavy metal</a> approach, often heavier though less aggressive than their American counterparts. The most notable bands from this scene are <a href="/wiki/Onslaught_(band)" title="Onslaught (band)">Onslaught</a>, <a href="/wiki/Xentrix" title="Xentrix">Xentrix</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sabbat_(English_band)" title="Sabbat (English band)">Sabbat</a>.</li>
<li><i><b><a href="/wiki/Brazilian_thrash_metal" title="Brazilian thrash metal">Brazilian thrash metal</a></b></i>: The Brazilian thrash scene is notable for producing a handful of bands that would become principal parts of thrash metal's prevalence in the early 1990s. There were three scenes where Brazilian thrash metal was originated: Belo Horizonte (the most prominent), São Paulo, and Rio de Janeiro. The most famous bands from this scene are <a href="/wiki/Sepultura" title="Sepultura">Sepultura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_Atl%C3%A2ntica" title="Dorsal Atlântica">Dorsal Atlântica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Executer" title="Executer">Executer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chakal" title="Chakal">Chakal</a>, <a href="/wiki/MX_(band)" title="MX (band)">MX</a>, <a href="/wiki/Korzus" title="Korzus">Korzus</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Sarc%C3%B3fago" title="Sarcófago">Sarcófago</a>.</li>
<li><i><b><a href="/wiki/Teutonic_thrash_metal" title="Teutonic thrash metal">Teutonic thrash metal</a></b></i>: The German and Swiss region spawned dozens of bands since the mid-1980s that developed its own style. The most prominent bands from this scene are <a href="/wiki/Kreator" title="Kreator">Kreator</a>, <a href="/wiki/Destruction_(band)" title="Destruction (band)">Destruction</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sodom_(band)" title="Sodom (band)">Sodom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tankard_(band)" title="Tankard (band)">Tankard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Coroner_(band)" title="Coroner (band)">Coroner</a>, <a href="/wiki/Holy_Moses" title="Holy Moses">Holy Moses</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Exumer" title="Exumer">Exumer</a>.</li>
<li><i><b>Australian thrash metal</b></i>: Despite being largely removed from the major regions, Australia developed a strong local scene in the 1980s and has undergone a renewal throughout the 2010s. Two of the most notable Australian thrash bands are <a href="/wiki/Mortal_Sin_(band)" title="Mortal Sin (band)">Mortal Sin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hobbs%27_Angel_of_Death" title="Hobbs&#39; Angel of Death">Hobbs' Angel of Death</a>.</li></ul>
