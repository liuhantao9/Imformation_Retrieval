		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Arabic names</b> were historically based on a long naming system; most <a href="/wiki/Arab" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab">Arabs</a> did not have <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given</a>/<a href="/wiki/Middle_name" title="Middle name">middle</a>/<a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family names</a>, but a full chain of names. <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_structure">Name structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ism"><i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Ism</i></i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">ism</i></i> (<span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">اسم</span>), is the given name, first name, or personal name; e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Ahmad" title="Ahmad">Ahmad</a>" or "<a href="/wiki/Fatimah" title="Fatimah">Fatimah</a>". Most Arabic names have meaning as ordinary <a href="/wiki/Adjectives" class="mw-redirect" title="Adjectives">adjectives</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nouns" class="mw-redirect" title="Nouns">nouns</a>, and are often aspirational of character. For example, <i><a href="/wiki/Muhammad_(name)" title="Muhammad (name)">Muhammad</a></i> means 'Praiseworthy' and <i><a href="/wiki/Ali" title="Ali">Ali</a></i> means 'Exalted' or 'High'.
</p><p>The syntactic context will generally differentiate the name from the noun/adjective. However Arabic newspapers will occasionally place names in brackets, or quotation marks, to avoid confusion.
</p><p>Indeed such is the popularity of the name <i>Muhammad</i> throughout parts of <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arabia" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabia">Arabia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, it is often  represented by the abbreviation "Md.", "Mohd.", "Muhd.", or just "M.".  In India, Pakistan, <a href="/wiki/Bangladeshi_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Bangladeshi name">Bangladesh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysian_names" title="Malaysian names">Malaysia</a>, Indonesia and the Philippines, due to its almost ubiquitous use as a first name, a person will often be referred to by their second name:
</p>
<ul><li>Md. Dinar ibn Raihan</li>
<li>Mohd. Umair Tanvir</li>
<li>Md. Osman</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nasab"><i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Nasab</i></i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nasab">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Patronymic#Arabic" title="Patronymic">Patronymic §&#160;Arabic</a></div>
<p>The <i>nasab</i> (<span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">نسب</span>) is a <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a> or series of patronymics.  It indicates the person's heritage by the word <i>ibn</i> (<span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">ابن</span> "son", colloquially <i>bin</i>) or <i>ibnat</i> ( "daughter", also <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">بنت</span> <i>bint</i>, abbreviated <i>bte.</i>).
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun" title="Ibn Khaldun">Ibn Khaldun</a> (<span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">ابن خلدون</span>) means "son of Khaldun". Khaldun is the father's personal name or, in this particular case, the name of a remote ancestor.
</p><p>Several <i>nasab</i> names can follow in a chain to trace a person's ancestry backwards in time, as was important in the <a href="/wiki/Tribe" title="Tribe">tribally</a> based society of the ancient Arabs, both for purposes of identification and for socio-political interactions. Today, however, <i>ibn</i> or <i>bint</i> is no longer used (unless it is the official naming style in a country, region, etc.: Adnen bin Abdallah). The plural is <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">'Abnā</i></i> for males and <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Banāt</i></i> for females. However, <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Banu</i></i> or <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Bani</i></i> is tribal and encompasses both sexes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Laqab"><i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Laqab</i></i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Laqab">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span id="Laqab"></span>
The <i><a href="/wiki/Laqab" class="mw-redirect" title="Laqab">laqab</a></i> (<span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">لقب</span>),&#160;pl. <i>alqāb</i> (<span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">القاب</span>); <a href="/wiki/Agnomen" title="Agnomen">agnomen</a>; <a href="/wiki/Cognomen" title="Cognomen">cognomen</a>; nickname; title, honorific; last name, <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surname</a>, family name.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The <i>laqab</i>  is typically descriptive of the person.
</p><p>An example is the <a href="/wiki/Abbasid_Caliphate" title="Abbasid Caliphate">Abbasid caliph</a> <a href="/wiki/Harun_al-Rashid" title="Harun al-Rashid">Harun al-Rashid</a> (of <i><a href="/wiki/One_Thousand_and_One_Nights" title="One Thousand and One Nights">One Thousand and One Nights</a></i> fame). <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Harun</i></i> is the Arabic version of the name <i><a href="/wiki/Aaron" title="Aaron">Aaron</a></i> and <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">al-Rasheed</i></i> means "the Rightly-Guided".
</p><p>In ancient Arab societies, use of a <i>laqab</i> was common, but today is restricted to the surname, or family name, of birth.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nisbah"><i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Nisbah</i></i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nisbah">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nisbat_(onomastics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nisbat (onomastics)">Nisbat (onomastics)</a></div>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Nisbat_(onomastics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nisbat (onomastics)">nisbah</a></i> (<span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">نسبة</span>) surname could be an everyday name, but is mostly the name of the ancestral tribe, city, country, or any other term used to show relevance. It follows a family through several generations. It most often appears as a demonym (ex. البغدادي "Al-Baghdadi", meaning that the person is of Baghdad or descendant of people from Baghdad).
</p><p>The <i>laqab</i> and <i>nisbah</i> are similar in use, thus, a name rarely contains both.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Kunya"><i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Kunya</i></i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Kunya">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Kunya_(Arabic)" title="Kunya (Arabic)">Kunya (Arabic)</a></div>
<p>A <i><a href="/wiki/Kunya_(Arabic)" title="Kunya (Arabic)">kunya</a></i> (<a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic language">Arabic</a>: <span lang="ar" dir="rtl">كنية</span>&#8206;, <i>kunyah</i>)<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Teknonym" class="mw-redirect" title="Teknonym">teknonym</a> in Arabic names. It is a component of an Arabic name, a type of <a href="/wiki/Epithet" title="Epithet">epithet</a>, in theory referring to the bearer's first-born son or daughter. By extension, it may also have hypothetical or metaphorical references, e.g. in a <i><a href="/wiki/Nom_de_guerre" class="mw-redirect" title="Nom de guerre">nom de guerre</a></i> or a nickname, without literally referring to a son or a daughter.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> For example, Sabri Khalil al-Banna was known as <a href="/wiki/Abu_Nidal" title="Abu Nidal">Abu Nidal</a>, "father of struggle".
</p><p>Use of a kunya implies a familiar but respectful setting.
</p><p>A kunya is expressed by the use of <i><a href="/wiki/Abu_(Arabic_term)" class="mw-redirect" title="Abu (Arabic term)">abū</a></i> (father) or <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Umm_(Arabic_term)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Umm (Arabic term) (page does not exist)">umm</a></i> (mother) in a <a href="/wiki/Idafah" class="mw-redirect" title="Idafah">genitive construction</a>, i.e. "father of" or "mother of" as an <a href="/wiki/Honorific" title="Honorific">honorific</a> in place of or alongside <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given names</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Arab_world" title="Arab world">Arab world</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Islamic_world" class="mw-redirect" title="Islamic world">Islamic world</a> more generally.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A kunya may also be a nickname expressing the attachment of an individual to a certain thing, as in <a href="/wiki/Abu_Bakr" title="Abu Bakr">Abu Bakr</a>, "father of the camel foal", given because of this person's kindness towards camels.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Common_naming_practices">Common naming practices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Common naming practices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Arabic_theophoric_names" title="List of Arabic theophoric names">List of Arabic theophoric names</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arab_Muslim">Arab Muslim</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Arab Muslim">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A common name-form among Arab Muslims is the prefix <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization"><a href="/wiki/Abd_(Arabic)" title="Abd (Arabic)">ʿAbd</a></i></i> ("slave", <abbr title="feminine">fem.</abbr> <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">ʿAmah</i></i>) combined with the name of <a href="/wiki/Allah" title="Allah">Allah</a> (God), <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Abdullah</i></i> (<span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">عبد الله</span> "slave of God"), or with one of the <a href="/wiki/Names_of_God_in_Islam" title="Names of God in Islam">epithets of Allah</a>. 
</p><p>As a mark of deference, <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">ʿAbd</i></i> is usually not conjoined with the prophets' names.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Nonetheless such names are accepted in some areas.  Its use is not exclusive to Muslims and throughout all Arab countries, the name <i>Abdel-Massih</i>, "Servant of Christ", is a common Christian last name.
</p><p>During the Persian <a href="/wiki/Ghurid_dynasty" title="Ghurid dynasty">Ghurid dynasty</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amir_Suri" title="Amir Suri">Amir Suri</a> and his son <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Suri" title="Muhammad ibn Suri">Muhammad ibn Suri</a> adopted Muslim names despite being non-Muslims.  Other non-Muslim peoples, such as the <a href="/wiki/Kalash_people" title="Kalash people">Kalash</a>, also take names such as Muhammad.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Converts to Islam may often continue using the native non-Arabic non-Islamic names that are without any polytheistic connotation, or association.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arab_Christian">Arab Christian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Arab Christian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To an extent <a href="/wiki/Arab_Christians" title="Arab Christians">Arab Christians</a> have names indistinguishable from Muslims, excepting some explicitly Islamic names, e.g. <i>Muhammad</i>. Some common Christian names are:
</p>
<ul><li>Arabic versions of Christian names (e.g. saints' names: <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Buṭrus</i></i> for <i><a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Saint Peter</a></i>).</li>
<li>Names of <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a>, <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">Armenian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Aramaic language">Aramaic</a> or <a href="/wiki/Neo-Aramaic_languages" title="Neo-Aramaic languages">Neo-Aramaic</a> origin.</li>
<li>Use of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> names, especially <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, Greek and, to a lesser extent, <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">Spanish</a> ones (in <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>). This has been a relatively recent centuries-long convention for Christian Arabs, especially in the <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">Levant</a>. For example: <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Edd%C3%A9" title="Émile Eddé">Émile Eddé</a>, <a href="/wiki/George_Habash" title="George Habash">George Habash</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Helou" title="Charles Helou">Charles Helou</a>, <a href="/wiki/Camille_Chamoun" title="Camille Chamoun">Camille Chamoun</a>.</li>
<li>Names in honor of Jesus Christ:</li></ul>
<dl><dd><i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Abd al-Yasuʿ</i></i> (<abbr title="masculine">masc.</abbr> ) / <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Amat al-Yasuʿ</i></i> (<abbr title="feminine">fem.</abbr>) ("Slave of Jesus")</dd>
<dd><i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Abd al-Masiḥ</i></i> (masc.) / <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Amat al-Masiḥ</i></i> (fem.) ("Slave of the <a href="/wiki/Messiah" title="Messiah">Messiah</a>")</dd>
<dd>Derivations of <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Maseeḥ</i></i> ("Messiah"): <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Masūḥun</i></i> ("Most Anointed"), <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Amsāḥ</i></i> ("More Anointed"), <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Mamsūḥ</i></i> "Anointed" and <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Musayḥ</i></i> "Infant Christ". The <a href="/wiki/Semitic_root" title="Semitic root">root</a>, <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">M-S-Ḥ</i></i>, means "to anoint" (as in <i><a href="/wiki/Masah" title="Masah">masah</a></i>) and is cognate to the <a href="/wiki/Messiah_in_Judaism" title="Messiah in Judaism">Hebrew <i><i lang="he-Latn" title="Hebrew-language romanization">Mashiah</i></i></a>.</dd></dl>
<ul><li><i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Abd al-Ilāh</i></i> ("Worshipper of God") is a Christian equivalent to the common Muslim name Abdullah.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynastic_or_family_name">Dynastic or family name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Dynastic or family name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some people, especially in the Arabian Peninsula, when descendant of a famous ancestor, start their last name with <i>Āl</i> "family, clan" (<span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">آل</span>&#8206;), like the <a href="/wiki/House_of_Saud" title="House of Saud">House of Saud</a> <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">ﺁل سعود</span> <i>Āl Ṣaʻūd</i> or <a href="/wiki/Al_ash-Sheikh" title="Al ash-Sheikh">Al ash-Sheikh</a> ("family of the <a href="/wiki/Sheikh" title="Sheikh">sheikh</a>"). <i>Āl</i> is distinct from the <a href="/wiki/Al-" class="mw-redirect" title="Al-">definite article</a> (<span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">ال</span>&#8206;).  If a reliably-sourced version of the Arabic spelling includes <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">آل</span>&#8206; (as a separate graphic word), then this is <em>not</em> a case of the definite article, so <i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Al</i> (capitalised and followed by a space, not a hyphen) should be used. <i>Ahl</i>, which has a similar meaning, is sometimes used and should be used if the Arabic spelling is <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">أهل</span>&#8206;.
</p><p>Dynasty membership alone does <em>not</em> necessarily imply that the dynastic <span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">آل</span>&#8206; is used – e.g. <a href="/wiki/Bashar_al-Assad" title="Bashar al-Assad">Bashar al-Assad</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Example">Example</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Example">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><big><b><span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">محمد بن سلمان بن امین الفارسی</span></b></big><br />
Muḥammad ibn Salmān ibn Amīn al-Farsī<br />
</p>
<dl><dd><i>Ism</i> - Muḥammad (proper name, <abbr title="literally">lit.</abbr> "praised")</dd>
<dd><i>Nasab</i> - Salmān (father's name, lit. "secure")</dd>
<dd><i>Nasab</i> - Amīn (grandfather's name, "trustworthy")</dd>
<dd><i>Nisbah</i> - al-Farsī ("the Persian").</dd></dl>
<p>"Muḥammad, son of Salmān, son of Amīn, the Persian"
</p><p>This person would simply be referred to as "Muḥammad" or by his <a href="/wiki/Kunya_(Arabic)" title="Kunya (Arabic)">kunya</a>, which relates him to his first-born son, e.g. <i>Abū Karīm</i> "father of Karīm". To signify respect or to specify which Muḥammad one is speaking about, the name could be lengthened to the extent necessary or desired.
</p>

<p>Non-Arabic speakers often make these mistakes:
</p>
<ul><li>Separating "the X of Y" word combinations (see <a href="/wiki/I%E1%B8%8D%C4%81fah" title="Iḍāfah">iḍāfah</a>):
<ul><li>With "Abdul": Arabic names may be written "Abdul (something)", but "Abdul" means "servant of the" and is not, by itself, a name. Thus for example, to address Abdul Rahman bin Omar al-Ahmad by his given name, one says "Abdul Rahman", not merely "Abdul". If he introduces himself as "Abdul Rahman" (which means "the servant of the Merciful"), one does not say "Mr. Rahman" (as "Rahman" is not a family name but part of his [<a href="/wiki/Theophoric" class="mw-redirect" title="Theophoric">theophoric</a>] personal name); instead it would be Mr. al-Ahmad, the latter being the family name.</li>
<li>People not familiar with Arabic <a href="/wiki/Sandhi" title="Sandhi">sandhi</a> in <i>iḍāfah</i>: <i>Habībullāh</i> = "beloved (<i>Habīb</i>) of (<i>ul</i>) God (<i><a href="/wiki/All%C4%81h" class="mw-redirect" title="Allāh">Allāh</a></i>)"; here a person may in error report the man's name as "forename <i>Habib</i>, surname <i>Ullah</i>". Likewise, people may confuse a name such as Jalālu-d-dīn ("The majesty of the religion") as being "Jalal Uddin", or "Mr. Uddin", when "Uddin" is not a surname, but the second half of a two-word name (the desinence <i>-u</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Construct_state" title="Construct state">construct state</a> nominative, plus the <a href="/wiki/Al-" class="mw-redirect" title="Al-">article</a>, appearing as <i>-d-</i>, plus the genitive <i>dīn[i]</i>).  To add to the confusion, some immigrants to Western countries have adopted Uddin as a surname, although it is grammatically incorrect in Arabic outside the context of the associated "first name". Even <a href="/wiki/Indian_Muslims" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian Muslims">Indian Muslims</a> commit the same error. If a person's name is Abd-ul-Rahim ("servant of <a href="/wiki/R-%E1%B8%A4-M" title="R-Ḥ-M">the Merciful</a>"), others may call him Mr. Abdul ("servant of the") which would sound quite odd to a native speaker of Arabic.</li></ul></li>
<li>Not distinguishing <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">ʻalāʾ</i></i> from <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Allah</i></i>: Some Muslim names include the Arabic word <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">ʻalāʾ</i></i> (<span lang="ar" dir="rtl" title="Arabic language text">علاء</span> "nobility"). Here, ⟨ʻ⟩ represents the <i><a href="/wiki/Ayin" title="Ayin">ayin</a></i>, a <a href="/wiki/Voiced_pharyngeal_fricative" title="Voiced pharyngeal fricative">voiced pharyngeal fricative</a>, ⟨ʾ⟩ represents the <a href="/wiki/Hamza" title="Hamza">hamza</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">glottal stop</a>, and ⟨l⟩ is spelled and pronounced at ordinary length, /l/. In <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Allāh</i></i>, the <i>l</i> is written twice (⟨ll⟩) and pronounced twice as long (a <a href="/wiki/Geminate" class="mw-redirect" title="Geminate">geminate</a>), as /lː/ or /ll/. In Arabic pronunciation, <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">ʻalāʾ</i></i> and <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Allāh</i></i> are clearly different. But <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Europe" title="Ethnic groups in Europe">Europeans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iranian_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Iranian people">Iranians</a>, and <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">Indians</a> may not pronounce some Arabic sounds as a native Arabic speaker would, and thus tend to pronounce them identically. For example, the name <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">ʻAlāʾ al-dīn</i></i> (<a href="/wiki/Aladdin_(name)" title="Aladdin (name)">Aladdin (name)</a>, "the Nobility of the Faith") is sometimes misspelled as <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">Allāh al-dīn</i></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> There is another name <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">ʻAlaʾ-Allah</i></i> (<i>Aliullah</i>, "the Nobility of God"), which uses both distinctly.</li>
<li>Taking <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">bin</i></i> or <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">ibn</i></i> for a middle name: As stated above, these words indicate the order of the family chain. Westerners often confuse them with middle names, especially when they're written as "Ben", as it is the case in some countries. For example, Sami Ben Ahmed would be mistakenly addressed as Mr. Ben Ahmed. To correctly address the person, one should use Mr. Sami Ahmed or Mr. Ahmed.</li>
<li>Grammar: As between all languages, there are differences between Arabic grammar and the grammar of other languages. Arabic forms noun compounds in the opposite order from Indo-Iranian languages, for example. During the war in <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">Afghanistan</a> in 2002, a <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> team found in <a href="/wiki/Kabul" title="Kabul">Kabul</a> an <a href="/wiki/Internally_displaced_person" title="Internally displaced person">internally displaced person</a> whose name they stated as "Allah Muhammad". This may be a misspelling for <i><i lang="ar-Latn" title="Arabic-language romanization">ʻalāʾ</i></i>, for if not, by the rules of Arabic grammar, this name means "the Allah who belongs to Muhammad", which would be unacceptable religiously. However, by the rules of <a href="/wiki/Iranian_languages" title="Iranian languages">Iranian languages</a> and most <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_India" title="Languages of India">languages of India</a>, this name does mean "Muhammad who belongs to Allah", being the equivalent of the Arabic "Muhammadullah". <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Afghanistan" title="Demographics of Afghanistan">Most Afghans</a> speak Iranian languages. Such Arabo-Iranian or Arabo-Indian mixed-language compound names are not uncommon in Afghanistan, Iran, Pakistan and Tajikistan. There is, for example, the Pakistani/Indian name <i>Allah-Ditta</i> which joins the Arabic <i>Allah</i> with the Punjabi <i>Ditta</i> "given".</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Arab_family_naming_convention">Arab family naming convention</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Arab family naming convention">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Arabic culture, as in many parts of the world, a person's ancestry and family name are very important. An example is explained below.
</p><p>Assume a man is called <i>Saleh ibn Tariq ibn Khalid al-Fulan</i>.
</p>
<ul><li><i>Saleh</i> is his personal name, and the one that his family and friends would call him by.</li>
<li><i>ibn</i> translates as "son of", so <i>Tariq</i> is Saleh's father's name.</li>
<li><i>ibn Khalid</i> means that Tariq is the son of Khalid, making Khalid the grandfather of Saleh.</li>
<li><i>al-Fulan</i> would be Saleh's family name.</li></ul>
<p>Hence, <i>Saleh ibn Tariq ibn Khalid al-Fulan</i> translates as "Saleh, son of Tariq, son of Khalid; of the family al-Fulan."
</p><p>The Arabic for "daughter of" is <i>bint.</i> A woman with the name <i>Fatimah bint Tariq ibn Khalid al-Goswami</i> translates as "Fatimah, daughter of Tariq, son of Khalid; of the family al-Goswami."
</p><p>In this case, ibn and bint are included in the official naming. Most Arab countries today, however, do not use 'ibn' and 'bint' in their naming system. If Saleh were an Egyptian, he would be called <i>Saleh Tariq Khalid al-Fulan</i> and Fatimah would be <i>Fatimah Tariq Khalid al-Goswami.</i>
</p><p>If Saleh marries a wife (who would keep her own <a href="/wiki/Maiden_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Maiden name">maiden</a>, family, and surnames), their children will take Saleh's family name. Therefore, their son Mohammed would be called <i>Mohammed ibn Saleh ibn Tariq al-Fulan.</i>
</p><p>However, not all Arab countries use the name in its full length, but conventionally use two- and three-word names, and sometimes four-word names in official or legal matters. Thus the first name is the personal name, the middle name is the father's name and the last name is the family name.
</p>

<ul><li>The popular romanization of the Arabized and Hebrew names are written first, then the standardized romanization are written in oblique. Notice that Arabized names may have variants.</li>
<li>If a literal Arabic translation of a name exists, it will be placed after the final standardized romanization.</li>
<li>If an Arabic correlation is ambiguous, <i>(?)</i> will be placed following the name in question.
<dl><dd>* Yassou' is the Arab Christian name, while <a href="/wiki/%CA%BF%C4%AAs%C4%81" class="mw-redirect" title="ʿĪsā">ʿĪsā</a> is the Muslim version of the name, as used in the <a href="/wiki/Qur%27an" class="mw-redirect" title="Qur&#39;an">Qur'an</a>. There is debate as to which is the better rendition of the Aramaic Yeshua, because both names are of late origin.</dd>
<dd>** Youhanna is the Arab Christian name of John, while Yahya is the Muslim version of the name, as used in the Qur'an. They have completely different triconsonantal roots: <i>H-N-N</i> ("grace") vs <i>H-Y-Y</i> ("Life").  Specifically, Youhanna may be the Biblical John the Baptist or the apostle.  Yahya refers specifically to John the Baptist.</dd></dl></li>
<li><i>El</i>, the Hebrew word for strength/might or deity, is usually represented as <i>īl</i> in Arabic, although it carries no meaning in classical and modern Arabic. The only exception is its usage in the archaic Iraqi dialect.</li></ul>
