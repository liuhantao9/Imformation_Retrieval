<p>

<b>Aboriginal Australian kinship</b> are the systems of <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">law</a> governing social interaction, particularly <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a>, in traditional <a href="/wiki/Australian_Aboriginal_culture" title="Australian Aboriginal culture">Australian Aboriginal cultures</a>. It is an integral part of the culture of every <a href="/wiki/List_of_Indigenous_Australian_group_names" title="List of Indigenous Australian group names">Aboriginal group</a> across Australia.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_subsection_system">The subsection system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_Aboriginal_kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The subsection system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Subsection systems are a unique social structure that divide all of Australian Aboriginal society into a number of groups, each of which combines particular sets of kin. In Central Australian Aboriginal English vernacular, subsections are widely known as "<b>skins</b>". Each subsection is given a name that can be used to refer to individual members of that group. Skin is passed down by a person's parents to their children.
</p><p>The name of the groups can vary. There are systems with two such groupings (these are known as '<a href="/wiki/Kinship#Lineages,_clans,_phratries,_moieties,_and_matrimonial_sides" title="Kinship">moieties</a>' in kinship studies), systems with four (sections), six and eight (subsection systems). Some language groups extend this by having distinct male and female forms, giving a total of sixteen skin names, for example the Pintupi (listed below) and Warlpiri. While membership in skin groups is ideally based on blood relations, Australian Aboriginal subsection systems are classificatory, meaning that even people who are not actual blood relations are assigned to a subsection. They are also universal, meaning that every member of the society is assigned a position in the system.
</p><p>Subsection systems are found in Aboriginal societies across much of Central, Western and Northern Australia. On the basis of detailed analysis and comparison of the various subsection systems and their terminologies, and in particular the apparent prefix /j-/ for male and /n-/ for female, it has been identified as a social innovation originally from the Daly River region of the Northern Territory, which then spread rapidly southwards to other groups.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Each Lardil person belongs to one of these groups. Their paternal <a href="/wiki/Grandfather" class="mw-redirect" title="Grandfather">grandfather</a>'s subsection determines their own; so a Balyarriny man or woman will have a Balyarriny grandfather. Members of each group may only marry members of one other, specified, group.
</p><p>Once a person's subsection group is known, their relationship to any other Lardil can be determined. A Ngarrijbalangi is a 'father' to a Bangariny, a 'father-in-law' to a Yakimarr and a 'son' to another Bangariny, either in a social sense or purely through linearship.
</p><p>The mechanics of the Lardil skin system means that generations of males cycle back and forth between two subsections. <i>Ngarrijbalangi</i> is father to <i>Bangariny</i> and <i>Bangariny</i> is father to <i>Ngarrijbalangi</i> and similarly for the three other pairs of subsections. Generations of women, however, cycle through four subsections before arriving back at the starting point. This means that a woman has the same subsection name as her (<a href="/wiki/Matrilineality" title="Matrilineality">matrilineal</a>) great-great-grandmother.
</p>

<p>Each person therefore has a <a href="/wiki/Patriline" class="mw-redirect" title="Patriline">patrimoiety</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Matriline" class="mw-redirect" title="Matriline">matrimoiety</a>, a father's and a mother's subsection group.

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extension_of_the_system_to_non-relatives">Extension of the system to non-relatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_Aboriginal_kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Extension of the system to non-relatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Outsiders who have significant interaction with such groups may be given a 'skin name', commonly based on the people they have interacted with and the types of interaction.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Some_common_kinship_terms_used_in_Aboriginal_English">Some common kinship terms used in Aboriginal English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Australian_Aboriginal_kinship&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Some common kinship terms used in Aboriginal English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Australian_Aboriginal_English" title="Australian Aboriginal English">Australian Aboriginal English</a></div>
<p>The variety of English used by many Australian Aboriginal people employs kinship terms in ways that are based on their equivalents in Australian Aboriginal languages.
</p>
<ul><li><i>Aunty</i> and <i>uncle</i>  are terms of address for older people, to whom the speaker may not be related.</li>
<li><i>Brother</i> and <i>sister</i>—as well as siblings this term is used to refer to children of one's mother's sister and of father's brother (cousin), just as in many indigenous languages.</li>
<li><i>Cousin-brother</i> and <i>cousin-sister</i> are often used to refer to children of one's mother's sister and father's brother.</li>
<li><i>Cousin</i> refers to children of one's father's sister and mother's brother, but may be extended to any relative of one's own generation, such as one who might share the same great grandparent as their own great grandparent, which is a second-cousin in Aboriginal terms.</li>
<li>In south-east Queensland, <i>daughter</i> is used to refer to any woman of one's great-grandparents' generation. This is due to the cyclical nature of traditional kinship systems and mirrors usage in many Australian languages.</li>
<li><i>Father</i> and <i>mother</i> include any relative of one's parents' generation, such as uncles, aunts, their own cousins and in-laws.</li>
<li><i>Grandfather</i> and <i>grandmother</i> can refer to anyone of one's grandparents' generation. <i>Grandfather</i> can also refer to any respected elderly man, to whom the speaker may not be related.</li>
<li><i>Poison</i> refers to a relation one is obligated to avoid. See <a href="/wiki/Avoidance_speech" title="Avoidance speech">avoidance speech</a>.</li>
<li>The term <i>second</i>, or <i>little bit</i> in northern Australia, is used with a distant relative who is described using a close kinship term. For example, one's <i>second father</i> or <i>little bit father</i> is a man of one's father's generation not closely related to the speaker. Usually having a <i>second mother</i> is having a woman of your own mother's generation who seems to act like a mother and would most likely care for you if anything were to happen to your own parents. It is contrasted with <i>close</i>, <i>near</i> or <i>true</i>.</li>
<li>A <i>skin</i> or <i>skin group</i> is a section determined by the skin of a person's parents, and determines who a person is eligible to marry.</li>
<li><i>Son</i> can refer to any male of the next generation, such as nephews, just as <i>daughter</i> can refer to any female of the next generation, including nieces.</li></ul>
