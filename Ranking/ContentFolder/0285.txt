<p>Many <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Chinese" title="Overseas Chinese">ethnic Chinese</a> people have lived in <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a> for many centuries. Over time, especially under social and political pressure during the <a href="/wiki/New_Order_(Indonesia)" title="New Order (Indonesia)">New Order</a> era, most <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Indonesians" title="Chinese Indonesians">Chinese Indonesians</a> have adopted names that better match the local language.<sup id="cite_ref-tionghoa_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tionghoa-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-irzanti_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irzanti-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_Chinese_Indonesian_surnames">History of Chinese Indonesian surnames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_Indonesian_surname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of Chinese Indonesian surnames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Colonial_era.E2.80.931965"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Colonial_era–1965">Colonial era–1965</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_Indonesian_surname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Colonial era–1965">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_East_Indies" title="Dutch East Indies">Dutch colonial era</a>, the Dutch administration recorded Chinese names in <a href="/wiki/Birth_certificate" title="Birth certificate">birth certificates</a> and other <a href="/wiki/Legal_document" class="mw-redirect" title="Legal document">legal documents</a> using an adopted spelling convention that was based primarily on the <a href="/wiki/Hokkien_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Hokkien language">Hokkien</a> (Southern <a href="/wiki/Min_Chinese" title="Min Chinese">Min</a>), the language of the majority of Chinese immigrants in the Dutch East Indies. The administrators used the closest Dutch pronunciation and spelling of Hokkien words to record the names. A similar thing happened in <a href="/wiki/British_Malaya" title="British Malaya">Malaya</a>, where the British administrators record the names using <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">English spelling</a>. (For instance, compare Lim (English) vs. Liem (Dutch), Wee or Ooi (English) vs. Oei or Oey (Dutch), Goh (English) vs. Go (Dutch), Chan (English) vs. Tjan (Dutch), Lee (English) vs. Lie (Dutch), Leong (English) vs Liong (Dutch).)<sup id="cite_ref-tionghoa_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tionghoa-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Hence, Lin (林, Mandarin) is spelled Liem in Indonesia. Chen (陳) is Tan, Huang (黃) is Oei or Oey, Wu (吳) is Go, Wei (魏) is Goei or Ngoei, Guo (郭) is Kwee, Yang (楊) is Njoo, and so on.
</p><p>Further, as <a href="/wiki/Pe%CC%8Dh-%C5%8De-j%C4%AB" title="Pe̍h-ōe-jī">Hokkien romanization standard</a> did not exist then, some romanized names varied slightly. For example, 郭 (Guo) could sometimes be Kwik, Que or Kwek instead of Kwee and Huang is often Oei instead of Oey.
</p><p>The spelling convention survived through the <a href="/wiki/Japanese_occupation_of_the_Dutch_East_Indies" title="Japanese occupation of the Dutch East Indies">Japanese occupation</a> (1942–1945) well into <a href="/wiki/Proclamation_of_Indonesian_Independence" title="Proclamation of Indonesian Independence">Indonesian independence</a> (1945) and sovereignty acknowledgment by the <a href="/wiki/States_General_of_the_Netherlands" title="States General of the Netherlands">Dutch government</a> (1949). Since the independent Indonesian government inherited the <a href="/wiki/Law_of_the_Netherlands" title="Law of the Netherlands">Dutch legal system</a>, it also survived until 1965 in the Sukarno era.
</p><p>The Indonesian government later began changing Indonesian spelling to harmonize it with the spelling used for Malay in <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brunei" title="Brunei">Brunei</a>, first under the <i><a href="/wiki/Republican_Spelling_System" title="Republican Spelling System">Ejaan Suwandi</a></i> introduced in 1947, and again under the <i><a href="/wiki/Enhanced_Indonesian_Spelling_System" title="Enhanced Indonesian Spelling System">Ejaan Yang Disempurnakan</a></i>, literally "Perfected Spelling", adopted in 1972. Under the Suwandi system of spelling, "oe", influenced by Dutch, became "u", influenced by English; for example, Loe was spelled as Lu. Since 1972, Dutch-style "j" became "y", meaning Njoo is now spelled Nyoo.
</p>
<h3><span id="1965.E2.80.931998"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="1965–1998">1965–1998</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_Indonesian_surname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 1965–1998">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Suharto" title="Suharto">Suharto</a> came to power, his regime created many <a href="/wiki/Anti-Chinese_legislation_in_Indonesia" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-Chinese legislation in Indonesia">anti-Chinese legislations in Indonesia</a>. One of them was <a href="/wiki/127/U/Kep/12/1966" class="mw-redirect" title="127/U/Kep/12/1966">127/U/Kep/12/1966</a> which strongly encouraged ethnic Chinese living in Indonesia to adopt Indonesian-sounding names instead of the standard three-word or two-word Chinese names.
</p><p>Many Indonesianized names are Hokkien surname syllables with western or Indonesian prefix or suffix - resulting in many exotic-sounding names. Although two Chinese individuals shared the same Chinese surname, they may employ different strategies for the Indonesian-sounding names. For example, one with the surname 林 (Lin) may adopt "Limanto", and the other may adopt "Halim" as Indonesian-sounding names. "Limanto" and "Halim" both contain "lim" that corresponds to the 林 surname (Mandarin: Lin, Hokkien: Liem or Lim = forest). The famous 1966 political activist and businessman <a href="/wiki/Sofjan_Wanandi" title="Sofjan Wanandi">Sofjan Wanandi</a> (Chinese romanization: Liem Bian Koen) translated Lin to old Javanese "wana", meaning forest, and added the male-suffix "ndi", resulting in the new clan name Wanandi.<sup id="cite_ref-irzanti_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-irzanti-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite the Indonesianization, the Hokkien surnames are still used today by the Chinese-Indonesian diaspora overseas (mostly in the Netherlands, Germany, and USA); by those Chinese-Indonesians courageous enough during Suharto's regime to keep their Chinese names (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Kwik_Kian_Gie" title="Kwik Kian Gie">Kwik Kian Gie</a>), or by those who couldn't afford to process the name change through Indonesia's civil bureaucracy.
</p>
<h3><span id="2000.E2.80.93present"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="2000–present">2000–present</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Chinese_Indonesian_surname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2000–present">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Suharto" title="Fall of Suharto">Suharto resigned from the presidency</a>, subsequent governments revoked the <a href="/wiki/Legislation_on_Chinese_Indonesians" title="Legislation on Chinese Indonesians">ban on the ethnic Chinese from speaking and learning Chinese in public</a>. Using the original Chinese surnames is no longer a taboo but only a small minority have decided to re-adopt the original Hokkien names of their grandparents or to use the Mandarin Chinese <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a> romanization, pronunciation and spelling and most retain their changed names as the post-1965 generations have been culturally Indonesianized.
</p>
