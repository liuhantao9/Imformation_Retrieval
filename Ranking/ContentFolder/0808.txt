<p><b>Simon Robert Armitage</b>, <span class="noexcerpt" style="font-size:100%;"><a href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Commander of the Order of the British Empire">CBE</a><span style="font-size:14px;">&#44;</span>&#32;<a href="/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society_of_Literature" class="mw-redirect" title="Fellow of the Royal Society of Literature">FRSL</a></span> (born 26 May 1963)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is an English poet, playwright and novelist. He is professor of poetry at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Leeds" title="University of Leeds">University of Leeds</a> and succeeded <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Hill" title="Geoffrey Hill">Geoffrey Hill</a> as <a href="/wiki/Oxford_Professor_of_Poetry" title="Oxford Professor of Poetry">Oxford Professor of Poetry</a> when he was elected to the 4-year part-time appointment from 2015–2019. <sup id="cite_ref-The_Guardian_19_June_2015_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Guardian_19_June_2015-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> On 10 May 2019 it was announced that Armitage would be the next <a href="/wiki/Poet_Laureate_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom">Poet Laureate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BBC_News_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC_News-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_Education">Early life and Education</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_Armitage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and Education">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Armitage was born in <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Riding_of_Yorkshire" title="West Riding of Yorkshire">West Riding of Yorkshire</a><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and grew up in the village of <a href="/wiki/Marsden,_West_Yorkshire" title="Marsden, West Yorkshire">Marsden</a>, where his family still live.<sup id="cite_ref-Profile_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Profile-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He has on older sister, Hilary. His father Peter is a former probation officer who is well-known locally for writing plays and <a href="/wiki/Pantomimes" class="mw-redirect" title="Pantomimes">pantos</a> for his all-male panto group, <i>The Avalanche Dodgers</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Profile_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Profile-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He wrote his first poem aged 10 as a school assignment.<sup id="cite_ref-Profile_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Profile-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Armitage first studied at <a href="/wiki/Colne_Valley_High_School" title="Colne Valley High School">Colne Valley High School</a>, <a href="/wiki/Linthwaite" title="Linthwaite">Linthwaite</a>, and went on to study geography at <a href="/wiki/Portsmouth_Polytechnic" class="mw-redirect" title="Portsmouth Polytechnic">Portsmouth Polytechnic</a>. He was a post-graduate student at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Manchester" title="University of Manchester">University of Manchester</a> where his MA thesis concerned the effects of television violence on young offenders. Finding himself jobless after graduation, he decided to train as a probation officer, like his father before him. Around this time he began writing poetry more seriously.<sup id="cite_ref-Profile_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Profile-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> He worked as a probation officer in Greater Manchester until 1994.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_Armitage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His first poetry collection was called <i>Human Geography</i> (1988). He published <i>Zoom!</i> in 1989.<sup id="cite_ref-Guardian_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He has lectured on creative writing at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Leeds" title="University of Leeds">University of Leeds</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Iowa" title="University of Iowa">University of Iowa</a>, and was senior lecturer at <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Metropolitan_University" title="Manchester Metropolitan University">Manchester Metropolitan University</a>. He has made literary, history and travel programmes for <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_3" title="BBC Radio 3">BBC Radio 3</a> and <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">4</a>; and since 1992 he has written and presented a number of TV documentaries. From 2009-2012 he was Artist in Residence at London's South Bank, and in February 2011 he became Professor of Poetry at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Sheffield" title="University of Sheffield">University of Sheffield</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Official_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Official-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-North_Guide_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-North_Guide-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>  In October 2017 he was appointed as the first Professor of Poetry at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Leeds" title="University of Leeds">University of Leeds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-leedsuni_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leedsuni-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He lives in the <a href="/wiki/Holme_Valley" title="Holme Valley">Holme Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, close to his family home in Marsden.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> He makes many references to supporting his local football team in his book <i>All Points North</i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_Armitage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Armitage's poetry collections include <i><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Matches" title="Book of Matches">Book of Matches</a></i> (1993) and <i>The Dead Sea Poems</i> (1995). He has written two novels, <i>Little Green Man</i> (2001) and <i>The White Stuff</i> (2004), as well as <i>All Points North</i> (1998), a collection of essays on <a href="/wiki/Northern_England" title="Northern England">Northern England</a>. He produced a dramatised version of <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">Homer</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Odyssey" title="Odyssey">Odyssey</a> and a collection of poetry entitled <i>Tyrannosaurus Rex Versus The Corduroy Kid</i> (which was shortlisted for the <a href="/wiki/T.S._Eliot_Prize" class="mw-redirect" title="T.S. Eliot Prize">T.S. Eliot Prize</a>), both of which were published in July 2006. Many of Armitage's poems appear in the <a href="/wiki/Assessment_and_Qualifications_Alliance" class="mw-redirect" title="Assessment and Qualifications Alliance">AQA</a> (Assessment and Qualifications Alliance) <a href="/wiki/GCSE" class="mw-redirect" title="GCSE">GCSE</a> syllabus for English Literature in the United Kingdom. These include "Homecoming", "Extract from Out of the Blue", "November", "Kid", "Hitcher", "remains", and a selection of poems from <i>Book of Matches</i>, most notably of these "Mother any distance...". His work also appears on CCEA's GCSE English Literature course.
</p><p>He is characterised by a dry Yorkshire wit combined with "an accessible, realist style and critical seriousness."<sup id="cite_ref-North_Guide_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-North_Guide-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> His translation of <i><a href="/wiki/Sir_Gawain_and_the_Green_Knight" title="Sir Gawain and the Green Knight">Sir Gawain and the Green Knight</a></i> (2007), was adopted for the ninth edition of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Norton_Anthology_of_English_Literature" title="The Norton Anthology of English Literature">The Norton Anthology of English Literature</a></i>, and he was the narrator of a 2010 BBC documentary about the poem and its use of landscape.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Armitage also writes for radio, television, film and stage. He is the author of five stage plays, including <i>Mister Heracles</i>, a version of <a href="/wiki/Euripides" title="Euripides">Euripides</a>' <i>The Madness of Heracles</i>.  <i>The Last Days of Troy</i> premiered at <a href="/wiki/Shakespeare%27s_Globe" title="Shakespeare&#39;s Globe">Shakespeare's Globe</a> in June 2014.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> He was commissioned in 1996 by the <a href="/wiki/Royal_National_Theatre" title="Royal National Theatre">National Theatre</a> in London to write <i>Eclipse</i> for the National Connections series, a play inspired by the real-life disappearance of a girl in <a href="/wiki/Hebden_Bridge" title="Hebden Bridge">Hebden Bridge</a>, and set at the time of the 1999 solar eclipse in <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most recently Armitage wrote the <a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">libretto</a> for an opera scored by Scottish composer <a href="/wiki/Stuart_MacRae_(composer)" title="Stuart MacRae (composer)">Stuart MacRae</a>, <i>The Assassin Tree</i>, based on a Greek myth recounted in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Golden_Bough" title="The Golden Bough">The Golden Bough</a></i>. The opera premiered at the 2006 <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh_International_Festival" title="Edinburgh International Festival">Edinburgh International Festival</a>, Scotland, before moving to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Opera_House" title="Royal Opera House">Royal Opera House</a>, <a href="/wiki/Covent_Garden" title="Covent Garden">Covent Garden</a>, London. <i>Saturday Night</i> (Century Films, BBC2, 1996)&#160;– wrote and narrated a fifty-minute poetic commentary to a documentary about night-life in Leeds, directed by Brian Hill. In 2010, Armitage walked the 264-mile <a href="/wiki/Pennine_Way" title="Pennine Way">Pennine Way</a>, walking south from Scotland to <a href="/wiki/Derbyshire" title="Derbyshire">Derbyshire</a>. Along the route he stopped to give poetry readings, often in exchange for donations of money, food or accommodation, despite the rejection of the free life seen in his 1993 poem "Hitcher", and has written a book about his journey, called <i>Walking Home</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Official_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Official-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>He has received numerous awards for his poetry, including <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times" title="The Sunday Times">The Sunday Times</a></i> Author of the Year, a <a href="/wiki/Forward_Prize" class="mw-redirect" title="Forward Prize">Forward Prize</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Lannan_Literary_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Lannan Literary Award">Lannan Award</a>, and an <a href="/wiki/Ivor_Novello_Award" class="mw-redirect" title="Ivor Novello Award">Ivor Novello Award</a> for his song lyrics in the <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a> film <i>Feltham Sings</i>. <i>Kid</i> and <i>CloudCuckooLand</i> were short-listed for the <a href="/wiki/Costa_Book_Awards" title="Costa Book Awards">Whitbread poetry prize</a>. The <i>Dead Sea Poems</i> was short-listed for the Whitbread, the <a href="/wiki/Forward_Poetry_Prize" class="mw-redirect" title="Forward Poetry Prize">Forward Poetry Prize</a> and the <a href="/wiki/T._S._Eliot_Prize" title="T. S. Eliot Prize">T. S. Eliot Prize</a>. <i>The Universal Home Doctor</i> was also short-listed for the T.S. Eliot. In 2000, he was the UK's official Millennium Poet and went on to judge the 2005 <a href="/wiki/Griffin_Poetry_Prize" title="Griffin Poetry Prize">Griffin Poetry Prize</a>, the 2006 <a href="/wiki/Man_Booker_Prize" class="mw-redirect" title="Man Booker Prize">Man Booker Prize for Fiction</a> and the 2010 <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Poetry_Prize" title="Manchester Poetry Prize">Manchester Poetry Prize</a>.
</p><p>In 2004, Armitage was elected a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Literature" title="Royal Society of Literature">Royal Society of Literature</a> and was appointed <a href="/wiki/Commander_of_the_Order_of_the_British_Empire" class="mw-redirect" title="Commander of the Order of the British Empire">Commander of the Order of the British Empire</a> (CBE) in the 2010 Birthday Honours.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> He is a vice president of the <a href="/wiki/Poetry_Society" title="Poetry Society">Poetry Society</a> and a patron of the <a href="/wiki/Arvon_Foundation" title="Arvon Foundation">Arvon Foundation</a>.
</p><p>In 2007 he released an album of songs co-written with the musician Craig Smith, under the band name <i>The Scaremongers</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For the <a href="/wiki/Stanza_Stones_Trail" title="Stanza Stones Trail">Stanza Stones Trail</a>, which runs through 47 miles (76&#160;km) of the Pennine region, Armitage composed six new poems on his walks. With the help of local expert Tom Lonsdale and letter-carver Pip Hall, the poems were carved  into stones at secluded sites. A book, containing the poems and the accounts of Lonsdale and Hall, has been produced as a record of that journey<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> and has been published by <a href="/wiki/Enitharmon_Press" title="Enitharmon Press">Enitharmon Press</a>. The poems, complemented with commissioned wood engravings by Hilary Paynter, were also published under the title 'In Memory of Water' by <a rel="nofollow" class="external free" href="http://www.finepresspoetry.com">http://www.finepresspoetry.com</a>
</p><p>In 2016 the arts programme <a href="/wiki/14-18_NOW" class="mw-redirect" title="14-18 NOW">14-18 NOW</a> commissioned a series of poems by Simon Armitage as part of a five-year programme of new artwork created specifically to mark the centenary of the First World War.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The poems are a response to six aerial or panoramic photographs of battlefields from the archive of the Imperial War Museum in London. The poetry collection "Still" premiered at the Norfolk &amp; Norwich Festival and has been published in partnership with Enitharmon Press.
</p><p>In 2019 Armitage's first poem as <a href="/wiki/Poet_Laureate_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Poet Laureate of the United Kingdom">Poet Laureate</a>, <i>Conquistadors</i>, commemorating the <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">1969 moon landing,</a> was published in <a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a>. <sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Armitage's second poem as Poet Laureate, 'Finishing it' was commissioned in 2019 by the Institute of Cancer Research. Micro-engraver, Graham Short, meticulously carved the entire 51-word poem clearly onto a cancer treatment tablet. <sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_Armitage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Armitage is married to radio producer Sue Roberts. They have a daughter, Emmeline, born in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Emmeline won the 2017 SLAMbassadors national youth <a href="/wiki/Poetry_slam" title="Poetry slam">poetry slam</a> for 13-18-year-olds.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> Continuing in both her father's and grandfather's tradition, she is a member of the <a href="/wiki/National_Youth_Theatre" title="National Youth Theatre">National Youth Theatre</a> and a singer.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Simon_Armitage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Armitage is the first poet laureate who is also a DJ.<sup id="cite_ref-Profile_6-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Profile-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> He is a massive music fan, especially of <a href="/wiki/The_Smiths" title="The Smiths">The Smiths</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Profile_6-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Profile-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> During what his wife Sue described as "a bit of a mid-life crisis", Armitage and his college friend Craig Smith founded the band <i>The Scaremongers</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Profile_6-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Profile-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Their only album, <i>Born in a Barn</i>, was released in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> 
</p>
