	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Emergency medical services in the United Kingdom</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>

<p><b>Emergency medical services in the United Kingdom</b> provide emergency care to people with acute illness or injury and are predominantly provided <a href="/wiki/Publicly_funded_health_care" title="Publicly funded health care">free at the point of use</a> by the four <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">National Health Services</a> of <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service_(England)" title="National Health Service (England)">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/NHS_Scotland" title="NHS Scotland">Scotland</a>, <a href="/wiki/NHS_Wales" title="NHS Wales">Wales</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Health_and_Social_Care_in_Northern_Ireland" title="Health and Social Care in Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. Emergency care including ambulance and <a href="/wiki/Emergency_department" title="Emergency department">emergency department</a> treatment is free to everyone, regardless of immigration or visitor status.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service" title="National Health Service">NHS</a> commissions most emergency medical services through the 14 NHS organisations with ambulance responsibility across the UK (11 in England, 1 each in the other three countries).
</p><p>As with other emergency services, the public normally access emergency medical services through one of the valid <a href="/wiki/Emergency_telephone_number" title="Emergency telephone number">emergency telephone numbers</a> (either <a href="/wiki/999_(emergency_telephone_number)" title="999 (emergency telephone number)">999</a> or <a href="/wiki/112_(emergency_telephone_number)" title="112 (emergency telephone number)">112</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-choices1_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-choices1-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to ambulance services provided by NHS organisations, there are also some private and volunteer emergency medical services arrangements in place in the UK, the use of private or volunteer ambulances at public events or large private sites, and as part of community provision of services such as <a href="/wiki/Community_first_responder" title="Community first responder">community first responders</a>.
</p><p>Most air ambulance services in the UK are not part of the NHS and are funded through charitable donations. Paramedics are usually seconded from a local NHS ambulance service.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Role_of_the_ambulance_services">Role of the ambulance services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Role of the ambulance services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Motorcycle_paramedic_London_Ambulance_Service.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Motorcycle_paramedic_London_Ambulance_Service.jpg/220px-Motorcycle_paramedic_London_Ambulance_Service.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Motorcycle_paramedic_London_Ambulance_Service.jpg/330px-Motorcycle_paramedic_London_Ambulance_Service.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Motorcycle_paramedic_London_Ambulance_Service.jpg/440px-Motorcycle_paramedic_London_Ambulance_Service.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3072" data-file-height="2304" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Motorcycle_paramedic_London_Ambulance_Service.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A paramedic's motorcycle in Birmingham</div></div></div>
<p>Public ambulance services across the UK are required by law to respond to four types of requests for care,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> which are:
</p>
<ul><li>Emergency calls (via the <a href="/wiki/999_(emergency_telephone_number)" title="999 (emergency telephone number)">999</a> or <a href="/wiki/112_(emergency_telephone_number)" title="112 (emergency telephone number)">112</a> system)</li>
<li>Doctor's urgent admission requests</li>
<li><div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:EEAS_Range_Rover_Disco_Sport_RRV.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/EEAS_Range_Rover_Disco_Sport_RRV.jpg/220px-EEAS_Range_Rover_Disco_Sport_RRV.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/EEAS_Range_Rover_Disco_Sport_RRV.jpg/330px-EEAS_Range_Rover_Disco_Sport_RRV.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/EEAS_Range_Rover_Disco_Sport_RRV.jpg/440px-EEAS_Range_Rover_Disco_Sport_RRV.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3264" data-file-height="2448" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:EEAS_Range_Rover_Disco_Sport_RRV.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>An <a href="/wiki/East_of_England_Ambulance_Service" title="East of England Ambulance Service">East of England Ambulance Service</a> Rapid Response Vehicle (RRV)</div></div></div>High dependency and urgent inter-hospital transfers</li>
<li>Major incidents</li></ul>
<p>Ambulance trusts and services may also undertake non-urgent patient transport services on a commercial arrangement with their local hospital trusts or health boards, or in some cases on directly funded government contracts,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> although these contracts are increasingly fulfilled by private and voluntary providers.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/NHS_ambulance_services_prior_to_2006" class="mw-redirect" title="NHS ambulance services prior to 2006">NHS ambulance services prior to 2006</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service_Act_1946" title="National Health Service Act 1946">National Health Service Act 1946</a> gave county and borough councils a statutory responsibility to provide an emergency ambulance service, although they could contract a voluntary ambulance service to provide this, with many contracting the <a href="/wiki/British_Red_Cross" title="British Red Cross">British Red Cross</a>, <a href="/wiki/St_John_Ambulance" title="St John Ambulance">St John Ambulance</a> or another local provider.  The last St John Division, (the original designation of a local unit, now called 'branches'), to be so contracted is reputed to have been at Whittlesey in Cambridgeshire, where the two-bay ambulance garage can still be seen at the branch headquarters. The Regional Ambulance Officers’ Committee reported in 1979 that “There was considerable local variation in the quality of the service provided, particularly in relation to vehicles, staff and equipment. Most Services were administered by Local Authorities through their Medical Officer of Health and his Ambulance Officer, a few were under the aegis of the Fire Service, whilst others relied upon agency methods for the provision of part or all of their services.”
</p><p>The 142 existing ambulance services were transferred by the <a href="/wiki/National_Health_Service_Reorganisation_Act_1973" title="National Health Service Reorganisation Act 1973">National Health Service Reorganisation Act 1973</a> from local authority to central government control in 1974, and consolidated into 53 services under <a href="/wiki/Regional_health_authority_(UK)" title="Regional health authority (UK)">regional or area health authorities</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HMSO_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HMSO-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This led to the formation of predominantly county based ambulance services, which gradually merged up and changed responsibilities until 2006, when there were <a href="/wiki/NHS_ambulance_services_prior_to_2006" class="mw-redirect" title="NHS ambulance services prior to 2006">31 NHS ambulance trusts</a> in England.
</p><p>The June 2005 report "Taking healthcare to the Patient", authored by Peter Bradley,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Chief Executive of the London Ambulance Service, for the Department of Health led to the merging of the 31 trusts into 13 organisations in England,<sup id="cite_ref-NHS1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHS1-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> plus one organisation each in Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland. Following further changes as part of the <a href="/wiki/NHS_foundation_trust" title="NHS foundation trust">NHS foundation trust</a> pathway, this has further reduced to 10 ambulance service trusts in England, plus the Isle of Wight which has its own provision.
</p><p>Following the passage of the <a href="/wiki/Health_and_Social_Care_Act_2012" title="Health and Social Care Act 2012">Health and Social Care Act 2012</a>, commissioning of the ambulance services in each area passed from central government control into the hands of regional <a href="/wiki/Clinical_commissioning_group" title="Clinical commissioning group">clinical commissioning groups</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Current_public_provision">Current public provision</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Current public provision">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The commissioners in each region are responsible for contracting with a suitable organisation to provide ambulance services within their geographical territory. The primary provider for each area is currently held by a public NHS body, of which there are 11 in England, and 1 each in the other three countries.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="England">England</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: England">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In England there are now ten NHS ambulance trusts, as well as an ambulance service on the Isle of Wight, run directly by <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight_NHS_Trust" title="Isle of Wight NHS Trust">Isle of Wight NHS Trust</a>, with boundaries generally following those of the former regional government offices. The ten trusts are:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/East_Midlands_Ambulance_Service" title="East Midlands Ambulance Service">East Midlands Ambulance Service</a> NHS Trust</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/East_of_England_Ambulance_Service" title="East of England Ambulance Service">East of England Ambulance Service</a> NHS Trust</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/London_Ambulance_Service" title="London Ambulance Service">London Ambulance Service</a> NHS Trust</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/North_East_Ambulance_Service" title="North East Ambulance Service">North East Ambulance Service</a> NHS Foundation Trust</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/North_West_Ambulance_Service" title="North West Ambulance Service">North West Ambulance Service</a> NHS Trust</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/South_Central_Ambulance_Service" title="South Central Ambulance Service">South Central Ambulance Service</a> NHS Foundation Trust</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/South_East_Coast_Ambulance_Service" title="South East Coast Ambulance Service">South East Coast Ambulance Service</a> NHS Foundation Trust</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/South_Western_Ambulance_Service" title="South Western Ambulance Service">South Western Ambulance Service</a> NHS Foundation Trust</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_Ambulance_Service" title="West Midlands Ambulance Service">West Midlands Ambulance Service</a> University NHS Foundation Trust</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Ambulance_Service" title="Yorkshire Ambulance Service">Yorkshire Ambulance Service</a> NHS Trust</li></ul>
<p>The English ambulance trusts are represented by the Association of Ambulance Chief Executives (AACE), with the Scottish, Welsh and Northern Irish providers all associate members.
On the 14 November 2018 <a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_Ambulance_Service" title="West Midlands Ambulance Service">West Midlands Ambulance Service</a> became the UK's first university-ambulance trust.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scotland">Scotland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Scotland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The service was operated before reorganisation in 1974 by the <a href="/wiki/St_Andrews%E2%80%99_Ambulance_Association" class="mw-redirect" title="St Andrews’ Ambulance Association">St Andrews’ Ambulance Association</a> under contract to the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Scotland" title="Secretary of State for Scotland">Secretary of State for Scotland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-HMSO_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HMSO-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Ambulance_Service" title="Scottish Ambulance Service">Scottish Ambulance Service</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Special_Health_Board" class="mw-redirect" title="Special Health Board">Special Health Board</a> that provides ambulance services throughout whole of Scotland, on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/Health_and_Social_Care_Directorates" title="Health and Social Care Directorates">Health and Social Care Directorates</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Government" title="Scottish Government">Scottish Government</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-scot_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-scot-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Due to the remote nature of many areas of Scotland compared to the other <a href="/wiki/Home_Nations" title="Home Nations">Home Nations</a>, the Scottish Ambulance Service has Britain's only publicly funded <a href="/wiki/Air_ambulance" class="mw-redirect" title="Air ambulance">air ambulance</a> service, with two <a href="/wiki/Airbus_Helicopters_H145" class="mw-redirect" title="Airbus Helicopters H145">Airbus Helicopters H145</a> Helicopters and two <a href="/wiki/Beechcraft_Super_King_Air" title="Beechcraft Super King Air">Beechcraft B200C King Air</a> fixed-wing aircraft. There is also a partnership to provide the <a href="/wiki/Emergency_Medical_Retrieval_Service" title="Emergency Medical Retrieval Service">Emergency Medical Retrieval Service</a> which gives rapid access to the skills of a consultant in emergency or intensive care medicine using Scottish Ambulance Service road and air assets.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Ireland">Northern Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Northern Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland_Ambulance_Service" title="Northern Ireland Ambulance Service">Northern Ireland Ambulance Service</a> (NIAS) was established in 1995 by order in council,<sup id="cite_ref-NIAS_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NIAS-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and serves the whole of <a href="/wiki/Northern_Ireland" title="Northern Ireland">Northern Ireland</a>. To date universities in Northern Ireland do not facilitate <a href="/wiki/Paramedic" title="Paramedic">Paramedic Science</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Science" title="Bachelor of Science">BSc</a>) degree meaning members must complete their degree either in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">Wales</a> or <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wales">Wales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Wales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Welsh_Ambulance_Service" title="Welsh Ambulance Service">Welsh Ambulance Service</a> NHS Trust (<i><b>Welsh:</b></i> <i>Ymddiriedolaeth GIG Gwasanaethau Ambiwlans Cymru</i>) was established on 1 April 1998, and has 2,500 staff providing ambulance and related services to the 2.9 million residents of Wales.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span id="Private.2C_voluntary_and_charity_ambulance_services"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Private,_voluntary_and_charity_ambulance_services">Private, voluntary and charity ambulance services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Private, voluntary and charity ambulance services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There is a large market for private and voluntary ambulance services, with the sector being worth £800m to the UK economy in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Since April 2011, all ambulance providers operating in England have been required by law to be registered with the <a href="/wiki/Care_Quality_Commission" title="Care Quality Commission">Care Quality Commission</a>, under the same inspection regime as NHS services, and there are currently around 250 credentialled providers.<sup id="cite_ref-CQCmarket_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CQCmarket-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The primary activities of the private and voluntary services include:
</p>
<ul><li>the provision of ambulances as part of a wider service of first aid at events, construction sites, film sets, or other private provision</li>
<li>the provision of additional resource to NHS ambulance trusts</li>
<li>urgent patient transport between points of care (such as between two hospitals)</li>
<li>non-urgent patient transport</li></ul>
<p>All providers, including NHS, private, and voluntary can bid for many of the available contracts for provision of ambulance services, and private ambulance services now undertake over half of hospital transfers.<sup id="cite_ref-vitallink_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vitallink-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> This places the voluntary providers in direct competition with private services, although the private sector has been growing at the expense of the voluntary services over time.
</p><p>There is a duty on Category 1 responders (including the NHS) to make appropriate arrangements for major incidents, and as such private and voluntary ambulance services are generally included as part of local planning for the provision of ambulance services during major incidents, such as mass casualty events (including <a href="/wiki/7_July_2005_London_bombings" title="7 July 2005 London bombings">7 July 2005 London bombings</a>), adverse weather, or severe staff shortage.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Private_ambulance_services">Private ambulance services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Private ambulance services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Private ambulance services are common in the UK, with over 200 providers, and their use under contract to the NHS to answer 999 calls has been growing year on year,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> with every NHS ambulance trust using private providers in each year from 2011–2014, and contracted providers answering three quarters of a million 999 calls in that three-year period.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Expenditure on private ambulances in England increased from £37m in 2011–12 to £67.5m in 2013/4, rising in London from £796,000 to more than £8.8m.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In 2014–15, these 10 ambulance services spent £57.6 million on 333,329 callouts of private or voluntary services – an increase of 156% since 2010–11.<sup id="cite_ref-Daily_Telegraph_15-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Daily_Telegraph-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> This use of private contractors for frontline services has been politically controversial,<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> although 56% of the British public believe that greater private sector involvement will help maintain or improve standards in the NHS.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, the <a href="/wiki/Care_Quality_Commission" title="Care Quality Commission">Care Quality Commission</a> found 97% of private ambulance services to be providing good care.<sup id="cite_ref-CQCmarket_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CQCmarket-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> These private, registered services are represented by the <a href="/wiki/Independent_Ambulance_Association" title="Independent Ambulance Association">Independent Ambulance Association</a>. In 2017 the Commission warned all independent ambulance providers that during its inspections it had found “problems with the safety” of the care offered. 70 independent ambulance providers had been inspected and improvement notices had been issued to 25 out of 39 whose reports had been published. Plymouth Central Ambulance Service and Intrim Medical and Rescue Services were closed down after very poor practice was found.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are also a number of unregistered services operating, who do not provide ambulance transport, but only provide response on an event site. These firms are not regulated, and are not subject to the same checks as the registered providers, although they may operate similar vehicles, and offer near identical services.<sup id="cite_ref-vitallink_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vitallink-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Voluntary_aid_services">Voluntary aid services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Voluntary aid services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are a number of voluntary ambulance providers, sometimes known as Voluntary Aid Services or Voluntary Aid Societies (VAS), with the main ones being the <a href="/wiki/British_Red_Cross" title="British Red Cross">British Red Cross</a> and <a href="/wiki/St_John_Ambulance_(England_and_the_Islands)" class="mw-redirect" title="St John Ambulance (England and the Islands)">St John Ambulance</a>. The history of the voluntary ambulance services pre-dates any government organised service, and includes service in both World Wars.
</p><p>As they are in direct competition for work with the private ambulance providers, the voluntary providers do operate with some paid ambulance staff to fulfil their contracts.
</p><p>Voluntary organisations have also provided cover for the public when unionised NHS ambulance trust staff have taken industrial action.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There are a number of smaller voluntary ambulance organisations, fulfilling specific purposes, such as <a href="/wiki/Hatzola" class="mw-redirect" title="Hatzola">Hatzola</a> who provide emergency medical services to the orthodox Jewish community in some cities.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-BBC_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kaplan_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kaplan-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> These have however run into difficulties due to use of vehicles not legally recognised as ambulances.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/British_Association_for_Immediate_Care" title="British Association for Immediate Care">British Association for Immediate Care (BASICS)</a> has numerous medical staff (including doctors, nurses and paramedics) who volunteer their time to respond to serious medical emergencies. <i>(see below: <b>Supporting services</b>)</i>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Charity_air_ambulances">Charity air ambulances</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Charity air ambulances">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Air_ambulances_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Air ambulances in the United Kingdom">Air ambulances in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>Most UK emergency air ambulances are funded by charitable organisations, with medical staff usually seconded from the local NHS trust. The only exceptions are in Scotland &amp; Wales. The <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Ambulance_Service" title="Scottish Ambulance Service">Scottish Ambulance Service</a> provides funding for two helicopters and two fixed wing aircraft. The Welsh Assembly Government fully funds the clinical and road components of the Emergency Medical Retrieval and Transfer (EMRTS) Cymru service, with aviation (HEMS capability) provided in partnership with the Wales Air Ambulance Charity. There is an additional helicopter, <a href="/wiki/Scotland%27s_Charity_Air_Ambulance" title="Scotland&#39;s Charity Air Ambulance">Scotland's Charity Air Ambulance</a>, which is funded by charitable donations only.
</p>
<h2><span id="Regulation.2C_governance_and_monitoring"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation,_governance_and_monitoring">Regulation, governance and monitoring</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Regulation, governance and monitoring">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>All emergency medical services in the UK are subject to a range of legal and regulatory requirements, and in many cases are also monitored for performance. This framework is largely statutory in nature, being mandated by government through a range of primary and secondary legislation.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">Regulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Regulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In England all ambulance services, as well as some medical response organisations like BASICS, are regulated by the <a href="/wiki/Care_Quality_Commission" title="Care Quality Commission">Care Quality Commission</a> (CQC) under the provisions of the <a href="/wiki/Health_and_Social_Care_Act_2008" title="Health and Social Care Act 2008">Health and Social Care Act 2008</a> and subsequent Health and Social Care Act 2008 (Regulated Activities) Regulations 2010.
</p><p>This requires all providers to register, to meet certain standards of quality, and to submit to inspection of those standards.<sup id="cite_ref-CQC_signposting_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CQC_signposting-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> Organisations not meeting the standards can be sanctioned, or have their registration removed, preventing them from offering any medical services.
</p><p>The CQC replaced the previous regulator of England's NHS ambulance services, the <a href="/wiki/Healthcare_Commission" title="Healthcare Commission">Healthcare Commission</a>, with its remit expanded to include all private and voluntary providers. Independent ambulance services have only been subject to formal regulation since 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-CQC_signposting_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CQC_signposting-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to regulation by the Care Quality Commission on matters of service provision, providers of NHS services are also subject to regulation by either <a href="/wiki/Monitor_(NHS)" title="Monitor (NHS)">Monitor</a> (for NHS foundation trusts or private providers) or the <a href="/wiki/NHS_Trust_Development_Authority" title="NHS Trust Development Authority">NHS Trust Development Authority</a> (for NHS services who are not yet foundation trusts) for economic and financial matters.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Measuring_performance">Measuring performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Measuring performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The performance of every NHS ambulance provider is measured and benchmarked by the government. Commonly called '<a href="/wiki/Operational_Research_CONsultancy" title="Operational Research CONsultancy">ORCON</a>',<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> after the consultancy used to formulate them, the New Ambulance Performance Standards (NAPS) were developed in the 1990s, and merged into the Clinical Quality Indicators used subsequently.  New targets were established in July 2017. See <a href="/wiki/NHS_ambulance_services" title="NHS ambulance services">NHS ambulance services</a>.
</p><p>The benchmarked targets include:<sup id="cite_ref-choices1_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-choices1-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ul><li><b>Service experience</b> – patient satisfaction with the service</li>
<li><b>Outcome from acute <a href="/wiki/Myocardial_Infarction" class="mw-redirect" title="Myocardial Infarction">STEMI</a></b> – the number of patients who recover from a heart attack</li>
<li><b>Outcome from cardiac arrest</b> – the number of patients who get a <a href="/wiki/Return_of_spontaneous_circulation" title="Return of spontaneous circulation">return of spontaneous circulation</a> and those who are discharged from hospital</li>
<li><b>Outcome following <a href="/wiki/Stroke" title="Stroke">stroke</a></b></li>
<li><b>Proportion of calls closed with telephone advice</b> – also known as "hear and treat"</li>
<li><b>Proportion of calls managed without transport to A&amp;E</b> – also known as "see and treat"</li>
<li><b>Recontact rate following discharge of care</b> – patients who have had 'hear and treat' or 'see and treat' and who subsequently call 999 again</li>
<li><b>Call abandonment rate</b> – the number of people who don't get through to an ambulance dispatcher</li>
<li><b>Time to answer calls</b> – the time it takes to answer the phone</li>
<li><b>Time until treatment by an ambulance</b> – the wait time between calling and a health care professional being dispatched</li>
<li><b>Category A response time</b> – in cases triaged as Category A (life-threatening) by the triage software (<a href="/wiki/Advanced_Medical_Priority_Dispatch_System" class="mw-redirect" title="Advanced Medical Priority Dispatch System">AMPDS</a> and <a href="/wiki/NHS_Pathways" title="NHS Pathways">NHS Pathways</a> are the two approved systems), services are targeted to reach the patient within eight minutes of the call. In England, ambulance services are targeted on reaching 75% of Category A calls in 8 minutes, compared to 65% in Wales.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Governance">Governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Every ambulance provider is responsible to the CQC for compliance with best practice. Best practice guidance is published by the <a href="/wiki/Joint_Royal_Colleges_Ambulance_Liaison_Committee" title="Joint Royal Colleges Ambulance Liaison Committee">Joint Royal Colleges Ambulance Liaison Committee</a> (JRCALC), and most providers follow the majority of this issued guidance.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Staffing">Staffing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Staffing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Emergency_medical_personnel_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Emergency medical personnel in the United Kingdom">Emergency medical personnel in the United Kingdom</a></div>
<p>There are a range of staff grades who work in emergency medical services in the UK. The majority of staff fall into four main categories (in ascending skill order):
</p>
<ul><li><b>Emergency care assistants</b> – Usually work as part of a crew, alongside a paramedic. They operate in support of a paramedic or technician, performing duties such as driving, and first aid skills. As with technician, there is significant variation in the training levels between providers.</li>
<li><b>Technician</b> – technicians either support paramedics, or work autonomously. There is no single definition or qualification which covers all technicians, and they vary by service, especially between NHS and private providers</li>
<li><b>Paramedic</b> – paramedics are the core medical treatment grade, and the title is protected (meaning only those registered with the <a href="/wiki/Health_and_Care_Professions_Council" title="Health and Care Professions Council">Health and Care Professions Council</a> are entitled to use it, paramedics have autonomous practice, and a range of skills including intravenous cannuulation and advanced airway. In 2014 pay starts between £21,500 to £28,000 depending on experience and rises to £34,500 for team leaders and extended skills training. A full-time working week is 37.5 hours including night and weekend shifts as well as public holidays. Annual leave starts at 27 days per year plus public holidays or time in lieu and rising to 33 after 10 years’ service.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><b>Specialist Advanced paramedic</b> – such as an advanced paramedic, critical care paramedic, paramedic practitioner, HEMS paramedic or emergency care practitioner, who may have extra skills such as prescribing medication or administering additional drugs. Many specialist paramedics are trained to aid in delivering roadside procedures that are performed by doctors from, for example, an air ambulance, most ambulance trusts train Specialist or Advanced Paramedics at a <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Science" title="Master of Science">MSc</a> standard.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>In 2014/5 Ambulance Trusts were forced to look overseas to fill vacancies for paramedics. Only one was recruited from outside the UK in 2013-4 but 183 had been recruited since April 2014. Of these 175 were recruited by the <a href="/wiki/London_Ambulance_Service" title="London Ambulance Service">London Ambulance Service</a> from Australia. Across England, ambulance services reported that 1,382 of 15,887 posts were vacant – a rate of 9%.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Other roles within the NHS Ambulance Services include Ambulance Nurse (presently only employed by South Central Ambulance Service and identical in role to Paramedic, allowing interchangeability), Emergency Care Practitioner and similar Specialist Paramedic/Nurse roles with a focus on admission avoidance, and a broad range of support staff.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Supporting_services">Supporting services</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Supporting services">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="BASICS">BASICS</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: BASICS">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/British_Association_for_Immediate_Care" title="British Association for Immediate Care">British Association for Immediate Care</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/British_Association_for_Immediate_Care" title="British Association for Immediate Care">British Association for Immediate Care</a> coordinates voluntary schemes, and individual medical and allied health professionals, providing immediate care throughout the UK.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> BASICS doctors, nurses or paramedics may assist NHS paramedics at the scenes of serious accidents or be on-hand at major sporting events. All professionals volunteer their time, but doctors must have undergone additional training to support their working-environment.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Blood_Bikes">Blood Bikes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Blood Bikes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:FJR1300_emergency_blood.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/FJR1300_emergency_blood.jpg/220px-FJR1300_emergency_blood.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="163" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/FJR1300_emergency_blood.jpg/330px-FJR1300_emergency_blood.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/FJR1300_emergency_blood.jpg/440px-FJR1300_emergency_blood.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2607" data-file-height="1930" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:FJR1300_emergency_blood.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A blood bike operated by <a href="/wiki/Freewheelers_EVS" title="Freewheelers EVS">Freewheelers EVS</a></div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Blood_Bikes" class="mw-redirect" title="Blood Bikes">Blood Bikes</a></div>
<p>Across the British Isles, a network of volunteer <a href="/wiki/Blood_Bikes" class="mw-redirect" title="Blood Bikes">Blood Bikes</a> charity groups provide <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_courier" title="Motorcycle courier">motorcycle courier</a> services for blood, tissue and organs which require transport to, or between, hospitals. Some are equipped with blue lights and sirens which can be used when transporting urgent blood or human tissue for transplant. Groups are largely independent, and operate in collaboration with their local healthcare providers. Many are represented through the <a href="/w/index.php?title=NABB&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="NABB (page does not exist)">Nationwide Association of Blood Bikes (NABB)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Community_first_responders">Community first responders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Community first responders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Community_first_responder" title="Community first responder">Community first responder</a></div>
<p>Volunteer community first responders (CFRs) are now common place resources for NHS Ambulance Service. CFRs are members of the public who have received training to answer ambulance 999 calls, and respond immediately within their local area, during their own time. The schemes originated to provide defibrillation in rural and remote areas, where ambulances could not quickly respond, although they are now present in both rural and urban areas.
</p><p>CFRs are often operated by a local group, in partnership with the regional NHS ambulance trust, and carry a defibrillator and oxygen, along with other equipment as decided by the clinical governance arrangements. Some schemes have their own vehicles and actively fundraise to support their schemes.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fire_Service_Responders">Fire Service Responders</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Emergency_medical_services_in_the_United_Kingdom&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Fire Service Responders">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In more rural areas where ambulance responses can take longer, fire personnel have been trained in basic first aid and pain management. They are trained in <a href="/wiki/CPR" class="mw-redirect" title="CPR">CPR</a>, use of an <a href="/wiki/Automated_external_defibrillator" title="Automated external defibrillator">AED</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Entonox" class="mw-redirect" title="Entonox">entonox</a>.
They normally take a call from the ambulance <a href="/wiki/Emergency_Operations_Centre" class="mw-redirect" title="Emergency Operations Centre">Emergency Operations Centre</a> and respond in a car fitted with blue lights, sirens and ambulance / fire service livery. This service is normally staffed by retained firefighters.
</p>
