	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Eponym</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Someone or something after which something is named</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Eponymous" redirects here. For the R.E.M. album, see <a href="/wiki/Eponymous_(album)" title="Eponymous (album)">Eponymous (album)</a>.</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"S/T" redirects here. For the albums, see <a href="/wiki/S/T_(Electric_President_album)" title="S/T (Electric President album)">S/T (Electric President album)</a> and <a href="/wiki/S/T_(Rainer_Maria_album)" title="S/T (Rainer Maria album)">S/T (Rainer Maria album)</a>.</div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Namesake" title="Namesake">Namesake</a>.</div>
<p>An <b>eponym</b> is a person, place, or thing after whom or after which something is named, or believed to be named. The adjectives derived from eponym include <i>eponymous</i> and <i>eponymic</i>. For example, <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_I_of_England" title="Elizabeth I of England">Elizabeth I of England</a> is the eponym of the <a href="/wiki/Elizabethan_era" title="Elizabethan era">Elizabethan era</a>, and "the <i>eponymous</i> founder of the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a>" refers to <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ford" title="Henry Ford">Henry Ford</a>.  Recently, especially in the recorded-music industry, <i>eponymous</i> has been used to mean "named after its <a href="/wiki/Central_character" class="mw-redirect" title="Central character">central character</a> or creator".<sup id="cite_ref-dictionary_com_eponym_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dictionary_com_eponym-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-m-w_eponym_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-m-w_eponym-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-dictionary_com_eponymous_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dictionary_com_eponymous-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-m-w_eponymous_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-m-w_eponymous-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eponym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Periods have often been named after a ruler or other influential figure:
</p>
<ul><li>One of the first recorded cases of eponymy occurred in the second millennium BC, when the <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_people" title="Assyrian people">Assyrians</a> named each year after a high official (<i>limmu</i>).</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">ancient Greece</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Eponymous_archon" title="Eponymous archon">eponymous archon</a> was the highest magistrate in <a href="/wiki/Classical_Athens" title="Classical Athens">classical Athens</a>. Eponymous archons served a term of one year which took the name of that particular archon (e.g., 594 BC was named for <a href="/wiki/Solon" title="Solon">Solon</a>). Later historians provided yet another case of eponymy by referring to the period of <a href="/wiki/Fifth-century_Athens" title="Fifth-century Athens">fifth-century Athens</a> as The Age of Pericles after its most influential statesman <a href="/wiki/Pericles" title="Pericles">Pericles</a>.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_Egypt" class="mw-redirect" title="Ptolemaic Egypt">Ptolemaic Egypt</a>, the head priest of the <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_cult_of_Alexander_the_Great" title="Ptolemaic cult of Alexander the Great">Cult of Alexander and the Ptolemies</a> was the eponymous priest after whom years were named.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">Hebrew Bible</a> explains the origins of peoples through individuals who bear their name. Jacob is renamed "Israel" (Gen 35:9) and his sons (or grandsons) name the original <a href="/wiki/Israelites" title="Israelites">12 tribes of Israel</a>, while Edomites (Gen. 25:30), Moabites and Ammonites (Gen. 19:30-38), Canaanites (Gen. 9:20-27) and other tribes (the Kenites named after Cain Gen. 4:1-16) are said to be named for other primal ancestors bearing their name.  In most cases, the experiences and behavior of the ancestor is meant to indicate the characteristics of the people who take their name.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">ancient Rome</a>, one of the two formal ways of indicating a year was to cite the two annual <a href="/wiki/Consul" title="Consul">consuls</a> who served in that year. For example, the year we know as 59 BC would have been described as "the consulship of <a href="/wiki/Bibulus" class="mw-redirect" title="Bibulus">Marcus Calpurnius Bibulus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">Gaius Julius Caesar</a>" (although that specific year was known jocularly as "the consulship of Julius and Caesar" because of the insignificance of Caesar's counterpart). Under the empire, the consuls would change as often as every two months, but only the two consuls at the beginning of the year would lend their names to that year.</li>
<li>During the <a href="/wiki/Christian_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian era">Christian era</a>, itself eponymous, many royal households used eponymous dating by <a href="/wiki/Regnal_year" title="Regnal year">regnal years</a>. The Roman Catholic Church, however, eventually used the <i><a href="/wiki/Anno_Domini" title="Anno Domini">Anno Domini</a></i> dating scheme based on the birth of Christ on both the general public and royalty. The regnal year standard is still used with respect to statutes and law reports published in some parts of the United Kingdom and in some Commonwealth countries (England abandoned this practice in 1963): a statute signed into law in Canada between February 6, 1994 and February 5, 1995 would be dated 43 Elizabeth II, for instance.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></li>
<li>Government administrations may become referred to eponymously, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Camelot_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Camelot era">Kennedy's Camelot</a></i> and <i>the Nixon Era</i>.</li>
<li>British monarchs have become eponymous throughout the English-speaking world for time periods, fashions, etc. <i><a href="/wiki/Elizabethan_era" title="Elizabethan era">Elizabethan</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Georgian_period_in_British_history" class="mw-redirect" title="Georgian period in British history">Georgian</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Edwardian_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Edwardian period">Edwardian</a></i> are examples of these.</li></ul>
<p><b>Trends</b>
</p>
<ul><li>Political trends or movements often become eponymous with a government leader. Examples include <i><a href="/wiki/Jacksonian_democracy" title="Jacksonian democracy">Jacksonian democracy</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Stalinism" title="Stalinism">Stalinism</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Maoism" title="Maoism">Maoism</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Obamacare" class="mw-redirect" title="Obamacare">Obamacare</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Thatcherism" title="Thatcherism">Thatcherism</a></i>.</li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_eponyms">Other eponyms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eponym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Other eponyms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>In <a href="/wiki/Intellectual_property_law" class="mw-redirect" title="Intellectual property law">intellectual property law</a>, an eponym can refer to a <a href="/wiki/Generic_trademark" title="Generic trademark">generic trademark</a> or brand name, a form of <a href="/wiki/Metonymy" title="Metonymy">metonymy</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Aspirin" title="Aspirin">aspirin</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-bayer_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bayer-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Heroin" title="Heroin">heroin</a><sup id="cite_ref-etymon_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-etymon-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Thermos" class="mw-redirect" title="Thermos">thermos</a><sup id="cite_ref-fed_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fed-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> in the United States.</li>
<li>In geography, places and towns can also be given an eponymous name through a relationship to an important figure. <a href="/wiki/Peloponnesus" class="mw-redirect" title="Peloponnesus">Peloponnesus</a>, for instance, was said to derive its name from the Greek hero <a href="/wiki/Pelops" title="Pelops">Pelops</a>. In historical times, new towns have often been named (and older communities renamed) after their founders, discoverers, or notable individuals. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Vancouver,_British_Columbia" class="mw-redirect" title="Vancouver, British Columbia">Vancouver, British Columbia</a>, named for explorer <a href="/wiki/George_Vancouver" title="George Vancouver">George Vancouver</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Prince_Albert,_Saskatchewan" title="Prince Albert, Saskatchewan">Prince Albert, Saskatchewan</a>, originally called <a href="/wiki/Isbister%27s_Settlement" class="mw-redirect" title="Isbister&#39;s Settlement">Isbister's Settlement</a> but renamed after <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">Queen Victoria</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Albert,_Prince_Consort" title="Albert, Prince Consort">husband and consort</a> in 1866.</li>
<li><span id="Scitech2014-09-30"></span>In science and technology, discoveries and innovations are often named after the discoverer or a figure influential in their advance. Examples are <a href="/wiki/Avogadro%27s_number" class="mw-redirect" title="Avogadro&#39;s number">Avogadro's number</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Diesel_engine" title="Diesel engine">Diesel engine</a>, <a href="/wiki/Meitnerium" title="Meitnerium">meitnerium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease" title="Alzheimer&#39;s disease">Alzheimer's disease</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Apgar_score" title="Apgar score">Apgar score</a>. For a different view of the process see <a href="/wiki/Stigler%27s_law_of_eponymy" title="Stigler&#39;s law of eponymy">Stigler's law of eponymy</a>.</li>
<li>In art:
<ul><li>Plays, books, and other forms of entertainment may have eponymous names, such as the ancient Greek epic <i><a href="/wiki/The_Odyssey" class="mw-redirect" title="The Odyssey">The Odyssey</a></i>, derived from its principal character, <a href="/wiki/Odysseus" title="Odysseus">Odysseus</a>, and the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Robinson_Crusoe" title="Robinson Crusoe">Robinson Crusoe</a></i>.</li>
<li>The term is also applied to <a href="/wiki/Music" title="Music">music</a>, usually with regard to record titles, where it is prevalent and leads to confusion. For example, <a href="/wiki/Bad_Company_(band)" class="mw-redirect" title="Bad Company (band)">Bad Company</a>'s first album was entitled <i><a href="/wiki/Bad_Company_(album)" title="Bad Company (album)">Bad Company</a></i> and contained a popular song named "<a href="/wiki/Bad_Company_(song)" title="Bad Company (song)">Bad Company</a>". Parodying this, the band <a href="/wiki/R.E.M." title="R.E.M.">R.E.M.</a> titled a 1988 compilation album <i><a href="/wiki/Eponymous_(album)" title="Eponymous (album)">Eponymous</a></i>.</li></ul></li>
<li>In tribal antiquity, both in ancient Greece and independently among the Hebrews, tribes often took the name of a legendary leader (as <a href="/wiki/Achaeus,_son_of_Xuthus" class="mw-redirect" title="Achaeus, son of Xuthus">Achaeus</a> for <a href="/wiki/Achaeans_(tribe)" title="Achaeans (tribe)">Achaeans</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Dorus" class="mw-redirect" title="Dorus">Dorus</a> for <a href="/wiki/Dorians" title="Dorians">Dorians</a>). The eponym gave apparent meaning to the mysterious names of tribes, and sometimes, as in the <a href="/wiki/Sons_of_Noah" class="mw-redirect" title="Sons of Noah">Sons of Noah</a>, provided a primitive attempt at <a href="/wiki/Ethnology" title="Ethnology">ethnology</a> as well, in the genealogical relationships of eponymous originators.</li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoology</a>, an animal name that includes the name of a person is properly called an eponym.<sup id="cite_ref-beo_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-beo-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Orthographic_conventions">Orthographic conventions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eponym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orthographic conventions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Capitalized_versus_lowercase">Capitalized versus lowercase</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eponym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Capitalized versus lowercase">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Because <a href="/wiki/Proper_nouns" class="mw-redirect" title="Proper nouns">proper nouns</a> are capitalized in English, the usual default for eponyms is to capitalize the eponymous part of a term. When used as <a href="/wiki/Proper_adjective" title="Proper adjective">proper adjectives</a> they are normally capitalized, for example <a href="/wiki/Victorian_era" title="Victorian era">Victorian</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shakespearean" class="mw-redirect" title="Shakespearean">Shakespearean</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Kafkaesque" class="mw-redirect" title="Kafkaesque">Kafkaesque</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-oxford_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>However, some eponymous adjectives and <a href="/wiki/Noun_adjunct" title="Noun adjunct">noun adjuncts</a> are nowadays entered in many dictionaries as lowercase when they have evolved a common status, no longer deriving their meaning from the proper-noun origin.<sup id="cite_ref-M-W_C_10_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M-W_C_10-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> For example, <i>Herculean</i> when referring to Hercules himself, but often <i>herculean</i> when referring to the figurative, generalized extension sense;<sup id="cite_ref-M-W_C_10_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M-W_C_10-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> and <i>quixotic</i> and <i>diesel engine</i> [lowercase only].<sup id="cite_ref-M-W_C_10_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-M-W_C_10-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AHD4_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AHD4-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> For any given term, one dictionary may enter only lowercase or only cap, whereas other dictionaries may recognize the capitalized version as a variant, either equally common as, or less common than, the first-listed styling (marked with labels such as "or", "also", "often", or "sometimes"). <i><a href="/wiki/The_Chicago_Manual_of_Style" title="The Chicago Manual of Style">The Chicago Manual of Style</a></i>, in its section "Words derived from proper names",<sup id="cite_ref-CMOS_14e_pp253-254_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CMOS_14e_pp253-254-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> gives some examples of both lowercase and capitalized stylings, including a few terms styled both ways, and says, "Authors and editors must decide for themselves, but whatever choice is made should be followed consistently throughout a work."</li>
<li>When the eponym is used together with a noun, the common-noun part is not capitalized (unless it is part of a title or it is the first word in a sentence). For example, in <i>Parkinson disease</i> (named after <a href="/wiki/James_Parkinson" title="James Parkinson">James Parkinson</a>), <i>Parkinson</i> is capitalized, but <i>disease</i> is not. In addition, the adjectival form, where one exists, is usually lowercased for medical terms (thus <i><a href="/wiki/Parkinsonian" class="mw-redirect" title="Parkinsonian">parkinsonian</a></i> although <i>Parkinson disease</i>),<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Gram-negative_bacteria" title="Gram-negative bacteria">gram-negative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gram-positive_bacteria" title="Gram-positive bacteria">gram-positive</a> although <a href="/wiki/Gram_stain" title="Gram stain">Gram stain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Uppercase Gram-positive or Gram-negative however are also commonly used in <a href="/wiki/Scientific_journal" title="Scientific journal">scientific journal</a> articles and publications.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>  In other fields, the eponym derivative is commonly capitalized, for example, <i>Newtonian</i> in physics,<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> and <i><a href="/wiki/Platonism" title="Platonism">Platonic</a></i> in philosophy (however, use lowercase <i>platonic</i> when describing love).<sup id="cite_ref-oxford_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> The capitalization is retained after a prefix and hyphen, e.g. <i>non-Newtonian</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-oxford_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-oxford-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<p>For examples, see the <a href="#Comparison_table_of_eponym_orthographic_styling">comparison table</a> below.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genitive_versus_attributive">Genitive versus attributive</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eponym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Genitive versus attributive">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>English can use either <a href="/wiki/Genitive" class="mw-redirect" title="Genitive">genitive</a> case or attributive position to indicate the adjectival nature of the eponymous part of the term. (In other words, that part may be either possessive or non-possessive.) Thus <i>Parkinson's disease</i> and <i>Parkinson disease</i> are both acceptable. Medical dictionaries have been shifting toward nonpossessive styling in recent decades.<sup id="cite_ref-AMAMOS2007ch16_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AMAMOS2007ch16-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Thus <i>Parkinson disease</i> is more likely to be used in the latest medical literature (especially in <a href="/wiki/Postprint" title="Postprint">postprints</a>) than is <i>Parkinson's disease</i>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="National_varieties_of_English">National varieties of English</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Eponym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: National varieties of English">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences" title="American and British English spelling differences">American and British English spelling differences</a> may apply to eponyms. For example, British style would typically be <i><a href="/wiki/Caesarean_section" title="Caesarean section">caesarean section</a></i>, which is also found in American medical publications, but <i>cæsarean section</i> (with a digraph) is sometimes seen in (mostly older) British writing, and <i>cesarean</i> is preferred by American dictionaries and some American medical works.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
