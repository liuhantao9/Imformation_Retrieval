<p><b>Wimpy</b> is the brand name of a multinational chain of former <a href="/wiki/Fast_food_restaurant" title="Fast food restaurant">fast food restaurants</a> that has been gradually upgrading units to become <a href="/wiki/Casual_dining" class="mw-redirect" title="Casual dining">casual dining</a> style <a href="/wiki/Diner" title="Diner">diners</a> with table service since a change of ownership that had occurred in 2007. The brand is currently headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Johannesburg" title="Johannesburg">Johannesburg</a>, South Africa. The chain originally began in 1934 in the United States and was based in  <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>. The brand was introduced to the United Kingdom in 1954 as "Wimpy Bar".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Wimpy grew to approximately 1,500 locations in several countries before narrowing to a few hundred locations in two countries. Wimpy's worldwide headquarters was located in the United States and the United Kingdom before relocating to South Africa.
</p><p>Wimpy's menu consists primarily of <a href="/wiki/Hamburger" title="Hamburger">hamburgers</a>, <a href="/wiki/French_fries" title="French fries">chips (fries)</a>, breakfast items, and beverages.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_in_the_United_States">Origins in the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wimpy_(restaurant)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins in the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Wimpy brand was established in 1934 by Edward Gold, when he opened his first location in <a href="/wiki/Bloomington,_Indiana" title="Bloomington, Indiana">Bloomington, Indiana</a> under the name <i>Wimpy Grills</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-erg-1977oct16_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-erg-1977oct16-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-cst-2004jul25_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cst-2004jul25-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> The name was inspired by the character of <a href="/wiki/J._Wellington_Wimpy" title="J. Wellington Wimpy">J. Wellington Wimpy</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Popeye" title="Popeye">Popeye</a> cartoons created by <a href="/wiki/E._C._Segar" title="E. C. Segar">E. C. Segar</a>. Gold did not open his first Chicago area location until two years later in 1936, after opening units in five other <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwestern</a> cities.<sup id="cite_ref-cdt-1935nov17_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cdt-1935nov17-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> By 1947, the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> reported that the chain had 26 units, and expected to sell eight million hamburgers annually in the Chicago area.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to a 1978 <i>Chicago Tribune</i> article, Gold's company Wimpy Grills Inc. of Chicago, had 25 locations in the United States at its peak, but only seven locations remained at the time of his death in 1977.<sup id="cite_ref-ct-1978jul28_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ct-1978jul28-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> The chain vanished within the United States after Gold's death because no one had purchased the rights and trademark to the Wimpy name from Gold's estate.<sup id="cite_ref-ct-1978jul28_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ct-1978jul28-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Wimpy.27s_International"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Wimpy's_International">Wimpy's International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wimpy_(restaurant)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wimpy&#039;s International">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1954, Gold sold a license to <a href="/wiki/J._Lyons_and_Co." title="J. Lyons and Co.">J. Lyons and Co.</a> to use the Wimpy name in the United Kingdom.<sup id="cite_ref-wp-1977oct17_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wp-1977oct17-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nyt-1977oct16_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1977oct16-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Wimpy Grills Inc. of Chicago later formed a joint company with Lyons called Wimpy's International Inc. in 1957. Wimpy's International was based in Chicago and allowed the brand to operate Wimpy Grills in the rest of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt-1957apr05_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1957apr05-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The joint company eventually grew to 1,500 locations, with Gold later selling his share to Lyons prior to his death.<sup id="cite_ref-ct-1978jul28_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ct-1978jul28-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> After obtaining full control of the international licensing outside of the United States, Lyons and its successors handled global franchising through their United Kingdom-based subsidiary <i>Wimpy International Ltd</i>. This arrangement ceased when Wimpy UK became a subsidiary of the South Africa-based Famous Brands in 2007. The South African company started to handle worldwide franchising duties directly from Johannesburg.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wimpy_(restaurant)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lyons obtained a license to use the Wimpy brand in the United Kingdom from Edward Gold's Chicago based Wimpy Grills Inc.<sup id="cite_ref-wp-1977oct17_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wp-1977oct17-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nyt-1977oct16_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-1977oct16-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> and, in 1954, the first "Wimpy Bar" Lyons was established at the <a href="/wiki/Lyons_Corner_House" class="mw-redirect" title="Lyons Corner House">Lyons Corner House</a> in Coventry Street, London.<sup id="cite_ref-moments_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moments-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wp-1955jun05_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wp-1955jun05-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Originally, the bar was a special fast food section within traditional Corner House restaurants, but the success soon led to the establishment of separate Wimpy restaurants serving only hamburger-based meals.
</p><p>In a 1955 newspaper column, <a href="/wiki/Art_Buchwald" title="Art Buchwald">Art Buchwald</a>, syndicated writer for the <i><a href="/wiki/Washington_Post" class="mw-redirect" title="Washington Post">Washington Post</a></i>, wrote about the recent opening of a "Wimpy's Hamburger Parlor" on Coventry Street and about the influence of American culture on the British.<sup id="cite_ref-wp-1955jun05_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wp-1955jun05-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Buchwald wrote, "Food served at the table within ten minutes of ordering and with atomic age efficiency. No cutlery needed or given. Drinks served in a bottle with a straw. Condiments in pre-packaged single serving packets." In addition to familiar Wimpy burgers and milkshakes, the British franchise initially had served ham or sardine rolls called torpedoes and a cold frankfurter with pickled cucumber sandwiches called Freddies.<sup id="cite_ref-wp-1955jun05_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wp-1955jun05-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During the 1970s Wimpy refused entry to women on their own after midnight. This may be because of an assumption they might be prostitutes.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By 1970, the business had expanded to over 1,000 restaurants in 23 countries.<sup id="cite_ref-moments_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-moments-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In July 1977, the business was acquired by <a href="/wiki/United_Biscuits" title="United Biscuits">United Biscuits</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-tgh-1977jul09_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tgh-1977jul09-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> By the end of the 1980s, Wimpy was beginning to lose ground to <a href="/wiki/McDonald%27s" title="McDonald&#39;s">McDonald’s</a>, which had opened its first restaurant in the country in 1974, and so the new management of Wimpy began to streamline the business, by converting some of the traditional <a href="/wiki/Table_service" class="mw-redirect" title="Table service">table service</a> restaurants to counter service. When United Biscuits decided to divest its restaurant division in 1989, it sold the business to <a href="/wiki/Grand_Metropolitan" title="Grand Metropolitan">Grand Metropolitan</a> (now <a href="/wiki/Diageo" title="Diageo">Diageo</a>). At the time of the sale, there were 381 locations in the United Kingdom. Grand Metropolitan had acquired <a href="/wiki/Burger_King" title="Burger King">Burger King</a> the previous year, and it began to convert the <a href="/wiki/Counter_service" class="mw-redirect" title="Counter service">counter service</a> restaurants to Burger King since it had a greater global brand recognition.<sup id="cite_ref-thg-1989aug08_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-thg-1989aug08-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 1990, the remaining 216 table service restaurants were purchased by a management buyout, backed by <a href="/wiki/3i" title="3i">3i</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-ct-1990feb07_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ct-1990feb07-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> These were locations that were considered less desirable by Grand Metropolitan. At the time of the buyout, there were also 140 franchised locations outside of the United Kingdom. In October 1999, Wimpy rolled out a chain of restaurants known as <i>Dr. Beaks</i>, to take on brands such as <a href="/wiki/KFC" title="KFC">KFC</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A second management buyout occurred in May 2002, backed by the <a href="/wiki/Bank_of_Scotland" title="Bank of Scotland">Bank of Scotland</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-dt-2002may26_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dt-2002may26-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> At the time of the sale in 2002, there were approximately 300 locations in the United Kingdom and Ireland.
</p><p>Although Wimpy outlets have decreased in numbers in the United Kingdom, they are still found in many cities, and at seafront/seasonal locations, such as <a href="/wiki/Clacton-on-Sea" title="Clacton-on-Sea">Clacton-on-Sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Clarence_Pier" title="Clarence Pier">Clarence Pier</a> in <a href="/wiki/Southsea" title="Southsea">Southsea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porthcawl" title="Porthcawl">Porthcawl</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brean_Leisure_Park" title="Brean Leisure Park">Brean Leisure Park</a> in Somerset.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> By the beginning of the 21st century, most Wimpys were found in less desirable low rent locations that primarily cater to pensioners and others on a fixed income. This was a change as they were not in the former high street locations of their earlier days of the company.<sup id="cite_ref-tg-2002jan31_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tg-2002jan31-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Another big change from earlier times was that most locations were now franchises and not company-owned operations.
</p><p>On 27 February 2007, <a href="/wiki/Famous_Brands" title="Famous Brands">Famous Brands</a>, which owns the Wimpy franchise in South Africa, announced that it had acquired Wimpy UK.<sup id="cite_ref-vm-2014apr29_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vm-2014apr29-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-allafrica.com-2007feb28_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-allafrica.com-2007feb28-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> Having acquired the brand, Famous Brands has rebranded Wimpy in the United Kingdom, to bring it in line with Wimpy South Africa. The "new" logo is actually the one used by Wimpy UK from the 1960s until the 1980s. In November 2009, Famous Brands began to upgrade its 170 locations in the United Kingdom to resemble United States-style diners.<sup id="cite_ref-mm-2009nov24_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mm-2009nov24-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> As of March 2019, only 67 restaurants remain in the United Kingdom,<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> down from over 500 during its height in the 1970s.<sup id="cite_ref-vm-2014apr29_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vm-2014apr29-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Wimpy International opened its first South African location in <a href="/wiki/Durban" title="Durban">Durban</a> in 1967.<sup id="cite_ref-tassiopoulos-2008_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tassiopoulos-2008-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The South African restaurants were sold to Bakers SA Ltd in the late 1970s, which in 1987 sold the South African chain to Pleasure Foods, then known as Juicy Lucy SA.<sup id="cite_ref-maas-2013_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-maas-2013-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Famous_Brands_Limited" class="mw-redirect" title="Famous Brands Limited">Famous Brands Limited</a>, then known as the Steers Holdings Limited, acquired Wimpy when it bought Pleasure Foods in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-fin24-2003aug19_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fin24-2003aug19-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Famous Brands acquired the United Kingdom-based Wimpy in February 2007, thus becoming the parent company for the chain and beginning to collect the franchise fees from the other franchises.<sup id="cite_ref-vm-2014apr29_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-vm-2014apr29-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By 2011, Famous Brands had 509 Wimpy restaurants in South Africa, making it the largest franchise in the Wimpy franchise system.<sup id="cite_ref-bdsa-2011oct12_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bdsa-2011oct12-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Former_locations">Former locations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wimpy_(restaurant)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Former locations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wimpy_(restaurant)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Europe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="France">France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wimpy_(restaurant)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In May 1961, French businessman <a href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Borel_(industriel)" class="extiw" title="fr:Jacques Borel (industriel)">Jacques Borel</a> opened his first Wimpy restaurant in <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">Paris</a> after obtaining an exclusive franchise to operate Wimpy restaurants in France.<sup id="cite_ref-ladepeche-2011may29_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ladepeche-2011may29-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> His restaurant was the first hamburger restaurant in France and had predated the opening of the first McDonald's restaurant by a decade.
</p><p>By the end of that year, he was able to open three more locations within Paris. By 1966, he began to expand outside the capital, starting with Lille.<sup id="cite_ref-tm-1966may06_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tm-1966may06-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Within a short time, Borel had 20 locations; 15 in Paris and five in the rest of the country.<sup id="cite_ref-ladepeche-2011may29_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ladepeche-2011may29-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Borel's company was so successful that the French newspaper called the French franchise "public enemy number 1" for introducing American hamburger cuisine to French public at large.<sup id="cite_ref-ladepeche-2011may29_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ladepeche-2011may29-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Although successful, Borel was forced to close the chain in 1969, owing to a disagreement he had with Lyons, the British parent company.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Ireland">Ireland</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wimpy_(restaurant)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ireland">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Wimpy has been in Ireland on two occasions, in the 1960s, and the early 2000s.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> In the 2000s, petrol station chain operator <a href="/wiki/Petrogas" title="Petrogas">Petrogas</a> took on the master franchise for Ireland,<sup id="cite_ref-caterersearch_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-caterersearch-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> and had operated a large number of sites until 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-petrogas2010_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-petrogas2010-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most have been replaced by Grill'n'Fill own branded areas, selling virtually identical products, or Burger King, for which Petrogas had obtained the franchise. Petrogas terminated its master franchise agreement with Wimpy International in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-petrogas2010_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-petrogas2010-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Netherlands">Netherlands</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wimpy_(restaurant)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Netherlands">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>In 1963, the Dutch supermarket chain <a href="/wiki/Albert_Heijn" title="Albert Heijn">Albert Heijn</a> obtain a license from Chicago-based Wimpy International to open the first Wimpy restaurant in the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> on <a href="/wiki/Leidsestraat" class="mw-redirect" title="Leidsestraat">Leidsestraat</a> in <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam" title="Amsterdam">Amsterdam</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> A second restaurant opened the following year on <a href="/wiki/Damrak" title="Damrak">Damrak</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Although there are no verifiable records showing when the last Wimpy closed in the Netherlands, but it appears that Wimpy may have left the Netherlands by 1969 since Albert Heijn ceased to mention Wimpy in their corporate annual reports after 1969.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> In 1971, Albert Heijn later helped McDonald's to open its first restaurant in Europe by opening a McDonald's franchise in <a href="/wiki/Zaandam" title="Zaandam">Zaandam</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="West_Germany">West Germany</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wimpy_(restaurant)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: West Germany">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The first Wimpy restaurant in <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West Germany</a> was open in <a href="/wiki/Bochum" title="Bochum">Bochum</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Bochum_Hauptbahnhof" title="Bochum Hauptbahnhof">Bochum Hauptbahnhof</a> in December 1964 by restaurateur Heinrich Lobenberg under a franchise license from Wimpy International, exactly seven years prior to the opening of first McDonalds in Germany, with plans to open up to 300 units in West Germany.<sup id="cite_ref-spiegel_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spiegel-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Lobenberg had cofounded the then five-unit <a href="/wiki/Kochl%C3%B6ffel" title="Kochlöffel">Kochlöffel</a> chain just three years before in <a href="/wiki/Lingen,_Germany" title="Lingen, Germany">Lingen</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-spiegel_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spiegel-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> There are no verifiable records that Lobenberg had opened a second Wimpy unit or how long the Bochum restaurant had operated before it finally closed. A food historian believes that Wimpy's failure to survive in <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> era West Germany was caused by West German consumers of the 1960s preferring to eat familiar German-style chicken meals at the local <a href="/wiki/Wienerwald_(restaurant)" title="Wienerwald (restaurant)">Wienerwald</a> chain instead of getting an unfamiliar American-style hamburger sandwich at Wimpy.<sup id="cite_ref-germanfoodhistory-2014_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-germanfoodhistory-2014-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oceania">Oceania</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wimpy_(restaurant)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Oceania">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wimpy_(restaurant)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Australia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The Wimpy license in Australia was held by Happysnaks Pty Ltd and was owned by restaurateur and entrepreneur Oliver Shaul.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ma-1964aug07_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ma-1964aug07-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> The first Wimpy bar opened in <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">Sydney</a> in November 1964, owned and operated directly by Happysnaks. Subsequent Wimpy bars operated as franchises.
</p><p>By 1971, there were 70 Wimpy bars around Australia, the most successful being in <a href="/wiki/Hindley_Street,_Adelaide" title="Hindley Street, Adelaide">Hindley Street, Adelaide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DoudleBaldwin_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DoudleBaldwin-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> Unable to compete with McDonald's, the Wimpy brand was withdrawn in 1975 and most Wimpy bars became independent fast food outlets.<sup id="cite_ref-DoudleBaldwin_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DoudleBaldwin-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wimpy_(restaurant)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Wimpy had branches in New Zealand, including <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">Auckland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hamilton,_New_Zealand" title="Hamilton, New Zealand">Hamilton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tauranga" title="Tauranga">Tauranga</a>, Paraparaumu, Wanganui, Palmerston North, New Plymouth and Taupo.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Are there any proof to support these claims? (August 2018)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> These were all rebranded in 2002. One store remained in Rotorua trading as Wimpy, although it was not part of the franchise chain. The last Wimpy in New Zealand was put up for sale in May 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wimpy_(restaurant)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Asia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wimpy_(restaurant)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Wimpy was one of the first fast food restaurant chains to open in <a href="/wiki/Economic_liberalisation_in_India" title="Economic liberalisation in India">India's free market economy</a>. With a run of initial success, Wimpy was often touted as the first icon of '<a href="/wiki/Westernization" title="Westernization">Westernization</a>' in India. A few of the restaurants which were still in business were located in <a href="/wiki/Punjab,_India" title="Punjab, India">Punjab</a> until 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Israel">Israel</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Wimpy_(restaurant)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Israel">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Wimpy entered the <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israeli</a> market in the 1960s, and for a time dominated the hamburger restaurant market.<sup id="cite_ref-lexicon_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lexicon-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-change_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-change-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Before its final failure and pullout in the 1980s, Wimpy was joined in an emerging market for hamburger restaurants by <a href="/wiki/Burgeranch" title="Burgeranch">Burgeranch</a> (since 1972) and <a href="/wiki/MacDavid" title="MacDavid">MacDavid</a> (since 1978). Wimpy restaurants were infamous in Israel for poor product flavor, and even for poor sanitation and hygiene; however, the chain did pioneer hamburger restaurants in Israel.<sup id="cite_ref-lexicon_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lexicon-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-change_49-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-change-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-falafel_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-falafel-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
