	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Messiah</i> Part II</h1>

<p><i><b><a href="/wiki/Messiah_(Handel)" title="Messiah (Handel)">Messiah</a></b></i> (<a href="/wiki/H%C3%A4ndel-Werke-Verzeichnis" title="Händel-Werke-Verzeichnis">HWV</a> 56), the English-language <a href="/wiki/Oratorio" title="Oratorio">oratorio</a> composed by <a href="/wiki/George_Frideric_Handel" title="George Frideric Handel">George Frideric Handel</a> in 1741, is <a href="/wiki/Structure_of_Handel%27s_Messiah" title="Structure of Handel&#39;s Messiah">structured</a> in three parts. This listing covers <b>Part II</b> in a table and comments on individual movements, reflecting the relation of the musical setting to the text. <a href="/wiki/Messiah_Part_I" title="Messiah Part I">Part I</a> begins with the prophecy of the <a href="/wiki/Messiah#Christianity" title="Messiah">Messiah</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nativity_of_Jesus" title="Nativity of Jesus">his birth</a>, shows the <a href="/wiki/Annunciation_to_the_shepherds" title="Annunciation to the shepherds">annunciation to the shepherds</a> and reflects the Messiah's deeds on earth. Part II covers the <a href="/wiki/Passion_(Christianity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Passion (Christianity)">Passion</a> in nine movements including the oratorio's longest movement, an <a href="/wiki/Aria" title="Aria">air</a> for <a href="/wiki/Alto" title="Alto">alto</a> <i><b>He was despised</b></i>, then mentions death, resurrection, <a href="/wiki/Ascension_of_Jesus" title="Ascension of Jesus">ascension</a>, and reflects the spreading of the Gospel and its rejection. The part is concluded by a scene called "God's Triumph" that culminates in the <i><a href="#44">Hallelujah Chorus</a></i>. <a href="/wiki/Messiah_Part_III" title="Messiah Part III">Part III</a> of the oratorio concentrates on <a href="/wiki/Paul_the_Apostle" title="Paul the Apostle">Paul</a>'s teaching of the <a href="/wiki/1_Corinthians_15#Resurrection_of_the_dead:_12-58" title="1 Corinthians 15">resurrection of the dead</a> and Christ's <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation#Outline" title="Book of Revelation">glorification in heaven</a>.
</p>

<h2><span id="Messiah.2C_the_oratorio"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Messiah,_the_oratorio">Messiah, the oratorio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messiah_Part_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Messiah, the oratorio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Christopher_Hogwood_2014.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Christopher_Hogwood_2014.jpg/170px-Christopher_Hogwood_2014.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="255" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Christopher_Hogwood_2014.jpg/255px-Christopher_Hogwood_2014.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Christopher_Hogwood_2014.jpg/340px-Christopher_Hogwood_2014.jpg 2x" data-file-width="970" data-file-height="1455" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Christopher_Hogwood_2014.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Christopher Hogwood in rehearsal in 2014</div></div></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">libretto</a> by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Jennens" title="Charles Jennens">Charles Jennens</a> is entirely drawn from the Bible, mostly from the <a href="/wiki/Authorized_King_James_Version" class="mw-redirect" title="Authorized King James Version">King James Bible</a>, whereas several <a href="/wiki/Psalms" title="Psalms">psalms</a> are taken from the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">Book of Common Prayer</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Vickers_2012_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vickers_2012-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Powell_2009_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Powell_2009-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The librettist commented: "... the Subject excells every other Subject. The Subject is Messiah ...".<sup id="cite_ref-Heighes_1997_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heighes_1997-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <i>Messiah</i> differs from Handel's other oratorios by telling no story, instead offering reflections on different aspects of the Christian <a href="/wiki/Messiah#Christianity" title="Messiah">Messiah</a>. <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Hogwood" title="Christopher Hogwood">Christopher Hogwood</a> comments:
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r886047036">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 40px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p><i>Messiah</i> is not a typical Handel oratorio; there are no named characters, as are usually found in Handel’s setting of the Old Testament stories, possibly to avoid charges of blasphemy. It is a meditation rather than a drama of personalities, lyrical in method; the narration of the story is carried on by implication, and there is no dialogue.</p><div class="templatequotecite">—&#8201;<cite><a href="/wiki/Christopher_Hogwood" title="Christopher Hogwood">Christopher Hogwood</a><sup id="cite_ref-Block_2001_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Block_2001-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup></cite></div>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure_and_concept">Structure and concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messiah_Part_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure and concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Structure_of_Handel%27s_Messiah" title="Structure of Handel&#39;s Messiah">Structure of Handel's Messiah</a></div>
<p>The oratorio's structure follows the <a href="/wiki/Liturgical_year" title="Liturgical year">liturgical year</a>; Part I corresponding with <a href="/wiki/Advent" title="Advent">Advent</a>, Christmas and the life of Jesus, Part II with <a href="/wiki/Lent" title="Lent">Lent</a>, Easter, <a href="/wiki/Feast_of_the_Ascension" title="Feast of the Ascension">Ascension</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pentecost" title="Pentecost">Pentecost</a>, Part III with the end of the church year, dealing with the end of time, the <a href="/wiki/1_Corinthians_15#Resurrection_of_the_dead:_12-58" title="1 Corinthians 15">Resurrection of the dead</a> and Christ's <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation#Outline" title="Book of Revelation">glorification in heaven</a>. The sources are drawn mostly from the <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">Old Testament</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Powell_2009_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Powell_2009-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Even the birth and death of Jesus are told in the words of the prophet <a href="/wiki/Isaiah" title="Isaiah">Isaiah</a>, the most prominent source of the libretto. The only true scene of the oratorio is taken from the <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">Gospel of Luke</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Annunciation_to_the_shepherds" title="Annunciation to the shepherds">annunciation to the shepherds</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Luckett_1992_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luckett_1992-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The imagery of shepherd and lamb features prominently, in the aria "He shall feed His flock like a shepherd", the only extended piece to talk about the Messiah on earth, in the opening of Part II, "Behold the Lamb of God", in the chorus "All we like sheep", and in the closing chorus of the work, "Worthy is the Lamb". Occasionally verses from different biblical sources are combined in one movement, but more often a coherent text section is set in different consecutive movements, such as the first "<a href="/wiki/Structure_of_Handel%27s_Messiah#Scenes" title="Structure of Handel&#39;s Messiah">scene</a>", the annunciation of <a href="/wiki/Christian_salvation" class="mw-redirect" title="Christian salvation">Christian salvation</a>, as a sequence of three movements, <a href="/wiki/Recitative" title="Recitative">recitative</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aria" title="Aria">aria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Choir" title="Choir">chorus</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messiah_Part_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When Handel composed <i>Messiah</i> in London, he was already a successful and experienced composer of Italian operas. He had started in 1713 to also compose sacred music on English texts, such as the <i><a href="/wiki/Utrecht_Te_Deum_and_Jubilate" title="Utrecht Te Deum and Jubilate">Utrecht Te Deum and Jubilate</a></i>. He set many <a href="/wiki/Oratorio" title="Oratorio">oratorios</a> on English <a href="/wiki/Libretto" title="Libretto">libretti</a>. In <i>Messiah</i> he used practically the same musical means as for those works, namely a structure based on chorus and solo singing. Only a few movements are a <a href="/wiki/Duet" title="Duet">duet</a> or a combination of solo and chorus. The solos are typically a combination of recitative and aria. The arias are called Air or Song, some of them have <a href="/wiki/Da_capo" title="Da capo">da capo</a> form, but rarely in a strict sense, repeating a first section after a sometimes contrasting middle section. Handel finds various ways to use the format freely, in order to convey the text. The movements marked "Recitative" (Rec.) are "secco", only accompanied by the <a href="/wiki/Figured_bass#Basso_continuo" title="Figured bass">basso continuo</a>. Recitatives marked "Accompagnato" (Acc.) are accompanied by additional string instruments. Handel uses four voice parts in both solo and chorus, <a href="/wiki/Soprano" title="Soprano">soprano</a> (S), <a href="/wiki/Alto" title="Alto">alto</a> (A), <a href="/wiki/Tenor" title="Tenor">tenor</a> (T) and <a href="/wiki/Bass_(voice_type)" title="Bass (voice type)">bass</a> (B). Only once is the chorus divided in an upper chorus and a lower chorus, it is <a href="/wiki/SATB" title="SATB">SATB</a> otherwise. The orchestra scoring is simple: <a href="/wiki/Oboe" title="Oboe">oboes</a>, <a href="/wiki/String_instrument" title="String instrument">strings</a> and basso continuo of <a href="/wiki/Harpsichord" title="Harpsichord">harpsichord</a>, <a href="/wiki/Violoncello" class="mw-redirect" title="Violoncello">violoncello</a>, <a href="/wiki/Violone" title="Violone">violone</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bassoon" title="Bassoon">bassoon</a>. Two trumpets and <a href="/wiki/Timpani" title="Timpani">timpani</a> highlight selected movements, such as the closing movements of Part II, <i>Hallelujah</i>. Handel uses a <a href="/wiki/Cantus_firmus" title="Cantus firmus">cantus firmus</a> on long repeated notes especially to illustrate God's speech and majesty, such as "King of Kings" in the <i>Hallelujah chorus</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Burrows_1991_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Burrows_1991-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="General_notes">General notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messiah_Part_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: General notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The following table is organized by movement numbers. There are two major systems of numbering the movements of <i>Messiah</i>: the historic <a href="/wiki/Novello_%26_Co" class="mw-redirect" title="Novello &amp; Co">Novello</a> edition of 1959 (which is based on earlier editions and contains 53 movements), and the <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A4renreiter" title="Bärenreiter">Bärenreiter</a> edition of 1965 in the <a href="/wiki/Hallische_H%C3%A4ndel-Ausgabe" title="Hallische Händel-Ausgabe">Hallische Händel-Ausgabe</a>. Not counting some short recitatives as separate movements, there are therefore 47 movements. In the table below, the Novello number (Nov) is given first and is the index for the notes to individual movements in the "movements" section, then the Bärenreiter number (Bär).
</p><p>To emphasise the movements in which the oboes (ob) and the rarely used trumpets (tr) and timpani (ti) play, the summary below does not mention the regular basso continuo and the strings in movements. Details on the development of <a href="/wiki/Key_signature" title="Key signature">keys</a>, different <a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">tempo</a> markings  <a href="/wiki/Time_signature" title="Time signature">times</a> within a movement are given in notes on the individual movements. Movements originally in Italian (It) are indicated in the Source column, however the exact origin is supplied in the notes on the movement.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Part_II_movements">Part II movements</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messiah_Part_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Part II movements">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scene_1">Scene 1</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messiah_Part_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Scene 1">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Structure_of_Handel%27s_Messiah#Scenes" title="Structure of Handel&#39;s Messiah">Scene 1</a> is the longest scene of the oratorio and reflects the Passion, in Jennens' words "Christ's Passion; the scourging and the agony on the cross", in nine individual movements, including the longest one, the Air for alto "<a href="#23">He was despised</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Heighes_1997_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heighes_1997-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Part II is the only part opened by a chorus, and continues to be dominated by choral singing. Block observes that the emphasis on the Passion differs from modern western popular Christianity, which prefers to stress the nativity of the Messiah.<sup id="cite_ref-Block_2001_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Block_2001-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="22">22</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messiah_Part_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 22">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p><b>Behold the Lamb of God</b>
</p><p>The opening chorus "Behold the Lamb of God" begins like a <a href="/wiki/French_overture" title="French overture">French overture</a> in <a href="/wiki/G_minor" title="G minor">G minor</a>, a key of "tragic presentiment", according to <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Hogwood" title="Christopher Hogwood">Christopher Hogwood</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Hogwood_1991_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hogwood_1991-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The continuo drops an <a href="/wiki/Octave" title="Octave">octave</a>, then the violins rise an octave, to express "Behold". After only three instrumental measures the voices proclaim the Testimony of <a href="/wiki/John_the_Baptist" title="John the Baptist">John the Baptist</a>, <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/John#1:29" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/John">John&#32;1&#58;29</a>, which recalls <a href="/wiki/Isaiah_53" title="Isaiah 53">Isaiah 53</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Block_2001_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Block_2001-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The alto begins, followed after half a measure each by the soprano, the bass, and finally the tenor. After the initial rise, the melody falls in dotted rhythms, but rises on "that taketh away the sin of the world". The melody shows similarity to the beginning of "<a href="/wiki/Messiah_Part_I#20" title="Messiah Part I">He shall feed his flock</a>", but "sharpened" from <a href="/wiki/Major_scale" title="Major scale">major</a> to <a href="/wiki/Minor_scale" title="Minor scale">minor</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Triplet_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Triplet (music)">triplets</a> to dotted rhythm, and by the octave leap in the beginning.
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="23">23</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messiah_Part_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 23">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer_-_Man_of_Sorrows_with_Hands_Bound_-_WGA7346.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer_-_Man_of_Sorrows_with_Hands_Bound_-_WGA7346.jpg/170px-Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer_-_Man_of_Sorrows_with_Hands_Bound_-_WGA7346.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="264" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer_-_Man_of_Sorrows_with_Hands_Bound_-_WGA7346.jpg/255px-Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer_-_Man_of_Sorrows_with_Hands_Bound_-_WGA7346.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer_-_Man_of_Sorrows_with_Hands_Bound_-_WGA7346.jpg/340px-Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer_-_Man_of_Sorrows_with_Hands_Bound_-_WGA7346.jpg 2x" data-file-width="550" data-file-height="853" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer_-_Man_of_Sorrows_with_Hands_Bound_-_WGA7346.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><i><a href="/wiki/Man_of_sorrows" class="mw-redirect" title="Man of sorrows">Schmerzensmann</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Albrecht_D%C3%BCrer" title="Albrecht Dürer">Albrecht Dürer</a></div></div></div>
<p><b>He was despised</b>
</p><p>Isaiah wrote in his <a href="/wiki/Servant_songs" title="Servant songs">Songs of the suffering servant</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Servant_songs#The_fourth_song" title="Servant songs">fourth song</a> about the <a href="/wiki/Man_of_Sorrows" title="Man of Sorrows">Man of Sorrows</a>: "He was despised, rejected of men, a man of sorrows, and acquainted with grief" (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Isaiah#53:3" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Isaiah">Isaiah&#32;53&#58;3</a>). Isaiah states in his songs that "the Messiah will play a substitutionary sacrificial role on behalf of his people".<sup id="cite_ref-Block_2001_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Block_2001-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Handel gives the pitiful description to the alto solo in the longest movement of the oratorio in terms of duration.<sup id="cite_ref-Heighes_1997_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Heighes_1997-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> It is a <a href="/wiki/Da_capo_aria" title="Da capo aria">da capo aria</a>, showing two contrasting moods, set in <a href="/wiki/E-flat_major" title="E-flat major">E-flat major</a> in the first section, <a href="/wiki/C_minor" title="C minor">C minor</a> in the middle section. The vocal line begins with an ascending <a href="/wiki/Perfect_fourth" title="Perfect fourth">fourth</a> on "he was" and adds another one on "despi-sed", ending as a sigh. The signal of a fourth has been observed by musicologist Rudolf Steglich as a unifying motif of the oratorio.<sup id="cite_ref-Luckett_1992_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Luckett_1992-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Handel breaks the beginning of the text up to a stammering "He was despised, – despised and rejected, – rejected of men, ... – despi-sed – rejected", the words interspersed with rests as long as the words, as if exhausted. Soft sighing <a href="/wiki/Motif_(music)" title="Motif (music)">motifs</a> of the violins, an echo of the singing, drop into these rests. Hogwood interprets the unaccompanied passages as emphasizing "Christ's abandonment".<sup id="cite_ref-Hogwood_1991_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hogwood_1991-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> The middle section is also full of dramatic rests, but now the voice is set on a ceaseless agitated pattern of fast dotted notes in the instruments, illustrating the hits of the smiters in text from the <a href="/wiki/Servant_songs#The_third_song" title="Servant songs">third song</a> (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Isaiah#50:6" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Isaiah">Isaiah&#32;50&#58;6</a>), where the words appear in the first person: "He gave his back – to the smiters – ... and His cheeks – to them – that plucked off the hair. – He hid – not his face – from shame – and spitting."
</p>

<p><b>Surely, He hath borne our griefs</b>
</p><p>The dotted rhythm returns in instruments and voices in the chorus "Surely, He hath borne our griefs and carried our sorrows", the continuation of Isaiah's text, set in <a href="/wiki/F_minor" title="F minor">F minor</a>. The chorus continues with the remainder of Isaiah 53:5 and ends on the words "the chastisement of our peace was upon him".
</p>

<p><b>And with His stripes we are healed</b>
</p><p>In the same key the chorus continues with a <a href="/wiki/Fugue" title="Fugue">fugue</a> "And with His stripes we are healed". The theme begins with a sequence of five long notes, which Mozart quoted in the <a href="/wiki/Kyrie" title="Kyrie">Kyrie</a>-fugue of his <i><a href="/wiki/Requiem_(Mozart)" title="Requiem (Mozart)">Requiem</a></i>. The characteristic ascending fourth opens the countersubject. The word "healed" is later stressed by both long <a href="/wiki/Melisma" title="Melisma">melismas</a> and long notes.
</p>

<p><b>All we like sheep</b>
</p><p>Still continuing Isaiah's text, "All we like sheep, have gone astray" is set as a fast chorus in F-major on a walking bass with irregular patterns and leaps. The voices utter twice together "All we like sheep", then two voice parts move simultaneously in different directions on "have gone astray", with the last syllable extended to eleven notes. The next bit of the text "we have turned" is illustrated by fast coloraturas, lacking direction. In a dramatic sudden adagio, full of <a href="/wiki/Chromatic" class="mw-redirect" title="Chromatic">chromatic</a> tension, the movement ends on "and the Lord hath laid on Him the iniquity of us all". Myers comments about the chorus, which seems out of place at first sight: "In Handel’s famous chorus sin glories in its shame with almost alcoholic exhilaration. His lost sheep meander hopelessly through a wealth of intricate semi quavers, stumbling over decorous roulades and falling into mazes of counterpoint that prove inextricable. A less dramatic composer than Handel would scarcely have rendered his solemn English text with such defiance, for the discrepancy between the self-accusing words and his vivacious music is patent to any listener emancipated from the lethargy of custom."<sup id="cite_ref-Manson_Myers_1948_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Manson_Myers_1948-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The movement is based on the final section of the duet for two sopranos "Nò, di voi non vo' fidarmi" (HWV 189, July 1741).<sup id="cite_ref-compositionslist_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-compositionslist-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="27">27</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messiah_Part_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: 27">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p><b>All they that see Him, laugh Him to scorn</b>
</p><p>The thought "All they that see Him, laugh Him to scorn" is taken from <a href="/wiki/Psalm_22" title="Psalm 22">Psalm 22</a> (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Psalms#22:7" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Psalms">Psalms&#32;22&#58;7</a>), the psalm from which Jesus quoted on the cross, according to <a href="/wiki/Mark_the_Evangelist" title="Mark the Evangelist">Mark</a> and <a href="/wiki/Matthew_the_Evangelist" class="mw-redirect" title="Matthew the Evangelist">Matthew</a>. The text is set as a short tenor accompagnato, again based on a pattern of dotted notes in the instruments. The strings through in violent figures after "laugh Him to scorn" and "shoot out their lips", similar to an outburst of laughter. The key of <a href="/wiki/B-flat_minor" title="B-flat minor">B-flat minor</a> is termed "remote and barbarous" by Hogwood.<sup id="cite_ref-Hogwood_1991_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hogwood_1991-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p><b>He trusted in God, that He would deliver Him</b>
</p><p>What they say is given to the chorus as a strict fugue in <a href="/wiki/C_minor" title="C minor">C minor</a>: "He trusted in God, that He would deliver Him, if He delight in Him." <a href="/wiki/Jonathan_Keates" title="Jonathan Keates">Jonathan Keates</a> observes that Handel depicts the mocking, menacing crowd here, comparable to the <a href="/wiki/Turba" title="Turba">turbae</a> in <a href="/wiki/Passions_(Bach)" title="Passions (Bach)">Bach's Passions</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Keates_2007_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keates_2007-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p><b>Thy rebuke hath broken His heart</b>
</p><p>The tenor returns to sing a verse of the Psalm 69: "Thy rebuke hath broken His heart" (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Psalms#69:20" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Psalms">Psalms&#32;69&#58;20</a>). Aching chromatic chords picture the broken heart. The accompagnato begins in <a href="/wiki/A-flat_major" title="A-flat major">A-flat major</a>, shifts without stability and ends in <a href="/wiki/B_major" title="B major">B major</a>. The tenor voice, going to report death and resurrection in scene 2, is comparable to the <a href="/wiki/Evangelist_(Bach)" title="Evangelist (Bach)">Evangelist</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Passions_(Bach)" title="Passions (Bach)">Passions</a> of Bach.<sup id="cite_ref-Keates_2007_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keates_2007-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p><b>Behold, and see if there be any sorrow</b>
</p><p>The tenor <a href="/wiki/Arioso" title="Arioso">arioso</a> "Behold, and see if there be any sorrow" (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Lamentations#1:12" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Lamentations">Lamentations&#32;1&#58;12</a>) is based on text from the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Lamentations" title="Book of Lamentations">Book of Lamentations</a> which is frequently associated with <a href="/wiki/Good_Friday" title="Good Friday">Good Friday</a>, both Jesus and his mother <a href="/wiki/Mary_(mother_of_Jesus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mary (mother of Jesus)">Mary</a>, although it originally lamented the <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Jerusalem_(587_BC)" title="Siege of Jerusalem (587 BC)">destruction of Jerusalem</a>. In the short movement in <a href="/wiki/E_minor" title="E minor">E minor</a>, the accompaniment pauses rather regularly on the first and third beat of a measure.
</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Structure_of_Handel%27s_Messiah#Scenes" title="Structure of Handel&#39;s Messiah">Scene 2</a> covers death and resurrection in two tenor solo movements.
</p>

<p><b>He was cut off out of the land of the living</b>
</p><p>In a restrained way, the death of the Messiah is told in another tenor accompagnato, as foretold by Isaiah, "He was cut off out of the land of the living" (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Isaiah#53:8" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Isaiah">Isaiah&#32;53&#58;8</a>). Long chords begin in <a href="/wiki/B_minor" title="B minor">B minor</a> and end in <a href="/wiki/E_major" title="E major">E major</a>.
</p>

<p><b>Lift up your heads</b>
</p><p>"Lift up your heads" is a line from <a href="/wiki/Psalm_24" title="Psalm 24">Psalm 24</a> (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Psalms#24:7" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Psalms">Psalms&#32;24&#58;7–10</a>). Since the text has questions ("Who is the King of Glory?") and answers ("He is the King of Glory"), Handel divides the choir in the first section to a high, announcing group (sopranos I and II, alto) and a low, questioning group (alto, tenor, bass).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scene_4">Scene 4</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messiah_Part_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Scene 4">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Structure_of_Handel%27s_Messiah#Scenes" title="Structure of Handel&#39;s Messiah">Scene 4</a> covers the Messiah's position in heaven, following the teaching from the <a href="/wiki/Epistle_to_the_Hebrews" title="Epistle to the Hebrews">Epistle to the Hebrews</a> in two verses, <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Hebrews#1:5" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Hebrews">Hebrews&#32;1&#58;5–6</a>.
</p>

<p><b>Unto which of the angels said he at any time</b>
</p><p>In a short recitative the tenor renders the first verse, quoting <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Hebrews" class="mw-redirect" title="Book of Hebrews">Hebrews</a> (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Hebrews#1:5" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Hebrews">Hebrews&#32;1&#58;5</a>), "Unto which of the angels said he at any time", about the Messiah as the begotten <a href="/wiki/Son_of_God" title="Son of God">Son of God</a>.
</p>

<p><b>Let all the angels of God worship Him</b>
</p><p>The second verse "Let all the angels of God worship Him" (Hebrews 1:6) is a festive chorus in <a href="/wiki/D_major" title="D major">D major</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scene_5">Scene 5</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messiah_Part_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Scene 5">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Structure_of_Handel%27s_Messiah#Scenes" title="Structure of Handel&#39;s Messiah">Scene 5</a> alludes to <a href="/wiki/Pentecost" title="Pentecost">Pentecost</a> and the beginning of preaching the Gospel.
</p>

<p><b>Thou art gone up on high</b>
</p><p>Pentecost is referred to rather indirectly, without naming the Holy Spirit. "Thou art gone up on high" from <a href="/wiki/Psalm_68" title="Psalm 68">Psalm 68</a> (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Psalms#68:18" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Psalms">Psalms&#32;68&#58;18</a>) reflects "gifts for men" and "that God might dwell among them", expressed in swinging 3/4 time. Originally written for bass, Handel rewrote the Air in London in 1750 for the castrato <a href="/wiki/Gaetano_Guadagni" title="Gaetano Guadagni">Gaetano Guadagni</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Keates_2007_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Keates_2007-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> However, the earlier editions (Novello, Best and Prout) all give this air to the Bass, in D minor; the current Novello edition by Watkins Shaw, as well as the Bäreneiter edition by John Tobin and the CF Peters edition by Donald Burrows all give the air to Alto (in D minor), and they provide transpositions for Soprano and Bass as well.
</p>

<p><b>The Lord gave the word</b>
</p><p>The thoughts are continued in an earlier verse from the same psalm (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Psalms#68:11" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Psalms">Psalms&#32;68&#58;11</a>) as a chorus in <a href="/wiki/B-flat_major" title="B-flat major">B-flat major</a>. "The Lord gave the word" is sung by just two voice parts, "Great was the company of the preachers" expanded for four parts with long coloraturas on "company".
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="38">38</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messiah_Part_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: 38">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>

<p><b>How beautiful are the feet of Him</b>
</p><p>The preachers are described tenderly in a duet in D minor and 3/4 time, as written first by Isaiah (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Isaiah#52:7" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Isaiah">Isaiah&#32;52&#58;7</a>) and quoted by Paul in his <a href="/wiki/Epistle_to_the_Romans" title="Epistle to the Romans">Epistle to the Romans</a> (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Romans#10:15" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Romans">Romans&#32;10&#58;15</a>: "How beautiful are the feet of Him". Two alto voices begin and are joined by the choir, stressing "good tidings", "break forth into joy" and culminating on a <a href="/wiki/Cantus_firmus" title="Cantus firmus">cantus firmus</a> of one repeated note: "Thy God reigneth!" Block, quoting <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Genesis#18:2" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Genesis">Genesis&#32;18&#58;2</a>, reflects that you see the feet of a messenger if you "fall prostrate before a superior. In the Bible, when people are confronted by a heavenly messenger (angel) the natural response is to fall down on one’s face before the messenger."<sup id="cite_ref-Block_2001_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Block_2001-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Handel's original version, a duet in D minor for two altos and chorus or soprano, alto and chorus, was later rewritten by him in 1749 as an aria for soprano in G minor and 12/8 time and in 1750 transposed for alto in C minor.
</p>

<p><b>Their sound is gone out into all lands</b>
</p><p>Based on a number of Bible references, a tenor arioso describes the preachers further: "Their sound is gone out into all lands" (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Romans#10:18" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Romans">Romans&#32;10&#58;18</a>, <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Psalms#19:4" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Psalms">Psalms&#32;19&#58;4</a>). In another Handel's version (so called version B), which is commonly preferred by performers now, the same text is set to new music and scored for chorus.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Scene_6">Scene 6</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Messiah_Part_II&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Scene 6">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Structure_of_Handel%27s_Messiah#Scenes" title="Structure of Handel&#39;s Messiah">Scene 6</a> shows the difficulties and rejection of the preaching, based on four consecutive verses from <a href="/wiki/Psalm_2" title="Psalm 2">Psalm 2</a>, <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Psalms#2:1" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Psalms">Psalms&#32;2&#58;1–4</a>. It is the first text in the oratorio actually referring to the Messiah, the "anointed one" (verse 2).<sup id="cite_ref-Block_2001_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Block_2001-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p><b>Why do the nations so furiously rage together</b>
</p><p>An Air for bass in <a href="/wiki/C_major" title="C major">C major</a>, accompanied by an orchestra in continuous motion, tells of the difficulties. "Why do the nations so furiously rage together". The term "rage" is expressed by a long melisma in <a href="/wiki/Triplet_(music)" class="mw-redirect" title="Triplet (music)">triplets</a>.
</p>

<p><b>Hallelujah</b>
</p><p>Part II closes with the chorus <i><a href="/wiki/Hallelujah" title="Hallelujah">Hallelujah</a></i>, in the key of <a href="/wiki/D_major" title="D major">D major</a> with trumpets and timpani. The choir introduces in <a href="/wiki/Homophony" title="Homophony">homophony</a> a characteristic simple <a href="/wiki/Motif_(music)" title="Motif (music)">motif</a> on the word, playing with the interval of a <a href="/wiki/Interval_number" class="mw-redirect" title="Interval number">second</a>, which re-appears throughout the piece. Several lines from the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">Book of Revelation</a> (<a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Revelation#19:6" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Revelation">Revelation&#32;19&#58;6,16</a>, <a href="https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Bible_(King_James)/Revelation#11:15" class="extiw" title="s:Bible (King James)/Revelation">Revelation&#32;11&#58;15</a>) are treated differently, as in a <a href="/wiki/Motet" title="Motet">motet</a>, but unified by "Hallelujah" as a conclusion or as a countersubject in a fugal section. The line "for the Lord God omnipotent reigneth" is sung by all voices, first in <a href="/wiki/Unison" title="Unison">unison</a>, then in imitation with Hallelujah-exclamations interspersed. The second line "The kingdom of this world is become" is sung in a four-part setting like a <a href="/wiki/Chorale" title="Chorale">chorale</a>. The third idea "and he shall reign for ever and ever" starts as a fugue on a theme with bold leaps, reminiscent in sequence of <a href="/wiki/Philipp_Nicolai" title="Philipp Nicolai">Philipp Nicolai</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Lutheran_chorale" title="Lutheran chorale">Lutheran chorale</a> "<a href="/wiki/Wachet_auf,_ruft_uns_die_Stimme" title="Wachet auf, ruft uns die Stimme">Wachet auf</a>".<sup id="cite_ref-Hogwood_1991_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hogwood_1991-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> As a countersubject, the words "for ever – and ever" assume the rhythm of the Hallelujah-motif. The final acclamation "King of Kings...and Lord of Lords" is sung on one note, energized by repeated calls "Hallelujah" and "for ever – and ever", raised higher and higher (the sopranos and the trumpets part), up to a rest full of tension and a final solemn "Hallelujah".
</p>
