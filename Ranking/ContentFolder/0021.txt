<p>The <b>University of Huddersfield</b> (informally <b>Huddersfield University</b>) is a public <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">university</a> located in <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/West_Yorkshire" title="West Yorkshire">West Yorkshire</a>, England. It has been a university since 1992, but has its origins in a series of institutions dating back to the 19th century. It has made teaching quality a particular focus of its activities, winning the inaugural Higher Education Academy Global Teaching Excellence Award,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and achieving a <a href="/wiki/Teaching_Excellence_Framework" title="Teaching Excellence Framework">Teaching Excellence Framework</a> (TEF) Gold Award,<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> both in 2017.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The present University of Huddersfield can trace its history back through several predecessor institutions.
</p>
<h3><span id="An_early_failure_.281825.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="An_early_failure_(1825)">An early failure (1825)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: An early failure (1825)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1825 there was an attempt to set up a Scientific and Mechanics Institution in the town. Supported by a group of donors, its patron was leading Whig and large local landowner <a href="/wiki/John_William_Ramsden" title="John William Ramsden">Sir John Ramsden</a>. Its aims were to instruct local mechanics and tradesmen in scientific principles relating to their work, through lectures and a circulation library, which by 1827 contained over 700 volumes.  The financial crisis of 1825–1826 led to the failure of the institution’s bankers, and it faltered and later became part of the Huddersfield Philosophical Society, an organisation with which its rules now more closely aligned.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  Some 19th century students earned qualifications as external students of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_London" title="University of London">University of London</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Young_Men.27s_Mental_Improvement_Society_.281841.E2.80.931843.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Young_Men's_Mental_Improvement_Society_(1841–1843)">Young Men's Mental Improvement Society (1841–1843)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Young Men&#039;s Mental Improvement Society (1841–1843)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The history of the University is usually traced to 1841. It was in that year that five young men who were employed by local industrialist Frederic Schwann,<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> who had been born in Frankfurt, approached their employer for support in establishing a new subscription library and some elementary educational classes, ‘to supply in some cases the deficiency of early instruction, and to procure for others the means of further improvement’.<sup id="cite_ref-O&#39;Connell_2016_4_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-O&#39;Connell_2016_4-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>  They first met in the Temperance Hotel, Cross Church Street, Huddersfield in May 1841. Classes began for the first 40 or so pupils in the room of the British School at Outcote Bank, and were taught by experienced staff from the local Collegiate Schools and businessmen like Schwann.  A subscription library was founded, and classes were delivered in Reading, writing, arithmetic, grammar, geography, design and French.
</p>
<h3><span id="Huddersfield_Mechanics.27_Institution_.281844.E2.80.931883.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Huddersfield_Mechanics'_Institution_(1844–1883)">Huddersfield Mechanics' Institution (1844–1883)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Huddersfield Mechanics&#039; Institution (1844–1883)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The increase in student numbers prompted a move to Nelson's Buildings in New Street, and the renaming of the institution to more closely reflect its remit.  The first Secretary, Robert Neil, was appointed in 1844, and acted not just as a Secretary but as a teacher-supervisor, influencing the formative development of the organisation.  In March 1844 he organised an Soiree (tea-party) for 700 at the town's Philosophical Hall, and in May a Rural Gala for 500 at Fixby Pastures.  Negotiations with the local railway company led to reduced fares into York for 300 membership to enjoy the cultural opportunities of the city.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  In 1846, Neil was succeeded by George Searle Phillips, who was described by historian John O'Connell as 'philosopher, propagandist and missionary'<sup id="cite_ref-O&#39;Connell_2016_4_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-O&#39;Connell_2016_4-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> of the institution.  He oversaw expansion of the curriculum, revision of the fee system to make the institution self-supporting, the visitation of absent students and the compilation of statistics of the institution's achievements.
</p><p>In 1850, growing attendance meant another move, to Wellington Buildings, Queen Street.  At this time, a 'Preliminary Savings Bank' scheme was also introduced in the Institution, linked to the Huddersfield Banking Company, which took almost 7000 deposits<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> during its first year of operation.  Based on the Scottish system of encouraging working people to make small, regular saving deposits, it was a forerunner of the <a href="/wiki/Postal_savings_system" title="Postal savings system">Post Office Savings Bank</a>, founded in 1861.  In 1854, after Phillips resignation, Frank Curzon took over as Secretary and remained in post until 1883.  During his tenure a prize giving and distribution ceremony was introduced to reward attendance and good conduct.  He not only oversaw a recruitment drive, but also the move to the first purpose built home of the institution, on Northumberland Street.  The institution took possession in 1861, as student numbers passed 800 annually.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The continued prosperity of the institution during the 1860s has been attributed to two main developments – the demand for more technical and scientific education, and the introduction of an examination system by the newly formed national <a href="/wiki/Science_and_Art_Department" title="Science and Art Department">Department of Science and Art</a>.  The institution managed an examinations system and gave grants to science schools (often based in Mechanics' Institutions) on a 'payment by results' basis.  In 1857 the Society of Arts held its first examinations outside London at Huddersfield.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Technical_School_and_Mechanics.27_Institute_.281883.E2.80.931896.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_School_and_Mechanics'_Institute_(1883–1896)">Technical School and Mechanics' Institute (1883–1896)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Technical School and Mechanics&#039; Institute (1883–1896)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A merger took place with the town’s Female Educational Institute in 1883, and the institution become the Technical School and <a href="/wiki/Mechanics%27_Institutes" title="Mechanics&#39; Institutes">Mechanics' Institute</a>. A new building was constructed on what it today the ring road called Queensgate that cost £20,000,<sup id="cite_ref-hud.ac.uk_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hud.ac.uk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and was funded by the subscriptions of members and philanthropic supporters.  Called the Ramsden Building, after the Ramsden family who owned the land upon which the building stood, the building is still in use by the Health and Human Sciences Department of the University today.  Before the building opened to students in 1884, it was used for a five-month Fine Art and Industrial Exhibition.  These kinds of exhibitions, inspired by the Great Exhibition, showcased the latest in textile technology alongside copies of great masters paintings or more contemporary works.
</p>
<h3><span id="Technical_College_and_College_of_Technology_.281896.E2.80.931970.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical_College_and_College_of_Technology_(1896–1970)">Technical College and College of Technology (1896–1970)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Technical College and College of Technology (1896–1970)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1896, the Technical School and Mechanics' Institute became the Technical College, which changed its name in 1958 to become the College of Technology.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> was a time of growth for the College. A Coal Tar Chemistry Department was created, funded by the government and by donations which were also used to provide research scholarships in Chemistry. More than 100 chemists worked at the College as research staff as products were created for British Dyes Ltd.<sup id="cite_ref-hud.ac.uk_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hud.ac.uk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>During <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the College housed student teachers evacuated from Avery Hill College of Education, Eltham, London (now part of the University of Greenwich), and trained members of the armed forces training to be radio mechanics, engineers, fitters and clerks. Civilians also trained at the College in more basic workshop skills.<sup id="cite_ref-hud.ac.uk_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hud.ac.uk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One notable visitor to the college in 1964 was <a href="/wiki/Dusty_Springfield" title="Dusty Springfield">Dusty Springfield</a>, who appeared at 'Sound Sixty-Four', an event organised by the Huddersfield Tech students as part of Rag Week.<sup id="cite_ref-hud.ac.uk_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hud.ac.uk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Huddersfield_Polytechnic_.281970.E2.80.931992.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Huddersfield_Polytechnic_(1970–1992)">Huddersfield Polytechnic (1970–1992)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Huddersfield Polytechnic (1970–1992)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was the College of Technology which, in 1970, merged with the Oastler College of Education to become Huddersfield Polytechnic. The Oastler College had been founded in 1963, as a day training college for school teachers. Huddersfield Polytechnic was officially inaugurated by the then Education Secretary <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">Margaret Thatcher</a> on 23 April 1971.<sup id="cite_ref-hud.ac.uk_13-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hud.ac.uk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:David_Blunkett_MP_Where_Next_for_Welfare.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/David_Blunkett_MP_Where_Next_for_Welfare.jpg/220px-David_Blunkett_MP_Where_Next_for_Welfare.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/David_Blunkett_MP_Where_Next_for_Welfare.jpg/330px-David_Blunkett_MP_Where_Next_for_Welfare.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/David_Blunkett_MP_Where_Next_for_Welfare.jpg/440px-David_Blunkett_MP_Where_Next_for_Welfare.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3200" data-file-height="3200" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:David_Blunkett_MP_Where_Next_for_Welfare.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>David Blunkett studied at Holly Bank College</div></div></div>
<p>In 1974, a further merger took place with the Technical Teaching Training College at Holly Bank. That college had been noted in education circles for its policy of training students over the age of 25, one of whom was the future Education Secretary <a href="/wiki/David_Blunkett" title="David Blunkett">David Blunkett</a>. However, after the merger, it became part of the Polytechnic's Faculty of Education, and those idiosyncrasies were gradually eroded.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Teacher training remained on the Holly Bank site until the land was sold by the University in 2001.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The campus precinct was redeveloped by the firm Wilson and Womersley. Awarding <a href="/w/index.php?title=J._L._Womersley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="J. L. Womersley (page does not exist)">J. L. Womersley</a> a Fellowship for the design, the Deputy Rector commented on the courage and vision of the plan, with the Central Services building in particular being a monument to that vision.  The large brown Central Services Building, now known as the Schwann Building, a prominent feature not just of the campus but also the town of Huddersfield itself, was completed in January 1977. The brown colour was chosen so it would better fit in with the local sandstone. The building was officially opened by <a href="/wiki/Katharine,_Duchess_of_Kent" title="Katharine, Duchess of Kent">the Duchess of Kent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hud.ac.uk_13-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hud.ac.uk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Several high-profile bands played the University's students' union during the late 1970s and early 1980s, including <a href="/wiki/The_Stranglers" title="The Stranglers">The Stranglers</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Jam" title="The Jam">The Jam</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_Undertones" title="The Undertones">The Undertones</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-hud.ac.uk_13-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hud.ac.uk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Former Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Harold_Wilson" title="Harold Wilson">Harold Wilson</a> returned to his hometown in 1983, the same year he stood down as an MP, to open a University building on Firth Street and to deliver a lecture.<sup id="cite_ref-hud.ac.uk_13-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hud.ac.uk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> He would later have a different building named after him on the campus.
</p>
<h3><span id="University_of_Huddersfield_.28since_1992.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="University_of_Huddersfield_(since_1992)">University of Huddersfield (since 1992)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: University of Huddersfield (since 1992)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Polytechnic became the University of Huddersfield in 1992.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Professor Ken Durrands was Vice-Chancellor at the time the institution achieved University status. He was later credited with having "transformed" it into "a thriving university".<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> However, in 1994 he was forced to leave his post before the end of his contract, following a row over the removal of independently-elected representatives from the University's governing body.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sir William Taylor served as Vice-Chancellor for a year during 1994 and 1995. He had previously been the Vice-Chancellor of Hull University. He only intended to remain at Huddersfield for 12 months, on an interim basis.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The next Vice-Chancellor was John Tarrant, appointed in October 1995. He served in the role until 2006, and was later Secretary General of the Association of Commonwealth Universities until his retirement in 2010.<sup id="cite_ref-hud.ac.uk_13-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hud.ac.uk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
The present Vice-Chancellor, Professor Bob Cryan, took over the job in January 2007. At the time, he was the youngest Vice-Chancellor in the UK. His strategy for the University during this time has been to lead an "inspiring, innovative University of international renown".<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> In May 2007, the University welcomed <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Queen Elizabeth</a> for a visit to the campus, during which she unveiled the foundation stone for the new Creative Arts Building.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The following year, the building was officially opened by <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward,_Duke_of_Kent" title="Prince Edward, Duke of Kent">the Duke of Kent</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2013, the University of Huddersfield was named University of the Year at the Times Higher Education Awards. Accepting the award, Prof Cryan said: "“It is a tribute to all the staff who have worked so hard to get us where we are today. We are now well established as one of the country’s innovative universities – and the best is yet to come.”<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The archives of all the university's predecessor institutions are held in Heritage Quay, the University Archive Service based in the Schwann Building.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Heritage Quay opened in 2014 and is open to the general public, allowing easier access to both students and non-students to the many collections within University's archives. It received more than £1.5m in Lottery funding, and in 2016 won the Buildings That Inspire category in the Guardian's University Awards.<sup id="cite_ref-hud.ac.uk_13-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hud.ac.uk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The University now attracts students from more than 130 countries. With an annual turnover of approximately £150m each year, it estimates it is worth £300m annually to the local economy.<sup id="cite_ref-hud.ac.uk_13-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hud.ac.uk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Campuses">Campuses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Campuses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Queensgate_Campus">Queensgate Campus</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Queensgate Campus">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Queensgate_Campus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Queensgate_Campus.jpg/220px-Queensgate_Campus.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Queensgate_Campus.jpg/330px-Queensgate_Campus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Queensgate_Campus.jpg/440px-Queensgate_Campus.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="683" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Queensgate_Campus.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Queensgate Campus</div></div></div> <div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Queensgate_Campus_Top.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Queensgate_Campus_Top.jpg/220px-Queensgate_Campus_Top.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Queensgate_Campus_Top.jpg/330px-Queensgate_Campus_Top.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Queensgate_Campus_Top.jpg/440px-Queensgate_Campus_Top.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="683" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Queensgate_Campus_Top.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A view of Queensgate Campus</div></div></div><p> The University's main campus, Queensgate, is south-east of Huddersfield town centre. Virtually all of the University's teaching takes place on the site. The campus is split in two by the <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_Narrow_Canal" title="Huddersfield Narrow Canal">Huddersfield Narrow Canal</a>. It features a mixture of converted mill buildings, and purpose-built facilities. More than £250&#160;million has been invested by the University in the campus.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A notable feature of the University of Huddersfield in recent years has been the amount of building work taking place on the Queensgate campus. In addition to the Creative Arts Building which opened in 2008, a new £17&#160;million Business School opened in 2010, followed by the £3&#160;million Buckley Innovation Centre in 2012, the £22.5&#160;million Student Central building in 2014, and the £27.5&#160;million Oastler Building for Law and the School of Music, Humanities and Media, in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As part of the University's 175th anniversary commemorations in 2016, several of the buildings on the Queensgate campus were renamed after significant figures with connections to the Huddersfield area and the University's predecessor institutions. The distinctive brown Central Services Building was renamed the Schwann Building, after Frederic and Mary Schwann who had been inspired to create the Young Men's Mental Improvement Society in 1841. Other buildings were named for <a href="/wiki/Edith_Key" title="Edith Key">Edith Key</a>, <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Priestley" title="Joseph Priestley">Joseph Priestley</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Bront%C3%AB_family" title="Brontë family">Brontë sisters</a> and the University's own Chancellor Emeritus, <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Stewart" title="Patrick Stewart">Sir Patrick Stewart.</a>
</p><p>In a second round of renaming in 2017, other campus buildings were named in honour of <a href="/wiki/Karen_Sp%C3%A4rck_Jones" title="Karen Spärck Jones">Karen Sparck Jones</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caroline_Haslett" title="Caroline Haslett">Dame Caroline Haslett</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Cockcroft" title="John Cockcroft">Sir John Cockcroft</a>, and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Charles_Sikes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Charles Sikes (page does not exist)">Charles Sikes</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The University's latest major building project, the <a href="/wiki/Barbara_Hepworth" title="Barbara Hepworth">Barbara Hepworth</a> Building, will be home to the School of Art, Design and Architecture. It is due for completion in summer 2019.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="University_Campus_Oldham">University Campus Oldham</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: University Campus Oldham">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>University Campus Oldham (UCO), located in <a href="/wiki/Oldham" title="Oldham">Oldham</a>, <a href="/wiki/Greater_Manchester" title="Greater Manchester">Greater Manchester</a>, opened in May 2005. It offers full and part-time courses at degree, diploma, foundation degree and postgraduate level. Since August 2012 University Campus Oldham (UCO) has been managed by <a href="/wiki/Oldham_College" title="Oldham College">Oldham College</a>. In 2017-2018, UCO Teacher Education programmes continue to be validated by the university, and lead to a University of Huddersfield award.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="University_Campus_Barnsley">University Campus Barnsley</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: University Campus Barnsley">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>University Campus Barnsley, located in <a href="/wiki/Barnsley" title="Barnsley">Barnsley</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Yorkshire" title="South Yorkshire">South Yorkshire</a>, opened in 2005. The campus offers facilities for subjects such as music, art and design, journalism and media production. £5.5&#160;million has been invested with the help of the Higher Education Funding Council for England, <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire_Forward" title="Yorkshire Forward">Yorkshire Forward</a> and Objective 1.
</p><p>From August 2013 University Campus Barnsley (UCB) has been managed by <a href="/wiki/Barnsley_College" title="Barnsley College">Barnsley College</a>. UCB programmes starting in September 2013 continue to be validated by the university, and lead to a University of Huddersfield award.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_Study_Centre">International Study Centre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: International Study Centre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The International Study Centre (ISC) at the university offers degree preparation courses for international students. The programmes are run by <a href="/wiki/Study_Group_International" title="Study Group International">Study Group</a>.  The ISC is located on the university’s main Queensgate campus. Students can mix with others and also have access to the library and other specialist IT facilities.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organisation_and_governance">Organisation and governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Organisation and governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chancellor">Chancellor</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Chancellor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Prince_Andrew,_Duke_of_York" title="Prince Andrew, Duke of York">The Duke of York</a> replaced actor <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Stewart" title="Patrick Stewart">Sir Patrick Stewart</a> to become the University’s fourth Chancellor in July 2015.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> The Duke had previously served as the Patron of the University since 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The University's first two Chancellors were Reginald Cross, who held the role between 1992 and 1994, and <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Hall_(businessman)" title="Ernest Hall (businessman)">Sir Ernest Hall</a>, who served between 1996 and 2004.<sup id="cite_ref-hud.ac.uk_13-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hud.ac.uk-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_Patrick_Stewart_Huddersfield_2017.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Sir_Patrick_Stewart_Huddersfield_2017.jpg/220px-Sir_Patrick_Stewart_Huddersfield_2017.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="148" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Sir_Patrick_Stewart_Huddersfield_2017.jpg/330px-Sir_Patrick_Stewart_Huddersfield_2017.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6f/Sir_Patrick_Stewart_Huddersfield_2017.jpg/440px-Sir_Patrick_Stewart_Huddersfield_2017.jpg 2x" data-file-width="740" data-file-height="499" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Sir_Patrick_Stewart_Huddersfield_2017.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Sir Patrick Stewart meeting students on the University campus in April 2017</div></div></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Patrick_Stewart" title="Patrick Stewart">Sir Patrick Stewart</a> served as Chancellor for 11 years, having first been appointed in 2004. He has been a regular visitor to the campus, sometimes leading workshops with drama students, in addition to being an overseas ambassador for the University.<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One feature of Sir Patrick's tenure as Chancellor was his attendance at summer graduation ceremonies, and his leading of parades of graduates through the town.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> The first such parade took place in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> He has continued his association with the University as Emeritus Chancellor.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2016, the building used by the University's Drama department, previously known as the Milton building, was renamed the Sir Patrick Stewart building. He returned to the campus to reopen it.<sup id="cite_ref-auto1_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto1-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Senior_management">Senior management</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Senior management">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The University is run by the Vice-Chancellor, Professor Bob Cryan. Underneath him is Deputy Vice-Chancellor, Professor Tim Thornton, and three Pro Vice-Chancellors. Each of the seven academic schools is led by a Dean, with an eighth Dean who oversees the Graduate School. Other University services are run by Directors.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Research">Research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Research">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:New_Business_School.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/New_Business_School.jpg/220px-New_Business_School.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/New_Business_School.jpg/330px-New_Business_School.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/New_Business_School.jpg/440px-New_Business_School.jpg 2x" data-file-width="640" data-file-height="427" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:New_Business_School.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>New Business School</div></div></div><p>The University has world-leading applied research groups in biomedical sciences, engineering and physical sciences, social sciences and arts and humanities. It has quadrupled its research income and its number of postgraduate researchers in recent years.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 2014 Research Excellence Framework (REF) exercise, the University of Huddersfield achieved its best ever result. It rose to 68th in the UK Research Power league table, having been placed 97th in the 2008 REF.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>More University of Huddersfield academics, 262, were entered than in any previous cycle, and the proportion of world leading research submitted increased to 15%.<sup id="cite_ref-auto_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-auto-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The University also host the <a href="/wiki/MIAMI_Facilities_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="MIAMI Facilities (UK)">MIAMI facilities</a>, a world-class laboratory addressed to materials sciences investigations and energy studies.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_connections">Royal connections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Royal connections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Creative_Arts_Building,_University_of_Huddersfield_-_geograph.org.uk_-_834099.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Creative_Arts_Building%2C_University_of_Huddersfield_-_geograph.org.uk_-_834099.jpg/220px-Creative_Arts_Building%2C_University_of_Huddersfield_-_geograph.org.uk_-_834099.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Creative_Arts_Building%2C_University_of_Huddersfield_-_geograph.org.uk_-_834099.jpg/330px-Creative_Arts_Building%2C_University_of_Huddersfield_-_geograph.org.uk_-_834099.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Creative_Arts_Building%2C_University_of_Huddersfield_-_geograph.org.uk_-_834099.jpg/440px-Creative_Arts_Building%2C_University_of_Huddersfield_-_geograph.org.uk_-_834099.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1024" data-file-height="683" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Creative_Arts_Building,_University_of_Huddersfield_-_geograph.org.uk_-_834099.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The Creative Arts Building.</div></div></div><p>The University has developed a significant association with the Royal family in recent years, in particular with the appointment of <a href="/wiki/Prince_Andrew,_Duke_of_York" title="Prince Andrew, Duke of York">The Duke of York</a> as Patron in 2013 and later Chancellor in 2015. The University's  centre for young entrepreneurs is named for the Duke, and was opened by him in 2013. It is situated on the first floor of the Buckley Innovation Centre, across Firth Street from the main campus.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The Duke's former wife, <a href="/wiki/Sarah,_Duchess_of_York" title="Sarah, Duchess of York">Sarah, Duchess of York</a>, was in 2016 given the title of Visiting Professor of <a href="/wiki/Philanthropreneur" title="Philanthropreneur">Philanthropreneurship</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Their elder daughter, <a href="/wiki/Princess_Beatrice_of_York" title="Princess Beatrice of York">Princess Beatrice</a>, has also visited the University to meet students and view their work.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_II" title="Elizabeth II">Queen</a> visited the campus in 2007 to lay the foundation stone of the Creative Arts Building. Other royals to have visited the University in the years since include the <a href="/wiki/Anne,_Princess_Royal" title="Anne, Princess Royal">Princess Royal</a>, who in 2015 opened the new Student Central building, the <a href="/wiki/Prince_Edward,_Duke_of_Kent" title="Prince Edward, Duke of Kent">Duke of Kent</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Prince_Richard,_Duke_of_Gloucester" title="Prince Richard, Duke of Gloucester">Duke of Gloucester</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Charles,_Prince_of_Wales" title="Charles, Prince of Wales">Prince of Wales</a> visited University Campus Barnsley in 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Student_life">Student life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Student life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accommodation">Accommodation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Accommodation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Storthes_Hall" title="Storthes Hall">Storthes Hall</a> Park Student Village and Ashenhurst Student Houses are the university affiliated accommodation. These residences are privately owned and operated by Digs Student, independently of the university.
</p><p>Storthes Hall is situated in a parkland to the south of the town with 1,386 en-suite single, double or disabled bedrooms in shared flats of six to eight students.
</p><p>Ashenhurst Houses are located in woodlands close to the campus and Storthes Hall Park. There are 280 single bedrooms in shared houses of six to eight students, all with shared facilities including a communal lounge and kitchen/dining area.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span id="Students.27_Union"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Students'_Union">Students' Union</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=University_of_Huddersfield&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Students&#039; Union">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Students' Union represents, supports and advises students on all aspects of their time at university.
</p><p>The union moved into its new purpose-built Student Central building in January 2014. The building cost £22.5&#160;million and includes an 800-capacity Sports Hall, catering outlets, a shop, offices for the student radio station Radio Hud and magazine T'Hud, and a range of support services including welfare and the careers centre.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>There was some controversy when the new building opened as it does not include a bar. Reports suggested the bar in the previous union building had been losing as much as £100,000 a year, with students instead choosing to drink at home or in other nearby pubs and clubs.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The University has been successful in winning a series of awards in recent years.
</p><p>It was ranked 701+ in the 2015–16 QS World University Rankings.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> The University was the Entrepreneurial University of the Year at the <i>THE awards</i> 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> It won the Outstanding Registry Team 2013 at the <i>THE Leadership and Management</i> awards 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> The University was University of the Year at the <i>THE awards</i> 2013.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup> In 2014 the University was named the Times Higher Education's (THE) Best University Workplace.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup> The University was the <i>New University of the Year at the Educate North Awards</i> 2015.
</p><p>In 2016, the University came top among all UK universities for having qualified teaching staff. More than 90% of its academic were found to have a teaching qualification, with the next-highest institution scoring 84%.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
