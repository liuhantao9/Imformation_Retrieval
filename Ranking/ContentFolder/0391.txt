<p>The <b>Ordnance Survey National Grid reference system</b> is a system of geographic grid references used in Great Britain, distinct from <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> and <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a>. It is often called <b>British National Grid</b> (<b>BNG</b>).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey" title="Ordnance Survey">Ordnance Survey</a> (OS) devised the national <a href="/wiki/Grid_reference" title="Grid reference">grid reference</a> system, and it is heavily used in their survey data, and in maps based on those surveys, whether published by the Ordnance Survey or by commercial map producers. Grid references are also commonly quoted in other publications and data sources, such as guide books and government planning documents.
</p><p>A number of different systems exist that can provide grid references for locations within the <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">British Isles</a>: this article describes the system created solely for Great Britain and its outlying islands (including the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man" title="Isle of Man">Isle of Man</a>); the <a href="/wiki/Irish_grid_reference_system" title="Irish grid reference system">Irish grid reference system</a> was a similar system created by the <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey_of_Ireland" class="mw-redirect" title="Ordnance Survey of Ireland">Ordnance Survey of Ireland</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey_of_Northern_Ireland" title="Ordnance Survey of Northern Ireland">Ordnance Survey of Northern Ireland</a> for the island of Ireland.  The <a href="/wiki/Universal_Transverse_Mercator_coordinate_system" title="Universal Transverse Mercator coordinate system">Universal Transverse Mercator coordinate system</a> (UTM) is used to provide grid references for worldwide locations, and this is the system commonly used for the <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">Channel Islands</a> and Ireland (<a href="/wiki/Irish_Transverse_Mercator" title="Irish Transverse Mercator">since 2001</a>). European-wide agencies also use UTM when mapping locations, or may use the <a href="/wiki/Military_Grid_Reference_System" title="Military Grid Reference System">Military Grid Reference System</a> (MGRS) system, or variants of it. OSGB uses Orthorectified images of many temporal resolution for one area.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General">General</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ordnance_Survey_National_Grid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Ordnance_Survey_1-250000_-_TF.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Ordnance_Survey_1-250000_-_TF.jpg/220px-Ordnance_Survey_1-250000_-_TF.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="220" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Ordnance_Survey_1-250000_-_TF.jpg/330px-Ordnance_Survey_1-250000_-_TF.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Ordnance_Survey_1-250000_-_TF.jpg/440px-Ordnance_Survey_1-250000_-_TF.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="4000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Ordnance_Survey_1-250000_-_TF.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Grid square TF. The map shows <a href="/wiki/The_Wash" title="The Wash">The Wash</a> and the <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">North Sea</a>, as well as places within the counties of <a href="/wiki/Lincolnshire" title="Lincolnshire">Lincolnshire</a>, Cambridgeshire and <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">Norfolk</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>The grid is based on the <i>OSGB36</i>  <a href="/wiki/Map_datum" class="mw-redirect" title="Map datum">datum</a> (Ordnance Survey Great Britain 1936,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> based on the <a href="/wiki/George_Biddell_Airy" title="George Biddell Airy">Airy</a> 1830 <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">ellipsoid</a>),<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and was introduced after the <a href="/wiki/Retriangulation_of_Great_Britain" title="Retriangulation of Great Britain">retriangulation of 1936–1962</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It replaced the previously used <a href="/wiki/Cassini_Grid" title="Cassini Grid">Cassini Grid</a> which,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> up to the end of <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, had been issued only to the military.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The Airy ellipsoid is a regional best fit for Britain; more modern mapping tends to use the GRS80 ellipsoid used by the <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">Global Positioning System</a> (the Airy ellipsoid assumes the Earth to be about 1&#160;km smaller in diameter than the <a href="/wiki/Geodetic_Reference_System_1980" title="Geodetic Reference System 1980">GRS80</a> ellipsoid, and to be slightly less flattened). The British maps adopt a <a href="/wiki/Transverse_Mercator_projection" title="Transverse Mercator projection">transverse Mercator projection</a> with an origin (the "true" origin) at <a href="/wiki/49th_parallel_north" title="49th parallel north">49°&#160;N</a>, <a href="/wiki/2nd_meridian_west" title="2nd meridian west">2°&#160;W</a> (an offshore point in the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> which lies between the island of <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">Jersey</a> and the French port of <a href="/wiki/Saint-Malo" title="Saint-Malo">St. Malo</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> Over the Airy ellipsoid a straight line grid, the National Grid, is placed with a new false origin to eliminate negative numbers, creating a 700&#160;km by 1300&#160;km grid. This false origin is located south-west of the Isles of Scilly.
</p><p>In order to minimize the overall scale error, a factor of 2499/2500 is applied. This creates two lines of longitude about 180&#160;km east and west of the central meridian along which the local scale factor equals 1, i.e. map scale is correct. Inside these lines the local scale factor is less than 1, with a minimum of 0.04% too small at the central meridian<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>. Outside these lines the local scale factor is greater than 1, and is about 0.04% too large near the east and west coasts. Grid north and true north are only aligned on the central meridian (400&#160;km easting) of the grid which is 2°&#160;W (OSGB36) and approx. <span class="nowrap">2° 0′ 5″ W</span> (<a href="/wiki/World_Geodetic_System" title="World Geodetic System">WGS 84</a>).
</p><p>OSGB 36 was also used by <a href="/wiki/Admiralty" title="Admiralty">Admiralty</a> <a href="/wiki/Nautical_chart" title="Nautical chart">nautical charts</a> until 2000<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> after which <a href="/wiki/World_Geodetic_System" title="World Geodetic System">WGS 84</a> has been used.
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">geodetic</a> transformation between OSGB 36 and other terrestrial reference systems (like <a href="/wiki/International_Terrestrial_Reference_System" class="mw-redirect" title="International Terrestrial Reference System">ITRF2000</a>, <a href="/wiki/ETRS89" class="mw-redirect" title="ETRS89">ETRS89</a>, or <a href="/wiki/World_Geodetic_System" title="World Geodetic System">WGS 84</a>) can become quite tedious if attempted manually. The most common transformation is called the <a href="/wiki/Helmert_transformation" title="Helmert transformation">Helmert datum transformation</a>, which results in a typical 7&#160;m error from true. The definitive transformation from ETRS89 that is published by the OSGB is called the National Grid Transformation OSTN15.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  This models the detailed distortions in the 1936–1962 retriangulation, and achieves backwards compatibility in grid coordinates to sub-metre accuracy.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Datum_shift_between_OSGB_36_and_WGS_84">Datum shift between OSGB 36 and WGS 84</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ordnance_Survey_National_Grid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Datum shift between OSGB 36 and WGS 84">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The difference between the coordinates on different datums varies from place to place. The <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">longitude</a> and <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">latitude</a> positions on OSGB 36 are the same as for <a href="/wiki/World_Geodetic_System" title="World Geodetic System">WGS 84</a> at a point in the Atlantic Ocean well to the west of Great Britain. In <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, the WGS 84 longitude <i>lines</i> are about 70 metres east of their OSGB 36 equivalents, this value rising gradually to about 120&#160;m east on the east coast of <a href="/wiki/East_Anglia" title="East Anglia">East Anglia</a>. The WGS 84 latitude <i>lines</i> are about 70&#160;m south of the OSGB 36 lines in South <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, the difference diminishing to zero in the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Borders" title="Scottish Borders">Scottish Borders</a>, and then increasing to about 50&#160;m north on the north coast of <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">Scotland</a>. (If the <i>lines</i> are further <i>east</i>, then the longitude <i>value</i> of any given point is further <i>west</i>. Similarly, if the lines are further south, the values will give the point a more northerly latitude.) The smallest datum shift is on the west coast of Scotland and the greatest in <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Datum_shift_between_OSGB_36_and_ED_50">Datum shift between OSGB 36 and ED 50</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ordnance_Survey_National_Grid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Datum shift between OSGB 36 and ED 50">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>These two datums are not both in general use in any one place, but for a point in the <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">English Channel</a> halfway between <a href="/wiki/Dover" title="Dover">Dover</a> and <a href="/wiki/Calais" title="Calais">Calais</a>, the <a href="/wiki/ED50" title="ED50">ED50</a> longitude lines are about 20&#160;m east of the OSGB36 equivalents, and the ED50 latitude lines are about 150&#160;m south of the OSGB36 ones.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grid_letters">Grid letters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ordnance_Survey_National_Grid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Grid letters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="floatright"><a href="/wiki/File:British_National_Grid.svg" class="image"><img alt="British National Grid.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/British_National_Grid.svg/204px-British_National_Grid.svg.png" decoding="async" width="204" height="377" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/British_National_Grid.svg/306px-British_National_Grid.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/British_National_Grid.svg/408px-British_National_Grid.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="204" data-file-height="377" /></a></div>
<p>For the first letter, the grid is divided into squares of size 500&#160;km by 500&#160;km, outlined in dark grey  on the map to the right. There are four of these which contain significant land area within Great Britain: S, T, N and H.  The O square contains a tiny area of <a href="/wiki/North_Yorkshire" title="North Yorkshire">North Yorkshire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beast_Cliff" title="Beast Cliff">Beast Cliff</a> at <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion" style="white-space: nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/en/54.385678764135;-0.46166034386492_region:GB_scale:25000?pagename=Ordnance_Survey_National_Grid">OV 0000</a></span>, almost all of which lies below mean high tide.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>For the second letter, each 500&#160;km square is subdivided into 25 squares of size 100&#160;km by 100&#160;km, each with a letter code from A to Z (omitting I) starting with A in the north-west corner to Z in the south-east corner. These squares are outlined in light grey on the map, with those containing land lettered. The central (2° W) meridian is shown in red.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grid_digits">Grid digits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ordnance_Survey_National_Grid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Grid digits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Within each square, <i>eastings</i> and <i>northings</i> from the south west corner of the square are given numerically. For example, NH0325 means a 1&#160;km square whose south-west corner is 3&#160;km east and 25&#160;km north from the south-west corner of square NH.  A location can be indicated to varying resolutions numerically, usually from two digits in each coordinate (for a 1&#160;km square) through to five (for a 1&#160;m square); in each case the first half of the digits is for the first coordinate and the second half for the other. The most common usage is the <i>six figure grid reference</i>, employing three digits in each coordinate to determine a 100&#160;m square. For example, the grid reference of the 100&#160;m square containing the summit of <a href="/wiki/Ben_Nevis" title="Ben Nevis">Ben Nevis</a> is <b><span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion" style="white-space: nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/en/56.796087881253;-5.0047119553754_region:GB_scale:25000?pagename=Ordnance_Survey_National_Grid">NN 166 712</a></span></b>.  (Grid references may be written with or without spaces; e.g., also NN166712.) NN has an easting of 200&#160;km and northing of 700&#160;km, so the OSGB36 National Grid location for Ben Nevis is at 216600, 771200.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="All-numeric_grid_references">All-numeric grid references</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ordnance_Survey_National_Grid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: All-numeric grid references">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Grid references may also be quoted as a pair of numbers: eastings then northings in metres, measured from the southwest corner of the SV square. Note that 14 digits may be required for locations in <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">Orkney</a> and further north. For example, the grid reference for <a href="/wiki/Sullom_Voe" title="Sullom Voe">Sullom Voe oil terminal</a> in the Shetland Islands may be given as <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion" style="white-space: nowrap"><a class="external text" href="https://tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/en/60.459519377967;-1.281867334551_region:GB_scale:25000?pagename=Ordnance_Survey_National_Grid">HU396753</a></span> or <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="//www.old-maps.co.uk/indexmappage2.aspx?action=forcexy&amp;easting=0439668&amp;northing=1175316">439668,1175316</a>.
</p><p>Another, distinct, form of all-numeric grid reference is an abbreviated alphanumeric reference where the letters are simply omitted, e.g. 166712 for the summit of Ben Nevis.  Unlike the numeric references described above, this abbreviated grid reference is incomplete; it gives the location relative to an OS 100×100&#160;km&#160;square, but does not specify which square. It is often used informally when the context identifies the OS 2-letter square.  For example, within the context of a location known to be on <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey#GB_map_range" title="Ordnance Survey">OS Landranger sheet</a> 41 (which extends from NN000500 in the south-west to NN400900 in the north-east) the abbreviated grid reference 166712 is equivalent to NN166712.  If working with more than one Landranger sheet, this may also be given as 41/166712.
</p><p>Alternatively, sometimes numbers instead of the two-letter combinations are used for the 100×100&#160;km squares.  The numbering follows a grid index where the tens denote the progress from West to East and the units from South to North.  In the north of Scotland, the numbering is modified: the 100&#160;km square to the north of 39 is numbered N30; the square to the north of 49 is N40, etc.
</p>
