<p><b>Herbert Chapman</b> (19 January 1878&#160;– 6 January 1934) was an <a href="/wiki/English_(people)" class="mw-redirect" title="English (people)">English</a> <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">association football</a> player and manager. Though he had an undistinguished playing career, he went on to become one of the most successful and influential managers in early 20th-century <a href="/wiki/Football_in_England" title="Football in England">English football</a>, before his sudden death in 1934.
</p><p>As a player, Chapman played for a variety of clubs, at <a href="/wiki/English_Football_League" title="English Football League">Football League</a> and <a href="/wiki/Non-League_football" title="Non-League football">non-League</a> levels. His record was generally unremarkable as a player; he made fewer than 40&#160;League appearances over the course of a decade and did not win any major honours. Instead, he found success as a manager, first at <a href="/wiki/Northampton_Town_F.C." title="Northampton Town F.C.">Northampton Town</a> between 1908 and 1912, whom he led to a <a href="/wiki/Southern_Football_League" title="Southern Football League">Southern League</a> title. This attracted the attention of larger clubs and he moved to <a href="/wiki/Leeds_City_F.C." title="Leeds City F.C.">Leeds City</a>, where he started to improve the team's fortunes before the <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">First World War</a> intervened. After the war ended, City were implicated in an illegal payments scandal and were eventually disbanded. Chapman was initially banned from football but successfully appealed. He took over at <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_Town_A.F.C." title="Huddersfield Town A.F.C.">Huddersfield Town</a>, winning an <a href="/wiki/FA_Cup" title="FA Cup">FA Cup</a> and two <a href="/wiki/Football_League_First_Division" title="Football League First Division">First Division</a> titles in the period of four years.
</p><p>In 1925, <a href="/wiki/Arsenal_F.C." title="Arsenal F.C.">Arsenal</a> successfully tempted Chapman to join them, and he led the club to its first FA Cup success and two First Division titles. His work at Arsenal resulted in them becoming the dominant team of the 1930s – they would win five League titles in the decade – but he did not live to see them do so, dying suddenly from <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> in 1934, at the age of 55.
</p><p>Not only credited with turning round the fortunes of both Huddersfield Town and Arsenal, he is regarded as one of the game's first modernisers. He introduced new <a href="/wiki/Football_tactics_and_skills" class="mw-redirect" title="Football tactics and skills">tactics</a> and training techniques into the English game, as well as championing innovations such as <a href="/wiki/Floodlights_(sport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Floodlights (sport)">floodlighting</a>, European club competitions and <a href="/wiki/Squad_number_(association_football)" title="Squad number (association football)">numbered shirts</a>, and has received many posthumous honours in recognition.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Chapman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chapman was born in <a href="/wiki/Kiveton_Park" title="Kiveton Park">Kiveton Park</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Rotherham" title="Rotherham">Rotherham</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-chrishobbs_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chrishobbs-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> His father, John, was a <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a> <a href="/wiki/Miner" title="Miner">miner</a>, but rather than spend his life working down the pit, the young Herbert was bright enough to win a place at Sheffield Technical College (later to become part of the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Sheffield" title="University of Sheffield">University of Sheffield</a>), where he studied <a href="/wiki/Mining_engineering" title="Mining engineering">mining engineering</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kivetonhistory_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kivetonhistory-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chapman was one of eleven children<sup id="cite_ref-familyhistory_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-familyhistory-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> and born into a keen sporting family, with two of his brothers also playing professional football. The most successful of these was his younger brother, <a href="/wiki/Harry_Chapman_(footballer,_born_1880)" title="Harry Chapman (footballer, born 1880)">Harry</a>, who played for <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_Wednesday_F.C." title="Sheffield Wednesday F.C.">The Wednesday</a> during the 1900s, winning two League Championships and an FA Cup.<sup id="cite_ref-familyhistory_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-familyhistory-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> His older brother Thomas played for <a href="/wiki/Grimsby_Town_F.C." title="Grimsby Town F.C.">Grimsby Town</a>, while another brother, Matthew, later became a director of the same club.<sup id="cite_ref-familyhistory_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-familyhistory-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Playing_career">Playing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Chapman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Playing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Chapman's playing career was that of a typical journeyman, owing mostly to the fact he often played as an amateur; this meant that whether he could play for a particular club was dictated by whether he could find an appropriate job nearby.<sup id="cite_ref-kivetonhistory_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kivetonhistory-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> He first played as a youth for his local side, <a href="/wiki/Kiveton_Park_F.C." title="Kiveton Park F.C.">Kiveton Park Colliery</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-chrishobbs_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chrishobbs-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> winning the <a href="/wiki/Hatchard_League" title="Hatchard League">Hatchard Cup</a> in 1896 alongside team-mates <a href="/wiki/Walter_Wigmore" title="Walter Wigmore">Walter Wigmore</a> and <a href="/wiki/William_Ross_(footballer,_born_1874)" title="William Ross (footballer, born 1874)">William Ross</a>. He left the village that summer to join <a href="/wiki/Ashton_North_End_F.C." title="Ashton North End F.C.">Ashton North End</a>, before moving on to <a href="/wiki/Stalybridge_Rovers_F.C." title="Stalybridge Rovers F.C.">Stalybridge Rovers</a><sup id="cite_ref-chrishobbs_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chrishobbs-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> and then <a href="/wiki/Rochdale_A.F.C._(1896)" title="Rochdale A.F.C. (1896)">Rochdale</a> (not to be confused with the modern-day <a href="/wiki/Rochdale_A.F.C." title="Rochdale A.F.C.">Rochdale A.F.C.</a>) – all three clubs being members of the <a href="/wiki/Lancashire_League_(football)" title="Lancashire League (football)">Lancashire League</a>. Chapman played at <a href="/wiki/Inside_forward" class="mw-redirect" title="Inside forward">inside right</a>, and although he lacked the skill of his brother Harry, he compensated for it with his strength and robustness.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1898, he moved to <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Second_Division" title="Football League Second Division">Second Division</a> <a href="/wiki/Grimsby_Town_F.C." title="Grimsby Town F.C.">Grimsby Town</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Though now playing in the professional <a href="/wiki/Football_League" class="mw-redirect" title="Football League">Football League</a>, Chapman was still an amateur at this stage and obtained a job with a firm of local <a href="/wiki/Solicitor" title="Solicitor">solicitors</a> to earn his way.<sup id="cite_ref-page46_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-page46-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Grimsby started the season poorly – they were near the bottom of the division by <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a> and were beaten 7–0 by <a href="/wiki/Preston_North_End_F.C." title="Preston North End F.C.">Preston North End</a> in the <a href="/wiki/FA_Cup" title="FA Cup">FA Cup</a>, but rallied to finish tenth at the end of <a href="/wiki/1898-99_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1898-99 in English football">the 1898–99 season</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> By this time Chapman had been dropped from the team, having been unsuccessfully moved to <a href="/wiki/Centre_forward" class="mw-redirect" title="Centre forward">centre forward</a>, an unfamiliar position for him.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> He was released by Grimsby and drifted down into non-league football with a brief spell at <a href="/wiki/Swindon_Town_F.C." title="Swindon Town F.C.">Swindon Town</a>, playing three games and scoring twice,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> but had to leave the club as he was unable to find a job in the area.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> He moved on to see out the season with <a href="/wiki/Sheppey_United_F.C." title="Sheppey United F.C.">Sheppey United</a>, who finished bottom of the <a href="/wiki/Southern_Football_League" title="Southern Football League">Southern League</a> in <a href="/wiki/1899-1900_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1899-1900 in English football">1899–1900</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-southernleague_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-southernleague-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Chapman finished as United's top scorer but was injured at the end of this season, and still unable to find a job.<sup id="cite_ref-page51_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-page51-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> Disheartened, he returned to his home town and turned out for <a href="/wiki/Worksop_Town_F.C." title="Worksop Town F.C.">Worksop Town</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Midland_Football_League_(1889)" title="Midland Football League (1889)">Midland League</a> in <a href="/wiki/1900-01_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1900-01 in English football">1900–01</a>, while resuming his studies, this time at Old Firth College in Sheffield.<sup id="cite_ref-page51_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-page51-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Because of his studies, he mainly played for Worksop's reserves, but in a first-team match against <a href="/wiki/Northampton_Town_F.C." title="Northampton Town F.C.">Northampton Town</a> he caught the opposition's eye and they offered him a contract, leading him to turn professional for the first time in 1901.<sup id="cite_ref-studd28_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-studd28-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> He played for Northampton for the whole <a href="/wiki/1901-02_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1901-02 in English football">1901–02</a> season, finishing as top scorer with 14&#160;goals in 22&#160;games for the club.<sup id="cite_ref-page55_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-page55-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> During that season he had impressed in an FA Cup match against <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_United_F.C." title="Sheffield United F.C.">Sheffield United</a>, leading them to offer Chapman a contract at the end of the season; Chapman accepted but dropped down to amateur status, wishing to make use of his engineering qualifications in the local area.<sup id="cite_ref-page55_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-page55-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> He played 22&#160;matches and scored twice for United, but struggled to keep his place in a team full of internationals, and was sold to <a href="/wiki/Notts_County_F.C." title="Notts County F.C.">Notts County</a> for £300 at the end of <a href="/wiki/1902-03_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1902-03 in English football">1902–03</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> Chapman turned professional again, but only made seven appearances in two years for County, scoring once.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1904, Chapman moved back to his old club Northampton Town, playing a season effectively on loan from Notts County (as they kept his registration), before being transferred permanently in 1905 to <a href="/wiki/Tottenham_Hotspur_F.C." title="Tottenham Hotspur F.C.">Tottenham Hotspur</a> for £70.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> He scored eleven goals for Spurs in their <a href="/wiki/1905-06_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1905-06 in English football">1905–06</a> Southern League campaign.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> He spent <a href="/wiki/1906-07_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1906-07 in English football">1906–07</a> in and out of the side, scoring just three goals. With the season drawing to a close, he decided that he had had "a good innings" and decided to leave Tottenham and professional football for good, in favour of pursuing his career in engineering.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Managerial_career">Managerial career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Chapman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Managerial career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northampton_Town">Northampton Town</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Chapman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Northampton Town">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1907, as he was about to leave Tottenham Hotspur, Chapman had recommended Spurs team-mate <a href="/wiki/Walter_Bull" title="Walter Bull">Walter Bull</a> to his old club Northampton Town, as their new manager. However, Bull changed his mind, and in turn recommended that Chapman take the job instead.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>  Chapman changed his mind about retiring from the game, and instead agreed to become player-manager of Northampton Town. Northampton had finished bottom of the Southern League two seasons running immediately before Chapman's appointment,<sup id="cite_ref-southernleague_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-southernleague-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> but Chapman turned the club around within a short period of time.
</p><p>At the time, teams rarely employed tactics of any sort – Chapman would later remark: "No attempt was made to organise victory. The most that I remember was the occasional chat between, say two men playing on the same wing."<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> As a manager, he sought to change that; after seeing Northampton lose to <a href="/wiki/Norwich_City_F.C." title="Norwich City F.C.">Norwich City</a> despite dominating, Chapman opined that "a team can attack for too long".<sup id="cite_ref-Wilson_40_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson_40-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> He thus set about creating a tactical framework for all his players; he dropped the <a href="/wiki/Wing_half" class="mw-redirect" title="Wing half">half backs</a> (midfield) back to give his <a href="/wiki/Forward_(football)" class="mw-redirect" title="Forward (football)">forwards</a> more space and draw the opposition defenders out of the <a href="/wiki/Penalty_area" title="Penalty area">penalty area</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-studd3738_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-studd3738-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> while encouraging his own back line to pass their way out of trouble.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> Gradually, he created a style of highly organised, counter-attacking football, which was at odds with the prevailing orthodoxy but nevertheless highly effective; <a href="/wiki/Swindon_Town_F.C." title="Swindon Town F.C.">Swindon Town</a>'s <a href="/wiki/England_national_football_team" title="England national football team">England international</a> <a href="/wiki/Harold_John_Fleming" class="mw-redirect" title="Harold John Fleming">Harold Fleming</a>, after losing 4–1 at home to Northampton, remarked to Chapman: "You have something more than a team: you have a machine."<sup id="cite_ref-studd46_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-studd46-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>To allow his system to reach its full potential, Chapman encouraged his chairman to spend money on new players. The club's first ever transfer fee, £400, was paid for <a href="/wiki/Wales_national_football_team" title="Wales national football team">Welsh international</a> <a href="/wiki/Lloyd_Davies" title="Lloyd Davies">Lloyd Davies</a> who remains Northampton's most-capped player,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> winger <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fred_McDiarmid&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fred McDiarmid (page does not exist)">Fred McDiarmid</a> and <a href="/wiki/Playmaker" title="Playmaker">playmaking</a> <a href="/wiki/Centre_half" class="mw-redirect" title="Centre half">centre half</a> <a href="/w/index.php?title=David_McCartney&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="David McCartney (page does not exist)">David McCartney</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-studd3738_25-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-studd3738-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> With this new talent, in his first season in charge, Chapman led Northampton to eighth place; with additional new signings, such as inside forward <a href="/w/index.php?title=Albert_Lewis_(footballer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Albert Lewis (footballer) (page does not exist)">Albert Lewis</a> from <a href="/wiki/Coventry_City_F.C." title="Coventry City F.C.">Coventry City</a>, he used this as a springboard to take the Southern League title in <a href="/wiki/1908-09_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1908-09 in English football">1908–09</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-southernleague_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-southernleague-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> with Lewis finishing as top scorer.<sup id="cite_ref-studd40_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-studd40-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By now, Chapman had retired as a player in first-class football, having played his last match against <a href="/wiki/Watford_F.C." title="Watford F.C.">Watford</a> in January 1909,<sup id="cite_ref-studd40_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-studd40-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> but filled the gap he left, as well as showing his eye for spotting talent, by signing players such as future England international <a href="/wiki/Fanny_Walden" title="Fanny Walden">Fanny Walden</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-studd46_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-studd46-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> As Southern League champions, Northampton contested and lost the <a href="/wiki/1909_FA_Charity_Shield" title="1909 FA Charity Shield">1909 FA Charity Shield</a>, 2–0 to <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_United_F.C." title="Newcastle United F.C.">Newcastle United</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> and although they did not win the Southern League title again during Chapman's time as manager, they finished in the top four in each of the following three seasons.<sup id="cite_ref-fchd_nt_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fchd_nt-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, they proved their mettle in the FA Cup against <a href="/wiki/Football_League_First_Division" title="Football League First Division">First Division</a> sides, knocking out <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_Wednesday_F.C." title="Sheffield Wednesday F.C.">The Wednesday</a> and taking <a href="/wiki/Nottingham_Forest_F.C." title="Nottingham Forest F.C.">Nottingham Forest</a> and Newcastle United to replays, losing 1–0 both times.<sup id="cite_ref-fchd_nt_31-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fchd_nt-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chapman was keen to get Northampton Town into the <a href="/wiki/Football_League" class="mw-redirect" title="Football League">Football League</a>, but with no automatic promotion or relegation rules at the time this proved very difficult. Chapman proposed a new two-division <a href="/wiki/Football_Alliance" title="Football Alliance">Football Alliance</a> underneath the two divisions of the Football League, with automatic promotion and relegation (a similar system to the four-division League setup introduced in 1921), but this was rejected at the time (and would not come about until 1920).<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> In the 1912 close season, he was offered the chance to manage <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Second_Division" title="Football League Second Division">Second Division</a> <a href="/wiki/Leeds_City_F.C." title="Leeds City F.C.">Leeds City</a>, and with Northampton's blessing moved north again to join the League side.<sup id="cite_ref-page84_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-page84-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Leeds_City">Leeds City</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Chapman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Leeds City">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Leeds City had finished 19th in the <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Second_Division" title="Football League Second Division">Second Division</a> in the season preceding Chapman's arrival, and were facing re-election. Chapman played an essential part in lobbying for the side's readmission and Leeds City were duly re-elected.<sup id="cite_ref-page84_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-page84-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> That done, Chapman signed new talent such as <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Speirs" title="Jimmy Speirs">Jimmy Speirs</a> from <a href="/wiki/Bradford_City_A.F.C." title="Bradford City A.F.C.">Bradford City</a> and despite some erratic performances – losing 6–0 in between two 5–1 wins, for example – City finished sixth in <a href="/wiki/1912-13_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1912-13 in English football">1912–13</a>, Chapman's first season.<sup id="cite_ref-mightyleeds_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mightyleeds-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> Attendances rose at <a href="/wiki/Elland_Road" title="Elland Road">Elland Road</a> from 8,500 to 13,000 in his first year,<sup id="cite_ref-mightyleeds_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mightyleeds-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> as Chapman's attacking side scored 70&#160;goals, the second-highest total in the entire division.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With a strengthening of the defence, City's form improved further the next season, in <a href="/wiki/1913-14_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1913-14 in English football">1913–14</a>, coming fourth, two points outside the promotion places.<sup id="cite_ref-wafll_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wafll-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Despite having failed on his promise to get the team promoted within two years, City's rising attendances and resulting better profits for the club kept the directors happy, and the club were confident of promotion in <a href="/wiki/1914-15_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1914-15 in English football">1914–15</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-wafll_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wafll-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> However, the declaration of <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> disrupted Leeds City's season, with attendances down as men signed up to fight. Chapman by now had amassed a very large squad and was unable to pick a consistent side, continually changing his first-choice lineup.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> Leeds City lost six of their last eight games of the season to finish just fifteenth.<sup id="cite_ref-wafll_36-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wafll-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>League football was suspended for the rest of the war, with Leeds City playing in regional competitions. With many players away fighting or having left the game due to a drop in wages, Leeds relied heavily on guest players during these matches.<sup id="cite_ref-leedsscandal_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leedsscandal-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Chapman, meanwhile, had decided to help the war effort by taking up a position as manager of a <a href="/wiki/Filling_Factories" class="mw-redirect" title="Filling Factories">munitions factory</a> at <a href="/wiki/Barnbow" title="Barnbow">Barnbow</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Cross_Gates" title="Cross Gates">Cross Gates</a> in 1916.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup> For the next two years, City's assistant manager, <a href="/w/index.php?title=George_Cripps&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="George Cripps (page does not exist)">George Cripps</a> stood in for Chapman on the administrative side, while chairman <a href="/wiki/Joe_Connor_(footballer_born_1877)" class="mw-redirect" title="Joe Connor (footballer born 1877)">Joe Connor</a> and another director took charge of the team.<sup id="cite_ref-leedsscandal_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leedsscandal-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chapman returned to Leeds City from Barnbow after hostilities had ended, but resigned suddenly in December 1918, eventually moving to <a href="/wiki/Selby" title="Selby">Selby</a> to take up a position as a superintendent at an <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coke_(fuel)" title="Coke (fuel)">coke</a> works.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup> No reason was given for his resignation, but as football resumed in <a href="/wiki/1919-20_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1919-20 in English football">1919–20</a>, Leeds City were accused by a former player of financial irregularities, involving illegal payments to guest players during wartime matches.<sup id="cite_ref-leedsscandal_38-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leedsscandal-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> No documentary evidence was produced, but Leeds' refusal to allow the authorities access to their financial records was deemed a sign of guilt, and they were expelled from the Football League in October 1919 and five club officials, including Chapman, were banned from football for life.<sup id="cite_ref-leedsscandal_38-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leedsscandal-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> The club was dissolved, with the players auctioned off and their Elland Road ground taken over by the newly formed <a href="/wiki/Leeds_United_F.C." title="Leeds United F.C.">Leeds United</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-leedsscandal_38-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-leedsscandal-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Huddersfield_Town">Huddersfield Town</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Chapman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Huddersfield Town">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Huddersfield_Town_1922.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/42/Huddersfield_Town_1922.jpg/250px-Huddersfield_Town_1922.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="197" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/42/Huddersfield_Town_1922.jpg/375px-Huddersfield_Town_1922.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/4/42/Huddersfield_Town_1922.jpg/500px-Huddersfield_Town_1922.jpg 2x" data-file-width="748" data-file-height="590" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Huddersfield_Town_1922.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chapman (standing far left), with the <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_Town_A.F.C." title="Huddersfield Town A.F.C.">Huddersfield Town</a> team of <a href="/wiki/1921-22_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1921-22 in English football">1921–22</a>, the season they first won the <a href="/wiki/FA_Cup" title="FA Cup">FA Cup</a>.</div></div></div>
<p>Chapman was still working at the coke works in Selby when his ban was imposed, but by Christmas 1920 the company was sold up and he was laid off.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Soon after, however, he was approached by <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield_Town_A.F.C." title="Huddersfield Town A.F.C.">Huddersfield Town</a> to become assistant to <a href="/wiki/Ambrose_Langley" title="Ambrose Langley">Ambrose Langley</a>, who had been a former team-mate of Herbert Chapman's brother Harry at The Wednesday.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilson_40_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson_40-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> Huddersfield Town backed Chapman in an appeal against his ban, arguing that as he had been working at the factory in Barnbow during the war, he had not been in charge of Leeds City during the time illegal payments were paid. The ban was overturned, and Chapman was formally installed as Langley's assistant on 1 February 1921.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chapman was promoted to full secretary-manager, replacing Langley, the following month,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> and soon made an impact, signing players such as <a href="/wiki/England_national_football_team" title="England national football team">England international</a> <a href="/wiki/Clem_Stephenson" title="Clem Stephenson">Clem Stephenson</a> from <a href="/wiki/Aston_Villa_F.C." title="Aston Villa F.C.">Aston Villa</a> (who became captain under Chapman)<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> and 18-year-old unknown <a href="/wiki/George_Brown_(English_footballer)" class="mw-redirect" title="George Brown (English footballer)">George Brown</a> (who went on to become Huddersfield's all-time top scorer).<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> In Chapman's first full season in charge (<a href="/wiki/1921-22_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1921-22 in English football">1921–22</a>), Huddersfield Town won the <a href="/wiki/FA_Cup" title="FA Cup">FA Cup</a>, beating <a href="/wiki/Preston_North_End_F.C." title="Preston North End F.C.">Preston North End</a> 1–0 in the <a href="/wiki/1922_FA_Cup_Final" title="1922 FA Cup Final">final</a> at <a href="/wiki/Stamford_Bridge_(stadium)" title="Stamford Bridge (stadium)">Stamford Bridge</a>, the club's first major trophy.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup> In the league, however, his side had spent most of the season fighting relegation and had finished 14th,<sup id="cite_ref-fchd_huddersfield_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fchd_huddersfield-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> so Chapman looked to strengthen his squad.
</p><p>As at Northampton, Chapman's tactics were based upon the principles of a strong defence and a fast, counter-attacking response, with the focus on quick, short passing and mazy runs from his wingers,<sup id="cite_ref-page135_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-page135-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> who would pass low inside the defence instead of crossing from the byline.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> He had been granted control of all footballing affairs at the club and made this responsibility work to his advantage, encouraging the club's reserve and third teams to play the same style of football so that their players would function effectively in the first team if selected.<sup id="cite_ref-page135_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-page135-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> He employed a wide-ranging scouting network to find the right players for his tactical system. Bolstered by the money from the cup run, Chapman was able to make further signings such as goalkeeper <a href="/wiki/Ted_Taylor_(footballer)" title="Ted Taylor (footballer)">Ted Taylor</a> and forward <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Wilson_(footballer_born_1895)" class="mw-redirect" title="Charlie Wilson (footballer born 1895)">Charlie Wilson</a> (later to be joined by <a href="/wiki/George_Cook_(footballer_born_1895)" class="mw-redirect" title="George Cook (footballer born 1895)">George Cook</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>With new players and using Chapman's system, Huddersfield were transformed. They finished third in <a href="/wiki/1922-23_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1922-23 in English football">1922–23</a><sup id="cite_ref-fchd_huddersfield_47-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fchd_huddersfield-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> and went on to win their first League title in <a href="/wiki/1923-24_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1923-24 in English football">1923–24</a>. This success was by the narrowest of margins – equal on points with <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_City_F.C." title="Cardiff City F.C.">Cardiff City</a>, Huddersfield won by a difference of 0.024 (1.818 to 1.794) in <a href="/wiki/Goal_average" class="mw-redirect" title="Goal average">goal average</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> The final goal by Brown in a 3–0 win over <a href="/wiki/Nottingham_Forest_F.C." title="Nottingham Forest F.C.">Nottingham Forest</a> on the final day of the season proved crucial, although ultimately it was Huddersfield's superior defensive record which had given them the advantage.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Following the title win, Chapman kept faith with his squad, making only one new signing – outside right <a href="/wiki/Joey_Williams" title="Joey Williams">Joey Williams</a><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> – as they successfully defended their League title in <a href="/wiki/1924-25_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1924-25 in English football">1924–25</a>. Huddersfield started brightly but a poor run of form in October and November (in part caused by an injury to goalkeeper Ted Taylor) saw them drop to ninth at one point.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup> Taylor was replaced by new signing <a href="/wiki/Billy_Mercer_(footballer_born_1892)" class="mw-redirect" title="Billy Mercer (footballer born 1892)">Billy Mercer</a> and a resurgence in form saw Huddersfield climb the table, regaining top spot with a 5–0 win over <a href="/wiki/Arsenal_F.C." title="Arsenal F.C.">Arsenal</a> in February,<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup> and eventually finishing two points clear of runners-up <a href="/wiki/West_Bromwich_Albion_F.C." title="West Bromwich Albion F.C.">West Bromwich Albion</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">&#91;56&#93;</a></sup> As a testament to Chapman's philosophy of relying on a strong defence, it was the first time a title-winning side had gone through a season without conceding more than two goals in any match.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">&#91;57&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arsenal">Arsenal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Chapman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Arsenal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Herbert_Chapman_bust_20050922.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Herbert_Chapman_bust_20050922.jpg/150px-Herbert_Chapman_bust_20050922.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="182" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Herbert_Chapman_bust_20050922.jpg/225px-Herbert_Chapman_bust_20050922.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Herbert_Chapman_bust_20050922.jpg/300px-Herbert_Chapman_bust_20050922.jpg 2x" data-file-width="645" data-file-height="781" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Herbert_Chapman_bust_20050922.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>A bronze bust of Chapman stands inside <a href="/wiki/Emirates_Stadium" title="Emirates Stadium">Emirates Stadium</a> as a tribute to his achievements at the club.</div></div></div>
<p>In the 1925 close season, Chapman had already set about improving his squad for Huddersfield to seek a third successive title (something which had never been achieved before). At the same time, <a href="/wiki/Arsenal_F.C." title="Arsenal F.C.">Arsenal</a> were looking to replace <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Knighton" title="Leslie Knighton">Leslie Knighton</a>, who had been sacked. Arsenal chairman <a href="/wiki/Henry_Norris_(businessman)" title="Henry Norris (businessman)">Sir Henry Norris</a> placed an advertisement in the <i>Athletic News</i>:<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">&#91;58&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: &#39;Times New Roman&#39;, Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“
</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">Arsenal Football Club is open to receive applications for the position of TEAM MANAGER. He must be experienced and possess the highest qualifications for the post, both as to ability and personal character. </td></tr>

</tbody></table>
<p>Although Arsenal had been fighting relegation in both the two previous seasons, and despite the chairman's restriction on spending, Chapman nevertheless moved to Arsenal soon afterwards, attracted both by the London club's larger crowds and a salary of £2,000, double what he earned at Huddersfield Town.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59">&#91;59&#93;</a></sup> At Arsenal, Chapman immediately made an impact by signing 34-year-old <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Buchan" title="Charlie Buchan">Charlie Buchan</a>, an England international and <a href="/wiki/Sunderland_A.F.C." title="Sunderland A.F.C.">Sunderland</a>'s all-time record goalscorer, whom he made Arsenal captain.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">&#91;60&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Football_Formation_-_WM.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Football_Formation_-_WM.png/150px-Football_Formation_-_WM.png" decoding="async" width="150" height="212" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Football_Formation_-_WM.png/225px-Football_Formation_-_WM.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Football_Formation_-_WM.png/300px-Football_Formation_-_WM.png 2x" data-file-width="409" data-file-height="577" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Football_Formation_-_WM.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The "WM" formation that Chapman implemented at Arsenal.</div></div></div>
<p>Chapman and Buchan's arrival at the club coincided with a change in the <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_the_Game_(association_football)" title="Laws of the Game (association football)">laws of the game</a> in June 1925, that modified the <a href="/wiki/Offside_(association_football)" title="Offside (association football)">offside law</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">&#91;61&#93;</a></sup> The change had reduced the number of opposition players that an attacker needed between himself and the goal-line from three to two (including the goalkeeper). Buchan's idea, implemented by Chapman, was to move the <a href="/wiki/Centre_half" class="mw-redirect" title="Centre half">centre half</a> from a roaming position in midfield to a "stopper" position in defence. With the <a href="/wiki/Inside_forward" class="mw-redirect" title="Inside forward">inside forwards</a> brought back to help the midfield, this changed the usual <a href="/wiki/Formation_(association_football)" title="Formation (association football)">formation</a> from 2–3–5 to 3–4–3, or a "WM", so called after the shape it formed spelled out the letters. This meant the offside trap was now the responsibility of the single centre half, while the <a href="/wiki/Leftback" class="mw-redirect" title="Leftback">full backs</a> were pushed wider to cover the wings.<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler4647_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler4647-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Arsenal were by no means the only team to have come up with the idea of dropping the centre half into defence – <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_United_F.C." title="Newcastle United F.C.">Newcastle United</a> beat Arsenal 7–0 that season employing such a system with <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Spencer" title="Charlie Spencer">Charlie Spencer</a> at centre-half; <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Park_F.C." title="Queen&#39;s Park F.C.">Queen's Park</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tottenham_Hotspur_F.C." title="Tottenham Hotspur F.C.">Tottenham Hotspur</a> had also adopted similar systems.<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler4647_62-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler4647-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Chapman, however, was able to refine and improve on the idea better than his rivals, melding the tactical change with his own ideas on counter-attacking football, pacy wingers and a strong defence.<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler4647_62-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler4647-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> Chapman himself summed it up by saying: "the most opportune time for scoring is immediately after repelling an attack, because opponents are then strung out in the wrong half of the field."<sup id="cite_ref-say77_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-say77-63">&#91;63&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Arsenal went on to finish second in <a href="/wiki/1925-26_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1925-26 in English football">1925–26</a>, five points behind Chapman's old side Huddersfield Town,<sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">&#91;64&#93;</a></sup> as they became the first club in England to win three titles in succession.<sup id="cite_ref-threeinarow_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-threeinarow-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> It proved to be an early dawn for Arsenal, who spent most of the rest of the 1920s in mid-table, as Chapman took his time finding the right players to fit his new system, outlining a five-year plan for success.<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler48_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler48-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> He retained relatively few players of his predecessor Knighton's era – <a href="/wiki/Bob_John" title="Bob John">Bob John</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alf_Baker" title="Alf Baker">Alf Baker</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Brain" title="Jimmy Brain">Jimmy Brain</a> being the exceptions – and instead looked to bring in talent from elsewhere.<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler4647_62-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler4647-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 1926, he signed the pacy winger <a href="/wiki/Joe_Hulme" title="Joe Hulme">Joe Hulme</a>, followed that summer by forward <a href="/wiki/Jack_Lambert_(footballer,_born_1902)" title="Jack Lambert (footballer, born 1902)">Jack Lambert</a> and full-back <a href="/wiki/Tom_Parker_(footballer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tom Parker (footballer)">Tom Parker</a>, who would later succeed Buchan as captain.<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler48_66-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler48-66">&#91;66&#93;</a></sup> Although Arsenal's league form was indifferent, in 1927 they reached the <a href="/wiki/1927_FA_Cup_Final" title="1927 FA Cup Final">FA Cup Final</a>, their first, but lost 1–0 to <a href="/wiki/Cardiff_City_F.C." title="Cardiff City F.C.">Cardiff City</a> after an error by goalkeeper <a href="/wiki/Dan_Lewis_(footballer)" title="Dan Lewis (footballer)">Dan Lewis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67">&#91;67&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The same year, Arsenal became embroiled in a scandal; footballers' pay at the time was limited by a <a href="/wiki/Maximum_wage" title="Maximum wage">maximum wage</a>, but an FA enquiry found that Charlie Buchan had secretly received illegal payments from Arsenal as an incentive to sign for the club.<sup id="cite_ref-spurling46_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spurling46-68">&#91;68&#93;</a></sup> Sir Henry Norris was indicted for his part and banned from football, but Chapman escaped punishment, and with the autocratic Norris replaced by the more benign <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Hill-Wood" title="Samuel Hill-Wood">Samuel Hill-Wood</a>, Chapman's power and influence within the club increased, allowing him control over all aspects of the club's business.<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler4647_62-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler4647-62">&#91;62&#93;</a></sup> He persevered in building the team, strengthening his attacking lineup with the signings of <a href="/wiki/David_Jack" title="David Jack">David Jack</a> in 1928, and <a href="/wiki/Alex_James_(footballer)" title="Alex James (footballer)">Alex James</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cliff_Bastin" title="Cliff Bastin">Cliff Bastin</a> in 1929.<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler51_69-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler51-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup> As at his previous clubs, Chapman worked on improving the defence, notably through the signings of <a href="/wiki/Herbie_Roberts" title="Herbie Roberts">Herbie Roberts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Hapgood" title="Eddie Hapgood">Eddie Hapgood</a> at centre half and left back respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler50_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler50-70">&#91;70&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was the signing of David Jack in particular that highlighted Chapman's cunning when it came to transfer negotiations. <a href="/wiki/Bolton_Wanderers_F.C." title="Bolton Wanderers F.C.">Bolton Wanderers</a> had originally asked for a fee of £13,000, almost double the existing record at the time. <a href="/wiki/Bob_Wall_(football_administrator)" title="Bob Wall (football administrator)">Bob Wall</a>, Chapman's personal assistant at the time, later recounted the negotiations he made with Bolton's directors as follows:<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler51_69-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler51-69">&#91;69&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Chapman's subterfuge succeeded, as he managed to bargain the inebriated Bolton directors down to £10,890.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">&#91;71&#93;</a></sup> He was less successful in his attempt to sign <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_McGrory" title="Jimmy McGrory">Jimmy McGrory</a> from <a href="/wiki/Celtic_F.C." title="Celtic F.C.">Celtic</a>. He had set up a meeting with Celtic manager <a href="/wiki/Willie_Maley" title="Willie Maley">Willie Maley</a> and young McGrory in summer 1928 when Maley and McGrory were on their way to a pilgrimage in Lourdes.<sup id="cite_ref-jimmy_72-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jimmy-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> A huge sum was offered (a blank cheque, some say) for the prolific McGrory, and Maley was more than ready to accept, for Celtic needed money to pay for their new stand. The deal fell through when McGrory, a homesick young Scotsman with an intense love for Celtic, refused to sign.<sup id="cite_ref-jimmy_72-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jimmy-72">&#91;72&#93;</a></sup> He later (in 1935) broke the all-time goalscoring record.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">&#91;73&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chapman had laid out a five-year plan for success in 1925, and it came to fruition exactly on schedule, as his Arsenal won their first major trophy in the <a href="/wiki/1930_FA_Cup_Final" title="1930 FA Cup Final">1930 FA Cup Final</a>, beating his old side, Huddersfield Town, 2–0.<sup id="cite_ref-1930cupfinal_74-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1930cupfinal-74">&#91;74&#93;</a></sup> Despite having only finished 14th in the League the same season, the win spurred Arsenal on and laid the foundations for a decade in which Arsenal would become the dominant team in England and eventually win five league titles.
</p><p>Chapman had by now perfected Arsenal's ruthless, counter-attacking strategy. He employed a robust front line of Lambert supported by David Jack and Alex James as deep-lying inside forwards, filling the gap vacated by the movement of the centre half into defence; Alex James in particular, with his passing supplying the front men, became celebrated as the engine of the team during the coming decade.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">&#91;75&#93;</a></sup> Chapman employed Bastin and Hulme as pacy wingers who could cut inside instead of hugging the touchline;<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler15-17_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler15-17-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> they could either shoot for goal themselves or pick each other out if the centre forward was marked out of the game.<sup id="cite_ref-page161-62_77-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-page161-62-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup> With the exception of James, keeping and dwelling on the ball and individuality were discouraged in favour of a quick passing game, although Chapman still conceded: "All the men are expected to play to plan, but not so as to stifle individuality."<sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">&#91;78&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chapman's tactics of fast-moving play meant the <a href="/wiki/Wing_half" class="mw-redirect" title="Wing half">wing half</a> line of John and Jones were now pushed in to cover central <a href="/wiki/Midfield" title="Midfield">midfield</a>, pivoting around the halfway line so that they could drop back to defend when necessary.<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler15-17_76-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler15-17-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> Chapman was equally committed to a strong defence, saying that maintenance of a back line was "the rock bottom of football".<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler15-17_76-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler15-17-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> Arsenal's defence were told to play deep and, with the support of the wing halves, fall back into their own <a href="/wiki/Penalty_area" title="Penalty area">penalty area</a> when the opposition had the ball; this allowed the opposition plenty of possession in Arsenal's half, until they reached the 18-yard line and faced a massed defence.<sup id="cite_ref-79" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-79">&#91;79&#93;</a></sup> Once Arsenal regained the ball – usually through the centre half Herbie Roberts – the ball would be quickly passed forward and the wing halves would push up to support the attackers, meaning Arsenal could quickly commit as many as seven men forward as a unit to rapidly attack and try to score.<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler15-17_76-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler15-17-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-page161-62_77-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-page161-62-77">&#91;77&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chapman's system demanded a high level of fitness from his players, something which he strongly emphasised. He balanced the need for players suited to each task – in which his skill in spotting the right players and his extensive scouting network proved vital – with adapting his system to account for their abilities.<sup id="cite_ref-80" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-80">&#91;80&#93;</a></sup> Though highly effective, Chapman's fast, counter-attacking passing approach to football contrasted with how the game was traditionally played in England at the time, with its emphasis on dribbling, possession and dwelling on the ball,<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">&#91;81&#93;</a></sup> and thus brought accusations of "Lucky Arsenal" or "Boring Arsenal" from commentators and opposition alike.<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler15-17_76-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler15-17-76">&#91;76&#93;</a></sup> Nevertheless, despite the stereotype, in Arsenal's first title-winning season of <a href="/wiki/1930-31_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1930-31 in English football">1930–31</a>, they scored 127&#160;goals in the League, which still stands as a club record to this day.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">&#91;82&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Having won both League and Cup in separate seasons with two clubs, Chapman was determined to go one better and win <a href="/wiki/Double_(association_football)" title="Double (association football)">the Double</a> – which had not yet been won in the 20th century – in <a href="/wiki/1931-32_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1931-32 in English football">1931–32</a> but ended up missing out on both, finishing second in the League behind <a href="/wiki/Everton_F.C." title="Everton F.C.">Everton</a><sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">&#91;83&#93;</a></sup> and losing the <a href="/wiki/1932_FA_Cup_Final" title="1932 FA Cup Final">1932 FA Cup Final</a> controversially to <a href="/wiki/Newcastle_United_F.C." title="Newcastle United F.C.">Newcastle United</a>, with Newcastle's equaliser coming after the ball had gone behind for a goal kick.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">&#91;84&#93;</a></sup> Undeterred, Chapman kept faith with his side and launched a bid for the <a href="/wiki/1932-33_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1932-33 in English football">1932–33</a> title. However, it was during this season that Arsenal suffered one of the most infamous defeats in their history.
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/FA_Cup" title="FA Cup">FA Cup</a> third round, Arsenal had been drawn against <a href="/wiki/Walsall_F.C." title="Walsall F.C.">Walsall</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Football_League_Third_Division_North" title="Football League Third Division North">Third Division North</a>. Arsenal, as the previous season's losing finalists and league leaders, were clear favourites to win the match. On the day, five of the Arsenal first team were out with injury or <a href="/wiki/Influenza" title="Influenza">flu</a> and had their places taken by reserves.<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">&#91;85&#93;</a></sup> Arsenal lost 2–0 in one of the greatest FA Cup upsets.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">&#91;86&#93;</a></sup> Chapman was enraged by the result, and showed his ruthlessness by selling one player, <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Black_(Scottish_footballer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tommy Black (Scottish footballer)">Tommy Black</a>, who had conceded a <a href="/wiki/Penalty_kick_(association_football)" title="Penalty kick (association football)">penalty</a> in the game, to <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Argyle_F.C." title="Plymouth Argyle F.C.">Plymouth Argyle</a> within a week of the result; another, striker <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Walsh_(footballer)" title="Charlie Walsh (footballer)">Charlie Walsh</a>, was sold to <a href="/wiki/Brentford_F.C." title="Brentford F.C.">Brentford</a> a week later.<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler64_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler64-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Despite the FA Cup setback, Arsenal bounced back in the League, and with the same scoring form as in 1930–31, finished the season having scored 118&#160;League goals in total, including a 5–0 win over rivals <a href="/wiki/Aston_Villa_F.C." title="Aston Villa F.C.">Aston Villa</a> in that season's title-deciding match.<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler60_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler60-88">&#91;88&#93;</a></sup> In the following close season, Chapman became the first professional manager in charge of <a href="/wiki/England_national_football_team" title="England national football team">England</a>, albeit in an <i>ad hoc</i> unofficial capacity, for a summer tour of Europe. He did not have any input into the selection process, the team being determined by the <a href="/wiki/The_Football_Association" title="The Football Association">FA</a>'s International Selection Committee, but did advise on tactics and gave pre-match team talks.<sup id="cite_ref-England_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-England-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup> Chapman was in charge for a friendly against <a href="/wiki/Italy_national_football_team" title="Italy national football team">Italy</a> in <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> on 13 May 1933, which finished 1–1, and England's 4–0 win over <a href="/wiki/Switzerland_national_football_team" title="Switzerland national football team">Switzerland</a> a week later.<sup id="cite_ref-England_89-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-England-89">&#91;89&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Wary of his ageing Arsenal team and the club's inadequate reserves (as proven by the Walsall match), it was around this time that Chapman noted to club director <a href="/wiki/George_Allison" title="George Allison">George Allison</a>: "The team's played out, Mr Allison, we must rebuild".<sup id="cite_ref-90" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-90">&#91;90&#93;</a></sup> Chapman started the process, signing <a href="/wiki/Ray_Bowden" title="Ray Bowden">Ray Bowden</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pat_Beasley" title="Pat Beasley">Pat Beasley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Dunne" title="Jimmy Dunne">Jimmy Dunne</a>, and had converted the young <a href="/wiki/George_Male" title="George Male">George Male</a> from left half to right back.,<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">&#91;91&#93;</a></sup> Chapman would not live to see the end of the season, let alone complete the task of rebuilding his side. Arsenal went into <a href="/wiki/1933-34_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1933-34 in English football">1933–34</a> looking to retain the title, and started consistently; they worked their way to the top of the league and were a comfortable four points clear after a goalless draw with <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_City_F.C." title="Birmingham City F.C.">Birmingham City</a> on 30 December 1933. This proved to be Chapman's last match in charge.<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler64_87-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler64-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Death">Death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Chapman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Herbert_Chapman_grave.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Herbert_Chapman_grave.jpg/120px-Herbert_Chapman_grave.jpg" decoding="async" width="120" height="90" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Herbert_Chapman_grave.jpg/180px-Herbert_Chapman_grave.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Herbert_Chapman_grave.jpg/240px-Herbert_Chapman_grave.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2333" data-file-height="1750" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Herbert_Chapman_grave.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Chapman is buried in <a href="/wiki/St_Mary%27s_Churchyard,_Hendon" title="St Mary&#39;s Churchyard, Hendon">St Mary's Churchyard, Hendon</a>, alongside his wife Annie, who died in 1958.</div></div></div>
<p>Chapman celebrated New Year in London before travelling north on a scouting trip to see <a href="/wiki/Bury_F.C." title="Bury F.C.">Bury</a> play <a href="/wiki/Notts_County_F.C." title="Notts County F.C.">Notts County</a> on 1 January 1934.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">&#91;92&#93;</a></sup> The following day, he travelled to his native Yorkshire to watch <a href="/wiki/Sheffield_Wednesday_F.C." title="Sheffield Wednesday F.C.">Sheffield Wednesday</a>, Arsenal's next opponents, before spending a final night in his home town of Kiveton Park.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">&#91;93&#93;</a></sup> He returned to London nursing a <a href="/wiki/Common_cold" title="Common cold">cold</a> but was well enough to watch an Arsenal third team match against <a href="/wiki/Guildford_City_F.C." title="Guildford City F.C.">Guildford City</a>. Soon afterwards, his illness suddenly worsened; <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">pneumonia</a> set in, and Chapman quickly succumbed. He died in the early hours of 6 January 1934 at his home in <a href="/wiki/Hendon" title="Hendon">Hendon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-soartyler64_87-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soartyler64-87">&#91;87&#93;</a></sup> He was buried four days later in <a href="/wiki/St_Mary%27s_Churchyard,_Hendon" title="St Mary&#39;s Churchyard, Hendon">St Mary's Churchyard, Hendon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-chrishobbs_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-chrishobbs-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chapman left behind a widow, Annie, two sons, <a href="/w/index.php?title=K_H_Chapman&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="K H Chapman (page does not exist)">Ken</a> (born 1908) and Bruce (born 1911), and two daughters, Molly (born 1915) and Joyce (born 1919). Ken was a <a href="/wiki/Rugby_union" title="Rugby union">rugby union</a> player for <a href="/wiki/Harlequin_F.C." title="Harlequin F.C.">Harlequins</a>, and later served as president of the <a href="/wiki/Rugby_Football_Union" title="Rugby Football Union">Rugby Football Union</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-familyhistory_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-familyhistory-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Chapman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Arsenal_FC_v_Everton_FC,_24_Oct_2015_-_17.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Arsenal_FC_v_Everton_FC%2C_24_Oct_2015_-_17.JPG/200px-Arsenal_FC_v_Everton_FC%2C_24_Oct_2015_-_17.JPG" decoding="async" width="200" height="267" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Arsenal_FC_v_Everton_FC%2C_24_Oct_2015_-_17.JPG/300px-Arsenal_FC_v_Everton_FC%2C_24_Oct_2015_-_17.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Arsenal_FC_v_Everton_FC%2C_24_Oct_2015_-_17.JPG/400px-Arsenal_FC_v_Everton_FC%2C_24_Oct_2015_-_17.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3000" data-file-height="4000" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Arsenal_FC_v_Everton_FC,_24_Oct_2015_-_17.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Statue of Chapman outside the Emirates Stadium</div></div></div> 
<p>Chapman was one of the first football managers in the modern sense of the word, taking full charge of the team, rather than letting board members pick the side. As well as his tactical innovations, he was also a strong believer in physical fitness in football – he instituted a strict training regime and the use of <a href="/wiki/Physiotherapist" class="mw-redirect" title="Physiotherapist">physiotherapists</a> and <a href="/wiki/Masseur" class="mw-redirect" title="Masseur">masseurs</a>. He encouraged his players to openly discuss tactics and the game, instituting weekly team meetings at his clubs, and encouraged them to socialise in extra-curricular activities such as <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">golf</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mightyleeds_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mightyleeds-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> He wrote regularly on football for the <i><a href="/wiki/Sunday_Express" class="mw-redirect" title="Sunday Express">Sunday Express</a></i> newspaper, and a collection of his writings was published after his death in a book, entitled <i>Herbert Chapman on Football</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-94" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-94">&#91;94&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Unlike many of his contemporaries in Britain, Chapman was a fan of the continental game and counted among his friends <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Meisl" title="Hugo Meisl">Hugo Meisl</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jimmy_Hogan" title="Jimmy Hogan">Jimmy Hogan</a>, coaches of the <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">Austrian</a> "<a href="/wiki/Wunderteam" title="Wunderteam">Wunderteam</a>" of the 1930s.<sup id="cite_ref-95" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-95">&#91;95&#93;</a></sup> As long ago as 1909, he had taken his Northampton side on a tour of Germany to play <a href="/wiki/1._FC_N%C3%BCrnberg" title="1. FC Nürnberg">1. FC Nürnberg</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-96" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-96">&#91;96&#93;</a></sup> and at Arsenal he had instituted an ongoing series of home-and-away friendlies against the likes of <a href="/wiki/Racing_Club_de_Paris" class="mw-redirect" title="Racing Club de Paris">Racing Club de Paris</a>. Chapman had proposed a Europe-wide club competition more than twenty years before the <a href="/wiki/UEFA_Champions_League" title="UEFA Champions League">European Cup</a> was instituted, and regularly took his teams abroad to play foreign sides.<sup id="cite_ref-97" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-97">&#91;97&#93;</a></sup> He was one of the first managers to consider signing black and foreign players; as well as signing <a href="/wiki/Walter_Tull" title="Walter Tull">Walter Tull</a>, one of the first black professionals in the game, for Northampton Town in 1911,<sup id="cite_ref-tull_98-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tull-98">&#91;98&#93;</a></sup> he attempted to recruit <a href="/wiki/Austria_national_football_team" title="Austria national football team">Austrian international</a> goalkeeper <a href="/wiki/Rudy_Hiden" class="mw-redirect" title="Rudy Hiden">Rudy Hiden</a> for Arsenal in 1930, but was blocked by the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Labour_(United_Kingdom)" title="Ministry of Labour (United Kingdom)">Ministry of Labour</a>, after protests from the <a href="/wiki/Players%27_Union" title="Players&#39; Union">Players' Union</a> and the Football League.<sup id="cite_ref-hiden_99-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiden-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup> He did however succeed in signing <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Keyser" class="mw-redirect" title="Gerard Keyser">Gerard Keyser</a>, the first Dutchman to play English league football, as an amateur the same year, and Hiden was signed by Jimmy Hogan for Racing Club de Paris.<sup id="cite_ref-hiden_99-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-hiden-99">&#91;99&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>After attending a night-time match in Belgium in 1930 with his friend Hugo Meisl, Chapman became an early advocate of <a href="/wiki/Floodlights_(sport)" class="mw-redirect" title="Floodlights (sport)">floodlights</a>. He had lights installed in Highbury's new West Stand when it was constructed in 1932; however, they were only used for training, and Arsenal would have to wait until the 1950s for their officially sanctioned use in matches.<sup id="cite_ref-arsenal_innovations_100-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arsenal_innovations-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup> Chapman oversaw much of the development of Highbury in the early 1930s, including the building of the West Stand and the addition of a clock which was eventually placed by the south terrace, giving it the name of the "Clock End".<sup id="cite_ref-arsenal_innovations_100-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arsenal_innovations-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup> He is also credited with being behind the renaming of <a href="/wiki/London_Underground" title="London Underground">London Underground</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Gillespie_Road" title="Gillespie Road">Gillespie Road</a> station to <a href="/wiki/Arsenal_tube_station" title="Arsenal tube station">Arsenal</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-101" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-101">&#91;101&#93;</a></sup> He even designed the <a href="/wiki/Scoreboard" title="Scoreboard">scoreboard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Turnstile" title="Turnstile">turnstiles</a> at the stadium.<sup id="cite_ref-arsenal_innovations_100-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arsenal_innovations-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Chapman also advocated the use of white <a href="/wiki/Association_football_ball" class="mw-redirect" title="Association football ball">footballs</a> and <a href="/wiki/Squad_number_(association_football)" title="Squad number (association football)">numbered</a> <a href="/wiki/Football_shirt" class="mw-redirect" title="Football shirt">shirts</a>, as well as adding hoops to Arsenal's socks to make it easier for players to pick each other out.<sup id="cite_ref-arsenal_innovations_100-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-arsenal_innovations-100">&#91;100&#93;</a></sup> He later made a further change to Arsenal's kit, adding white sleeves to the previously all-red shirt and brightening the colour, before a match against <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_F.C." title="Liverpool F.C.">Liverpool</a> on 4 March 1933; the same kit theme of red with white sleeves or trim survives to this day.<sup id="cite_ref-kitdesign_102-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kitdesign-102">&#91;102&#93;</a></sup> The tradition of both teams walking out together at the FA Cup Final was started in 1930 due to Herbert Chapman's involvement with both clubs, and has continued since.<sup id="cite_ref-103" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-103">&#91;103&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honours">Honours</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Herbert_Chapman&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Honours">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Herbert_Chapman_blue_plaque.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Herbert_Chapman_blue_plaque.jpg/250px-Herbert_Chapman_blue_plaque.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="168" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Herbert_Chapman_blue_plaque.jpg/375px-Herbert_Chapman_blue_plaque.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Herbert_Chapman_blue_plaque.jpg/500px-Herbert_Chapman_blue_plaque.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1296" data-file-height="872" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Herbert_Chapman_blue_plaque.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a> <a href="/wiki/Blue_plaque" title="Blue plaque">blue plaque</a> outside Chapman's former residence in <a href="/wiki/Hendon" title="Hendon">Hendon</a>, the first such plaque to commemorate a football player or manager</div></div></div>
<p>Although he did not win any major honours as a player, as a manager Chapman won a Southern League title in <a href="/wiki/1908-09_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1908-09 in English football">1908–09</a> with Northampton Town, four Football League titles (<a href="/wiki/1923-24_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1923-24 in English football">1923–24</a> and <a href="/wiki/1924-25_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1924-25 in English football">1924–25</a> with Huddersfield Town, <a href="/wiki/1930-31_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1930-31 in English football">1930–31</a> and <a href="/wiki/1932-33_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1932-33 in English football">1932–33</a> with Arsenal) and two FA Cups (<a href="/wiki/1922-23_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1922-23 in English football">1922–23</a> with Huddersfield Town, <a href="/wiki/1929-30_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1929-30 in English football">1929–30</a> with Arsenal). After his death the team he had built at Arsenal, under his successors <a href="/wiki/Joseph_E._Shaw" class="mw-redirect" title="Joseph E. Shaw">Joe Shaw</a> and <a href="/wiki/George_Allison" title="George Allison">George Allison</a>, went on to win the <a href="/wiki/1933-34_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1933-34 in English football">1933–34</a> and <a href="/wiki/1934-35_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="1934-35 in English football">1934–35</a> titles, emulating his Huddersfield Town team by completing a hat-trick. No team was to repeat the feat until <a href="/wiki/Liverpool_F.C." title="Liverpool F.C.">Liverpool</a> between 1982 and 1984.<sup id="cite_ref-threeinarow_65-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-threeinarow-65">&#91;65&#93;</a></sup> Chapman was honorary president of Scottish amateur football club <a href="/wiki/Chirnside_F.C." title="Chirnside F.C.">Chirnside United</a> until his death in 1934.<sup id="cite_ref-104" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-104">&#91;104&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 2003, Chapman was inducted into the <a href="/wiki/English_Football_Hall_of_Fame" title="English Football Hall of Fame">English Football Hall of Fame</a> in recognition of his impact as a manager.<sup id="cite_ref-105" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-105">&#91;105&#93;</a></sup> An <a href="/wiki/English_Heritage" title="English Heritage">English Heritage</a> <a href="/wiki/Blue_plaque" title="Blue plaque">blue plaque</a> commemorating Chapman was unveiled in March 2005, at the house in <a href="/wiki/Hendon" title="Hendon">Hendon</a> where Chapman lived from 1926 until his death. Chapman was the first footballer or football manager to be commemorated in this way by English Heritage.<sup id="cite_ref-blueplaque_106-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blueplaque-106">&#91;106&#93;</a></sup> In 2004, on the seventieth anniversary of his death, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times" title="The Sunday Times">The Sunday Times</a></i> named him the greatest British manager of all time in a poll.<sup id="cite_ref-107" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-107">&#91;107&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In tribute to his achievements at Arsenal, a bronze <a href="/wiki/Bust_(sculpture)" title="Bust (sculpture)">bust</a> of Chapman, sculpted by <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Epstein" title="Jacob Epstein">Jacob Epstein</a> resided inside the marble halls of the East Stand of <a href="/wiki/Arsenal_Stadium" title="Arsenal Stadium">Arsenal Stadium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Highbury" title="Highbury">Highbury</a> until its closure in 2006 and will be reinstated there once redevelopment work in the stadium is completed.<sup id="cite_ref-bust_108-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bust-108">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup> A replica sits in the Directors' Entrance at <a href="/wiki/Emirates_Stadium" title="Emirates Stadium">Emirates Stadium</a>; he is one of only two Arsenal managers to be honoured this way, the other being <a href="/wiki/Ars%C3%A8ne_Wenger" title="Arsène Wenger">Arsène Wenger</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-bust_108-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bust-108">&#91;108&#93;</a></sup> Furthermore, Arsenal's white away kit for the <a href="/wiki/2007-08_in_English_football" class="mw-redirect" title="2007-08 in English football">2007–08</a> season was dedicated to Chapman and his achievements.<sup id="cite_ref-kittribute_109-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kittribute-109">&#91;109&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Huddersfield Town were presented with a replica of Chapman's bust by Arsenal, to celebrate their centenary in 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">&#91;110&#93;</a></sup> Additionally, as part of their centennial, Huddersfield also contested the inaugural Herbert Chapman Trophy against Arsenal at the <a href="/wiki/Galpharm_Stadium" class="mw-redirect" title="Galpharm Stadium">Galpharm Stadium</a> on 6 August 2008, which Arsenal won 2–1.<sup id="cite_ref-111" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-111">&#91;111&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 9 December 2011, Arsenal unveiled a statue of Chapman outside Emirates Stadium in celebration of the club's 125th anniversary. The club also unveiled statues of former players <a href="/wiki/Tony_Adams_(footballer)" class="mw-redirect" title="Tony Adams (footballer)">Tony Adams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thierry_Henry" title="Thierry Henry">Thierry Henry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-112" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-112">&#91;112&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
