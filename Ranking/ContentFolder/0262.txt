<p><b>Croatian names</b> follow complex and unique lettering, structuring, composition, and naming customs that have considerable similarities with most other European name systems, and with those of other <a href="/wiki/Slavic_peoples" class="mw-redirect" title="Slavic peoples">Slavic peoples</a> in particular.
</p><p>Upon the Croatian populace's arrival on what is currently modern-day continental Croatia in the early 7th century, <a href="/wiki/Croats" title="Croats">Croats</a> used <a href="/wiki/Slavic_names" title="Slavic names">Slavic names</a> and corresponding naming customs. With modernization and globalization in the last century, <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given names</a> and <a href="/wiki/Surname" title="Surname">surnames</a> have expanded past typical Slavic traditionalism and have included <a href="/wiki/Croatian_Americans" title="Croatian Americans">borrowed names</a> from all over the world. However, although given names vary from region to region in Croatia and can be heavily influenced by other countries' names, surnames tend to be Slavic. Croatian names usually, but not always, consist of a given name, followed by a <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a>; however certain names follow naming customs that diverge from the norm. Naming customs have been a part of Croatian culture for over 400 years.
</p><p>Historically, <a href="/wiki/Croatian_nobility" title="Croatian nobility">Croatian royalty</a> were all given traditional titles of nobility designating them with certain privileges and social standing; the titles were usually followed by the full name or simply their surname. In modern-day society, families all over the country use <a href="/wiki/Honorific" title="Honorific">honorific titles</a> when speaking to family members who are older, or as a sign of general respect. Traditionally only close friends or direct family members address themselves by their first names.
</p>

<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Croatian_nobility" title="Croatian nobility">Croatian nobility</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_noble_families_of_Croatia" title="List of noble families of Croatia">List of noble families of Croatia</a></div>
<p>During 925 - 1102, the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Croatia_(925%E2%80%931102)" title="Kingdom of Croatia (925–1102)">Kingdom of Croatia</a>'s nobility had various titles and forms of address that varied from region to region and position to position. The King of Croatia was afforded the right of choosing his royal handle, for example in 1941, <a href="/wiki/Prince_Aimone,_Duke_of_Aosta" title="Prince Aimone, Duke of Aosta">Prince Adimone, Duke of Aosta</a>, took the name of <a href="/wiki/Prince_Aimone,_Duke_of_Aosta" title="Prince Aimone, Duke of Aosta">King Tomislav II</a> upon his succession to the Croatian throne. Titles were exclusive to members of the King's High Court and included the Queen consort and the following:
</p>
<ol><li>Duke (the highest ranking a noble could receive in his majesty's court)</li>
<li>Marquess (a lineage rank through European peerage)</li>
<li>Count (a successive rank to the Marquess)</li>
<li>Baron (title of honor bestowed on a civilian whose actions warranted the title)</li></ol>
<p>The titles were usually followed by the full name or more commonly by their surname.
</p>

Titles are also bestowed on members of the <a href="/wiki/Judiciary_of_Croatia" title="Judiciary of Croatia">Croatian Judiciary</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Honorific_titles">Honorific titles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Honorific titles">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It is common etiquette in Croatia to address members of society with <a href="/wiki/Honorific" title="Honorific">honorific titles</a> as a sign of respect and societal distance. It is only with close friends or direct family members that first names are used. Honorific titles include the following and are usually followed by the surname of the addressed.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<ol><li>Gospodin (corresponding to Mr. or Sir in English and Monsieur in French)</li>
<li>Gospođa (corresponding to Ms. or Mrs. in English and Madame in French)</li>
<li>Gospođica (corresponding to Miss in English and Mademoiselle in French)</li></ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Croatian_given_names">Croatian given names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Croatian given names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Since their 7th century arrival in today's homeland, <a href="/wiki/Croats" title="Croats">Croats</a> have used <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic</a> names. Through the following centuries, foreign names were also accepted, especially those that mark <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christian</a> faith. However, <a href="/wiki/Slavic_names" title="Slavic names">Slavic names</a> remained dominant until the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">Council of Trent</a> (1545–63) when the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic church</a> decided that every Christian should have Christian name instead of native one. This lasted until the 19th century, when Croats again started to use neglected traditional names—especially those of mediæval Croatian <a href="/wiki/Kings_of_Croatia" class="mw-redirect" title="Kings of Croatia">kings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dukes_of_Croatia" class="mw-redirect" title="Dukes of Croatia">dukes</a>. More recently, as a result of globalization, unusual and exotic names of various cultures have also gained in wide spread popularity.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Frequency">Frequency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Frequency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Borrowed_or_foreign_names">Borrowed or foreign names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Borrowed or foreign names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to globalization and remnants of historical significance (i.e. <a href="/wiki/Croatia%E2%80%93Italy_relations" title="Croatia–Italy relations">Croatia–Italy relations</a>, <a href="/wiki/Illyrian_Provinces" title="Illyrian Provinces">Illyrian Provincial nationalism</a>, etc.) many people in Croatia have American, French, Swedish, Finnish, German, Italian and English first names (given names). However, due to the alphabetical limitation of the <a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">Croatian language</a> many names take on new pronunciations, are respelled, or are restructured to comply with the country's naming customs. Uncharacteristic names are never <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">declined</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">Croatian language</a>, and by nationality of origin include: (<a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American</a>): Thomas, Charles, Max, Jacob, William, Isabella, Emma, Madison, Matthew, Alexander; (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>): Hans, Peter, Stephan, Gerhard, Edith, Gabriele, Monika, Wolfgang, Dennis; (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>): Jean-Louis, Lucus, Marie, Clément, Camille, Baptiste, Léonie, Julien, Françoise<i>,</i> Jeanne; (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>): Alessandro, Andrea, Alessia, Claudia, Christian, Riccardo, Luca, Matteo, Leonardo, Sofia ...
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Croatian_family_names">Croatian family names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Croatian family names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Family names started to appear among Croats in the 12th century. Since the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Trent" title="Council of Trent">Council of Trent</a>, both the given and family names would be written down.
</p>

<p>Croatian family names have five different origins:
</p>
<dl><dt>Given names, <a href="/wiki/Matronymic" title="Matronymic">matronymics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymics</a>&#160;</dt>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_customs">Naming customs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Naming customs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_customs">Northern customs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Northern customs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Naming customs vary region to region in Croatia, and differ slightly from that of typical naming customs, such a <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_name" title="Portuguese name">Brazilian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_name" title="Portuguese name">Portuguese</a> customs; Croatian naming customs closely mimic that of <a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions" title="Roman naming conventions">Roman naming conventions</a>.
</p><p>For instance in the <a href="/wiki/Brod-Posavina_County" title="Brod-Posavina County">Brod-Posavina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Slavonski_Brod" title="Slavonski Brod">Slavonski Brod</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dubrovnik" title="Dubrovnik">Dubrovnik</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pazin" title="Pazin">Pazin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krapina" title="Krapina">Krapina</a>, <a href="/wiki/Osijek" title="Osijek">Osijek</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Karlovac_County" title="Karlovac County">Karlovac</a> regions, naming traditionally, but not always, follows this structure:
</p><p>Example: If "Darko Stevnich Horatio Horvat" is the full legal name of a resident of <a href="/wiki/Brod-Posavina_County" title="Brod-Posavina County">Brod-Posavina</a> tradition would dictate that his family name would be "Horatio" and not the assumed "Horvat" (which is another <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>, usually named after the family's <a href="/wiki/Patriarch" title="Patriarch">patriarch</a>); this person would go by "Darko (given name) Horatio (family name)".<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Southern_customs">Southern customs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Croatian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Southern customs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In selected regions of <a href="/wiki/Me%C4%91imurje_County" title="Međimurje County">Međimurje</a> and <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0ibenik" title="Šibenik">Šibenik</a>, naming custom diverge again. Many residents traditionally go by their middle names, and reserve the full declaration of their names for formal occasions such as court, marriage or death.
</p><p>Example: If "Kolinada Blaga Lončar" is the full legal name of a resident of one of these regions, she could choose to go by, and legally declare her name as "Blaga Lončar."
</p><p>In this region of the country usually the following mechanism is used in naming, one that has been in practice for over four centuries:<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>  
</p>
<ol><li>The oldest son is named after the father's father.</li>
<li>The oldest daughter is named after the father's mother.</li>
<li>The second oldest son is named after the mother's father.</li>
<li>The second oldest daughter is named after the mother's mother.</li></ol>
<p>Other offspring of father are either named after favorite aunts or uncles or, sometimes, after the saint of the day they were born.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
