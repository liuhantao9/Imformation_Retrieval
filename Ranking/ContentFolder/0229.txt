<p>A <b>hypocorism</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;h&#39; in &#39;hi&#39;">h</span><span title="/aɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;tide&#39;">aɪ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;p&#39; in &#39;pie&#39;">p</span><span title="/ɒ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;body&#39;">ɒ</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/ər/: &#39;er&#39; in &#39;letter&#39;">ər</span><span title="/ɪ/: &#39;i&#39; in &#39;kit&#39;">ɪ</span><span title="&#39;z&#39; in &#39;zoom&#39;">z</span><span title="/əm/: &#39;m&#39; in &#39;rhythm&#39;">əm</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling">hy-<span style="font-size:90%">POK</span>-ər-iz-əm</i></a>; from&#32;<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Ancient Greek language">Ancient Greek</a>&#32;<i> </i>ὑποκόρισμα<i> (hypokorisma)</i>, from&#32;<i> </i>ὑποκορίζεσθαι<i> (hypokorizesthai)</i>, meaning 'to use child-talk'<sup id="cite_ref-OED-etym_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-etym-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>), also called a <b>hypocoristicon</b>, is a diminutive form of a name. Hypocorisms include <b>pet names</b> or <b>calling names</b>,<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> often a <a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">diminutive</a> or <a href="/wiki/Augmentative" title="Augmentative">augmentative</a> form of a word or <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> when used as a <a href="/wiki/Nickname" title="Nickname">nickname</a> or <a href="/wiki/Term_of_endearment" title="Term of endearment">term of endearment</a>.
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dutch">Dutch</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypocorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Dutch">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Increasingly, the official form of <a href="/wiki/Dutch_given_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Dutch given name">Dutch given names</a> as registered at birth is one that originally was hypocoristic. The English forms <i>Johnny</i> or <i>Johnnie</i> and <i>Bobby</i> or <i>Bobbie</i> are quite common in the Netherlands.

<p>Also, initials of complex names are often used as the hypocorism, e.g.: <i>Brandon William</i> → <i>B W</i> → <i>B Dub</i>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Finnish">Finnish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypocorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Finnish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">Finland</a> people may have both Finnish and Swedish names. Younger people and females have more international or unique names, whereas older names may also revive. Hypocorisms of short names tend to extend and long names are shortened in multiple ways with certain patterns. Some official names were originally hypocorisms like male names <i>Riku</i> (originally Rikhard), <i>Rami</i> (from Raimo) or <i>Vilppu</i> (originally Greek Filippos, in English Philip). <i>Minttu</i> is a female name in its own right, referring to plant genus <i><a href="/wiki/Mentha" title="Mentha">Mentha</a></i>, as well as it is hypocorism of the name <i>Minna-Maria</i>. Some old names in their official form were actually Swedish like <i>Carl</i> → <i>Kalle</i> or <i>Amanda</i> → <i>Manta</i>. Alphabets like C are foreign to Finnish language.

<p>Some of the hypocoristic names do not derive from first names but last names, like <i>Kaippa</i> from surname Kaivola, <i>Koippari</i> from <a href="/wiki/Tomi_Koivusaari" title="Tomi Koivusaari">Koivusaari</a>, <i>Hissu</i> from <a href="/wiki/Kari_Hietalahti" title="Kari Hietalahti">Hietalahti</a> <i>Holle</i> from Holopainen, <i>Vode</i> from Voutilainen or <i>Purtsi</i> from Pursiainen. This style is more common to men's names.
</p><p>Also, hypocorisms occur in common language, and "-is" is a very usual ending added. Metro station is <i>metroasema</i> → <i>metris</i>, Coca-Cola → <i>Kokis</i>, <a href="/wiki/T%C3%B6%C3%B6l%C3%B6_Sports_Hall" title="Töölö Sports Hall">Töölö Sports Hall</a> i.e. <i>Kisahalli</i> → <i>Kisis</i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Hakaniemi" title="Hakaniemi">Hakaniemi</a></i> → <i>Hakis</i> or <i>Hagis</i>. Another common ending is "-ari". Examples: <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle" title="Motorcycle">motorcycle</a> is <i>moottoripyörä</i> → <i>motskari</i>, <a href="/wiki/Living_room" title="Living room">living room</a> is <i>olohuone</i> → <i>olkkari</i>, <a href="/wiki/Shopping_mall" title="Shopping mall">shoping center</a> is <i>ostoskeskus</i> → <i>ostari</i>, <a href="/wiki/Vacuum_flask" title="Vacuum flask">vacuum flask</a> is <i>termospullo</i> → <i>termari</i>, and <a href="/wiki/Swimsuit" title="Swimsuit">swimsuit</a> is <i>uimapuku</i> → <i>uikkari</i>. <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">Television</a> is <i>televisio</i> → <i>telkkari</i>, but there are a lot of other hypocorisms for that appliance as well, like <i>telkka</i>, <i>telkku</i>, <i>telsu</i>, <i>töllö</i>, <i>töllötin</i> etc. The word <i>töllötin</i> comes from the verb <i>töllöttää</i>, meaning to gawp. That's the same as in German Die Glotze (TV-set) comes from ther verb glotzen. One of the endings is -(t)ski, -(t)sku. <a href="/wiki/Ice_cream" title="Ice cream">Ice cream</a> is <i>jäätelö</i> → <i>jätski</i>, <a href="/wiki/Art_museum" title="Art museum">art museum</a> <a href="/wiki/Ateneum" title="Ateneum">Ateneum</a> → <i>Atski</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Boat" title="Boat">boat</a> (in Swedish <i>båt</i>) is <i>laiva</i> or <i>vene</i> → <i>botski</i> (also <i>paatti</i>), <a href="/wiki/Zipper" title="Zipper">zipper</a> is <i>vetoketju</i> → <i>vetsku</i>, <i>vetskari</i>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="French">French</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypocorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: French">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Informal French</i> has a number of diminutive nicknames, although not as systematically as in English.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>, for both male and female names, hypocorisms are most commonly formed by dropping the last syllable:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Catherine</i> → <i>Cathy</i></li>
<li><i>Christelle</i> → <i>Chris</i></li>
<li><i>Christophe</i> → <i>Chris</i></li>
<li><i>Frédéric</i>, <i>Frédérique</i> → <i>Fred</i></li>
<li><i>Grégory</i>, <i>Grégoire</i> → <i>Greg</i></li>
<li><i>Jean-Michel</i> → <i>Jean-Mi</i></li>
<li><i>Joannie</i> → <i>Jo</i></li>
<li><i>Marie-Charlotte</i> → <i>Macha</i></li>
<li><i>Marie-Josée</i> → <i>Marie-Jo</i></li>
<li><i>Maxime</i> → <i>Max</i></li>
<li><i>Michaël</i> → <i>Mic</i></li>
<li><i>Philippe</i> → <i>Phil</i></li>
<li><i>Stéphane</i> → <i>Steph</i></li>
<li><i>Stéphanie</i> → <i>Steph</i></li></ul>
<p>Dropping the first syllable is also attested:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Christophe</i> → <i>Tophe</i></li></ul>
<p>Sometimes, only central syllables are kept:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Augustin</i> → <i>Gus</i></li>
<li><i>Emmanuel</i> → <i>Manu</i></li>
<li><i>Emmanuelle</i> → <i>Manu</i></li></ul>
<p>Another method commonly used is doubling one syllable of the name:
</p>
<ul><li><i>André</i> → <i>Dédé</i></li>
<li><i>Annie</i> → <i>Nini</i></li>
<li><i>Augustin</i> → <i>Tintin</i></li>
<li><i>Christine</i> → <i>Kiki</i>, <i>Kikine</i></li>
<li><i>Christophe</i> → <i>Totophe</i></li>
<li><i>Joseph</i> → <i>Jojo</i></li>
<li><i>Julie</i> → <i>Juju</i></li>
<li><i>Louise</i> → <i>Loulou</i></li>
<li>all female names ending in <i>-tine</i> → <i>Titine</i></li></ul>
<p>For male names, the ending <i>-ot</i> is attested, although its use is rather dated:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Charles</i> → <i>Charlot</i></li>
<li><i>Jean</i> → <i>Jeannot</i></li>
<li><i>Jules</i> → <i>Julot</i></li>
<li><i>Pierre</i> → <i>Pierrot</i></li></ul>
<p>It was also sometimes (but rarely) used for females:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Marguerite</i> → <i>Margot</i></li></ul>
<p>The ending <i>-et</i> for males was used around the Renaissance, and is now obsolete:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Henri</i> → <i>Henriquet</i></li>
<li><i>Jacques</i> → <i>Jacquet</i></li></ul>
<p>For female names, the ending <i>-ette</i> was used in the first half of the 20th century, and even often given as the official name:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Anne</i> → <i>Annette</i></li>
<li><i>Jeanne</i> → <i>Jeannette</i></li>
<li><i>Marie</i> → <i>Mariette</i></li>
<li><i>Paule</i> → <i>Paulette</i></li></ul>
<p>Some names in <i>-ette</i> are not actual hypocorisms, but the only existing feminized form of a male name:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Antoine</i> (male) → <i>Antoinette</i> (female)</li>
<li><i>Pierre</i> (male) → <i>Pierrette</i> (female)</li>
<li><i>Nicolas</i> (male) → <i>Nicolette</i> (female) (rare and dated) → <i>Colette</i></li></ul>
<p>The ending <i>-on</i> is rarer, often dated or obsolete, used for both genders:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Antoinette</i> → <i>Toinon</i></li>
<li><i>Françoise</i> → <i>Fanchon</i></li>
<li><i>Henri</i> → <i>Riton</i></li>
<li><i>Marie</i> → <i>Marion</i></li>
<li><i>Louis</i> → <i>Louison</i></li>
<li><i>Louise</i> → <i>Louison</i></li></ul>
<p>The ending <i>-ou</i> is also rare:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Anne</i> → <i>Nanou</i></li></ul>
<p>A special case is the ending in <i>-ick</i>/ <i>-ic</i>, which is the French writing for the hypocoristic form in <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">Breton</a> "-ig", used for both genders. The "-ig" form in Breton means "Little ...". This diminutive, in its French form of "ick" or "ic", became in vogue for official names in the second half of the 20th century:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Annick</i> (original in Breton: Anna<i>ig</i>), from <i>Anne</i></li>
<li><i>Soizic</i> (original: Soaz<i>ig</i>), from <i>Frañsoaz</i>, the Breton writing for the French "Françoise"</li>
<li><i>Loïc</i>, probably from the French <i>Louis</i></li>
<li><i>Yannick</i> (original: Yann<i>ig</i>), from <i>Yann</i>, meaning "John" in Breton</li></ul>
<p>In Breton, the diminutive form "...ig" can be given to any kind of names, nouns or adjectives, (<i>un tammig</i>, a few), while in French it relates only to given names.
</p><p>The name <i>Soazig</i> shows more than the ending "ig". Often in Breton a hypocoristic form of a given name can be made by putting away the first syllable. "Frañsoaz" becomes a familiar "Soaz" then, given to a child, the name is "Soazig", but not as an official name. This is also a difference between French and Breton: the diminutive ending "...ig" in Breton is only used as a temporary form for young children, while "...ick" is official and permanent in French names, and has lost his sense of a diminutive.
</p><p>For words, French often produces hypocorisms either by truncating a word after the letter <i>o</i>, or by chopping off the end of the word and adding an <i>o</i>: <i>McDo</i> from <i>McDonald's</i>; <i>gynéco</i> from <i>gynécologue</i>; <i>dico</i> from <i>dictionnaire</i>; <i>dodo</i> (childish word for sleep, from <i>dormir</i>, to sleep); <i>écolo</i> from <i>écologiste</i>; <i>coco</i> from communiste; <i>catho</i> from <i>catholique</i>; <i>psycho</i> from <i>psychologie</i>.
</p><p>The ending -oche (with or without an intervening consonant or phoneme to make it easier to pronounce) is also sometimes used: <i>téloche</i> (telly, from <span style="padding-right:0.1em;"><i>télévision</i></span>), <i>cinoche</i> (<span style="padding-right:0.1em;"><i>cinéma</i></span>), <i>MacDoche</i> (<span style="padding-right:0.1em;"><i>McDonald's</i></span>), <i>fastoche</i> (easy-peezy, from <i>facile</i>, easy). Words or names may also be shortened or abbreviated without an O: <i>fixs</i> from <i>fixations</i>, 'ski bindings'; <i>Jean-Phi</i> from Jean-Philippe; <i>amphi</i> from amphithéatre (large classroom or lecture hall); <i>ciné</i> (another informal word for <span style="padding-right:0.1em;"><i>cinéma</i></span>). These words are familiar/informal versions of the underlying words.
</p><p>The connotation of familiarity (my friend <i>Jean-Phi</i>, as opposed to my new colleague <i>Jean-Philippe</i>; <i>cinoche</i>, the place I often go for entertainment, as opposed to <i>cinéma</i>, the neutral word for a movie theater) is what makes them hypocorisms.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="German">German</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypocorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: German">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hypocorisms of first names are commonly based on truncation, only keeping the first (<i>Max</i>) or last (<i>Hans</i>) syllable(s), sometimes in contracted form as these examples show. Often the ending <i>-i</i> is added to these truncated nameforms. The name might also undergo a sound shift (<i>Sepp</i>). Further diminutives can be added with the suffixes <i>-lein</i>, <i>-(e)l</i> or <i>-chen</i>, e.&#160;g. <i>Gretel</i> or <i>Gretchen</i> as a diminutive for <i>Grete</i>.
</p>
<ul><li><i>Anna</i>, <i>Anne</i> → <i>Anni</i>, <i>Änni</i></li>
<li><i>Angela</i>, <i>Angelika</i> → <i>Geli, Angie (with an English-leaning pronunciation)</i></li>

<li><i>Gabriela</i>, <i>Gabriele</i> → <i>Gaby</i></li>
<li><i>Georg</i> → <i>Schorsch</i> (mostly Bavarian and Swiss German)</li>
<li><i>Helene</i> → <i>Leni</i></li>
<li><i>Ignatz</i>, <i>Ignaz</i> → <i>Nazi</i> (Bavarian; out of use), <i>Nazl</i> (mostly Bavarian German, still in use)</li>
<li><i>Johanna → Hanna</i></li>
<li><i>Johannes</i> → <i>Hannes</i> → <i>Hans</i> → <i>Hansi</i></li>
<li><i>Josef</i>, <i>Joseph</i> → <i>Sepp</i> (Bavarian), <i>Jupp</i> (Rhinelandic), <i>Pepi</i></li>
<li><i>Karl</i> → <i>Kalle</i>, <i>Kalli</i>, <i>Karli</i></li>
<li><i>Katharina</i> → <i>Kathi,</i></li>

<li><i>Maximilian</i> → <i>Max</i></li>

<p>Sometimes female names may have the ending <i>-el</i> instead of <i>-i</i>, or any other shortening, especially in southern Germany:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Barbara</i> → <i>Babsi</i> → <i>Bärbel</i></li>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Icelandic">Icelandic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypocorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Icelandic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Italian">Italian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypocorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Italian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the Italian language there are various ways of composing a hypocoristic:
</p>
<ul><li>Doubling of an internal syllable to the name; this phenomenon is typical of childhood language:
<ul><li><i>An<b>to</b>nio</i> → <i>Totò</i>; <i>Salva<b>to</b>re</i> → <i>Totò</i>; <i>Al<b>fo</b>nso</i> → <i>Fofò</i></li></ul></li>
<li>Fall of all the proton syllables of the name, that is, those that precede the tonic accent:
<ul><li><i>Alberto</i> → <i>Berto</i>; <i>Vincenzo</i> → <i>Enzo</i>; <i>Giovanni</i> → <i>Vanni</i>; <i>Matteo</i> → <i>Teo</i>; <i>Francesco</i> → <i>Cesco</i></li></ul></li>
<li>Truncation of syllables following the tonic accent, usually used to address the person directly:
<ul><li><i>Andrea</i> → <i>Andrè</i>; <i>Giulia</i> → <i>Giù</i>; <i>Antonio</i> → <i>Antò</i></li></ul></li>
<li>Doubling of the final syllable of the name, using the last consonant as initial:
<ul><li><i>Giuseppe</i> → <i>Beppe</i>; <i>Luigi</i> → <i>Gigi</i>; <i>Daniele</i> → <i>Lele</i></li></ul></li>
<li>Contraction of the name, due to the fall of one or more internal consonants and vowels:
<ul><li><i>Giovanni</i> → <i>Gianni</i>; <i>Durante</i> → <i>Dante</i></li></ul></li>
<li>The contraction is widespread even in the case of double or compound names:
<ul><li><i>Maria Luisa</i> → <i>Marisa</i>; <i>Maria Gabriella</i> → <i>Mariella</i>; <i>Pier Luigi</i> → <i>Pigi</i></li></ul></li>
<li>Truncation to the first or second syllable of the name, with displacement of the tonic accent on the penultimate:
<ul><li><i>Daniele</i> → <i>Dani</i>; <i>Simonetta</i> → <i>Simo</i>;</li></ul></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese">Japanese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypocorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Japanese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, diminutive names are made by adding an <a href="/wiki/Japanese_honorifics" title="Japanese honorifics">honorific suffix</a> to a person's name, or to part of the name.  The suffix <i>-chan</i> is typically added to a girl's name as a term of endearment.  The suffix <i>-kun</i> is added to a male's name.  Outside of family, the suffix <i>-kun</i> typically implies a relationship between an authority (the caller) and a subordinate.  Thus, it is often used by teachers calling on male students, and a boss or supervisor calling on male employees.
</p><p>The term <i>-chan</i> is occasionally added to the name of an effeminate boy or man.  While the addition of <i>-chan</i> to a girl's name is endearment and intimacy, when applied to a male's name, it may be either a term of endearment or it may be added as a derogatory taunt, depending on the context and the nature of the relationship.
</p>
<dl><dt>Female names</dt></dl>
<ul><li><i>Ayaka</i> → <i>Ayaka-chan</i> → <i>Aya-chan</i></li>
<li><i>Mako</i> → <i>Mako-chan</i> → <i>Mā-chan</i> or <i>Macchan</i></li>
<li><i>Mao</i> → <i>Mā-chan</i></li>
<li><i>Reina</i> → <i>Reina-chan</i> → <i>Rei-chan</i></li></ul>
<dl><dt>Male names</dt></dl>
<ul><li><i>Keita</i> → <i>Keita-kun</i></li>
<li><i>Kentarō</i> → <i>Kentaro-kun</i> → <i>Kenta-kun</i></li>
<li><i>Tarō</i> → <i>Taro-kun</i> → <i>Takkun</i></li>
<li><i>Tatsuya</i> → <i>Tatsuya-kun</i> → <i>Tatsu-kun</i></li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polish">Polish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypocorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Polish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">Polish</a>, there are multiple affixes used to create the diminutive. Some of them are <i>-ka, -sia, -cia, -unia, -enka, -śka, -lka</i> for feminine nouns and <i>-ek, -uś, -ciek, -czek, -uń, -eńki, -lki</i> for masculine (among others). Some of the stems change, particularly to more archaic forms of the name (e.g. <i>Andrzej</i> → <i>Jędrek</i> or <i>Agnieszka</i> → <i>Jagusia</i>). Some masculine names take an <i>-o</i> suffix that is considered archaic form, present in Polish since pagan times. Masculine names occasionally take an <i>-a</i> suffix, which is an archaic Slavic form<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> as in Russian (e.g. <i>Jakub</i> → <i>Kuba</i>). Here is a list of common names with some of them:

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Portuguese">Portuguese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypocorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Portuguese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">Portuguese</a>, abbreviations of the name are common, as are suffixes for diminutive and augmentative. For males, the suffixes <i>-inho</i> (diminutive) and <i>-ão</i> (augmentative) are the most used. In several parts of <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, <i>-inho</i> is informally replaced by <i>-im</i> in diminutive words. The same occurs with hypocorisms as, for example, <i>Luisim</i> instead of <i>Luisinho</i>. For females, <i>-inha</i> (diminutive) is the most used in Portuguese; augmentatives are uncommon. In compound names some mixed forms can occur, such as <i>José Carlos</i> being called <i>Zeca</i>, or <i>Maria Luísa</i> being called <i>Malu</i>.
</p><p>The phenomenon also occurs with terms of address other than personal names; for example, a <i>cachorro</i> or <i>cão</i> (both meaning "dog") can be affectionately called <i>cachorrinho</i> or <i>cãozinho</i> (the most common translations of the English word <a href="/wiki/Puppy" title="Puppy">puppy</a>).

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russian">Russian</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypocorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Russian">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">Russian</a> has a wide variety of diminutive forms for names, to the point that for non-Russian speakers it can be difficult to connect a nickname to the original. Diminutive forms for nouns are usually distinguished with -ик (<i>-ik</i>), -ок (<i>-ok</i>), -ёк (<i>-yok</i>) (masculine gender), -чк-/-шк- (<i>-chk-/-shk-</i>) and -оньк-/-еньк- (<i>-on’k-/-en’k-</i>) suffixes. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Slovene">Slovene</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypocorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Slovene">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">Slovene</a>, diminutives are very common. In many cases they have almost completely replaced their originals (such is the case of Špela for Elizabeta, Branko for Branislav, or Alenka, Majda, and Magda for Magdalena). Especially among female names, the etymological link of the diminutive with the original name has been lost and the diminutive is perceived by most speakers to be a separate name (such is the case for Mojca, deriving from Marija, or Maja and Alenka deriving from Magdalena). In other cases, especially among male names, this link has been kept, but frequently boys are given diminutives as their given names (such as Miha instead of Mihael, Ivo instead of Ivan, or Nejc instead of Jernej etc.)

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Swedish">Swedish</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypocorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Swedish">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<h3><span id="Yiddish_.28and_contemporary_Hebrew.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Yiddish_(and_contemporary_Hebrew)">Yiddish (and contemporary Hebrew)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypocorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Yiddish (and contemporary Hebrew)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As rule of thumb, adding the "suffix" 'le, 'ale, 'ele or sometimes simply an "L" to the name makes it diminutive and endearing, for both female and male names. It is also in common practice in Hebrew, mostly by Ashkenazi Jewish people. In most cases, as the names would be spelled using the Hebrew alphabet, an apostrophe would separate the name from the suffix.
</p>
<ul><li>חיה <i>Chaya</i> → חיה'לה <i>Chayale</i>, <i>Chaya'le</i></li>
<li>משה <i>Moyshe</i> → משה'לה <i>Moyshele</i>, <i>Moyshe'le</i></li>
<li>דוד <i>David</i> → דוד'לה <i>Davidle</i>, <i>David'le</i>, דוד'ל <i>Davidl</i></li>
<li>רינה <i>Rina</i> → רינה'לה <i>Rinale</i>, <i>Rina'le</i></li>
<li>זלדה <i>Zelda</i> → זלדה'לה <i>Zeldale</i>, <i>Zelda'le</i></li>
<li>דב <i>Dov</i> → דב'לה <i>Dovale</i>, <i>Dov'ale</i></li></ul>
<p>It can also work with names which are not predominantly Jewish, for example:
</p>
<ul><li><i>Craig</i> → <i>Craigele</i>, <i>Craigale</i></li>
<li><i>Gerda</i> → <i>Gerdale</i></li></ul>
<p>Another suffix used by Yiddish and Hebrew speakers could be 'ke, 'ka and also "inka"/"yinka":
</p>
<ul><li>דוד <i>David</i> → דוד'קה <i>Davidke</i>, <i>David'ke</i></li>
<li>טל <i>Tal</i> → טלינקה <i>Talinka</i></li>
<li>צבי <i>Zvi</i> → צביקה <i>Zvika</i></li></ul>
<p>Other diminutive and endearing suffixes in common use by Yiddish and Hebrew speakers are "ush", "chook" and "inyu":
</p>
<ul><li>רבקה <i>Rivka</i> → רבקוש <i>Rivkush</i></li>
<li>גיא <i>Guy</i> → גיאוש <i>Guyush</i></li>
<li>דן <i>Dan</i> → דנצ'וק <i>Danchook</i></li>
<li>חן <i>Chen</i> → חנצ'וק <i>Chenchook</i></li>
<li>בר <i>Bar</i> → בריניו <i>Barinyu</i></li></ul>
<p>With single syllable names a form of endearment exists where the name is repeated twice consecutively, or with longer names, the last syllable might be repeated, for example:
</p>
<ul><li>גיא <i>Guy</i> → גיאגיא <i>Guyguy</i></li>
<li>דן <i>Dan</i> → דןדן <i>Dandan</i></li>
<li>טל <i>Tal</i> → טלטל <i>Taltal</i></li>
<li>רון <i>Ron</i> → רונרון <i>Ronron</i></li>
<li>נטלי <i>Natalie</i> → נטלילי <i>Natalilie</i></li></ul>
<p>Many names have their own versions of nicknames specifically for them which are common in Yiddish, or in contemporary Hebrew:
</p>
<ul><li>רפאל <i>Refael</i> → רפי <i>Rafi</i></li>
<li>יוסף <i>Yosef</i> → ספי <i>Sefi</i>, יוסי <i>Yosi</i></li>
<li>אברהם <i>Avraham</i> → אבי <i>Avi</i></li>
<li>אליהו <i>Eliyahu</i> → אלי <i>Eli</i> (The same goes to most names that has the prefix of Eli, like Elimelech, Eliezer, Elisha, etc.)</li>
<li>רבקה <i>Rivka</i> → ריבי <i>Rivi</i> , ריקה <i>Rika</i> , ריקי <i>Riki</i></li>
<li>שמואל <i>Shmuel</i> → שמוליק <i>Shmulik</i> , מולי <i>Muli</i></li>
<li>משה <i>Moshe</i> → מויש <i>Moysh</i> , מוש <i>Mosh</i> , מושון <i>Mushon</i></li>
<li>בנימין <i>Binyamin</i> → בני <i>Beni</i></li>
<li>דוד <i>David</i> → דודו <i>Dudu</i></li>
<li>יחזקאל <i>Yehezkel</i> → חזי <i>Hezi</i></li>
<li>חנה <i>Chana</i> → חני <i>Chani</i></li>
<li>יעקב <i>Ya'akov (Jacob)</i> → קובי <i>Kobi</i></li>
<li>יצחק <i>Yitzhak</i> → איציק <i>Itzik</i></li>
<li>ישראל <i>Israel</i> → שרול <i>Srool</i>, שרוליק <i>Sroolik</i></li>
<li>בלהה <i>Bilha</i> → בילי <i>Bili</i></li>
<li>גאולה <i>Geula</i> → גולי <i>Guli</i></li>
<li>אריאל <i>Ariel → אריק Arik</i></li>
<li>זכריה Zecharia → זכי <i>Zaki</i></li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Yoruba">Yoruba</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hypocorism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Yoruba">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Many Yoruba names are complex and normally can be shortened into many small simple phrases. Many Yoruba names have the common roots of Ade, meaning Crown, Olu, meaning Lord, Ola, meaning Wealth, Ifa, the God of Oracle, and Akin, meaning Valor. Yoruba hypocorisms are often based off these roots or the suffixes. Many include just dropping the first syllable of the name as well.
</p>
<ul><li><i>Abimbola</i> → <i>Bimbo</i></li>
<li><i>Olubukola</i> → <i>Bukky</i>, <i>Bukki</i>, <i>Bukola</i></li>
<li><i>Ololade</i> → <i>Lade</i>, <i>Lolade</i>, <i>Lola</i></li>
<li><i>Abosede</i> → <i>Bose</i>, <i>Bosede</i></li>
<li><i>Folasade</i> → <i>Fola</i>, <i>Ola</i>, <i>Sade</i></li>
<li><i>Oluwadamilola</i> → <i>Dammy</i>, <i>Dami</i></li>
<li><i>Fehintoluwa</i> → <i>Fehin</i>, <i>Tolu</i>, <i>Olu</i></li>
<li><i>Olubode</i> → <i>Bode</i></li>
<li>"Oluwadamilare" → "Dami", "Dare"</li>
<li>"Gbolahan" → "Gbola" "Bola"</li>
<li>"Abisola" → "Bisi", "Bisola"</li>
<li>"Oluwole" → "Wole"</li>
<li>"Akinwande" → "Akin", "Wande"</li>
<li>"Folajinmi" → "Fola", "Jimmy", <i>Jinmi</i></li>
<li>"Olusegun" → "Segun"</li></ul>
