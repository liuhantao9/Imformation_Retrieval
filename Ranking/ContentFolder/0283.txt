<p><b>Indonesian names</b> and naming customs reflect the <a href="/wiki/Multicultural" class="mw-redirect" title="Multicultural">multicultural</a> and <a href="/wiki/Polyglot" class="mw-redirect" title="Polyglot">polyglot</a> nature of the over 17,000 islands in the <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_archipelago" class="mw-redirect" title="Indonesian archipelago">Indonesian archipelago</a>. The world's fourth most populous nation, Indonesia is home to approximately 365 tribal-ethnic groups, each with their own <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">culture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Customs" title="Customs">customs</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">language</a>. The state officially recognises more than 300 of these ethnic groups. The <a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Javanese</a> are the largest single group, comprising around 40 percent of Indonesia's population.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Honorifics">Honorifics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Honorifics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In Indonesia, ranks and professional titles are used. It is also customary to use <i>Pak</i>, <i>Bapak</i> or <i>Saudara</i> to address men and <i>Bu</i>, <i>Saudari</i> or <i>Ibu</i> to address women. <i>Pak</i> and <i>Bapak</i> are literally translated as "father". <i>Bapak</i> is more formal and is used much like the English word, "sir". <i>Saudara</i> (for men) or <i>Saudari</i> (for women) is another term of greater respect and formality. It translates to "kinsman", "lady" or "gentleman". <i>Ibu</i> is literally translated as "mother". It is used as "ma'am" or "lady" would be in English. If addressing a man whose name is unknown, one uses <i>Bapak</i> and if addressing a woman whose name is unknown, one uses <i>Ibu</i>.
</p><p>An informal way to address a significantly older person is to use <i>Om</i>, <i>Paman</i>, <i>Bibi</i> or <i>Tante</i>, which mean "uncle" and "aunt". The terms are Dutch-influenced and quite commonly used in the big cities.
</p><p>Local honorifics continue to be employed throughout Indonesia. In a casual situation, <i>Kakak</i> is used to address a person as an older sister or brother. In <a href="/wiki/Javanese_language" title="Javanese language">Javanese</a> <i>Mbak</i> is used for "older sister" and <i>Mas</i> is used for "older brother". <i>Mbak</i> and <i>Mas</i> are used for example to address junior staff in cafes and restaurants in Jakarta.
</p><p>In Sumatran Malay or Minangkabau cultural spheres, an older brother is addressed as <i>Abang</i>. However, it is also common to address a becak driver, angkot driver, vegetable vendor, fishmonger or hawker with <i>Abang</i>.
</p><p>"Gus" (from <i>bagus</i>) is used exclusively to address an honorable Eastern Javanese person with a strong traditional and religious identity. For example, the Indonesian former president, <a href="/wiki/Abdurrahman_Wahid" title="Abdurrahman Wahid">Abdurrahman Wahid</a>, was often addressed as <i>Gus Dur</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Naming_forms">Naming forms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Naming forms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Legally, Indonesian <a href="/wiki/Personal_name" title="Personal name">personal names</a> are not divided into first and family names. A <a href="/wiki/Mononymous_person" title="Mononymous person">single name</a> is recognized as a full personal name, and the addition further components–such as additional given names, regional or ethnic <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family/clan names</a> or <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymics</a>–is a matter of parents' choice when registering the child's name. Even then, family names or patronymics are just considered part of the full personal name and have no official relevance (for instance, alphabetic ordering of names is always done by the first letter of the full personal name).
</p><p>The majority of Indonesians do not have <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family names</a>. Rather, their given names are geographically and culturally specific. Names beginning with "Su" in Indonesian spelling ("Soe" in <a href="/wiki/Dutch_orthography" title="Dutch orthography">Dutch orthography</a>) or ending with an "o" are usually <a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Javanese people</a>. For example, people called "Suprapto" or "Soeprapto, Joko" are likely to be of Javanese descent. <a href="/wiki/Suharto" title="Suharto">Suharto</a> is another example.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Balinese_name" title="Balinese name">Balinese names</a> are quite distinct, as they have a naming system which denotes birth order. <i>Wayan</i> means first born. <i>Made</i> means second born. A Balinese name may also indicate <a href="/wiki/Caste" title="Caste">caste</a>. For instance, a <a href="/wiki/Kshatriya" title="Kshatriya">Kshatriya</a> person may be named "I Gusti".
</p><p>"Sitompul" and "Rajagukguk" are clan names usually found in people with <a href="/wiki/Batak" title="Batak">Batak</a> or <a href="/wiki/North_Sumatera" class="mw-redirect" title="North Sumatera">North Sumatran</a> heritage.
</p><p>In general, Indonesian names fall into one of the following categories:
</p>
<ul><li>A <a href="/wiki/Mononymous_person" title="Mononymous person">single name</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Sukarno" title="Sukarno">Sukarno</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suharto" title="Suharto">Suharto</a></li>
<li>Two (or more) names <i>without</i> a <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Susilo_Bambang_Yudhoyono" title="Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono">Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono</a></li>
<li>Two (or more) names <i>with</i> a <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family/clan name</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Abdul_Haris_Nasution" title="Abdul Haris Nasution">Abdul Haris Nasution</a> or <a href="/wiki/Mahyadi_Panggabean" title="Mahyadi Panggabean">Mahyadi Panggabean</a></li>
<li>Two (or more) names <i>with</i> a <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Megawati_Sukarnoputri" title="Megawati Sukarnoputri">Megawati Sukarnoputri</a> or <a href="/wiki/Abdurrahman_Wahid" title="Abdurrahman Wahid">Abdurrahman Wahid</a></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indonesian_naming_system">Indonesian naming system</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Indonesian naming system">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Until recently, most Indonesians did not have family names. Both men and women usually have a given name and then take the name of their father as a last name. Some, but not all, married Indonesian women take the last name of their husband. This name is usually added after their own "last" name. Therefore, it is not rare for married couples to have different last or family names.
</p><p>Naming also differs regionally. Some Javanese, especially those of the old generation have <a href="/wiki/Mononymous_person" title="Mononymous person">only one name</a>. Bataks have clan names which are used as their surnames. Some Chinese Indonesians have Chinese-style names. In Indonesian telephone directories, names are listed under the first or given name and not under the last or family name.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mononymic_names">Mononymic names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mononymic names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Example:
</p>
<dl><dd>Child's name: Hasan</dd>
<dd>Father's name: Suparman</dd>
<dd>Mother's name: Wulandari</dd></dl>
<p>On the birth certificate, the child's name would be written as "Hasan child of Suparman and Wulandari". The birth certificate of an extramarital child would bear only the mother's name. On a school diploma, the child's name would be written as "Hasan, child of Suparman". On all other official documents (ID card, driver's license, and passport), only the child's name would appear, "Hasan".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polynymic_names_without_a_family_name">Polynymic names without a family name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Polynymic names without a family name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Example:
</p>
<dl><dd>Child's name: Hasan Prasetyo</dd>
<dd>Father's name: Suparman Prakoso</dd>
<dd>Mother's name: Wulandari Hartono</dd></dl>
<p>On the birth certificate, the child's name would be written as "Hasan Prasetyo child of Suparman Prakoso and Wulandari Hartono". On all other official documents, the child's name would be written as "Hasan Prasetyo".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polynymic_names_with_family_name">Polynymic names with family name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Polynymic names with family name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>If the parents want a family name (or surname) to appear on official documents, the family name should be included on the child's birth certificate.
</p><p>Example:
</p>
<dl><dd>Child's name: Yovan Gunardio Darmawan</dd>
<dd>Father's name: Budi Darmawan</dd>
<dd>Mother's name: Natalena Tirrand</dd></dl>
<p>On the birth certificate, the child's name would be written as "Yovan Gunardio Darmawan child of Budi Darmawan and Natalena Tirrand". On all other official documents, the child's name would be written as "Yovan Gunardio Darmawan".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Polynymic_names_with_patronymic">Polynymic names with patronymic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Polynymic names with patronymic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The patronymic is usually constructed from the father's name, with the word <i>putra</i> (for males, "son" in <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>) or <i>putri</i> (for females, "daughter" in Sanskrit) appended.
</p><p>Example:
</p>
<dl><dd>Child's name: Hasan Suparmanputra</dd>
<dd>Father's name: Suparman</dd>
<dd>Mother's name: Wulandari</dd></dl>
<p>On the birth certificate, the child's name would be written as "Hasan Suparmanputra child of Suparman and Wulandari". On all other official documents, the child's name would be written as "Hasan Suparmanputra". This would be somewhat analogous to the practice in <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_name" title="Icelandic name">Iceland</a>, where patronymics are used.
</p><p>Occasionally, the father's name will be used as the surname, without appending <i>putra</i> or <i>putri</i> (in this example it would be Hasan Suparman). This might be done unofficially, that is, not matching the birth certificate. Nevertheless, this format sometimes appears on government documents.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Modifications_to_the_name_outside_of_Indonesia">Modifications to the name outside of Indonesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Modifications to the name outside of Indonesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Other nations may modify an official Indonesian name to conform to local standards. This is most apparent in nations where personal names are divided by law into given/first name and family/last name.
</p><p>In the Netherlands, for example, a person without an official family name would be given the surname <i>Onbekend</i> (which means "unknown"). Individuals with multiple-word names will often be given this surname, particularly if the last name on the birth certificate differs from the father's family name. Individuals with a distinct family name may also be given this surname if it is recorded differently on the birth certificate.
</p><p>Referring to the examples above, a Netherlands identification card would record the individual's name as:
</p>
<dl><dd>Example 1: Hasan Onbekend</dd>
<dd>Example 2: Hasan Prasetyo Onbekend</dd>
<dd>Example 3: Hasan Herianto</dd>
<dd>Example 4: Hasan Suparmanputra Onbekend</dd></dl>
<p>In Germany, the one-word name is used as both given name and surname. This is often displayed on official documents as "Hasan Hasan" or "H. Hasan".
</p><p>In the U.S., there are three ways to record people with a single-word name. One way is to use the existing single word name as the surname. Then, an official body will add <a href="/wiki/Placeholder_name#Fnu_Lnu" title="Placeholder name">"Fnu"</a> (family name unknown) as the first or given name. This can lead to a false belief that "Fnu" is a common Indonesian first name.
</p><p>Conversely, the existing single word name can be used as the given or first name and then <a href="/wiki/Placeholder_name#Fnu_Lnu" title="Placeholder name">"Lnu"</a> (last name unknown) may be added as the family, surname or last name. This can lead to the misconception that "Lnu" is a common Indonesian surname. In some cases "Fnu" will be used as the surname or last name.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lastly, the existing single word name may be duplicated to give a first name and surname such as "Hasan Hasan".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_names_in_Indonesia">Origin of names in Indonesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Origin of names in Indonesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span id="Local_family.2Fclan_names"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Local_family/clan_names">Local family/clan names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Local family/clan names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are some Indonesian ethnic groups or tribes whose people do maintain a family, last or surname. These include the:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Alas_people" title="Alas people">Alas people</a> of <a href="/wiki/Aceh" title="Aceh">Aceh</a>, for example, Keruas, Kepale Dese, or Pagan.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Bantenese_people" title="Bantenese people">Bantenese people</a> of <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a>, for example, Djajadiningrat, Soerjaatmadja, or Prawiranegara.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Batak_(Indonesia)" class="mw-redirect" title="Batak (Indonesia)">Batak</a> people of <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a>, for example, Nasution, Hutagaol, Sitompul, Karokaro, Rajagukguk, or Sinaga.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Nias" title="Nias">Nias</a> people and people of the islands off the west coast of Sumatra, for example, Amuata, Falakhi, Laoly, Marunduri, Ote, or Wau.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Minangkabau_people" title="Minangkabau people">Minangkabau people</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">Sumatra</a>, for example, Bodi, Caniago, Sikumbang, Koto, or Piliang.</li>
<li>Tribes in <a href="/wiki/Lampung" title="Lampung">Lampung</a>, for example, Badak, Limau, Gunungalip, or Way Tube.</li>
<li>Christian <a href="/wiki/Betawi_people" title="Betawi people">Betawi people</a>, for example, Baidan, Djaim, or Senen.</li>
<li>The royals and nobles from the <a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Javanese people</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sundanese_people" title="Sundanese people">Sundanese people</a> of <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">Java</a>, for example, Sastrowardoyo, Djojohadikusumo, or Natalegawa.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Dayak_people" title="Dayak people">Dayak people</a> of <a href="/wiki/Kalimantan" title="Kalimantan">Kalimantan</a>, for example, Belawan, Manuwu, Ngayoh, Kuntai, or Unus.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Minahasan_people" title="Minahasan people">Minahasan people</a> from <a href="/wiki/North_Sulawesi" title="North Sulawesi">North Sulawesi</a>, for example, Gerung, Mogot, Sondakh, or Tendean.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sangirese_people" title="Sangirese people">Sangirese people</a> from <a href="/wiki/North_Sulawesi" title="North Sulawesi">North Sulawesi</a> for example, Kansil, Manganang, Mohede, or Tatengkeng.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Toraja" title="Toraja">Toraja</a> people from <a href="/wiki/South_Sulawesi" title="South Sulawesi">South Sulawesi</a> for example, Batusura, Lumba, Rambulangi, or Manganan.</li>
<li>Tribes in the <a href="/wiki/Molucca_Islands" class="mw-redirect" title="Molucca Islands">Molucca Islands</a>, for example, Malaiholo, Hehamahua, Sahanaya, or Sahilatua.</li>
<li>Tribes in the <a href="/wiki/East_Nusa_Tenggara" title="East Nusa Tenggara">East Nusa Tenggara</a>, for example, Hurek, Riberu, Fernandez, da Costa, da Gama, Lamaborak, or Leyn.</li>
<li>Tribes in the <a href="/wiki/West_Papua_(region)" class="mw-redirect" title="West Papua (region)">Papua</a>, for example, Suebu, Maniani, Wanggai, or Wonda.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sanskrit-derived_names">Sanskrit-derived names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sanskrit-derived names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Most Indonesians regardless of personal religion, especially the <a href="/wiki/Javanese_people" title="Javanese people">Javanese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sundanese_people" title="Sundanese people">Sundanese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Balinese_people" title="Balinese people">Balinese</a>, have names derived from <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>. This is because of the Indian cultural influence which came to the archipelago since thousands of years ago during the Indianization of South East Asian kingdoms, and ever since, it is seen as part of the Indonesian culture, especially Javanese, Balinese, and some part of Sumatran culture. Thus, related Hindu or Indian culture in Indonesia is present not only as part of religion, but also culture. As a result, it is common to find Muslim or Christian Indonesians with Indo-Sanskritic sounding names. Unlike Sanskrit-derived names in <a href="/wiki/Thai_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Thai names">Thai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Khmer_language" title="Khmer language">Khmer</a>, the pronunciation of such names in either Javanese or Indonesian is similar to the original Indian pronunciation, except that the "v" is changed to a "w".
</p><p>Some common names derived from Sanskrit and using the names of Indian Hindu gods or heroes include <a href="/wiki/Indra" title="Indra">Indra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Krishna" title="Krishna">Krisna</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">Wisnu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Surya" title="Surya">Surya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dharma" title="Dharma">Dharma</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rama" title="Rama">Rama</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lakshmana" title="Lakshmana">Lesmana</a>, Sudarto (Javanese for <a href="/wiki/Gautama_Buddha" title="Gautama Buddha">Siddharta</a>), <a href="/wiki/Devi" title="Devi">Dewi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Prithvi" title="Prithvi">Pertiwi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sri" title="Sri">Sri</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sita" title="Sita">Sinta</a>, Ratna, <a href="/wiki/P%C4%81ramit%C4%81" title="Pāramitā">Paramitha</a>, and Kumala. Other Sanskrit derived names used widely in Indonesia also include such as: Wibisana or Wibisono (from the <a href="/wiki/Ramayana" title="Ramayana">Ramayana</a> figure <a href="/wiki/Vibhisana" class="mw-redirect" title="Vibhisana">Vibhisana</a>), <a href="/wiki/Arya_(name)" title="Arya (name)">Arya</a> or Aryo, <a href="/wiki/Subrata" title="Subrata">Subrata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aditya" class="mw-redirect" title="Aditya">Aditya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Abimanyu" class="mw-redirect" title="Abimanyu">Abimanyu</a>, Bima, Sena, Satya, Cakra (read Chakra), Putri, Putra, Mahardhika, Gatot or Gatut (from the <a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a> figure <a href="/wiki/Ghatotkacha" title="Ghatotkacha">Ghatotkacha</a>), Perdana (from the word "Pradhan"), Prameswara or Prameswari, Pertiwi (from Pritvhi), Dewi (Devi), Wijaya (from Vijay), and many more. Even Indonesian government names of institutions, mottoes, and other terms also use <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a>, such as to address an <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_Navy" title="Indonesian Navy">Indonesian Navy</a> general, the word "Laksamana" (from the Ramayan figure <a href="/wiki/Lakshman" class="mw-redirect" title="Lakshman">Lakshman</a>) is used. The "Adipura award" (Indonesian: <i>Penghargaan Adipura</i>) which is an award given to cities throughout Indonesia from the central government for cleanliness and urban environmental management also uses from Sanskrit language which is <i>Adi</i> and <i>Pura</i>. There are also many mottoes of Indonesian institutions which use <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> language, such as the motto of the <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_Military_Academy" title="Indonesian Military Academy">Indonesian Military Academy</a> which sounds "<i>Adhitakarya Mahatvavirya Nagarabhakti</i>".
</p><p>For example, <a href="/wiki/Susilo_Bambang_Yudhoyono" title="Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono">Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono</a>, former Indonesian president, has a Sanskrit-derived name. "Susilo" comes from <i>sushila</i> meaning "good character" and "Yudhoyono" comes form <i>yudha</i> meaning "war" or "battle" and <i>yana</i> meaning "an epic story". <a href="/wiki/Sukarno" title="Sukarno">Sukarno</a> is derived from the Sanskrit <i>su</i> (good) and <i>karno</i> or <i><a href="/wiki/Karna" title="Karna">Karna</a></i> (a warrior) in <a href="/wiki/Mahabharata" title="Mahabharata">Mahabharata</a>.
</p><p>Some of these Sanskrit-derived names might be used by <i>ningrat</i> or <i>menak</i> (noble) families, especially among Javanese and Sundanese, in much the same way as some family names in western culture indicate lineage and nobility. Some such names are Adiningrat, Notonegoro, Suryasumantri, Dharmokusumo, Wongsoatmodjo, Natalegawa, Kusumaatmadja, Kartadibrata, Kartapranata and Kartasasmita.
</p><p>In the case of the Indonesian foreign minister, <a href="/wiki/Marty_Natalegawa" title="Marty Natalegawa">Marty Natalegawa</a>, "Marty" is his first name, indicating his birth in March. "Natalegawa" is his family name, which indicates that he is from a Sundanese noble family.
</p><p>Many Indonesians use Sanskrit-derived names to indicate their position among siblings (birth order). The first-born child might bear the name <i>Eka</i> or <i>Eko</i> (mostly Javanese), the second-born child might be named <i>Dwi</i>, the third-born <i>Tri</i>, the fourth-born <i>Catur</i>, and the fifth-born <i>Panca</i> or <i>Ponco</i> (usually Javanese). Some examples are <a href="/wiki/Eko_Yuli_Irawan" title="Eko Yuli Irawan">Eko Yuli Irawan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rizky_Dwi_Ramadhana" class="mw-redirect" title="Rizky Dwi Ramadhana">Rizky Dwi Ramadhana</a>, <a href="/wiki/Triyaningsih" title="Triyaningsih">Triyaningsih</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Catur_Pamungkas" title="Catur Pamungkas">Catur Pamungkas</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_names">Chinese names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Chinese names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Under President Suharto, Indonesia attempted to deconstruct organisations and groups that might represent an internal security threat. As a part of the policy to limit the influence of the Chinese <a href="/wiki/Communism" title="Communism">Communists</a> and to encourage the ethnic Chinese to assimilate, the state required <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Indonesian" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese Indonesian">Chinese Indonesian</a> individuals to change their names. This was a difficult balance because while the names were changed, laws continued to identify them as 'different' from indigenous Indonesian groups. Indonesian businessman, Liem Sioe Liong, for example, had his name changed to <a href="/wiki/Sudono_Salim" title="Sudono Salim">Sudono Salim</a>. With Suharto's downfall came new laws, one of which allowed the Chinese to revert to Chinese family names.
</p><p>Many of the later generations have kept the Indonesian form of the name. Other Chinese Indonesians, however, maintain their Chinese name as well as their family names. As is customary with <a href="/wiki/Chinese_family_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese family names">Chinese names</a>, the family name (or surname) is traditionally placed in front of the given (or first) name.
</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Indonesian-sounding_names_adopted_by_Chinese_Indonesians" class="mw-redirect" title="Indonesian-sounding names adopted by Chinese Indonesians">Indonesian-sounding names adopted by Chinese Indonesians</a></div>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arabic_names">Arabic names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Arabic names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>As <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">Islam</a> is the largest religion in Indonesia, it is quite common to find <a href="/wiki/Arabic_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Arabic names">Arabic</a> first names or words. Popular Arabic names include <a href="/wiki/Muhammad" title="Muhammad">Muhammad</a> or Mohammad, <a href="/wiki/Abdul" title="Abdul">Abdul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ali" title="Ali">Ali</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amir" class="mw-redirect" title="Amir">Amir</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Annisa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Annisa (page does not exist)">Annisa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aisyah" class="mw-redirect" title="Aisyah">Aisyah</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aziz" title="Aziz">Aziz</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ahmad" title="Ahmad">Ahmad</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hassan_(given_name)" title="Hassan (given name)">Hassan</a>, <a href="/wiki/B._J._Habibie" title="B. J. Habibie">Habibie</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hidayat" title="Hidayat">Hidayat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ibrahim" class="mw-redirect" title="Ibrahim">Ibrahim</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nur_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nur (name)">Nur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nurul" class="mw-redirect" title="Nurul">Nurul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rahman_(name)" title="Rahman (name)">Rahman</a>, <a href="/wiki/Taufik" class="mw-redirect" title="Taufik">Taufik</a> and <a href="/wiki/Umar" title="Umar">Umar</a>. Such names are used by Indonesians not of Arab descent, both as first names and as surnames.
</p><p>Some names derived from Arabic are only found the <a href="/wiki/Nusantara" title="Nusantara">Nusantara</a> region, such as Nuraini.
</p><p>Ethnic groups with strong Islamic influence, such as the <a href="/wiki/Acehnese_people" title="Acehnese people">Acehnese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_Malay" class="mw-redirect" title="Ethnic Malay">Malay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minangkabau_people" title="Minangkabau people">Minangkabau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Betawi_people" title="Betawi people">Betawi</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bugis" class="mw-redirect" title="Bugis">Bugis</a>, tend to use Arabic names. For example, Indonesian politicians <a href="/wiki/Teuku_Muhammad_Hasan" title="Teuku Muhammad Hasan">Teuku Muhammad Hasan</a> (from Aceh) and <a href="/wiki/Mohammad_Hatta" title="Mohammad Hatta">Mohammad Hatta</a> (from Minangkabau) have Arabic names. However, some of these ethnic groups with strong Islamic influences, such as the <a href="/wiki/Betawi_people" title="Betawi people">Betawi people</a>, have Arabic names which have been suitable with the local pronunciation, such as Rojak (Razak), Leman (Sulaiman), etc.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Arab_Indonesian" class="mw-redirect" title="Arab Indonesian">Arabs</a> settled in Indonesia many generations ago, and their descendants still use their family names, for example, Assegaf, Alhabsyi, or Shihab.
</p><p>The name "Maysaroh" is a common female name amongst the Betawi people. However, this name is actually a male name in Arab countries. It is probably mistaken with the name Saroh, which is in turn derived from <a href="/wiki/Sarah_(given_name)" title="Sarah (given name)">Sarah</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_names">Western names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Western names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Western names were brought to Indonesia by the Portuguese and later the Dutch. Names with a Dutch origin include Henry, Agus, Johan, Andri, Anto (Antonius), Siska, Roni, Jono, Riska, Suzanna, Rian, and Markus. These names are found in Muslim and Christian people of Java.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Catholicism" class="mw-redirect" title="Catholicism">Catholic</a> Indonesians may use <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> first or Christian names such as Antonius, Ignasius, Yohanes, Andreas, Matius, Markus, Paulus, Anastasia, Fransiska, Maria, Gregorius, and Theresia. <a href="/wiki/Protestantism" title="Protestantism">Protestant</a> Indonesians may use Anglicised names such as Kevin, Andrew, Anthony, Martin, George, James, John, Paul, Caroline, Eva, Stephanie, Mary, or Melinda.
</p><p>There are some similarities between the Indonesian Catholic and Muslim names such as <a href="/wiki/Fatima_(given_name)" title="Fatima (given name)">Fatima</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Omar_(name)_(disambiguation)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Omar (name) (disambiguation) (page does not exist)">Omar</a>, and Soraya.
</p><p>Due to <a href="/wiki/Globalisation" class="mw-redirect" title="Globalisation">globalisation</a> non-Christian Indonesians may use shortened Western names such as Courtney, Tony, Tommy, Franky or Julie.
</p><p>Combinations such as "Ricky Hidayat" (Western-Arabic) or "Lucy Wiryono" (Western-Javanese) are to be found as well.
</p><p>Western-derived names may indicate the month of birth. For example,
</p>
<ul><li>January: Januri (m), Yanuar (m). Example: <a href="/wiki/Yanuar_Tri_Firmanda" title="Yanuar Tri Firmanda">Yanuar Tri Firmanda</a>.</li>
<li>February: usually identified from prefix <i>Febr-</i>. Example: <a href="/wiki/Febriyanto_Wijaya" title="Febriyanto Wijaya">Febriyanto Wijaya</a>.</li>
<li>March: Marti (derived from <i>Maret</i>, Indonesian word for March, which in turn is derived from <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> <i>Maart</i>). Example: <a href="/wiki/Marty_Natalegawa" title="Marty Natalegawa">Marty Natalegawa</a>.</li>
<li>April: usually identified from prefix <i>Afri-</i> or <i>Apri-</i>. Example: <a href="/wiki/Aprilia_Yuswandari" title="Aprilia Yuswandari">Aprilia Yuswandari</a>.</li>
<li>May: Mei or its derivations such as Meilanie, Meiliana, Meiliani (all are feminine). Example: <a href="/wiki/Meiliana_Jauhari" title="Meiliana Jauhari">Meiliana Jauhari</a>.</li>
<li>June: Yuni (f) or its derivations such as Yuniar (f), Yuniarti (f), Yuniarto (m). Example: <a href="/wiki/Yuni_Shara" title="Yuni Shara">Yuni Shara</a>.</li>
<li>July: usually identified from prefix <i>Juli-</i> or <i>Yuli-</i>. Example: <a href="/wiki/Alvent_Yulianto" title="Alvent Yulianto">Alvent Yulianto</a>.</li>
<li>August: Agus (n) or its derivations such as Agustin (n), Agustina (f), Agustinus (m, usually borne by Indonesian Catholic). Example: <a href="/wiki/Agus_Ngaimin" title="Agus Ngaimin">Agus Ngaimin</a>.</li>
<li>September: usually identified from prefix <i>Seft-</i> or <i>Sept-</i>. Example: <a href="/wiki/Seftia_Hadi" title="Seftia Hadi">Seftia Hadi</a>.</li>
<li>October: usually identified from prefix <i>Oct-</i> or <i>Okt-</i>. Example: <a href="/wiki/Yati_Octavia" class="mw-redirect" title="Yati Octavia">Yati Octavia</a>.</li>
<li>November: usually identified from prefix <i>Nov-</i>. Example: <a href="/wiki/Novita_Dewi" title="Novita Dewi">Novita Dewi</a>.</li>
<li>December: Deasy, Desi, Dessy (all are feminine). Example: <a href="/wiki/Desi_Anwar" title="Desi Anwar">Desi Anwar</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Patronymic_family_names">Patronymic family names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Patronymic family names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Indonesian patronymic family name is usually constructed of the father's name, with the word <i>putra</i> (for male) or <i>putri</i> (for female) appended. One example is former Indonesian President <a href="/wiki/Megawati_Sukarnoputri" title="Megawati Sukarnoputri">Megawati Sukarnoputri</a>, the daughter of former President <a href="/wiki/Sukarno" title="Sukarno">Sukarno</a>. However, it is also common for the father's name alone to form the child's surname (for instance, 'Ali Ahmad' from the father 'Ahmad Sudharma').
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Matronymic_family_names">Matronymic family names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Matronymic family names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Located in Western Sumatra, the <a href="/wiki/Minangkabau_people" title="Minangkabau people">Minangkabau</a> are the largest matrilineal culture in the world and the fourth largest ethnic group in Indonesia. Tribe, clan (or <i>suku</i>) titles, properties and names are all handed down through the female line.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nicknames">Nicknames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Nicknames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is uncommon, and considered rude, to refer to an Indonesian person by their full first name, unless that name has only one or two syllables. Former Indonesian president <a href="/wiki/Abdurrahman_Wahid" title="Abdurrahman Wahid">Abdurrahman Wahid</a>, for example, had "Abdurrahman" shortened to "Dur". Many Indonesians use a different name altogether; a woman born as "Khadijah" may be known as "Ida" or "Ijah" to all her friends and family.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Sundanese_people" title="Sundanese people">Sundanese culture</a> it is common for the nickname to become integrated as the first name. For example, someone with the name "Komariah", "Gunawan" or "Suryana" written in their birth certificate may become known as "Kokom" for Komariah, "Gugun" or "Wawan" for Gunawan, and "Yaya" or "Nana" for Suryana. The result is rhyming names such as "Kokom Komariah", "Wawan Gunawan", and "Nana Suryana". Notable people having such names include politician <a href="/wiki/Agum_Gumelar" title="Agum Gumelar">Agum Gumelar</a>, comedian <a href="/wiki/Sule_(comedian)" title="Sule (comedian)">Entis Sutisna</a> (with stage name <a href="/wiki/Sule_(comedian)" title="Sule (comedian)">Sule'</a>), and sportsman <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ajat_Sudrajat&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ajat Sudrajat (page does not exist)">Ajat Sudrajat</a>.
</p><p>Indonesian people might also take a Western style  nickname such as Kevin, Kenny, Tommy (Tomi), Jimmy, Ricky, Dicky, Bob, Nicky, Nico, Susy, Taty, Lucy (Lusi), Nancy, Marry (Maria) and so on. It does not necessarily mean their names are Thomas (for Tommy) or James (for Jimmy). For instance, <a href="/wiki/Suharto" title="Suharto">Suharto</a>'s son, Hutomo Mandalaputra is popularly known as "Tommy Suharto". The "Tommy" here was not derived from "Thomas", but from the Javanese name, "Hutomo".
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Noble_titles_as_part_of_personal_name">Noble titles as part of personal name</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Noble titles as part of personal name">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In some ethnic groups it is common to include a nobility title into the formal personal name. Due to the various traditions of nobility in each ethnic group, it may be difficult for people from outside a particular ethnic group to discern the nobility title from the personal name.
</p><p>Acehnese have titles such as <i>Teuku</i> (male) and <i>Cut</i> (female). Celebrities with such titles include, for example, Teuku Ryan, Teuku Wisnu, and Cut Tari.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Bantenese_people" title="Bantenese people">Bantenese</a> have titles such as <i>Tubagus</i> (male) and <i>Ratu</i> (female). People with such titles include, for example, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tubagus_Ismail&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Tubagus Ismail (page does not exist)">Tubagus Ismail</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ratu_Atut_Chosiyah" title="Ratu Atut Chosiyah">Ratu Atut Chosiyah</a>.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Bugis" class="mw-redirect" title="Bugis">Bugis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Makassar" title="Makassar">Makassar</a> people have titles such as <i>Daeng</i> and <i>Andi</i> and the Buton people have titles such as <i>La</i> (male) and <i>Wa</i> (female), for example, La Nyalla Mattalitti or Wa Ode Nurhayati.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Indexing">Indexing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Indonesian_names&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Indexing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Manual_of_Style" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago Manual of Style">Chicago Manual of Style</a>, Indonesian names are <a href="/wiki/Subject_indexing" title="Subject indexing">indexed</a> differently according to the individual practices and customs. If there is one name, it is only indexed under that name. If the family name is printed first, index under the family name with no comma and no inversion.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
