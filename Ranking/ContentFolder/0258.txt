<p><a href="/wiki/Personal_name" title="Personal name">Personal names</a> in Hong Kong generally contain differences from those in <a href="/wiki/Mainland_China" title="Mainland China">mainland China</a> due to a lack of <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a> standardization, ethnic diversity, and the presence of English as a second language.
</p><p>Generally, the Cantonese majority employ one or another <a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Cantonese" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanization of Cantonese">romanization of Cantonese</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> However, non-Cantonese immigrants may retain their hometown spelling in English. For example, use of <a href="/wiki/Shanghainese" title="Shanghainese">Shanghainese</a> romanization in names is more common in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong_English" title="Hong Kong English">Hong Kong English</a> than in official use in <a href="/wiki/Shanghai" title="Shanghai">Shanghai</a> where pinyin is used.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Chinese_name" title="Chinese name">Chinese names</a> and sometimes <a href="/wiki/Chinese_surname" title="Chinese surname">Chinese surnames</a> in Hong Kong may be supplemented by or replaced by an English name when using English. As with the unrelated actors <a href="/wiki/Tony_Leung_Chiu-Wai" class="mw-redirect" title="Tony Leung Chiu-Wai">Tony Leung Chiu-Wai</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tony_Leung_Ka-fai" title="Tony Leung Ka-fai">Tony Leung Ka-fai</a>, Hong Kong names may follow an English personal name + Cantonese family name + Cantonese personal name format. The use of English names in Hong Kong is not well researched or documented.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> English names in Hong Kong can include names that are not often found in the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a>, with some examples being <a href="/wiki/Rimsky_Yuen" title="Rimsky Yuen">Rimsky Yuen</a>, <a href="/wiki/York_Chow" title="York Chow">York Chow</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Moses_Chan" title="Moses Chan">Moses Chan</a>. Inspiration for English names in Hong Kong can come from the names of months, sports brands, and luxury labels. More conventional English names can undergo distortion by the adding, substitution, or deletion of letters (e.g. Sonija, Garbie, Kith), as well using suffixes like <i>-son</i> (e.g. Rayson). They also sometimes come from mimicking the sounds of the Chinese name, like <a href="/wiki/Hacken_Lee" title="Hacken Lee">Hacken Lee</a> from Lee Hak-kan (<span lang="zh" title="Chinese language text">李克勤</span>).<sup id="cite_ref-man_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-man-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> These categories (addition, substitution, phonetic-based, etc) are the fundamental ways of generating creative Hong Kong names.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Hong Kongers may also append a <a href="/wiki/Middle_name" title="Middle name">middle name</a>. For example, the 4th Chief Executive of Hong Kong, <a href="/wiki/Carrie_Lam_Cheng_Yuet_Ngor" class="mw-redirect" title="Carrie Lam Cheng Yuet Ngor">Carrie Lam Cheng Yuet Ngor</a>, Cheng Yuet Ngor is her given name, Lam being the surname of her spouse. 
</p>
