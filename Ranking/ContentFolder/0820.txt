	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">David Brown (entrepreneur)</h1>

<p><b>Sir David Brown</b> (10 May 1904 – 3 September 1993) was an English industrialist, managing director of his grandfather's  gear and machine tool business <a href="/wiki/David_Brown_Ltd." title="David Brown Ltd.">David Brown Limited</a> and more recently <a href="/wiki/David_Brown_Ltd.#David_Brown_Tractors_Group" title="David Brown Ltd.">David Brown Tractors</a>, and one time owner of shipbuilders <a href="/wiki/VT_Group" title="VT Group">Vosper Thorneycroft</a> and car manufacturers <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin" title="Aston Martin">Aston Martin</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lagonda" title="Lagonda">Lagonda</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Brown_(entrepreneur)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Valveless_Car.JPG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Valveless_Car.JPG/300px-Valveless_Car.JPG" decoding="async" width="300" height="225" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Valveless_Car.JPG/450px-Valveless_Car.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Valveless_Car.JPG/600px-Valveless_Car.JPG 2x" data-file-width="3648" data-file-height="2736" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Valveless_Car.JPG" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>1914 DBS (David Brown &amp; Sons) <a href="/wiki/Valveless" title="Valveless">Valveless</a> at <a href="/wiki/Tolson_Museum" title="Tolson Museum">Tolson Museum</a></div></div></div>
<p>Brown was born in Park Cottage in the Yorkshire town of Huddersfield to Caroline and Frank Brown in 1904. Park Cottage was pulled down in the Second World War to make way for a new factory — on their 17-acre Park Works site in <a href="/wiki/Huddersfield" title="Huddersfield">Huddersfield</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yorkshire" title="Yorkshire">Yorkshire</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TT64742_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TT64742-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Brown attended <a href="/wiki/King_James%27s_Grammar_School,_Almondbury" class="mw-redirect" title="King James&#39;s Grammar School, Almondbury">King James's Grammar School, Almondbury</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rossall_School" title="Rossall School">Rossall School</a>.
</p>
<h2><span id="David_Brown_.26_Sons"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="David_Brown_&amp;_Sons">David Brown &amp; Sons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Brown_(entrepreneur)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: David Brown &amp; Sons">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After leaving school Brown started work aged 17 in 1921 as if just another apprentice in his family's business, <a href="/wiki/David_Brown_Ltd." title="David Brown Ltd.">David Brown &amp; Sons (Huddersfield)</a>, cycling 6 miles to work by 7.30 a.m.<sup id="cite_ref-NoakesP16_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NoakesP16-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This company which had been founded by his grandfather David, specialized in transmission components. While his father had no interest in cars and did not drive, his mother was a keen driver and as a small child he had ridden beside Frederick Tasker Burgess on test runs of David Brown &amp; Sons new <a href="/wiki/Valveless" title="Valveless">Valveless</a> car but he did not learn to drive until aged 11.
</p><p>Brown's father offered to buy him a motorcycle to help him get to work on time, so relying on his father’s ignorance of motor vehicles, instead of a small docile motorcycle, Brown after considering a Harley Davidson obtained a powerful Reading Standard 1,000 cc V-twin. He subsequently improved its performance by modifying the engine and raced it in weekend hill climb competitions at Axe Edge Moor in Derbyshire and at Sutton Bank in Yorkshire.<sup id="cite_ref-NoakesP16_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NoakesP16-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
After achieving the fastest time of the day at Axe Edge Moor he was invited to be a reserve rider for the official <a href="/wiki/Douglas_(motorcycles)" title="Douglas (motorcycles)">Douglas</a> motorcycle team to complete at the upcoming next <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Man_TT" title="Isle of Man TT">International Isle of Man TT (Tourist Trophy)</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DBDTS_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DBDTS-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> While he attended the practice session, his father forbade him from competing in the competition.<sup id="cite_ref-NoakesP16_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NoakesP16-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By this time Brown was romantically involved with Daisy Muriel Firth, three years his senior. In an attempt to break up the  relationship, his father sent Brown to South Africa in 1922 to assist a director of the company in overseeing the installation of the company's gears in gold mines near <a href="/wiki/Johannesburg" title="Johannesburg">Johannesburg</a>. When the director's drinking affected his  ability to do the work, Brown took over responsibility for the project.<sup id="cite_ref-DBDTS_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DBDTS-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Upon his return from South Africa Brown decided to design and build his own car. Working in his bedroom each night until 2:00 am, he designed a 1.5-litre twin-cam, straight-eight engine. Then using the firm’s foundry, he made patterns and cast the cylinder block, while using the machine shop to produce the other components. His father put a stop to the work when he caught his son working on the project in company time. Undaunted, Brown constructed a chassis, fitting it with a Sage 2-litre engine, coupled to a <a href="/wiki/Henry_Meadows" title="Henry Meadows">Meadows</a> gear box. He called the result the “Daybro”.<sup id="cite_ref-DBDTS_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DBDTS-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> David Brown &amp; Sons' gear-grinding skills brought him early contacts with Bertelli of <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin" title="Aston Martin">Aston Martin</a> for gears and <a href="/wiki/Amherst_Villiers" title="Amherst Villiers">Amherst Villiers</a> for his superchargers. With the consent of Villiers, Brown modified a <a href="/wiki/Vauxhall_Motors" title="Vauxhall Motors">Vauxhall</a> which then won its class at <a href="/wiki/Shelsley_Walsh" title="Shelsley Walsh">Shelsley Walsh</a> for three consecutive years. It was said to be capable of more than 140 mph on the sand.<sup id="cite_ref-DBDTS_3-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DBDTS-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As his grounding continued in various parts of their works he progressed to foreman and then assistant works manager.
</p><p>Sent to the United States, Africa and Europe in 1928 to study business methods and factory conditions he returned and started a bronze and steel foundry in <a href="/wiki/Penistone" title="Penistone">Penistone</a> where unemployment was severe. The new foundry used a new technique of steel casting and was a rapid success. As well as meeting his group's own needs the foundry made precision castings for a wide range of industries and use in aircraft airframes, aero engines, electricity power stations, oilfields and oil refineries.<sup id="cite_ref-TT64742_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TT64742-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1929 he was made a director and on his uncle Percy's death in 1931 was appointed joint managing director in 1932.<sup id="cite_ref-TT64742_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TT64742-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> In 1933 he became managing director. Under Brown's leadership, the company significantly expanded its operations.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tractors">Tractors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Brown_(entrepreneur)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tractors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1939 David Brown &amp; Sons acquired the old United thread mills factory on a site at <a href="/wiki/Meltham" title="Meltham">Meltham</a>, on the south side of Huddersfield. Brown, who also owned a farm, started the <a href="/wiki/Ferguson-Brown_Company" title="Ferguson-Brown Company">Ferguson-Brown Company</a> building tractors with <a href="/wiki/Harry_Ferguson" title="Harry Ferguson">Harry Ferguson</a>  in 1936 in a corner of park gear works  but they disagreed over design details, which led David Brown to design his own version the David Brown VAK1 which was introduced to the market in 1939, with over 7,700 units eventually sold,. Harry Ferguson went to America and did a deal with <a href="/wiki/Henry_Ford" title="Henry Ford">Henry Ford</a> to incorporate his system in the <a href="/wiki/Ford_N-Series_tractor" class="mw-redirect" title="Ford N-Series tractor">Ford N-Series tractor</a>, before setting up <a href="/wiki/Ferguson_Tractors" class="mw-redirect" title="Ferguson Tractors">Ferguson Tractors</a>.
</p><p>The Second World War saw a massive increase in the production of gears and gearboxes by David Brown Ltd for use in military equipment. The rising income from the company's traditional products and the manufacture of tractors made Brown a wealthy man.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Aston_Martin">Aston Martin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Brown_(entrepreneur)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Aston Martin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In late 1946, Brown saw a <a href="/wiki/Classified_advertisement" class="mw-redirect" title="Classified advertisement">classified advertisement</a> in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">The Times</a></i>, offering for sale a "High Class Motor Business".<sup id="cite_ref-DowseyP10_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DowseyP10-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The asking price was £30,000.<sup id="cite_ref-NoakesP22_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NoakesP22-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>  On inquiring further Brown discovered that the company was <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin" title="Aston Martin">Aston Martin</a>. A few days later Brown visited the company's headquarters at Feltham and test drove their new prototype design, the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_Atom" title="Aston Martin Atom">Atom</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NoakesP22_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NoakesP22-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While he felt it had good road handing he considered that its 2.0 litre four cylinder pushrod engine lacked power. However seeing its potential he entered into negotiations which ended in February 1947 with him acquiring the company for £20,500.<sup id="cite_ref-Classic_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Classic-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
Following the purchase work began on converting the Atom into a production car. While the prototype was a saloon, Brown preferred convertibles so the chassis was redesigned to accommodate an open top.<sup id="cite_ref-NoakesP22_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NoakesP22-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> eventually it entered production as the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_2-Litre_Sports" title="Aston Martin 2-Litre Sports">Aston Martin 2-Litre Sports</a>, now commonly called the DB1.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lagonda">Lagonda</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Brown_(entrepreneur)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lagonda">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1947 Brown heard through Tony Scratchard, the distributor of <a href="/wiki/Lagonda" title="Lagonda">Lagonda</a> cars in Bradford that Lagonda was in financial trouble and was for sale. While he initially took no interest in the company, when a liquidator was later appointed to sell off the company's assets, Brown sensing an opportunity visited the company.<sup id="cite_ref-NoakesP29_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NoakesP29-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> There he met with the famed engine designer <a href="/wiki/W._O._Bentley" title="W. O. Bentley">W. O. Bentley</a>, who showed him an engine called the LB6 which he had been working on for the company. It was a modern twin-cam 6-cylinder engine of 2,580 cc. Brown saw the engine as ideal for his new generation of Aston Martin models.<sup id="cite_ref-NoakesP29_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NoakesP29-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Aware that Armstrong Siddeley, Jaguar and Rootes were also interested in the company and the liquidator was seeking offers of £250,000 Brown still decided to submit an offer, though he knew it would be the lowest.<sup id="cite_ref-DowseyP10_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DowseyP10-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Because of the tight economic conditions and the rationing of steel, the other bidders dropped out. While the liquidator was able to sell the factory buildings to another company, to Brown's surprise he was able to obtain for £52,500 the rest of the company as well as the rights to the new engine.<sup id="cite_ref-NoakesP29_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NoakesP29-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As Lagonda had to vacate its premises Brown stored his new assets in some rented hangers at the London Air Park in Hanworth, which was close to the Aston Martin factory. The newly acquired engine soon saw service in the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB2" title="Aston Martin DB2">Aston Martin DB2</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In late 1955 Brown acquired the coachbuilder <a href="/wiki/Tickford" title="Tickford">Tickford</a>. He subsequently concentrated all the Aston Martin and Lagonda manufacturing at the Tickford premises in <a href="/wiki/Newport_Pagnell" title="Newport Pagnell">Newport Pagnell</a>.
</p><p>The legendary 'DB' series of Aston Martin cars, including the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_2-Litre_Sports" title="Aston Martin 2-Litre Sports">DB1 (2 Litre Sports)</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB2" title="Aston Martin DB2">DB2</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB3" title="Aston Martin DB3">DB3</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB4" title="Aston Martin DB4">DB4</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB5" title="Aston Martin DB5">DB5</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB6" title="Aston Martin DB6">DB6</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DBS" title="Aston Martin DBS">DBS</a> were named after Brown using his initials. While at the helm of the Aston Martin company, he used a rival product, a <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_XJ" title="Jaguar XJ">Jaguar XJ</a> Series I, as personal transport as it was cheaper to run.<sup id="cite_ref-Classic_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Classic-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In February 1972 with the David Brown Corporation in financial difficulties the other members of the board forced Brown to sell the tractor division to <a href="/wiki/Tenneco_International" class="mw-redirect" title="Tenneco International">Tenneco International</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and Aston Martin Lagonda to a separate buyer.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The new owner of Aston Martin Lagonda dropped the DB model designation, which in 1993 was restored during Ford ownership with the introduction of the DB7. <a href="/wiki/Walter_Hayes" title="Walter Hayes">Walter Hayes</a> chairman of Aston Martin Lagonda invited and Brown accepted the position of Honorary Life President of Aston Martin Lagonda.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Vospers_Thornycroft">Vospers Thornycroft</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Brown_(entrepreneur)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vospers Thornycroft">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1963 the David Brown Corporation purchased a controlling interest in <a href="/wiki/Vosper_%26_Company" title="Vosper &amp; Company">Vosper &amp; Company</a>, at which Sir David Brown became Chairman. The company merged with John I. Thornycroft &amp; Company to create <a href="/wiki/VT_Group" title="VT Group">Vosper Thornycroft</a> in 1966. The warship building division of the company was <a href="/wiki/Nationalised" class="mw-redirect" title="Nationalised">nationalised</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Labour_(UK)" class="mw-redirect" title="Labour (UK)">Labour</a> Government in 1977, becoming a division of <a href="/wiki/British_Shipbuilders" title="British Shipbuilders">British Shipbuilders</a>.. The rest of the company remained publicly quoted as a subsidiary of David Brown Corporation.  Bitter about the nationalization Brown left Britain to live in retirement in Monte Carlo.<sup id="cite_ref-Levi_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levi-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sells_the_company">Sells the company</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Brown_(entrepreneur)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Sells the company">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In January 1990, Brown sold his shares in the David Brown Corporation for £46 million, but retained a link with the company in his role as its honorary president.<sup id="cite_ref-Levi_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Levi-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=David_Brown_(entrepreneur)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In his personal life Brown played <a href="/wiki/Polo" title="Polo">polo</a> at <a href="/wiki/Ham_Polo_Club" title="Ham Polo Club">Ham Polo Club</a> in the summer and during the winter he spent most weekends hunting; he was joint Master of the South Oxford hounds. He also bred hunters and <a href="/wiki/Racehorse" class="mw-redirect" title="Racehorse">racehorses</a> on his 700-acre farm in Buckinghamshire. His greatest success was his horse Linwell winning the 1957 <a href="/wiki/Cheltenham_Gold_Cup" title="Cheltenham Gold Cup">Cheltenham Gold Cup</a>. Brown was a qualified pilot, had his personal De Havilland Dove, which was normally flown by his personal pilot and also established his own airfield at Crosland Moor, to the south-west of Huddersfield.
</p><p>During his life he was a member of the Board of Governors of the Huddersfield Royal Infirmary, the Council of Huddersfield Chamber of Commerce and Lloyd’s.
</p><p>He was knighted in 1968 for services to industry.<sup id="cite_ref-TT64742_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TT64742-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1926 against the wishes of Brown’s parents, who refused to attend the wedding, Brown married Daisy Muriel Firth, who he had known since he was 14.<sup id="cite_ref-DBDTS_3-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DBDTS-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> They had two children, David and Angela, both of whom entered the family business. Angela married <a href="/wiki/George_Abecassis" title="George Abecassis">George Abecassis</a> the racing driver.  Following his divorce from Daisy, Brown married his secretary Marjorie Deans in 1955. This marriage also ended in divorce and he subsequently married his personal assistant, Paula Benton Stone in 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-TT64742_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TT64742-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Sir David Brown died in September 1993 in <a href="/wiki/Monte_Carlo" title="Monte Carlo">Monte Carlo</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-TT64742_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TT64742-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Eight years later David Brown Limited was acquired by <a href="/wiki/Textron" title="Textron">Textron</a>.
</p>
