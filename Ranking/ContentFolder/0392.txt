	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Districts of England</h1>

<p>The <b>districts of England</b> (also known as <b>local authority districts</b> or <b>local government districts</b> to distinguish from unofficial city districts) are a level of <a href="/wiki/Subdivisions_of_England" title="Subdivisions of England">subnational division of England</a> used for the purposes of <a href="/wiki/Local_government" title="Local government">local government</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>  As the structure of local government in England is not uniform, there are currently four principal types of district-level subdivision.  There are a total of 317 districts made up of 36 <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan boroughs</a>, 32 <a href="/wiki/London_boroughs" title="London boroughs">London boroughs</a>, 192 <a href="/wiki/Non-metropolitan_district" title="Non-metropolitan district">non-metropolitan districts</a>, and 55 <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authorities_of_England" title="Unitary authorities of England">unitary authorities</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/City_of_London" title="City of London">City of London</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Isles_of_Scilly" title="Isles of Scilly">Isles of Scilly</a> which are also districts, but do not correspond to any of these categories. Some districts are styled as <a href="/wiki/Borough" title="Borough">boroughs</a>, <a href="/wiki/City_status_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="City status in the United Kingdom">cities</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Royal_borough" class="mw-redirect" title="Royal borough">royal boroughs</a>; these are purely honorific titles, and do not alter the status of the district.  All boroughs and cities, and a few districts, are led by a <a href="/wiki/Mayors_in_England" title="Mayors in England">mayor</a> who in most cases is a ceremonial figure elected by the <a href="/wiki/Local_government_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Local government in the United Kingdom">district council</a>, but—after local government reform—is occasionally a <a href="/wiki/Elected_mayors_in_the_United_Kingdom" class="mw-redirect" title="Elected mayors in the United Kingdom">directly elected mayor</a> who makes most of the policy decisions instead of the council.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Districts_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Subdivisions_of_England" title="Subdivisions of England">Subdivisions of England</a></div>
<p>Prior to the establishment of districts in the 1890s, the basic unit of local government in England was the parish overseen by the <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">parish church</a> <a href="/wiki/Vestry#Vestry_committees_in_England_and_Wales" title="Vestry">vestry committee</a>. Vestries dealt with the administration of both <a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/parochial" class="extiw" title="wiktionary:parochial">parochial</a> and <a href="/wiki/Secular" class="mw-redirect" title="Secular">secular</a> governmental matters. Parishes were the successors of the manorial system and historically had been grouped into <a href="/wiki/Hundred_(county_subdivision)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hundred (county subdivision)">hundreds</a>. Hundreds once exercised some supervising administrative function. However, these powers ebbed away as more and more civic and <a href="/wiki/Judicial" class="mw-redirect" title="Judicial">judicial</a> powers were centred on <a href="/wiki/County_towns" class="mw-redirect" title="County towns">county towns</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> From 1834 these parishes were grouped into <a href="/wiki/Poor_Law_Union" class="mw-redirect" title="Poor Law Union">Poor Law Unions</a>, creating areas for administration of the <a href="/wiki/English_Poor_Laws" title="English Poor Laws">Poor Law</a>. These areas were later used for <a href="/wiki/Registration_district" title="Registration district">census registration</a> and as the basis for <a href="/wiki/Sanitary_district" title="Sanitary district">sanitary provision</a>. In 1894, based on these earlier subdivisions, the <a href="/wiki/Local_Government_Act_1894" title="Local Government Act 1894">Local Government Act 1894</a> created <a href="/wiki/Urban_district_(Great_Britain_and_Ireland)" title="Urban district (Great Britain and Ireland)">urban districts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rural_district" title="Rural district">rural districts</a> as sub-divisions of <a href="/wiki/Administrative_county" title="Administrative county">administrative counties</a>, which had been created in 1889. Another reform in 1900 created 28 <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan boroughs</a> as sub-divisions of the <a href="/wiki/County_of_London" title="County of London">County of London</a>. Meanwhile, from this date parish-level local government administration was transferred to <a href="/wiki/Civil_parish" title="Civil parish">civil parishes</a>.
</p><p>The setting-down of the current structure of districts in England began in 1965, when Greater London and its 32 <a href="/wiki/London_boroughs" title="London boroughs">London boroughs</a> were created. They are the oldest type of district still in use. In 1974, metropolitan counties and non-metropolitan counties (also known as "<a href="/wiki/Shire_counties" class="mw-redirect" title="Shire counties">shire counties</a>") were created across the rest of England and were split into <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">metropolitan districts</a> and <a href="/wiki/Non-metropolitan_district" title="Non-metropolitan district">non-metropolitan districts</a>. The status of the London boroughs and metropolitan districts changed in 1986, when they absorbed the functions and some of the powers of the metropolitan county councils and the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Council" title="Greater London Council">Greater London Council</a> which were abolished. In London power is now shared again, albeit on a different basis, with the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Authority" title="Greater London Authority">Greater London Authority</a>.
</p><p>During the 1990s a further kind of district was created, the <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authorities_in_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Unitary authorities in England">unitary authority</a>, which combined the functions and status of county and district.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metropolitan_boroughs">Metropolitan boroughs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Districts_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Metropolitan boroughs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_borough" title="Metropolitan borough">Metropolitan borough</a></div>
<p>Metropolitan boroughs are a subdivision of a <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_county" title="Metropolitan county">metropolitan county</a>. These are similar to unitary authorities, as the metropolitan county councils were abolished in 1986. Most of the powers of the county councils were devolved to the districts but some services are run by joint boards and organisations. The districts typically have populations of 174,000 to 1.1 million.
</p>
<h2><span id="Non-metropolitan_district_.28shire_district.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Non-metropolitan_district_(shire_district)">Non-metropolitan district (shire district)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Districts_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Non-metropolitan district (shire district)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Non-metropolitan_district" title="Non-metropolitan district">Non-metropolitan district</a></div>
<p>Non-metropolitan districts (also known as <b>shire districts</b>) are second-tier authorities, which share power with <a href="/wiki/County_council" title="County council">county councils</a>. They are subdivisions of <a href="/wiki/Shire_county" class="mw-redirect" title="Shire county">shire counties</a> and the most common type of district. These districts typically have populations of 25,000 to 200,000.
</p><p>In this two-tier system, county councils are responsible for some local services, such as education, social services, and roads, while district councils run other services, such as waste collection, local planning, and <a href="/wiki/Council_house" title="Council house">council housing</a>.
</p><p>The number of non-metropolitan districts has varied over time. Initially there were 296; after the creation of unitary authorities in the 1990s, 2000s, and 2010s, their numbers were reduced to 192.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Unitary_authorities">Unitary authorities</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Districts_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Unitary authorities">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Unitary_authorities_of_England" title="Unitary authorities of England">Unitary authorities of England</a></div>
<p>These are single-tier districts which are responsible for running all local services in their areas, combining both county and district functions. They were created in the mid-1990s out of non-metropolitan districts, and often cover large towns and cities as this is deemed to be more efficient than a two-tier structure. In addition, some of the smaller counties such as <a href="/wiki/Rutland" title="Rutland">Rutland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">Herefordshire</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a> are unitary authorities. There are a total of 55 unitary authorities, including two introduced in 2019.
</p><p>Unitary authorities are actually a slightly modified type of non-metropolitan district; most are established as individual counties containing a single district, with a district council but no county council. <a href="/wiki/Berkshire" title="Berkshire">Berkshire</a> is unusual, being a non-metropolitan county with no county council and six unitary authority districts. <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">Cornwall</a>, <a href="/wiki/County_Durham" title="County Durham">Durham</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Isle_of_Wight" title="Isle of Wight">Isle of Wight</a>, <a href="/wiki/Northumberland" title="Northumberland">Northumberland</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">Wiltshire</a> were established as counties with a single district, but have non-metropolitan county councils with no district council. In practice, these function in the same way as other unitary authorities.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="London_boroughs">London boroughs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Districts_of_England&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: London boroughs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/London_boroughs" title="London boroughs">London boroughs</a></div>
<p>The London boroughs are sub-divisions of <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">Greater London</a>. They were established in 1965. Between 1965 and 1986 a two-tier structure of government existed in Greater London and the boroughs shared power with the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Council" title="Greater London Council">Greater London Council</a> (GLC). When the GLC was abolished in 1986 they gained similar status to the unitary authorities. In 2000 the <a href="/wiki/Greater_London_Authority" title="Greater London Authority">Greater London Authority</a> was established and a two-tier structure was restored, albeit with a change to the balance of powers and responsibilities.
</p><p>Each London borough is responsible for many of the services within their area, <a href="/wiki/London_boroughs#London_borough_councils" title="London boroughs">such as schools, waste management, planning applications, social services, libraries and others</a>.
</p>
