<p><b>Metonymy</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/ɛ/: &#39;e&#39; in &#39;dress&#39;">ɛ</span><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span><span title="/ɒ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;body&#39;">ɒ</span><span title="&#39;n&#39; in &#39;nigh&#39;">n</span><span title="/ə/: &#39;a&#39; in &#39;about&#39;">ə</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="/i/: &#39;y&#39; in &#39;happy&#39;">i</span></span>/</a></span></span>)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> is a <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_speech" title="Figure of speech">figure of speech</a> in which a thing or concept is referred to by the name of something closely associated with that thing or concept.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metonymy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The words <i>metonymy</i> and <i>metonym</i> come from the <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">Greek</a> <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">μετωνυμία</span>, <i lang="el-Latn" title="Greek language text">metōnymía</i>, "a change of name", from <span lang="el" title="Greek language text">μετά</span>, <i lang="el-Latn" title="Greek language text">metá</i>, "after, beyond", and <span lang="el" title="Greek language text">-ωνυμία</span>, <i lang="el-Latn" title="Greek language text">-ōnymía</i>, a suffix that names figures of speech, from <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">ὄνυμα</span>, <i lang="el-Latn" title="Greek language text">ónyma</i> or <span lang="grc" title="Ancient Greek language text">ὄνομα</span>, <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek language text">ónoma</i>, "name".<sup id="cite_ref-Welsh_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Welsh-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metonymy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Metonymy and related figures of speech are common in everyday speech and writing. <a href="/wiki/Synecdoche" title="Synecdoche">Synecdoche</a> and <a href="/wiki/Metalepsis" title="Metalepsis">metalepsis</a> are considered specific types of metonymy. <a href="/wiki/Polysemy" title="Polysemy">Polysemy</a>, multiple meanings of a single word or phrase, sometimes results from relations of metonymy. Both metonymy and <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphor</a> involve the substitution of one term for another.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In metaphor, this substitution is based on some specific <a href="/wiki/Analogy" title="Analogy">analogy</a> between two things, whereas in metonymy the substitution is based on some understood association or <a href="/wiki/Contiguity_(psychology)" title="Contiguity (psychology)">contiguity</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-tompkins_and_lawley_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tompkins_and_lawley-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>American literary theorist <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Burke" title="Kenneth Burke">Kenneth Burke</a> considers metonymy as one of four "master <a href="/wiki/Trope_(literature)" title="Trope (literature)">tropes</a>": <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">metaphor</a>, metonymy, <a href="/wiki/Synecdoche" title="Synecdoche">synecdoche</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Irony" title="Irony">irony</a>. He discusses them in particular ways in his book <i><a href="/wiki/A_Grammar_of_Motives" class="mw-redirect" title="A Grammar of Motives">A Grammar of Motives</a></i>. Whereas <a href="/wiki/Roman_Jakobson" title="Roman Jakobson">Roman Jakobson</a> argued that the fundamental dichotomy in trope was between metaphor and metonymy, Burke claims that the fundamental dichotomy is between irony and synecdoche, which he also describes as the dichotomy between dialectic and representation, or again between reduction and perspective.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition to its use in everyday speech, metonymy is a figure of speech in some <a href="/wiki/Poetry" title="Poetry">poetry</a> and in much <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetoric</a>. Greek and Latin scholars of rhetoric made significant contributions to the study of metonymy.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Meaning_relationships">Meaning relationships</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metonymy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Meaning relationships">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-More_citations_needed_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metonymy&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p><a href="/wiki/Synecdoche" title="Synecdoche">Synecdoche</a>, in which a specific part of something is used to refer to the whole, is usually understood as a specific kind of metonymy. Sometimes an absolute distinction is made between a metonymy and a synecdoche, treating metonymy as different from, rather than inclusive of, synecdoche. There is a similar problem with the terms <a href="/wiki/Simile" title="Simile">simile</a> and metaphor.
</p><p>When the distinction is made, it is the following: when "A" is used to refer to "B", it is a synecdoche if A is a component of B or if B is a component of A, and a metonym if A is commonly associated with B but not part of its whole or a whole of its part. Thus, "20,000 hungry mouths to feed" is a synecdoche because mouths (A) are a part of the people (B) referred to. "Australia votes" is also a synecdoche because Australia is a whole of which the people who voted are a part. On the other hand, "The White House said" is metonymy, but not synecdoche, for the president and their staff, because, although the White House is associated with the president and their staff, the building is not a part of the people.
</p><p>Similarly, <a href="/wiki/Metalepsis" title="Metalepsis">metalepsis</a> is closely related to metonymy, and is sometimes understood as a specific kind of metonymy. Metalepsis is a figure of speech in which a word or a phrase from figurative speech is used in a new context. The new figure of speech refers to an existing one.<sup id="cite_ref-Bloom_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bloom-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> For example, in the idiom "lead foot", meaning someone who drives fast, <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> is a heavy substance, and a heavy foot on the <a href="/wiki/Throttle" title="Throttle">accelerator pedal</a> would cause a vehicle to go quickly. The use of "lead foot" to describe a person follows the intermediate substitution of "lead" for "heavy".<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The figure of speech is a "metonymy of a metonymy".<sup id="cite_ref-Bloom_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bloom-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The concept of metonymy also informs the nature of <a href="/wiki/Polysemy" title="Polysemy">polysemy</a>, i.e., how the same phonological form (word) has different semantic mappings (meanings). If the two meanings are unrelated, as in the word <i>pen</i> meaning both <i>writing instrument</i> and <i>enclosure</i>, they are considered <a href="/wiki/Homonym" title="Homonym">homonyms</a>. Within logical polysemies, a large class of mappings may be considered to be a case of metonymic transfer (e.g., "chicken" for the bird, as well as its meat; "crown" for the object, as well as the institution). Other cases where the meaning is polysemous, however, may turn out to be more metaphorical, e.g., "eye" as in the "eye of the needle".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Metaphor_and_metonymy">Metaphor and metonymy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metonymy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Metaphor and metonymy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Metaphor_and_metonymy" title="Metaphor and metonymy">Metaphor and metonymy</a></div>
<p>Metonymy works by the <a href="/wiki/Contiguity_(psychology)" title="Contiguity (psychology)">contiguity</a> (association) between two concepts, whereas the term "metaphor" is based upon their analogous similarity. When people use metonymy, they do not typically wish to transfer qualities from one referent to another as they do with metaphor.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> There is nothing press-like about reporters or crown-like about a monarch, but "the press" and "the crown" are both common metonyms. Some uses of figurative language may be understood as both metonymy and metaphor; for example, the relationship between "a crown" and a "king" could be interpreted metaphorically (i.e., the king, like his gold crown, could be seemingly stiff yet ultimately malleable, over-ornate, and consistently immobile).
</p><p>Two examples using the term "fishing" help clarify the distinction.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The phrase "to fish pearls" uses metonymy, drawing from "fishing" the idea of taking things from the ocean. What is carried across from "fishing fish" to "fishing pearls" is the domain of metonymy. In contrast, the metaphorical phrase "fishing for information" transfers the concept of fishing into a new domain. If someone is "fishing" for information, we do not imagine that the person is anywhere near the ocean; rather, we transpose elements of the action of fishing (waiting, hoping to catch something that cannot be seen, probing) into a new domain (a conversation). Thus, metaphor works by presenting a target set of meanings and using them to suggest a similarity between items, actions, or events in two domains, whereas metonymy calls up or references a specific domain (here, removing items from the sea).
</p><p>Sometimes, metaphor and metonymy may both be at work in the same figure of speech, or one could interpret a phrase metaphorically or metonymically. For example, the phrase "<a href="/wiki/Friends,_Romans,_countrymen,_lend_me_your_ears" title="Friends, Romans, countrymen, lend me your ears">lend me your ear</a>" could be analyzed in a number of ways. One could imagine the following interpretations:
</p>
<ul><li>Analyze "ear" metonymically first – "ear" means "attention" (because we use ears to pay attention to someone's speech). Now, when we hear the phrase "lending an ear (attention)", we stretch the base meaning of "lend" (to let someone borrow an object) to include the "lending" of non-material things (attention), but, beyond this slight extension of the verb, no metaphor is at work.</li>
<li>Imagine the whole phrase literally – imagine that the speaker literally borrows the listener's ear as a physical object (and the person's head with it). Then the speaker has temporary possession of the listener's ear, so the listener has granted the speaker temporary control over what the listener hears. We then interpret the phrase "lend me your ear" metaphorically to mean that the speaker wants the listener to grant the speaker temporary control over what the listener hears.</li>
<li>First, analyze the <a href="/wiki/Verb_phrase" title="Verb phrase">verb phrase</a> "lend me your ear" metaphorically to mean "turn your ear in my direction," since we know that, literally, lending a body part is nonsensical. Then, analyze the motion of ears metonymically – we associate "turning ears" with "paying attention," which is what the speaker wants the listeners to do.</li></ul>
<p>It is difficult to say which analysis above most closely represents the way a listener interprets the expression, and it is possible that different listeners analyse the phrase in different ways, or even in different ways at different times. Regardless, all three analyses yield the same interpretation. Thus, metaphor and metonymy, though different in their mechanism, work together seamlessly.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metonymy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/List_of_metonyms" title="List of metonyms">List of metonyms</a></div>
<p>Here are some broad kinds of relationships where metonymy is frequently used:
</p>
<ul><li>Containment: When one thing contains another, it can frequently be used metonymically, as when "dish" is used to refer not to a plate but to the food it contains, or as when the name of a building is used to refer to the entity it contains, as when "<a href="/wiki/The_White_House" class="mw-redirect" title="The White House">the White House</a>" or "<a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">the Pentagon</a>" are used to refer to the U.S. presidential staff or the military leadership, respectively.</li>
<li>A physical item, place, or body part used to refer to a related concept, such as "the bench" for the judicial profession, "stomach" or "belly" for "appetite" or hunger, "mouth" for speech, various terms for the genitalia for sexual desire or satisfaction of said desire, being "in diapers" for infancy, "palate" for taste, "the altar" or "the aisle" for marriage, "hand" for someone's responsibility for something ("he had a hand in it"), "head" or "brain" for mind or intelligence, or "nose" for concern about someone else's affairs, (as in "keep your nose out of my business"). A reference to <a href="/wiki/Timbuktu" title="Timbuktu">Timbuktu</a>, as in "from here to Timbuktu," usually means a place or idea is too far away or mysterious. Metonymy of objects or body parts for concepts is common in dreams.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Tools/instruments: Often a tool is used to signify the job it does or the person who does the job, as in the phrase "the press" (referring to the printing press), or as in the proverb, "The pen is mightier than the sword."</li>
<li>Product for process: This is a type of metonymy where the product of the activity stands for the activity itself. For example, in "The book is moving right along," <i>the book</i> refers to the process of writing or publishing.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li>Punctuation marks often stand metonymically for a meaning expressed by the punctuation mark. For example, "He's a big <i>question mark</i> to me" indicates that something is unknown.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In the same way, 'period' can be used to emphasise that a point is concluded or not to be challenged.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Synecdoche" title="Synecdoche">Synecdoche</a>: A part of something is often used for the whole, as when people refer to "head" of cattle or assistants are referred to as "hands." An example of this is the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_dollar" title="Canadian dollar">Canadian dollar</a>, referred to as the <a href="/wiki/Loonie" title="Loonie">loonie</a> for the image of a bird on the one-dollar coin. <a href="/wiki/United_States_one_hundred-dollar_bill" title="United States one hundred-dollar bill">United States one hundred-dollar bills</a> are often referred to as "Bens", "Benjamins" or "Franklins" because they bear a portrait of <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin" title="Benjamin Franklin">Benjamin Franklin</a>. Also, the whole of something is used for a part, as when people refer to a municipal employee as "the council" or police officers as "the law".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Toponyms" class="mw-redirect" title="Toponyms">Toponyms</a>: A country's <a href="/wiki/Capital_city" title="Capital city">capital city</a> or some location within the city is frequently used as a metonym for the country's government, such as <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">Washington, D.C.</a>, in the United States; <a href="/wiki/Ottawa" title="Ottawa">Ottawa</a> in Canada; <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a> in <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>; <a href="/wiki/New_Delhi" title="New Delhi">New Delhi</a> in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>; <a href="/wiki/Downing_Street" title="Downing Street">Downing Street</a> or <a href="/wiki/Whitehall" title="Whitehall">Whitehall</a> in the <a href="/wiki/UK" class="mw-redirect" title="UK">UK</a>; and the <a href="/wiki/Moscow_Kremlin" title="Moscow Kremlin">Kremlin</a> in <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>. Similarly, other important places, such as <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madison_Avenue" title="Madison Avenue">Madison Avenue</a>, <a href="/wiki/Silicon_Valley" title="Silicon Valley">Silicon Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hollywood" title="Hollywood">Hollywood</a>, <a href="/wiki/Las_Vegas" title="Las Vegas">Vegas</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Metro_Detroit" title="Metro Detroit">Detroit</a> are commonly used to refer to the industries that are located there (<a href="/wiki/Finance" title="Finance">finance</a>, <a href="/wiki/Advertising" title="Advertising">advertising</a>, <a href="/wiki/High_tech" title="High tech">high technology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">entertainment</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gambling" title="Gambling">gambling</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Big_Three_automobile_manufacturers" class="mw-redirect" title="Big Three automobile manufacturers">motor vehicles</a>, respectively). Such usage may persist even when the industries in question have moved elsewhere, for example, <i><a href="/wiki/Fleet_Street" title="Fleet Street">Fleet Street</a></i> continues to be used as a metonymy for the British national <a href="/wiki/Journalism" title="Journalism">press</a>, though it is no longer located in the physical street of that name.</li></ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Places_and_institutions">Places and institutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metonymy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Places and institutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>A place is often used as a metonym for a government or other official institutions, for example, <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">Brussels</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Institutions_of_the_European_Union" title="Institutions of the European Union">institutions of the European Union</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Hague" title="The Hague">The Hague</a> for the <a href="/wiki/International_Court_of_Justice" title="International Court of Justice">International Court of Justice</a> or <a href="/wiki/International_Criminal_Court" title="International Criminal Court">International Criminal Court</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nairobi" title="Nairobi">Nairobi</a> for the <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Kenya" title="Government of Kenya">government of Kenya</a>, the <a href="/wiki/White_House" title="White House">White House</a> and <a href="/wiki/Capitol_Hill" title="Capitol Hill">Capitol Hill</a> for the executive and legislative branches, respectively, of the United States federal government, or <a href="/wiki/Foggy_Bottom" title="Foggy Bottom">Foggy Bottom</a> for the <a href="/wiki/US_State_Department" class="mw-redirect" title="US State Department">US State Department</a>. A place can represent an entire industry: for instance <a href="/wiki/Wall_Street" title="Wall Street">Wall Street</a> is often used metonymically to describe the entire U.S. <a href="/wiki/Financial_center" class="mw-redirect" title="Financial center">financial and corporate banking sector</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gibbs_jr._16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gibbs_jr.-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Common nouns and phrases can also be metonyms: <a href="/wiki/Red_tape" title="Red tape">red tape</a> can stand for <a href="/wiki/Bureaucracy" title="Bureaucracy">bureaucracy</a>, whether or not that bureaucracy actually uses red tape to bind documents. In <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_realm" title="Commonwealth realm">Commonwealth realms</a>, <a href="/wiki/The_Crown" title="The Crown">The Crown</a> is a metonym for the <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">state</a> in all its aspects.<sup id="cite_ref-Jackson20_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jackson20-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Rhetoric_in_ancient_history">Rhetoric in ancient history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metonymy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Rhetoric in ancient history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-One_source plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-one_source" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="Question book-new.svg" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>relies largely or entirely on a single <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Reliable sources">source</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Relevant discussion may be found on the <a href="/wiki/Talk:Metonymy##" title="Talk:Metonymy">talk page</a>. Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metonymy&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by introducing <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">citations</a> to additional sources.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">December 2013</span>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p><a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western culture</a> studied poetic language and deemed it to be <a href="/wiki/Rhetoric" title="Rhetoric">rhetoric</a>. A. Al-Sharafi supports this concept in his book <i>Textual Metonymy</i>, "Greek rhetorical scholarship at one time became entirely poetic scholarship."<sup id="cite_ref-Al-Sharafi_2004_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Sharafi_2004-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Philosopher" title="Philosopher">Philosophers</a> and rhetoricians thought that metaphors were the primary figurative language used in rhetoric.  Metaphors served as a better means to attract the audience’s attention because the audience had to read between the lines in order to get an understanding of what the speaker was trying to say. Others did not think of metonymy as a good rhetorical method because metonymy did not involve symbolism. Al-Sharafi explains, "This is why they undermined practical and purely referential discourse because it was seen as banal and not containing anything new, strange or shocking."<sup id="cite_ref-Al-Sharafi_2004_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Sharafi_2004-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Greek scholars contributed to the definition of metonymy. For example, <a href="/wiki/Isocrates" title="Isocrates">Isocrates</a> worked to define the difference between poetic language and non-poetic language by saying that, "Prose writers are handicapped in this regard because their discourse has to conform to the forms and terms used by the citizens and to those arguments which are precise and relevant to the subject-matter." In other words, Isocrates proposes here that metaphor is a distinctive feature of poetic language because it conveys the experience of the world afresh and provides a kind of defamiliarisation in the way the citizens perceive the world.<sup id="cite_ref-Al-Sharafi_2004_18-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Sharafi_2004-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Democritus" title="Democritus">Democritus</a> described metonymy by saying, "Metonymy, that is the fact that words and meaning change."<sup id="cite_ref-Al-Sharafi_2004_18-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Sharafi_2004-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>  <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> discussed different definitions of metaphor, regarding one type as what we know to be metonymy today.
</p><p>Latin scholars also had an influence on metonymy. The treatise <i><a href="/wiki/Rhetorica_ad_Herennium" title="Rhetorica ad Herennium">Rhetorica ad Herennium</a></i> states metonymy as, "the figure which draws from an object closely akin or associated an expression suggesting the object meant, but not called by its own name."<sup id="cite_ref-Al-Sharafi_2004_18-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Sharafi_2004-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> The author describes the process of metonymy to us saying that we first figure out what a word means. We then figure out that word’s relationship with other words. We understand and then call the word by a name that it is associated with. "Perceived as such then metonymy will be a figure of speech in which there is a process of abstracting a relation of proximity between two words to the extent that one will be used in place of another."<sup id="cite_ref-Al-Sharafi_2004_18-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Al-Sharafi_2004-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">Cicero</a> viewed metonymy as more of a stylish rhetorical method and described it as being based on words, but motivated by style.
</p>
<h2><span id="Jakobson.2C_structuralism.2C_and_realism"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Jakobson,_structuralism,_and_realism">Jakobson, structuralism, and realism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metonymy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Jakobson, structuralism, and realism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Metonymy became important in French <a href="/wiki/Structuralism" title="Structuralism">structuralism</a> through the work of <a href="/wiki/Roman_Jakobson" title="Roman Jakobson">Roman Jakobson</a>. In his 1956 essay "The Metaphoric and Metonymic Poles", Jakobson relates metonymy to the linguistic practice of [syntagmatic] combination and to the literary practice of <a href="/wiki/Literary_realism" title="Literary realism">realism</a>. He explains:
</p>
<blockquote><p>The primacy of the metaphoric process in the literary schools of Romanticism and symbolism has been repeatedly acknowledged, but it is still insufficiently realized that it is the predominance of metonymy which underlies and actually predetermines the so-called 'realistic' trend, which belongs to an intermediary stage between the decline of Romanticism and the rise of symbolism and is opposed to both.  Following the path of contiguous relationships, the realistic author metonymically digresses from the plot to the atmosphere and from the characters to the setting in space and time.  He is fond of synecdochic details.  In the scene of <a href="/wiki/Anna_Karenina" title="Anna Karenina">Anna Karenina</a>'s suicide Tolstoy's artistic attention is focused on the heroine's handbag; and in <i><a href="/wiki/War_and_Peace" title="War and Peace">War and Peace</a></i> the synecdoches "hair on the upper lip" or "bare shoulders" are used by the same writer to stand for the female characters to whom these features belong.<sup id="cite_ref-Poles_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Poles-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup></p></blockquote>
<p>Jakobson's theories were important for <a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude Lévi-Strauss">Claude Lévi-Strauss</a>, <a href="/wiki/Roland_Barthes" title="Roland Barthes">Roland Barthes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Lacan" title="Jacques Lacan">Jacques Lacan</a>, and others.<sup id="cite_ref-Dirven_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Dirven-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Dreams can use metonyms.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Metonyms_and_art">Metonyms and art</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Metonymy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Metonyms and art">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Metonyms can also be wordless. For example, <a href="/wiki/Roman_Jakobson" title="Roman Jakobson">Roman Jakobson</a><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> argued that cubist art relied heavily on nonlinguistic metonyms, while surrealist art relied more on metaphors.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Lakoff" title="Lakoff">Lakoff</a> and Turner<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> argued that all words are metonyms: “Words stand for the concepts they express.” Some artists have used actual words as metonyms in their paintings. For example, <a href="/wiki/Mir%C3%B3" class="mw-redirect" title="Miró">Miró</a>’s 1925 painting "Photo: This is the Color of My Dreams" has the word “photo” to represent the image of his dreams. This painting comes from a series of paintings called peintures-poésies (paintings-poems) which reflect Miró’s interest in dreams and the subconscious<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> and the relationship of words, images, and thoughts. <a href="/wiki/Picasso" class="mw-redirect" title="Picasso">Picasso</a>, in his 1911 painting "Pipe Rack and Still Life on Table" inserts the word “Ocean” rather than painting an ocean: These paintings by Miró and Picasso are, in a sense, the reverse of a <a href="/wiki/Rebus" title="Rebus">rebus</a>: the word stands for the picture, instead of the picture standing for the word.
</p>
