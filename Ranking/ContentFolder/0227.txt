Commonly used to express affection, it is a form of endearment and amusement. As a concept, it is distinct from both <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stage_name" title="Stage name">stage name</a>, and also from a title (for example, City of Fountains), although there may be overlap in these concepts. The term '<a href="/wiki/Hypocoristic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypocoristic">hypocoristic</a>' refers to a nickname of affection between those in love or with a close emotional bond.
</p><p>"<b>Moniker</b>" also means a nickname or personal name.<sup id="cite_ref-merriam-webster_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-merriam-webster-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The compound word <i>ekename</i>, literally meaning "additional name", was attested as early as 1303.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> This word was derived from the <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">Old English</a> phrase <i>eaca</i> "an increase", related to <i>eacian</i> "to increase".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> By the 15th century, the <a href="/wiki/Rebracketing" title="Rebracketing">misdivision of the syllables</a> of the phrase "an ekename" led to its rephrasing as "a nekename".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Though the spelling has changed, the pronunciation and meaning of the word have remained relatively stable ever since.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conventions_in_various_languages">Conventions in various languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Conventions in various languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>To inform an audience or readership of a person's nickname without actually calling them by their nickname, English nicknames are generally represented in quotes between the bearer's first and last names (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight David "Ike" Eisenhower</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Bubba_Franks" title="Bubba Franks">Daniel Lamont "Bubba" Franks</a></i>, etc.). However, it is also common for the nickname to be identified after a comma following the full real name or later in the body of the text, such as in an obituary (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Frankie_Frisch" title="Frankie Frisch">Frankie Frisch, "The Fordham Flash"</a></i>). The middle name is generally eliminated (if there is one), especially in speech. Like English, German uses (German-style) quotation marks between the first and last names (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Niki_Lauda" title="Niki Lauda">Andreas Nikolaus „Niki“ Lauda</a></i>). Other languages may use other conventions; for example, Italian writes the nickname after the full name followed by <i>detto</i> 'called' (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Salvatore_Schillaci" title="Salvatore Schillaci">Salvatore Schillaci detto Totò</a></i>), in Spanish the nickname is written in formal contexts at the end in quotes following <i>alias</i> (e.g. <i>Alfonso Tostado, alias «el Abulense»</i>), and Slovenian represents nicknames after a dash or hyphen (e.g., <i><a href="/wiki/Franc_Rozman_%E2%80%93_Stane" class="mw-redirect" title="Franc Rozman – Stane">Franc Rozman – Stane</a></i>). The latter may cause confusion because it resembles an English convention sometimes used for <a href="/wiki/Married_and_maiden_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Married and maiden names">married and maiden names</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses_in_various_societies">Uses in various societies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Uses in various societies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Viking" class="mw-redirect" title="Viking">Viking</a> societies, many people had <i>heiti</i>, <i>viðrnefni</i>, or <i>kenningarnöfn</i> (Old Norse terms for nicknames) which were used in addition to, or instead of the first name. In some circumstances, the giving of a nickname had a special status in Viking society in that it created a relationship between the name maker and the recipient of the nickname, to the extent that the creation of a nickname also often entailed a formal <a href="/wiki/Ceremony" title="Ceremony">ceremony</a> and an <a href="/wiki/Gift_exchange" class="mw-redirect" title="Gift exchange">exchange of gifts</a> known in Old Norse as <i>nafnfestr</i> ('fastening a name').
</p><p>Slaves have often used nicknames, so that the master who heard about someone doing something could not identify the slave. In <a href="/wiki/Capoeira" title="Capoeira">capoeira</a>, a Brazilian martial art, the slaves had nicknames to protect them from being caught, as practising capoeira was illegal for decades.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In Anglo-American culture, a nickname is often based on a shortening of a person's proper name. However, in other societies, this may not necessarily be the case. For example: "my nickname is farmer Phil"
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Indian_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian culture">Indian</a> society, for example, generally people have at least one nickname (<i><a href="/wiki/Call_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Call name">call name</a></i> or <i>affection name</i>) and these affection names are generally not related to the person's proper name. Indian nicknames very often are a trivial word or a <i>diminutive</i> (such as <i>Bablu</i>, <i>Dabbu</i>, <i>Banti</i>, <i>Babli</i>, <i>Gudiya</i>, <i>Golu</i>, <i>Sonu</i>, <i>Chhotu</i>, <i>Raju</i>, <i>Adi</i>, <i>Ritu</i>, etc.).
</p><p>In Hispanic culture, a nickname is used for a term of endearment and family love, for example: "Papi". It is a colloquial term for “daddy” in Spanish, but in many Spanish-speaking cultures, particularly in the Caribbean, it is often used as a general term of affection for any man, whether it's a relative, friend, or love.
</p><p>In Australian society, Australian men will often give ironic nicknames. For example, a man with <a href="/wiki/Red_hair" title="Red hair">red hair</a> will be given the nickname 'Blue' or 'Bluey'. A tall man will be called 'Shorty', an obese person 'Slim' and so on.
</p><p>In England, some nicknames are traditionally associated with a person's surname. A man with the surname 'Clark' will be nicknamed 'Nobby': the surname 'Miller' will have the nickname 'Dusty' (alluding to the flour dust of a miller at work): the surname 'Adams' has the nickname 'Nabby'. There are several other nicknames linked traditionally with a person's surname, including Chalky White, Bunny Warren, Tug Wilson, and Spud Baker. Other English nicknames allude to a person's origins. A Scotsman may be nicknamed 'Jock', an Irishman 'Paddy' (alluding to St Patrick, the patron saint of Ireland) or 'Mick' (alluding to the preponderance of Roman Catholicism in Ireland), and a Welshman may be nicknamed 'Taffy'. Some nicknames referred ironically to a person's physical characteristics, such as 'Lofty' for a short person, or 'Curly' for a bald man. Traditional English nicknaming - usually for men rather than women - was common through the first half of the 20th century, and was frequently used in the armed services during World War I and World War II, but has become less common since then.
</p><p>In Chinese culture, nicknames are frequently used within a community among relatives, friends and neighbors. A typical southern Chinese nickname often begins with a "阿" followed by another character, usually the last character of the person's given name. For example, Taiwanese politician <a href="/wiki/Chen_Shui-bian" title="Chen Shui-bian">Chen Shui-bian</a> (陈水扁) is sometimes referred as "阿扁" (A-Bian). In many Chinese communities of Southeast Asia, nicknames may also connote one's occupation or status. For example, the landlord might be known simply as <i>Towkay</i> (<a href="/wiki/Hokkien" title="Hokkien">Hokkien</a> for "boss") to his tenants or workers while a bread seller would be called "Mianbao Shu" 面包叔 (literally, Uncle Bread). Among <a href="/wiki/Cantonese_language" class="mw-redirect" title="Cantonese language">Cantonese</a>-speaking communities, the character "仔" (pronounced "zai") may be used in a similar context of "Junior" in Western naming practices.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Performing_arts_and_literature">Performing arts and literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Performing arts and literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many writers, performing artists, and actors have nicknames, which may develop into a <a href="/wiki/Stage_name" title="Stage name">stage name</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a>. A <a href="/wiki/Bardic_name" title="Bardic name">bardic name</a> may also result from a nickname. Many writers have pen names which they use instead of their real names. Famous writers with a <a href="/wiki/Pen_name" title="Pen name">pen name</a> go as far back as <a href="/wiki/Plato#Name" title="Plato">Plato</a> (according to a late tradition) and <a href="/wiki/Paul_the_Apostle#Names" title="Paul the Apostle">Paul</a>, and see also this <a href="/wiki/List_of_pen_names" title="List of pen names">List of pen names</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">Sports</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sports">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Athletic_nickname" title="Athletic nickname">Athletic nickname</a> and <a href="/wiki/List_of_sportspeople_by_nickname" title="List of sportspeople by nickname">List of sportspeople by nickname</a></div>
<p>It is not uncommon for sportspeople or a sports team to have nicknames. Some, such as those of sports clubs or athletic teams, are official while others are adopted over time.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Computing">Computing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Computing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/User_(computing)" title="User (computing)">User (computing)</a></div>
<p>In the context of information technology, a nickname (usually called a <b>nick</b>) is a common synonym for the <i>screen name</i> or <i>handle</i> of a <a href="/wiki/User_(computing)" title="User (computing)">user</a>. In <a href="/wiki/Computer_network_programming" title="Computer network programming"><b>computer networks</b></a> it has become a common practice for every person to also have one or more nicknames for the purposes of <a href="/wiki/Pseudonymity" title="Pseudonymity">pseudonymity</a>, to avoid <a href="/wiki/Ambiguity" title="Ambiguity">ambiguity</a>, or simply because the natural name or technical <a href="/wiki/IP_address" title="IP address">address</a> would be too long to type or take too much space on the <a href="/wiki/Computer_display" class="mw-redirect" title="Computer display">screen</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="People">People</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: People">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Nicknames are usually awarded to a person and they are not always chosen by the recipient themselves. Some nicknames are derogatory <a href="/wiki/Name_calling" title="Name calling">name calls</a>. Note: the majority of the following examples are <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a> usage. Please see <a href="/wiki/Bullying" title="Bullying">bullying</a> definition.
Nicknames may be based on a person's name or various attributes. Attributes upon which a nickname may be based include:
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Title">Title</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Title">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nicknames may refer to a person's occupation, social standing, or title. They may also refer to characteristics of a person.
</p>
<ul><li>"<a href="/wiki/Bones_(disambiguation)#Fictional_characters" class="mw-disambig" title="Bones (disambiguation)">Bones</a>" or "Sawbones" for a <a href="/wiki/Temperance_Brennan_(Bones)" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperance Brennan (Bones)">forensic scientist</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leonard_McCoy" title="Leonard McCoy">surgeon</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Mortician" class="mw-redirect" title="Mortician">mortician</a></li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/DOC_(disambiguation)#People" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="DOC (disambiguation)">Doc</a>" for a doctor or, in the military, medic.</li>
<li>"Sparky" for an <a href="/wiki/Electrician" title="Electrician">electrician</a> or <a href="/wiki/Radio_operator" title="Radio operator">radio operator</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Geek" title="Geek">Geek</a> for a computer technician</li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/Sarge_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Sarge (disambiguation)">Sarge</a>" for a military <a href="/wiki/Sergeant" title="Sergeant">Sergeant</a> as in the comic strip <i><a href="/wiki/Beetle_Bailey" title="Beetle Bailey">Beetle Bailey</a></i></li>
<li>"Lou" for a Lieutenant (for example, a police lieutenant)</li>
<li>Similarly, "Chief" for a police or fire chief or <a href="/wiki/Chief_petty_officer" title="Chief petty officer">chief petty officer</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Moneybags" class="mw-redirect" title="Moneybags">Moneybags</a> for a wealthy person.</li></ul>
<h3><span id="Physical_characteristics.2C_personality.2C_or_lifestyle"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics,_personality,_or_lifestyle">Physical characteristics, personality, or lifestyle</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics, personality, or lifestyle">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">Physical characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:277px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Weimaraner_wb.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Weimaraner_wb.jpg/275px-Weimaraner_wb.jpg" decoding="async" width="275" height="207" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Weimaraner_wb.jpg/413px-Weimaraner_wb.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/32/Weimaraner_wb.jpg 2x" data-file-width="500" data-file-height="376" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Weimaraner_wb.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>The <a href="/wiki/Weimaraner" title="Weimaraner">Weimaraner</a>'s coat color led to its nickname of the "Silver Ghost".</div></div></div>
<p>Nicknames can be a descriptor of a physical characteristic or the opposite of a physical characteristic. In English, such nicknames are often considered offensive or <a href="/wiki/Derogatory" class="mw-redirect" title="Derogatory">derogatory</a>, unless the nickname is based on a trait that is viewed positively.
Some examples of nicknames related to physical characteristics include:
</p>
<ul><li>Weight: "Fatso" or "Slim" for a person who is <a href="/wiki/Overweight" title="Overweight">overweight</a> or thin, respectively.</li>
<li>Height: "Beanpole" or "Long John" (or other name) for a person who is tall, "Shortie" or "small-fry" for a <a href="/wiki/Short_stature" title="Short stature">short person</a>.</li>
<li>Hair colour: "Red", "Ginger", "Ranga", or "Bluey" for a person with red hair. "Blondie" a girl with blonde hair.</li>
<li>Type of hair: "Curley" or "Cue Ball" for a person without hair as in "Curley" from "<a href="/wiki/The_Three_Stooges" title="The Three Stooges">The Three Stooges</a>"</li>
<li>Baldness: "Chrome dome" for a person whose scalp reflects the light</li>
<li>Complexion: "Pinky" for a person with <a href="/wiki/Rosacea" title="Rosacea">Rosacea</a>, "Zit" or "pizza-face" for severe <a href="/wiki/Acne" title="Acne">acne</a>, various <a href="/wiki/Racial_slurs" class="mw-redirect" title="Racial slurs">racial slurs</a> for skin color.</li>
<li>Hand dominance: "Lefty" for a <a href="/wiki/Left-handed" class="mw-redirect" title="Left-handed">left-handed</a> person.</li></ul>
<p>Sometimes nicknames are based on things that are not a part of a person's body. Such nicknames can be temporary.
</p>
<ul><li>"Four-eyes" for a person with <a href="/wiki/Glasses" title="Glasses">glasses</a></li>
<li>"<a href="/wiki/Rail_tracks" class="mw-redirect" title="Rail tracks">Train tracks</a>", "tin teeth", "metal mouth", or "braceface" for a person with <a href="/wiki/Dental_braces" title="Dental braces">braces</a>, such as Sharon Spitz on the animated series <i><a href="/wiki/Braceface" title="Braceface">Braceface</a></i></li></ul>
<p>Occasionally the description can be ironically reversed. Thus in schoolboy or army usage "Lofty" may be applied to somebody very short or "Titch" to an unusually tall person.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>All of the above examples would be offensive in most contexts.
</p>

</p>

<li>Cautious: "Nervous Nellie"</li>
<li>Tired Demeanor: "Sleepy" as in a dwarf from <a href="/wiki/Snow_White" title="Snow White">Snow White</a></li>
<li>Pessimistic: "Sad Sack"</li>
<li>Negative: "Debbie Downer"</li>
<li>Glamorous: "Stunning Sign"</li>
<li>Boring: "Plain Jane"</li>
<li>Typical: "Average Joe"</li>
<li>Strong-willed: "<a href="/wiki/The_Iron_Lady" class="mw-redirect" title="The Iron Lady">The Iron Lady</a>"</li>
<li>Successful: "<a href="/wiki/Superstar" title="Superstar">Superstar</a>"</li></ul>

<p>A nickname may allude to a person's apparent <a href="/wiki/Intelligence" title="Intelligence">intelligence</a> (though often used sarcastically):
</p>
<ul><li>Encyclopedia, as in <a href="/wiki/Donald_Sobol" class="mw-redirect" title="Donald Sobol">Donald Sobol</a>'s fictional child detective <a href="/wiki/Encyclopedia_Brown" title="Encyclopedia Brown">Leroy "Encyclopedia" Brown</a></li>

<li><a href="/wiki/Brainiac_(character)" title="Brainiac (character)">Brainiac</a>, as in the fictional <a href="/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a> character.</li></ul>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Abbreviation_or_modification">Abbreviation or modification</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Abbreviation or modification">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A nickname can be a <a href="/wiki/Abbreviation" title="Abbreviation">shortened</a> or modified variation on a person's real name.
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Contraction_(grammar)" title="Contraction (grammar)">Contractions</a> of longer names: Margaret to <a href="/wiki/Greta_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Greta (disambiguation)">Greta</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Initials" class="mw-redirect" title="Initials">Initials</a>: Using the first letters of a person's first and middle/last name, e.g. "DJ" for Daniel James</li>
<li>Dropping letters: With many nicknames, one or more letters, usually R, are dropped: <a href="/wiki/Fanny_(given_name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Fanny (given name)">Fanny</a> from Frances, <a href="/wiki/Walt" title="Walt">Walt</a> from Walter.</li>
<li>Phonetic spelling&#160;: Sometimes a nickname is created through the phonetic spelling of a name: Len from Leonard.</li>
<li>Letter swapping: During the <a href="/wiki/Middle_ages" class="mw-redirect" title="Middle ages">middle ages</a>, the letter R would often be swapped for either L or D: <a href="/wiki/Hal_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Hal (disambiguation)">Hal</a> from Harry, <a href="/wiki/Molly_(name)" title="Molly (name)">Molly</a> from Mary, <a href="/wiki/Sadie_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Sadie (disambiguation)">Sadie</a> from Sarah, from <a href="/wiki/Robert" title="Robert">Robert</a>: Hob, Dob, Rob, Bob and Nob, from <a href="/wiki/Richard" title="Richard">Richard</a>: Rick, Dick, and Hick; Bill from <a href="/wiki/William_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="William (name)">Will</a> (which in turn comes from William), and Margaret: Peg, and Meg.</li>
<li>In 19th-century frontier America, Mary and Molly were often given the nickname <a href="/wiki/Polly" title="Polly">Polly</a>.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_portions">Name portions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Name portions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul><li>Front of name: Sometimes a nickname can come from the beginning of a given name: <a href="/wiki/Chris" title="Chris">Chris</a> from Christopher/Christina; <a href="/wiki/Ed_(disambiguation)#Ed_(or_ED)_as_a_name" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Ed (disambiguation)">Ed</a> from Edward, Edmond, Edgar or Edwin, <a href="/wiki/IZ_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="IZ (disambiguation)">Iz</a> or Izzy from Isaac, Isaiah, Isidore, Isabel, or Isabella; <a href="/wiki/Joe_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Joe (disambiguation)">Joe</a> or Jo from Joseph, Josephine, or Joanna.</li>
<li>End of name: Drew from Andrew, Xander from Alexander, Enzo or Renzo from Lorenzo, Beth from Elizabeth, Bel, Bell, Bella or Belle from Isabelle/Isabella</li>
<li>Middle of name: Liz from Elizabeth, Tori from Victoria or Del or Della from Adelaide</li>
<li>Addition of diminutives: Before the 17th century, most nicknames had the diminutive ending "-in" or "-kin", where the ending was attached to the first syllable: Watkin for Walter via Wat-kin;  Hobkin from Robert via Hob-kin; or Thompkin from Thomas via Thom-Kin. While most of these have died away, a few remain, such as <a href="/wiki/Robin_(name)" title="Robin (name)">Robin</a> (Rob-in, from Robert), Hank (Hen-Kin from <a href="/wiki/Henry_(given_name)" title="Henry (given name)">Henry</a>), <a href="/wiki/Jack_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Jack (disambiguation)">Jack</a> (Jan-kin from John), and <a href="/wiki/Colin_(given_name)" title="Colin (given name)">Colin</a> (Col-in from Nicolas).</li>
<li>Many nicknames drop the final one or two letters and add ether ie/ee/y as a diminutive ending: Davy from David, Charlie from Charles, Mikey from Michael, Jimmy from James and Marty from Martin.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Acronym_and_initialism" class="mw-redirect" title="Acronym and initialism">Initialization</a>, which forms a nickname from a person's initials: <a href="/wiki/A.C._Slater" class="mw-redirect" title="A.C. Slater">A.C. Slater</a> from Albert Clifford Slater, or Dubya for <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">George W. Bush</a>, a Texan pronunciation of the name of the letter 'W', President Bush's middle initial.</li>
<li>Nicknames are sometimes based on a person's last name ("Tommo" for Bill Thompson, "Campo" for <a href="/wiki/David_Campese" title="David Campese">David Campese</a>) or a combination of first and last name such as "A-Rod" for <a href="/wiki/Alex_Rodriguez" title="Alex Rodriguez">Alex Rodriguez</a>)</li>
<li>Loose ties to a person's name with an attached suffix: Gazza for English footballer <a href="/wiki/Paul_Gascoigne" title="Paul Gascoigne">Paul Gascoigne</a> (though used more widely in Australia for <a href="/wiki/Gary_(given_name)" title="Gary (given name)">Gary</a>) and similar "zza" forms (<a href="/wiki/Michael_Heseltine" title="Michael Heseltine">Hezza</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Prescott" title="John Prescott">Prezza</a>, etc.) for other prominent personalities whose activities are frequently reported in the British press. (See also <a href="/wiki/Oxford_%22-er%22" title="Oxford &quot;-er&quot;">Oxford "-er"</a> for a similar but wider phenomenon.)</li>
<li>Use of the <a href="/wiki/Second_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Second name">second name</a>.</li>
<li>Combination of first and middle name, or variations of a person's first and middle name. For example, a person may have the name Mary Elizabeth but has the nickname "Maz" or "Miz" by combining Mary and Liz.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship">Relationship</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Relationship">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A nickname may refer to the relationship with the person. This is a <a href="/wiki/Term_of_endearment" title="Term of endearment">term of endearment</a>.
</p>
<ul><li>In <a href="/wiki/Japanese_culture" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese culture">Japanese culture</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_honorifics" title="Japanese honorifics">Japanese honorifics</a> are designed so that a term of endearment conveys the exact status of the relationship between two people. However, the recipient of the honorific is allowed to restrict the use when used by a certain person.</li></ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Surname">Surname</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Surname">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A few surnames have a generic and traditional nickname, at least in England. Examples of this are:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Nobby" title="Nobby">Nobby</a>  for Clark or Clarke</li>
<li>Dusty for Miller</li>
<li>Chalkie for White</li>
<li>Bunny for Reed</li>
<li>Yosser for Hughes</li>
<li>Smudger for Smith</li></ul>
<p>To avoid confusion between peer groups with the same given names, surnames may be used as a nickname. Also common prefixes for names can be used as a nickname:
</p>
<ul><li>Mac for someone with the name Macmillan, MacIntyre, McCarthy, M'Clure, etc.</li>
<li>Fitz for someone with the name Fitzgerald, FitzPatrick, etc.</li></ul>
<p>And other variations on the surname, such as:
</p>
<ul><li>Brownie for someone with the name Brown</li>
<li>Jeff for someone with the name Geoffrey, Jeffry, Jeffrys, etc.</li>
<li>Klu (or Ski) for someone with the name Kluszewski</li>
<li>Smittie (or Smitty) for someone with the name Smith, Smythe, Goldsmith, etc.</li></ul>
<h3><span id="Action.2Fincident"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Action/incident">Action/incident</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Action/incident">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A specific incident or action can sometimes generate a nickname:
</p>
<ul><li><a href="/wiki/Capability_Brown" title="Capability Brown">Capability Brown</a> because he would frequently say to a client that his landscape was "capable of improvement".</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Ali_Hassan_al-Majid" title="Ali Hassan al-Majid">Chemical Ali</a> and <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Saeed_al-Sahhaf" title="Muhammad Saeed al-Sahhaf">Comical Ali</a>.</li>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Titles_of_geographical_places">Titles of geographical places</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Titles of geographical places">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many geographical places have titles, or alternative names, which have positive implications. Paris, for example, is the "City of Light", Venice is "La Serenissima", and New Jersey is the "Garden State". It is not correct to call these titles nicknames; these alternative names are often used to boost the status of such places, contrary to the usual role of a nickname.
Many places or communities, particularly in the USA, adopt titles because they can help in establishing a civic identity, help outsiders recognize a community or attract people to a community, promote civic pride, and build community unity.<sup id="cite_ref-Wisconsin_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wisconsin-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Titles and slogans that successfully create a new community "ideology or myth"<sup id="cite_ref-Branding_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Branding-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> are also believed to have economic value.<sup id="cite_ref-Wisconsin_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wisconsin-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Their economic value is difficult to measure,<sup id="cite_ref-Wisconsin_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wisconsin-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> but there are anecdotal reports of cities that have achieved substantial economic benefits by "branding" themselves by adopting new slogans.<sup id="cite_ref-Branding_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Branding-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>By contrast, older city nicknames may be critical: <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">London</a> is still occasionally referred to as "The Smoke" in memory of its notorious "pea-souper" <a href="/wiki/Smog" title="Smog">smogs</a> (smoke-filled fogs) of the 19th and early 20th centuries, and <a href="/wiki/Edinburgh" title="Edinburgh">Edinburgh</a> was "Auld Reekie" for the same reason, as countless coal fires polluted its atmosphere.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collective_nicknames_of_inhabitants_of_a_geographical_place">Collective nicknames of inhabitants of a geographical place</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nickname&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Collective nicknames of inhabitants of a geographical place">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Besides or replacing the <a href="/wiki/Demonym" title="Demonym">demonym</a>, some places have collective nicknames for their inhabitants. Many examples of this practice are found in <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">Wallonia</a> and in Belgium in general, where such a nickname is referred to in French as "<a href="/wiki/Blason_populaire" title="Blason populaire">Blason populaire</a>".
</p>
