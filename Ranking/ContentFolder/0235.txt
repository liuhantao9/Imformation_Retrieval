<p>A <b>pen name</b> (<i>nom de plume</i> or <i>literary double</i>) is a <a href="/wiki/Pseudonym" title="Pseudonym">pseudonym</a> (or, in some cases, a variant form of a real name) adopted by an <a href="/wiki/Author" title="Author">author</a> and printed on the title page or by-line of their works in place of their real name. A pen name may be used to make the author's name more distinctive, to disguise the author's gender, to distance the author from their other works, to protect the author from retribution for their writings, to combine more than one author into a single author, or for any of a number of reasons related to the marketing or aesthetic presentation of the work. The author's name may be known only to the publisher or may come to be common knowledge.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Western_literature">Western literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pen_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Western literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe_and_the_United_States">Europe and the United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pen_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Europe and the United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An author may use a pen name if their real name is likely to be confused with that of another author or notable individual. For instance, from 1899 the British politician <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">Winston Churchill</a> wrote under the name <a href="/wiki/Winston_S._Churchill" class="mw-redirect" title="Winston S. Churchill">Winston S. Churchill</a> to distinguish his writings from those of the <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill_(novelist)" title="Winston Churchill (novelist)">American novelist of the same name</a>, who was at the time much better known.
</p><p>An author may use a pen name implying a rank or title which he has never actually held. <a href="/wiki/W._E._Johns" title="W. E. Johns">William Earl Johns</a> wrote under the name "Captain W. E. Johns" although the highest army rank he held was acting <a href="/wiki/Lieutenant" title="Lieutenant">lieutenant</a> and his highest air force rank was <a href="/wiki/Flying_officer" title="Flying officer">flying officer</a>. 
</p><p>Authors who regularly write in more than one genre may use different pen names for each, sometimes with no attempt to conceal a true identity. <a href="/wiki/Romance_novel" title="Romance novel">Romance</a> writer <a href="/wiki/Nora_Roberts" title="Nora Roberts">Nora Roberts</a> writes <a href="/wiki/Erotic_thriller" title="Erotic thriller">erotic thrillers</a> under the pen name J. D. Robb (such books are titled "Nora Roberts writing as J. D. Robb"); Scots writer <a href="/wiki/Iain_Banks" title="Iain Banks">Iain Banks</a> wrote mainstream or literary fiction under his own name and published <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> under Iain M. Banks; <a href="/wiki/Mark_Twain" title="Mark Twain">Samuel Langhorne Clemens</a> used the aliases Mark Twain and Sieur Louis de Conte for different works. Similarly, an author who writes both fiction and non-fiction (such as the mathematician and fantasy writer Charles Dodgson, who wrote as <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a>) may use a pseudonym for fiction writing. Science fiction author <a href="/wiki/Harry_Turtledove" title="Harry Turtledove">Harry Turtledove</a> has used the name H. N. Turtletaub for a number of historical novels he has written because he and his publisher felt that the presumed lower sales of those novels might hurt book store orders for the novels he writes under his own name.
</p><p>Occasionally, a pen name is employed to avoid overexposure. Prolific authors for <a href="/wiki/Pulp_magazine" title="Pulp magazine">pulp magazines</a> often had two and sometimes three short stories appearing in one issue of a magazine; the editor would create several fictitious author names to hide this from readers. <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">Robert A. Heinlein</a> wrote stories under pseudonyms of Anson MacDonald (a combination of his middle name and his then-wife's maiden name) and Caleb Strong so that more of his works could be published in a single magazine.  <a href="/wiki/Stephen_King" title="Stephen King">Stephen King</a> published four novels under the name <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bachman" title="Richard Bachman">Richard Bachman</a> because publishers didn't feel the public would buy more than one novel per year from a single author.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> Eventually, after critics found a large number of style similarities, publishers revealed Bachman's true identity.
</p><p>Sometimes a pen name is used because an author believes that their name does not suit the genre he or she is writing in. Western novelist Pearl Gray dropped his first name and changed the spelling of his last name to become <a href="/wiki/Zane_Grey" title="Zane Grey">Zane Grey</a>, because he believed that his real name did not suit the Western genre. Romance novelist <a href="/wiki/Angela_Knight_(author)" title="Angela Knight (author)">Angela Knight</a> writes under that name instead of her actual name (Julie Woodcock) because of the <a href="/wiki/Double_entendre" title="Double entendre">double entendre</a> of her surname in the context of that genre. <a href="/wiki/Romain_Gary" title="Romain Gary">Romain Gary</a>, who was a well-known French writer, decided in 1973 to write novels in a different style under the name <a href="/wiki/%C3%89mile_Ajar" class="mw-redirect" title="Émile Ajar">Émile Ajar</a> and even asked his cousin's son to impersonate Ajar; thus he received the <a href="/wiki/Goncourt_Prize" class="mw-redirect" title="Goncourt Prize">most prestigious French literary prize</a> twice, which is forbidden by the prize rules. He revealed the affair in a book he sent his editor just before committing suicide in 1980.
</p><p>Some pen names have been used for long periods, even decades, without the author's true identity being discovered, such as <a href="/wiki/Elena_Ferrante" title="Elena Ferrante">Elena Ferrante</a> and <a href="/wiki/Torsten_Krol" title="Torsten Krol">Torsten Krol</a>.
</p><p>A pen name may be shared by different writers in order to suggest continuity of authorship. Thus the <i><a href="/wiki/Bessie_Bunter" title="Bessie Bunter">Bessie Bunter</a></i> series of English boarding-school stories, initially written by the prolific <a href="/wiki/Charles_Hamilton_(writer)" title="Charles Hamilton (writer)">Charles Hamilton</a> under the name Hilda Richards, was taken on by other authors who continued to use the same pen-name.
</p><p>In some forms of fiction, the pen name adopted is the name of the lead character, to suggest to the reader that the book is a (fictional) autobiography. <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Handler" title="Daniel Handler">Daniel Handler</a> used the pseudonym <a href="/wiki/Lemony_Snicket" title="Lemony Snicket">Lemony Snicket</a> to present his <i><a href="/wiki/A_Series_of_Unfortunate_Events" title="A Series of Unfortunate Events">A Series of Unfortunate Events</a></i> books as memoirs by an acquaintance of the main characters. Some, however, do this to fit a certain theme. One example, <a href="/wiki/Pseudonymous_Bosch" title="Pseudonymous Bosch">Pseudonymous Bosch</a>, used his pen name just to expand the theme of secrecy in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Secret_Series" title="The Secret Series">The Secret Series</a></i>.
</p><p>Authors also may occasionally choose pen names to appear in more favourable positions in <a href="/wiki/Bookshop" class="mw-redirect" title="Bookshop">bookshops</a> or <a href="/wiki/Library" title="Library">libraries</a>, to maximise visibility when placed on shelves that are conventionally arranged alphabetically moving horizontally, then upwards vertically. <sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Female_authors">Female authors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pen_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Female authors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Some female authors have used pen names to ensure that their works were accepted by publishers and/or the public. Such is the case of Peru's <a href="/wiki/Clarinda_(poet)" title="Clarinda (poet)">Clarinda</a>, whose work was published in the early 17th century. More often, women have adopted masculine pen names. This was common in the 19th century, when women were beginning to make inroads into literature but, it was felt, would not be taken as seriously by readers as male authors. For example, Mary Ann Evans wrote under the pen name <a href="/wiki/George_Eliot" title="George Eliot">George Eliot</a>; and Amandine Aurore Lucile Dupin, Baronne Dudevant, used the pseudonym <a href="/wiki/George_Sand" title="George Sand">George Sand</a>.  <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Bront%C3%AB" title="Charlotte Brontë">Charlotte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Emily_Bront%C3%AB" title="Emily Brontë">Emily</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anne_Bront%C3%AB" title="Anne Brontë">Anne Brontë</a> published under the names Currer, Ellis, and Acton Bell respectively. French-Savoyard writer and poet <a href="/wiki/Am%C3%A9lie_Gex" title="Amélie Gex">Amélie Gex</a> chose to publish as Dian de Jeânna ("John, son of Jane") during the first half of her career. <a href="/wiki/Karen_Blixen" title="Karen Blixen">Karen Blixen</a>'s very successful <i><a href="/wiki/Out_of_Africa" title="Out of Africa">Out of Africa</a></i> (1937) was originally published under the pen name Isak Dinesen. <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Benedictsson" title="Victoria Benedictsson">Victoria Benedictsson</a>, a Swedish author of the 19th century, wrote under the name Ernst Ahlgren. The <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> author Alice B. Sheldon for many years published under the masculine name of <a href="/wiki/James_Tiptree,_Jr." class="mw-redirect" title="James Tiptree, Jr.">James Tiptree, Jr.</a>, the discovery of which led to a deep discussion of gender in the genre.  
</p><p>More recently, women who write in genres commonly written by men sometimes choose to use initials, such as <a href="/wiki/K._A._Applegate" title="K. A. Applegate">K. A. Applegate</a>, <a href="/wiki/C._J._Cherryh" title="C. J. Cherryh">C. J. Cherryh</a>, <a href="/wiki/P._N._Elrod" title="P. N. Elrod">P. N. Elrod</a>, <a href="/wiki/D._C._Fontana" title="D. C. Fontana">D. C. Fontana</a>, <a href="/wiki/S._E._Hinton" title="S. E. Hinton">S. E. Hinton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gail_Riplinger" title="Gail Riplinger">G. A. Riplinger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nora_Roberts#J.D._Robb" title="Nora Roberts">J. D. Robb</a>, and <a href="/wiki/J._K._Rowling" title="J. K. Rowling">J. K. Rowling</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;a&#93;</a></sup> Alternatively, they may use a unisex pen name, such as <a href="/wiki/Robin_Hobb" title="Robin Hobb">Robin Hobb</a> (the second pen name of novelist <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Astrid_Lindholm_Ogden" class="mw-redirect" title="Margaret Astrid Lindholm Ogden">Margaret Astrid Lindholm Ogden</a>).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Collective_names">Collective names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pen_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Collective names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <b>collective name</b>, also known as a <b>house name</b>, is sometimes used with <a href="/wiki/Serial_(literature)" title="Serial (literature)">series fiction</a> published under one pen name even though more than one author may have contributed to the series. In some cases the first books in the series were written by one writer, but subsequent books were written by <a href="/wiki/Ghost_writer" class="mw-redirect" title="Ghost writer">ghost writers</a>. For instance, many of the later books in <i><a href="/wiki/Simon_Templar" class="mw-redirect" title="Simon Templar">The Saint</a></i> adventure series were not written by <a href="/wiki/Leslie_Charteris" title="Leslie Charteris">Leslie Charteris</a>, the series' originator.  Similarly, <i><a href="/wiki/Nancy_Drew" title="Nancy Drew">Nancy Drew</a></i> mystery books are published as though they were written by <a href="/wiki/Carolyn_Keene" title="Carolyn Keene">Carolyn Keene</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hardy_Boys" title="The Hardy Boys">The Hardy Boys</a></i> books are published as the work of <a href="/wiki/Franklin_W._Dixon" title="Franklin W. Dixon">Franklin W. Dixon</a>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Bobbsey_Twins" class="mw-redirect" title="The Bobbsey Twins">The Bobbsey Twins</a></i> series are credited to <a href="/wiki/Laura_Lee_Hope" title="Laura Lee Hope">Laura Lee Hope</a>, although numerous authors have been involved in each series. <a href="/wiki/Erin_Hunter" title="Erin Hunter">Erin Hunter</a>, author of the <i><a href="/wiki/Warriors_(novel_series)" title="Warriors (novel series)">Warriors</a></i> novel series, is actually a collective pen name used by authors <a href="/wiki/Kate_Cary" title="Kate Cary">Kate Cary</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cherith_Baldry" title="Cherith Baldry">Cherith Baldry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tui_T._Sutherland" title="Tui T. Sutherland">Tui T. Sutherland</a>, and the editor <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Holmes" title="Victoria Holmes">Victoria Holmes</a>.
</p><p>Collaborative authors may also have their works published under a single pen name. Frederic Dannay and Manfred B. Lee published their mystery novels and stories under the pen name <a href="/wiki/Ellery_Queen" title="Ellery Queen">Ellery Queen</a>, as well as publishing the work of <a href="/wiki/Ghost-writer" class="mw-redirect" title="Ghost-writer">ghost-writers</a> under the same name. The writers of <i><a href="/wiki/Atlanta_Nights" title="Atlanta Nights">Atlanta Nights</a></i>, a deliberately bad book intended to embarrass the publishing firm <a href="/wiki/PublishAmerica" class="mw-redirect" title="PublishAmerica">PublishAmerica</a>,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> used the pen name Travis Tea. Additionally, the credited author of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Expanse_(novel_series)" title="The Expanse (novel series)">The Expanse</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/James_S.A._Corey" class="mw-redirect" title="James S.A. Corey">James S.A. Corey</a>, is an amalgam of the middle names of collaborating writers <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Abraham_(author)" title="Daniel Abraham (author)">Daniel Abraham</a> and Ty Franck respectively, while S.A. are the initials of Abraham's daughter. Sometimes multiple authors will write related books under the same pseudonym; examples include <a href="/wiki/T._H._Lain" title="T. H. Lain">T. H. Lain</a> in fiction.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The Australian fiction collaborators who write under the pen name <a href="/wiki/Alice_Campion" title="Alice Campion">Alice Campion</a> is a group of women who have so far written two novels together - <i>The Painted Sky</i> (2015)<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> / <i>Der Bunte Himmel</i> (2015) written by five and <i>The Shifting Light</i> (2017)<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> by four.
</p><p>In the 1780s, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Federalist_Papers" title="The Federalist Papers">The Federalist Papers</a></i> were  written under the pseudonym "Publius" by <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Hamilton" title="Alexander Hamilton">Alexander Hamilton</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Madison" title="James Madison">James Madison</a>, and <a href="/wiki/John_Jay" title="John Jay">John Jay</a>. The three men chose the name "Publius" because it recalled the founder of the Roman Republic, and using it implied a positive intention.<sup id="cite_ref-Furtwangler1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Furtwangler1-7">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/Pure_mathematics" title="Pure mathematics">pure mathematics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Bourbaki" title="Nicolas Bourbaki">Nicolas Bourbaki</a> is the pseudonym of a group of mostly French connected mathematicians attempting to expose the field in an axiomatic and self-contained, encyclopedic form.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Concealment_of_identity">Concealment of identity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pen_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Concealment of identity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pen_name&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">July 2016</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>A pseudonym may be used to protect the writer for exposé books about espionage or crime.  Former SAS soldier Steven Billy Mitchell used the pseudonym <a href="/wiki/Andy_McNab" title="Andy McNab">Andy McNab</a> for his book about a failed <a href="/wiki/Special_Air_Service" title="Special Air Service">SAS</a> mission titled <i><a href="/wiki/Bravo_Two_Zero" title="Bravo Two Zero">Bravo Two Zero</a></i>. The name <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Warraq" title="Ibn Warraq">Ibn Warraq</a> ("son of a papermaker") has been used by dissident Muslim authors. Author <a href="/wiki/Brian_O%27Nolan" title="Brian O&#39;Nolan">Brian O'Nolan</a> used the pen names Flann O'Brien and Myles na gCopaleen for his novels and journalistic writing from the 1940s to the 1960s because Irish <a href="/wiki/Civil_servant" class="mw-redirect" title="Civil servant">civil servants</a> were not allowed at that time to publish works under their own names.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The identity of the enigmatic twentieth century novelist <a href="/wiki/B._Traven" title="B. Traven">B. Traven</a> has never been conclusively revealed, despite thorough research.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>A <a href="/wiki/Multiple-use_name" title="Multiple-use name">multiple-use name</a> or <b>anonymity pseudonym</b> is a pseudonym open for anyone to use and these have been adopted by various groups, often as a protest against the cult of individual creators. In Italy, two anonymous groups of writers have gained some popularity with the collective names of <a href="/wiki/Luther_Blissett_(nom_de_plume)" title="Luther Blissett (nom de plume)">Luther Blissett</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wu_Ming" title="Wu Ming">Wu Ming</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_literature">Eastern literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pen_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Eastern literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pen_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: India">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Takhallus" title="Takhallus">Takhallus</a></div>
<p>In Indian languages, writers may put a pen name at the end of their names, like <a href="/wiki/Ramdhari_Singh_Dinkar" title="Ramdhari Singh Dinkar">Ramdhari Singh Dinkar</a>. Sometimes they also write under their pen name without their actual name like <a href="/wiki/Firaq_Gorakhpuri" title="Firaq Gorakhpuri">Firaq Gorakhpuri</a>.
</p><p>In early Indian literature, we find authors shying away from using any name considering it to be egotistical. Due to this notion, even today it is hard to trace the authorship of many earlier literary works from India. Later, we find that the writers adopted the practice of using the name of their deity of worship or Guru's name as their pen name. In this case, typically the pen name would be included at the end of the prose or poetry.
</p><p>Composers of Indian classical music used pen names in compositions to assert authorship, including <a href="/wiki/Sadarang" title="Sadarang">Sadarang</a>, Gunarang (<a href="/w/index.php?title=Fayyaz_Ahmed_Khan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Fayyaz Ahmed Khan (page does not exist)">Fayyaz Ahmed Khan</a>), Ada Rang (court musician of <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Shah" title="Muhammad Shah">Muhammad Shah</a>), Sabrang (<a href="/wiki/Bade_Ghulam_Ali_Khan" title="Bade Ghulam Ali Khan">Bade Ghulam Ali Khan</a>), and Ramrang (<a href="/wiki/Ramashreya_Jha" title="Ramashreya Jha">Ramashreya Jha</a>). Other compositions are apocryphally ascribed to composers with their pen names.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pen_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Japanese poets who write <a href="/wiki/Haiku" title="Haiku">haiku</a> often use a <i>haigō</i> (俳号). The haiku poet <a href="/wiki/Matsuo_Bash%C5%8D" title="Matsuo Bashō">Matsuo Bashō</a> had used two other haigō before he became fond of a banana plant (<i>bashō</i>) that had been given to him by a disciple and started using it as his pen name at the age of 36.
</p><p>Similar to a pen name, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> <a href="/wiki/Artist" title="Artist">artists</a> usually have a <i>gō</i> or <a href="/wiki/Art-name" class="mw-redirect" title="Art-name">art-name</a>, which might change a number of times during their career. In some cases, artists adopted different <i>gō</i> at different stages of their career, usually to mark significant changes in their life. One of the most extreme examples of this is <a href="/wiki/Hokusai" title="Hokusai">Hokusai</a>, who in the period 1798 to 1806 alone used no fewer than six. <a href="/wiki/Mangaka" title="Mangaka">Manga artist</a> Ogure Ito uses the pen name <a href="/wiki/Oh!_great" title="Oh! great">Oh! great</a> because his real name Ogure Ito is roughly how the Japanese pronounce "oh great".
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Persian_and_Urdu_poetry">Persian and Urdu poetry</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pen_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Persian and Urdu poetry">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Takhallus" title="Takhallus">Takhallus</a></div>
<dl><dd><i>Note: <a href="/wiki/List_of_Urdu_language_poets" class="mw-redirect" title="List of Urdu language poets">List of Urdu language poets</a> provides pen names for a range of <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a> poets.</i></dd></dl>
<p>A <i>shâ'er</i> (<a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a> from Arabic, for poet) (a <a href="/wiki/Poet" title="Poet">poet</a> who writes <i><a href="/wiki/Sher_(poem)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sher (poem)">she'rs</a></i> in <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">Urdu</a> or <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">Persian</a>) almost always has a "takhallus", a pen name, traditionally placed at the end of the name (often marked by a graphical sign placed above it) when referring to the poet by his full name. For example, <a href="/wiki/Hafez_(poet)" class="mw-redirect" title="Hafez (poet)">Hafez</a> is a pen-name for <i>Shams al-Din</i>, and thus the usual way to refer to him would be <i>Shams al-Din Hafez</i> or just <i>Hafez</i>. <i>Mirza Asadullah Baig Khan</i> (his official name and title) is referred to as <i>Mirza Asadullah Khan <a href="/wiki/Ghalib" title="Ghalib">Ghalib</a></i>, or just <i>Mirza Ghalib</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Pen_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The French phrase <i>nom de plume</i> is occasionally still seen as a synonym for the English term "pen name": this is a "back-translation" and originated in England rather than France. <a href="/wiki/H._W._Fowler" class="mw-redirect" title="H. W. Fowler">H. W. Fowler</a> and F. G. Fowler, in <i><a href="/wiki/The_King%27s_English" title="The King&#39;s English">The King's English</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> state that the term <i>nom de plume</i> "evolved" in Britain, where people wanting a "literary" phrase failed to understand the term <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudonym#Noms_de_guerre" title="Pseudonym">nom de guerre</a></i>, which already existed in French. Since <i>guerre</i> means "war" in French, <i>nom de guerre</i> did not make sense to the British, who did not understand the French metaphor.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> See also <a href="/wiki/French_phrases_used_by_English_speakers" class="mw-redirect" title="French phrases used by English speakers">French phrases used by English speakers</a>.
</p>
