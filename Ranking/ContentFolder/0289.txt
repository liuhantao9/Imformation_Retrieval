	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Japanese name</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><div class="shortdescription nomobile noexcerpt noprint searchaux" style="display:none">Naming customs of Japanese culture</div><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Names_of_Japan" title="Names of Japan">Names of Japan</a>.</div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:122px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Yamada-vertical.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Yamada-vertical.svg/120px-Yamada-vertical.svg.png" decoding="async" width="120" height="586" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Yamada-vertical.svg/180px-Yamada-vertical.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Yamada-vertical.svg/240px-Yamada-vertical.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="188" data-file-height="918" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Yamada-vertical.svg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Yamada" title="Yamada">Yamada</a> <a href="/wiki/Tar%C5%8D_(given_name)" title="Tarō (given name)">Tarō</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">山田太郎</span></span></span>), a Japanese <a href="/wiki/Placeholder_name#People" title="Placeholder name">placeholder name</a> (male), equivalent to <i>John Smith</i> in English.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The equivalent of <i>Jane Smith</i> would be Yamada Hanako (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">山田花子</span></span></span>).</div></div></div>
<p><b>Japanese names</b><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">日本人の氏名</span><span style="display:none">,</span>&#32;<i>Nihonjin no Shimei</i>)</span> in modern times usually consist of a <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a> (surname), followed by a <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a>. More than one given name is not generally used.   Japanese names are usually written in <i><a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a></i>, which are characters usually <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a> in origin but <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">Japanese</a> in pronunciation. The <i>kanji</i> for a name may have a variety of possible Japanese pronunciations, hence parents might use <a href="/wiki/Hiragana" title="Hiragana">hiragana</a> or <a href="/wiki/Katakana" title="Katakana">katakana</a> when giving a birth name to their newborn child. Names written in <a href="/wiki/Hiragana" title="Hiragana">hiragana</a> or <a href="/wiki/Katakana" title="Katakana">katakana</a> are phonetic renderings, and so lack the visual meaning of names expressed in the <a href="/wiki/Logogram" title="Logogram">logographic</a> kanji.
</p><p>Japanese family names are extremely varied: according to estimates, there are over 100,000 different surnames in use today in Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The three most common family names in Japan are <a href="/wiki/Sat%C5%8D" title="Satō">Satō</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">佐藤</span></span></span>), <a href="/wiki/Suzuki_(surname)" title="Suzuki (surname)">Suzuki</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">鈴木</span></span></span>), and <a href="/wiki/Takahashi" title="Takahashi">Takahashi</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">高橋</span></span></span>).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> This diversity is in stark contrast to the situation in other nations of the <a href="/wiki/East_Asian_cultural_sphere" title="East Asian cultural sphere">East Asian cultural sphere</a>, which reflects a different history: while <a href="/wiki/Chinese_surnames" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese surnames">Chinese surnames</a> have been in use for millennia and were often reflective of an entire clan or adopted from nobles (with or without any genetic relationship) and were thence transferred to Korea and Vietnam via noble names, the vast majority of modern Japanese family names date only to the 19th century, following the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="Meiji restoration">Meiji restoration</a>, and were chosen at will. The recent introduction of surnames has two additional effects: Japanese names became widespread when the country had a very large population (over 30&#160;million during the early Meiji era – see <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Imperial_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="Demographics of Imperial Japan">Demographics of Imperial Japan</a>) instead of dating to ancient times (estimated population at 1&#160;CE is 300,000, for instance – see <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Japan_before_Meiji_Restoration" class="mw-redirect" title="Demographics of Japan before Meiji Restoration">Demographics of Japan before Meiji Restoration</a>), and since little time has passed, Japanese names have not experienced as significant a <a href="/wiki/Surname_extinction" class="mw-redirect" title="Surname extinction">surname extinction</a> as has occurred in the much longer history in China.<sup id="cite_ref-Du92_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Du92-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Surnames occur with varying frequency in different regions; for example, the names Chinen (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">知念</span></span></span>), Higa (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">比嘉</span></span></span>), and Shimabukuro (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">島袋</span></span></span>) are common in <a href="/wiki/Okinawa" class="mw-redirect" title="Okinawa">Okinawa</a> but not in other parts of Japan; this is mainly due to differences between the language and culture of <a href="/wiki/Yamato_people" title="Yamato people">Yamato people</a> and Okinawans. Many Japanese family names derive from features of the rural landscape; for example, <a href="/wiki/Ishikawa_(surname)" title="Ishikawa (surname)">Ishikawa</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">石川</span></span></span>) means "river of the stones", <a href="/wiki/Yamamoto_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Yamamoto (disambiguation)">Yamamoto</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">山本</span></span></span>) means "the base of the mountain", and <a href="/wiki/Inoue" title="Inoue">Inoue</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">井上</span></span></span>) means "above the well".
</p><p>While family names follow relatively consistent rules, given names are much more diverse in <a href="/wiki/Pronunciation" title="Pronunciation">pronunciation</a> and character usage. While many common names can easily be spelled or pronounced, many parents choose names with unusual characters or pronunciations, and such names cannot in general be spelled or pronounced unless both the spelling and pronunciation are given. Unusual pronunciations have especially become common, with this trend having increased significantly since the 1990s.<sup id="cite_ref-JT2012_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JT2012-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> For example, the popular masculine name 大翔 is traditionally pronounced "Hiroto", but in recent years alternative pronunciations "Haruto", "<a href="/wiki/Yamato_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Yamato (disambiguation)">Yamato</a>", "Taiga", "Sora", "Taito", "Daito", and "Masato" have all entered use.<sup id="cite_ref-JT2012_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JT2012-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Male names often end in <i>-rō</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">郎</span></span> "son", but also <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">朗</span></span> "clear, bright"; e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Ichir%C5%8D" title="Ichirō">Ichirō</a>") <i>-ta</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">太</span></span> "great, thick"; e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Kenta" title="Kenta">Kenta</a>") or <i>-o</i> (男 / 雄 / 夫 "man"; e.g. "Teruo" or "<a href="/wiki/Akio" title="Akio">Akio</a>"),<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> or contain <i>ichi</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">一</span></span> "first [son]"; e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Ken%27ichi" title="Ken&#39;ichi">Ken'ichi</a>"), <i>kazu</i> (also written with <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">一</span></span> "first [son]", along with several other possible characters; e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Kazuhiro" title="Kazuhiro">Kazuhiro</a>"), <i>ji</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">二</span></span> "second [son]" or <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">次</span></span> "next"; e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Jiro_(given_name)" title="Jiro (given name)">Jirō</a>"), or <i>dai</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">大</span></span> "great, large"; e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Daichi_(given_name)" title="Daichi (given name)">Daichi</a>").
</p><p>Female names often end in <i>-ko</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">子</span></span> "child"; e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Keiko_(given_name)" title="Keiko (given name)">Keiko</a>") or <i>-mi</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">美</span></span> "beauty"; e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Yumi_(name)" title="Yumi (name)">Yumi</a>"). Other popular endings for female names include <i>-ka</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">香</span></span> "scent, perfume" or <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">花</span></span> "flower"; e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Reika" title="Reika">Reika</a>") and <i>-na</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">奈</span></span>, or <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">菜</span></span>, meaning "greens" or "apple tree"; e.g. "<a href="/wiki/Haruna_(name)" title="Haruna (name)">Haruna</a>").
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The majority of <a href="/wiki/Japanese_people" title="Japanese people">Japanese people</a> have one surname and one given name with no other names, except for the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_House_of_Japan" title="Imperial House of Japan">Japanese imperial family</a>, whose members bear no surname. The family name – <i>myōji</i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">苗字</span></span></span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">名字</span></span></span>), <i>uji</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">氏</span></span>) or <i>sei</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">姓</span></span>) – precedes the given name, called the "name" – (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">名</span></span></span> <i>mei</i>) or "lower name" (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">下の名前</span></span></span> <i>shita no namae</i>). The given name may be referred to as the "lower name" because, in vertically written Japanese, the given name appears under the family name.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> People with mixed Japanese and foreign parentage may have middle names.<sup id="cite_ref-PowerpC4-2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PowerpC4-2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Historically, <i>myōji</i>, <i>uji</i> and <i>sei</i> had different meanings. <i>Sei</i> was originally the <a href="/wiki/Patrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrilineal">patrilineal</a> surname which is why up until now it has only been granted  by the emperor as a title of male rank. The lower form of the name <i>sei</i> being <i>tei</i> which is a common name in Japanese men, although there was a male ancestor in ancient Japan from whom the name 'Sei' originally came. There were relatively few <i>sei</i>, and most of the medieval noble clans trace their lineage either directly to these <i>sei</i> or to the courtiers of these <i>sei</i>. <i>Uji</i> was another name used to designate <a href="/wiki/Patrilineal" class="mw-redirect" title="Patrilineal">patrilineal</a> descent, but later merged with <i>myōji</i> around the same time. <i>Myōji</i> was, simply, what a family chooses to call itself, as opposed to the <i>sei</i> granted by the emperor. While it was passed on patrilineally in male ancestors including in male ancestors called haku (uncles), one had a certain degree of freedom in changing one's <i>myōji</i>. See also <a href="/wiki/Kabane" title="Kabane">Kabane</a>.
</p><p>Multiple Japanese characters have the same pronunciations, so several Japanese names have multiple meanings. A particular kanji itself can have multiple meanings and pronunciations. In some names, Japanese characters phonetically "spell" a name and have no intended meaning behind them. Many Japanese personal names use puns.<sup id="cite_ref-HanksHardcastleHodgesAp8_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HanksHardcastleHodgesAp8-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Very few names can serve either as surnames or as given names (for example Mayumi <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">真弓</span></span></span>, Kaneko <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">金子</span></span></span>, Masuko <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">益子</span></span></span>, or Arata <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">新</span></span>). Therefore, to those familiar with Japanese names, which name is the surname and which is the given name is usually apparent, no matter which order the names are presented in. This thus makes it unlikely that the two names will be confused, for example, when writing in English while using the family name-given name naming order. However, due to the variety of pronunciations and differences in languages, some common surnames and given names may coincide when Romanized: e.g., Shoji<span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">昌司, 昭次, or 正二</span>)</span> (given name) and Shoji<span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">庄司, 庄子, 東海林, or 小路</span>)</span> (surname).
</p><p>Although usually written in Kanji, Japanese names have distinct differences from <a href="/wiki/Chinese_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Chinese names">Chinese names</a> through the selection of characters in a name and pronunciation. A Japanese person can distinguish a Japanese name from a Chinese name by looking at it. Akie Tomozawa, author of "Japan's Hidden Bilinguals: The Languages of 'War Orphans' and Their Families After Repatriation From China," said that this was equivalent to how "Europeans can easily tell that the name 'Smith' is English and 'Schmidt' is German or 'Victor' is English or French and 'Vittorio' is Italian".<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characters">Characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Japanese names are usually written in <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a> (Chinese characters), although some names use <a href="/wiki/Hiragana" title="Hiragana">hiragana</a> or even <a href="/wiki/Katakana" title="Katakana">katakana</a>, or a mixture of kanji and <a href="/wiki/Kana" title="Kana">kana</a>. While most "traditional" names use <a href="/wiki/Kunyomi" class="mw-redirect" title="Kunyomi">kun'yomi</a> (native Japanese) kanji readings, a large number of given names and surnames use <a href="/wiki/Onyomi" class="mw-redirect" title="Onyomi">on'yomi</a> (Chinese-based) kanji readings as well. Many others use readings which are only used in names (<a href="/wiki/Nanori" title="Nanori">nanori</a>), such as the female name Nozomi (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">希</span></span>). The majority of surnames comprise one, two or three kanji characters. There are also a small number of four or five kanji surnames, such as Teshigawara (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">勅使河原</span></span></span>), Kutaragi (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">久多良木</span></span></span>) and Kadenokōji (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">勘解由小路</span></span></span>), but these are extremely rare.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The sound <i>no,</i> indicating possession (like the apostrophe in English), and corresponding to the character <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">の</span></span>, is often included in names but not written as a separate character, as in the common name <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">井上</span></span> (<i>i-no-ue,</i> well-(possessive)-top/above, top of the well), or historical figures such as <a href="/wiki/Sen_no_Riky%C5%AB" title="Sen no Rikyū">Sen no Rikyū</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most personal names use one, two, or three kanji.<sup id="cite_ref-HanksHardcastleHodgesAp8_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HanksHardcastleHodgesAp8-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Four syllable given names are common, especially in eldest sons.<sup id="cite_ref-Hakes122_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hakes122-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>As mentioned above, female given names often end in the syllable <i>ko</i>, written with the kanji meaning "child" (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">子</span></span>), or <i>mi</i>, written with the kanji meaning "beautiful" (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">美</span></span>).<sup id="cite_ref-Hakes121_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hakes121-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The usage of <i>-ko</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">子</span></span>) has changed significantly over the years: prior to the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a> (1868), it was reserved for members of the imperial family. Following the restoration, it became popular and was overwhelmingly common in the <a href="/wiki/Taish%C5%8D_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Taishō period">Taishō</a> and early <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%8Dwa_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Shōwa period">Shōwa</a> era.<sup id="cite_ref-JT2012_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JT2012-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The suffix <i>-ko</i> increased in popularity after the mid-20th century. Around the year 2006, due to the citizenry mimicking naming habits of popular entertainers, the suffix <i>-ko</i> was declining in popularity. At the same time, names of western origin, written in kana, were becoming increasingly popular for naming of girls.<sup id="cite_ref-HanksHardcastleHodgesAp8_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-HanksHardcastleHodgesAp8-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> By 2004 there was a trend of using hiragana instead of kanji in naming girls. Molly Hakes, author of <i>The Everything Conversational Japanese Book: Basic Instruction For Speaking This Fascinating Language In Any Setting</i>, said that this may have to do with using hiragana out of cultural pride, since hiragana is Japan's indigenous writing form, or out of not assigning a meaning to a girl's name so that others do not have a particular expectation of her.<sup id="cite_ref-Hakes121_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hakes121-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Names ending with <i>-ko</i> dropped significantly in popularity in the mid 1980s, but are still given, though much less than in the past. Male names occasionally end with the syllable <i>ko</i> as in <i><a href="/wiki/Mako_(actor)" title="Mako (actor)">Mako</a></i>, but very rarely using the kanji <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">子</span></span> (most often, if a male name ends in <i>-ko</i>, it ends in <i>-hiko</i>, using the kanji <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">彦</span></span> meaning "boy"). Common male name endings are <i>-shi</i> and <i>-o</i>; names ending with <i>-shi</i> are often adjectives, e.g., <i>Atsushi</i> which might mean, for example, "(to be) faithful." In the past (before <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>), names written with katakana were common for women, but this trend seems to have lost favour. Hiragana names for women are not unusual. Kana names for boys, particularly those written in hiragana, have historically been very rare. This may be in part because the hiragana script is seen as feminine; in medieval Japan, women generally were not taught kanji and wrote exclusively in hiragana.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Names cannot begin with the syllable <i>n</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">ん</span></span>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">ン</span></span>); this is in common with other proper Japanese words, though colloquial words may begin with <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">ん</span></span>, as in <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">んまい</span></span></span> (<i>nmai</i>, variant of <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">うまい</span></span> <i>umai</i>, delicious). Some names end in <i>n</i>: the male names Ken, Shin, and Jun are examples. The syllable <i>n</i> should not be confused with the consonant <i>n</i>, which names <i>can</i> begin with; for example, the female name Naoko (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">尚子</span></span></span>) or the male Naoya (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">直哉</span></span></span>). (The consonant <i>n</i> needs to be paired with a vowel to form a syllable). 
</p><p>One large category of family names can be categorized as "<i>-tō</i>" names. The kanji <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">藤</span></span>, meaning <i><a href="/wiki/Wisteria" title="Wisteria">wisteria</a></i>, has the on'yomi <i>tō</i> (or, with <a href="/wiki/Rendaku" title="Rendaku">rendaku</a>, <i>dō</i>). Many Japanese people have surnames that include this kanji as the second character. This is because the <a href="/wiki/Fujiwara_clan" title="Fujiwara clan">Fujiwara clan</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">藤原家</span></span></span>) gave their <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a> surnames (myōji) ending with the first character of their name, to denote their status in an era when commoners were not allowed surnames. Examples include <a href="/wiki/Ato_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Ato (disambiguation)">Atō</a>, <a href="/wiki/And%C5%8D" title="Andō">Andō</a>, <a href="/wiki/It%C5%8D_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Itō (name)">Itō</a> (although a different final kanji is also common), <a href="/wiki/Udo_(disambiguation)" class="mw-disambig" title="Udo (disambiguation)">Udō</a>, <a href="/wiki/ETO_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="ETO (disambiguation)">Etō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Endo_(surname)" class="mw-redirect" title="Endo (surname)">Endō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Goto_(name)" class="mw-redirect" title="Goto (name)">Gotō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Jit%C5%8D" title="Jitō">Jitō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kat%C5%8D_(surname)" title="Katō (surname)">Katō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kit%C5%8D_(surname)" title="Kitō (surname)">Kitō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kudo_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Kudo (disambiguation)">Kudō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kond%C5%8D" title="Kondō">Kondō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Saito_(surname)" class="mw-redirect" title="Saito (surname)">Saitō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sat%C5%8D" title="Satō">Satō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Shind%C5%8D_(surname)" title="Shindō (surname)">Shindō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sudo" title="Sudo">Sudō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Naito_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Naito (disambiguation)">Naitō</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bito" title="Bito">Bitō</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Muto_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Muto (disambiguation)">Mutō</a>. As already noted, some of the most common family names are in this list.
</p><p>Japanese family names usually include characters referring to places and geographic features.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Difficulty_of_reading_names">Difficulty of reading names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Difficulty of reading names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Japanese_name&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. Unsourced material may be challenged and <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">removed</a>.</span>  <small class="date-container"><i>(<span class="date">July 2009</span>)</i></small><small class="hide-when-compact"><i> (<a href="/wiki/Help:Maintenance_template_removal" title="Help:Maintenance template removal">Learn how and when to remove this template message</a>)</i></small></div></td></tr></tbody></table>
<p>A name written in kanji may have more than one common pronunciation, only one of which is correct for a given individual. For example, the surname written in kanji as <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">東海林</span></span></span> may be read either <i>Tōkairin</i> or <i>Shōji</i>. Conversely, any one name may have several possible written forms, and again, only one will be correct for a given individual. The character "<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">一</span></span>" when used as a male given name may be used as the written form for "Hajime," "Hitoshi," "Ichi- / -ichi" "Kazu- / -kazu," and many others. The name "<a href="/wiki/Hajime" title="Hajime">Hajime</a>" may be written with any of the following: <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">始</span></span>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">治</span></span>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">初</span></span>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">一</span></span>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">元</span></span>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">肇</span></span>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">創</span></span>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">甫</span></span>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">基</span></span>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">哉</span></span>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">啓</span></span>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">本</span></span>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">源</span></span>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">東</span></span>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">大</span></span>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">孟</span></span>, or <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">祝</span></span>. This many-to-many correspondence between names and the ways they are written is much more common with male given names than with surnames or female given names, but can be observed in all these categories. The permutations of potential characters and sounds can become enormous, as some very overloaded sounds may be produced by over 500 distinct Kanji and some Kanji characters can stand for several dozen sounds. This can and does make the <a href="/wiki/Collation" title="Collation">collation</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pronunciation" title="Pronunciation">pronunciation</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Romanization" title="Romanization">romanization</a> of a Japanese name a very difficult problem. For this reason, business cards often include the pronunciation of the name as <a href="/wiki/Furigana" title="Furigana">furigana</a>, and forms and documents often include spaces to write the reading of the name in kana (usually katakana).
</p><p>A few Japanese names, particularly family names, include <a href="/wiki/Kyuujitai" class="mw-redirect" title="Kyuujitai">archaic versions of characters</a>. For example, the very common character <i>shima</i>, island, may be written as <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">嶋</span></span> or <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">嶌</span></span> instead of the usual <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">島</span></span>. Some names also feature very uncommon <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a>, or even <a href="/wiki/Jinmeiy%C5%8D_kanji" title="Jinmeiyō kanji">kanji which no longer exist in modern Japanese</a>. Japanese people who have such names are likely to compromise by substituting similar or simplified characters. This may be difficult for input of kanji in computers, as many kanji databases on computers only include common and regularly used kanji, and many archaic or mostly unused characters are not included. An odd problem occurs when an elderly person forgets how to write their name in old Kanji that is no longer used.
</p><p>An example of such a name is <a href="/wiki/Saito_(surname)" class="mw-redirect" title="Saito (surname)">Saitō</a>. There are two common kanji for <i>sai</i> here. The two <i>sai</i> characters have different meanings: <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">斉</span></span> means "together" or "parallel", but <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">斎</span></span> means "to purify". These names can also exist written in archaic forms, as <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">齊藤</span></span></span> and <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">齋藤</span></span></span> respectively.
</p><p>Family names are sometimes written with periphrastic readings, called <a href="/wiki/Jukujikun" class="mw-redirect" title="Jukujikun">jukujikun</a>, in which the written characters relate indirectly to the name as spoken. For example, <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">四月一日</span></span></span> would normally be read as <i>shigatsu tsuitachi</i> ("April 1st"), but as a family name it is read <i>watanuki</i> ("unpadded clothes"), because April 1 is the traditional date to switch from winter to summer clothes. In the same way <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">小鳥遊</span></span></span> would normally be read as <i>kotori asobi</i> ("little birds play") or <i>shōchōyū</i>, but is read <i>Takanashi</i>, because little birds (<i>kotori</i>) play (<i>asobi</i>) where there are no (<i>nashi</i>) hawks (<i>taka</i>).
</p><p>Most Japanese people and agencies have adopted customs to deal with these issues. <a href="/wiki/Address_book" title="Address book">Address books</a>, for instance, often contain <a href="/wiki/Furigana" title="Furigana">furigana</a> or <a href="/wiki/Ruby_characters" class="mw-redirect" title="Ruby characters">ruby characters</a> to clarify the pronunciation of the name. Japanese nationals are also required to give a <a href="/wiki/Romanized_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Romanized name">romanized name</a> for their <a href="/wiki/Passport" title="Passport">passport</a>. The recent use of katakana in Japanese media when referring to Japanese celebrities who have gained international fame has started a fad among young <a href="/wiki/Socialite" title="Socialite">socialites</a> who attempt to invoke a cosmopolitan flair using katakana names as a badge of honor.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> All of these complications are also found in <a href="/wiki/Japanese_place_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese place name">Japanese place names</a>.
</p><p>Not all names are complicated. Some common names are summarized by the phrase <i>tanakamura</i> ("the village in the middle of the rice fields"): the three kanji: <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">田</span></span> (<i>ta</i>, rice field), <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">中</span></span> (<i>naka</i>, middle) and <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">村</span></span> (<i>mura</i>, village), together in any pair, form a simple, reasonably common surname: <a href="/wiki/Tanaka" title="Tanaka">Tanaka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nakamura_(surname)" title="Nakamura (surname)">Nakamura</a>, <a href="/wiki/Murata_(surname)" title="Murata (surname)">Murata</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nakata_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Nakata (disambiguation)">Nakata</a> (Nakada), <a href="/wiki/Muranaka" title="Muranaka">Muranaka</a>, <a href="/wiki/Tamura_(surname)" title="Tamura (surname)">Tamura</a>.
</p><p>Despite these difficulties, there are enough patterns and recurring names that most native Japanese will be able to read virtually all family names they encounter and the majority of personal names.
</p><p>Some common interesting names with phonetic puns include <a href="/wiki/Michio_Kaku" title="Michio Kaku">Michio Kaku</a>, which could mean "Draw a path" or "Lead the way", and <a href="/wiki/Tsutomu_Hata" title="Tsutomu Hata">Tsutomu Hata</a>, which can mean "Work for the flag (nation)", but the Kanji used to write them obscure these meanings.
</p>

<p>Kanji names in Japan are governed by the Japanese Ministry of Justice's rules on kanji use in names. As of January 2015<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Japanese_name&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, only the 843 "name kanji" (<a href="/wiki/Jinmeiy%C5%8D_kanji" title="Jinmeiyō kanji">jinmeiyō kanji</a>) and 2,136 "commonly used characters" (<a href="/wiki/J%C5%8Dy%C5%8D_kanji" title="Jōyō kanji">jōyō kanji</a>) are permitted for use in personal names. This is intended to ensure that names can be readily written and read by those literate in Japanese. Rules also govern names considered to be inappropriate; for example, in 1993 two parents who tried to name their child Akuma (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">悪魔</span></span></span>, which literally means "devil") were prohibited from doing so after a massive public outcry.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Though there are regulations on the naming of children, many archaic characters can still be found in adults' names, particularly those born prior to the <a href="/wiki/Second_World_War" class="mw-redirect" title="Second World War">Second World War</a>. Because the legal restrictions on use of such <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a> cause inconvenience for those with such names and promote a proliferation of identical names, many recent changes have been made to increase rather than to reduce the number of <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a> allowed for use in names. The Sapporo High Court held that it was unlawful for the government to deny registration of a child's name because it contained a kanji character that was relatively common but not included in the official list of name characters compiled by the Ministry of Justice. Subsequently, the Japanese government promulgated plans to increase the number of kanji "permitted" in names.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The use of a space in given names (to separate first and middle names) is not allowed in official documents, because technically, a space is not an allowed character. However, spaces are sometimes used on business cards and in correspondence.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Customs">Customs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Customs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In ancient times, people in Japan were considered the property of the Emperor<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> and their surname reflected the role in the government they served. An example is <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Ctomo_clan" title="Ōtomo clan">Ōtomo</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">大友</span></span></span> 'great attendant, companion'). Names would also be given in the recognition of a great achievement and contribution.
</p><p>Until the <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Restoration" title="Meiji Restoration">Meiji Restoration</a>, Japanese common people (people other than <a href="/wiki/Kuge" title="Kuge">kuge</a> and <a href="/wiki/Samurai" title="Samurai">samurai</a>) had no surnames, and when necessary, used a substitute such as the name of their birthplace. For example, Ichirō born in Asahi-mura (Asahi village) in the province of <a href="/wiki/Musashi_Province" title="Musashi Province">Musashi</a> would say "Ichirō from Asahi-mura of Musashi". Merchants were named after their stores or brands (for example, Denbei, the owner of Sagamiya, would be Sagamiya Denbei), and farmers were named after their fathers (for example, Isuke, whose father was Genbei, would be "Isuke, son of Genbei"). After the Meiji Restoration, the government ordered all commoners to assume surnames in addition to their given names, as part of modernization and Westernization; this was specified in the Family Register Law of 1898.<sup id="cite_ref-JT2012_5-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-JT2012-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Many people adopted historical names, others simply made names up, chose names through <a href="/wiki/Divination" title="Divination">divination</a>, or had a Shinto or Buddhist priest choose a surname for them. This explains, in part, the large number of surnames in Japan, as well as their great diversity of spelling and pronunciation, and makes tracing ancestry past a certain point extremely difficult in Japan.
</p><p>During the period when typical parents had several children, it was a common practice to name sons by numbers suffixed with <i>rō</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">郎</span></span>, "son"). The first son would be known as "Ichirō", the second as "Jirō", and so on. Girls were often named with <i>ko</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">子</span></span>, "child") at the end of the given name; this should not be confused with the less common male suffix <i>hiko</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">彦</span></span>). Both practices have become less common, although many children still have names along these lines.
</p><p>While some people may still believe this, <a href="/wiki/Lafcadio_Hearn" title="Lafcadio Hearn">Lafcadio Hearn</a> (see below), in <i>Shadowings</i>, makes it clear that at least in his time (1880 to 1905, the date of publication), the ending <i>-ko</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">子</span></span>) was not any part of the name, but an honorific suffix like <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">さん</span></span></span> <i>-san</i>. Particularly, even though the symbol was "child", it meant "Lady" and was used only by upper-class females. It would have been ridiculous to apply to middle-class or lower-class women. Pretty much the same names were used by all classes, but Hana-ko was upper class, while lesser women would be O-Hana-san, with honorific prefix as well as suffix.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Speaking_to_and_of_others">Speaking to and of others</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Speaking to and of others">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Japanese_honorifics" title="Japanese honorifics">Japanese honorifics</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japanese_pronouns" title="Japanese pronouns">Japanese pronouns</a></div>
<p>The way in which a name is used in conversation depends on the circumstances and the speaker's relationships with the listener and the bearer of the name. Typically the family name is used, with given names largely restricted to informal situations and cases where the speaker is older than, superior to, or very familiar with the named individual. When addressing someone, or referring to a member of one's <a href="/wiki/Uchi-soto" title="Uchi-soto">out-group</a>, a title such as <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">さん</span></span></span> <i>-san</i> is typically added.
</p><p>Japanese people often avoid referring to their seniors or superiors by name at all, using just a title: within a family this might be a kinship relation such as <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">お母さん</span></span></span> <i>okāsan</i> ("mother"), in a school it could be <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">先生</span></span></span> <i>sensei</i> ("teacher"), while a company president would be addressed as <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">社長</span></span></span> <i>shachō</i> ("company president").
</p><p>On the other hand, pronominals meaning "you" ( <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">あなた</span></span></span> <i>anata</i>, <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">きみ</span></span></span> <i>kimi</i>, <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">お前</span></span></span> <i>omae</i> ) are used rather little in Japanese. Using such words sometimes sounds disrespectful, and people will commonly address each other by name, title and honorific even in face-to-face conversations.
</p><p>Calling someone's name (family name) without any title or honorific is called <i>yobisute</i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">呼び捨て</span></span></span>), and may be considered rude even in the most informal and friendly occasions. This <a href="/wiki/Faux_pas" title="Faux pas">faux pas</a>, however, is readily excused for foreigners.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nicknames">Nicknames</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nicknames">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Corresponding to any given name there are one or more <a href="/wiki/Hypocoristic" class="mw-redirect" title="Hypocoristic">hypocoristics</a>, affectionate nicknames. These are formed by adding the suffix <a href="/wiki/-chan" class="mw-redirect" title="-chan">-chan</a> <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">ちゃん</span></span></span> to a stem. There are two types of stem. One consists of the full given name. Examples of this type are <i>Tarō-chan</i> from <i>Tarō</i>, <i>Kimiko-chan</i> from <i>Kimiko</i>, and <i>Yasunari-chan</i> from <i>Yasunari</i>. The other type of stem is a modified stem derived from the full given name. Examples of such names are: <i>Ta-chan</i> from <i>Tarō</i>, <i>Kii-chan</i> from <i>Kimiko</i>, and <i>Yā-chan</i> from <i>Yasunari</i>. Hypocoristics with modified stems are more intimate than those based on the full given name.
</p><p>Hypocoristics with modified stems are derived by adding -chan to a stem consisting of an integral number, usually one but occasionally two, of <a href="/wiki/Foot_(prosody)" title="Foot (prosody)">feet</a>, where a foot consists of two <a href="/wiki/Mora_(linguistics)" title="Mora (linguistics)">moras</a>. A mora <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">音節</span></span></span> is the unit of which a light syllable contains one and a heavy syllable two. For example, the stems that may be derived from <i>Tarō</i> are /taro/, consisting of two light syllables, and /taa/, consisting of a single syllable with a long vowel, resulting in <i>Taro-chan</i> and <i>Tā-chan</i>. The stems that may be derived from <i>Hanako</i> are /hana/, with two light syllables, /han/, with one syllable closed by a consonant, and /haa/, with one syllable with a long vowel, resulting in <i>Hanachan</i>, <i>Hanchan</i>, and <i>Hāchan</i>. The segmental content is usually a left substring of that of the given name. However, in some cases it is obtained by other means, including the use of another reading of the <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a> used to write the name. For example, a girl named <i>Megumi</i> may be called <i>Keichan</i> or just <i>Kei</i>, because the character used to write the <i>Megumi</i>, <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">恵</span></span>, can also be read <i>Kei</i>.
</p><p>The common Japanese practice of forming abbreviations by concatenating the first two <a href="/wiki/Mora_(linguistics)" title="Mora (linguistics)">morae</a> of two words is sometimes applied to names (usually those of celebrities). For example, <a href="/wiki/Takuya_Kimura" title="Takuya Kimura">Takuya Kimura</a><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">木村 拓哉</span><span style="display:none">,</span>&#32;<i>Kimura Takuya</i>)</span>, a famous Japanese actor and singer, becomes <i>Kimutaku</i><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">キムタク</span>)</span>. This is sometimes applied even to non-Japanese celebrities: <a href="/wiki/Brad_Pitt" title="Brad Pitt">Brad Pitt</a>, whose full name in Japanese is <i>Buraddo Pitto</i><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">ブラッド・ピット</span>)</span> is commonly known as <i>Burapi</i><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">ブラピ</span>)</span>, and <a href="/wiki/Jimi_Hendrix" title="Jimi Hendrix">Jimi Hendrix</a> is abbreviated as <i>Jimihen</i><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">ジミヘン</span>)</span>. Some <a href="/wiki/Tarento" title="Tarento">Japanese celebrities</a> have also taken names combining kanji and katakana, such as <a href="/wiki/Terry_Ito" title="Terry Ito">Terry Ito</a><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">テリー伊藤</span>)</span>. Another slightly less common method is doubling one or two syllables of the person's name, such as the use of "MamiMami" for <a href="/wiki/Mamiko_Noto" title="Mamiko Noto">Mamiko Noto</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Names_from_other_ethnic_groups_in_Japan">Names from other ethnic groups in Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Names from other ethnic groups in Japan">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-Unreferenced_section plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This section <b>does not <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">cite</a> any <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">sources</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Japanese_name&amp;action=edit">improve this section</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p>Many ethnic minorities, mostly Korean and Chinese, living in Japan adopt Japanese names. The roots of this custom go back to the colonial-era policy of <a href="/wiki/S%C5%8Dshi-kaimei" title="Sōshi-kaimei">sōshi-kaimei</a>, which permitted many Koreans to change their names to Japanese names. Nowadays, ethnic minorities, mostly Korean, who immigrated to Japan after the WWII, take on Japanese names, sometimes called <i>pass names</i>, to ease communication and, more importantly, to avoid <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_issues_in_Japan" title="Ethnic issues in Japan">discrimination</a>. A few of them (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Han_Chang-Woo" class="mw-redirect" title="Han Chang-Woo">Han Chang-Woo</a>, founder and chairman of Maruhan Corp., pronounced <i>Kan Shōyū</i> in Japanese) still keep their native names. Sometimes, however, these ethnic <a href="/wiki/Chinese_people_in_Japan" title="Chinese people in Japan">Chinese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Zainichi_Korean" class="mw-redirect" title="Zainichi Korean">Koreans</a> in Japan who choose to renounce Permanent Resident status to apply for Japanese citizenship have to change the characters in their names, because not all characters are legally recognized in Japan for naming purposes.
</p><p>Japanese citizenship used to require adoption of a Japanese name. In recent decades, the government has allowed individuals to simply adopt katakana versions of their native names when applying for citizenship, as is already done when referring to non-East Asian foreigners: <a href="/wiki/National_Diet_of_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="National Diet of Japan">National Diet</a> member <a href="/wiki/Marutei_Tsurunen" title="Marutei Tsurunen">Tsurunen Marutei</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">ツルネン マルテイ</span></span></span>), originally <i>Martti Turunen</i>, who is <a href="/wiki/Finnish_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Finnish people">Finnish</a>, is a famous example. Others transliterate their names into phonetically similar kanji compounds, such as activist <a href="/wiki/Arudou_Debito" class="mw-redirect" title="Arudou Debito">Arudou Debito</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">有道 出人</span></span></span>), an <a href="/wiki/Americans" title="Americans">American</a> previously known as <i>David Aldwinckle</i> (Tsurunen has similarly adopted 弦念 丸呈), although these renderings are artificial and would not exist in Japan otherwise. Still others have abandoned their native names entirely in favor of traditional Japanese names, such as <a href="/wiki/Lafcadio_Hearn" title="Lafcadio Hearn">Lafcadio Hearn</a> (who was half <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish" class="mw-redirect" title="Anglo-Irish">Anglo-Irish</a> and half <a href="/wiki/Greek_people" class="mw-redirect" title="Greek people">Greek</a>), who used the name "Koizumi Yakumo" (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">小泉 八雲</span></span></span>). At the time, to gain Japanese citizenship, it was necessary to be adopted by a Japanese family (in Hearn's case, it was his wife's family) and take their name.
</p><p>Individuals born overseas with Western given names and Japanese surnames are usually given a katakana name in Western order when referred to in Japanese. <a href="/wiki/Eric_Shinseki" title="Eric Shinseki">Eric Shinseki</a>, for instance, is referred to as <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">エリック シンセキ</span></span></span> (Erikku Shinseki). However, sometimes Japanese parents decide to use Japanese order when mentioning the child's name in Japanese. Also, Japanese parents tend to give their children a name in kanji, hiragana or katakana, particularly if it is a Japanese name. Even individuals born in Japan, with a Japanese name, might be referred to using katakana, if they've established residency or a career overseas. <a href="/wiki/Yoko_Ono" title="Yoko Ono">Yoko Ono</a>, for example, was born in Japan, with the name <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">小野 洋子</span></span></span>, and spent the first twenty years of her life there. However, having lived outside the country for more than fifty years, and basing her career in the United States, Ono is often referred to in the press as <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">オノ・ヨーコ</span></span></span>, preserving the Japanese order of her name (Ono Yōko), but rendering it in katakana.
</p><p>There is a restriction (as of 2001<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Japanese_name&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>) on the use of the "v" character in a name unless at least one of the parents is of foreign origin.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2007)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The closest corresponding katakana is <span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">ヴ</span></span> (vu), which can be romanized as v or b. This affects issuing of Japanese <a href="/wiki/Passport" title="Passport">passports</a> or other documentation where a romanization of the name is given; the letter v is replaced with b. This affects names such as Kevin (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">ケヴィン</span></span></span>), which would be written as Kebin.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Japan's Christians traditionally have Christian names in addition to their native Japanese names. These Christian names are written using katakana, and are adapted to Japanese phonology from their original Latin forms rather than being borrowed from any particular language like English. Peter, for example, is <i>Petoro</i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">ペトロ</span></span></span>), John is <i>Yohane</i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">ヨハネ</span></span></span>), Jacob is <i>Yakobu</i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">ヤコブ</span></span></span>), Martin is <i>Maruchino</i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">マルチノ</span></span></span>), Dominic is <i>Dominiko</i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">ドミニコ</span></span></span>), and so on.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> For most purposes in real life, the Christian names aren't used; for example, <a href="/wiki/Taro_Aso" class="mw-redirect" title="Taro Aso">Taro Aso</a> has a Christian name, Francis (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">フランシスコ</span></span></span> <i>Furanshisuko</i>), which is not nearly as well-known.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Imperial_names">Imperial names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Imperial names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Akishinodera1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Akishinodera1.jpg/250px-Akishinodera1.jpg" decoding="async" width="250" height="171" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Akishinodera1.jpg/375px-Akishinodera1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Akishinodera1.jpg/500px-Akishinodera1.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1250" data-file-height="853" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Akishinodera1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Akishino-dera in <a href="/wiki/Nara,_Nara" title="Nara, Nara">Nara</a>, from which <a href="/wiki/Prince_Akishino" class="mw-redirect" title="Prince Akishino">Prince Akishino</a> took his name</div></div></div>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Household_of_Japan" class="mw-redirect" title="Imperial Household of Japan">Imperial Household of Japan</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_Japan" title="Emperor of Japan">Japanese emperor</a> and his families have no surname for historical reasons, only a given name such as <a href="/wiki/Hirohito" title="Hirohito">Hirohito</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">裕仁</span></span></span>), which is almost universally avoided in Japan: Japanese prefer to say "the Emperor" or "the Crown Prince", out of respect and as a measure of politeness.
</p><p>When children are born into the Imperial family, they receive a standard given name, as well as a special title. For instance, the Emperor emeritus <a href="/wiki/Akihito" title="Akihito">Akihito</a> was born <i>Tsugu-no-miya Akihito</i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">継宮明仁</span></span></span>), his title being Tsugu-no-miya (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">継宮</span></span></span> "Prince Tsugu"), and was referred to as "Prince Tsugu" during his childhood. This title is generally used until the individual becomes heir to the throne or inherits one of the historical princely family names (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">常陸宮</span></span></span> <i>Hitachi-no-miya</i>, <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">三笠宮</span></span></span> <i>Mikasa-no-miya</i>, <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">秋篠宮</span></span></span> <i>Akishino-no-miya</i>, etc.).
</p><p>When a member of the Imperial family becomes a noble or a commoner, the emperor gives him or her a family name. In medieval era, a family name "<a href="/wiki/Minamoto_clan" title="Minamoto clan">Minamoto</a>" was often used. In modern era, princely family names are used. For example, many members of the extended Imperial family became commoners after <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, and adopted their princely family names, minus the honorific "no-miya" (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">宮</span></span></span> "Prince"), as regular surnames. Conversely, at the time that a noble or a commoner become a member of the Imperial family, such as through marriage, his or her family name is lost. An example is <a href="/wiki/Empress_Michiko" title="Empress Michiko">Empress Michiko</a>, whose name was Michiko Shōda before she married prince <a href="/wiki/Akihito" title="Akihito">Akihito</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_names">Historical names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Historical names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The current structure (<i>family name</i> + <i>given name</i>) did not materialize until the 1870s when the government made the new family registration system.
</p><p>In feudal Japan, names reflected a person's social status, as well as their affiliation with Buddhist, Shintō, feudatory-military, Confucian-scholarly, mercantile, peasant, slave and imperial orders.
</p><p>Before feudal times, <a href="/wiki/Japanese_clan" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese clan">Japanese clan</a> names figured prominently in history: names with <i>no</i> fall into this category. <i>No</i> means <i>of</i> and is similar in usage to the aristocratic <i>von</i> in German although the association is in the opposite order in Japanese, and is not generally explicitly written in this style of name. Thus, <a href="/wiki/Minamoto_no_Yoritomo" title="Minamoto no Yoritomo">Minamoto no Yoritomo</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">源 頼朝</span></span></span>) was Yoritomo (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">頼朝</span></span></span>) of the <a href="/wiki/Minamoto" class="mw-redirect" title="Minamoto">Minamoto</a> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">源</span></span>) clan. <a href="/wiki/Fujiwara_no_Kamatari" title="Fujiwara no Kamatari">Fujiwara no Kamatari</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">藤原 鎌足</span></span></span>), <a href="/wiki/Ki_no_Tsurayuki" title="Ki no Tsurayuki">Ki no Tsurayuki</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">紀 貫之</span></span></span>), and <a href="/wiki/Taira_no_Kiyomori" title="Taira no Kiyomori">Taira no Kiyomori</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">平 清盛</span></span></span>) are additional examples. These family names were recorded in <a href="/wiki/Shinsen_Sh%C5%8Djiroku" title="Shinsen Shōjiroku">Shinsen Shōjiroku</a>. <a href="/wiki/Ryukyuan_people" title="Ryukyuan people">Ryukyuan</a> ruling class used names composed of Chinese characters, usually of one or two syllables and read in their <a href="/wiki/Ryukyuan_languages" title="Ryukyuan languages">languages</a>, like Korean and Chinese names.
</p><p>Before the government formalized the naming system in 1868, Japanese personal names were fluid.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Men changed their names for a variety of reasons: to signify that they had attained a higher social status, to demonstrate their allegiance to a house or clan, to show that they had succeeded to the headship of a family or company, to shed bad luck that was attached to an inauspicious name, or simply to avoid being mistaken for a neighbor with a similar name.<sup id="cite_ref-Plutschow_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Plutschow-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Upper-class men often changed their names upon coming of age (<a href="/wiki/Genpuku" title="Genpuku">genpuku</a>), leaving behind their childhood name (which often ended with -maru) and taking on an adult name.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> When nobles and samurai received promotions in rank, they received new names, which might contain a syllable or character from their lord's name as a mark of favor.<sup id="cite_ref-Plutschow_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Plutschow-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Women's personal name changes were recorded less often, so they may not have changed their names as frequently as men did, but women who went into service as maids or entertainers frequently changed their names for the duration of their service. During their employment, their temporary names were treated as their legal names. For example, a maid who was involved in legal dealings in Kyoto in 1819-1831 signed legal documents as Sayo during one period of employment and as Mitsu during a later period of employment, but she signed as Iwa, presumably her birth name, when she was between jobs.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A Japanese person could go by one of several names, depending on the occasion. For example, the famous 18th-century author, poet, and artist Iwase Samuru wrote under the name <a href="/wiki/Sant%C5%8D_Ky%C5%8Dden" title="Santō Kyōden">Santō Kyōden</a> and worked as an illustrator under the name Kitao Masanobu. Artists and authors adopted a new name for each medium or form they worked in, whether or not they worked professionally. Some types of artistic names (<i>gō</i> [<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">号</span></span>]) were referred to by special terminology—for example, <i>haigō</i> or <i>haimei</i> for a <a href="/wiki/Haiku" title="Haiku">haiku</a> poet, and <i>kagō</i> for a <a href="/wiki/Waka_(poetry)" title="Waka (poetry)">Waka</a> poet. Scholars also gave themselves a scholarly name, which was often the Chinese reading of the characters of their Japanese name. People who entered a religious order adopted a religious name.
</p><p>Death added to the number of a person's names. When a person died, their personal name was referred to as an <i>imina</i><span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">諱</span>)</span> and was no longer used. Instead, the person was referred to by their posthumous name<span style="font-weight: normal">&#32;(<span lang="ja">諡</span><span style="display:none">,</span>&#32;<i>okurina</i>)</span>.
</p><p>The personal names of Japanese emperors were also referred to as <i>imina</i>, even if the emperor was alive. Prior to <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Jomei" title="Emperor Jomei">Emperor Jomei</a>, the <i>imina</i> of the emperors were very long and not used. The number of characters in each name diminished after Jomei's reign.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i>Azana</i> (<span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">字</span></span>), which is given at <a href="/wiki/Genpuku" title="Genpuku">genpuku</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">元服</span></span></span>), is used by others and one himself uses his real name to refer to him. <i>Gō</i> are commonly named after places or houses; e.g., Basho, as in the Haiku poet <a href="/wiki/Matsuo_Bash%C5%8D" title="Matsuo Bashō">Matsuo Bashō</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">松尾 芭蕉</span></span></span>), is named after his house, <i>Bashō-an</i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">芭蕉庵</span></span></span>).
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/Late_shogunate_period" class="mw-redirect" title="Late shogunate period">late shogunate period</a>, many anti-government activists used several false names to hide their activities from the <a href="/wiki/Shogunate" class="mw-redirect" title="Shogunate">shogunate</a>. Examples are Saidani Umetarō (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">才谷 梅太郎</span></span></span>) for <a href="/wiki/Sakamoto_Ry%C5%8Dma" title="Sakamoto Ryōma">Sakamoto Ryōma</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">坂本 龍馬</span></span></span>), Niibori Matsusuke (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">新堀 松輔</span></span></span>) for <a href="/wiki/Kido_Takayoshi" title="Kido Takayoshi">Kido Takayoshi</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">木戸 孝允</span></span></span>) and Tani Umenosuke (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">谷 梅之助</span></span></span>) for <a href="/wiki/Takasugi_Shinsaku" title="Takasugi Shinsaku">Takasugi Shinsaku</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">高杉 晋作</span></span></span>). The famous writer <a href="/wiki/Kyokutei_Bakin" class="mw-redirect" title="Kyokutei Bakin">Kyokutei Bakin</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">曲亭 馬琴</span></span></span>) is known to have had as many as 33 names.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_names">Professional names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Professional names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Actor" title="Actor">Actors</a> and actresses in <a href="/wiki/Western_culture" title="Western culture">Western</a> and Japanese <a href="/wiki/Drama" title="Drama">dramatic</a> forms, <a href="/wiki/Comedian" title="Comedian">comedians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sumo" title="Sumo">sumo</a> wrestlers, <a href="/wiki/Puroresu" title="Puroresu">Western-style professional</a> wrestlers, and practitioners of traditional <a href="/wiki/Craft" title="Craft">crafts</a> often use professional names. Many stage names of <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> and <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">film</a> actors and actresses are unremarkable, being just like ordinary Japanese personal names, but a few are tongue-in-cheek. For example, <a href="/wiki/Kamatari_Fujiwara" title="Kamatari Fujiwara">Kamatari Fujiwara</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">藤原 釜足</span></span></span>) chose the name of the aforementioned founder of the <a href="/wiki/Fujiwara_family" class="mw-redirect" title="Fujiwara family">Fujiwara family</a>, while <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hino_Yojin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Hino Yojin (page does not exist)">Hino Yōjin</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">日野 陽仁</span></span></span>)'s name sounds like <i>be careful with fire</i> (although written differently). Many stand-up comics like the duo <a href="/wiki/Takeshi_Kitano" title="Takeshi Kitano">Beat Takeshi</a> and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Beat_Kiyoshi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Beat Kiyoshi (page does not exist)">Beat Kiyoshi</a> choose a Western name for the act, and use their own (or stage) given names. Writers also tend to be clever about their names, for example <a href="/wiki/Edogawa_Ranpo" title="Edogawa Ranpo">Edogawa Ranpo</a> which is designed to sound like "<a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">Edgar Allan Poe</a>".
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Sumo" title="Sumo">Sumo</a> wrestlers take wrestling names called <i><a href="/wiki/Shikona" title="Shikona">shikona</a></i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">醜名</span></span></span> or <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">四股名</span></span></span>). While a shikona can be the wrestler's own surname, most upper-division <a href="/wiki/Rikishi" title="Rikishi">rikishi</a> have a shikona different from their surname. A typical shikona consists of two or three kanji, rarely just one or more than three. Often, part of the name comes from the wrestler's master, a place name (such as the name of a <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Japan" title="Provinces of Japan">province</a>, a <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">sea</a>), the name of a weapon, an item identified with Japanese tradition (like a <a href="/wiki/Koto_(musical_instrument)" class="mw-redirect" title="Koto (musical instrument)">koto</a> or <a href="/wiki/Brocade" title="Brocade">nishiki</a>), or a term indicating superiority. Often, <i>waka</i> indicates a wrestler whose father was also in sumo; in this case, the meaning is <i>junior</i>. Wrestlers can change their shikona, as Takahanada did when he became Takanohana (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">貴ノ花</span></span></span>) and then <a href="/wiki/Takanohana_Koji" class="mw-redirect" title="Takanohana Koji">Takanohana</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">貴乃花</span></span></span>). Another notable example is the wrestler <a href="/wiki/Sentoryu" class="mw-redirect" title="Sentoryu">Sentoryu</a>, which means <i>fighting war dragon</i> but is also homophonous with <a href="/wiki/St._Louis,_Missouri" class="mw-redirect" title="St. Louis, Missouri">St. Louis</a>, his city of origin.
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Geisha" title="Geisha">Geisha</a> and practitioners of traditional crafts and arts such as <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">pottery</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Tea_ceremony" title="Tea ceremony">tea ceremony</a>, <a href="/wiki/Calligraphy" title="Calligraphy">calligraphy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irezumi" title="Irezumi">irezumi</a> (tattooing) and <a href="/wiki/Ikebana" title="Ikebana">ikebana</a> (flower arranging) often take professional names. In many cases, these come from the master under whom they studied. <a href="/wiki/Kabuki" title="Kabuki">Kabuki</a> actors take one of the traditional surnames such as Nakamura (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">中村</span></span></span>), Bandō or Onoe. Some names are inherited on succession, such as that of the famous Kabuki actor <a href="/wiki/Band%C5%8D_Tamasabur%C5%8D_V" title="Bandō Tamasaburō V">Bandō Tamasaburō V</a> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">五代目 坂東 玉三郎</span></span></span> <i>Godaime Bandō Tamasaburō</i>) through a <a href="/wiki/Sh%C5%ABmei" title="Shūmei">naming ceremony</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese_names_in_English_and_other_Western_languages">Japanese names in English and other Western languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Japanese names in English and other Western languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In English, the names of living or recently deceased Japanese are generally given surname last and without macrons.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> Historical figures are given surname first and with macrons, if available.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:MomoiExpoCrop.png" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/MomoiExpoCrop.png/220px-MomoiExpoCrop.png" decoding="async" width="220" height="186" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/MomoiExpoCrop.png/330px-MomoiExpoCrop.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/MomoiExpoCrop.png/440px-MomoiExpoCrop.png 2x" data-file-width="1592" data-file-height="1344" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:MomoiExpoCrop.png" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div><a href="/wiki/Haruko_Momoi" title="Haruko Momoi">Haruko Momoi</a> at the <a href="/wiki/Anime_Expo" title="Anime Expo">Anime Expo</a> 2007 in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>; her name card features a spelling of her name ("Halko Momoi") written surname last. In Japanese, her name is <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">桃井はるこ</span></span></span> (<i>Momoi Haruko</i>).</div></div></div>
<p>As of 2008, when using English and other Western languages Japanese people usually give their names in an order reversed from the traditional Japanese naming order, with the family name after the given name, instead of the given name after the family name.<sup id="cite_ref-PowerpC4-2_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PowerpC4-2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Beginning in <a href="/wiki/Meiji_Era" class="mw-redirect" title="Meiji Era">Meiji Era</a> Japan, in many English-language publications the naming order of modern-day Japanese people is reversed into the family name last order.<sup id="cite_ref-Mextname_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mextname-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> Japanese people adopted using western naming order in European languages as a part of the Meiji era adoption of aspects of western culture, as part of proving to the wider world that Japan was a developed country rather than an undeveloped country. When Japanese people attended events for the international community, such as balls, Japanese people used the western naming order.<sup id="cite_ref-Saeki_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saeki-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Japanese often have nicknames that are shortened forms of their actual names, and they use these names with foreigners. For instance "Kazuyuki" may call himself "Kaz."<sup id="cite_ref-PowerpC4-2_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PowerpC4-2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Some Japanese living abroad adopt nicknames that they use with friends who are non-Japanese; these names are not considered middle names.<sup id="cite_ref-PowerpC4-2_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PowerpC4-2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tleft"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Orikasa_Fumiko_(3).jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Orikasa_Fumiko_%283%29.jpg/220px-Orikasa_Fumiko_%283%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="146" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Orikasa_Fumiko_%283%29.jpg/330px-Orikasa_Fumiko_%283%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Orikasa_Fumiko_%283%29.jpg/440px-Orikasa_Fumiko_%283%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4256" data-file-height="2832" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Orikasa_Fumiko_(3).jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Here <a href="/wiki/Fumiko_Orikasa" title="Fumiko Orikasa">Fumiko Orikasa</a>'s name is presented family name first in Japanese while it is presented given name first in English.</div></div></div>
<p>Most foreign publications reverse the names of modern individuals, and most Japanese reverse their own names when creating materials for foreign consumption. A Japanese executive or official usually has two business cards (<a href="/wiki/Meishi" class="mw-redirect" title="Meishi">meishi</a>): one in Japanese and intended for fellow Japanese, using Japanese order, and another intended for foreigners, with the name in Western order.<sup id="cite_ref-Terry632_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Terry632-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> In popular journalism publications, western order is used.<sup id="cite_ref-Saeki_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saeki-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In English many historical figures are still referred to with the family name first.<sup id="cite_ref-Mextname_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Mextname-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> This is especially the case in scholarly works about Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-Saeki_28-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saeki-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Many scholarly works use Japanese order with Japanese names in general, and a scholarly work is more likely to use Japanese order if the author is a Japanologist. John Power, author of "Japanese names," wrote "People who can speak and read Japanese have a strong resistance to switching Japanese names to the Western order."<sup id="cite_ref-PowerpC4-2_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PowerpC4-2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Books written by these authors often have notes stating that Japanese names are in the original order.<sup id="cite_ref-PowerpC4-2_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PowerpC4-2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Some books do not have consistent naming order practices. Shizuka Saeki of <i><a href="/wiki/Look_Japan" title="Look Japan">Look Japan</a></i> said, "This is not only a headache for writers and translators, it is also a source of confusion for readers."<sup id="cite_ref-Saeki_28-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saeki-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> Lynne E. Riggs of the Society of Writers, Editors and Translators (SWET), a professional writing organization headquartered in <a href="/wiki/Tokyo" title="Tokyo">Tokyo</a>, said, "When you publish a book about Japan, you are publishing it for people who want to know about Japan. So they are interested in learning something new or something as it is supposed to be."<sup id="cite_ref-Saeki_28-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saeki-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Edith Terry, author of <i>How Asia Got Rich</i>, said that because Japanese people are "mastering" a "Western game" people have some pride and at the same time feel insecurity because the "game" is on "Western terms" rather than "Japanese terms."<sup id="cite_ref-Terry632_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Terry632-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup> The standard presentation of Japanese names in English differs from the standard presentations of modern <a href="/wiki/Chinese_name" title="Chinese name">Chinese names</a> and <a href="/wiki/Korean_name" title="Korean name">Korean name</a>, which are usually not reversed to fit the western order in English, except when the person is living or traveling abroad.<sup id="cite_ref-Terry632_29-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Terry632-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-PowerpC4-2_9-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PowerpC4-2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Power wrote that the difference between the treatment of Japanese names and of Chinese and Korean names often results in confusion.<sup id="cite_ref-PowerpC4-2_9-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PowerpC4-2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Terry wrote, "it was one of the ironies of the late twentieth century that Japan remained stranded in the formal devices underlining its historical quest for equality with the West, while China set its own terms, in language as in big-power politics."<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Saeki said in 2001 that most Japanese people writing in English use western order, but that some figures began to promote the use of Japanese order as Japan became a major economic power in the 20th century. The <i>Japan Style Sheet</i>, a 1998 guide for producing English language works about Japan written by SWET, advocates the use of the Japanese naming order as often as possible because the translators wanted to promote a consistency in naming order. In 1987, one publisher of English language textbooks in Japan used Japanese order, while in 2001 six of the eight publishers of English language textbooks in Japan use Japanese order. In December 2000 the Council on the National Language of the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Education,_Culture,_Sports,_Science_and_Technology" title="Ministry of Education, Culture, Sports, Science and Technology">Ministry of Education</a> recommended that English language productions begin using the Japanese naming order because "it is in general desirable that personal names be presented and written in a way that preserves their unique forms, except for registries and other documents with specific standards." It recommended using capitalization (YAMADA Taro) or commas (Yamada, Taro) to clarify which part of the personal name is the family name and which part is the given name. In a January 2000 opinion poll from the Agency for Cultural Affairs on the preferred order of Japanese names in the English language, 34.9% had a preference for Japanese order, 30.6% had a preference for Western order, and 29.6% had no preference. In 1986 the <a href="/wiki/Japan_Foundation" title="Japan Foundation">Japan Foundation</a> decided that it would use the Japanese naming order in all of its publications. A Japan Foundation publishing division spokesperson stated around 2001 that some SWET publications, including popular anglophone newspapers, continue to use western order. As of 2001 the agency's style sheet recommends using a different naming order style depending upon the context. For instance it advocates using the western order in publications for readers who are not familiar with Japan, such as international conference papers.<sup id="cite_ref-Saeki_28-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Saeki-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Manual_of_Style" class="mw-redirect" title="Chicago Manual of Style">Chicago Manual of Style</a></i> recommends indexing Japanese names according to the way the original text treats the name. If the text uses the Western order, the Japanese name is reinverted and indexed by the family name with a comma. If the text uses Japanese order, the name is listed by the family name with no inversion and no comma.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On 21 May 2019 <a href="/wiki/Japanese_Foreign_Minister" class="mw-redirect" title="Japanese Foreign Minister">Japanese Foreign Minister</a> <a href="/wiki/Tar%C5%8D_K%C5%8Dno" title="Tarō Kōno">Tarō Kōno</a> expressed his hope that foreign media would refer to Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Shinz%C5%8D_Abe" title="Shinzō Abe">Shinzō Abe</a> in the Japanese custom: family name first (as "Abe Shinzō"). He added that he was currently planning to issue an official request to the international media in that respect.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> Some others in the government support moving to retaining the original order of names, in line with Chinese and Korean practice, in time for the several major global events the country will be host to during 2020, while others seem not to.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>On September 6, 2019, Japan announced that it will start using the traditional order for Japanese names in English in official documents.<sup id="cite_ref-yamaguchi_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-yamaguchi-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese_names_in_Chinese">Japanese names in Chinese</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Japanese_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Japanese names in Chinese">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<table class="box-More_citations_needed plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation"><tbody><tr><td class="mbox-image"><div style="width:52px"><a href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" decoding="async" width="50" height="39" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" data-file-width="512" data-file-height="399" /></a></div></td><td class="mbox-text"><div class="mbox-text-span">This article <b>needs additional citations for <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">verification</a></b>.<span class="hide-when-compact"> Please help <a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Japanese_name&amp;action=edit">improve this article</a> by <a href="/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_referencing_with_Wiki_Markup/1" title="Help:Introduction to referencing with Wiki Markup/1">adding citations to reliable sources</a>. <p>In Chinese-speaking communities, Japanese names are pronounced according to the Chinese pronunciation of the characters.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> For example, in Mandarin, <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">山田 太郎</span></span></span> (<i>Yamada Tarō</i>) becomes <i>Shāntián Tàiláng</i>, while <span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">鳩山 由紀夫</span></span></span> (<i>Hatoyama Yukio</i>) becomes <i>Jiūshān Yóujìfū</i>. As a result, a Japanese person without adequate knowledge of Chinese would not understand their name when it is spoken in Chinese. Simply porting the kanji into Chinese and reading them as if they were Chinese is also different from the usual Chinese practice of approximating foreign names with similar-sounding Chinese characters.
</p><p>Sometimes, a Japanese name includes <a href="/wiki/Kokuji" class="mw-redirect" title="Kokuji">kokuji</a>. These <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">kanji</a> resemble <a href="/wiki/Chinese_characters" title="Chinese characters">Chinese characters</a> but originate in Japan and do not have widely-known Chinese pronunciations. For example, the word <i>komu</i> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">込</span></span></span>, read as <i>Yū</i> in Chinese) is rarely used in modern Chinese reading. When words like this are encountered, usually the rule of "<span lang="zh-Hant" title="Chinese language text">有邊讀邊，沒邊讀中間</span>" ("<a href="/wiki/Youbian_dubian" title="Youbian dubian">read the side if any</a>, read the middle part if there is no side") applies. Therefore, "<span class="nowrap"><span class="t_nihongo_kanji"><span lang="ja" title="Japanese language text">込</span></span></span>" is read as "rù" which is derived from <span lang="zh-Hans" title="Chinese language text">入</span>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Heng Ji, the author of "Improving Information Extraction and Translation Using Component Interactions," wrote that because Japanese names have "flexible" lengths, it may be difficult for someone to identify a Japanese name when reading a Chinese text.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> When consulting English texts a Chinese reader may have difficulty identifying a Japanese name; an example was when Chinese media mistook Obama's pet turkey <i>Abe</i> taken from <a href="/wiki/Abe_Lincoln" class="mw-redirect" title="Abe Lincoln">Abe Lincoln</a> (monosyllabic) for <a href="/wiki/Shinzo_Abe" class="mw-redirect" title="Shinzo Abe">Shinzo Abe</a> (disyllabic).<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One place where Japanese names may be translated into Chinese languages phonetically is in Japanese video games, anime and manga series. In May 2016, <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo</a> sparked anger among fans in <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">Hong Kong</a> by announcing that its new <i><a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon" title="Pokémon">Pokémon</a></i> games, <a href="/wiki/Pok%C3%A9mon_Sun_and_Moon" title="Pokémon Sun and Moon"><i>Sun</i> and <i>Moon</i></a>, would be translated into Mandarin across all parts of China and <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, meaning that names of well-known characters such as <a href="/wiki/Pikachu" title="Pikachu">Pikachu</a> would no longer have the correct pronunciation when read in places such as Hong Kong and other regions where Mandarin is not the main language spoken.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
