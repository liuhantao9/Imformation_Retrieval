	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Latvian name</h1>
	
	<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
		<div id="siteSub" class="noprint">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
		<div id="contentSub"></div>
		
		
		
		<div id="jump-to-nav"></div>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#mw-head">Jump to navigation</a>
		<a class="mw-jump-link" href="#p-search">Jump to search</a>
		<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><div class="mw-parser-output"><p><b>Latvian names,</b> like in most <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> cultures, consist of two main elements: the <a href="/wiki/Given_name" title="Given name">given name</a> (<i>vārds</i>) followed by <a href="/wiki/Family_name" class="mw-redirect" title="Family name">family name</a> (<i>uzvārds</i>). During the <a href="/wiki/Latvian_Soviet_Socialist_Republic" title="Latvian Soviet Socialist Republic">Soviet occupation</a> (1940–1941;1944–1991) the practice of giving a <a href="/wiki/Middle_name" title="Middle name">middle name</a> (<i>otrais vārds</i>) was discouraged, but since the restoration of Independence Latvian legislation again allows giving of up to two given names and it has become more common to give a middle name to children.
</p><p>Latvian male names end in <a href="/wiki/Latvian_declension#Masculine_declensions" title="Latvian declension">1st or 2nd declension masculine endings</a>, either <i>-s/-š</i> or <i>-is</i> (with a handful of mostly foreign exceptions ending in indeclinable <i>-o</i>, such as <i><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Ivo#Latvian" class="extiw" title="wikt:Ivo">Ivo</a></i>, <i>Raivo</i>, <i>Gvido</i>, <i>Bruno</i>, <i>Oto</i> and only a couple belonging to the 3rd declension ending in <i>-us</i>, such as <i>Ingus</i>, <i>Mikus</i>, <i>Edžus</i>, <i>Zemgus</i>.) Latvian female names have the <a href="/wiki/Latvian_declension#Feminine_declensions" title="Latvian declension">feminine 4th or 5th declension endings</a> <i>-a</i> or <i>-e</i> respectively. 
</p><p>For centuries one of the most popular Latvian names has been <i><a href="/wiki/J%C4%81nis" title="Jānis">Jānis</a></i>, whose written use dates back to 1290.<sup id="cite_ref-Jacobs_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobs-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> The vocative case is used when addressing someone directly, for example, <i>Jāni</i> for <i>Jānis.</i> The diminutive form is often used to express endearment or when addressing children, for example, addressing <i>Jānis</i> as <i>Jānīt</i> (vocative diminutive).
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Spelling">Spelling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latvian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Spelling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Writing of Latvian names always conform to the highly phonetic <a href="/wiki/Latvian_orthography" title="Latvian orthography">Latvian orthography</a> and in the case of foreign born Latvian nationals or marriages between Latvian women and foreigners (whence they assume the family name of their husband) the foreign names are modified to conform to the phonetic spelling and to acquire the respective case ending. For example, <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Depardieu" class="mw-redirect" title="Gerard Depardieu">Gerard Depardieu</a> is <i>Žerārs Depardjē</i>, <a href="/wiki/Joaquin_Phoenix" title="Joaquin Phoenix">Joaquin Phoenix</a> is <i>Hoakins Fīnikss</i> and <a href="/wiki/Donald_Trump" title="Donald Trump">Donald Trump</a> is <i>Donalds Tramps</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>This has given rise to at least half a dozen lawsuits over the last couple decades, mostly ethnic Russian Latvian nationals not content with addition of case endings as well as a Latvian woman contesting her foreign husband's name being transcribed phonetically in her documents (Mentzen <i>alias</i> Mencena v. Latvia case) where the plaintiffs were turned down as well as legal proceedings by a Latvian couple to allow them to register their child as <i>Otto</i> (instead of <i>Oto</i>) and a claim filed with UN HRC by a Latvian national of Russian-Jewish <a href="/wiki/Raihman_v._Latvia" title="Raihman v. Latvia">Leonid Raihman</a> whose claims were upheld.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latvian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Before the <a href="/wiki/Christianization_of_Latvia" class="mw-redirect" title="Christianization of Latvia">Christianization of Latvia</a> in 13th century Latvians commonly gave their children names of objects from natural surroundings, such as <a href="/wiki/Irbe" title="Irbe">Irbe</a> (<a href="/wiki/Partridge" title="Partridge">partridge</a>), <a href="/wiki/L%C4%81cis" title="Lācis">Lācis</a> (<a href="/wiki/Bear" title="Bear">bear</a>), <a href="/wiki/Ieva" title="Ieva">Ieva</a> (<a href="/wiki/Cherry_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Cherry tree">cherry tree</a>) and <a href="/w/index.php?title=%C4%80bele&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ābele (page does not exist)">Ābele</a> (<a href="/wiki/Apple_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Apple tree">apple tree</a>), many of whom later became last names. After the Christianization Latvians began giving their children Christian first names, such as Marija, Anna, and Pēteris. Lutheran priest Christoph Harder also coined a number of new names from Latvian words for different <a href="/wiki/Virtue" title="Virtue">virtues</a> like Dievmīlis (God-lover), Strādulis (hard-worker), Žēlite (sorrowful person), and Skaidrīte (clear person).<sup id="cite_ref-Jacobs_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jacobs-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Before the emancipation from serfdom (1817 in <a href="/wiki/Courland" title="Courland">Courland</a>, 1819 in <a href="/wiki/Vidzeme" title="Vidzeme">Vidzeme</a>, 1861 in <a href="/wiki/Latgale" title="Latgale">Latgale</a>) only noblemen, free craftsmen or people living in towns had surnames. Therefore, the oldest Latvian surnames originate from German or Low German, reflecting the dominance of German as an official language in Latvia till the 19th century. Examples: <i>Meijers/Meijere</i> (German: <i>Meier</i>, farm administrator; akin to Mayor), <i>Millers/Millere</i> (German: <i>Müller</i>, miller), <i>Šmits/Šmite</i> (German: <i>Schmidt</i>, smith), <i>Šulcs/Šulca</i> (German: <i>Schulze</i>, constable), <i><a href="/wiki/Ulmanis" title="Ulmanis">Ulmanis</a></i> (German: <i>Ullmann</i>, a person from <a href="/wiki/Ulm" title="Ulm">Ulm</a>), <i>Godmanis</i> (a God-man), <i>Pētersons</i> (son of Peter). Some Latvian surnames, mainly from Latgale are of Polish or Belorussian origin by changing the final <i>-ski/-cki</i> to <i>-skis/-ckis</i>, <i>-czyk</i> to <i>-čiks</i>  or <i>-vich/-wicz</i> to <i>-vičs</i>, such as <i>Sokolovskis/Sokolovska</i>, <i>Baldunčiks/Baldunčika</i> or <i>Ratkevičs/Ratkeviča</i>.
</p><p>The official records of Latvian names were often variously forcibly assimilated into the foreign culture dominant at times in Latvian lands.  For example, local <a href="/wiki/Pastor" title="Pastor">pastors</a>, who were often of <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">German</a> descent, used to issue marriage and birth certificates with <a href="/wiki/Germanization" class="mw-redirect" title="Germanization">Germanized</a> names: e.g., <i>Kalns</i> was written as <i>Berg</i> (both meaning "<a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">mountain</a>" in Latvian and German respectively). Sometimes "de-Germanization" produced a slightly different name, e.g., <i>Daugmants</i> was Germanized as <i>Daugmann</i> and then de-Germanized into <i>Daugmanis.</i><sup id="cite_ref-bilmanis_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bilmanis-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most Latvian peasants received their surnames in 1826 (in <a href="/wiki/Vidzeme" title="Vidzeme">Vidzeme</a>), in 1835 (in <a href="/wiki/Courland" title="Courland">Courland</a>), and in 1866 (in <a href="/wiki/Latgale" title="Latgale">Latgale</a>). Diminutives were the most common form of family names. Examples: <i>Kalniņš/Kalniņa</i> (small hill), <i>Bērziņš/Bērziņa</i> (small birch).
</p><p>During the times when <a href="/wiki/Latvia" title="Latvia">Latvia</a> was part of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Empire" title="Russian Empire">Russian Empire</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a>, in official usage Latvian names were commonly <a href="/wiki/Russification" title="Russification">russified</a>. In particular, it followed the three-part pattern of <a href="/wiki/Russian_names" class="mw-redirect" title="Russian names">Russian names</a>: given name, <a href="/wiki/Patronymic" title="Patronymic">patronymic</a>, family name. Also, the masculine endings of first names were often truncated. For example, poet <a href="/wiki/Imants_Ziedonis" title="Imants Ziedonis">Imants Ziedonis</a> was officially called Imant Yanovich Ziedonis (Имант Янович Зиедонис<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>)
</p><p>In the 20th century, in particular in the <a href="/wiki/Interbellum" class="mw-redirect" title="Interbellum">interbellum</a> period of the Latvian national movement and during the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ulmanis_authoritarian_regime&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ulmanis authoritarian regime (page does not exist)">Ulmanis authoritarian regime</a> in the late 1930s, when <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Germans" title="Baltic Germans">Baltic Germans</a> left Latvia, there was a tendency to change the Germanic names back to their Latvian origins or to adopt Latvian versions.<sup id="cite_ref-bilmanis_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bilmanis-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> In one such example Minister of Interior Kornēlijs Veitmanis became <a href="/w/index.php?title=Korn%C4%93lijs_Veidnieks&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Kornēlijs Veidnieks (page does not exist)">Kornēlijs Veidnieks</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Name_day">Name day</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latvian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Name day">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Name_day#Latvia" title="Name day">Name day §&#160;Latvia</a></div>
<p>Latvia is among the European countries that celebrate <a href="/wiki/Name_day" title="Name day">name days</a> (<i>vārda dienas</i>), a celebration almost comparable in importance to that of a birthday. Most of them are related to the Saints' days in the Church calendar, but in recent decades new names have been added to the calendar by a special commission. Some names and their name days bear a connection with important holidays, for example, arguably one of the most important holidays, summer solstice, referred to as <i><a href="/wiki/J%C4%81%C5%86i" title="Jāņi">Jāņi</a></i> starts on June 23 with <i>Līgo diena</i> (name day for females named <i>Līga</i>) and continues through June 24 or <a href="/wiki/J%C4%81%C5%86i" title="Jāņi">Jāņi</a> – name day for males named <i>Jānis</i>. Similarly <i><a href="/wiki/M%C4%81rti%C5%86i" title="Mārtiņi">Mārtiņi</a></i> on November 10 coincides with the name day for males named <i>Mārtiņš</i>, <i>Mārcis</i> and <i>Markuss</i>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Most_common_Latvian_names">Most common Latvian names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Latvian_name&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Most common Latvian names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Below are the most common ethnic Latvian names in 2006. However taking into account the large Eastern Slavic diaspora (<a href="/wiki/Russians_in_Latvia" title="Russians in Latvia">Russians</a>, <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ukrainians_in_Latvia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ukrainians in Latvia (page does not exist)">Ukrainians</a>, <a href="/wiki/Belarusians_in_Latvia" title="Belarusians in Latvia">Belarusians</a>) that make up around one third of <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Latvia#Ethnic_groups" title="Demographics of Latvia">Latvia's population</a>, names popular among the Slavic population make it high on this list, for example, the most popular male name in Russia <i><a href="/wiki/Aleksandr" class="mw-redirect" title="Aleksandr">Aleksandr</a></i> (or <i><a href="/wiki/Aleksandrs" title="Aleksandrs">Aleksandrs</a></i> in its Latvian rendition) makes it as the second most common name in Latvia if all ethnicities are counted.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
