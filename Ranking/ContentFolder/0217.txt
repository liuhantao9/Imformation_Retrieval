<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Aptronym&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <i><a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica" title="Encyclopædia Britannica">Encyclopædia Britannica</a></i> attributes the term to <a href="/wiki/Franklin_P._Adams" title="Franklin P. Adams">Franklin P. Adams</a>, a writer who coined it as an anagram of <i>patronym</i>, to emphasize "apt".<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to Frank Nuessel, in <i>The Study of Names</i> (1992), an <i>aptonym</i> is the term used for "people whose names and occupations or situations (e.g., workplace) have a close correspondence."
</p><p>In the book <i>What's in a Name?</i> (1996), author <a href="/wiki/Paul_Dickson_(writer)" title="Paul Dickson (writer)">Paul Dickson</a> cites a long list of aptronyms originally compiled by Professor Lewis P. Lipsitt, of <a href="/wiki/Brown_University" title="Brown University">Brown University</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Psychologist <a href="/wiki/Carl_Jung" title="Carl Jung">Carl Jung</a> wrote in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Synchronicity_(book)" title="Synchronicity (book)">Synchronicity</a></i> that there was a "sometimes quite grotesque coincidence between a man's name and his peculiarities".<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Nominative_determinism" title="Nominative determinism">Nominative determinism</a> is a hypothesis which suggests a causal relationship based on the idea that people tend to be attracted to areas of work that fit their name.
</p>
